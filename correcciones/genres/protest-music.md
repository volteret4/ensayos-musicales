# genre - Protest Music

## curiosities
**Anti-Thatcher Protest Songs – Heaven 17, English Beat, Morrissey, Poll Tax Coalition** : Margaret Thatcher as Prime Minister generated numerous protest songs: "We Don't Need This Fascist Groove Thing" by Heaven 17, "Stand Down Margaret" by the English Beat, and Morrissey's "Margaret on the Guillotine." Towards the end of the 1980s, the Poll Tax — a new system of taxation widely seen as unfair to the poor — inspired anti-Poll Tax songs from The Jam, Billy Bragg, the Exploited, and others. "Musicians expressed fierce opposition to something known as the Pole Tax, a new system of taxation put in place by Thatcher." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YsNRNKvd1GI
**Civil Rights Song Catalog – Thousands of Tracks; Sam Cooke, Nina Simone, Curtis Mayfield** : The civil rights movement generated a massive catalog of protest music, including "A Change Is Gonna Come" by Sam Cooke (1964), "Mississippi Goddam" by Nina Simone (1964), "People Get Ready" by Curtis Mayfield (1965), and Gil Scott-Heron's "The Revolution Will Not Be Televised" (1971 B-side), considered one of the greatest black liberation songs of all time and an important precursor to rap. "If you were to create a playlist of civil rights songs, it would contain literally thousands of tracks." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dCelQ8Zj_cg
**Cold War Songs 1984 – OMD "Enola Gay," Rush "Distant Early Warning," Pink Floyd, Nena** : In addition to Frankie Goes to Hollywood's "Two Tribes," the nuclear fears of 1984 generated anti-war protest songs including "Red Skies" by The Fix, "Enola Gay" by OMD, "Two Suns in the Sunset" from Pink Floyd, "99 Luftballons" by Nena, and "Distant Early Warning" from Rush. This was a period when the US and USSR genuinely came close to nuclear conflict on multiple occasions. "It was a very scary time." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YsNRNKvd1GI
**Definition of a Protest Song – Phil Ochs: Specific, Oppositional, and Cannot Support the State** : Phil Ochs defined a protest song as one "so specific that you cannot mistake it for BS." Such songs must be in direct opposition to something, offer some sort of alternate solution, and arouse emotion in both performer and listener. Protest music has been heard around the world and in all cultures — not just North America, but Europe, Africa, and Asia — and cannot support the state. "If you like things as they are, what's your problem? There's nothing to protest against." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dCelQ8Zj_cg
**Origins of Protest Songs – 1860s African-American Spirituals Through IWW Little Red Songbook** : The earliest Western protest songs include African-American spirituals and work songs protesting slavery, such as "No More Auction Block" dating to the 19th century. By the 1900s, the labor movement generated protest music, with the IWW distributing its Little Red Songbook featuring songs by Joe Hill, many set to popular hymn tunes so they could spread quickly. "Messages of protests were often encoded in work songs, religious chants, and various compositions of lament and woe." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dCelQ8Zj_cg
**Punk as Protest – British Punks Against Monarchy, Class System, Unemployment** : When punk exploded in the UK, it was very much "us against them": against the monarchy, the government, the class system, and the bleak economic prospects for young people. Beyond the Sex Pistols, protest punk included Crass (anarchists), Stiff Little Fingers (Northern Ireland and the Troubles), and The Jam (Down in the Tube Station at Midnight, Going Underground). "British punks were against the monarchy, the government, the class system, and their crappy chances at meaningful employment." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dCelQ8Zj_cg
**Riot Grrrl Movement – Olympia WA, Feminist Punk, Girls to the Front Policy, Third Wave Feminism** : The riot grrrl movement began around Olympia, Washington as a very indie, underground wave of feminist punk bands tired of the testosterone-filled rock scene. Bands included Bikini Kill, Bratmobile, Team Dresch, Heavens to Betsy, and British band Huggy Bear. Shows featured a "girls to the front" policy and were made into safe spaces. The movement is considered a branch of third wave feminism, which pushed for individualism, diversity, sex positivity, and women's roles in politics and society. It also generated zines, manifestos, and activism beyond music. "The first riot girls were tired of the testosterone-filled, dude-heavy rock scene and demanded that they get a voice." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YsNRNKvd1GI
**Why Protest Songs Seem to Disappear – Media Fragmentation, Ultra-Short Attention Spans, 100,000 Songs Uploaded Daily** : The perception that protest songs have disappeared is explained by media fragmentation. The 24-hour news cycle was born June 1, 1980 when CNN signed on. Social media has shortened even that to 12 hours. By the time a moment arrives that might inspire a protest song, the world has already moved on. Meanwhile, 100,000 new songs are uploaded to streaming services every single day, making it nearly impossible for any protest song to gain the traction it once could. As a comparison, Neil Young heard about the Kent State massacre on May 4, 1970; saw pictures in Life magazine a week later; wrote Ohio; recorded it with CSNY on May 21; and it was in stores in early June — yet that one-month timeline produced one of the greatest protest songs ever. "How does an artist capture a moment with a protest song if that moment disappears in less than half a day?" ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YsNRNKvd1GI
**Women's Rights Protest Songs – Leslie Gore, Aretha Franklin, Loretta Lynn (Banned)** : Key protest songs for women's rights include "You Don't Own Me" by Lesley Gore (1963, a woman demanding autonomy), "Respect" by Aretha Franklin (1967, recontextualized into a women's anthem), "I Am Woman" by Helen Reddy (1972), and "The Pill" by Loretta Lynn (1975), which was banned by many radio stations for its discussion of birth control. "A woman demanding autonomy and refusing to be dominated by a man? Radical stuff at the time." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dCelQ8Zj_cg
