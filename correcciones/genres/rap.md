# genre - Rap

## curiosities
**"Licensed to Ill" Billboard Milestone** : The Beastie Boys' album *Licensed to Ill* made history by becoming the first rap record to reach number one on the Billboard charts. "a monster album that became the first rap record to reach number one on the Billboard charts." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sn5TWBFd0XM
**80s Alt-Rock Radio Inclusion** : Early rap was among the diverse genres featured on alt-rock radio playlists in the 1980s, demonstrating the broad range of music played before the rise of grunge. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eqTFinLk3oU
**Birth on New York Streets** : Rap and hip hop originated on the streets of New York during the 1970s, concurrent with the disco era in clubs. This scene involved quick-handed DJs grabbing beats off records while MCs rhymed over them, leading to competitions for "fattest beats" and "dopest rhymes." "When disco was happening in the clubs back in the 70s, rap and hip hop was being born on the streets of New York." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OiqdZfxdBP0
**Blamed for Violence and Crime** : Rap music, alongside hip hop, has frequently been blamed for contributing to violence and crime in society. This accusation is part of a recurring historical pattern where new music genres are scapegoated for social ills, similar to how hot jazz, rhythm and blues, and rock and roll were blamed in previous decades. "How many times have you heard that about hip hop and rap?" ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=L0j9iDuHQVA
**Development with SL-1200 Turntable** : The Technics SL-1200 turntable and its inspired technologies were crucial for the emergence of rap, facilitating the innovative scratching and beat mixing techniques central to the genre. "Hip-hop and rap could not have been invented if it were not for the SL-1200 and the machines it inspired." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IIJySSo276k
**Early Perception as a Fad** : In the late 1970s and early 1980s, rap and hip hop struggled to gain acceptance outside of New York urban areas. A significant majority of the music market, including the industry and fans, viewed rap as a passing "fad" that would quickly die out, dismissing it with phrases like "rap, crap." They believed "real music" involved traditional singing and instruments, not scratching and sampling. "As hard as it is to believe today, a sizeable majority of the music market, the industry and fans, looked at Rap as a fad, something that would quickly die out." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=cYwk-B3qwwk
**Evolution in the 1990s** : In the 1990s, rap rhymes "got tougher and more complicated," and the overall vibe of the genre began to change, influencing alt rock. "Rhymes got tougher and more complicated. The whole vibe began to change." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5bQz8R0gbXc
**Historical Roots in Flyting** : Rap battles have a long history, dating back as far as the fifth century in England and Scotland. This ancient practice was known as "Flyting" (F-L-Y-T-I-N-G), which involved verbal throwdowns of clever and insulting verses exchanged between combatants. "Rap Battles. Go back as far as the fifth century in England and Scotland with the practice of something called Flighting, F-L-Y-T-I-N-G." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GO0K_N7Ys7I
**Mainstream Dismissal of Early Stars** : Despite the presence of genuine stars and musical geniuses like Run DMC, Curtis Blow, Afrika Bambaataa, Grandmaster Flash, and LL Cool J, the rap scene and its sounds were largely not taken seriously by the mainstream music industry and public in the early to mid-1980s. "Run DMC, Curtis Blow, Africa Bambada, Grandmaster Flash, LL Cool J, The Scene and the Sounds were just not taken seriously." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=cYwk-B3qwwk
**Reaching White Ears and Performers** : Rap, while huge on its own, increasingly saw its sound and attitude reach "white ears" and be adopted by "white rock performers," leading to a new sound that was part rock and part rap. "Rap was huge on its own, but more and more of that sound and attitude not only began to reach white ears, but that sound and attitude was adopted by white rock performers." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5bQz8R0gbXc
**Sound World vs. Music World** : Hip hop thrives in a "world of sound," meaning its sonic possibilities are broader and less constrained than traditional rock music because sounds can be sourced from anywhere. This approach is likened to early avant-garde electronic composers who prioritized finding ways to play sounds beyond traditional notes. "See, hip hop lives in a world of sound, not just in a world of music." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OiqdZfxdBP0
**Straight Up Rap Artists** : The 1990s included "straight up rap" artists such as Tupac, Biggie, Dre, and Snoop, alongside various blends and hybrids that crossed into the alternative world. "in addition to straight up rap like Tupac and Biggie and Dre and Snoop." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5bQz8R0gbXc
**Streaming Success in Rap** : Rap has significantly benefited from the "musical moneyball" approach, demonstrating high streaming success that leads labels to prioritize time and money in promoting this genre. "Hip hop, rap, R&B, and pop have benefited the most from this musical money ball because those genres have demonstrated the most streaming success." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Sc9HApnyBLM
**Strong Performance in 2000** : Along with hip hop, rap was "incredibly strong, incredibly popular" in 2000, becoming the second biggest selling genre after pop, with artists like Eminem, Nelly, and Dr. Dre. "Second place was rap and hip hop, thanks to people like Eminem, Nelly, and Dr. Dre." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=MK7L0eCpDHc
**Vinyl Preference for Sampling (Post-CD Era)** : Rap and hip-hop performers continued to use old school records for samples and grooves, revering their historical importance and preferring vinyl for its tactile nature in mixing and scratching. "Rap and hip-hop performers. They kept going back to old school records for samples and grooves." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GES0Ms_kaCI
**Vocal Expression Controversy** : Rappers were among the artists whose new forms of vocal expression, facilitated by microphones, generated decades of strong criticism from purists. Their singing styles were considered by some to be "degenerate forms of singing," challenging the established vocal norms of earlier generations. "R&B acts, Elvis, The Beatles, Dylan, Jagger, New Waivers, rappers all the way up to and beyond death medillars." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=L0j9iDuHQVA
