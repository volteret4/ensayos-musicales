# genre - Soul

## curiosities
**Les Paul Guitar's Versatility** : The Gibson Les Paul guitar has been continuously in production since its 1952 introduction and is widely used across virtually every genre, including soul. Its solid-body design resolved feedback issues common with older hollow-body guitars, making it ideal for amplified soul music. "The Les Paul has been in production ever since, and has been used by guitars into rock, punk, metal, pop, country, soul, R&B, jazz, reggae, and virtually every other genre you want to mention." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Y0HW8LQKvJw
**R&B Mutation** : Soul, identified as one of the primary sounds within R&B, experienced rapid mutation after 1978, influencing the emergence of new styles such as disco and hip hop. "R&B's main sounds, which included fun console, began mutating even faster, expanding to include disco, and within a year, hip hop." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bYsV3ozbk4M

**Black Music for Black People** : During the Black Power movement, soul music was increasingly made by and for African Americans, reflecting the movement's goals of self-determination. Artists like Curtis Mayfield emphasized that the primary intention was to create music for the black community, with crossover success to white audiences being a secondary consideration, if it happened at all. "Curtis Mayfield said that black musicians were making black music for black people and if it crossed over it did. But that was not the point." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 22 - Power to the People - The Music of the Black Power Movement | https://seventies.libsyn.com/022-power-to-the-people-62520-11-01-am
**British Youth Culture Appeal** : Soul music, alongside R&B, Reggae, and Ska (known as Bluebeat), was embraced by segments of British youth culture like the mods, rockers, and teddy boys, and later by early British punks. "This slightly evolved form of SCAT lived side by side with R&B and Soul and Reggae. **Decline of a "Brand of Soul"** : By the mid-1970s, a particular "brand of soul" music began to fade. Radio programmers perceived it as repetitive, and listeners felt its themes about persistent life problems were no longer resonating as powerfully. This decline coincided with the rise of disco, which offered an escape through dance. "From the perspective of the music, radio programmers thought that if it was getting kind of repetitive and that the people who had been buying it felt like it had more than thin that it was time to move on." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 22 - Power to the People - The Music of the Black Power Movement | https://seventies.libsyn.com/022-power-to-the-people-62520-11-01-am
