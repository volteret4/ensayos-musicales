# genre - Heavy Metal

## curiosities
**Birth through Devil's Triad (1970)** : Heavy metal is often considered by some to have originated in 1970 with Black Sabbath's debut album and title track. This birth was characterized by Tony Iomi's distinctive use of the tri tone—a flattened fifth played with a trill and distortion—an adaptation inspired by Gustav Holst's "Mars, the Bringer of War." "For some, this is the birth of heavy metal." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=41eKVp8JIhU
**Component of Trap Metal** : Heavy metal is identified as a core component of the Trap Metal genre, blending with trap music to create this hybrid sound. "this genre brings together trap music, a type of hip hop that came out of Atlanta and heavy metal" ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tdTs-4Irv8c
**Early Print Musical Reference (1970)** : The earliest verified print reference to "heavy metal" in a musical context appeared on February 7, 1970, in Cream Magazine. Music critic Lester Bangs described The Guess Who as "heavy metal robots," though this was intended as an insult rather than a definitive genre label. "They're quite refreshing in the wake of all the heavy metal robots of the year past." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=D8GU8ZpSgY8
**Emergence as Genre Descriptor (Mid-1970s)** : The term "heavy metal" entered the music vernacular in the mid-1970s, gaining widespread use to describe a new wave of "loud, heavy guitar rock." This sound was pioneered by bands like Led Zeppelin, Black Sabbath, and Deep Purple, solidifying "heavy metal" as one of rock's most significant subgenres. "By the mid-1970s, everybody was using it." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=D8GU8ZpSgY8
**Enhanced Wood Consumption by Termites** : Studies have indicated that termites consume wood at double the rate when exposed to heavy metal music. "Germites will eat wood two times faster if they're exposed to heavy metal." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0D4wGWI-_z8
**Etymological Research (2013)** : The origin of the term "heavy metal" when applied to music was extensively researched by Dina Weinstein, a professor of sociology at DePaul University in Chicago. In 2013, she published a paper titled "Just So Stories, How Heavy Metal Got Its Name, A Cautionary Tale," attempting to solve this etymological puzzle by examining various sources. "She wrote a paper entitled Just So Stories, How Heavy Metal Got Its Name, A Cautionary Tale." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=D8GU8ZpSgY8

**Initial Lack of Soundgarden Recognition (1991)** : In early 1991, during the filming of *Singles*, Soundgarden's *Louder Than Love* album, despite being on a major label, did not register significantly "outside the metal community." This indicates that even within heavy music subgenres, the broader appeal of what would become grunge was still limited. "Yes, Soundgarden had signed to a major label and had released their louder than love album, but outside the metal community, it didn't really register." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zIkjd1OjCXM
**Lollapalooza Purist Conflict** : The inclusion of Metallica, a heavy metal band, on the 1996 Lollapalooza tour caused a rift with "true alternative people" who felt they did not belong, highlighting a perceived genre incompatibility within the alternative scene. "Many of the early adopters of alternative, true alternative people, felt that Metallica and their fans didn't belong." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tFBSrKMEZxI

Critics viewed this music as corrupting youth, turning them into "rebellious, disrespectful, and anti-establishment goons," prompting calls for its immediate cessation. "Then, the metal and punk of the 1970s just got them into more of a froth. This music is turning our children into rebellious, disrespectful, and anti-establishment goons. It must be stopped." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=41eKVp8JIhU

**Origins from Black Sabbath's Sound** : The distinctive sound of Black Sabbath, characterized by its heaviness, sinister quality, and power, which resulted from Tony Iommi's adaptive guitar techniques after an industrial accident, significantly contributed to the creation of heavy metal as a genre. "And the Sabbath sound went a long way to creating the sound of heavy metal as we know it today." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=V7WjNR1ujhk
**Ozzy Osbourne's 1982 World Tour Promotion** : An advertisement for Ozzy Osbourne's 1982 World Tour promised an "awesome garage of sound and light" that would "rip through you in a rage of heavy metal fury," showcasing the genre's aggressive appeal. "an awesome garage of sound in light will rip through you in a rage of heavy metal fury." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OCkov6FTliE
**Proliferation of Distorted Chords (1960s-1970s)** : The rise of heavy distorted music in the 1960s and 1970s led to a widespread proliferation of chords like the devil's tri tone. This sonic characteristic became ubiquitous, notably in songs by foundational artists such as Black Sabbath and Jimi Hendrix. "And as heavy distorted music took off in the 1960s and 70s, these kinds of chords proliferated everywhere." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=41eKVp8JIhU
**Scientific Origin** : The term "heavy metals" originates from chemistry and metallurgy, referring to actual metals with high densities such as iron, copper, tin, silver, gold, platinum, gallium, thallium, and hafnium. This scientific usage predates its musical application. "Heavy metals are actual metals with high densities." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=D8GU8ZpSgY8
**Shift in UK Market Dominance** : This increased chart presence marked a shift from a long period where American metal and hard rock bands, with few exceptions like Accept and Scorpions, largely dominated the British market. "And this was a nice change from a long stretch where American metal and hard rock bands pretty much had the British market to themselves, with a couple of bands like Accept and Scorpions thrown in." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sOU54d8mr5k
**William S. Burrows' Literary Usage (1961-1965)** : Writer and poet William S. Burrows used the character "Uranian Willie, the Heavy Metal Kid" in his 1961 novel "The Soft Machine" and a 1965 spoken word performance. While the term appeared, Burrows was discussing aliens and addictive drugs, not music, and thus his work is not considered the origin of the musical genre's name. "Burrows isn't talking about music at all. He's actually talking about aliens and addictive drugs." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=D8GU8ZpSgY8
