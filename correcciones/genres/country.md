**1950s Classification** : Country was one of only four main classifications for music in the 1950s, alongside rock, pop, and R&B, illustrating the much simpler genre landscape of that era compared to contemporary music. "This meant more country and folk and hillbilly and R&B were heard on the radio." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GES0Ms_kaCI

**Growth During Musicians' Strike (1942)** : During the American Federation of Musicians strike of 1942, country musicians were not considered "real musicians" by the performing rights organization ACAPE, and thus were not covered by recording prohibitions. This loophole allowed them to continue making records, leading to a rapid spread of country music in the absence of new Big Band recordings. "Acape also did not consider country musicians, western performers, and R&B acts to be real musicians. As a result, they were not covered by any prohibitions." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=V7WjNR1ujhk
**Increased Radio Exposure (WWII Era)** : With local American radio stations allowed local programming after 7 pm, and records becoming more cost-effective than live acts, country music gained significant airplay. This allowed people to hear the genre for the first time, contributing to its spread alongside other previously less-heard styles. "But it also made room for country, western, hillbilly, and R&B records. People started to hear other genres on the radio for the very first time." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=V7WjNR1ujhk
