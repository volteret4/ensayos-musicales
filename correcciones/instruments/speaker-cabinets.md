# instrument - Speaker Cabinets

## curiosities
**Curved Line Arrays (Post-1990s)** : As the 1990s progressed, line arrays evolved to incorporate a slight curve. This design refinement enabled "more clear, more focused sound" by minimizing interference and efficiently directing sound. Due to their "highly directional nature," little amplifier power is wasted on walls or the space above the audience, a technology now dominant in the concert industry. "As the 1990s passed, the up and down line arrays developed a slight curve." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=qrrwqdpCjpQ
**Hoisting from Rafters (Bill Hanley)** : Bill Hanley pioneered the practice of hoisting speaker cabinets from venue rafters using chain-driven electric motors. This innovation not only "freed up more performance space on the stage" but also allowed the music to "flow out over the crowd rather than down into it," significantly improving sound dispersion. "Hanley was the first guy to hoist speaker cabinets from the rafters using chain driven electric motors." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=qrrwqdpCjpQ
**Horizontal Piles (1970s)** : Throughout most of the 1970s, typical concert sound systems consisted of "horizontal piles of black speaker boxes" stacked on either side of the stage. These boxes were often "aimed haphazardly" without much consideration for venue acoustics, reflecting the prevailing attitude of "make it loud enough, and nobody's going to care." "During this period, which, honestly, consisted most of the 1970s, concert sound systems consisted of horizontal piles of black speaker boxes on either side of the stage." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=qrrwqdpCjpQ
**Integral to Marshall Stack – 12-inch and 19-inch Speakers** : The speaker cabinets developed by Jim Marshall and his staff in 1963, featuring 12-inch and 19-inch speakers, were a crucial component alongside the Marshall amplifier. These cabinets were designed to be "loudest, cleanest, most rugged" and quickly became a hit with British musicians, establishing themselves as standard gear for guitar players. "This amp and the speaker cabinets with 12 inch and 19 inch speakers became a big hit with the new generation of British musicians and Jim stuff is still standard gear for guitar players who want to go big or go home." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gsSTJWN6cUo
**Line Source Systems (1990s)** : The 1990s brought about the major development of "line source systems," which are single rows of speakers hanging from rigging. While this is one of the oldest PA system concepts, it only became practical in the 90s due to more powerful and affordable amplifiers and efficient, safe hanging methods, making setup and teardown quick and easy. "The big development was line source systems. These are single rows of speakers hanging from the rigging of the rafters." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=qrrwqdpCjpQ
**Marshall's Early Innovations – Home-Made Cabinets** : Jim Marshall, an engineer with an interest in sound and amplification, began selling musical gear at his London drum shop, opened in 1960. Among the items he sold were giant speaker cabinets that he made at home, preceding the development of the iconic Marshall amplifier and stack. "But it began to sell gear including some giant speaker cabinets that he made at home." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gsSTJWN6cUo
**Semi-Cylindrical Vertical Clusters (1970s-1980s)** : PA technicians discovered that organizing speakers into "semi-cylindrical vertical clusters" and hanging them from scaffolding or roofs produced improved results for sound dispersion. Although this was a significant step forward, it was later found that these clusters could only be made "so big before an unacceptable amount of distortion set in." "PA text learned that organizing speakers into semi-cylurgical vertical clusters produced the best results." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=qrrwqdpCjpQ
**Visual Impact of Stacking** : As amplifier technology evolved, it became common practice for heavy bands to visually enhance their stage presence by stacking numerous Marshall speaker cabinets, creating "giant walls." Often, only a portion of these cabinets were actually plugged in, but their sheer volume conveyed an impressive aesthetic. "It became a thing for heavy bands to have giant walls of Marshall cabinets on stage. And to be honest, only some of them were actually plugged in." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Y0HW8LQKvJw
