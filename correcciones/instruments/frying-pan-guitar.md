# instrument - Frying Pan (guitar)

## curiosities
**A25 Naming Convention** : The "A" in Rickenbacker A25 indicated it was the company's first guitar model, while "25" referred to the 25-inch length of its neck. A later model, the A22, became a better seller. "The A indicated that it was the company's first guitar, and the 25 was the length in inches of its neck. There was soon an A22, which was a better seller." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=E6cXyaHALBQ
**Commercial Availability and Initial Sales** : The frying pan was the first commercially available electric guitar, with the A25 model being introduced by Roe Pa Tyn in 1932. Despite its historical significance, it was not an immediate commercial success, selling only 28 units in 1932 due to the Great Depression. However, sales increased to almost 1300 by 1935. "While the frying pan was the first commercially available electric guitar, it wasn't exactly a hit. A few people were impressed by it, but even fewer thought that it had any kind of potential or future, but they were wrong. ... the first electric guitar to go on sale was the Rickenbacher A25 in 1932. ... only 28 were sold in 1932. That, however, increased to almost 1300 by 1935." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=E6cXyaHALBQ
**Elvino Ray's Modification for Aesthetics** : Shortly after its release, guitarist Elvino Ray purchased a frying pan guitar and modified its appearance by mounting it within a wooden frame to make it resemble a more conventional Spanish guitar, seeking to avoid questions about its unusual look. "Shortly afterwards, a guitarist named Elvino Ray bought a frying pan and mounted it inside a wooden frame so it looked more like a regular Spanish guitar. He was bored with people asking him about that weird instrument he was playing." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=E6cXyaHALBQ
**First Prototype and Appearance** : The prototype for George Beechum's electric guitar featured a distinctive round body attached to a long neck, resembling a banjo, but it became known as "the frying pan" due to its visual similarity to the kitchen utensil. "After much experimentation, a prototype was unveiled. It featured a round body attached to a long neck. It looked a lot like a banjo, but instead it acquired a different name, the frying pan. It really did look like one." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=E6cXyaHALBQ
**Gage Brewer as First Customer** : Gage Brewer was the first customer to purchase a Rickenbacker A25, acquiring it on September 21, 1932. "It seems to be a guy named Gage Brewer, who bought an A25 on September 21, 1932. In fact, he was the guitar maker's first ever customer." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=E6cXyaHALBQ
**Mechanism of Sound Production** : When the steel strings of the frying pan guitar were struck, picked, or strummed, their vibrations generated an electrical signal within the wire coil around a magnet (the pickup). This signal then traveled through a wire to an amplifier, allowing for adjustable volume control. "When its steel strings were struck or picked or strummed, the vibrations created an electrical signal in that coil of wire around the magnet. That signal then traveled down a wire to an amplifier, which could be adjusted to whatever volume you wanted." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=E6cXyaHALBQ
