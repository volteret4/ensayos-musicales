# instrument - Fuzz Box

## curiosities
**Accidental Origin (1960)** : The concept of the fuzz box emerged serendipitously in 1960 when session musician Grady Martin's amplifier and guitar unexpectedly produced a cool, fuzzy, and distorted sound during a recording session with Marty Robbins in Nashville. Martin and his collaborators subsequently reverse-engineered this accidental effect to create an electronic circuit, activated by a foot pedal, capable of reproducing the sound on demand. "They called this a fuzz box." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PNiL63fY1Y8
**Commercialization and Musical Impact (Unspecified Year)** : The Maestro Fuzz Tone FZ1 is widely recognized as the first commercially available fuzz box, quickly followed by other brands such as Big Muff, Fuzz Face, and Tone Bender. These devices dramatically changed the electric guitar's sound, making it more powerful, menacing, and capable of sustained notes, profoundly influencing rock music by popularizing what was previously considered "painful noise." "The fuzz box became glorious and primal and fierce." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PNiL63fY1Y8
**Distinctive Sound of "Spirit in the Sky"** : The distinctive fuzz tone heard in Norman Greenbaum's "Spirit in the Sky" originated from a fuzz box that was built directly into his Fender guitar. This gear choice was instrumental in giving the song its unique sound.
