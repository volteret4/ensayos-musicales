**Funky Drummer (1970)** : James Brown's 1970 song "Funky Drummer" features a drum break by Clyde Stubblefield that has been sampled in nearly 2,000 other songs. This specific drum beat became a highly influential and frequently used sample in various musical genres. "That's James Brown's drummer Clyde Stubbelfield from a 1970 song called Funky Drummer." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ak6OuOUkRrQ ← james-brown
**Give It Up or Turnit a Loose (1969)** : This classic funk track, released in 1969, contains a distinctive "break" that is frequently sampled and is considered woven into the DNA of hip hop. The "break" refers to a 10-15 second section of bass guitar and drums that formed the backbone of 70s funk. Dean of rock critics Robert Christgau noted that between 1969 and 1971, Brown scored 17 top 10 R&B hits that reshaped black dancing and paved the way for disco. "The break in this 1969 classic from James Brown is woven into the DNA of hip hop." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 6 - The 70s Roots of Hip Hop | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-6-the-70s-roots-of-hip-hop
**Say It Loud – I'm Black and I'm Proud (1968)** : Written by James Brown and his band leader, Pee Wee Ellis, this song was released in the summer of 1968 and became a number one hit on the Billboard R&B charts for over a month. It served as a powerful anthem for black power and black pride. A legend, possibly started by Brown himself, suggests he wrote it after the Black Panther Party left a hand grenade on his doorstep, though its importance and sincerity remain undiminished. Randall Kennedy, a Harvard law professor, noted in 2018 that singing this song felt exhilarating and marked a period where the term "Negro" was rejected in favor of "black," until Jesse Jackson led a movement in 1988 to replace "black" with "African American." It was James Brown's last pop hit, and he lost many white fans due to its message, as white listeners often interpreted "black power" as "anti-white." "I was trying to do two things. One, give the power structure, which in America means the white power structure, a way to understand how we felt and know that we had people who could do things and just wanted a fair shake. Two, I wanted young black kids to wake up and realize that they should be proud of who they were, get an education and try to make something of themselves." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 22 - Power to the People - The Music of the Black Power Movement | https://seventies.libsyn.com/022-power-to-the-people-62520-11-01-am
**The Original Disco Man (1979)** : Released ten years after "Give It Up or Turnit a Loose," this song appears to be James Brown giving himself credit for inventing disco. Robert Christgau, a tough critic, rated this song an A-. This release highlights Brown's significant influence on dance music, extending from funk to the emerging disco genre. "10 years later, Brown released the original disco man which appears to be JB giving himself credit for inventing disco?" ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 6 - The 70s Roots of Hip Hop | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-6-the-70s-roots-of-hip-hop

**Influence on Disco and R&B** : Between 1969 and 1971, James Brown scored 17 top 10 R&B hits, which profoundly influenced black dancing and laid the groundwork for disco. During this period, while white audiences were largely listening to bands like Creedence and The Stones, Brown maintained significant interracial fame, particularly appealing to younger black audiences, before largely fading from mainstream white consciousness. His contributions were crucial in shaping the sound that would later be adopted by house DJs and sampled by hip hop artists.
