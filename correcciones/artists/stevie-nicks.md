## curiosities

**Aspiration for "Straight Up Rock and Roll"** : Upon becoming a solo artist, Stevie Nicks expressed a desire to make a "Tom Petty album" and pursue "straight up rock and roll," indicating a preference for a harder sound than Fleetwood Mac's 70s "softer rock." "She wanted to make a Tom Petty album. At least that's what she said when she became a solo artist. She wanted to do what she called straight up rock and roll." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 7 - Women Who Rocked the 70s | http://www.ftr70.com
**Double Induction into Rock and Roll Hall of Fame** : Stevie Nicks was inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame twice: first with Fleetwood Mac in 1998, and then as a solo artist 21 years later, making her the first woman to achieve this honor. Twenty-two men had received this honor previously. "Stevie Nicks was inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame for the second time. She was inducted with Fleetwood Mac in 1998, and then 21 years later as a solo artist." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 7 - Women Who Rocked the 70s | http://www.ftr70.com
**Early Career Sexism Experience** : Stevie Nicks recalled a single instance early in her career where a man in the crowd made "lute remarks," and legendary concert promoter Bill Graham immediately intervened, telling him to leave. She considered her overall experience with sexism in the industry to be relatively easy. "She can remember one time, very early in her career, when a guy in the crowd made some lute remarks to her, and the legendary concert promoter Bill Graham told him to essentially get the fuck out, and that was that." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 7 - Women Who Rocked the 70s | http://www.ftr70.com
**Entry into Fleetwood Mac (1975)** : She joined Fleetwood Mac in 1975, as her boyfriend Lindsey Buckingham made her inclusion a non-negotiable condition for his own acceptance into the band. "Lindsey Buckingham insisted that his girlfriend Stevie Nicks be accepted into what remained of Fleetwood Mac at that time when Lindsey Buckingham was invited to fill the void." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 51 - The Softer Side of 70s Rock | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-51-the-softer-side-of-70s-rock
**Involvement in Internal Love Triangle** : She was part of a "messy love triangle" with Lindsey Buckingham and Mick Fleetwood, a significant source of the intense internal drama and lyrical material for the band during the *Rumours* era. "relationship drama Stevie and Mick and Stevie and Lindsey form a messy love triangle." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 51 - The Softer Side of 70s Rock | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-51-the-softer-side-of-70s-rock
**Pledge with Christine McVie** : On the day Stevie Nicks joined Fleetwood Mac, she and Christine McVie made a mutual vow never to be treated as "less than" or "second class citizens" within the male-dominated rock environment. They promised to fight for their place, ensure their songs and music were equally as good as the men's, and be included in creative discussions. "She said that they said to each other, we will never be treated like second class citizens. We will never be not allowed to hang out in a room full of intelligent, crazy rock and roll stars, because we are just as crazy and just as intelligent as they are." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 7 - Women Who Rocked the 70s | http://www.ftr70.com
**San Francisco Scene Influence** : Stevie Nicks was part of the San Francisco music scene, learning from artists like Janis Joplin, Grace Slick, and Buffalo Springfield, which she considered a "literal rock and roll school." "Now, Stevie and Lindsey Buckingham were part of that whole San Francisco scene that Janice Joplin was part of. Stevie learned from watching Janice and Grace Slick and Buffalo Springfield, a literal rock and roll school." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 7 - Women Who Rocked the 70s | http://www.ftr70.com

**Dreams (1977)** : She wrote this song, which became Fleetwood Mac's sole number one hit. Ranked #9 on *Rolling Stone*'s "500 greatest songs of all time," its lyrics resonated deeply with listeners, particularly "boomers" navigating divorce and relationship breakdowns in the late 1970s, as they explored themes of freedom and the pain of lost love.
