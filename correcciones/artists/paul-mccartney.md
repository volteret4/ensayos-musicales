**Band on the Run (1973)** : This album, McCartney's fifth since The Beatles' breakup, was recorded at EMI studios in Nigeria, a session described as "a mess" and "one misadventure after another." The challenges began with two band members quitting Wings before departure, and intensified when Paul and Linda McCartney were robbed at knifepoint in Nigeria, losing the original demo tapes for the title track. Despite McCartney's hopes for a tourist-like recording experience, the local equipment quality was subpar. To overcome these hurdles, McCartney hired Tony Visconti to write and conduct a 60-piece orchestra. The album itself does not meet the full criteria for a rock opera, but its title track arguably does. "The recording of band on the run at the EMI studios in Nigeria was a mess. In terms of it was one misadventure after another." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 42 - Rock Operas of the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-42-rock-operas-of-the-70s

**Band on the Run (1974)** : Released as a single in the US in April 1974, following "Jet," this song quickly ascended to number one on the Billboard charts. The track is structured in three distinct parts, chronicling a vague story of a persecuted band's escape from prison. McCartney described it as a "nod" to the prevailing sentiment among young people of "getting away, vigilantes, desparados." The song transitions from a slow, languid opening ("Back inside these four walls...") to a funk rock statement ("Never ever get out of here...") before an orchestra dramatically heralds the band's liberation in the third section, creating a "totally rock opera" feel. A shorter version was typically heard on the radio. In 1975, McCartney and Wings earned a Grammy for Best Pop Performance by a Duo or Group for this song. "On the run I think around about that time there was a sort of spirit amongst the young people of like getting away, vigilantes, desparados, there were records coming out a bit like that and I think that was me doing a nod to that kind of thing." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 42 - Rock Operas of the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-42-rock-operas-of-the-70s

**Accidental Feedback Discovery** : On October 18, 1964, during a recording session at Abbey Road Studios, Paul McCartney accidentally initiated the controlled feedback sound that would be used on "I Feel Fine." He plucked a string on his bass, and when John Lennon laid his semi-acoustic Gibson guitar against Paul's bass amp, the guitar picked up the bass note and started feeding back. Paul and John recognized the coolness of the sound and requested producer George Martin to record it. "The discovery began when Paul McCarty picked a string on his bass, Appropole of Nothing, and then John laid his semi-acoustic Gibson guitar against Paul's bass amp and started to walk away." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iGav9uJwA_w
