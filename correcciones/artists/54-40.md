# artist - 54-40

## members
- Brad Merritt
- Darryl Neudorf
- Matt Johnson
- Neil Osborne
- Phil Comparelli

## labels
- Modern Dance Music (Mode of Move)
- Reprise / Warner Bros. USA

## albums
**Dear Dear (1992) – First Album Without a US Release; Went Platinum in Canada; Recorded at Tom Petty's Studio** : Dear Dear was recorded at Tom Petty's Los Angeles studio and was the first 54-40 album not to receive an American release. It went gold while they opened for Tom Cochran on his "Life Is a Highway" tour — approximately 10 dates across Canada — and then went platinum. The Cochran tour put them in front of a new audience that had previously never encountered the band, and many of those fans have stayed loyal ever since. "Until this day, there's fans who are like, I was a fan as soon as I saw you guys at that Tom Cochran show." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GI4aaHX9Izo
**Fight for Love (1989 / 1990) – Last Warner Bros. Record; Recorded in Vancouver the Band's Way** : Recorded at home in Vancouver with live sound engineer Glenn Reely and Dave Ogilvie rather than in Los Angeles, Fight for Love was the third and final album of 54-40's deal with Warner Bros. USA. With the end of the deal in the air, the band insisted on making it their own way. The label still provided some tour support — described as "a goodbye kiss" — covering a Midwest and West Coast run finishing at the Whiskey, then continuing to Richmond, Virginia, to join the Bob Mould tour for six or seven shows. "We could tell that there was a divorce coming." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GI4aaHX9Izo
**Selection EP (1982) – First Release; Groovy and Instrumental-Leaning; 16-Track Recording in North Vancouver** : The first 54-40 recording, recorded at a 16-track studio in North Vancouver, was the Selection EP in 1982. The band described the sound as "groovy" and "viby" with lots of instrumental sections and a "loopy, spaced-out guitar thing." It was self-released through Mode of Move (Modern Dance Music), a Vancouver cooperative label that included bands Junky, Juniper, Animal Slaves, Popular Front, and others. The co-op met weekly in a house on Columbia Street and organized hall gigs and college mailing campaigns. "We would meet in Annie's house and have these meetings as if we were revolutionaries." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GI4aaHX9Izo
**Set the Fire (1984) – First Full Indie Album; Personnel Shift Changed Sound** : After Selection, drummer Ian Franion left and was replaced by Darryl Neudorf, who had been in a Kelowna band called Empty Set. Phil Comparelli, who had played trumpet at a show where Neil arranged horns, joined on bass, making the band a four-piece. Neil had always wanted a four-piece; the new lineup shifted the sound away from the instrumental groove-focused style and toward more conventional rock songwriting. "You get one guy gone and two guys in, your sound's going to change." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GI4aaHX9Izo
**Show Me (1987) – Overproduced by Band's Own Assessment; One Day in Your Life and One Gun Were Singles** : Recorded in Los Angeles with producer Dave Jordan, Show Me came out at the very beginning of what is now called the Can Rock Renaissance. The band wanted to return to the sound of the Green Album, but the record label pushed for a bigger production influenced by the rising sound of U2. Singles included "One Day in Your Life" and "One Gun." Neil Osborne later called it his least favorite 54-40 record, describing it as overproduced. A bridge for "One Day in Your Life" — unusual for a band that "didn't even know what bridges were" — was literally cut into a tape and physically spliced in by producer Jordan when he flew up from Los Angeles to Toronto during a show at the Diamond. "That was our first sort of compromise that I felt we made against our instinct." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GI4aaHX9Izo
**Smiley Buddha Cabaret (1994) – Named After Vancouver Venue; Ocean Pearl and Radio Love Song Were Singles** : Recorded during 1993–94, this album was named after a live music venue in Vancouver. Singles included "Ocean Pearl," "Radio Love Song," and others. The album appeared alongside a wave of other Canadian rock records during the height of the Can Rock Renaissance, alongside Matt Good, Tea Party, I Mother Earth, Barenaked Ladies, Sloan, Tragically Hip, Blue Rodeo, Odds, Grapes of Wrath, Cowboy Junkies, Alanis Morissette, Treble Charger, The Watchmen, Crash Test Dummies, and many others. The band was notably never on any edition of Big Shiny Tunes. "The 90s is when the Canadian rock scene was healthy, wealthy and wise." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GI4aaHX9Izo
**The Green Album / Self-Titled (1986) – First Major Label Release; Contained "Baby Ran" and "I Go Blind"** : The 1986 self-titled album (known as the Green Album) was recorded with and co-produced by Dave Jordan, who had mixed the Selection EP. Initially released independently, it was picked up by Reprise/Warner Bros. USA after 54-40 toured California — Warner Brothers Canada had shown no interest, and the band still has the rejection letter from Bob Roper at WB Canada. "Baby Ran" became a significant Canadian rock hit; the wall of guitars felt unusual in the mid-80s amid Technopop and ascending pop music. "I Go Blind" was another track from the record that would later find massive exposure through a Hootie & the Blowfish cover. Matt Johnson replaced Darryl Neudorf on drums starting September 1985 — Neudorf later went on to work with Sarah McLachlan — and played on "Take My Hand" on the album. "Baby Rand got a little MTV, but there was nobody pushing it because everybody was afraid." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GI4aaHX9Izo

## curiosities
**"I Go Blind" – Massive Second-Life Hit via Hootie & the Blowfish** : "I Go Blind" was a track on the 1986 Green Album. When Hootie & the Blowfish covered it and included it on a Friends soundtrack compilation in the 1990s, it became a massive hit on American radio. The story of what happened with that song and its second life was presumably covered in Part 2 of the "In Their Own Words" series. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GI4aaHX9Izo
**Band Name Origin – American History, James Polk's "54-40 or Fight" Manifest Destiny Slogan (1840s)** : The name 54-40 came from Brad Merritt's stream-of-consciousness naming session in which he generated ideas including "Bridge View Dogs" (after their Surrey rehearsal space area) and "Safeway Rebels." He landed on 54-40, the slogan of US presidential candidate James Polk, who in the 1840s argued that America should take the entire Oregon Territory up to latitude 54°40' North — which included what is now British Columbia, at the time shared with Britain. The two founders had both moved around extensively (Merritt in the US, Osborne across Canada) and had met in high school in Tsawwassen, literally on the US border. "I wanted something which was big and yet small at the same time." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GI4aaHX9Izo
**Featured in "In Their Own Words" Series** : 54-40 was featured in the "In Their Own Words" series, a program designed to capture the personal histories of bands directly from their members. "the Trues, and 5440." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bI6TTA-fn7c
**First Gig – December 8, 1980; John Lennon Shot That Day** : 54-40 played their first gig on December 8, 1980 in Vancouver — the same day John Lennon was shot. They learned of the shooting on the radio on the way to the show. They performed approximately four original songs on an open mic night, and the timing gave the performance an unexpectedly profound emotional weight. "One of those all quiet on the Western front type of moments where it's like, you know, Johnny's just been shot." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GI4aaHX9Izo
**Mode of Move Cooperative Label – Communist/Collective Label Run by Bands Including Future Manager Alan White** : Mode of Move stood for Modern Dance Music. Co-founder Alan White is still the band's manager today. The label operated as a collective, with bands meeting at a house on Columbia Street to divide up responsibilities: mailing cassettes to college stations, writing letters, organizing hall shows. The anarcho-cooperative spirit of the whole enterprise was very much in line with the Vancouver underground scene of the time. "We had these meetings as if we were revolutionaries." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GI4aaHX9Izo
**Vancouver Origins – Fear and Loathing Road Trip Down the Coast, Following Young Canadians (1980)** : Before forming 54-40 in the fall of 1980, Brad Merritt, Neil Osborne, and Randy (an early member) followed the Young Canadians on a Fear and Loathing–style road trip down the California coast in 1980, visiting venues like the Whisky a Go Go in Los Angeles. Vancouver's punk and alternative scene, including D.O.A. and the Young Canadians, regularly traveled the north-south corridor to Seattle, Portland, and San Francisco. "We felt super fortunate to have been a part of that, to see these bands and be part of that scene." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GI4aaHX9Izo
