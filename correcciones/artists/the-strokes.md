## albums
**Angles (2011)** : The band's fourth album was a slow process, taking two years for writing, demoing, and recording, marked by more producer problems before the band decided to handle production themselves. Julian Casablancas was increasingly absent, sending vague instructions via email, aiming to give other members a chance at songwriting, as he had written about 95% of previous material. This shift altered band dynamics and sound. Albert Hammond Jr. also suffered a drug problem from a breakup, requiring rehab. Out of 18 initial demos, 17 were discarded or reworked. The album was released on February 9, 2011, and was warmly received, described as "a return to form" and "back to basics." "Overall, the fourth album was warmly received." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jEfofyobGiI
**Come Down Machine (2013)** : The fifth album was conceived as another collaborative effort, with all members contributing to songwriting. Upon its release in March 2013, the band imposed a full media blackout, with no interviews, gigs, tours, photo shoots, or videos. Reviews for the album were positive, which was fortunate given the lack of promotion. However, it was not a sales monster, reflecting the industry's transition from physical sales to streaming, where counting physical copies became less important. "Reviews were good, fortunate, given the media block out, but the album wasn't exactly a sales monster." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jEfofyobGiI
**First Impressions of Earth (2006)** : This album, released approximately two years after "Room on Fire," was the band's third studio effort. It again faced producer issues, with Gordon Raphael tapping out in favor of David Khan, who had previously worked with Paul McCartney. This album was less well-received by critics and even garnered a lukewarm response from fans. Sales were anemic despite three singles receiving radio play. "This was the difficult third album, not a lot of love from the Quittix." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jEfofyobGiI
**Future Present Past (2016) – EP** : This four-track EP was released digitally and on two colors of vinyl, and unlike "Come Down Machine," it received promotion. The promotional efforts included an Instagram campaign, Julian Casablancas hosting a satellite radio show to discuss it, some live shows, and a pop-up shop in New York City. It marked the first Strokes release on Cult, Julian's own imprint label. "This one did get promoted." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jEfofyobGiI
**Is This It (2001) – Debut Album** : The Strokes' 2001 debut album featured dual cover art designs. The North American version prominently displayed a scientific photograph of subatomic particles in a bubble chamber, while international releases depicted a provocative shot of a woman's naked rear and hip adorned with a black glove, photographed by Colin Lane. The band ultimately preferred the scientific image for global release, but it was too late for the initial pressings. "If you bought the record in North America, we see something very scientific." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_tbKuJR0cPs
**Is This It (Year Unspecified - Post 9/11)** : This debut record, released as New York was dealing with 9/11's aftermath, created a significant buzz, sounding "very fresh, very raw, very exciting." It became one of the foundational albums for the indie rock movement later in the decade. "But next to what else was being released and it sounded very fresh, very raw, very exciting. The name of the band? The Strokes." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=MK7L0eCpDHc
**Is This It? (2001) – Debut Full-Length Album** : The full album released after a bidding war among American labels. It was available in the UK on July 30, 2001, and in North America on October 9, 2001. The album's release suggested to listeners that "maybe the rock scene doesn't seem so hopeless anymore." "The UK got it right away, July the 30th. North America got it October 9th." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=raRCZ0Y5up8
**Room on Fire (2003)** : The second Strokes album began with producer Nigel Godrich but eventually reverted to Gordon Raphael. Released in October 2003, it featured the single "1251" with a distinctive guitar tone that became a signature sound for many Strokes songs, originating from a studio accident. While it didn't match "Is This It" commercially, it was a critical success and solidified the band's position in the Indy Rock Revival. "Room on Fire didn't do as well commercially as Is This It. But it was certainly a hit with the Quittix." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jEfofyobGiI
**The Modern Age (2001) – Debut EP** : Released by Rough Trade Records in London on January 29, 2001. This EP was so popular that Rough Trade struggled to keep enough copies in stock in stores. Its success garnered attention from American labels, leading to a bidding war for the band. "It was so popular that Rough Trade struggled to keep enough of these EP's in stock in stores." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=raRCZ0Y5up8
**The New Abnormal (2020)** : The sixth Strokes record was released on April 10, 2020, amidst the COVID-19 pandemic, a timing that made its title particularly fitting. Produced by Rick Rubin in California and Hawaii, this change in producer and venue was exactly what the band needed. The album garnered great reviews and earned a Grammy Award for Best Rock Album. "This record was made with producer Rick Rubin in both California and Hawaii. And the change of producer and venue was exactly what the band needed." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jEfofyobGiI
**This Is It (2001)** : The Strokes' debut album, released in July 2001, initially experienced a slow start. Its trajectory changed significantly after the 9/11 attacks, as public taste shifted away from "happy, dancing pop music" and back towards rock, which offered "more teeth, with more bite." "The result was an album called This Is It." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=UpWz3X33vSY
