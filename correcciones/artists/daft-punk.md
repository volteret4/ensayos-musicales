## albums
**Discovery (2001) – Robots Appear; One More Time, Digital Love; Influenced by Sheik, Post-Disco, R&B, Auto-Tune** : For their second album, recorded at Daft House (a studio built in Thomas's bedroom in Paris — the gear took up so much space he had to move the bed), Guy-Manuel and Thomas shifted toward a slicker sound incorporating post-disco and R&B, samples from the late 1970s and early 1980s techno-pop era (Depeche Mode, OMD, Thomas Dolby, Erasure), vintage synthesizers, and Auto-Tune. The album had more song structure than Homework. The lead single "One More Time" had been sitting around since 1998 before being resurrected. "Digital Love" sampled "I Love You More" by American songwriter and producer George Duke. The fiction of the robot transformation — the sampler explosion at 9:09am on September 9, 1999 — emerged around this time, tied to the debut of the helmets. "Their gear took up so much space that he had to move the bed somewhere else." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QwLvq9j5_hQ
**Homework (1997) – Recorded at Home; Not Planned as an Album; Charted in Dozens of Countries; Bigger in France** : Homework was not intended to be an album — Guy-Manuel and Thomas put out a bunch of individual songs without planning to collect them, but then it happened. They recorded it at home (hence the name). The album was hyped by the music press and charted in more than a dozen countries, mostly on dance charts. The only place where it was a genuine across-the-board hit was France. The single "Da Funk" sold more as a single than Homework sold as an album. "They called it that because they recorded it at Home, which makes sense." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QwLvq9j5_hQ
**Human After All (2005) – Recorded in Six Weeks; Alive 2006–2007 Tour Kicked Off EDM in North America** : The third Daft Punk record was assembled in just six weeks and was considered fine but perhaps a little derivative and repetitive by fans. The subsequent Alive 2006–2007 tour, which featured the band performing in front of a giant LED-covered pyramid, was a massive success, especially in North America, and helped kick-start the entire EDM phenomenon on this side of the Atlantic. The live version of "Harder, Better, Faster, Stronger" was released as a single from the tour and netted Daft Punk Grammy Awards in 2008. Their performance with Kanye West at the 2008 ceremony was the first televised Daft Punk performance. "This tour, known as the Alive 2006-2007 tour, was a massive success, especially in North America." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QwLvq9j5_hQ
**Random Access Memories (2013)** : This album is noted for being pressed on multiple heavy vinyl discs, with its specific mastering requiring it to be played at 45 RPM, differentiating it from standard 33 RPM albums. "My copy of Daft Punk's random axis memories is like this." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=B2g4QSREzpw
**Random Access Memories (2013) – Worked with Nile Rodgers, Giorgio Moroder, Julian Casablancas, Paul Williams; Only Number One Album in North America; "Get Lucky" #1 in 30 Countries** : After an eight-year gap between albums, Random Access Memories involved collaborations with Giorgio Moroder (pioneering synth-dance producer), Nile Rodgers of Chic (a personal hero who also produced Bowie's Let's Dance), Julian Casablancas of the Strokes, songwriter Paul Williams, Canadian pianist Chilly Gonzalez, Panda Bear from Animal Collective, jazz bassist Nathan East, and producer Todd Edwards among others. Influences studied included Fleetwood Mac, The Cars, Steely Dan, the Eagles, the Doobie Brothers, Michael Jackson, and Pink Floyd's Dark Side of the Moon. They recorded too much material and briefly considered a four-disc box set before cutting everything to 74 minutes (just under a CD's maximum capacity). Released May 17, 2013, it became their only number one album in North America. "Get Lucky" hit number one in 30 countries and became one of the biggest-selling digital singles of all time. The album won multiple Grammys in 2014. "Random access memories became the only number one Daft Punk album in North America." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QwLvq9j5_hQ

## songs
**Robot Rock (2005)** : Released in 2005, "Robot Rock" by Daft Punk is presented as a prime example of the fragmentation and segmentation occurring in alt rock during the 2000s, where alt rock sensibilities were blended with a multitude of dance influences like disco, house, R&B, and hip hop. "Daphpunk and Robot Rock from 2005, and by this time, things were definitely segmenting separated and fragmenting again." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_yslM5oanRo

## curiosities
**Around the World (1997) – Goal Was to Sound Like Chic; Repeated 144 Times; Declared One of the Most Repetitive Songs Ever** : "Around the World" was designed to sound like Chic (Nile Rodgers's disco-era band). The song has five instruments and a robotic voice saying "Around the World." In the original version, the phrase is repeated 144 times (80 times in the short edit). A scientific analysis of music declared it one of the most repetitive songs ever released — and that is its genius, as repetition is what creates the sensation of losing yourself in music. "A scientific analysis of music declared this to be one of the most repetitive songs ever released. And that is its genius." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QwLvq9j5_hQ
**Blending Alt Rock and Dance** : Daft Punk is cited as one of the best examples of acts that successfully blended alt rock sensibilities with a diverse range of dance influences, including old school raves, disco, house, R&B, and hip hop, contributing to the fragmentation and redefinition of alt rock in the 2000s. "one of the best examples of what I'm talking about is Daphpunk." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_yslM5oanRo

**Daft Punk Origins – Darlin' 1992; Melody Maker Called Them "Daft Punky Trash"; Became Daft Punk** : Guy-Manuel de Homem-Christo (full first name: Guillaume Manuel) is from just west of Paris; his great-great-grandfather was a famous Portuguese military figure and his grandfather a famous Portuguese poet. Thomas Bangalter is from Paris; his father was a songwriter and record producer and Thomas started piano at age six. The two met in high school in 1987 and formed the rock band Darlin' (named from a Beach Boys song they covered) in 1992. Darlin' recorded four songs, played two gigs, and lasted six months before dissolving after a Melody Maker review called them "daft punky trash" — that phrase became their new name. The third member of Darlin', Laurent Boutonnat, went on to work with Phoenix. "Owing to that snarky review and melody maker, they started Daffed punk." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QwLvq9j5_hQ

**First Daft Punk Track (1994) – Demo at Euro Disney Rave; Soma Label** : Guy-Manuel and Thomas began attending raves and dance events. A demo tape was handed over and soon the first-ever Daft Punk track was released in 1994 — called "The New Wave," it later appeared in pre-recorded form on Homework. **Origin of Daft Punk's Helmets – 1974 Brian De Palma Film Phantom of the Paradise; Sampler Explosion Fiction September 9, 1999** : After their rock band Darlin' dissolved in 1992 (following a Melody Maker review calling them "daft punky trash"), Thomas Bangalter and Guy-Manuel de Homem-Christo shifted to synths, drum machines, and sequencers. One of them saw the 1974 Brian De Palma film Phantom of the Paradise, whose disfigured main character Winslow Leech wore a helmet to hide his injuries — and Daft Punk adopted the helmet idea. The fictional mythology was that at 9:09am on September 9, 1999, a sampler exploded in their studio, horribly injuring both men and requiring helmets to be permanently installed; they had also become robots, losing all human memories but retaining the ability to create music. The helmets had built-in air conditioning (handy for hot live shows) and communication links. One set was made by the same special effects company that worked on one of the Spider-Man movies. Thomas and Guy-Manuel could walk through crowds at their own shows unrecognized. "For us, it was sci-fi glam. We're not performers, we're not models. It would not be enjoyable for humanity to see our features, but the robots are exciting to people." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DiyIPBKiVW4
**The Robot Helmets – 1974 Phantom of the Paradise; Air Conditioning; Communication Links; Spider-Man Effects Company** : The robot mythology was inspired partly by the 1974 Brian De Palma film Phantom of the Paradise, in which disfigured character Winslow Leech wore a helmet to hide his injuries, and partly by a 1970s band called Space, whose members wore helmets and spacesuits for anonymity. The first helmets had wigs (Thomas had curly hair, Guy-Manuel had long straight hair) until on the way to a 2001 magazine photo shoot they decided robots should look bald and ripped out the fake hair. The helmet designs became increasingly ornate: some reportedly had built-in air conditioning and communication links so they could talk to each other and their crew during performances. One set of helmets was made by the same special effects company that worked on one of the Spider-Man movies. The anonymity was so effective they could walk right through crowds at their own shows unrecognized. One impostor ran up a huge bar tab at a club in Ibiza by claiming to be Thomas Bangalter. "People thought the helmets were marketing or something, but for us, it was sci-fi glam." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QwLvq9j5_hQ
