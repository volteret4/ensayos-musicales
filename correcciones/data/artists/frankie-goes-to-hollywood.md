## songs
**Relax (1983)** : Between 1984 and 1985, Frankie Goes to Hollywood achieved millions of sales by issuing at least 13 different versions of this single, demonstrating a highly effective strategy for encouraging multiple purchases of the same song through remixes. "In 1984 and 1985, they managed to get people to buy millions of copies of at least 13 different versions of Relax." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=08i9lVd6T8c
**Relax (1984)** : This debut single was released in 1984 and quickly became embroiled in controversy. The song and its associated hype were characterized by themes of drugs and anonymous bathhouse sex, further emphasized by newspaper ads featuring Holly Johnson in a sailor's cap with the caption, "all the nice boys love seamen." Its commercial performance was initially sluggish until DJ Mike Reed initiated an on-air diatribe, leading the BBC to ban the song for "obscene, homo erotic overtones." This ban ironically propelled the single to number one on the charts, making it one of the most recognizable songs of the entire 1980s. "The newspaper ads for the record featured Holly in a sailor's cap and a leather vest with the caption, all the nice boys love sea men." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hswtuenFwvM
**Two Tribes** : This song addresses the theme of nuclear war, a prevalent concern and subject of protest during the 1980s. "Two tribes from Frankie Ghost of Hollywood." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0ARYt4sYT90
**Two Tribes (1984) – Cold War Protest Song; Video Featured Reagan vs. Chernenko Wrestling Match** : "Two Tribes" was one of the most popular anti-war protest songs of the 1980s. The video featured a wrestling match between Ronald Reagan and Soviet leader Constantine Chernenko, and it was released at a moment when the US and USSR had come dangerously close to nuclear war. In 1983 alone: NORAD ran a training tape on Soviet attack on the live systems causing a false alarm; a Soviet fighter shot down Korean Air Lines Flight 007; a Soviet early warning system falsely detected multiple US ICBM launches, with one submarine commander — Lieutenant Colonel Stanislav Petrov — unilaterally deciding not to launch a retaliatory strike. "The video featured a wrestling match between Ronald Reagan and Constantine Ternenko. It remains chilling to this day." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YsNRNKvd1GI

## curiosities
**All-Gay Lineup (Claimed)** : It is rumored, though somewhat "murky" and possibly part of the band's "pure hype," that every member of Frankie Goes to Hollywood was gay. This claim contributed to their controversial and provocative image. "It's a bit murky, but it is said that every member of the band was gay." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hswtuenFwvM
**Fairlight Keyboard Use** : Frankie Goes to Hollywood utilized the Fairlight, a sophisticated keyboard device that allowed for the digital excision, manipulation, and implantation of musical material, in the creation of their remixes during the 1980s. "Frankie and New Order used a Fairlight for their remixes." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=08i9lVd6T8c
**Glam Connection to LGBT Community** : Frankie Goes to Hollywood is cited as part of the gay and non-binary club scene that developed adjacent to the New Romantic movement, demonstrating glam's lasting connection to the LGBT community and the popularization of androgynous fashion. "and Frankie Ghost of Hollywood." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=blDNqVFheAw
**Origins at Eric's Club (1980)** : The band's beginnings trace back to 1980 in Liverpool, where most of the members were regulars at a club called Eric's. This venue served as a formative hub for the group before their rise to fame. "The beginnings of the band go back to 1980 and the frontman was Holly Johnson." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hswtuenFwvM
**Pioneers of Remix Sales Strategy** : Frankie Goes to Hollywood was exceptionally skilled at using remixes to drive sales in the early 1980s. From 1984 to 1987, they released only seven singles, but these were extensively remixed and re-released, resulting in three dozen different remix versions. This strategy provided "very low overhead, big profits" for the band and their label. "Frankie goes to Hollywood was really good at the remix trick. Between 1984 and 1987, Frankie released just seven singles. But those singles were remixed and re-released again and again and again. Those seven singles stretched into three dozen remixes." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=08i9lVd6T8c
**Technopop Descendant** : Listed as one of the artists who descended from the original Technopop scene. "There was Bronsky beat, aha, yellow, Elfaville, Frankie Ghost of Hollywood. They all descended from the original Tecno Pop scene." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1KF5MwJVYng
