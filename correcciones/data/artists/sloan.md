## albums
**A Side's Wind (2005) - Greatest Hits Collection** : Released in May 2005, this collection aimed to address the music industry's crash due to file-sharing by offering a special edition. It included a DVD featuring videos for all songs, interviews, and other exclusive content not available for download. The title is a play on their song "A Side Winds" from the "One Chord to Another" album and featured several bonus tracks. "They called this collection of singles A Side's Wind, playing off a title of a song called A Side Winds, back on the one chord to another album." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU
**Action Packed (2003)** : Sloan's seventh album, recorded in Los Angeles with producer Tim Rothrock, who had previously worked with indie artists like Elliot Smith and The Toadies. It received an international release on several labels, but its major impact remained primarily in Canada. This album served as a warm-up for Rothrock, as he later produced James Blunt's multi-platinum album "Back to Bedlam." "Great sounding record though. It was called Action Packed." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU
**Between the Bridges (1999) - Fifth Studio Album** : This album was considered a "real tank" in terms of career success and sales, despite receiving a "respectable 3 out of 5 stars" from Rolling Stone. The title is a geographical reference to Sloan's Halifax roots, specifically the Macdonald and McKay bridges in the municipality. "But that was one that totally tanks. Nobody even knows about that one." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU
**Live Album (1999)** : Released as a record for fans, this live album was not heavily promoted. Its release closely preceded "Between the Bridges," leading to issues where copies were being returned as the new studio album was being shipped, causing it to "die" in terms of sales. "The live record was supposed to be like, hey, it's for fans. Just sort of throw it out there. We won't really promote it." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU
**Navy Blues (1998)** : This album performed very well for the band, featuring "Money City Maniacs," which remains one of their most recognizable songs and significantly boosted its sales. Chris Murphy considered this album, along with "Between the Bridges," almost a double album due to their close recording proximity and shared recording location. "Navy Blues did really well. We had a song on it called Money City Maniacs, which is still probably our most recognizable song." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU
**Never Hear the End of It (2006) - Double Studio CD** : This eighth album by Sloan featured an ambitious 30 songs and over 76 minutes of music spread across two discs. The band considered the sheer volume of songs a "hook" that helped the album perform well and receive positive write-ups. Chris Murphy noted that 14 of the 30 songs were his, making it feel "like a solo record of mine hidden within its low record." "Our eighth record has 30 songs on it. We didn't need 30 new songs in our set." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU
**Parallel Play (2008) - Ninth Studio Record** : Sloan's shortest studio album, clocking in at just over 37 minutes with 12 or 13 songs. The title is a child psychology term for children aged one to three who play beside each other without interaction, serving as a humorous comment on the band's often independent songwriting and recording process. This album was recorded quickly at the band's rehearsal studio. "Well, Parallel Play, I'll start you with the title is a reference to a child psychology term about children between one and three that play beside each other, but they don't interact." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU
**Pretty Together (2001) - Comeback Record** : Released on October 16, 2001, a month after 9/11 and a week before the iPod's release, this album was seen as a comeback after the lukewarm reception of "Between the Bridges." It generated two videos that performed well on audio radio and marked a period where Sloan was doing "alright" on BMG in Canada. "That was sort of a comeback record, I guess, in a way, because the last one had been pretty lukewarm." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU
**Smeared (1992)** : Released in 1992, this album was their first and led to the band being signed to Sub Pop. Sloan's emergence from Halifax contributed to the city being considered the "new Seattle" for a period, and the band went on to become a major part of the Can Rock Revolution. "Sloan, from the smeared album of 1992, they would go on to and continue to be a major part of the Can Rock Revolution." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6PGgNCaW4xo

## songs
**All Used Up (2005)** : This song was included as a bonus track on Sloan's 2005 greatest hits collection, "A Side's Wind." "This is all used up. Sloan with a bonus track from their 2005 greatest hits collection called A Side's Wind." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU
**Believe in Me (2008)** : This song was the first single released from Sloan's 2008 album, "Parallel Play." "Sloan. With Believe in Me, the first single from their 2008 album, Parallel Play." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU
**If It Feels Good to Do It (2001)** : This song was released as a single from "Pretty Together," and its video performed "quite well in the audio radio." It is identified as one of Patrick Pentland's songs, which, along with others, led Chris Murphy to feel that the band's singles made them seem like they "only had sort of one trick." "If it feels good to do it came out, I kind of felt like we had been known." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU
**Il Placed Trust (2006)** : The second single from "Never Hear the End of It," this song was written by Patrick Pentland "way back in the day," specifically around 1981. It achieved a minor chart success in the American market, reaching number 48 on one of Billboard magazine's specialized charts, marking a small victory for the band after slugging it out since 1991. "Il Placed Trust, a song that Patrick Pendlin wrote way back in the day. Oh, 1981-ish actually made an American record chart." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU
**Living the Dream (2008)** : A song written by Chris Murphy that was planned for a new record around 2008. Patrick Pentland reportedly disliked the title, associating the phrase with "Jocks" and suggesting it "might as well have called the song Hurt in Unit." "We're going to do a song called Living the Dream. And it's a song that Patrick says, he can't even say the word out loud because he thinks it's something that Jock's say." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU
**Losing California (1999)** : Featured on the album "Between the Bridges," this song was described as "about the only bright spot from a single's point of view" from an album that otherwise did not sell well. It is also noted as one of Patrick Pentland's songs. "Losing California is one of our songs, that was on the record between the bridges." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU
**Money City Maniacs (1998)** : From the album "Navy Blues," this song is likely Sloan's most recognizable and contributed significantly to that album's strong sales. It is identified as one of Patrick Pentland's "rockers in the key of B" that helped sustain the band's career. "We had a song on it called Money City Maniacs, which is still probably our most recognizable song." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU
**The Other Man (2001)** : A slower, more musically diverse song from "Pretty Together," described as a "slime ball adulterer song." Chris Murphy, who wrote it, was happy it offered a stylistic departure from their usual sound, hoping to reach a broader audience like "housewives and little girls." However, Chris also felt it was "kind of unslown" and believes "a lot of Sloan fans hated it." This song is rumored to be based on a real love triangle within the Canadian indie community involving Chris Murphy, Andrew Whiteman (Broken Social Scene), and Leslie Feist. "But anyway, it was enough of a musical change up that we could reach some housewives and little girls." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU
**The Rest of My Life (2003)** : This song is featured on Sloan's 2003 album, "Action Packed." "playing The Rest of My Life, which was from their 2003 album Action Packed." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU
**Underwhelmed (1992)** : This single was released as the follow-up to Sloan's debut album, "Smeared." "That snaps us all the way back to the first album smeared in 1992. That was the follow up to the single underwhelmed." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU
**Who Taught You to Live Like That (2006)** : Written by Jay Ferguson, this was his first single for the band since "The Lines You Amend" and was featured on their 2006 double studio CD, "Never Hear the End of It." The song also served as the theme song for the CBC TV series "MVP," a Canadian adaptation of the British show "Footballers Wives." "Jay Ferguson wrote it and it was his first single for the band since the Lions U of M." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU

## curiosities
**American Market Attempts** : Sloan made multiple efforts to break into the lucrative American market. Their first attempt was in 1992 with the "Smeared" album on Geffen, Nirvana's label, but it fell apart due to label politics. A mid-1990s deal with The Enclave also failed when the label went out of business. Even in 2003, they tried again, managing only about two appearances on MTV's "120 Minutes." "Yes, we never had any kind of, you know, we were on 120 minutes on MTV in the States." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU
**Canada's Answer to Nirvana** : Sloan, from Halifax, was initially perceived as "Canada's answer to Nirvana," leading to Halifax being briefly considered "Seattle East" due to the widespread frenzy and saturation of grunge. "Sloan originally pegged as Canada's answer to Nirvana. And for a short while, Halifax was considered to be Seattle East." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tFBSrKMEZxI
**Canadian Band Challenges** : Operating as a rock band in Canada, the world's second-largest country, presents unique challenges due to its vast geography (nearly 10 million square kilometers spread over six time zones with just over three people per square kilometer). Touring bands become intimately familiar with the 7,604 kilometers of the Trans-Canada Highway. Despite 77% of the population living in cities, bands must work extremely hard to reach fans, especially while living next door to the largest net exporter of popular culture in the universe, the United States, which has "chewed up the domestic careers of countless bands." "Geography has chewed up the domestic careers of countless bands." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU
**Collaborated with Weezer** : The guys from Sloan collaborated with Weezer on the album "Ratitude," though their contributions were uncredited and recorded in 2003. "And the guys from Sloan." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LYRPxtP61JM
**Cult Phenomenon in the States** : Despite limited mainstream success in the United States, Sloan has achieved a "cult phenomenon" status there. American fans typically discover them from their college days when "Smeared" was on Geffen, or through independent research online, appreciating their pop and rock music. This underground following becomes "more and more underground" the further south one travels. "So we are a cult phenomenon in the States, which is fun. But we make our money in Canada because we benefit from mainstream media and stuff like that." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU
**Democratic Band Structure** : Sloan operates with an "uber democratic structure" where every member writes songs and sings lead vocals. All money is split equally among the four members, which has been crucial in keeping the band together and avoiding the conflicts over financial distribution that have plagued other bands, such as The Smiths. "Every single member of Sloan writes songs and every single member gets a chance to sing lead." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU
**Desire for Critical Acclaim** : Chris Murphy jokingly expressed a long-term goal for Sloan to amass an "enormous body of work" in hopes that prestigious publications like Mojo Magazine or Record Collector Magazine would eventually acknowledge their extensive discography. He envisions a future box set and coffee table book, but only if a "huge hit" makes it financially viable for a larger audience. "Just to have an enormous body of work so that Mojo Magazine finally goes, we are sorry. We never wrote about you guys." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU
**Domestic Focus and Longevity** : Sloan has primarily operated as a domestic act in Canada since 1991, running their own label and partnering with larger distributors. They have consistently adapted to the music business's challenges, maintaining their band with zero lineup changes, a feat that is considered "pretty remarkable." "A Canadian band who has worked mainly as a domestic act since 1991, running their own label, working deals with other bigger labels to distribute their stuff, and surviving everything in music business has thrown at them with zero lineup changes." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU
**Early 1990s Prime Time Success** : Sloan was among the Canadian bands that got "a shot at prime time" in the early 1990s. "There was Pluto and Jail and Doeboys and Rusty and Sloan." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U2kYleeMnl8
**Evolution of Songwriting** : Since Andrew Scott moved to Toronto in 1993, the band's collaborative process evolved, encouraging individual members to sing, write, and creatively contribute independently. This approach, combined with equal money splitting, has been key to the band's longevity and cohesion. "So we've all kind of evolved into this band that writes sort of individually." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU
**Featured in "In Their Own Words" Series** : Sloan was the first band to be featured in the "In Their Own Words" series, which aims to bring multiple band members into the studio to share their personal history. "Sloan was first..." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bI6TTA-fn7c
**Halifax Roots** : Sloan originated from Halifax, Nova Scotia. The title of their 1999 album, "Between the Bridges," directly references the Macdonald and McKay bridges in the municipality, signifying their deep connection to their hometown. "Sloan is originally from Halifax, and there are two main bridges in the municipality." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU
**Internal Competition and Ego** : Despite their democratic and egalitarian financial structure, there is acknowledged competition and ego among the members regarding songwriting and singles. Chris Murphy expressed his happiness about "The Other Man" being released as a single, partly because it was his song and stylistically different, especially after Patrick Pentland had achieved "five or six singles in a row." "Well, as I say, I was playing down my bile, but by the time Patrick had about five or six singles in a row, I was like, I had had it." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU
**Learning Songs for Tour** : Sloan has an unusual approach to preparing for tours, often recording albums with many new songs and then needing to learn them later for live performance. Chris Murphy humorously notes, "most bands know how to play the record. And then they go make the record. But we're opposite. I basically have to go buy the record to learn how to play the album." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU
**Love Triangle Rumor** : There is an internet rumor suggesting that Sloan's song "The Other Man" is based on a real-life love triangle within the Canadian indie music community, supposedly involving Chris Murphy of Sloan, Andrew Whiteman from Broken Social Scene, and Leslie Feist. "I have heard that this song is based on a real love triangle within the Canadian indie community." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU
**Major Part of Can Rock Revolution** : Sloan became and continued to be a major part of the Can Rock Revolution, solidifying their place in Canadian music history through their sustained influence and success. "they would go on to and continue to be a major part of the Can Rock Revolution." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6PGgNCaW4xo
**Persistence and Success** : Sloan has successfully navigated the challenges of the Canadian music industry, maintaining a solid and respectable living as rock stars. They have developed efficiencies, established a loyal fan base, and perfected their routine since forming in 1991. "They may not be rich, but they are making a solid and respectable living as rock stars in Canada. Sloan is one of those bands." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU
**Post-Can Rock Revolution Survival** : Sloan's career continued successfully long after the 1990s, indicating the enduring impact of the Can Rock Revolution. "Many acts survived long after the decade ended. The tragically hip, Sloan, R80p, Serma Glockland, Tea Party, Matt Good, many others." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U2kYleeMnl8
**Recording Philosophy** : Chris Murphy prefers making records "on the cheap" to ensure they are seen as a success, prioritizing low cost over high sales figures. He contrasts this with their second album which cost $120,000 due to major label funding, whereas their first record, remixed by Geffen, cost only $1,200. "I like to make everything on the cheap. So that it can be seen as a success." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU
**Signed to Sub Pop** : Sloan was signed to Sub Pop for their first album, "Smeared." This significant label signing, coupled with their origin in Halifax, contributed to the city's brief reputation as the "new Seattle" in the early 1990s. "Sloan, from the smeared album of 1992, they would go on to and continue to be a major part of the Can Rock Revolution." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6PGgNCaW4xo
**Zero Lineup Changes** : Since their formation in 1991, Sloan has remarkably maintained the same four members, a testament to their enduring chemistry and "uber democratic structure." This stability is a significant factor in their ability to survive the music business for decades. "Many band who has survived this long in this country with zero lineup changes has got to have some stories." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QB3nyFH_tRU
