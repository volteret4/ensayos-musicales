## curiosities
**Composing with Synthetic Sounds and Esoteric Equipment** : After World War II, Oram delved into composing music using synthetic sounds created with esoteric audio equipment such as sine wave oscillators combined with magnetic tape machines. This was before synthesizers were invented, making her studio appear "like something out of a science fiction movie." "So she used things like sine wave oscillators combined with magnetic tape machines." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=oRR7N-YUcRE
**First All-Electronic Score** : In 1957, Daphne Oram created the soundtrack for her play titled "Ampatrium 38," marking it as the first-ever all-electronic score for any production. This achievement highlighted her innovative approach to musical composition and laid a foundation for electronic sound in media. "In 1957, she created the soundtrack for her play entitled Ampatrium 38, which was the first ever all electronic score for anything." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=oRR7N-YUcRE
**Founding Her Own Studio for New Sounds** : After leaving the BBC in 1959, Daphne Oram established her own studio devoted to creating new electronic sounds. Her work there extended to film, where she produced all the electronic sound effects for the first three James Bond movies: "Dr. No," "From Russia with Love," and "Goldfinger," showcasing her unique sonic artistry. "When she left the Beebe in 1959, she founded her own studio devoted to creating new sounds." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=oRR7N-YUcRE
**Pioneering Junior Studio Engineer at BBC** : In 1942, Daphne Oram began her career at the BBC as a junior studio engineer. Her initial responsibility was to ensure continuous music broadcasts by seamlessly switching to recorded music if transmissions were interrupted by Nazi bombing raids, which sparked her experimentation with audio recording gear. "In 1942, she became a junior studio engineer at the BBC." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=oRR7N-YUcRE
