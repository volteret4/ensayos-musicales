# genre - Jazz

## curiosities
**"Selling Out" Perception** : Accusing a jazz player of "selling out" is considered extremely offensive by many fans, akin to a grave insult. Jazz is widely regarded as a very serious art form where the pursuit of its artistic goals should not be corrupted by commercial interests. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sfie4YYncxk
**20th Century Cultural Dominance** : Jazz, in all its forms, was the dominant music that propelled Western culture forward during the first half of the 20th century, before being superseded by rock and roll in the 1950s. "The first half of the 20th century was dominated by jazz in all its forms. That was the music that pushed Western culture forward." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Z7glrAoXtkA
**Bebop Development in the 1940s** : In the 1940s, a form of jazz called bebop developed, which was characterized by the abilities of smaller combos typically consisting of three to five players, often including a piano player. This development occurred as larger dance bands downsized during World War II due to members being drafted and increased costs. "In the 1940s, a form of jazz based on the abilities of the combo called the bebop developed, and such groups often had a piano player." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dJiSZ4X0F2g
**Big Band Era Dominance (Pre-WWII)** : Before World War II, jazz, particularly the Big Band sound, was the dominant musical style. Big Bands consisted of ten or more musicians led by a prominent band leader, who was the star, and all other members, including singers, were employees expected to follow rigid instructions. "Before the war, jazz was the thing, specifically the Big Band sound." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=V7WjNR1ujhk
**Charlie Christian's Impact** : The early growth of the electric guitar's reputation within a specific community was significantly boosted by Charlie Christian, a black jazz player from Oklahoma, who became the first guitar hero. "And much of that early attention was because of Charlie Christian, a black jazz player from Oklahoma." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=E6cXyaHALBQ
**Current Cultural Influence** : While jazz is not considered "doomed to die," it no longer holds the position of "calling the shots" on a major cultural scale, having been eclipsed by hip hop as the driving force in popular culture. "This does not mean that rock or jazz aren't any less important or that they're doomed to die. It's just that when it comes to calling the shots on a major scale in terms of culture, it's not their call anymore." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Z7glrAoXtkA
**Decline During WWII** : The Big Band jazz sound suffered significantly during World War II due to several factors: many players were drafted into the military, making it hard to find musicians; dance halls, traditional venues for Big Bands, began closing; and gasoline rationing made touring difficult and expensive. "When the war started, Big Band started to suffer." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=V7WjNR1ujhk
**Decline and Rise of Rock and Roll** : After dominating music culture for 50 years, jazz began to fade, creating an opening for new musical forms. This decline coincided with the emergence of rock and roll, which, despite initial chauvinistic attitudes, eventually created new opportunities for female drummers. "Meanwhile, after 50 years of dominating music culture, jazz was fading so the new thing was rock and roll and that opened some new opportunities." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9GHpC0kCgHY
**Decline of Big Band Jazz and Rise of R&B (1920s-1950s)** : Throughout the 1920s and 1930s, jazz was primarily performed by "big bands," which could comprise up to 20 or more players. However, these large ensembles became expensive to maintain, especially with the onset of the Depression, World War II (which drew musicians to war), and a paralyzing US musician strike from 1942-1944 that shut down the recording industry. Post-war labor shortages further impacted musician availability, forcing groups to downsize to 4-6 players. This led to the emergence of less danceable bebop, while others began to emphasize rhythm and beat, leading to the emergence of rhythm and blues (R&B) by the late 1940s, setting the stage for rock in the 1950s. "And by the late 1940s, this became known as rhythm and blues." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_Pi7JLwAeOI
**Deep Jazz Program (N/A)** : The Deep Jazz project, developed by G. Sun Kim at Princeton University, was a neural network designed to create jazz music. It operated by assigning probabilities to notes and integrated principles from Jeffrey Lims' thesis on human-feel rhythms to ensure the output was not robotic. Kim made the source code publicly available, noting it was "an AI built to make jazz." "This is an AI built to make jazz." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QFtS8fl553Y
**Domain of Early Albums** : Prior to the mid-1960s, albums were primarily the format for "adult music," including serious genres like jazz, classical, and Broadway show recordings. This changed as rock music evolved and began releasing sophisticated albums, gaining similar respect. "Prior to the mid-1960s, albums had been the domain of adult music, serious music, jazz, classical, Broadway show recordings." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yPok3MemDmE
**EMI's Ragtime Compositions (N/A)** : The computer program EMI (Experiments in Musical Intelligence), developed by David Cope, demonstrated its versatility by learning from music written by humans to compose convincing ragtime pieces in the style of Scott Joplin. This showed its capability to mimic various musical genres beyond classical compositions. "EMI turned out to be a pretty good student of Bach, Chopin, Stravinsky, and even some Scott Dropplin-Jaz." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QFtS8fl553Y
**Early Adoption of Electric Guitar** : Jazz musicians were at the forefront of adopting the electric guitar, with Jack Miller performing the first amplified gig in 1932 and Eddie Durham recording the first electric guitar solo in 1935. This innovation allowed guitarists to compete with the volume of horns in Big Band Orchestras. "The answer is a jazz player named Jack Miller." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gP3oQeYEvso
**Early LP Content (Late 1940s)** : With the introduction of the 12-inch long-playing (LP) vinyl album by Columbia Records in June 1948, jazz music was among the "high-brow" genres, including classical music, Broadway show tunes, and movie soundtracks, for which these LPs were initially reserved. This necessitated the inclusion of more detailed information in liner notes to satisfy the audience's demand. "since these LPs were first reserved for serious high-brow music, classical music, Broadway show tunes, movie soundtracks, and jazz, more information was called for, because that's what the audience wanted." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KaIp6KVn-co

This trend, exemplified by songs like Tampa Red and Georgia Tom's "It's Tight Like That" (1928), indicated a growing tension between traditional morality and new forms of musical expression. "When we got to the 1920s, early blues and jazz recordings appeared with very suggestive titles and lyrics." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=L0j9iDuHQVA

**Emergence and Social Reaction (c. 1900)** : Jazz emerged from New Orleans around 1900 and was widely condemned by elders as "vile and evil" and an "affront to proper music." This negative perception was compounded by its origins among African Americans. As jazz spread, it faced a significant backlash, with ballroom dancing and waltzes being promoted as an alternative to its "cacophony." "When jazz started coming out of New Orleans around 1900, it was vile and evil and a front to proper music. And it was made largely by African Americans, which compounded the problem." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=m7MbV-eCLyE
**Evolution and Cultural Dominance of Jazz (Late 1800s - Mid-1950s)** : Jazz was born out of Ragtime, first appearing in St. Louis in the very late 1800s and developing further as an offshoot in New Orleans, particularly among African Americans. Characterized by its syncopated rhythms, jazz quickly became the music that drove American culture, maintaining this prominent role until the mid-1950s. "And that was jazz, which became the music that drove American culture until the mid-1950s." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_Pi7JLwAeOI

**Impact on Time Signatures** : Jazz, particularly its early form known as ragtime, played a significant role in precipitating a "great time signature shift." Ragtime was among the first new musical forms to utilize the left hand on lower notes to provide a steady rhythm, which influenced the standardization of four-four time as the primary beat for most popular songs in the 20th century. "And along with having a completely different sort of groove, jazz also helped precipitate the great time signature shift. But with the rise of the piano and the spread of jazz, especially in early form called ragtime, which was among the first new musical forms to use the left hand and the lower notes to supply a steady rhythm to what the right hand was playing, time signatures fell into four four time, the standard beat for most of today's popular songs." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dJiSZ4X0F2g
