# genre - Hillbilly

## curiosities
**Early Radio Exposure (1940s)** : Hillbilly music, along with country, folk, and R&B, saw increased exposure on radio in the 1940s as stations turned to music from performers outside of the ASCAP collective due to a format war. "This meant more country and folk and hillbilly and R&B were heard on the radio." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GES0Ms_kaCI
**Increased Radio Exposure (WWII Era)** : During World War II, changes in radio programming, including local stations being allowed local programming after 7 pm and the cost-effectiveness of playing records, created space for "hillbilly" records. This allowed listeners to hear the genre, contributing to a broader dissemination of diverse musical styles across the American airwaves. "But it also made room for country, western, hillbilly, and R&B records. People started to hear other genres on the radio for the very very first time." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=V7WjNR1ujhk
**More Granular Classification** : Hillbilly music was one of the more granular genre classifications available in 1955, illustrating that even during a period of broad musical categorization, there were efforts to define more specific styles. "You could slice certain genres into thinner slices to include big band, Dixie Land, Scott, Hillbilly, and Jazz, gospel, Broadway show music." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hstJ8M2laho
