# instrument - Sampler

## curiosities
**Akai S1000 (1988) – Industry Standard Sampler** : Following earlier samplers like the Emulator and Sinclair Vier, the Japanese company Akai introduced a line of rack-mounted samplers, including the S1000 in 1988. This model was particularly significant because it was very affordable and quickly became the industry standard for digital sampling. Its accessibility played a crucial role in making sampling technology more widespread among musicians. "But then a Japanese company called Akai got into the act, with a line of rack-mounted samplers, including the very affordable S1000 in 1988, and it became the industry standard." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Somq2MoRi5Q
**Catalyst for Genre Explosion** : The introduction of the sampler as a new technology significantly contributed to the "explosion" in genre classifications in popular music. Along with synthesizers, drum machines, and digital recording, it opened up "entirely new vistas of unimaginable sound and creativity," leading to the rapid proliferation of genres and sub-genres. "Add a new technology like the synthesizer, the drum machine, the sampler, and digital recording technology, an entirely new vistas of unimaginable sound and creativity opened up." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=vJszsJQpINQ
**Early 80s Music Technology Breakthrough** : Early samplers were among the significant music-making technologies that emerged in the early 1980s. Their arrival, alongside better polyphonic synths, drum machines, and MIDI, marked the biggest explosion in musical technology since the electric guitar's advent three decades prior. "The early 80s saw the biggest explosion in music-making technology since the arrival of the electric guitar 30 years earlier. Better and more powerful polyphonic synths, drum machines, early samplers, and perhaps most important of all, a machine connecting language called MIDI." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5PJRCC6EwUY
**Fairlight CMI Cost and Limitations (1980)** : Despite its innovative capabilities, the Fairlight CMI was prohibitively expensive upon its debut in 1980, costing between $25,000 and $36,000. Additionally, the technology had significant limitations, with sampled soundfiles restricted to a maximum size of 20 kilobytes. Despite these drawbacks, over 300 studios and record labels invested in this cutting-edge technology. "Getting on options, these massive things cost anywhere from $25 to $36,000." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Somq2MoRi5Q
**Fairlight Computer Musical Instrument (CMI) (1980) – First Keyboard Digital Sampler** : Developed by the Australian company Fairlight throughout the 1970s, the CMI made its debut in 1980 as the world's first keyboard-based digital sampler. This groundbreaking instrument allowed users to digitally record any acoustic sound, store it on a floppy disk, visualize its waveform on a monitor, manipulate it with a light pen, and then play it back using a standard 88-note keyboard. This capability meant sounds could be shaped, molded, and transformed into entirely new sonic creations. Historically, the first sound ever digitally sampled on a Fairlight was a barking dog. "The Fairlight allowed the operator to digitally record any acoustic sound, stored on a floppy disc, display a visual image of that sound on a monitor, manipulate that waveform using a light pen, and then play it back using a standard sized 88-note keyboard." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Somq2MoRi5Q
