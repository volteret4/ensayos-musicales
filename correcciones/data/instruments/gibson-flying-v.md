# instrument - Gibson Flying V

## curiosities
**Initial Commercial Failure and 1967 Relaunch** : The Flying V was initially too radical for the market, selling fewer than 100 units upon its 1958 debut, leading to a halt in production. However, it was relaunched in 1967 after Gibson observed rock stars actively seeking out and using the original models. "less than 100 were sold when it debuted in 1958. Production stopped for a while, but restarted in 1967." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=d9HNqb9gJSU
**Playability and Aesthetic** : While visually striking and "really cool" in appearance, the Flying V's design made it challenging to play while sitting down due to the absence of a cutout for the knee. "It was hard to play sitting down because there was no cutout for your knee, but it did look really cool." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=d9HNqb9gJSU
**Popularity with Rock Stars (1980s Hair Metal)** : The Flying V gained significant popularity among rock stars, including Dave Davies, Jimi Hendrix, Tom Petty, Paul Stanley, and Richie Sambora. Its "pointy" design found its best years in the 1980s, becoming synonymous with the Hair Metal genre. "the point you guitar from Gibson had its best years." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=d9HNqb9gJSU
**Radical Modernist Design (1958)** : Introduced in 1958 as part of Gibson's "modernist line," the Flying V featured a body shaped like a large V or an arrowhead, a radical design reflecting the aesthetics of the 1950s with its fins and rocket-like shapes. "The body is shaped like a big V, or maybe an arrowhead." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=d9HNqb9gJSU
