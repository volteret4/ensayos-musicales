# instrument - Mellotron

## curiosities
**Chamberlain Rhythm Mate** : Approximately ten units of the Chamberlain Rhythm Mate were built, representing the Mellotron's initial iteration focused on drum sounds before its inventor moved to other instrument sounds. "No where the ten of such machines, known as the Chamberlain rhythm mate, were built, because Chamberlain moved from drum sounds to other instrument sounds for his next models." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dablAKDOOV0
**Description and Sound** : The Mellotron is described as a "taped piano" that, when played as a piano chord, can produce sounds like an actual recorded flute or even a string arrangement. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ocMHRLQMQY0
**Early Sampling Device (Unspecified)** : The Mellotron is considered an early form of sampling device. Bands could commission custom tapes with their own unique sounds and special effects, allowing them to reproduce specific audio from their records in a live setting that couldn't be achieved otherwise. "But you know, at the time, they were basically the first sampling device. And a lot of bands would actually, you could get your own tapes made up." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0fQQubt9UTI
**Famous Examples in Music (1967-1970s)** : Notable uses include the flute sound at the beginning of The Beatles' "Strawberry Fields Forever," Procol Harum's "A Whiter Shade of Pale," a violin sound in OMD's "Made of All" (often mistaken for bagpipes), Lynyrd Skynyrd's "Tuesday's Gone," and extensive use by Pink Floyd. "The flute on strawberry fields is probably the most famous melaton." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0fQQubt9UTI
**Famous Uses** : The Mellotron is most famously known for its background flute sound on Led Zeppelin's "Stairway to Heaven." It was also used by Brendan O'Brien on the Red Hot Chili Peppers' "Blood Sugar Sex Magik" album, particularly on the song "Breaking the Girl," and later on Billy Talent's "Saint Veronica" and "White Sparrows." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ocMHRLQMQY0
**Live Performance Unreliability (Unspecified)** : Mellotrons were notoriously difficult to use in live settings, especially with unstable power sources. Voltage fluctuations could cause pitch issues, and the instrument was prone to breakdowns, requiring the player to "chase the pitch wheel" or wait for stable power conditions (e.g., waiting for people in a French town to stop cooking). "He's, you know, which just happened to us a few times. I said, he's chasing the pitch wheel, trying to, it's going slow, it's going slow. I'm losing the tune." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0fQQubt9UTI
**Origins as a Drum Machine** : While known today as a musical keyboard, the Mellotron originally began as a drum machine. Its inventor, Harry Chamberlain, recorded drum rhythms on tape loops that could be sped up or slowed down. "But it began as a drum machine." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dablAKDOOV0
**Tape-Based Operation (Unspecified)** : The Mellotron is an antique, tape-based instrument where each key triggers a specific piece of tape, which plays for approximately 11 seconds. When the key is released, a spring retracts the tape. Sustain is limited to the tape's length, and changing sounds requires manually unscrewing the top and replacing entire looms of 36 tapes. "Okay. So basically, there is a tape. It's not a tape loop. It's a piece of tape that lasts about 20 seconds. No, it's 11. Oh, is it? Yeah." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0fQQubt9UTI
**Used in "Mad World" Cover** : Michael Andrews stripped down Tears for Fears' "Mad World" cover to its basics, utilizing a mellotron alongside piano and other flourishes, partly for artistic reasons and partly due to budget constraints. "Andrews stripped the song down to its basics, just piano, a melaton and a few other flourishes." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yuA9Xor8vfw
