# instrument - 7-inch single

## curiosities
**Challenges with Long Songs** : To fit lengthy songs, like Meat Loaf's 7-minute 56-second "Paradise by the Dashboard Light," onto a 7-inch single, record grooves had to be cut incredibly close together and made narrower. This process compromised bass storage and audio quality, particularly towards the center of the record, though this was generally unnoticed on the cheap turntables and AM radios for which they were intended. "Step two involved cutting the grooves on this 7-inch record incredibly close together. The grooves had to be made narrower and by doing that, you sacrifice how much bass can be stored in those grooves." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=08i9lVd6T8c
**Initial Format for Dance Mixes** : Initially, all dance mixes, following Tom Molten's innovations, were pressed as 7-inch singles before the 12-inch format was conceived due to a shortage of 7-inch blanks. "At first, all these dance mixes were pressed as seven-inch singles." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=08i9lVd6T8c
**Remixing for AM Radio Sound** : During the 1960s and 1970s, many 7-inch singles were specifically remixed in studios to optimize their sound for AM radio, which lacked good sonic properties. Engineers would mix using "junky 5-inch full-range speakers" similar to car speakers, resulting in recordings that often sounded "unnatural and often unbearably bright" on proper stereos. "That's why a lot of 7-inch releases from the 60s and 70s sound so unnatural and often unbearably bright when you play them on a proper stereo." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=08i9lVd6T8c
