**Origin and Early Use** : The Doc Marten shoe was invented by Dr. Klaus Martens, who, after sustaining a skiing accident in 1945, collaborated with his engineer friend Herbert Funk to design a comfortable, shock-absorbing shoe incorporating air cushions. For its initial two decades, the footwear primarily served as work boots and orthopedic wear, manufactured in Seeshaupt, a small German town in Bavaria. "Now there was a doctor Martin. **Peak Popularity and Sales in 1990s** : Doc Martens reached their zenith of popularity in the early 1990s, becoming an indispensable accessory for participants in the "Lollapalooza nation." The classic 1460 model, featuring eight holes, was highly sought after, and despite requiring significant breaking-in time, the boots were one of the few fashion labels to which alt-rock fans pledged allegiance. By 1994, the company was selling 125,000 pairs monthly in the US and 10 million worldwide annually. "Docks reached the peak of popularity in the early 1990s as they became an essential accessory for anyone who was part of the Lava Polluzination. They needed a lot of breaking in and blisters were common, but now is besides the point. Docks were one of the few fashion labels to which alt-rock kids pledged allegiance. **Post-Grunge Decline and Evolution** : Following the decline of grunge and the rise of pop music in the mid-1990s, the Doc Marten company experienced a downturn but subsequently underwent significant "Darwinian evolution." While the classic boot remains a staple, the company now produces a diverse range of dress and casual shoes, alongside updated versions of the 1460, offering as many as 70,000 variations from three-eyed shoes to 20-eyed boots. "But then the bottom fell out in the middle 1990s after the demise of Grunge and the rise of pop music. Since then, the company has undergone some serious Darwinian evolution. The classic boot is still sold, but the company also makes a variety of dress and casual shoes in addition to an updated version of the 1460. **Transition to Fashion Accessory** : Initially introduced in the UK in 1960 as a medical aid, Doc Martens rapidly evolved into a significant fashion accessory by the early 1970s. Punks and skinheads adopted them as a form of protest fashion, valuing their unpretentious, working-class aesthetic, which starkly contrasted with the Birkenstocks favored by hippies, and whose steel toe also proved practical in altercations. "These shoes were first introduced into the UK as a medical aid in 1960, but by the early 1970s, the boots had become a fashion accessory. Punk's and skinheads adopt the shoe as a protest fashion thing. These were unpretentious working-class boots, the exact opposite of the Birken stops worn by hippies. And they went perfectly with a punk uniform of jeans instead of jackets and mohawks. The steel toe was also handy in a fight." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mhQ36Y-c9fM
