# label - Ticketron

## curiosities
**Demise and Acquisition by Carlyle Group (1990)** : By 1990, Ticketron's operational and financial difficulties became insurmountable, forcing the company to sell. It was acquired by the Carlyle Group, a private equity firm. "By 1990, Ticketron was such a mess that they had no choice but to sell. And the buyer was the Carlyle group, a private equity firm founded in Washington, D.C." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OCkov6FTliE
**Emergence from TRS (1969)** : The company Ticketron officially emerged in July 1969 when Ticket Reservation Systems Inc. (TRS) acquired the name from another struggling computer ticketing company and rebranded itself. Ticketron quickly became a major player in the ticket-selling industry. "And in July 1969, TRS officially became Ticketron." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OCkov6FTliE
**Failed Diversification Attempts** : Ticketron explored diversification into other ventures, including selling lottery tickets and enabling horse betting through its terminals. These attempts, however, did not succeed in bolstering the company's failing business model. "Ticketron also had this idea that they could sell lottery tickets through their terminals. They had the idea of betting on horses through their terminals." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OCkov6FTliE
**Inventory Allocation Issues** : A significant problem for Ticketron was its inability to secure full ticket allocations for shows, often receiving only about 50% of the available inventory. Fans discovered this limitation, bypassing Ticketron to purchase directly from venue box offices for better seats, which severely impacted the company's revenue. "Another problem was the Ticketron never got all of the tickets for a given show. They would do well to be allocated 50%." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OCkov6FTliE
**Reverting to Hard Tickets for Major Shows** : Due to its system limitations, Ticketron was forced to revert to selling traditional "hard tickets" for high-demand events involving artists like the Rolling Stones and Led Zeppelin. This undermined the benefits of computerized ticketing and pushed fans back into long lines and scalper issues. "It got so bad that for big shows, Rolling Stone, Led Zeppelin II examples, the Ticketron had to become a seller of hard tickets just like the old days." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OCkov6FTliE
**Technical and Client Service Struggles (Late 1980s)** : Despite achieving significant scale, selling tens of millions of tickets and deploying nearly a thousand terminals across the US, Canada, and Europe, Ticketron struggled with client relations and system capacity. Processing a single ticket request could take up to five minutes on a bad day, leading to customer dissatisfaction and a loss of market share. "The company just couldn't handle the volume of requests on its machines. Instead of 15 seconds to process a ticket request, which would be an eternity today, it might take up to five minutes on a bad day." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OCkov6FTliE
