# label - Hammond Organs

## curiosities
**Design Features of Large Hammonds** : Large Hammond organs are characterized by having two 61-key keyboards stacked one above the other. As an electric instrument, a note continues to play as long as its key is depressed, and tones can be modified and created using a series of pull mechanisms called drawbars. "The big Hammonds are known for having two 61 key keyboards stacked one above the other, and because it's an electric instrument, a note plays as long as the key is depressed." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Y0HW8LQKvJw
**Founding and Early Market** : The manufacturer of Hammond organs dates back to 1935. The company initially targeted churches, offering their organs as a more affordable alternative to large, expensive pipe organs or pianos that were too quiet for big choirs and congregations. "The manufacturer of Hammond organs dates back to 1935, and the first organs were marketed to churches as an alternative to giant expensive pipe organs, or pianos, which could be too quiet for large choirs and congregations." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Y0HW8LQKvJw
**Resurgence in the 1990s** : Although Hammond organs were dismissed as outdated "dinosaur rocker" instruments during the punk era, musical trends dictated a resurgence. By the early 1990s, the Hammond B3, or at least its characteristic sound, experienced a significant comeback. "But like everything else in music trends come and go, and by the time we get to the early 1990s, the Hammond B3, or at least the Hammond B3 sound, was back." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Y0HW8LQKvJw
**The Iconic B3 Model** : Among the many Hammond models, the B3, introduced in 1954, particularly captured the attention of rock musicians. It remained in production for 20 years and became a staple for its distinctive sound. "There are many models of Hammonds, but the one that captured the attention of rock players was the B3 introduced in 1954, and they stayed in production for 20 years." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Y0HW8LQKvJw
**Use with Leslie Speakers** : Many Hammond players chose to route their organ's sound through an external tone cabinet, with the Leslie speaker being the most recognized. The Leslie speaker featured a rotating horn, which could create unique Doppler effects by adjusting its rotation speed. "And the best known of all these external cabinets was the Leslie speaker, which had a rotating horn, without getting too complicated, you can get some pretty cool Doppler effects by increasing and decreasing the speed at which the horn rotates." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Y0HW8LQKvJw
