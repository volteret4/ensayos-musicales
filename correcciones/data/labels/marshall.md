# label - Marshall

## curiosities
**Designed for Distortion** : Unlike contemporary amplifiers that aimed for clean sound, Marshall amplifiers were explicitly engineered for distortion, a crucial characteristic for the evolving rock sound. Their unique circuitry and use of vacuum tubes produced a signature type of distortion. "And unlike all the other amps on the market that promised clear distortion for e-sound, Marshall's were designed specifically for distortion. Its circuitry and use of vacuum tubes were unique, resulting in a signature sort of sound." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fQCOj3_zy54
**First Amplifier (1962)** : Despite his initial lack of knowledge about amplifiers, Jim Marshall ventured into manufacturing. His first amplifier, a 35-watt model largely based on a Fender design, went on sale in 1962. "The first was a 35 watt amp based largely on a fender design that went on sale in 1962, and it was okay." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fQCOj3_zy54
**First Marshall Amplifier, the JTM45** : By 1963, Jim Marshall had created the first Marshall amplifier, the JTM45, after musicians frequented his shop seeking louder amps. This amplifier was revolutionary for its time, pushing a "screaming" 100 Watts and utilizing tubes for a warm sound, contrasting with emerging solid-state circuitry. "And by 1963, he had created the first ever Marshall amplifier, the JT-M45. It pushed out a screaming 100 Watts, an unheard of amount for 1963." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Y0HW8LQKvJw
**Founding and Early Business (1960)** : Jim Marshall, a British drummer and tap dancer, opened a shop in London in 1960, initially selling only drums. He later expanded his inventory to include other musical instruments, laying the groundwork for his entry into amplifier manufacturing. "Starting with a shop in London that sold just drums in 1960, Jim added more musical instruments to his inventory." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fQCOj3_zy54
**Head and Cabinet Design** : Marshall amplifiers pioneered the design where the amplifier (the "head") was separate from the speaker cabinets. This innovative approach allowed bands to string together more heads and cabinets, leading to the visual phenomenon of "giant walls of Marshall cabinets" on stage, often with only some actually plugged in. "The amplifier was separate from the speaker cabinets. It's known as a head, which was a new way of looking at things." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Y0HW8LQKvJw
**Iconic Super Lead Model 1959** : The Marshall stack, officially known as the Marshall Super Lead Model 1959, became the foundational design for the "walls of Marshall amps and cabinets" seen today. Available in half-stack (one cabinet) or full-stack (two cabinets driven by a head) configurations, it is considered the most iconic amp setup for guitarists seeking volume, power, and distortion. "This was the Marshall stack, officially known as the Marshall Super Lead Model 1959. It is the basis for all the walls of Marshall amps and cabinets that we see today, either with a half-stack design with one speaker cabinet or a full stack with a head unit driving two cabinets." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fQCOj3_zy54
**Innovation of the Marshall Stack** : Jim Marshall and his partners revolutionized amplifier design by separating the amplifier electronics (the "head") from the speakers (the "cabinet"). This innovation resulted in a head capable of pumping out up to 100 watts—significantly more power than other available amps—and a cabinet typically containing four 12-inch speakers. "What if we separated the amplifier from the speakers? How's them in two separate units? The result was an enclosure featuring all the electronics called the head and a second enclosure with four 12 inch speakers." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fQCOj3_zy54
**Pete Townshend's Influence** : Pete Townshend, whose father was a professional dance band musician and friend of Jim Marshall, frequently purchased guitars from Marshall's store. Townshend, supported by other customers, strongly urged Jim Marshall to provide the burgeoning London rock scene with more powerful and suitable amplification. "Because he was friends with Pete Townsend's father, who had been a professional dance band musician, Pete bought a lot of his guitars from Marshall's store." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fQCOj3_zy54
**Profound Contribution to Rock and Roll** : The impact of Marshall amps and cabinets on rock and roll is considered monumental, second only to the electric guitar itself. They became indispensable for achieving the genre's characteristic volume, power, and distorted sound. "It's not overstretching it to say that next to the electric guitar itself, Marshall amps and cabinets are the thing that contributed to rock and roll the most." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fQCOj3_zy54
**Tube-Driven Warm Sound** : Marshall amplifiers, including the JTM45, were designed with tubes instead of the then-new solid-state circuitry. This choice gave them a wonderfully warm sound that became a signature characteristic. "They also used tubes rather than the hot new solid state circuitry, which gave them a wonderfully warm sound." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Y0HW8LQKvJw
**Volume Control to 20** : Marshall once sold an amplifier model that featured a volume control dial extending from 0 to 20, playfully indicating an even greater output capacity than the proverbial "turned up to 11." "And by the way, Marshall once sold an amp where the volume control went from 0 to 20. Just saying." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=qrrwqdpCjpQ
