This meant Epiphone models, though produced under their own name, were owned by Gibson. "Epiphone was a standalone company formed in Izmir, Turkey in 1873, and their business was largely devoted to making banjos. **Introduction of the Les Paul Guitar** : Following new ownership in 1950, Gibson introduced its iconic Les Paul guitar in 1952. Developed in collaboration with jazz and pop artist Les Paul and designer Tim McCarty, this solid-body instrument addressed the feedback issues prevalent in hollow-body guitars of the era. "By 1950, there was new ownership. In 1952, the company introduced their iconic guitar, the Les Paul." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Y0HW8LQKvJw
**Leadership During Lean Times** : Guy Hart managed Gibson from 1924 to 1948, navigating the company through the challenging periods of the Depression and World War II. During these lean times, he diversified production to include high-quality wooden toys and, later, metal parts for military use. "Between 1924 and 1948, the company was run by Guy Hart. During lean times, he had Gibson manufacture high quality wooden toys." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Y0HW8LQKvJw
**Les Paul Standard Discontinuation (1960)** : The Les Paul Standard model, initially introduced in 1958, was discontinued in 1960. The guitars produced during this roughly three-year period are now highly esteemed, considered among the finest electric guitars ever made, and are extremely rare and expensive today. "I've heard that I mentioned in a previous episode that the model was introduced in 1958, but then discontinued in 1960." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fQCOj3_zy54
**Maestro Fuzz Tone (1962)** : Gibson introduced the Maestro Fuzz Tone in 1962, marking the release of the first-ever commercial fuzz pedal. This foot pedal, developed with engineer Glen Snottie and TV engineer Revis Hobbs following an inquiry about replicating Grady Martin's fuzzy sound, allowed guitarists to deliberately distort their electric guitar sound. Initially, 5,000 units were shipped but sold poorly in 1964; however, sales skyrocketed after Keith Richards used one on The Rolling Stones' "(I Can't Get No) Satisfaction" in 1965. "And in 1962, they started selling the first ever Fuzz pedal, a gizmo that could carefully and deliberately distort the sound of an electric guitar." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iGav9uJwA_w
**Missed Rock and Roll Revolution (1961-1968)** : In the interim period between 1961 and 1968, when the original Les Paul Standard was out of production, Gibson faced the risk of missing out on the burgeoning Rock and Roll Revolution, as demand for high-quality electric guitars soared. This gap in their product line created an opportunity for other manufacturers. "In that interim, though, 1961 through to 1968, Gibson almost missed out on the Rock and Roll Revolution." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fQCOj3_zy54
**Modernist Line Guitars (1950s)** : In the 1950s, as Rock and Roll gained momentum, Gibson introduced its "modernist line" of guitars, including the Flying V and Explorer, featuring radical designs intended to reflect the new music's energy. "It was the first of what was envisioned to be the company's modernist line." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=d9HNqb9gJSU
**Post-1969 Manufacturing Changes** : Following the sale of Gibson in 1969, the new owners implemented a series of changes in the construction and manufacturing processes of their guitars. These alterations resulted in the Les Paul models that are commonly available for purchase today. "Then Gibson was sold in 1969, and the new owners implemented a series of changes in the way guitars were constructed and manufactured. **Post-1969 Production Shifts** : After 1969, Gibson relocated production of some Epiphone models to Korea, then China, and subsequently Indonesia. This move resulted in a complex production landscape where some Epiphone ES-335s were produced by Gibson, while others were made by various licensed companies. "Things get a little murky after 1969 when Gibson moved production to Korea and then China and then Indonesia." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Y0HW8LQKvJw
**Reintroduction of Les Paul Standard (1968)** : Due to a resurgence of interest in the Les Paul Standard in the mid-1960s, Gibson resumed production of the model in 1968. However, these reissued guitars were generally not regarded as being of the same superior quality as those manufactured between 1958 and 1960. "In the middle 60s, there was a resurgence in the interest in the standard, so Gibson started making them again in 1968. They were okay, but somehow weren't as good as those produced between 1958 and 1960." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fQCOj3_zy54
**Response to Fender (1940s)** : In the 1940s, after Fender's successful introduction of electric guitars like the Telecaster and Stratocaster, Gibson, already experimenting with electrics, realized they needed a swift and strong response to compete. "Gibson, who had also been fiddling with electrics, realized that Fender had blown past them, so the company needed to respond and fast." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=d9HNqb9gJSU
**Sale to Ecuador Conglomerate (1969)** : In 1969, Gibson was sold to a beer and cement conglomerate based in Ecuador. This acquisition initiated a challenging 17-year period for the company, marked by the introduction of unpopular guitar models and significant problems with quality control. "So let's go to that sale in 1969. The buyer was a beer and cement conglomerate out of Ecuador, and for the next 17 years, Gibson guitars went through a really rough patch with unpopular models and serious quality control problems." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fQCOj3_zy54
**The ES-335 Innovation** : In 1958, Gibson introduced the ES-335, a semi-acoustic, semi-hollow body guitar designed by Tim McCarty. This model resolved the issue of dark and warm tones, offering a lighter body and double cutaways for easy access to the 22nd fret, blending solid-body amplification with acoustic warmth. "The solution came in 1958 with the Gibson ES-335, a semi-acoustic semi-holobody guitar." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Y0HW8LQKvJw
**Wartime Production and Workforce** : During World War II, Gibson's factories shifted to producing metal parts for the military. Despite this, they continued to make guitars, employing women in the factories to compensate for the significant number of men who had been drafted into service. "During the war, factories started producing metal parts for use by the military. There were still making guitars, but so many men had been drafted that women were employed in the factories." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Y0HW8LQKvJw
