## curiosities
**CD Development and Naming (1974-1979)** : In 1974, a group of Philips engineers led by Lou Ottens began researching ways to improve vinyl records. They experimented with a 20-centimeter disk before settling on an 11.5-centimeter diameter, a size chosen because it matched the diagonal width of Philips' own compact cassette. In 1979, Philips teamed with Sony to develop the compact disk, contributing expertise in manufacturing processes and proposing the "compact disk" name, leveraging its prior success with the "compact cassette." `"In 1974, a group of engineers within Phillips led by Lou Ottens started to look for ways to improve on the vinyl record."` ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OuFvfZJKcpc
**Cassette Development (early 1960s)** : Philips developed the compact cassette in the early 1960s. The diagonal width of these cassettes, 11.5 centimeters, influenced the initial diameter experiments for compact discs, with Philips' engineers first experimenting with 20 cm disks before settling on 11.5 cm. "Because Phillips also developed the cassette in the early 1960s. The diagonal width of a cassette is 11.5 centimeters." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=BHkJFA0LMro
**Cassette Standardization (1965)** : Despite initial competition from companies like Grundig and Telefunken, Philips' cassette design achieved global standardization after a 1965 licensing deal with Sony, which allowed Sony to use the design for free. This strategic move ensured Philips' design became the worldwide standard, leading to billions of cassettes being produced. `"It wasn't until Philips made a licensing deal with Sony in 1965, giving them the right to use the design for free, that things really began to take off."` ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OuFvfZJKcpc
**Collaboration with Sony on CD Standards (1979)** : Philips teamed up with Sony in 1979 after both companies realized they were working toward the same goal for a new music storage format. This collaboration involved splitting research costs, sharing knowledge, and establishing the proper standards, resulting in the "Red Book CDDA standard." "In 1979, when Sony and Phillips realized that they were working towards the same goal, the companies teamed up to split the costs of research, to share knowledge, and to come up with all the proper standards for making this new music storage format." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=BHkJFA0LMro
**Collaboration with Sony on CD Technology** : Philips, a Dutch company, worked with Sony for several years on the development of Compact Disk Technology. They released their own CD player, the CD100, about a month after Sony's CDP 101. "Sony had been working on Compact Disk Technology with a Dutch company called Phillips for a number of years, which released their own machine, the CD100, a bit of a month later." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=BHkJFA0LMro
**Compact Cassette Invention and Unveiling (1962-1963)** : Lou Ottens and his team at Philips dramatically shrunk the reel-to-reel mechanism by 1962, encasing it in wood. The size (4x2.5x0.5 inches) was determined by a shirt pocket, with a 3.81mm wide tape (rounded to 4mm) divided into four tracks, running at 1 7/8 inches per second. Initially intended for office dictation, high fidelity was not a priority. The wooden prototype was replaced by plastic, and the "compact cassette" was unveiled at the Berlin Radio Show (Funko-Stellen) on August 30, 1963, promoted as "smaller than a pack of cigarettes." `"By 1962, he and his team had shrunk the reel-to-reel mechanism dramatically, encasing it in a wooden case."` ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OuFvfZJKcpc
**Compact Disc Naming (1963-1979)** : The name "Compact Disc" was Philips' idea, inspired by their earlier invention, the "compact cassettes" in 1963. This name was agreed upon for the new format during the collaboration with Sony. "As for the name, well, that was Phillips idea. They invented the compact cassettes in 1963. That's what you actually call these things compact cassettes. So it was agreed that the new format should be called the compact disk." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=BHkJFA0LMro

**Initial CD Manufacturing Plant in Germany** : One of the two original compact disc manufacturing factories was run by Philips near Hanover, Germany, serving as a key production site for the new format. "Two factories were originally set up. Philips ran one near Hanover, Germany, and the other was run by Sony in Japan." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=BHkJFA0LMro
**Lou Ottens' Engineering Career (1952-1986)** : Lou Ottens joined Philips, the Dutch electronics company, as an engineer in 1952. By 1960, he was heading the company's new product development team. His dissatisfaction with "clunky user unfriendly reel-to-reel system[s]" led to his pivotal role in developing the compact cassette. He retired in 1986 and later acknowledged that his invention was obsolete but regretted that Sony, not Philips, introduced the revolutionary Walkman. Before retiring, he also worked on the compact disk. `"Lou Ottens worked for Phillips, the Dutch electronics company, first taking a job as an engineer in 1952. Eight years later, he headed up the company's new product development team."` ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OuFvfZJKcpc
