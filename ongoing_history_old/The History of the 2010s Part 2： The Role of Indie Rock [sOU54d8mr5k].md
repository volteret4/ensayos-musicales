# genre - Active Rock

## curiosities
**New Radio Format (2010s)** : a new radio format that rose in the 2010s, focusing on the harder end of alt rock, born in the US and largely remaining an American phenomenon (with half a dozen substations in Canada and a couple in Australia).
**Programming Focus** : played hard and heavy stuff, with a bit of classic rock but mostly new material; notably, "zero bands playing banjos and dulcimers."
**Polarization and Values** : considered by some as polarizing and too driven by testosterone, but by others as upholding the traditional rock values of alternative music.
**Historical Homage** : while focusing on new material, active rock radio stations also paid homage to classic artists like Guns N' Roses, AC/DC, Led Zeppelin, Van Halen, Ozzy Osbourne, Black Sabbath, Def Leppard, and Jimi Hendrix, plus a few metal bands from the 80s and 2000s.
**Crossover and Appeal** : incorporated alternative rock artists like Offspring, Foo Fighters, and Bush to become less of a niche, attracting listeners beyond its target demographic of men aged 18-34.
**Ratings Growth and Industry Impact (2010s)** : many active rock stations saw ratings growth in the 2010s as people looked for something louder and different, and for some, "angry music in the era of Donald Trump," attracting attention from independent record labels who saw it as a viable third promotional option behind alternative stations and mainstream rock outlets.

# genre - Pop Punk

## curiosities
**Decline in Early 2010s** : saw a decline in the early part of the 2010s, with overall popularity less than in the early 2000s.
**Pop Radio Exclusion** : pop radio stations, which previously incorporated pop punk, squeezed this music out in favor of more dance-oriented tunes.
**Magazine Decline** : magazines championing this subgenre either shrank (e.g., Alternative Press) or disappeared entirely (e.g., AMP).
**Emo's Decline** : Emo, which had been a driving force of pop punk a decade earlier, largely went out of fashion, contributing to pop punk's decline.
**Warped Tour Disappearance** : the disappearance of the Warped Tour negatively impacted the pop punk scene.
**Artist Aging** : big pop punk bands from the early 90s and 2000s had grown up, finding it hard to maintain youthful angst when approaching 50, and their fans also outgrew the music.
**Festival and Venue Shift** : pop punk bands no longer headlined major festivals like Coachella or Lollapalooza, and venues once known for booking them either closed or moved to booking different acts.
**Folk-Pop Competition** : the pop side of Alt Rock became dominated by folk, acoustic guitars, and banjo bands, which seemed to consume all available attention, contributing to pop punk's decline.

# curiosity

**Recorded Music Industry Structure** : traditionally dominated by a shrinking number of major labels (now Universal, Sony, Warner Music), but independent musicians and labels account for about 96% of the approximately 110,000 songs uploaded daily to streaming services.
**Music's Reflection of Economy - General Trend** : historically, when the economy recovers from hardship and gloom lifts, music tends to become happier and poppier in the short term, a trend observed after the 1970s oil crisis, the 1980 recession, and the 1990 recession.
**Financial Crisis of 2008 Impact** : unlike previous recessions, the recovery from the 2008 financial crisis reinforced the strength of pop in the decades-long pop versus rock cycle and helped extend its dominance, influenced by a booming stock market and a shift in public mood.
**Barack Obama's Election Influence (2009)** : the election of Barack Obama on a platform of hope, combined with a humming economy, contributed to music having a "happy tune," as music is "always downstream from what's happening in society."
**Hipster Culture (Post-2000)** : emerged with a history back to at least 2000, becoming ingrained by 2010, characterized by a contrarian lifestyle, valuing retro styles (clothing, beer, cigarettes, music), environmental consciousness, sustainable/organic living, and a desire to be recognized as different from the mainstream.
**"Hipstermatic" Book (2011)** : a book published in 2011 that described the "uprising" of hipster culture, contrasting with mainstream society's focus on reality television, dance music, and celebrity gossip.
**"The Year the Music Died" (2016)** : CNN reportedly coined this phrase for 2016 due to the unusually high number of prominent music hero deaths, and the phenomenon reportedly led to a new category on Wikipedia.
**Mid-2010s Music Hero Deaths** : a grim realization about the inevitability of music hero deaths began around December 3, 2015, with Scott Weiland's death, and intensified significantly in 2016 with David Bowie's death on January 10, 2016, leading to a "snowball" effect of deaths throughout the year.
**Causes of Music Hero Deaths (2010s)** : most deaths were attributed to some kind of medical issue or old age, but there were also many suicides, including Keith Emerson (2016), Craig Gill (2016), Chris Cornell (2017), and Chester Bennington (2017).
**Aging Music Heroes and Future Deaths** : many musical heroes are in their 70s and 80s, having lived hard lives filled with "sex, drugs, and rock and roll," late nights, lack of sleep, bad food, and too much alcohol; actuarial tables suggest that the "in-memoriam list is going to contain a lot of big, big names" in the 2020s.
**Rock Fragmentation (2010s)** : the 2010s saw an explosion of new genres, sub-genres, and even sub-sub-sub-genres of rock, driven by new ideas, creative cross-pollination, and new technology, a continuation of a trend that began as soon as rock was born in the 1950s.
**Ongoing History of New Music Podcasts** : a podcast series hosted by Alan Cross, with episodes available for download on podcast platforms and on his website.
**A Journal of Musical Things.com** : Alan Cross's website, updated daily with a free newsletter offering summaries of the day's coolest and most interesting music stories; Alan Cross is also active on Twitter, Facebook, Threads, and TikTok.
**Rob Johnston's Role** : provided technical production for the "Ongoing History of New Music Podcasts."
