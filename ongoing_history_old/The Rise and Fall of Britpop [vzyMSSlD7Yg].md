This included influences from David Bowie, Roxy Music, T-Rex, The Smiths, Morrissey, British Psych, Jagger Swagger, and Glam guitars.
**Dandy English Pop Persona** : Singer Brett Anderson cultivated the persona of a "dandy English pop," proudly singing in a London accent, explicitly contrasting with the grunge aesthetic.
**Britpop Status (Late 1995)** : By the end of 1995, the band had "crept back into the scene" amidst the dominance of Oasis and Blur.
**Post-Britpop Status (Late 1997)** : By the end of 1997, as Britpop ended, the band "evaporated."

# artist - Blur

## members
Damon Albarn

## genres
Post-Madchester
British Pop
Alternative Rock
Low-fi American Alternative
German Progressive Rock
Sub-Saharan African Music

## albums
**Park Life (1994)** : A "career altering and game-changing" and "very, very British album" released on April 25, 1994, just 17 days after Kurt Cobain's death. The album had multi-generational appeal, embraced by "everyone from indie hipsters to mums and dads" due to its distinct "Englishness." It was at its peak when Oasis released their debut album *Definitely Maybe* in August 1994.
**The Great Escape (1995)** : The much-anticipated follow-up to *Parklife*, announced for release on September 11, 1995. The album's single, "Country House," was part of the "Battle of Britpop." The album, however, only reached a disappointing number 150 on the American charts, especially when compared to Oasis's success.
**Blur (1997) – Self-Titled** : Released in February 1997, this self-titled album marked a significant "aesthetic reversal" for Damon Albarn and the band, signaling a new sonic path that was "not Britpop." The album reflected Albarn's fascination with low-fi American alternative music, obscure German progressive rock, and music from sub-Saharan Africa.

## songs
**Country House (1995)** : The first single from *The Great Escape*. Its release date was moved up to August 14, 1995, to directly compete with Oasis's "Roll With It." In the "Battle of Britpop," Blur won, selling 274,000 copies compared to Oasis's 216,000. It entered the charts at number one and was certified triple platinum in the UK within a year.
**Song 2 (1997)** : A "massive international hit" from their 1997 self-titled album, showcasing the band's new sonic direction away from Britpop.

## curiosities
**Post-Madchester Creative Struggles (Early 1990s)** : The band was struggling creatively, stuck in a "post-Madchester funk," leading Damon Albarn to adopt a new mission statement.
**Anti-Grunge Mission Statement (1993)** : Damon Albarn declared a new mission for Blur, aiming to counter grunge's influence in the UK. He stated, "If punk was about getting rid of hippies... then I'm getting rid of grunge. People should smarten up, be a little more energetic. They're walking around like hippies again. They're stooped, they've got greasy hair. There's no difference. Whether they like it or not, they're listening to Black Sabbath again. It irritates me." This generated significant positive press.
**Role in Britpop (Mid-1990s)** : The band was perceived as the "articulate defenders of the realm and diarists of modern British culture," contrasting with Oasis during their rivalry.
**Britpop's First Couple (Mid-1990s)** : Damon Albarn was in a relationship with Elastica's frontwoman, Justin Frischmann, making them "Britpop's first couple."
**The Battle of Britpop Winner (August 1995)** : Blur emerged as the chart winner in the intense showdown with Oasis on August 14, 1995, selling 274,000 copies of "Country House" against Oasis's "Roll With It."
**Aesthetic Reversal (1997)** : By 1997, Damon Albarn underwent a "stunning aesthetic reversal," moving away from the proudly British sound he championed. He became fascinated by low-fi American alternative music (like Pavement and The Pixies), obscure 1970s German progressive rock, and sub-Saharan African music. This shift led him to spend less time in London and more in Iceland, manifesting in their 1997 self-titled album.
**Post-Britpop Status (Late 1997)** : As Britpop officially ended, Blur "broke up for a while before reforming for some big money."

# artist - Oasis

## members
Noel Gallagher
Meg Matthews (Noel's then-wife)

## genres
British Rock
Alternative Rock

## albums
**Definitely Maybe (1994)** : The band's debut album, released on August 30, 1994, was a "sensation," hitting stores just as Blur's *Park Life* was at its peak. It presented a sound that felt "extremely familiar," drawing comparisons to The Beatles and The Rolling Stones.
**(What's the Story) Morning Glory? (1995)** : The band's second album, released on schedule on October 2, 1995. On its release day, it sold at a rate of "1 every 2 minutes." Within a week, it sold 350,000 copies, becoming the fastest-selling British album ever to that point. It "tuteled along selling 200,000 copies a week" in America, contrasting with Blur's lower chart performance there. This album made Oasis "the biggest band on Planet Earth" for a time.
**Be Here Now (1997)** : The band's third album, which served as "the first concrete indication that something wasn't right" with Britpop's decline. Its production was marred by "astronomical" alcohol and cocaine budgets. The songs were criticized for being "too long," the melodies "uninspired," and the record "too thick with unnecessary layers of guitars," sounding "kind of shrill to a lot of people" due to cocaine's effect on high-frequency hearing. Due to the band's immense success, "no one willing to tell Oasis to go back and do anything again," allowing Noel Gallagher to proceed as he saw fit. Noel Gallagher later admitted, "I wasn't prepared to make things any better. I'd get to a certain point and just say, screw it, that's good enough. We made a record to justify the drug habit. I was making the record to justify spending thousands on drugs." In 2015, Noel declined an opportunity to fix the record for a deluxe reissue, stating, "screw it, I'll just butcher it. Let's leave it alone. Actually, I kind of like the way it is anyway."

## songs
**Supersonic (1994)** : Released as a single two weeks before Blur's *Park Life* album, indicating "perfect timing." The song was recorded in just one day. The "Elsa" mentioned in the song was a giant Rottweiler at the studio, and the "Elka Celso" reference referred to her "bad gas."
**Roll With It (1995)** : The first single from their album *Morning Glory*. Originally set for a mid-September release, its date was controversially moved up to mid-August, and then to August 14, 1995, to clash directly with Blur's "Country House." The band sold 216,000 copies, losing the chart battle to Blur.
**Stand By Me (1997)** : A song from the *Be Here Now* album.

## curiosities
**Early Hype and Familiar Sound (1993-1994)** : Hype built around the band throughout 1993 and early 1994, with early singles hinting at something new, fresh, yet "extremely familiar," evoking The Beatles and The Rolling Stones.
**Controversial Public Image (Mid-1990s)** : The band was known for generating "great press" due to "outrageous comments, fights, arrests, drugs, being banned from various places."
**Role in Britpop (Mid-1990s)** : The band was characterized as "foul mouth, hard drinking, drug-taken yobbs, who were always acting as if you walked into the pub and spilled their pint on purpose," providing a stark contrast to Blur during their rivalry.
**The Battle of Britpop Instigator (August 1995)** : Oasis deliberately escalated the rivalry with Blur into an "actual war" by moving up the release date of their single "Roll With It" to compete directly with Blur's "Country House" on August 14, 1995.
**Overall Britpop Winner (1995)** : Despite losing the single chart battle to Blur, Oasis was considered "the clear winner" in the "overall war" of Britpop due to the monumental success of their album * (What's the Story) Morning Glory?*.
**Excessive Lifestyles and Drug Use (Late Britpop)** : Rampant alcohol and cocaine use permeated the band, particularly during the recording of *Be Here Now*. Noel Gallagher's then-wife, Meg Matthews, also had her shopping sprees detailed in daily papers and was given her own column to write about her excesses.
**Political Co-option Controversy (Late Britpop)** : Noel Gallagher was invited to 10 Downing Street by Prime Minister Tony Blair. This created "bad optics" for Noel, as it conveyed that "this music of the people had been co-opted by the establishment and the government."
**Post-Britpop Status (Late 1997)** : While Britpop officially ended, Oasis "stuck together but were in their own little bubble that allowed them to continue for another decade."

# genre - Britpop

## curiosities
**Cultural Storm (1991-1997)** : An era that acted as a "perfect cultural storm," running from approximately 1991 to 1997. During this time, "London was swaying again," and "British acts were selling tens of millions of albums the world over." It was characterized as "fresh and fun and cool."
**UK's Defense Against Grunge (Early 1990s)** : From its inception, Britpop's primary objective was to "defend the empire from the grunge marauders from the colonies," which had invaded and were conquering British lands, supplanting the "proud tradition of British rock and wall."
**Emergence from a Musical Vacuum (Early 1990s)** : The movement arose to fill a void in British music. The 1980s club and rave culture had waned, the Manchester scene (featuring The Stone Roses and Happy Mondays) was "pretty much over," and Shoegaze had "failed to ignite the masses." This coincided with the rise of Nirvana's *Nevermind*, which allowed grunge and American alt-rock to enter the British Isles.
**Origin of the Term (1994-1995)** : The word "Britpop" first appeared in the May 1994 issue of *The Face* magazine, an abbreviation riffing on Blur's view for a "renewed emphasis on British pop." A few months later, *The Guardian* newspaper used the term. The "real tipping point" came in *NME* in January 1995, after which the term became widely adopted.
**Peak Hysteria (Summer 1995)** : The movement reached its peak in the summer of 1995, primarily fueled by the intense rivalry, or "war," between Blur and Oasis. Media coverage was "non-stop," and the public engagement was so high that bookies even took bets on the outcome of their chart battle.
**Decline and Collapse (Late 1995-1997)** : The "Cool Britannia" era collapsed under the weight of "overexposure, boredom, and drugs." Factors contributing to its decline included a loss of focus with many inferior bands, overexposure of major acts like Oasis, the challenge for major players to release successful follow-up albums, and rampant heroin and cocaine use within the industry.
**Political Co-option (Late Britpop)** : Prime Minister Tony Blair, a self-professed fan, invited Noel Gallagher of Oasis to 10 Downing Street. This event was perceived as the "music of the people" being "co-opted by the establishment and the government," which was seen as damaging to the movement's image.
**Official End (Late 1997)** : By the end of 1997, "almost everyone was distancing themselves from anything to do with Britpop." The "party was officially over," characterized by a "bad hangover," the toll of drugs, and public and media boredom. It was subsequently replaced by phenomena like the Spice Girls, and bands like Radiohead and Coldplay. The "six-year wave" had concluded.

# curiosity

**Cultural Storms in Music** : These phenomena are described as moments when musical, social, political, and demographic conditions converge, capturing public imagination. They typically last about six years: two years of build-up, a 12-18 month "frenzied peak," and a "long, slow denouement." Examples include The Beatles (1964-1970), Punk Rock (1976-1982), and Grunge (1990-1996).
**Canadian Music Renaissance (Early 1990s)** : During the early 1990s, alongside Britpop and American alternative rock, the Canadian music scene experienced a renaissance with the rise of bands such as The Tragically Hip, Our Lady Peace, and Sloan.
**American Alternative Nation (Early 1990s)** : In the early 1990s, the US music scene was dominated by grunge and the punk revival, and the Lollapalooza generation also showed interest in industrial music and hip hop.
**British Music Press Influence (Early-Mid 1990s)** : Initially "only too happy to welcome their new alien overlords" (American grunge) due to a void in British music, the British music press later played a crucial role in promoting Britpop. New monthly magazines like *Select* and *The Face*, along with weekly papers *NME* and *Melody Maker*, devoted extensive coverage to homegrown indie bands.
**Impact of Kurt Cobain's Death (1994)** : Kurt Cobain's suicide "prematurely killed any momentum Grunge had," creating a "vacuum" that unexpectedly "opened the door for everything that happened in British rock for the next several years."
**Cool Britannia / Swinging London Feature (Mid-1990s)** : *Vanity Fair* magazine in America featured a story on "Cool Britannia" and "Swinging London," demonstrating the international recognition and cultural impact of the Britpop movement.
**"The Battle of Britpop" (August 1995)** : A media-fueled "war" between Blur and Oasis that peaked in August 1995. The bands deliberately moved up their single release dates ("Country House" and "Roll With It") to clash on August 14, 1995. This frenzy caused record sales across Britain to surge by 41% for that week.
**Tony Blair's Britpop Connection (Late Britpop)** : Tony Blair, who played in a rock band during university and was a fan of Britpop, became Prime Minister. His invitation of Noel Gallagher to 10 Downing Street was seen as "bad optics," suggesting that the "music of the people had been co-opted by the establishment and the government."
