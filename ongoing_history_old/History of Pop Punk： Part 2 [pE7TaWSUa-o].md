# genre - Pop Punk

## curiosities
**Origin as Hybrid** : A hybrid genre formed by the merger of punk and pop over decades, responsible for selling hundreds of millions of records and concert tickets.
**Purity Controversy** : Faced accusations of "selling out" from purists who felt the music was "softer in intensity" and too commercially successful, disqualifying it from being true punk and labeling bands as "illegitimate pretenders."
**Attitude Shift** : Characterized by an attitude shift away from politics, social messages, and class struggles, embracing fun, almost carefree themes, often written in major keys to sound inherently happy, and encouraging goofiness and juvenile humor.
**Economic & Cultural Context (Mid-1990s)** : Flourished during a period of economic improvement in the mid-1990s, serving as an "antidote" and "release" from the gloom of grunge and the early 90s recession.
**Bifurcation in Mid-2000s** : Experienced a "bifurcation" where some bands were deemed "too poppy for punk" by alt-rock radio stations and audiences, who considered their music "kiddie punk" appealing to the "wrong kind of people" (i.e., too young).
**Decline in Late 2000s** : Declined as video channels stopped showing music videos, supporting magazines (Alternative Press, Spin) shrunk to online publications, commercial radio shifted focus, and bands moved towards pure pop.
**Emergence of "New Punk Pop" (Post-2010)** : A new generation of bands emerged after 2010, often with a "darker" sound compared to 1990s pop punk, possibly influenced by the 2008 financial crisis or the election of Donald Trump.
**Enduring Nature** : Proved to be pretty enduring, with its measure of "snotiness" ensuring it will likely never completely disappear, despite not reaching its mid-90s peak due to competition from hip hop and rap.

# genre - Punk Rock

## curiosities
**Original Purpose** : Originally created as an "attack on pop" music.
**Early Characteristics (1970s/Early 1980s)** : Characterized by being loud, fast, angry, nihilistic, and strident, often considered "too intense" for mass consumption.
**Revival in Mid-1990s** : Experienced a revival, largely powered by bands later categorized as pop punk, which was more accessible and easier to digest than old school punk or early 1980s hardcore.

# genre - Grunge

## curiosities
**Dominant Era (Early 1990s)** : Defined the early 1990s era, featuring bands like Nirvana, Pearl Jam, and Soundgarden, and Alice in Chains.
**Musical Style** : Characterized by music that was "slow and low," sometimes employing a quiet-loud dynamic.
**"Gold Rush" Phenomenon** : Its sudden popularity, particularly after Nirvana's Nevermind sales figures were revealed by SoundScan, sparked a "Grunge Gold Rush" where record labels sought out similar acts.
**Decline by 1994** : Began "slowly losing momentum" by 1994, with its "heaviness and bleakness" seeming "a little too much" after Kurt Cobain's death, and remaining bands sounding "more and more derivative."

# genre - Alt-Rock

## curiosities
**"Music for Weirdos and Outsiders" (Pre-1990s)** : Up until the early 1990s, this genre was largely considered music for "weirdos and outsiders."
**Post-Grunge Appetite** : After the Grunge Gold Rush, record labels realized there was an appetite for alt-rock beyond grunge, leading them to sign bands like Jane's Addiction, Red Hot Chili Peppers, Sonic Youth, and R.E.M.
**Increased Accessibility (Mid-1990s)** : The pop punk form of alt-rock became even more accessible to more people than grunge.
**Musical Evolution (2010s)** : Overall, alt-rock in the 2010s moved towards slower tempos, away from guitar-heavy sounds, incorporating more keyboards, samples, and banjos, and went through a profound pop phase.

# curiosity

**SoundScan Implementation (1991)** : A new system introduced on March 1, 1991, which counted album sales by scanning barcodes at checkout, providing a "much, much, much more accurate picture" of sales compared to previous methods.
**Pre-SoundScan Chart Compilation** : Until February 28, 1991, Billboard magazine's weekly album charts (Top 200) were compiled based on "verbal reports" and "estimates" from a "select group of record stores," which were prone to "mistakes, lies, [and] bribery" and "manipulation."
**SoundScan's Impact on Genre Perception** : The first SoundScan report revealed that country music and "left of center rock music" (alt-rock) were selling significantly better than previously known, leading record labels to rethink their strategies.
**Record Labels' Pivot to Alt-Rock** : Following the revelation of Nirvana's Nevermind selling 300,000 copies a week after SoundScan, record labels initiated a "Grunge Gold Rush" to find more alt-rock; this later expanded to include other alt-rock bands like Jane's Addiction, Red Hot Chili Peppers, Sonic Youth, and R.E.M.
**Kurt Cobain's Death and Music Trend Shift (1994)** : Kurt Cobain was found dead on April 8, 1994; this event contributed to a public sentiment that the "heaviness and bleakness of Grunge seemed a little too much," opening a space for the lighter, more tuneful sound of pop punk.
**Economic Improvement and Happier Music (Mid-1990s)** : After a severe recession, the improving world economy and people's moods in the mid-1990s fostered an appetite for lighter music, with pop punk serving as an "antidote" to grunge's gloom and the recession's impact.
**MTV/MuchMusic's Role in Pop Punk Breakthrough** : These video channels played "all the videos" from pop punk bands, significantly contributing to the genre's mainstream breakthrough in the mid-1990s.
**Radio's Adoption of Alt-Rock Format (Mid-1990s)** : Even more radio stations adopted the alternative rock format in the mid-1990s, joining the trend that started with grunge and further propelled the punk rock revival, largely powered by pop punk bands.
**Rock's Last Years at Cultural Peak (Mid-1990s)** : The mid-1990s were described as "rock's last years at the top of the cultural heat" before hip hop and rap began to exert their power and influence.
**Generation X Demographics (Mid-1990s)** : Generation X was at its peak, and the large cohort of kids in this demographic loved pop punk, contributing to its success.
**Democrat in White House Correlation with Music Mood** : The presence of a Democrat in the White House was noted as almost always resulting in happier sounding music overall.
**Decline of Traditional Music Media (Late 2000s)** : As the 2000s ended, video channels "no longer showed videos," and once-prominent magazines like Alternative Press and Spin (big supporters of pop punk) "shrunk to online publications," diminishing visibility for music.
**Alt-Rock's Musical Evolution (2010s)** : In the 2010s, alt-rock overall moved away from guitar-heavy sounds to incorporate more keyboards, samples, and banjos, and went through a "pop phase more profound" than seen since the 1980s, making pop punk sound "heavy in comparison."
