# genre - Alt-Rock

## curiosities

**Early Radio Career (1986)** : Alan Cross's career in alt-rock radio began in 1986, encountering a diverse range of music.
**1980s Outsider Culture** : the scene in the 80s reveled in its "outsider-ness," wanting nothing to do with the mainstream, characterized by a tribal feel.
**Radio Station Diversity in the 80s** : commercial alt-rock radio stations in the 80s were not connected by charts or airplay tracking; each station curated its own playlist, leading to a "staggering breadth of music."
**"Golden Age" of Commercial Alt-Rock Radio** : some retrospectively consider the 80s as the golden age, despite low salaries for staff who were "hardcore music fans" on a mission to deliver good music, prioritizing music over ratings initially.
**Grunge's Impact (1990s)** : the rise of grunge (Pearl Jam, Soundgarden, Alice in Chains, Nirvana) caused alt-rock to coalesce around hard guitar rock, leading to a homogenization of sound and the squeezing out of earlier diverse sounds.
**Post-Grunge Evolution** : after the original grunge bands died off and New Metal emerged, things "loosened up" with the explosion of Indie Rock, leading to a greater variety of sounds than in the late 90s.
**Modern State of Alt-Rock** : the variety of sounds is much greater, with less reliance on grunge guitars, to the point that some argue there are "too few big guitar bands" today.
**Shift in Radio Demands** : modern alt-rock radio is unlikely to return to its pre-grunge diversity due to irrevocable changes in public demands and listening habits over 40 years.

# genre - Grunge

## curiosities

**Rise in Early 1990s** : became a dominant force in alternative music around 1991-1992, supplanting the mainstream.
**Impact on Alt-Rock Homogenization** : its popularity led alt-rock to coalesce around hard guitar rock, reducing the variety of sounds on alt-rock radio and leading to an "unstoppable march towards a homogeneity in sound."
**"Alt Rock with Training Wheels"** : described as a genre that made great music and lured millions into the alternative nation with a "different yet familiar approach to traditional guitar rock."

**"Well Ran Dry" in Late 90s** : the original grunge bands died off, and for a while in the late 90s, there was "nothing concrete to replace it," leading to "Dreck-like New Metal."
**Alt-Rock's Addiction to 90s Music** : the popularity of grunge and other 90s music led to audiences continually demanding it, and many acts from that era remained popular, making it harder for new artists to break through in the 21st century.

**Lollapalooza 1992** : by this time, alternative music was no longer an alternative and was supplanting the mainstream, largely due to grunge.
**Homogenization of Alt-Rock Radio** : after grunge, industry magazines started tracking charts and airplay, leading smaller stations to emulate larger ones (e.g., Des Moines following K-Rock in LA), resulting in an "unstoppable march towards a homogeneity in sound."
**Alt-Rock's Role in Mainstream Introduction** : grunge served as "Alt Rock with training wheels," luring millions into the alternative nation and inspiring them to explore deeper into alt-rock and beyond.
**21st Century Superstar Decline Theory** : Alan Cross theorizes that the 21st century has seen a decline in the creation of new superstar acts comparable to past decades (60s-90s), with existing superstars largely originating from earlier eras.
**Factors Contributing to Superstar Decline** : - **Nostalgia/Legacy Acts**: Audiences kept demanding popular 90s music, and many acts from that era remained popular, "sucking up much oxygen and a lot of airplay," making it hard for new artists. - **Industry Shift**: Decline of physical media sales and closure of record stores. - **Streaming Empowerment**: Streaming shifted power from "star-making machinery" (labels, radio, stores, magazines, video channels) to the individual listener, who became their "own music directors." - **Increased Competition**: Current acts compete with "the entire musical history of the human race" due to instant access to 70 million+ songs. - **Lack of Consensus**: Streaming makes it impossible to achieve the same kind of public consensus about "good music" that existed in pre-streaming days.
**Streaming's Impact on Music Consumption and Creation Theory** : Alan Cross theorizes that streaming is "ruining music" by changing listening habits and warping the music itself.
**Pre-Streaming Listening Behavior (Physical Media)** : - **Financial Relationship**: Buying physical music (records, CDs, tapes) created a financial investment, encouraging repeated listening to "absorb all the music" and avoid feeling money was wasted. - **Supplementary Resources**: Physical formats came with album artwork, lyric sheets, and liner notes, enriching the experience. - **Scarcity**: Limited affordability and store stock (e.g., 100,000 albums in largest stores) fostered deeper engagement with owned music.
**Streaming-Era Listening Behavior**: - **Access vs. Possession**: Streaming is about access and convenience, not possession. - **Skip Behavior**: Data shows 24% of listeners skip a song within 5 seconds, 29% after 10 seconds, and 35% after 30 seconds if they don't like or recognize it. - **30-Second Payout Rule**: Streaming services do not pay artists for a song unless it's listened to for at least 30 seconds, influencing production. - **Impact on Algorithms**: Skipping songs drives down their appeal with algorithms, preventing them from appearing on playlists.
**Streaming's Impact on Song Structure**: - **Front-Loaded Production**: Producers and songwriters "throw everything they can at the first 30 seconds" to hold attention (shorter intros, choruses upfront, more hooks). - **Shorter Song Lengths**: Spotify's algorithms favor shorter songs; average Billboard Hot 100 song length decreased from 4 minutes (2000) to 3 minutes 30 seconds (today); number of songs under 2.5 minutes has increased annually. - **Financial Incentive for Brevity**: Artists are incentivized to write shorter songs because "more listens equals more money," and a 40-second song can earn the same payout as a 10-minute epic.
**Streaming's Impact on Albums**: - **Focus on Singles**: Streaming makes it "all about individual songs, specifically singles," with album tracks receiving less attention. - **Album Longevity**: Albums are still the currency for industry contracts and awards, and physical formats (CDs, vinyl) will persist short-term despite falling sales. - **Continuous Release Model**: The hip hop world's "drip, drip, drip" of new songs is replacing the traditional cycle of year-long recording followed by three years of touring an album.
**History of Album vs. Single Dominance**: - **Pre-1965 Single Dominance**: Before roughly 1965, rock and roll and pop music existed primarily as 7-inch 45 RPM singles; albums were for "proper music" (classical, jazz, soundtracks, Broadway). Early Beatles/Elvis albums were mostly single compilations. - **Post-1965 Album Revolution**: Around 1965, artists like The Beatles and Bob Dylan began treating albums as larger artistic works, writing longer, complex, sequenced collections (e.g., _Sgt. Pepper's_ in 1967). - **Industry Shift to Albums**: Record labels favored albums due to higher profit margins, moving rock and pop away from singles. - **Return to Single Dominance**: Streaming, playlists, and emphasis on individual songs are pushing music back towards a pre-1965 single-centric model.
**Vinyl Resurgence Theory** : Alan Cross discusses skepticism about the long-term sustainability of the vinyl resurrection.
**Origin of Record Store Day (2008)** : created by independent record store owners in Baltimore, inspired by comic book day, to attract customers with free and exclusive product and revive their businesses amidst digital music and piracy.
**Vinyl Sales Growth Post-2008** : after the first Record Store Day in 2008, vinyl sales experienced "year after year of double digit growth" globally.
**Revival of Pressing Plants** : old, mothballed pressing plants came back online, and new manufacturers started up with state-of-the-art equipment due to demand.
**Vinyl Outselling CDs (2020)** : in 2020, the dollar value of vinyl sold in the US exceeded that of CDs, a phenomenon not seen since 1986.
**Threats to Vinyl Resurgence**: - **Record Label Greed**: Labels are selling new vinyl at "ridiculous prices" (e.g., Daft Punk's _Random Access Memories_ for $45). - **Supply Chain Issues**: A fire at a California plant in early 2020, which made 80% of the world's lacquer masters (essential for vinyl manufacturing), led to production delays and higher prices. - **COVID-19 Impact**: The pandemic "killed off a lot of record stores," affecting both new and used vinyl markets.
**Phantom of the Paradise (1974)** : a strange rock musical horror comedy loosely based on _The Phantom of the Opera_, directed by Brian De Palma, starring and with music by Paul Williams.
**Phantom of the Paradise Plot Summary** : poor Winslow Leech has his music stolen by the evil Swan (Paul Williams' character), is disfigured in a vinyl pressing plant accident, dons a disguise, and terrorizes The Paradise music venue; things end poorly for everybody involved.
**Winnipeg's Obsession with Phantom of the Paradise** : the movie was a box office flop everywhere except Winnipeg, where it stayed in theaters for months, becoming an "essential part of the musical DNA of that city," leading to tribute bands and annual festivals attended by movie stars.
**Frankenstein Novel (1818) as Goth Genesis** : Mary Shelley's novel, published in 1818, is considered the first to blend horror, death, romance, science fiction, and the macabre in a dark, spooky way, serving as the "whole genesis of everything goth."
