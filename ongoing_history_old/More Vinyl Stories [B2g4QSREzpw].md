### The "Death" and "Resurrection" of Vinyl

At the dawn of the 21st century, vinyl records were widely considered obsolete, declared "dead, dead" by many within the music industry and consumer market. The prevailing sentiment was that the world was inexorably moving towards digital formats, rendering the ancient vinyl format irrelevant.

*   **Reasons for Demise:** Vinyl faced numerous criticisms: it was perceived as dusty, scratchy, and noisy. Records consumed significant storage space and were susceptible to warping and water damage. However, the most significant drawback cited against vinyl was its lack of portability, especially in comparison to the burgeoning MP3 technology of the era. The ability to carry "a thousand songs on a single device that could fit in your pocket was pretty sexy," marking a stark contrast to the cumbersome nature of vinyl.
*   **Industry Contraction:** The decline in demand led to a drastic reduction in vinyl production. Pressing plants ceased operations, and their specialized machinery was either sold for parts or entirely scrapped. This made it increasingly difficult and expensive to acquire new turntables or cartridges, further cementing vinyl's perceived obsolescence.
*   **Custodians of the Format:** Despite the widespread abandonment, two distinct groups championed the format, preventing its complete extinction:
    *   **Hardcore Collectors:** These enthusiasts never fully embraced the promises of digital music, maintaining their commitment to physical media.
    *   **DJs:** Many DJs preferred the tactile experience and unique mixing capabilities of spinning records over mixing CDs.
*   **The Unforeseen Revival:** Against all odds, vinyl began its remarkable resurgence in 2008. Described as rising "from the dead like some zombie in one of those old Italian horror movies," the format experienced an unexpected revival.
*   **Current Market Dominance (Revenue):** Today, vinyl is experiencing a level of commercial success it hasn't seen since the early 1990s, generating more revenue than compact discs. While the global volume of CD sales remains higher, vinyl's premium pricing allows it to outpace CDs in total earnings. This phenomenon persists despite ongoing challenges such as supply chain disruptions, shortages of polyvinyl chloride (PVC), extensive backlogs at pressing plants, and steadily increasing prices.
*   **Personal Journey of a "Vinyl Addict":** Alan Cross, the podcast host, confesses to being a "vinyl addict" despite having previously strayed into digital formats like CDs and iTunes downloads (and even admitted to possibly downloading illegal files from Napster). His own turntable was relegated to a crawl space, and his existing vinyl collection remained in the basement out of sheer "laziness" rather than active interest. His "road to Damascus moment" led him to embrace vinyl fully again, purchasing new equipment, frequenting record shows and Record Store Day events, and even planning visits to vinyl stores during his travels. He now exclusively buys vinyl for physical music and high-resolution digital files for digital consumption, boasting a personal collection of approximately 7,000 records.

### The Analog vs. Digital Debate: Sound Quality and Technicalities

The perennial debate over whether vinyl or digital formats offer superior sound quality is often highly emotional and complex. The text delves into the technical aspects of both, concluding that the answer is highly conditional.

*   **Illustrative Example: Roxy Music's *Avalon***: The 1982 album *Avalon* by Roxy Music is cited as an "exquisite" sounding vinyl record, frequently used for testing audio equipment. It was recorded under optimal analog conditions at Compass Point Studios in the Bahamas, likely using two-inch tape running at 30 inches per second, before being "lovingly transferred to vinyl."
*   **Technical Foundations:**
    *   **Vinyl (Analog):** This format is inherently analog, meaning it captures and reproduces real-world sound waves smoothly and continuously, mirroring the original acoustic signal.
    *   **Compact Disc (Digital):** CDs, based on standards set in the 1970s, convert analog sound into a digital stream. This involves:
        *   **Sampling Rate:** Breaking each second of music into 44,100 segments (44.1 kHz), effectively taking 44,100 "snapshots" of the sound per second.
        *   **Bit Depth:** Storing 16 bits of information per sample, allowing for 65,536 levels of information to describe the dynamic range of the signal. This precisely measures how each of the 44,100 samples was recorded.
        *   Human ears typically perceive information up to 12-14 bits, suggesting that 16-bit depth is generally sufficient for average listening.
*   **CDs vs. MP3s:** The text strongly asserts that a CD will "always, always sound better than an MP3" to a listener with "proper ears." This is because MP3s are compressed files, where significant amounts of information are removed to reduce file size. Our brains can detect these missing gaps, subconsciously attempting to "fill them in," which can result in a less satisfying listening experience compared to the richer data on a CD.
*   **The Conditional Superiority of Vinyl:** The assertion that "vinyl sounds better than a CD" is met with a definitive "it depends" due to several crucial factors:
    *   **Vinyl's Condition and Playback System:** Optimal sound requires vinyl in pristine condition (free from dust, static, and wear) played on a high-quality turntable and a proper audio system. The source material itself must also be recorded with care and to a high technical standard.
    *   **Frequency Range:**
        *   Vinyl excels at preserving mid-range frequencies, which can make "fuzzy rock guitars" sound particularly good.
        *   However, vinyl struggles with reproducing extreme high and low frequencies with the same accuracy as CDs.
        *   The perceived "warmth" of vinyl might, in some cases, be attributed to slightly distorted bass frequencies.
    *   **Inner Groove Distortion:** As the stylus moves closer to the center of a record, the groove circumference shrinks. The stylus's linear velocity relative to the groove increases, making it harder to trace the grooves accurately. This can lead to a degradation of sound quality towards the inner tracks.
    *   **Original Recording Medium:** If a vinyl record is pressed from a digitally recorded master, it is simply an analog copy of a digital recording. A "noticeable difference" in sound quality is more likely if the entire production chain, "from the studio to the pressing of the record," remains analog.
    *   **Manufacturing Quality Over Time:** The quality of vinyl pressings has varied significantly throughout history:
        *   **Late 1970s and Early 1980s:** This period is described as producing "unmitigated crap" in terms of vinyl quality. Raw polyvinyl chloride was often recycled and impure, and labels frequently skimped on the amount of vinyl used (e.g., 90-gram records compared to today's 180-gram standards). Consequently, a record from 1972 is likely to sound superior to one manufactured in 1979.
        *   **Early CD Era (vs. contemporary vinyl):** When CDs first emerged, they often sounded demonstrably better than the poorly manufactured vinyl records of that time.
    *   **Upgraded Digital Audio Formats:** High-resolution digital formats, such as Super Audio CDs (SACDs) or DVD-audio versions often found in box sets, offer significantly upgraded audio (e.g., 96 kHz, 24-bit) and are described as sounding "really, really awesome."

Ultimately, the choice of format boils down to personal preference, as "whatever sounds and feels better to you" is the most important factor.

### The "Loudness Wars" and Mastering Differences

A critical distinction between how music is prepared for vinyl versus CD lies in the mastering process, which has profoundly impacted sound quality, especially in the digital era.

*   **Different Mastering Approaches:** Vinyl and CD mastering require distinct approaches due to their inherent technical characteristics.
*   **Vinyl Mastering for Warmth:** A common observation is that many finished vinyl records sound "warmer." This is often a deliberate choice in the mastering process for vinyl, aiming for a richer, more rounded tonal quality.
*   **CD Mastering and Excessive Compression:** In contrast, a significant number of CDs are mastered with "way too much compression." The primary goal of this practice is to make the music sound louder, leading to a perceived competitive advantage in a retail environment (e.g., on a radio or in a store).
*   **Consequences of Over-Compression:**
    *   **Dynamic Range Squishing:** Excessive compression "squishes" the dynamic range of the music, reducing the difference between the loudest and quietest parts. This removes much of the music's expressiveness and impact.
    *   **Clipping and Distortion:** When dynamic range is severely compressed, the loudest parts of the audio waveform are often "clipped," resulting in digital distortion. This makes the music sound harsh and fatiguing.
*   **The "Loudness Wars":** This industry-wide trend of compressing mixes to achieve maximum loudness has become infamous as the "loudness wars." The text notes that engineers are "compressing the crap out of a final mix just so the music seems louder coming off the CD."
*   **Notable Offenders:** Several prominent artists and albums are singled out for their poor mastering during the loudness wars:
    *   **Metallica:** Both *St. Anger* (2003) and *Death Magnetic* (2008) are cited as being "unlistenable" due to extreme compression.
    *   **Red Hot Chili Peppers:** Anything released by the band "since 1999" is criticized, with specific mention of audible distortion in Flea's bass on their recordings.
    *   **Green Day:** The album *American Idiot*.
    *   **Oasis:** *What's the Story Morning Glory*.
    *   **Foo Fighters:** *Echoes Silence Patience and Grace*.

### Environmental Impact of Music Formats

The environmental footprint of music consumption is a topic explored with surprising conclusions, challenging common assumptions about digital versus physical media.

*   **Polyvinyl Chloride (PVC) - The Material:** Vinyl records are made from polyvinyl chloride, the third most popular type of plastic globally. Beyond records, PVC is used in a vast array of products, including bottles, credit cards, electrical cable insulation, fake leather, flooring, windows, doors, inflatable items, and plumbing products (dating back to sewer pipes in the 1920s). Most PVC production originates from China and Southeast Asia.
*   **Environmental Concerns with PVC:**
    *   **Fossil Fuel Byproduct:** PVC is derived from fossil fuels, contributing to carbon emissions and dependence on non-renewable resources.
    *   **Chemical Additives:** Raw PVC is brittle and requires chemical stabilizers, known as plasticizers. Historically, these have included toxic heavy metals like lead and cadmium. Carbon is also added to give records their characteristic black color.
    *   **Non-Biodegradable and Leaching:** As a plastic, vinyl is "forever" – it does not decompose. When records end up in landfills, they remain there indefinitely, potentially leaching hazardous chemicals into the environment for "years, decades, centuries."
    *   **Legacy Issues:** In the 1970s, a typical year saw 160 million kilos of PVC converted into records, leaving a massive environmental legacy. Melting down old vinyl, while possible, is costly and produces highly toxic hydrogen chloride gas.
*   **Mitigation and Eco-Friendly Innovations:**
    *   **Low Landfill Rate:** The good news is that relatively little vinyl ends up in landfills, as people tend to retain their collections.
    *   **Sustainable Manufacturing:** Efforts are underway to create more eco-friendly vinyl records. Some European pressing plants are nearing "99% carbon neutral" manufacturing processes.
    *   **Recycled Vinyl:** Companies are working with recycled vinyl, aiming for higher quality than the impure, recycled records of the 1970s (which were a response to the oil crisis).
    *   **Novel Substitutes and Experiments:**
        *   **Nick Movy:** A British singer released a record made from "junk vinyl" recovered from the ocean.
        *   **Biodegradable Alternatives:** Some researchers are experimenting with PVC substitutes that are biodegradable under specific conditions.
        *   **Edible Vinyl:** There have even been attempts to create edible vinyl substitutes.
        *   **Cannabis Resin Record:** The band Slightly Stupid famously experimented with pressing a record from cannabis resin ("Dabbington"). The concept involved listening to the record and then melting it down to smoke. This proved impractical due to the high cost ($6,000 worth of resin per record) and poor sound quality; the band ultimately smoked the resin and released the track on regular vinyl. Only two copies of a bubble hash resin record were ever made as an experiment.
*   **Vinyl/CDs vs. Streaming: A Counterintuitive Conclusion:** Surprisingly, physical formats like vinyl and CDs are presented as **more environmentally friendly than streaming** in the long run.
    *   **Energy Consumption of Digital:** Generating and transmitting audio streams consumes vast amounts of energy to power computers, servers, routers, and user devices (phones, laptops). Even receiving data uses energy.
    *   **Downloading vs. Streaming Energy:** Downloading a song once requires the same energy as streaming it once. However, playing a downloaded song subsequently requires significantly less energy than repeatedly streaming it.
    *   **Reduced Carbon Emissions with Downloads:** It's estimated that an 80% reduction in carbon emissions for digital music consumption could be achieved if all songs were downloaded instead of streamed.
    *   **Battery Life Impact:** Streaming consumes twice the battery power on phones and laptops compared to playing a locally stored, downloaded file.
    *   **The "27 Streams" Rule:** The text suggests that if an album is streamed 27 times, it becomes more environmentally sound to purchase a physical copy (vinyl or CD).
    *   **Conclusion:** "Vinyl is cleaner than the cloud." Therefore, if environmental concerns are paramount, purchasing a record is advocated as a more eco-conscious choice.

### Unique Vinyl Formats and Pressing Innovations

Beyond the standard black disc, the vinyl world has a rich history of innovative and often experimental pressing techniques designed to attract collectors and offer novel experiences.

*   **Colored Vinyl:** A common strategy to entice collectors, records are issued in a vast array of colors, including multi-color "Jackson Pollock-style splatters."
*   **Picture Discs:** These records feature images "sprayed delicately onto the vinyl" surface.
    *   **Historical Context:** Early examples appeared in the UK in the 1920s. Notably, Adolf Hitler issued picture discs of his speeches for the Hitler Youth in the 1930s. The first modern picture disc is attributed to a German label, Metronome, in 1969, featuring bands like The Doors and MC5.
    *   **Popularity Peak:** Picture discs gained significant popularity in the late 1970s and early 1980s, with a trend of featuring artist interviews.
    *   **Sound Quality Warning:** Generally, picture discs are noted for having the worst sound quality. Other formats with compromised audio include glow-in-the-dark records and those with glitter or spattered colors. For optimal sound, "stick with basic black."
*   **Optical Illusion Discs:** Some records are designed with images that create an optical illusion when rotating on a turntable.
    *   **Fisher Z - "The Worker" (1979):** The single for this British band featured a special picture disc with a train image, which rotated in sync with the song's lyrics about a train, creating a unique visual effect.
*   **Augmented Reality (AR) Discs:** Modern innovations include records that incorporate augmented reality experiences.
    *   **Eno and Hid - *Someday World***: This album features images that, when viewed through a smartphone, unlock an AR experience.
*   **Extreme and Novelty Pressings:** Labels like Jack White's Third Man Records are renowned for pushing the boundaries of vinyl production, creating highly collectible and often outlandish editions.
    *   **Oil-Filled Records:** Records composed of two halves with "wild colored oil inside."
    *   **Incorporated Materials:** Other pressings have famously incorporated materials such as blood, hair, urine, marijuana leaves, razor blades, gunpowder, and even cremated human remains.
    *   **Functional Records:** The label Romanus created a record for the Indianapolis band Brother O Brother that doubled as a functional guitar pedal.
    *   **Extraterrestrial Dust:** The band Emperor Yes released their album *An Island Called Earth* in limited editions (100 copies) on green, yellow, and orange vinyl, each sprinkled with authentic dust from a 16th-century asteroid crash.

### Historical Oddities and Collectibles

Vinyl's long history is filled with fascinating anecdotes, niche formats, and unique cultural phenomena.

*   **Diverse Vinyl Sizes:** Records come in various diameters: 5, 7, 10, 12, and even 16 inches. Nine Inch Nails once released a 9-inch diameter record.
*   **The Smallest "Vinyl" Record:** Researchers at the Nano-Scale Materials and Devices (Nano-Made) section of the Technical University of Denmark created what they called the "smallest vinyl record ever." Though not made of PVC, it functions like a full-size record, playing in stereo for 25 seconds. It measures a minuscule 15 micrometers across, making it invisible to the naked eye, and features Brenda Lee's "Rockin' Around the Christmas Tree."
*   **The 2020-Present PVC Shortage:** The global pandemic led to a significant shortage of polyvinyl chloride, exacerbated by supply chain disruptions and an unexpected surge in demand for plastic takeout containers, which coincided with increased online record sales and existing pressing plant backlogs.
*   **Soviet Union "Ribs" (Roentgenizdat):** During the Soviet era, Western music was officially banned. To circumvent this, resourceful music fans would scavenge old medical X-rays from hospital dumpsters. They then used repurposed gramophones to cut grooves onto the X-ray film at 78 RPM, creating clandestine recordings. The center hole was often burned through with a cigarette. These black market records, known as "ribs" (or "Roentgenizdat" in Russian), were extremely low fidelity but cheap (about one ruble). They earned their name because the bones of the original X-ray subject were still visible on the film. Possession of these "ribs" could lead to severe penalties, including imprisonment in a gulag. Today, these "ribs," particularly those featuring The Beatles, The Rolling Stones, Elvis, and The Beach Boys, are highly sought-after collectibles.
*   **Flexi Discs:** A thin, flexible sheet of vinyl designed to be included as an insert in books or magazines.
    *   **Inception:** First appearing in 1962 under the name "Eva-Tone Soundsheet."
    *   **The Beatles' Christmas Messages:** Between 1963 and 1969, The Beatles famously sent out special Christmas Flexi Discs to their fan club members.
    *   **Magazine Inserts:** Flexi Discs became popular inserts in various magazines during the 1970s, from *Mad Magazine* to *National Geographic*, as well as numerous music publications.
    *   **The Clash's "Train in Vain" Anecdote:** The iconic Clash song "Train in Vain" was originally conceived as a free Flexi Disc giveaway for the *NME* music magazine, which the band initially considered a "throwaway" track. When the deal with *NME* fell through, The Clash found themselves with an extra song for their *London Calling* album and decided to add it at the very last second, resulting in it being unlisted on the album jackets. Paradoxically, this "throwaway" track eventually became The Clash's most commercially successful single.
    *   **Modern Relevance:** Flexi Discs are still produced today, sometimes appearing on Record Store Day. The Foo Fighters have released at least one, and they remain popular with many punk bands.
    *   **McDonald's Million-Dollar Contest (1988):** McDonald's ran a contest using Flexi Discs, offering a grand prize of a million dollars. The discs featured school kids singing "The Menu Song." The winning disc required the *entire class* to sing the full song, with chances of winning estimated at 1 in 80 million. A 13-year-old from Virginia serendipitously found the winning Flexi Disc in a pile of newspapers he was preparing to burn.

### Collecting Vinyl: Considerations and Advice

For both new and seasoned collectors, understanding the nuances of vinyl manufacturing and proper terminology is crucial.

*   **The Plural of "Vinyl":** An important linguistic and social tip for collectors is that the plural of "vinyl" is "vinyl," not "vinals." Using "vinals," especially in public or during record shopping, is cautioned against as it "will just unmask you as a newbie" and can lead to "grief and embarrassment."
*   **New vs. Used Vinyl – "It Depends":** The decision to buy new or used vinyl is also conditional.
    *   **Caution with Specific Eras:** Collectors are advised to be "very careful" when buying records pressed in the mid to late 1970s and early 1980s, as manufacturing quality during this period was "terrible."
        *   **Exceptions:** "Audio-file editions" from this era might be exceptions, as they were made with greater care using "virgin vinyl" (not recycled, impure PVC). Records made before 1972 are also generally considered better quality.
    *   **Caution with 80s/90s Pressings:** Many records from the 1980s and 1990s might have been directly sourced from CD masters without proper mastering for vinyl, often resulting in inferior sound quality.
    *   **Confidence in Modern New Vinyl (Since 2008):** Since around 2008, purchasing new vinyl comes with less risk. Modern pressings typically use virgin vinyl and are produced as "nice and heavy" discs (180, 200, or even 220 grams).
        *   **Benefits of Heavy Vinyl:** These heavier records are less prone to warping, sit flatter on the turntable, and allow the stylus to track the grooves more accurately, which "could, could result in better sound" depending on the source material and playback equipment.
*   **Checking Source Material:** When buying new reissues, it's advisable to check liner notes for details on the mastering process. For example, a new pressing of Alice in Chains' *Dirt*, remastered from original audio, is considered a good candidate for a "full end-to-end analog production" if the original recording era and studio suggest analog tape was used.
*   **Optimal Playback Setup:** A high-quality playback system is essential for appreciating vinyl. Alan Cross mentions his own studio setup: a Technics SL-1200 turntable, an Ortofon Red cartridge, and a pre-amplifier.
