### The Pre-Digital Record Store Experience

Before the advent of the internet and digital music consumption, the acquisition of music was a multi-faceted experience deeply intertwined with emotional and financial investment, discovery, and social interaction.

*   **Emotional and Financial Investment:** Purchasing physical music, such as a vinyl record or CD, represented a significant investment. Beyond the monetary cost, there was an implicit commitment to extract maximum enjoyment from the purchase, lest one feel they had "wasted money." This financial stake often compelled listeners to engage more deeply with their purchased music.
*   **The Quest for Music:** Acquiring music necessitated leaving home and physically navigating a record store. This "quest" involved sifting through shelves, often hoping to find a specific item. If a desired record was out of stock, special ordering it added another layer of anticipation and emotional investment.
*   **Discovery and Education:** The act of browsing alone was a form of education. Flipping through record stacks exposed customers to unknown artists and albums, broadening their musical horizons organically.
*   **Social Hub:** Record stores served as vital social spaces. Friends would often visit together, fanning out to explore and later compare their finds. Strangers might strike up conversations, sharing recommendations and insights. For regulars, the staff behind the counter often became trusted sources of musical advice and new discoveries.
*   **Connection to Live Music:** The physical record store experience was also linked to the broader music ecosystem, serving as venues for artist autograph sessions or a stepping stone to attending live performances.
*   **Nostalgia:** For generations who grew up with this model, the record store evokes a strong sense of nostalgia, representing a bygone era of tangible connection to music and a specific way of life before smartphones, streaming, and instant downloads. Many patrons would spend hours just browsing, listening to the clerks' selections, and absorbing the atmosphere.

### Early History and Evolution of Music Retail

The concept of selling recorded music has a rich history, beginning shortly after its invention and evolving through various retail channels before dedicated record stores became prevalent.

*   **Inception in Furniture Stores:** Pre-recorded music, in the form of Edison cylinders and Berliner discs, was first introduced in the late 1880s. These were initially sold in furniture stores, alongside the phonographs and gramophones that played them. This made sense at the time, as early playback units were often regarded as high-end furniture pieces. By 1906, an astounding 25,000 record dealers existed in the United States, predominantly furniture stores, a network established by early industry giants like Victor and Edison.
*   **Pioneering Sales Techniques:** Early sales often involved demonstration recordings designed to introduce customers to the novel concept of recorded audio. Examples include a 1906 Edison holograph recording, charmingly extolling the device's ability to provide melody and joy, and a 1910 Columbia double-disc demonstration record, highlighting the then-innovative feature of music on both sides, offering "double value."
*   **Diversification of Retailers:** Beyond furniture stores, records soon appeared in department store chains like Sears, Kresge's, Kmart, and Woolworths. Independent stores also emerged, and even local drug stores would carry small displays, typically with a limited selection of genres including rock, pop, country, and instrumental "mood music" albums for older demographics.
    *   **Personal Anecdote:** Alan Cross recalls his first album purchase, Elton John's *Greatest Hits Volume One*, from a bin at Robinson's drug store for $4.99 plus tax, earned from his paper route. His mother's fury over him "wasting money" on something "trivial" like a record illustrates the societal perception of music as a luxury item at the time.
*   **The First Dedicated Record Stores:**
    *   **Spillers Records (Cardiff, Wales):** Often cited as the world's first dedicated record store, opening in 1894 by Henry Spiller, initially selling phonographs, Edison cylinders, and early discs. Remarkably, it remains in business today, having moved several times.
    *   **Demato Records (Valetta, Malta):** A disputed claim places Demato Records' opening in 1885, just seven years after Thomas Edison's talking machine demonstration, potentially making it the very first.
    *   **Modern Stance:** Spillers Records made headlines in 2019 for banning all Morrissey records due to the artist's perceived association with far-right politics.
*   **Early Radio and Record Stores:** Some of the world's first radio stations, such as XWA in Montreal and KDKA in Pittsburgh, relied on local record stores (like Layton Brothers Records) for their programming. Engineers would obtain records free of charge in exchange for on-air announcements acknowledging the source, long before record labels had dedicated promotional teams.
*   **Economic Setbacks:** While new record stores proliferated in the 1920s, the Great Depression dealt a devastating blow, causing record sales to plummet by an astonishing 95% between 1930 and 1932. The industry experienced a slow but eventual recovery.
*   **Oldest Existing Stores:** In the U.S., the title of the oldest existing record store is contested between Ryan Hertz Music and Video (a piano store that started selling records in 1901) and Georgia Sound Shop (opened 1932). In Canada, Wilson and Lee in Oshawa had a history dating back to 1922 but closed in 2019.

### The Birth of "Rock and Roll" and Record Stores

Record stores played an unexpected and pivotal role in the popularization and naming of one of the 20th century's most influential musical genres: rock and roll.

*   **Alan Freed and Leo Mintz's Partnership:** In the late 1940s, radio announcer Alan Freed, working at WAKR in Akron, Ohio, developed a friendship with Leo Mintz, owner of Rendezvous Records, a prominent record store in Cleveland. Mintz observed a significant trend: white teenagers were avidly purchasing R&B records imported from the American South.
*   **Radio Integration:** Mintz suggested Freed play these R&B records on his radio show. When Freed moved to Cleveland for a job at WJW, Mintz bought airtime on the station. Freed hosted, playing R&B records directly supplied by Rendezvous Records, with the first broadcast airing on July 11, 1951.
*   **"Moon Dog" and the First Rock Concerts:** The partnership proved immensely successful. Freed became a sensation, branding himself "the Moon Dog" (due to his show's late-night slot). He then began promoting live shows featuring the R&B acts, which he called "Moon Dog Balls"—retrospectively recognized as the very first rock concerts.
*   **Coining "Rock and Roll":** Faced with a legal threat from a New York street musician who held dibs on the "Moon Dog" moniker, Freed needed a new term for the music he championed. The widely accepted theory traces the term "Rock and Roll" directly back to a record Mintz provided him: Billy Ward and the Dominoes' 1951 recording, "60 Minute Man."
    *   **Lyrical Inspiration:** The song contains the line, "I rock them, roll them all night long," which was African American slang for sexual activity. Given Freed's deep knowledge of R&B and his target audience, it is understood that he was aware of the coded meaning and that his listeners would likely grasp it, making the term resonate with a rebellious undertone.
    *   **Mintz's Enduring Influence:** This historical chain of events highlights Leo Mintz and Rendezvous Records as an essential catalyst, planting the seed for the modern usage of the term "rock and roll" through the records they supplied.

### The Business of Music Retail: Format Wars and Risks

Operating a record store has always been a challenging endeavor, fraught with financial risks and the constant pressure of evolving music formats.

*   **Inherent Risks:** Running a record store involves significant capital investment in space, inventory, and staff. The music market is inherently unpredictable; outside of guaranteed superstar releases, knowing what will sell is a constant gamble.
*   **Format Transitions and "Shelving Disputes":** The industry has repeatedly introduced new formats, each requiring stores to adapt their infrastructure and inventory.
    *   **Early 1980s CD Introduction:** After investing heavily in shelving for 45s, LPs, 8-tracks, and cassettes, record stores in the early 1980s strongly resisted the industry's push to adopt the new Compact Disc (CD) format. They were reluctant to spend more money on new shelving and inventory during a deep recession.
    *   **Resolution and Industry Savior:** The resistance subsided when it was demonstrated that two CDs could be stacked side-by-side in the same space as a single 12-inch album, easing shelving concerns. More importantly, the CD ultimately proved to be the "thing everybody needed," revitalizing the struggling music industry during the recession and eclipsing the earlier format disputes.
*   **HMV's Early History:** In the UK, one of the earliest prominent stores was HMV (His Master's Voice), opened on Oxford Street in London in 1921. It was owned by The Gramophone Company, a record label that released music under the HMV label. A decade later, The Gramophone Company merged with another manufacturer to form Electrical and Musical Industries (EMI), which became a globally recognized record label.

### Iconic Record Store Chains: A Global Overview

The 20th century saw the rise of numerous record store chains, many of which became cultural institutions before facing the challenges of the digital age.

#### HMV (His Master's Voice)

*   **Global Reach and Peak:** HMV expanded into a worldwide phenomenon, operating 320 stores at its peak in the 1990s. Its flagship store at 383 Oxford Street, London, was once the largest record store in the world, capable of hosting significant in-store performances. It had a presence across Australia, Ireland, Singapore, Hong Kong, India, Italy, and a brief, less successful foray into the U.S. (mostly Manhattan, closing by 2004).
*   **Canadian HMV's Distinctives:** Canadian HMV stores were known for their customer-friendly policies, including a no-hassle returns policy and a popular loyalty program ("Buy 10 CDs, Get the 11th Free"), which was particularly appealing when CDs were priced at $17-20 each.
*   **Decline and Bankruptcies:** By 2010, the entire HMV group was in financial distress. Rising vinyl sales could not offset the massive decline in CD sales, compounded by increasing rents, falling foot traffic, and an unsuccessful attempt to diversify into music merchandise. A particularly poor Christmas season in 2010, exacerbated by bad weather, led to store closures starting in January 2011. HMV UK went bankrupt in 2013, followed by a failed revival attempt and another bankruptcy. HMV Canada, operating as a subsidiary, also faced bankruptcy, closing all its stores by April 30, 2017.

#### Sunrise Records: From Local Chain to Global Leader

*   **Origins and Canadian Growth:** Sunrise Records began in 1977 with a single store on Yonge Street in Toronto. Over the next decade, it grew to become one of Canada's five major record chains (alongside HMV, Sam the Record Man, Music World, and A&A Records), reaching a peak of 85 locations.
*   **Near Collapse and Resurgence:** Like other chains, Sunrise struggled and by fall 2014, was reduced to just a couple of locations. However, it was then purchased by Doug Putnam, a toy company owner with a new vision for record retailing.
*   **Strategic Acquisitions and Global Expansion:**
    *   **HMV Canada Takeover:** When HMV Canada went bankrupt, Putnam's Sunrise Records shrewdly acquired the leases for 70 of its locations across Canada, transforming them into Sunrise stores.
    *   **HMV UK Acquisition:** Following HMV UK's second bankruptcy, Sunrise bought Hillco's (the previous owner's) interest, keeping the HMV brand alive in the UK and Ireland. This included opening a new flagship store, "HMV Vault," in Birmingham in 2019.
    *   **U.S. Expansion:** In 2020, Sunrise further expanded by acquiring f.y.e. (Five Entertainment), which owned 206 record stores under various brand names across the United States.
*   **World's Largest Operator:** Through these strategic moves, Sunrise Records, based in Ancaster, Ontario, has become the world's largest operator of brick-and-mortar record stores, boasting over 400 locations globally.

#### Sam Goody

*   **Founding and Innovation:** Sam Goody was founded by Sam Goodowitz in New York City in 1948, establishing a chain based out of Minneapolis in 1951. His initial strategy focused on selling only brand new 33 1/3 LP records.
*   **Unique Gimmick:** Goody gained notoriety for a marketing gimmick: customers who bought $25 worth of records (at a time when LPs cost $4.85, equivalent to over $50 today) received a free record player.
*   **Peak and Decline:** The company reached its peak with over 1,300 stores under the Sam Goody and Musicland names. Today, only two locations remain, both now owned by Canada's Sunrise Records.

#### Virgin Megastores

*   **Richard Branson's Vision:** The first Virgin store opened in January 1971, founded by Richard Branson, who would establish Virgin Records a year later. The company experienced rapid growth.
*   **Megastore Concept:** The first "Megastore" opened on Oxford Street, London, in 1979, near HMV's flagship. Virgin Megastores proliferated globally, with 125 in the UK, 35 in France, 23 in the U.S. (including the iconic Times Square location, and large stores in LA and San Francisco), Australia, Japan, Greece, Hungary, Italy, Spain, Germany, Netherlands, 32 across the Middle East, five in Morocco, and even one on Reunion Island.
*   **Canadian Presence and Decline:** There was only one Virgin Megastore in Canada (Vancouver), though plans for a Toronto flagship fell through. Smaller Virgin Books and Music locations existed in airports worldwide. Today, the Megastores are all gone, though some smaller Virgin music stores might still be found in the Middle East.

#### Tower Records

*   **West Coast Origins:** Established in Sacramento, California, in 1960 by Russell Solomon, who named it after his father's drug store located in the same building as the Tower Theater. The first expansion was to San Francisco in 1967.
*   **Global Empire and Iconic Locations:** At its peak, Tower Records operated 200 locations, many as standalone stores. The most famous was on the Sunset Strip in Los Angeles, a celebrity magnet where Elton John, Michael Jackson, and Bruce Springsteen were regulars. It was renowned for its vast selection of imports and its iconic rooftop billboard. Tower expanded internationally to 18 countries, with Japan being a particularly important market, alongside Malaysia, Hong Kong, Singapore, Thailand, Philippines, South Korea, Taiwan, Ireland, Israel, and the UK.
*   **Canadian Foray and Decline:** Tower had two Canadian stores in Toronto (Eaton Centre 1995-2001, and a north downtown location also closing in 2001). Alan Cross famously claims to have been the first person inside on opening day at the Eaton Centre store.
*   **Financial Struggles and Revival:** Despite selling $1 billion worth of CDs at its peak, Tower Records faced severe financial difficulties in the early 2000s. By 2006, all but the Tokyo store had closed. Russell Solomon passed away in 2018 at 92. In 2020, the brand was revived as an online retailer.
*   **Dave Grohl's Connection:** Foo Fighters frontman Dave Grohl famously worked at a Tower Records location in Washington, D.C., in the 1980s, stating it was "the only place" he could get a job with his long hair and fashion sense. This anecdote was featured in Colin Hanks' 2015 documentary, *All Things Must Pass*, chronicling Tower Records' rise and fall.

#### Sam the Record Man (Canada)

*   **Canadian Landmark:** Founded by Sam Sniderman in 1937, Sam the Record Man became Canada's largest chain with 140 stores. Sniderman began by selling records out of his family's radio shop, opened in 1920.
*   **Iconic Flagship:** The flagship store, initially at 259 Yonge Street in Toronto (1959), later moved to a cavernous location at 347 Yonge in 1961. It was instantly recognizable by its large, iconic spinning neon records on its storefront—a cultural landmark for Toronto. The store expanded across three floors, taking over adjacent buildings and even hosting a Chinese restaurant for a time. It sold every format imaginable.
*   **Widespread Presence:** Sam's locations were ubiquitous in Canadian malls, providing many, including Alan Cross in his youth, with hours of browsing and musical discovery.
*   **Devo Anecdote:** Cross vividly recounts buying a Devo 45 of "I Can't Get No Satisfaction" in the late 1970s, expecting "snarling punk guitars" based on what he'd read, only to be confused by the synth-pop rendition. He still owns that 45.
*   **Decline and Legacy:** The chain faced significant troubles in the early 2000s, with all locations eventually closing except for a single store in Belleville, Ontario (at last report). The Yonge Street flagship building was torn down to make way for a Ryerson University building, but the historically significant neon spinning records were saved, restored, and now overlook Dundas Square from a nearby building.

#### A&A Records (Canada)

*   **Post-WWII Founding:** Founded at the end of World War II by Alice and Aaron Kenner (the "A&A"), along with Alice's husband Mac.
*   **Canadian Dominance and Bankruptcy:** For a period, A&A was the biggest chain in Canada, boasting 260 shops, with its flagship store located directly next door to Sam the Record Man on Yonge Street. However, rapid expansion led to financial difficulties, and the company went bankrupt in 1993.

#### The Warehouse

*   **Depeche Mode Riot:** This American chain gained notoriety for a specific event: an infamous riot during an autograph session for Depeche Mode's *Violator* album on March 20, 1990, at its Los Angeles store. Thousands of fans overwhelmed private security, necessitating a tactical alert from the LAPD, which deployed 20 units (still insufficient). The chaos involved fans climbing trees, multiple arrests, a fainting incident requiring hospitalization, and even damage to a Fox News microwave van. The band was forced to leave early.

### Notable Events, Anecdotes, and Cultural Impact

Record stores have been the backdrop for significant cultural moments and have directly contributed to the preservation and revival of physical music formats.

*   **Musical References:**
    *   **Buc-o-Nine – "A Record Store" (1997):** A "sky punk" song from San Diego, featured on the album *28 Teeth*, directly referencing the act of record shopping.
    *   **Radio Stars – "Buy Shazak Records" (1977):** A song specifically about record shopping, highlighting its continued relevance in the popular imagination.
*   **Record Store Day (RSD): The Savior of Independent Retailers and Vinyl:**
    *   **Inception:** In 2007, a meeting of independent record store owners in Baltimore, facing severe financial hardship, sought ways to revitalize their businesses. Inspired by the success of "Free Comic Book Day," they conceived of a similar event for record stores.
    *   **Concept and Growth:** The idea was to create special products and host artist appearances to draw customers back into stores and showcase their unique appeal. The first Record Store Day was held in 2007 and quickly spread worldwide, involving hundreds of thousands of independent stores. Record labels, recognizing a powerful marketing opportunity, embraced the initiative with special limited-edition releases, predominantly on vinyl.
    *   **Impact:** RSD proved to be an overwhelming success, saving dozens upon dozens of small record shops and directly contributing to the exponential resurgence of vinyl record sales.
    *   **Ambassadors and Timing:** Prominent artists such as Metallica, Ozzy Osbourne, Iggy Pop, and Pearl Jam have served as official Record Store Day Ambassadors. The event typically occurs on the third Saturday of April, with a secondary "Black Friday" event in November. In 2020, due to COVID-19, there were three distinct Record Store Days in addition to the Black Friday sale.
    *   **Collectibles:** RSD has led to the release of many unique and valuable items, such as Alan Cross's special white vinyl copy of U2's debut album *Boy*, limited to 10,000 copies, released as part of Black Friday 2020.
    *   **Prince's Final Public Sighting:** Poignantly, one of Prince's last public appearances occurred on Record Store Day 2016, when he was spotted at the Electric Fetus record store, just five days before his passing.

### The Modern Era: Challenges, Revival, and the Enduring Spirit

Despite the profound shifts in music consumption, record stores continue to adapt and hold a vital, if different, place in the cultural landscape.

*   **Continued Stress:** Record stores remain under "tremendous stress" in the digital age, facing fierce competition from streaming and online downloads.
*   **Enduring Value:** Nevertheless, they are still celebrated as fantastic places to "hang out, browse, and shop." The unique environment fosters learning through discovery in the racks, observation of fellow enthusiasts, and engaging conversations with knowledgeable staff.
*   **Passion-Driven Industry:** The individuals who work in record stores today are often driven by a genuine love for music, as the financial rewards in music retail are no longer significant.
*   **Call to Action:** There is a strong encouragement for listeners, especially younger generations unfamiliar with the experience, to patronize record stores. The call extends to seeking out local record shops while traveling, citing examples like Amoeba in Los Angeles and Rough Trade in London (Notting Hill and Brick Lane).
*   **Global Appreciation:** The immersive "vibe" of a record store is universal, cherished by enthusiasts who have visited these establishments in diverse locations, from Russia and the Middle East to "Fiery Street," finding them "brilliant" and loving to "hang around those places."
*   **Ongoing History of New Music:** The podcast *Ongoing History of New Music* by Alan Cross itself serves as a contemporary platform for musical discovery and historical context, an evolution from the physical record store experience, yet one that champions the same spirit of exploration and appreciation.
