### Historical Echoes: Generational Disdain for "New" Music

The apprehension towards new musical forms is far from a modern phenomenon. Historical records demonstrate a consistent pattern of older generations viewing the music of the young with suspicion or outright hostility:

*   **Ancient Philosophy:**
    *   **Socrates (c. 400 BCE):** While not directly criticizing music, his lament about the youth of his era – their luxury, bad manners, contempt for authority, disrespect for elders, and tyrannical behavior – sets a precedent for generational disapproval, framing youthful culture as disruptive.
    *   **Plato:** More explicitly, Plato warned of the profound societal impact of musical change. He posited that "new modes and rhythms in music are never altered without producing changes in the entire fabric of society." He believed these new forms, initially "seemingly harmless diversion," would imperceptibly "creep in," spreading into manners, pursuits, and eventually "attack the laws and the Constitution," leading to the "overthrowing the whole structure of public and private life." Critically, Plato disliked both slow music (which he thought promoted laziness and sloth) and fast music (which he believed ruined "sober contemplation").

*   **Late 19th and Early 20th Century Musical Morality:**
    *   **Church hymns and classical music** were considered uplifting and sophisticated.
    *   **Folk songs** were dismissed as "vulgar."
    *   **Show tunes** were feared to cause "heart attacks" due to their bouncy melodies.
    *   **Jazz (c. 1900, New Orleans):** This emergent genre was vehemently condemned as "vile and evil" and "an affront to proper music." Its origins, "largely by African Americans," further "compounded the problem" for contemporary critics. Elders at the time suggested "military marches" as the acceptable form of "exciting music." The spread of jazz led to a backlash, with ballroom dancing (especially waltzes) promoted as an alternative.

*   **Mid-20th Century Shifts:**
    *   As **big bands faded in the 1940s**, the youth embraced R&B, blues, country western, and bebop, openly "sneering at the old, outdated music of the 20s and 30s."
    *   The advent of **rock and roll** created "huge generation gaps," further cementing this pattern of musical schism between age groups.

### Scientific Underpinnings of Musical Evolution

Modern research provides a deeper understanding of the biological and psychological mechanisms behind changing musical preferences:

*   **Neurological Development:** By adolescence, the brain has developed sufficiently to process the complexities of music, coinciding with the strong emotions of puberty. This confluence creates powerful and enduring bonds linking memory, emotion, and identity directly to music. This early love of music profoundly influences "who we become and who we are for the rest of our lives."
*   **Generational Consumption Habits:** While Gen Z (late 1990s-early 2010s) streams an average of 4.5 hours of music daily—exceeding current listening habits of Gen Y, Gen X, and Boomers—it's suggested that earlier generations were equally immersed in music during their youth, but their engagement patterns shifted later in life.
*   **The Mid-20s Shift:** As individuals enter their mid-twenties, the "real world" intrudes with responsibilities like jobs, families, and financial commitments. This significantly reduces the time, money, and inclination previously devoted to music discovery. A common sentiment emerges: "new music isn't as good as it was when I was young."
*   **Spotify Data Analysis (New York Times):** This analysis reveals distinct patterns in musical taste formation:
    *   **Men:** The most impactful period for forming musical taste is between ages 13 and 16, with their all-time favorite song often released when they were 14. An example cited is Radiohead's "Creep" (1993) being most popular for 38-year-old men (who were 14 at its release), but not for those 10 years older or younger.
    *   **Women:** Their most impressionable years are between 11 and 14, with their favorite song typically released when they were 13. This suggests that "childhood or at least preteen musical influences are stronger with women than they are with men."
*   **"Open-Earedness" and Its Decline:** Young people exhibit greater "open-earedness" towards unfamiliar music. A 2013 study involving 250,000 participants found adolescents dedicated 20% of their waking time to music, a figure that dropped to 13% in adulthood. This decrease is attributed to "psychosocial maturation"—adults, having established their identity, no longer rely on external factors like music for self-discovery. The desire for musical discovery and preference for new music peaks at age 23 for women and 24 for men. By their mid-thirties, tastes are considered "matured," correlating with a decrease in the personality trait "openness to experience." As people age, they transition from an "anything goes" approach to music to becoming more discerning, often seeking music that is "cooler, more substantial, more meaningful, more complex."
*   **Changes in Hearing Acuity:** With age, hearing changes. Individuals may develop a "lower tolerance for loud sounds and audio with high frequencies." This physiological shift can contribute to a preference for "milder" music, as "volume and thumping beats with high BPMs stops being a thing." High-energy music, which once provided an "adrenaline rush," may become irritating due to increased stress and cortisol levels in adult life. While the "high volume, high energy music of your youth" remains beloved, new music with similar characteristics is less appealing.

### The Cyclical Nature of Musical Innovation and Perceived Stagnation

As listeners mature, their vast experience shapes how they perceive new music, often leading to a sense of repetition rather than originality:

*   **The Experienced Consumer's "Déjà Vu":** Older listeners become "experienced and knowledgeable consumer[s] of music."
