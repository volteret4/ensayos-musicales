# artist - Pink Floyd

## songs
**Money (1973)** : The introduction to this song features a famous cash register sequence, which was created using old-school, labor-intensive sound manipulation techniques. This involved carefully measuring out magnetic tape, cutting the desired sound bites with a razor blade, and then piecing them together with special splicing tape to form a loop. The process was described as mind-numbingly complex and took many hours to complete.

**Pump Up The Volume (1987) – Groundbreaking Single** : This song was a musical collage created by five producers and musicians (Martin, Alex, Rudy, Ivo Watts Russell, and Stephen, whose initials form the band's name). It featured samples from an Israeli singer named Ofra Haza, James Brown, the Iranian Revolutionary Army chorus, and a rap duo called Eric B. & Rakim, with the latter's sample providing the track's title. The single was initially distributed on an anonymous white label to 500 club DJs, from where its sounds and ideas spread through dance clubs and eventually into mainstream pop and rock. Although not the first hit to use advanced sampling, it had a significant and lasting impact on music.

**Pizza Order at Zoo TV Tour (March 27, 1992)** : During their Zoo TV tour in the early 1990s, U2's frontman Bono made an impromptu order of pizza for the audience at their Detroit concert on March 27, 1992. Part of Bono's stage act involved using a remote control to surf TV channels displayed on the stage's many screens. He chanced upon a commercial for a local pizza place, Speedy Pizza, and proceeded to call them on a cell phone from the stage, requesting 10,000 pizzas for the 21,064 people in attendance. While the order was an exaggeration, three Speedy Pizza employees later appeared with a cart carrying 100 pepperoni pizzas, which were flung into the chanting crowd. This incident was so notable that the next leg of the tour featured t-shirts with the slogan "I'd like to order 10,000 pizzas, please."

**Vince Clarke's Departure (December 1, 1981)** : Vince Clarke, who was the band's sole songwriter at the time, announced he was quitting Depeche Mode on December 1, 1981. His reason for leaving was a desire to be in a band that would stay in the studio and avoid touring. This departure left Depeche Mode without a primary songwriter, creating a significant challenge for the band's future.
**Martin Gore Assumes Songwriting Role (Post-1981)** : Following Vince Clarke's departure, Martin Gore, an ex-bank clerk, stepped up to fill the void as the band's chief songwriter. Since Clarke left, Gore has been responsible for writing "99.9%" of Depeche Mode's material. His prolific songwriting has been instrumental in the band's success, helping Depeche Mode become one of the biggest "new rock" bands of all time.

## curiosities
**Ricky Wilson's Death from AIDS (October 12, 1985)** : Ricky Wilson, the guitarist for The B-52s, became the first notable musician to die from AIDS on October 12, 1985. He had been experiencing illness since 1983 and was very sick for most of 1985, suffering from Kaposi's Sarcoma, infections, and pneumonia. Due to the significant stigma surrounding AIDS patients and gay men, Ricky kept his condition a secret, even from his sister and bandmate, Cindy Wilson, who only learned of his diagnosis three days before his death. He passed away at the age of 32 at Sloan Kettering Hospital in New York and was buried in Okeneh Hill Cemetery in Athens, Georgia. His tombstone bears the inscription, "The Breeze of Grace is always blowing. Set your sail to catch that breeze."

## songs
**Do They Know It's Christmas? (1984)** : The creation of this charity single was spearheaded by Bob Geldof, inspired by a BBC documentary on the Ethiopian famine he watched on November 11, 1984. Initially, Geldof thought of a benefit record for The Boomtown Rats, but realizing it wouldn't sell well, he enlisted Midge Ure of Ultravox, who took one of Geldof's half-finished songs ("It's My World") and composed the music. With lyrics written in a taxi by Geldof, 25 of Britain's most famous musicians, including Sting, Simon Le Bon, Bono, David Bowie, and Paul Weller, gathered at Sarm Studios in Notting Hill, London, on November 25, 1984, to record the track. The single was released on December 7, entered the charts at number one on December 15, and held the top position for five weeks, selling over three million copies in the UK (making it one of the biggest-selling UK singles of all time) and an additional one million in the US. This project marked the beginning of Band Aid, raising significant funds and inspiring subsequent charity efforts like Live Aid and other charity singles, ultimately fostering a belief that "Rock and Roll did have a conscience and that music could make a difference."

# instrument - Turntable

## curiosities
**Technics SP-10 Introduction (1970)** : The Technics SP-10, introduced in 1970 by Technics (a division of Japan's Panasonic), was the world's first direct drive turntable. Developed by a "shadowy group of engineers and scientists" known as the Special Products Group, this innovation eliminated belts and pulleys, directly driving the platter with a motor. This design significantly reduced "rumble" (low frequency noise) and allowed the platter to reach operating speed much more quickly. It utilized a linear frequency generator to ensure stable speed, which could also be pitched up or down by as much as 8%.
**Technics SL-1200 Introduction (1972)** : Introduced in 1972, the Technics SL-1200 was the successor to the groundbreaking SP-10 direct drive turntable. It quickly became renowned for its power, ease of use, versatility, and "indestructible" nature. The SL-1200, particularly after the introduction of its Mark II version around 1979 which featured a beefier motor and the now-familiar slide-pitch control, became the turntable of choice for DJs worldwide. Its widespread adoption was foundational for the growth of club culture, DJ culture, beat mixing, and the popularization of 12-inch records and extended remixes. Even in the post-turntable digital age, thousands of SL-1200 units are still sold annually, all hand-made in a factory in Osaka, Japan. The introduction of this turntable is considered a pivotal moment, without which figures like Fat Boy Slim might not have emerged.

# instrument - Sampler

## curiosities
**Early Sound Manipulation (Pre-1980)** : Before the advent of digital samplers in 1980, manipulating sound in non-traditional ways required extensive use of magnetic tape. Engineers would meticulously measure out reels of tape with timers or rulers, cut desired sound bites with razor blades, and then piece them together using special splicing tape. This "old school, literally cut and paste form of sound manipulation" was a time-consuming and "mind numbing" process, exemplified by the cash register sequence in Pink Floyd's 1973 song "Money."
**Fairlight Computer Musical Instrument (1980)** : Developed by the Australian company Fairlight throughout the 1970s, the Fairlight Computer Musical Instrument (CMI) made its debut in 1980 as the world's first keyboard-based digital sampler. This revolutionary device allowed operators to digitally record any acoustic sound, store it on a floppy disk, visualize its waveform on a monitor, manipulate the waveform using a light pen, and then play it back via a standard 88-note keyboard. This enabled musicians to take any sound, "shape it, mold it, and manipulate it into something totally new." The first ever digitally sampled sound in history was a barking dog. Despite its groundbreaking capabilities, the Fairlight CMI was extremely expensive, costing between $25,000 and $36,000, and could only store sound files up to 20 kilobytes in size. Over 300 studios and record labels acquired this cutting-edge technology.
**Akai S1000 (1988)** : Following the early innovations of samplers like the Fairlight CMI, Emulator, and Sinclair Vier, the Japanese company Akai introduced a line of rack-mounted samplers that became an industry standard. The Akai S1000, released in 1988, was particularly notable for its affordability. Its accessibility and capabilities made it a crucial tool for musicians, with figures like Trent Reznor being among the first to fully utilize its potential for their music.

# instrument - Computer

## curiosities
**Apple Macintosh Introduction (January 22, 1984)** : The Apple Macintosh computer was first introduced during Super Bowl 18 on January 22, 1984, with its famous "Big Brother" commercial directed by Ridley Scott. This event marked a significant moment that would deeply impact the music industry. The Mac quickly became a favorite among musicians, especially with the development of software like Pro Tools by DigiDesign, which established the Macintosh as standard equipment in both recording studios and live performance settings. The growing adoption by musicians led Apple and third-party software companies to increasingly cater to the specific needs and demands of the music community. Today, modern recording studios are heavily reliant on computers, hard drives, and various software solutions like Pro Tools for recording and manipulating music, a trend that largely began with the introduction of the Macintosh.

# curiosity

**Ecstasy's Influence on Music (Post-1985)** : In 1985, a new drug called ecstasy (methylene dioxyan, methyl-emphetamine), derived from sassafras tree oil, was introduced into the UK and European club scene. Functioning as a stimulant that also broke down emotional inhibitions, it quickly became the substance of choice in clubs and parties by 1988, even surpassing beer. Its widespread popularity fundamentally altered the nature of music over the next few years. To enhance the "ecstasy experience," musicians and DJs began to focus on creating special beats, mixes, and arrangements for raves – large, often illegal outdoor parties held in fields, warehouses, and abandoned buildings across England. Melody became secondary to rhythm, and the dance floor, along with the DJ's beats, became the central focus of a new youth revolution, profoundly shaping dance music and club culture.

**AIDS Epidemic and Public Awareness (1981-1985)** : In 1981, a new disease, initially referred to as GRID (gay-related immune deficiency), began to surface, with early reports focusing on gay men, hemophiliacs, and Haitian immigrants. By July 1982, the disease was officially named Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndrome (AIDS), and in May 1983, the Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV) was isolated as its cause. The epidemic's scale grew rapidly, with 7,669 AIDS cases reported in the United States by the end of 1984, a number that nearly doubled to 15,948 cases just twelve months later. The public's awareness and understanding of the disease were significantly impacted by the deaths of public figures, notably actor Rock Hudson on October 3, 1985, and just nine days later, Ricky Wilson of The B-52s, who became the first notable musician to die from AIDS.
