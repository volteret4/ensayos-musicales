# artist - The Beatles

## albums

**The White Album (1968) – Self-Titled Double Album** : This 1968 release contains the song "Ob-La-Di, Ob-La-Da," a track noted for its extreme simplicity.

## songs

**Ob-La-Di, Ob-La-Da (1968)** : A ridiculously simple song recorded in 1968 and released on _The White Album_. The composition is set in the key of B-flat major, though it is easily transposable to the key of C.

# artist - Robin Thicke

## songs

**Blurred Lines (2013)** : This song became the center of an infamous copyright trial because it was felt to mimic the "vibe" of Marvin Gaye’s 1977 song "Got to Give It Up". A jury eventually awarded $7.2 million to the Gaye estate based on a "note by note" comparison of the sheet music rather than the recording itself, relying heavily on music theory arguments.

# artist - Marvin Gaye

## songs

**Got to Give It Up (1977)** : A 1977 soul track that served as the foundational evidence in the "Blurred Lines" copyright trial. The Gaye estate successfully argued that Robin Thicke's hit infringed upon the musical building blocks of this composition.

# artist - Led Zeppelin

## songs

**Stairway to Heaven (1971)** : The band faced legal charges alleging this song was lifted from the 1967 instrumental "Taurus" by the band Spirit. While the two tracks sound similar on record, the band successfully defended the song in court by demonstrating that the sheet music showed "very, very stark and pronounced" differences.
**Babe I'm Gonna Leave You (1969)** : A 1967 recording that features a descending riff similar to those found in Chicago’s "25 or 6 to 4" and Green Day’s "Brain Stew". The riff was conceived around the same time George Harrison was writing "While My Guitar Gently Weeps".

# artist - Tom Petty

## songs

**Mary Jane's Last Dance (1993)** : A 1993 release noted for its similarity to the Red Hot Chili Peppers' later hit "Dani California". Petty declined to sue, stating that many rock songs sound alike and he doubted there was negative intent.
**American Girl (1976)** : A song from which The Strokes admitted to borrowing elements for their hit "Last Nite".
**I Won't Back Down (1989)** : A 1989 hit that shares significant melodic similarities with Sam Smith’s 2014 song "Stay With Me". This led to an out-of-court settlement where Petty was awarded songwriting royalties.

# artist - Oasis

## members

## albums

**Definitely Maybe (1994) – Debut Album** : The band's debut featuring "Shaker Maker".
**(What's the Story) Morning Glory? (1995)** : This album originally featured the song "Step Out," which was later removed from subsequent editions due to copyright issues.

## songs

**Shaker Maker (1994)** : A song whose melody was found to infringe on "I'd Like to Teach the World to Sing". Guitarist Bonehead admitted the band "ripped it off," and the group eventually paid damages, leading Liam Gallagher to jokingly sing the lyrics of the original Coca-Cola commercial during live sets.
**Step Out (1995)** : This track bore such a strong resemblance to Stevie Wonder's "Uptight (Everything's All Right)" that Wonder successfully sued the band. As a result, the song was scrubbed from future album pressings, and Noel Gallagher’s songwriting credit was reduced from 100% to 25%.
**Whatever (1994)** : A 1994 release that borrowed from Monty Python’s "How Sweet to Be an Idiot," resulting in a co-writing credit for Neil Innes.

## curiosities

**Rehearsal Room Criticism** : While working on their debut in a Manchester communal rehearsal space, another band posted a sign on Oasis’s door that read "Get Your Own Riffs".

# curiosity

**The 12-Note Scale Limitation** : Western music is built on a finite scale of 12 notes related by semitone intervals. There are an estimated 479 billion combinations of these notes (or over one quintillion when considering duration), but only a small fraction sound pleasing to the ear, making duplication in songwriting inevitable.
**Non-Copyrightable Musical Elements** : Under current legal standards, song titles, rhythms (such as the Bo Diddley beat), chord progressions, and guitar riffs are generally not protected by copyright.
**The "Earworm" and "Mondegreen"** : An "earworm" is a song clip that loops repeatedly in the mind, while a "mondegreen" is a misheard lyric, such as mishearing Jimi Hendrix's "kiss the sky" as "kiss this guy".
**The Anonymous 1985 Settlement** : A songwriter co-wrote a top 20 hit in 1985 and later noticed a European superstar band used similar melodies in a new song. An out-of-court deal awarded the original writer royalties for a song they didn't write, which eventually went to number one in America and sold 8 million copies.
