# artist - Alexander Graham Bell

## instruments

Microphone
Speaker

## curiosities

**Primitive Robot Contraption (c. 1859)** : At age 12, Bell built a device featuring a head, lips, and mouth operated by bellows. This early experiment was capable of "speaking" and demonstrated his foundational interest in synthesizing human speech.
**Mother's Deteriorating Hearing** : A personal tragedy that served as the primary motivation for Bell to develop "visible speech." This was intended as a method for the deaf to communicate verbally.
**Truve the Speaking Terrier** : Bell experimented with a stray sky terrier named Truve, teaching the dog to "speak" human-like sounds such as "Mama." This research into sound transmission was a precursor to his methods for teaching deaf children.
**Mohawk Language Translation** : Bell translated the unwritten Mohawk language of the Six Nations Reserve into his visible speech symbols. For this work, he was named an honorary chief, and the experience deepened his understanding of how sound could be visually represented and converted into electrical signals.
**Invention of the Telephone (1876)** : Completed on March 10, 1876, Bell's device transformed sound vibrations into electrical signals and back again. This became the foundation for the first practical working telephone and the modern microphone/speaker relationship.
**Telephone System Flaws** : Bell's initial telephone design was impractical for widespread networks because it required two phones per person (one for outgoing and one for incoming calls) and dedicated wires between every desired caller.

# artist - Bing Crosby

## genres

Crooner

## instruments

Magnetophone
Ampex Magnetic Tape

## curiosities

**Intimate Vocal Delivery** : Like Sinatra, Crosby utilized microphone technology to move away from the "big lungs" requirement of the acoustic era. He sang intimately and close to the microphone, creating a sense of romance and emotion previously impossible to capture.
**Involvement with the Magnetophone (1947)** : Crosby’s musical director, Merton McKenzie, introduced him to Jack Mullen’s demonstration of the Nazi-invented magnetophone in 1947. Crosby was immediately interested in the technology's potential for radio.
**Desire for Golf Time** : As a major radio star, Crosby hated performing two live shows daily to cover both East and West Coast time zones. He realized the magnetophone would allow him to record a show once and then go play golf while technicians broadcast the tape later.
**Investment in Ampex** : Crosby hired Jack Mullen as his chief engineer and invested $50,000 (valued at over half a million dollars today) into Ampex. This investment helped Ampex set the global standard for audio and video tape recording.

# artist - Jack Mullen

## instruments

Magnetophone

## curiosities

**Discovery of the Magnetophone** : A member of the US Army Signal Corps, Mullen discovered the Nazi "magnetophone" near Frankfurt, Germany. The machine used reels of magnetic tape to provide high-quality audio storage and playback, explaining how Hitler had broadcast synchronized speeches across different cities.
**Post-War Technology Transfer** : Due to the cancellation of German wartime patents, Mullen was able to bring two magnetophone machines and several reels of magnetic tape back to the United States.

# artist - Lee DeForest

## instruments

Triode Vacuum Tube
Audio Amplifier

## curiosities

**Accidental Gas Flame Observation** : While conducting an experiment, DeForest mistakenly believed he saw a gas flame shiver in response to electromagnetic signals. Although his conclusion was scientifically incorrect, this observation inspired him to create the triode vacuum tube.
**Invention of the Triode Vacuum Tube (1912)** : Working by 1912, this tube could amplify any electrical signal, including sound from a microphone. It is the foundational technology for all modern audio amplification.
**First Audio Amplifier Prototype (1914)** : DeForest’s work led to the first prototype amplifier, which was quickly adopted for use in telephones, radio, movies, and public address systems.

# artist - Hildegard von Bingen

## genres

Sacred Monophony
Liturgical Chanting

## albums

**Ordo Virtutum (c. 1156)** : Her most famous musical work, described as beautiful and haunting, which showcased her innovative approach to composition in the 12th century.

## curiosities

**Mother of Science** : Born in 1098 in Germany, von Bingen was a nun, mystic, and visionary often referred to as the "mother of science" in her home country.
**Invention of Sacred Monophony** : She essentially invented the specific sound of liturgical chanting that became the standard in Catholic churches and monasteries from the 12th century onward.
**Architectural Influence on Composition** : Von Bingen’s slow, somber, and unadorned chanting style was a direct response to the acoustics of large stone Gothic cathedrals. Rapid chord changes or beats would become an "unlistenable mess" due to the heavy reverb, so her music was designed to let the echo enhance the sound.

# artist - Nathaniel Baldwin

## instruments

Headphones

## curiosities

**Invention of Early Headphones** : A Mormon fundamentalist, Baldwin invented an improved telephone receiver that functioned as the first set of headphones. He personally assembled early orders at his kitchen table.
**US Navy Adoption** : The US Navy adopted Baldwin’s invention for clearer radio signals, which provided the funding to start his business.
**Baldwin Radio Company** : Baldwin’s company employed other polygamist Mormons, and the profits were used to support a fundamentalist Church offshoot.
**Lack of Patent** : Baldwin notably never patented his headphone invention, which allowed the technology to be developed freely by other inventors and companies later on.
**Financial Ruin** : Despite the success of his technology, Baldwin went bankrupt in 1924 and was later hit with a fraud conviction.

# artist - John Koss

## instruments

Koss SP3s
Portable Record Player

## curiosities

**JC Koss Television Rental Company** : Koss’s initial success came from renting televisions to patients in hospitals.
**Portable Record Player Privacy Switch** : In the 1950s, Koss developed one of the first truly portable record players. It featured a "privacy switch" that allowed users to route audio to headphones instead of the internal speaker.
**Koss SP3s (1958)** : Recognizing a market for private listening, Koss introduced these as the first standalone consumer-grade headphones. They became an immediate sensation and sparked the widespread public adoption of headphones for music.

# artist - George Coy

## instruments

Quarter-inch Phone Plug

## curiosities

**Central Telephone Exchange Invention** : Coy solved the problem of early telephone connectivity by proposing a central exchange where operators manually connected calls using physical plugs and wires.
**Quarter-inch Phone Plug (1878)** : Coy developed a durable, quick-connect plug (patented in 1902) that became the universal standard for connecting guitars, microphones, and headphones. He reportedly experimented with women's bra wires during the design process.

# instrument - Compact Disc (CD)

## curiosities

**Market Introduction (1982)** : Introduced in late 1982, the CD arrived when vinyl quality was at its lowest due to the oil crisis.
**Rapid Adoption** : The superior audio quality of the CD compared to thin, recycled vinyl prompted a swift public transition in the late 1980s, leading many consumers to discard their entire vinyl collections.

# curiosity

**Windows 95 as Music Catalyst** : Released August 15, 1995, Windows 95 required 60 megabytes of space. Bill Gates chose to distribute it on CD-ROM because of its size, which forced the widespread adoption of CD-ROM drives in PCs. Users soon realized these drives could "rip" digital data from music CDs to hard drives.
**WinAMP (1997)** : Released as a free download on April 12, 1997, this software made it easy for mainstream users to convert CD tracks into MP3 files on their computers.
**Napster (1999)** : Launched June 1, 1999, Napster catalyzed the illegal file-sharing revolution, causing a massive decline in CD sales and nearly collapsing the traditional music industry.
**iTunes Music Store** : Steve Jobs' solution to the file-sharing crisis captured 70% of the global market for legal digital downloads.
**The 1973-74 Oil Crisis and Vinyl Quality** : Soaring petrochemical prices led record labels to use recycled vinyl and make records thinner. This resulted in "rumble, hiss, clicks, and pops," shallower grooves with less musical information, and records that warped easily. By the late 70s, a new record was often sonically inferior to a used one from the early 70s.
**Origin of "Stick a Sock in it"** : Early gramophones had no volume control. To quiet the music, listeners would literally stuff a ball of fabric or a sock into the acoustic horn.
**3.5mm Stereo Connector** : This common modern headphone jack is a direct descendant of George Coy’s 1878 quarter-inch phone plug design.
