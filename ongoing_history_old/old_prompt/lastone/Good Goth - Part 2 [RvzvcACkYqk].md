# genre - Goth Rock

## curiosities
**Origin (1970s)** : Began as a spin-off from British punk in the 1970s.
**Rise to Prominence (Early 1980s)** : Became "quite the rage" by the early 1980s.
**Enduring Sub-Culture (N/A)** : Unlike many rock-based sub-cultures, it did not wither and die but spread and grew stronger, extending its influence beyond music into film, fashion, television, literature, and general pop culture.
**Misunderstanding (N/A)** : Can be very misunderstood, often perceived as scary and intimidating to the uninitiated due to its look and sound.
**Worldwide Presence (N/A)** : One of the strongest, most solid, and most widespread rock sub-cultures, found globally in places like Los Angeles, Australia, Spain, Italy, France, Belgium, Scandinavia, Russia, Japan, and New Zealand.
**Sub-Types and Terminology (N/A)** : Includes traditional Goths, neo-Goths, mall Goths, mini-Goths, and baby bats (teenagers too young for Goth clubs). Other regional terms include "death rock" (Los Angeles), "Gogan" (Australia), "Cucuracha" (Spain), and "dark" (Italy). "Spooky kid" is used for followers of Marilyn Manson.
**First Generation (Late 1970s-1985)** : The period from the late 1970s to about 1985 is identified as First Generation Goth.
**Second Generation (Latter Half of 1980s)** : The latter half of the 1980s saw the emergence of Second Generation Goth.
**Formalization of Culture (N/A)** : Aspects of Goth culture began to be formalized through the spread of Goth-themed magazines.
**Cleavage and Stratification (N/A)** : Goth itself began to cleave and stratify, adapting to different musical, cultural, and fashion environments.
**Influence on Post-New Wave Techno Pop (N/A)** : Infected a number of post-new wave techno pop bands, turning them from "happy, bouncy, and lightweight" to "dark and powerful and even sinister."
**Cross-Pollination with Industrial Music (N/A)** : The next stage of Goth's development came from cross-pollination with industrial music.
**Beginner's Guide (N/A)** : Considered one of the longest surviving alternative rock subcultures in the world, often requiring a "beginner's guide" for understanding.
**Softer and Slower Sound (N/A)** : Compared to industrial music, Goth can be "softer and slower" and "introspective."
**Keyboard-Based Approach (N/A)** : Has a keyboard-based approach more akin to early 1980s techno pop and smoother modern electronic music than hard techno, making it "a little more danceable."
**Expansion in the 1990s (Latter Half of 1990s)** : Its influence continued to expand, leading to more specific niches, with many bands remaining underground.
**Goth-Tinged Genres (N/A)** : Industrial music and some emo-ish bands fall into this category, as do certain metal scenes (death metal, doom metal, black metal).
**Death Goths (N/A)** : Purists who are faithful to the original musical, fashion, and lifestyle aesthetics created at the end of the 1970s. For them, guitar is a very important instrument.
**Least Understood (N/A)** : One of the least understood post-punk genres by the general public and mass media.
**Mischaracterization in Media (N/A)** : Often gets widespread attention only in negative contexts, such as school shootings, leading to misperceptions of Goth people as death-obsessed or violent.
**Reality of Goth Culture (N/A)** : The vast majority of people associated with Goth culture are described as "gentle, literate people" who enjoy hanging out with like-minded individuals.
**Fashion Spread (Decades)** : Goth fashion has spread throughout pop culture, influencing movies, TV, mainstream fashion, literature, and art for decades, showing no signs of going away.
