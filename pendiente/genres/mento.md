# genre - Mento

## curiosities
**Live Performance Focus (Early Period)** : Mento was primarily a music genre intended for live performance, especially in its early stages. This was largely due to the scarcity of recording facilities in Jamaica during that time. "Mentor was primarily a music for live performance, at least at first." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**Marketing as Calypso** : Mento groups often marketed their music as "Calypso" by incorporating the term into their names or song titles, primarily to appeal to wider audiences unfamiliar with the subtle distinctions between the two genres. "Musicians would also call their music Collipso in order to tell it to people like me who don't know the difference." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**Primary Jamaican Genre (1950s)** : In the 1950s, Mento was the primary musical genre produced by the Jamaican population, preceding the emergence of Ska. "The music that the Jamaican population made was mostly a genre called Mentor." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**Reggae Influence** : Mento, described as Jamaican folk or country music, served as one of the foundational musical styles that contributed to the birth and development of reggae. "So if you take some Jamaican Mento, which is kind of like their folk or country music, mix in some dancehall swing, like Benny Goodman or Glenn Miller, throw in a heavy dose of American rhythm and blues or early rock, like that's domino." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 29 - Every Little Thing is Gonna Be All Right - The Influence of 70s Reggae | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-29-every-little-thing-is-gonna-be-all-right-the-influence-of-70s-reggae
**Rhythm in Ska Development** : Edward Seaga's innovation in Ska's development involved incorporating a Mento rhythm into an R&B record by having the guitarist stress the off-beats, a common characteristic of Mento where the banjo would typically emphasize these beats. "Siaga had the idea to incorporate a little bit of a mental rhythm into an R&B record he was producing... in mental, you'd often have a banjo stress the off beats." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**Rural Mento Characteristics** : Rural Mento was characterized by instruments such as the Banjo and a homemade bass instrument called a Rumba box. It bore a resemblance to American country music or British Skiffle, and is sometimes still referred to as country music in Jamaica. "Rural Mentor, was based around instruments like the Banjo, and a homemade bass instrument called a Rumba box, and had a resemblance to a lot of American country music or British Skiffle." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**Urban Mento Characteristics** : Urban Mento differed from its rural counterpart by eschewing acoustic and homemade instruments in favor of those typically used by R&B or Jazz bands, such as guitars and basses. It is said to be distinct from Calypso, though the difference is often described only by geographic origin. "Urban Mentor, which dropped the acoustic and homemade instruments, and replaced them with the same sort of instruments that R&B or Jazz Bans used." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
