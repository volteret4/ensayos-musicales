**Definition and Scope** : Heartland Rock is a subgenre of rock music that emerged from the political and cultural environment bridging the 1970s and early 1980s. While the American Heartland is geographically defined as about 12 Midwest states with rural areas, cities like Chicago, and many small towns, Heartland Rock itself is not regional but rather a culture. "Defining Heartland Rock, well, there are a lot of people who will not even acknowledge that that's a thing." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 55 - Heartland Rock is about More than the Heartland | https://www.ftr70.com/

This perspective highlights its connection to white male working-class culture, though the podcast notes the problematic exclusion of African Americans from rock predated this era. **Musical Characteristics** : Characterized as a storytelling genre, similar to country music, Heartland Rock's lyrics often draw from folk and country music, while its backbeat stems from rhythm and blues and rock. John Fornara, chief pop music critic for The New York Times, described it as "neo-traditional," "self conscious about seeking roots," and not a "local homegrown style but one that wishes it were," with a "scrappy but respectable" sound and attitude from the 1980s. **Optimism of the Downtrodden** : A core characteristic of Heartland Rock is its consistent celebration of optimism, particularly as experienced and expressed by individuals who are downtrodden or facing adversity. **Political Appeal** : The themes of weariness, escape, and universal struggles in Heartland Rock, while not exclusively politically conservative, often resonated with and were gravitated towards by conservative fans. This was particularly true in the 1980s, a period of conservative politics, with figures like Ronald Reagan often trying to appropriate these songs for political benefit, even when their messages did not align. **Themes and Messaging** : The genre's culture is characterized by themes of common men, blue-collar workers, and the working class, focusing on struggles, a longing for better days, and hope in times of despair. Despite its universal appeal, the messaging was often claimed by a smaller, politically conservative segment of society, even though most Heartland rockers held left-liberal beliefs. "Heartland Rock is very much about working class America, which is surely not the exclusive domain of white men.
