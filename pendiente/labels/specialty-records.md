# label - Specialty Records

## curiosities
**Discovery of Little Richard** : Specialty Records signed Little Richard after A&R head Bumps Blackwell, looking for a gospel-sounding R&B singer to compete with Ray Charles, heard Richard's demo tape. They bought Richard's contract from Peacock Records for $600. "Blackwell and Roup made an agreement with Don Roby to buy out his contract for $600." ← Episode 34： ＂Tutti Frutti＂ by Little Richard | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7mk9SW46Li4
**Early Signings of Black Artists** : Specialty Records was among the pioneering labels to sign and record black gospel acts like The Soul Stirrers in the late 1940s. "In the late 40s The Soul Sturlers signed to specialty records, one of the first acts to sign to the label" ← Episode Sixty： ＂You Send Me＂ by Sam Cooke | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=92tD5qJR-yY
**Half-Rate Mechanical Licensing** : Specialty Records, owned by Art Rupe, licensed songs like "Tutti Frutti" from a publishing company also owned by Art Rupe. This cross-collateralization meant Specialty only paid half the normal mechanical licensing rate (one cent per record instead of two cents), a common but frustrating practice for artists like Little Richard. "That company licensed the song to specialty records, for half the normal mechanical licensing rate." ← Episode 34： ＂Tutti Frutti＂ by Little Richard | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7mk9SW46Li4
**Jesse Belvin's First Solo Contract** : Jesse Belvin signed with Specialty Records for his first foray into a solo career after recording two singles with Big Jay McNeely's group, Three Dots and a Dash. His initial solo single, "Baby Don't Go," was not particularly successful on this label, leading to his subsequent collaboration with Marvin Phillips. "After recording two singles with McNally, Belvin went off to make his own records, signing to specialty records." ← Episode 47： ＂Goodnight My Love＂ by Jesse Belvin | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Eoyk3H0M6r8
**Royalty Practices** : Specialty Records, under Art Rupe, had a policy that artists should ideally receive no more than one cent per record sold, reflecting Rupe's aversion to what he perceived as "greed" from artists demanding higher royalties. "Why should he pay the artist more than one cent per record sold?" ← Episode Sixty： ＂You Send Me＂ by Sam Cooke | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=92tD5qJR-yY
**Shift from Gospel to Secular Music** : The label, initially known for its gospel releases, began refocusing towards more secular styles of black music after Lloyd Price's "Lordy Miss Claude" became its biggest hit, recognizing the greater commercial potential. "maybe he should refocus the label away from gospel and towards more secular styles of music." ← Episode Sixty： ＂You Send Me＂ by Sam Cooke | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=92tD5qJR-yY
**Sonny Bono's Early Career** : Sonny Bono worked as a young songwriter for Specialty Records, writing songs for artists such as Sam Cooke, Larry Williams, and Dillon Di. "Sonny Barrow was a young songwriter who had written songs its specialty records for people like Sam Cook, Larry Williams and Dolan Jui." ← Episode 113： ＂Needles and Pins＂ by The Searchers | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=izC9ZBI0UHM
**Source of R&B Influence** : This label was known for recording artists such as Little Richard and Larry Williams, whose R&B music significantly influenced Richie Valens' early musical interests. "Valens' big interest in school was music, especially R&B, and especially the music of Little Richard and Larry Williams, and other people who had recorded for specialty records." ← Episode 73： ＂La Bamba＂ by Ritchie Valens | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1v9iSCWnLsc
