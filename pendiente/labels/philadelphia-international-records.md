**Black-Owned Hit Factory** : Philadelphia International Records (PIR) was a pioneering Black-owned record company established by Kenny Gamble and Leon Huff, recognized as the undisputed leader in Philly Soul hits and a "hit factory" often compared to Motown. "The leader of Philly Soul was the Black owned record company, Philadelphia International Records." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 37 - Philly Soul and the Sound of the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-37-philly-soul-and-the-sound-of-the-70s
**CBS Records Deal** : Clive Davis, then president of CBS Records, offered Kenny Gamble and Leon Huff a deal to create PIR, motivated by his desire for CBS to establish a major presence in the "Black Music Market," acknowledging a gap in their roster. "Clive Davis, the president of CBS Records, offered Gamble and Huff a deal that allows them to create Philadelphia international records." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 37 - Philly Soul and the Sound of the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-37-philly-soul-and-the-sound-of-the-70s
**Conflict Over Political Focus (Late 70s)** : By the late 1970s, some writers, including McFadden and Whitehead, felt that PIR, particularly under Kenny Gamble's influence, had become overly focused on a political perspective emphasizing "black unity through music," which they believed alienated white audiences and resulted in decreased record sales. "It had grown to view from their perspective and present so much from a political point of view, which made them less popular with the white audience, going for quote, black unity through music, meant fewer record sales." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 37 - Philly Soul and the Sound of the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-37-philly-soul-and-the-sound-of-the-70s
**Emphasis on Social Statements** : Kenny Gamble and Leon Huff explicitly intended for PIR's music to incorporate social statements, believing that the act of songwriting inherently involved imparting "part of your soul," a principle they applied to both love songs and tracks addressing contemporary real-world issues. "gamble and huff were clear that they wanted to also make a social statement." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 37 - Philly Soul and the Sound of the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-37-philly-soul-and-the-sound-of-the-70s
**Home of MFSB and Disco Innovation** : In 1973, Philadelphia International Records was the base for MFSB, its house band. It was during this time, while working as a session drummer for MFSB, that Earl Young developed the "four on the floor" disco beat, an innovation that would profoundly influence late 70s music. "While working as a session drummer for MFSB, the house band at Philadelphia International Records in 1973, early young had an idea for kind of jazzing up the love I lost by Harold Melvin in the blue notes." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 50 - The Travolta Trilogy | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Independent Label Challenges** : PIR's remarkable success was particularly notable given the inherent difficulties faced by independent record labels to survive and flourish, especially in a 1970 market where half of the pop music was controlled by just five labels and the vast majority of singles and albums failed to make a profit. "It was very difficult for an independent record label to survive let alone thrive." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 37 - Philly Soul and the Sound of the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-37-philly-soul-and-the-sound-of-the-70s
**Shift in Message Music Strategy** : As PIR solidified its reputation as an established "hit factory," it began to release fewer songs that were overtly message-driven, such as "The Love I Lost" or "When Will I See You Again," suggesting a possible evolution in its thematic focus or a more subtle integration of social commentary. "Nevertheless, as PIR became an established hit factory, they released fewer songs like The Love I Lost or When Will I See You Again." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 37 - Philly Soul and the Sound of the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-37-philly-soul-and-the-sound-of-the-70s
**Significant Black-Owned Business** : Philadelphia International Records evolved into one of the five largest Black-owned businesses in America, a remarkable achievement that occurred within the complex social and political landscape of the 1970s, following the civil rights legislation of the 1960s.
