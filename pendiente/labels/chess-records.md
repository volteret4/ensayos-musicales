# label - Chess Records

## curiosities
**Chicago Blues Audience Preference** : Chess Records' traditional Chicago blues fans did not take to Billy Stewart's music because it was perceived as "too close to soul music," highlighting a clear preference for a specific blues sound within their audience. "too close to soul music for the chess Chicago blues fans to take him to heart." ← Episode 49： ＂Love is Strange＂ by Mickey and Sylvia | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=FkyLPVvPbcI
**Competition with Major Labels through Payola** : Chess Records was one of the smaller labels that utilized payola—the practice of paying DJs to play their records—as a means to level the playing field against the major labels. These smaller labels saw payola as a way to get their records heard, countering the inherent advantage of larger companies. "the small labels that built rock and roll, labels like Sun or Chess or Atlantic, all saw it as a way that they could equalise things a little bit." ← Episode 67： ＂Johnny B. Goode＂, by Chuck Berry | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sHlA-qihuNw
**Distribution for Harvey Fuqua's Brother-in-Law** : Chess Records distributed a small number of records for a Detroit label owned by Harvey Fuqua's brother-in-law, a label that eventually went bankrupt despite producing co-written hits for Jackie Wilson. "Chess also distributed a handful of records from a small Detroit label owned by Harvey Foucault's brother-in-law, who co-wrote several hits for Jackie Wilson before that label went bankrupt." ← Episode 93： ＂Please Mr. Postman＂ by the Marvelettes | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EPg8Wx30-uM
**Influence on Chuck Berry's Piano Style** : Leonard Chess, of Chess Records, played a pivotal role in shaping the piano style on Chuck Berry's records. Convinced of the effectiveness of Jerry Lee Lewis's "ripping" technique, he insisted that Berry's pianist, Johnny Johnson, adopt this flashier style, even demonstrating it himself during rehearsals. "Chess insisted to the extent that when they were rehearsing the tracks, Chess would work over and rip his hand down the keys himself to show Johnson what he wanted." ← Episode 67： ＂Johnny B. Goode＂, by Chuck Berry | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sHlA-qihuNw
**Interest in Songwriters, Not Groups** : Chess Records was persuaded by Billy Davis to sign The Four Tops, but their primary interest lay in Davis as a songwriter, subsequently using his material for other groups like The Moon Glows and The Flamingos. "Chess hadn't really been interested in the four tops themselves. They'd instead been interested in Billy Davis as a songwriter." ← Episode Sixty-Four： ＂Reet Petite＂ by Jackie Wilson | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=BYU-H4NvnmE
**Maurice White's Session Work** : Maurice White started his career as a session musician, playing drums on countless recordings for Chess Records before founding Earth Wind & Fire. "Maurice White started as a session musician, playing drums on countless recordings for chess records." ← Hit Parade Music History and Music Trivia > Give Up the Funk Edition Part 2 | https://shows.acast.com/hit-parade/episodes/695d4f2ad1ba84fb8f045799
**Potential Motown Footnote** : It is suggested that Motown might have been reduced to a mere "footnote" in the history of Chess Records if "Please Mr. Postman" had not become a hit, highlighting the precarious early financial situation of the label. "If it hadn't come along, it's possible that Motown would just be a footnote in histories of chess records." ← Episode 93： ＂Please Mr. Postman＂ by the Marvelettes | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EPg8Wx30-uM
**Preference for Black Artists** : Chess Records primarily released tracks by black artists, a factor that influenced Stan Lewis's strategy to pitch Dale Hawkins, a white artist, to their subsidiary Checker, believing Checker might be open to a white act if it was an "answer record" to a previous successful white release. "While chess records put out almost solely tracks by black artists" ← Episode 63： ＂Susie Q＂, by Dale Hawkins | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4vqj1DcXOfw
**Rejection of Jackie Wilson's Sound** : Chess Records showed no interest in signing Jackie Wilson, preferring a "harder blues sound" over Wilson's more soulful style. "Davis tried to get Wilson signed to chess records, but they weren't interested in Wilson's sound. They wanted a harder blues sound, rather than Wilson's more soulful sound." ← Episode Sixty-Four： ＂Reet Petite＂ by Jackie Wilson | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=BYU-H4NvnmE
**Stan Lewis's Influence and Talent Scouting** : Stan Lewis, owner of Stan's Record Shop, served as a talent scout for Chess Records and was one of its largest customers. Chess highly valued Lewis's endorsement, knowing that any record he backed would sell well, particularly in Louisiana. Lewis also operated as a major record distributor across the entire region. "Stan Lewis was, among other things, both a talent scout for chess records and one of the biggest customers of the label." ← Episode 63： ＂Susie Q＂, by Dale Hawkins | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4vqj1DcXOfw
