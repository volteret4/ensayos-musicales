## curiosities
**Move to Atlantic Records (Early 1970s)** : Dissatisfied with their progress at Motown, The Spinners decided to leave for Atlantic Records, viewing it as a suitable label given its success with artists like Aretha Franklin. This move, combined with Philippe Wynne joining the group (replacing G.C. Cameron, who remained at Motown) and their collaboration with producer Thom Bell, enabled them to become "the perfect vehicle for that Philly sound," characterized by gorgeous falsetto, lush orchestration, and a smoothly polished sound. "So they left for Atlantic records because they were thinking, look, if Atlantic records is good enough for Arita Franklin, it's good enough for us." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 33 - Zaire '74, Muhammad Ali, and The Rumble in the Jungle | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-33-zaire-74-muhammad-ali-and-the-rumble-in-the-jungle
**Poor Treatment at Motown** : The Spinners experienced unfavorable conditions at Motown, where they received significantly less attention compared to groups like The Temptations and The Four Tops and were even assigned duties as chauffeurs for other artists. "The spinners, they'd been done wrong by Motown, who gave a lot more attention to the temptations and to the four tops." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 37 - Philly Soul and the Sound of the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-37-philly-soul-and-the-sound-of-the-70s
**Shared Appearances on American Bandstand and Soul Train (1976-1977)** : The Spinners were among several notable artists, including The Undisputed Truth, KC and the Sunshine Band, and Donna Summer, who appeared on both American Bandstand and Soul Train during 1976 and 1977. This demonstrated the capacity for both shows to coexist and feature popular musical talent. "the undispeed of truth is on both shows. **Tom Bell's Impact** : Tom Bell played a transformative role in The Spinners' career after their departure from Motown, producing a string of hits at Atlantic Records that elevated them to stardom. **Tom Bell's Wager** : To persuade The Spinners to allow him to produce their next record, Tom Bell offered a wager: he would pay each member $1,000 if he failed to deliver a number one hit, but if successful, they would owe him a custom Cadillac. He achieved a number one hit with "I'll Be Around" but graciously forewent collecting the Cadillac, humorously noting he couldn't drive anyway. "If I do not get you a number one record, I will give each one of you $1,000. If I do get you a number one record, you owe me a custom Cadillac." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 37 - Philly Soul and the Sound of the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-37-philly-soul-and-the-sound-of-the-70s
