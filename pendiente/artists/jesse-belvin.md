## albums
**Just Jesse Belvin (1959)** : This was Jesse Belvin's first album, recorded for RCA, a major label he signed with to pursue a career as a smooth ballad singer, similar to Nat King Cole. The album's lead single was "Guess Who," which earned Grammy nominations for Best R&B Performance and Best Male Vocal Performance. Belvin recorded this album as part of a strategic career move managed by his wife, Joanne. **Mr. Easy (1960)** : Jesse Belvin's second album, which was recorded just before his death and ended up being a posthumous release. This album was intended to further consolidate his shift towards pop balladry in the style of Nat King Cole, moving him towards mainstream pop stardom. Its release after his passing cemented its place as a poignant part of his legacy. ## curiosities
**Abandoning The Cliques' Success** : After forming The Cliques with Eugene Church, their song "The Girl in My Dreams" reached number 45 on the Pop Chart, a clear commercial success. However, Belvin made the decision to abandon the group name and pursue a solo career rather than capitalize on or follow up on this minor hit. "And so of course, at this point, Belvin ditched the Cliques name, rather than follow up on the minor hit, and started making records as a solo artist instead." ← Episode 47： ＂Goodnight My Love＂ by Jesse Belvin | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Eoyk3H0M6r8
**Advice on Royalties** : Jesse Belvin taught Richard Berry that musicians in the 1950s rarely received royalties from recording contracts, regardless of their terms, influencing Berry's strategy to prioritize session fees. "he'd learned from Jesse Bellvin that it didn't matter what the contract said, you were never going to get any royalties when you made records." ← Episode 106：＂Louie Louie＂ by the Kingsmen | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=S3agGJsxjpE
**Cavalier Attitude Towards Songwriting Credit** : Jesse Belvin frequently sold his songwriting rights for as little as $100, often resulting in him not receiving label credit for his compositions. This "cavalier attitude" is exemplified by the alleged sale of the bridge he wrote for "Good Night My Love" to John Mariscalco for $400, reflecting his focus on immediate cash over long-term royalties. "Most of the time, he would also be writing the songs, but he didn't get label credits on most of them, because he would just sell all his rights to the songs for $100." ← Episode 47： ＂Goodnight My Love＂ by Jesse Belvin | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Eoyk3H0M6r8

**Tragic Death in Car Crash (February 6, 1960)** : Jesse Belvin died instantly in a car crash on February 6, 1960, shortly after performing at a concert in Little Rock, Arkansas. His wife, Joanne, who he reportedly shielded from the worst of the impact, was seriously injured and succumbed to her injuries a few days later. The circumstances of their deaths are shrouded in rumor and lack of official investigation due to institutional racism at the time. "On February the 6th, 1960, there was a concert in Little Rock, Arkansas at the Robinson Auditorium." ← Episode 47： ＂Goodnight My Love＂ by Jesse Belvin | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Eoyk3H0M6r8
**Uninvestigated Death and Sabotage Rumors** : Following the fatal car crash that killed Jesse and Joanne Belvin, no thorough investigation was conducted due to institutional racism prevalent in the Deep South at the time. Rumors quickly circulated, including newspaper reports of slashed tires on Belvin's car, unconfirmed death threats against Belvin, and car troubles experienced by Jackie Wilson and other musicians on the same night, leading to speculation of sabotage or racist murder.
