## songs
**It's My Party (1963)** : Leslie Gore's debut single, produced by Quincy Jones. As a "one woman girl group," Gore delivered the sass and melodrama reminiscent of the Shangri-Las in this tale of a teen who finds her boy Johnny with a "hussey named Judy" at her own celebration. Gore, then a junior in high school, liked the lyric, and Jones threw all his musical savvy at the recording, hiring Jazz Arranger Klaus Ogerman to conduct the backing track and speckling it with "spunky horns." Upon its release in the spring of 1963, it hurtled from number 60 on the Hot 100 to number one in just four weeks, marking Quincy Jones's first number one hit as a producer. Its lyrics detail Gore's party girl character getting her revenge by luring back Johnny, and it peaked at number five on the charts in the summer of 1963. **You Don't Own Me (1964)** : Considered by critics and Leslie Gore herself as her signature hit, this proto-feminist anthem was brought to Quincy Jones by Gore. Jones provided a stately arrangement and a major promotional push, convinced it would return Gore to number one. It peaked at number two in the winter of 1964, behind The Beatles' number one smash "I Want to Hold Your Hand," and was Gore's final top ten hit. ## curiosities
**Feminist and LGBTQ Icon** : Decades after her hits, Leslie Gore not only became a feminist icon due to songs like "You Don't Own Me," but also an LGBTQ advocate after she publicly came out. She spoke highly of her producer, Quincy Jones, in an interview with Rolling Stone. "Decades later, Gore, who not only became a feminist icon but an LGBTQ advocate after she came out, told Rolling Stone, quote, Quincy was a great mentor and a wonderful teacher. **Quincy Jones's Discovery** : Quincy Jones personally signed Leslie Gore (birth name Leslie Sue Goldstein), a white Jewish teenager from Tenafly, New Jersey, to Mercury Records. Jones noted her "mellow distinctive voice" and her ability to "sing in tune," a quality he found lacking in many adult rock and roll singers of the time. "Of this discovery, a white Jewish teenager from Tenefly, New Jersey, Jones later wrote, quote, she had a mellow distinctive voice and sang in tune, which a lot of grown-up rock and roll singers couldn't do.
