## curiosities
**"Charlie Brown" Extended Writing Process** : Unlike the rapid creation of "Yakati Yak," the song "Charlie Brown" took significantly longer to develop. While Mike Stoller quickly came up with appropriate music, Jerry Lieber spent weeks agonizing before he found the title and conceived the basic idea for the lyrics, with Stoller assisting in the final refinement. "Charlie Brown took significantly longer to come up with than the ten minute that Yakite had taken." ← Episode 68： ＂Yakety Yak＂ by the Coasters | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dtMHDD0-oOo
**"Yakati Yak" Writing Process** : The song "Yakati Yak" was famously written by Lieber and Stoller in about 10 minutes during a session at Lieber's house. Stoller was playing a piano rhythm that he thought might suit The Coasters when Lieber, from the kitchen, called out the first line: "take out the papers and the trash." Stoller immediately responded with "or you don't get no spending cash," and they rapidly traded lines to complete the song. "They traded off lines and had the song written in about 10 minutes." ← Episode 68： ＂Yakety Yak＂ by the Coasters | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dtMHDD0-oOo
**Classic Songwriting Split** : Jerry Lieber and Mike Stoller embodied a classic songwriting split: Lieber was primarily a lyricist who could not play an instrument but composed tunes in his head, while Stoller was an accomplished and sophisticated jazz pianist responsible for the music. Though they wrote together, they occasionally contributed ideas to each other's domain. "Lieber had started out as a songwriter who couldn't play an instrument or like music. He'd just written the mix down and remembered the tune in his head. While Stoller was already an accomplished and sophisticated jazz pianist by the time the two started collaborating." ← Episode 68： ＂Yakety Yak＂ by the Coasters | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dtMHDD0-oOo
**Criticism Over Racial Portrayals** : Lieber and Stoller faced criticism, notably from Johnny Otis, that their songs for The Coasters perpetuated racist stereotypes. Otis suggested they "dwelled entirely on a sort of street society," which, while perhaps reflecting a slice of life, wasn't typical of the black community. The controversy was heightened by their direction of black performers on songs written by white writers, drawing an analogy to the racial implications of "Amos 'n' Andy." "Johnny Otis in particular would make this criticism of the group's records, and it's one that must be taken seriously." ← Episode 68： ＂Yakety Yak＂ by the Coasters | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dtMHDD0-oOo
**Dual Label Contracts** : Lieber and Stoller held simultaneous contracts with two distinct labels. For RCA, they worked with white pop stars like Elvis Presley and Perry Como, a collaboration that produced some hits but was uncomfortable for both parties and ended after about a year. Concurrently, for Atlantic, they exclusively recorded with black performers they admired, such as Ruth Brown, LeVern Baker, and The Drifters, with The Coasters being their personal project. "They were contracted to RCA to work with white pop stars. Not just Elvis, though he was obviously important to them, but people like Perry Como, who were very far from Lieber and Stoller's normal music." ← Episode 68： ＂Yakety Yak＂ by the Coasters | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dtMHDD0-oOo
**Independent Writer-Producers** : Jerry Lieber and Mike Stoller had a unique and unusual setup for the 1950s, operating as independent writer-producers. They were effectively under contract to two different labels, RCA and Atlantic, whose markets and audiences did not significantly overlap.
