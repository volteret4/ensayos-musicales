**Honky Tonk Part 1 and Part 2 (1956)** : Bill Doggett's biggest hit, an instrumental born from an accidental jam during a dance in Lima, Ohio. Guitarist Billy Butler started noodling a 12-bar blues pattern, the audience started dancing, and the band improvised along, with Clifford Scott taking the lead on honking saxophone. The song was over five minutes long, requiring it to be released as a double-sided 78 RPM record (Parts 1 and 2), a format disliked by jukebox operators but agreed to by King Records' Sid Nathan due to its undeniable popularity. It was recorded in a single take, selling 5,000 copies the first week, then 12,500 the next, and eventually 100,000 copies a week. It reached number one on the R&B chart for 13 weeks, becoming the biggest-selling R&B song of 1956, and remarkably also peaked at number two on the pop chart. This song became a staple for cover bands and influenced many artists, including Dwayne Eddy, whose entire style seems to have stemmed from it, and was covered by acts like The Ventures, Buddy Holly, The Beach Boys, and James Brown. "Honky Tongue Parts 1 and 2 came about almost by accident." ← Episode 50： ＂Honky Tonk＂, by Bill Doggett | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H2rYnKmeZF0

## curiosities
**"Honky Tonk" - Accidental Creation** : Bill Doggett's biggest hit, "Honky Tonk," originated entirely by accident at a dance in Lima, Ohio. It started as guitarist Billy Butler's unplanned noodling on his bass strings between songs, which captivated the audience and led the band to improvise around the riff. "Honky Tongue Parts 1 and 2 came about almost by accident." ← Episode 50： ＂Honky Tonk＂, by Bill Doggett | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H2rYnKmeZF0

**Audience Demand for "Honky Tonk"** : After the spontaneous creation of "Honky Tonk," an audience member requested "that hot new song," prompting the band to recreate it from memory.
