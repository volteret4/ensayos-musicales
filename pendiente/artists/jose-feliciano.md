**José Feliciano Felicinavi Dott (1970)** : This Christmas album was released after Feliciano wrote his signature Christmas song, "Felicinovi Dott," in 1970. Despite earlier controversies surrounding his rendition of the Star-Spangled Banner, the album was very well-received. Bob Stewart of the Baltimore Sun praised its soulful blend of Mexican rhapsodic guitar improvisations, subtle walking bass, and cocktail-type strings, especially in "White Christmas," and its colorful blending of old and new in "We Three Kings." The album was seen as appealing to both sides of the "Generation Gap." "This is from Bob Stewart of the Baltimore Sun. How simply, how intensely he sings and plays these songs. **Felicinovi Dott (1970)** : Jose Feliciano wrote this song in Los Angeles in 1970, inspired by missing his family and holiday traditions, after his producer suggested he write a Christmas song. While other bilingual Christmas songs existed, Feliciano considered this an entity unto itself, expressing both joy and loneliness. It has become a classic, currently sitting at number 8 on Billboard's Holiday 100 chart. "It was expressing the joy I felt on Christmas and the fact that I felt very lonely. I missed my family, I missed Christmas carols with them, I missed the whole Christmas scene." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 52 - 70s Christmas Music | https://www.ftr70.com/

## curiosities
**Comparison to Marvin Gaye's Anthem** : Ironically, the night before Jose Feliciano's controversial rendition of the Star-Spangled Banner at the World Series, Marvin Gaye had sung the anthem at the request of Tigers Broadcasting legend Ernie Harwell. Gaye, known for his Motown sound, sang his version "straight" rather than in his characteristic style, in stark contrast to Feliciano's interpretation that upset many. "The irony of people being upset about Feliciano's rendition of the Anthem is that Marvin Gaye sang at the night before and at the request of the Tiger's Broadcasting legend Ernie Harwell." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 52 - 70s Christmas Music | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Early Life and Discovery** : Jose Feliciano was born in Puerto Rico in 1945, one of 12 children, and began playing the ukulele at age three. After his family moved to New York, he was performing in coffee houses in Greenwich Village by the age of 15, where he was discovered by a music executive from RCA. "Jose Feliciano was born in Puerto Rico in 1945. He was one of 12 children. He started playing the ukulele when he was three. And by the age of 15, he was playing coffee houses and Greenwich Village after his family moved to New York." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 52 - 70s Christmas Music | https://www.ftr70.com/

**Puerto Rican Heritage** : Jose Feliciano is Puerto Rican. He expressed hurt when advocacy groups criticized his involvement (as a Puerto Rican) in writing the theme song for "Chico and the Man," a show about a Mexican American character. "Feliciano said that it actually heard his feelings that this would upset people and that he wanted to represent the Latin community as a whole." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 25 - The Hispanic Influence on 70s Popular Music | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-25-the-hispanic-influence-on-70s-popular-music
**Representation of Latin Community** : Feliciano intended for his work, including the "Chico and the Man" theme song, to represent the entire Latin community rather than being confined to specific national identities. He argued that mariachi music would not fit the gritty vibe of the show. "Feliciano said that it actually heard his feelings that this would upset people and that he wanted to represent the Latin community as a whole. Regarding the charges that it didn't sound Mexican enough, he said that mariachi music would not fit and I think he was right about that." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 25 - The Hispanic Influence on 70s Popular Music | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-25-the-hispanic-influence-on-70s-popular-music
**Star-Spangled Banner Controversy (1968)** : At Game 5 of the World Series in Tiger Stadium, Detroit, Jose Feliciano performed a unique rendition of the Star-Spangled Banner in 1968, which was deemed "not traditional enough" by some, with Carlos Santana even calling it "too black." This performance caused significant backlash, leading American Legion clubs to call for a law dictating how the song should be sung, and Feliciano was controversially labeled a communist during the Cold War era. This event reportedly led to a period where he struggled to get radio play in America and had to perform in other countries, though sources debate if he was truly blacklisted, as he still secured a TV special in 1969 and played to packed houses. "I had to leave America and play in other countries because I wasn't getting any radio play after I did the Star Spangled Banner." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 52 - 70s Christmas Music | https://www.ftr70.com/
