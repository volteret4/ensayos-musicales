**Honey Hush (1956)** : This song was the A-side of the single recorded at their Nashville session. It was originally written and recorded by Big Joe Turner three years earlier, and while some critics noted its misogynistic lyrics, the Rock and Roll Trio's cover is considered by many to be the best and most exciting version, inspiring subsequent covers by artists like Chuck Berry, Paul McCartney, Elvis Costello, and Jerry Lee Lewis. **Train Kept A Rollin' (1956)** : The B-side of their Nashville-recorded single, this track became a classic despite the single not being a commercial success. It was radically rearranged from Tiny Bradshaw's version (itself adapted from Elma Morse's "Cow Cow Boogie") to feature an octave-double distorted guitar riff, apparently invented by Dorsey Burnette but played by Burlison and Martin. This version introduced a deliberately distorted guitar sound that profoundly influenced British guitar players and became a standard in rock music. ## curiosities
**Accidental Sound Discovery in Nashville** : During their Nashville sessions at Owen Bradley's studio, they achieved a unique and superior sound by accident. Paul Burlison's amplifier, damaged from being dropped, produced a deliberately recorded distorted guitar sound, a pioneering moment in music history. "In fact, they got something all together different, and better than that sound, and it happened entirely by accident." ← Episode 44： ＂Train Kept A-Rollin'＂, by Johnny Burnette and the Rock 'n' Roll Trio | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=u3zYAJ2qeR4
**Appearance in "Rock, Rock, Rock" Film** : The band was scheduled to appear in "Rock, Rock, Rock," a cheap exploitation film starring Alan Freed, but Dorsey Burnette quit days before the shoot, leading to Johnny Black temporarily replacing him. "They were due to appear in rock, rock, rock, a cheap exploitation film starring Alan Fried." ← Episode 44： ＂Train Kept A-Rollin'＂, by Johnny Burnette and the Rock 'n' Roll Trio | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=u3zYAJ2qeR4
**Augmentation by Nashville A-Team** : For their Nashville sessions, the trio was augmented by studio musicians from the Nashville A-Team, including Bob Moore on bass (as Dorsey preferred playing rhythm guitar in the studio), Buddy Harmon on drums, and Grady Martin on an additional electric guitar. "At these sessions, the trio were augmented by a few studio musicians from the Nashville A team, the musicians who made most of the country hits of the time." ← Episode 44： ＂Train Kept A-Rollin'＂, by Johnny Burnette and the Rock 'n' Roll Trio | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=u3zYAJ2qeR4
**Breakthrough on Ted Mack Amateur Hour** : Their big break came in early 1956 when they appeared on the Ted Mack Amateur Hour, winning three times in a row, a television series comparable to later talent shows like "The X Factor" or "American Idol." "Their big break came in early 1956 when they appeared on the Ted Mac Amateur Hour and won three times in a row." ← Episode 44： ＂Train Kept A-Rollin'＂, by Johnny Burnette and the Rock 'n' Roll Trio | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=u3zYAJ2qeR4
**Demand for Nashville Recordings** : Unhappy with their New York recordings and the lack of understanding from orchestral musicians, the band refused to record there again and insisted on making records in Nashville. "But now, the Ruck and Rolls-Veele weren't happy at all about their recordings. They didn't want to make any more records in New York, with a bunch of orchestral musicians who didn't know anything about their music." ← Episode 44： ＂Train Kept A-Rollin'＂, by Johnny Burnette and the Rock 'n' Roll Trio | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=u3zYAJ2qeR4
**Disastrous New York Recording Session** : Their first recording session for Coral in New York was unproductive, as they were met by a 32-piece orchestra that had no understanding of rock and roll music. They eventually sent all the orchestral musicians home, keeping only the drummer, to achieve their desired sound. "Their initial session for cover was in New York, and when they got there, they were surprised to find a 32 piece orchestra waiting for them, none of whom had any more clue about playing Ruck and Roll music than the Ruck and Roll trio had about playing orchestral pieces." ← Episode 44： ＂Train Kept A-Rollin'＂, by Johnny Burnette and the Rock 'n' Roll Trio | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=u3zYAJ2qeR4
**Distinctive Guitar Style of Burlison** : Paul Burlison's guitar style and the deliberate distortion effect he applied were absolutely distinctive and unlike anything heard from session guitarist Grady Martin at that point, underscoring Burlison's unique contribution to their sound. "Certainly, Burlison's guitar style is absolutely distinctive, and the effect he puts on his guitar is absolutely unlike anything else that you hear from Brady Martin at this point." ← Episode 44： ＂Train Kept A-Rollin'＂, by Johnny Burnette and the Rock 'n' Roll Trio | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=u3zYAJ2qeR4
**Early Country Style** : Despite changing their name to "The Rock and Roll Trio" a year after forming in 1952, the band was initially a country band, with early set lists including songs like Hank Snow's "I'm Moving On." "While they were called the Rock and Roll trio they were still basically a country band and their early set lists included songs like Hank Snow's and Moving On." ← Episode 44： ＂Train Kept A-Rollin'＂, by Johnny Burnette and the Rock 'n' Roll Trio | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=u3zYAJ2qeR4
**Goodwin Institute Jamboree Development** : The band regularly played the Saturday Night Jamboree at the Goodwin Institute Auditorium, a country music show in Memphis. The backstage area of this venue was crucial, as musicians would jam, share new riffs, and exchange ideas, which was formative for the Rock and Roll Trio and many other rockabilly acts. "They also regularly played the Saturday night Jamboree at the Goodwin Institute Auditorium and regular country music show that was occasionally broadcast on the same station that Burlesons Old Bands had performed on KWEM." ← Episode 44： ＂Train Kept A-Rollin'＂, by Johnny Burnette and the Rock 'n' Roll Trio | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=u3zYAJ2qeR4
**Grand Ole Opry Incident** : The band drove to Nashville to ask Hank Snow to help them get on the Grand Ole Opry, but Snow refused, stating it took him 20 years to earn his spot. This refusal led to Johnny Burnette's expletive-laden response and the band dropping all Hank Snow songs from their live performances. "They wanted to get on the Grand Ole Opry and so they drove up, found Snow who was going to be on that night's show and asked him if he could get them onto the show." ← Episode 44： ＂Train Kept A-Rollin'＂, by Johnny Burnette and the Rock 'n' Roll Trio | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=u3zYAJ2qeR4
**Name Credit Dispute Leading to Split** : Coral Records decided to release their recordings as "by Johnny Burnette and the Rock and Roll Trio," infuriating the other members who considered themselves a band, not just Johnny's backing musicians. This dispute led to Dorsey Burnette temporarily quitting before their film appearance and eventually to the group's permanent split when later tracks were released solely under "Johnny Burnette." "While they had always performed as the rock and roll trio, Coral Records decided to release their recordings as by Johnny Burnett and the rock and roll trio, and the other two members were understandably furious." ← Episode 44： ＂Train Kept A-Rollin'＂, by Johnny Burnette and the Rock 'n' Roll Trio | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=u3zYAJ2qeR4
