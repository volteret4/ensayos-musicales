## curiosities
**"Country Boy" - Pioneering Horn Part** : "Country Boy" featured a distinct "Dan. Da da" horn part that, while clichéd today, was unprecedented in an R&B record at its time of release. "that was the first time anything like that had been used in an A&B record." ← Episode 8： The Fat Man and Fats Domino | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=A-1R-DbVdcM
**"Juncker's Blues" Lyrics - Unsuitable for Radio** : Dave Bartholomew initially wanted Fats Domino to record "Juncker's Blues," but its original lyrics, largely about heroin and cocaine, were deemed inappropriate for radio airplay. "it was felt that the lyrics weren't quite suitable for the radio, what would them be pretty much entirely about heroin and cocaine." ← Episode 8： The Fat Man and Fats Domino | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=A-1R-DbVdcM
**"The Fat Man" Lyrics - Blues Trope of Body Positivity and Attraction** : Dave Bartholomew's lyrics for "The Fat Man" employ a common blues trope where a large man boasts about his attractiveness to women despite his size. "follow a standard blues trope of being fat but having the ability to attract women anyway." ← Episode 8： The Fat Man and Fats Domino | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=A-1R-DbVdcM
**Attempt to Block "Blueberry Hill"** : Initially unenthusiastic about recording "Blueberry Hill," Dave Bartholomew famously declared "we got nothing" at the end of the session and later tried to prevent its release, telling Imperial Records owner Lou Chudd that it would "ruin Domino's career forever." He later admitted this was a "huge mistake," recognizing the song's massive success and stating that his arrangement for it would be his lasting legacy. "When I'm dead and gone a million times, they'll still be playing, that the dad the dad did that." ← Episode 45： ＂Blueberry Hill＂, by Fats Domino | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VqIwZGJHgBg

**Dave Bartholomew's Formal Musical Training - Military Brass Band** : His time in a military brass band necessitated Dave Bartholomew's acquisition of more formal musical skills, including composition and arrangement. **Dave Bartholomew's Military Service and Musical Development** : Like many New Orleans musicians of the late 1940s, Dave Bartholomew honed and expanded his musical abilities during his service in the Army during World War II. "Dave Bartholomew learned his musical skills while he was in the Army during World War II." ← Episode 8： The Fat Man and Fats Domino | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=A-1R-DbVdcM
**Dave Bartholomew's Post-Army Career - A&R and Orchestra Leader** : After his military service, Dave Bartholomew secured an A&R position with Imperial Records and established the Dave Bartholomew Orchestra, which achieved a hit with the song "Country Boy." "After getting out of the Army, he got work as an A&R man for Imperial Records, and he also formed his own band, the Dave Bartholomew Orchestra, who had a hit with Country Boy." ← Episode 8： The Fat Man and Fats Domino | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=A-1R-DbVdcM
**Dave Bartholomew's Rhythmic Innovation - Habanera vs. Backbeat** : Dave Bartholomew pioneered the practice of placing the Habanera rhythm against a drum shuffle or backbeat, an innovation that gave New Orleans R&B its unique flavor. "That rhythm was put against drums playing a shuffle or a backbeat." ← Episode 8： The Fat Man and Fats Domino | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=A-1R-DbVdcM
**Dave Bartholomew's Role in Fats Domino's Signing** : Dave Bartholomew, the leading New Orleans musician of his time and an A&R man for Imperial Records, was instrumental in suggesting the label sign Fats Domino, recognizing his talent. "Bartholomew, who was working for Imperial Records, suggested that the label signed Domino." ← Episode 8： The Fat Man and Fats Domino | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=A-1R-DbVdcM

**Matassa and Bartholomew - Quintessential New Orleans Sound** : The collaborations between Cosimo Matassa as engineer and Dave Bartholomew with his band produced quintessential New Orleans records that, even after 70 years, remain definitive of the city's unique sound. "the records that Matassa would go onto engineer with Dave Bartholomew in his band... would be the quintessential New Orleans records that still, 70 years on, sum up the sound of that city." ← Episode 8： The Fat Man and Fats Domino | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=A-1R-DbVdcM
**Producer and Co-Writer for Fats Domino** : Dave Bartholomew served as a producer and co-writer for Fats Domino, contributing original compositions to Domino's singles. He also brought "Treceo rhythms," described as having a "jelly roll, molten, Spanish tinge," to his arrangements, which became a signature element of New Orleans R&B. "One side of each of his singles would be written by Domino or his producer Dave Bartholomew." ← Episode 45： ＂Blueberry Hill＂, by Fats Domino | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VqIwZGJHgBg
**Signature Bass Line Motif** : Dave Bartholomew frequently incorporated a distinctive bass line motif into his arrangements, which became a staple of New Orleans R&B. He used this bass line in "Ain't That a Shame" and also in "Blue Monday," a song he wrote for Smiley Lewis. "It seems to have been taken up by Bartholomew as a signature motif." ← Episode 45： ＂Blueberry Hill＂, by Fats Domino | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VqIwZGJHgBg
