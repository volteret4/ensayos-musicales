# artist - Rupert Holmes

## genres
- Musical Theatre
- Pop

## songs
**Escape (The Piña Colada Song) (1979)** : This song became the last number one song of the 1970s, hitting the top spot on December 22, 1979, and remaining there for three weeks into the 1980s. Originally titled "Escape," it was renamed due to public demand in record shops. Rupert Holmes wrote the song when he needed an upbeat track to complement an album full of ballads, focusing on lyrics due to the repetitive music. The inspiration came from personal ads in The Village Voice, with Holmes pondering what would happen if he answered one, surmising it would probably be his own wife. His slightly less-than-perfect singing voice was considered crucial to the song's success, making him sound like a relatable character. ## curiosities
**Lyric Writing Process** : Rupert Holmes wrote "Escape (The Piña Colada Song)" when he needed an upbeat track to balance an album of ballads. With the music already composed and being repetitive, he focused intensely on the lyrics, trying various ideas. He discarded some, like "That's the law of the jungle in the school of the street," finding them bad and sounding like a Billy Joel song. He also considered "Cruel to Be Kind" but realized Nick Lowe already had a song with that title. **Multi-Talented Artist** : Rupert Holmes is most remembered for "Escape (The Piña Colada Song)," though he also has a distinguished career in musical theatre, having written the musical "The Mystery of Edwin Drood" for which he won Emmys and Tonys. He has also written a novel that was adapted into a movie. Despite these accomplishments, he remains predominantly identified with his most famous song. **Personal Ad Inspiration** : The inspiration for the lyrics of "Escape (The Piña Colada Song)" came from a copy of The Village Voice and its personal ads, which happened to be lying nearby. Rupert Holmes wondered what would happen if he answered one of these ads, immediately thinking it would probably turn out to be his own wife. This spontaneous thought became the core narrative of the song. "As sometimes happens with writers, he was inspired by something that just happened to be laying nearby. **Song Title Evolution** : The song "Escape (The Piña Colada Song)" was originally just titled "Escape." However, due to popular demand and the way people requested it in record shops, it was officially retitled to "The Piña Colada Song" to match how the public identified it. **Vocal Delivery as Key to Success** : Rupert Holmes's singing voice, described as "not that great," was considered a crucial element in the success of "Escape (The Piña Colada Song)." It made him sound like an ordinary person who would respond to a personal ad and contemplate cheating on his partner, lending authenticity to the song's narrative that a more polished singer, like Neil Diamond, might not have achieved.
