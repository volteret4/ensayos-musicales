**Influence on Early R&B and Ray Charles's Band** : The Count Basie band was a significant influence on early R&B, combining jump band energy with classy jazz, and Ray Charles explicitly modeled his own touring band after Basie's, albeit in a slimmed-down configuration for practical touring purposes. "The Count Basie band was, like Lionel Hamptons, one of the bands that had most influenced early R&B, and its music was exactly the kind of combination of jump band and classy jazz that Charles liked." ← Episode 32： ＂I Got A Woman＂ by Ray Charles | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0oeI9f2x_fo
**Influence on James Brown's Funk** : Count Basie's style of jazz and swing music, which was blending into R&B, significantly influenced James Brown. While not evident in Brown's earlier records, his much later funk recordings owed a lot to Basie, with Brown's famous funk music being described as the logical conclusion to the style developed by musicians like Basie. "That style of music wouldn't show up in his earlier records, which were mostly fairly standard vocal group R&B, but if you listen to his much later funk recordings, they owe a lot to Basie and Lionel Hampton." ← Episode 39： ＂Please Please Please＂ by James Brown and the Famous Flames | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DSkNVh93tAs
**Influence on Jump Band Music** : Count Basie's music was a significant influence on the development of jump band music, the style pioneered by Louis Jordan and His Tympany Five. "it was a style that owed a lot to Lionel Hampton's band and to Count Basie." ← Episode 4： ＂Choo Choo Ch'Boogie＂ by Louis Jordan | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DFZnwvsXGJM
**Jamboree Film Appearance** : Count Basie, with Joe Williams as his lead vocalist, appeared in the film "Jamboree," a 1957 exploitation film featuring various musical performances. He was considered a musician who wasn't strictly rock and roll but was "close enough that the kids would probably accept them." **Musician Band Leader** : Count Basie was an excellent musician and band leader, capable of holding his own on his instrument among most musicians. **Perceived Dance Popularity Due to Strike** : Basie's band appeared to get more people dancing during the cutting contest, but this was due to Whitey's Lindy Hoppers striking and refusing to dance to Chick Webb's music, not necessarily a reflection of audience preference. "But everyone also agreed that Basie's band had got people dancing more, a lot more. What nobody realised at the time was that Whitey's Lindy Hoppers had gone on strike." ← Episode 4： ＂Choo Choo Ch'Boogie＂ by Louis Jordan | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DFZnwvsXGJM
**Rivalry with Chick Webb** : Count Basie, unlike Duke Ellington, was a direct rival to Chick Webb, operating in a similar musical style. They were slated for an after-hours cutting contest following Benny Goodman's Carnegie Hall concert, an event eagerly anticipated by jazz fans. "Count Basie, though, was a different matter. He was trying to do the same kind of thing as Chick Webb, and he was doing it well." ← Episode 4： ＂Choo Choo Ch'Boogie＂ by Louis Jordan | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DFZnwvsXGJM
