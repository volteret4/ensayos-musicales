## curiosities
**"The Chocolate Cowboy" Persona** : By the 1980s, O.B. McClinton was known as "the chocolate cowboy," an upbeat entertainer who often took requests and performed impressions of other singers in his shows. "He was a very upbeat entertainer by all accounts who by the 1980s called himself the chocolate cowboy." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 53 - More than Charley Pride - African Americans in 70s Country | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Career Shift to Country (after Charlie Pride)** : Originally from Mississippi, O.B. McClinton began his career as an R&B singer but struggled to gain traction and felt no real connection to the genre. Inspired by Charlie Pride, he transitioned to country music, finding "inner satisfaction" and a vocal style that better suited him. "he started as an R&B singer but he wasn't really able to break through there and didn't really feel any connection to it anyway. So he decided to give country a try after he heard Charlie Pride." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 53 - More than Charley Pride - African Americans in 70s Country | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Death (1987)** : O.B. McClinton died of cancer in the mid-1980s at the age of 47 in 1987, cutting short a promising career. "McClinton got cancer in the mid 80s and he died far too young at the age of 47 in 1987." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 53 - More than Charley Pride - African Americans in 70s Country | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Discomfort with R&B** : O.B. McClinton expressed that he did not feel authentic singing R&B, noting that his voice didn't blend well with the genre. In 1973, he articulated a sense of liberation in pursuing country music, stating he "turned the world out" to find satisfaction in it. "If you tried to listen to me sing rhythm and blues you wouldn't think it was the same person. My voice just didn't blend with that. It didn't sound good." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 53 - More than Charley Pride - African Americans in 70s Country | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Perspective on Race and Country Music** : When asked about his race and why he entered country music, O.B. McClinton deflected the question, likening it to asking "why am I black?" He believed that stereotypes, such as the "bibbed overalls" image, prevented more African Americans from engaging with country music as fans or performers. "He said I say don't ask me. I don't know. It's like saying hey man why am I black?" ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 53 - More than Charley Pride - African Americans in 70s Country | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Regina Taylor's "Star Category" (1975)** : In a 1975 article in the Terahot Tribune, Regina Taylor identified O.B. McClinton as one of three African Americans deserving of "star category" status in country music, alongside Charlie Pride and Stoney Edwards. "Obi-Miklinton was the third." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 53 - More than Charley Pride - African Americans in 70s Country | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Stage Impressions (1984)** : In 1984, O.B. McClinton described his live shows, which included impressions of various country singers. He deliberately performed Charlie Pride impressions to highlight their differences, followed by Dolly Parton impressions to avoid being seen as a carbon copy of Pride. He also illustrated the contrast between country and R&B by singing the same song in the style of Johnny Cash and then Muddy Waters. "I do Charlie Pride just to make sure they hear that I'm different than I go right into an impression of Deli Parton just so they wouldn't say that I'm a carbon copy of her either." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 53 - More than Charley Pride - African Americans in 70s Country | https://www.ftr70.com/
