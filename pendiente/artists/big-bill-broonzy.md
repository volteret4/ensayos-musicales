## curiosities
**Appearance on 6-5 Special** : He made an appearance on the BBC's television program "6-5 Special," which was considered one of the show's exceptional moments. **Second Career as Folk Blues Musician** : His performance at "Spirituals to Swing" jump-started Broonzy's later career as a folk blues musician, where he primarily played for white audiences, a shift from his earlier role as a Chicago blues performer for black audiences. "That had in fact kick-started Bruinsy's later second career as a folk blues musician, playing for largely white audiences, rather than as a photo Chicago blues performer, playing for blackwings." ← Episode 97： ＂Song to Woody＂ by Bob Dylan | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3DufLVcZyc4
**Skiffle Repertoire** : His songs were a primary component of the repertoire for skiffle groups, alongside those of Lead Belly and Woody Guthrie. He was notable as the one blues musician that even the least knowledgeable skifflers could name, despite his relatively limited commercial success in the US. "a repertoire that was made up primarily of songs by Lead Belly, Woody Guthrie and Big Bill Brunze, who was the one blues musician that even the least knowledgeable Skiffle could name, despite his relative lack of commercial success in the US." ← Episode 70： ＂Move It＂ by Cliff Richard and the Drifters | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uoEWP94f6S4
**Spirituals to Swing Appearance** : He was a then-obscure folk musician who replaced a deceased Johnson (unrelated to Pete Johnson) in the lineup for John Hammond's "Spirituals to Swing" concert in 1938. His appearance at this pivotal concert significantly contributed to his rise in popularity. "Johnson's place was taken by a then obscure folk musician called Big Bill Roonsy, who became popular largely on the basis of that appearance." ← Episode 2： ＂Roll Em Pete＂ by Big Joe Turner | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=thpMb1A_fAs
**Substitute for Robert Johnson (1938)** : Big Bill Broonzy was recruited to perform at the 1938 "Spirituals to Swing" concerts after its organizer, John Hammond, discovered that Robert Johnson had recently died. Broonzy also played a couple of Johnson's records from the stage.
