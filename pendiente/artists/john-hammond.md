- Columbia Records

- Cafe Society

## curiosities
**"Primitive Music" Narrative** : Hammond was among "progressive white men" who put forward a "deeply mistaken" narrative about "primitive music," characterizing black musical forms as less sophisticated due to their sound in the 1930s. "most of the narrative about primitive music, a narrative that was put forward by very progressive white men like John Hammond or the Lomaxes, is deeply mistaken." ← Episode 5： ＂This Train＂ by Sister Rosetta Tharpe | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=II-hDnwUgT8
**Cafe Society Booking** : He booked Sister Rosetta Tharp to play at the Cafe Society, an integrated club, after the Cotton Club closed. "a venue booked by John Hammond, which was an integrated club and which fit her rather better." ← Episode 5： ＂This Train＂ by Sister Rosetta Tharpe | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=II-hDnwUgT8
**Introducer of Robert Johnson to Dylan** : Hammond gave Bob Dylan an unreleased re-issue of old blues records, "King of the Delta Blues Singers" by Robert Johnson, to listen to. He had previously wanted Johnson to perform at "Spirituals to Swing" but learned of his death. "Hammond also gave Dylan an album to take home and listen to, a record which hadn't come out yet, a v-issue of some old blues records called King of the Delta Blues Singers by Robert James." ← Episode 97： ＂Song to Woody＂ by Bob Dylan | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3DufLVcZyc4
**Organiser of Spirituals to Swing** : John Hammond was known for his legendary "spirituals to swing" concerts at Carnegie Hall, which were important but also problematic in their narrative of musical evolution. "John Hammond's legendary spirituals to swing concerts." ← Episode 5： ＂This Train＂ by Sister Rosetta Tharpe | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=II-hDnwUgT8
**Organizer of Spirituals to Swing Concerts** : John Hammond was the organizer of the influential 1938 "Spirituals to Swing" concerts at Carnegie Hall, which later became an album and influenced Bob Dylan. "Hammond had, of course, organised the spirituals to swing concerts which had influenced Dylan" ← Episode 97： ＂Song to Woody＂ by Bob Dylan | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3DufLVcZyc4
**Producer of Dylan's Debut** : Hammond produced Bob Dylan's first album, finding Dylan "incredibly difficult to work with" due to his microphone technique and refusal of second takes. "Hammond found Dylan incredibly difficult to work with, saying he had a pulling mic technique" ← Episode 97： ＂Song to Woody＂ by Bob Dylan | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3DufLVcZyc4
**Producer's Rejection of "Strange Fruit"** : John Hammond, Billy Holiday's producer and known for progressive actions like integrating Benny Goodman's band and sponsoring Communist Party shows, found "Strange Fruit" too political and inflammatory to release. "That song was too political, even for her producer, John Hammond." ← Episode 4： ＂Choo Choo Ch'Boogie＂ by Louis Jordan | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DFZnwvsXGJM
**Signing Bob Dylan** : Hammond was impressed by Bob Dylan, deciding to sign him to Columbia Records despite resistance from Mitch Miller, the head of A&R, who didn't understand Hammond's vision for Dylan. Hammond's strong track record at the label secured the deal. "He decided to sign Dylan, and even though Mitch Miller, Columbia's head of A&R at the time, had no clue what Hammond saw in Dylan." ← Episode 97： ＂Song to Woody＂ by Bob Dylan | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3DufLVcZyc4
**Talent Scout and Producer** : A highly influential figure, Hammond discovered artists like Billy Holiday, Count Basie, and Charlie Christian. He later worked for Columbia Records, where he produced Aretha Franklin's first secular records. "he'd been the person to discover Billy Holiday, and Count Basie, and Charlie Christian." ← Episode 97： ＂Song to Woody＂ by Bob Dylan | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3DufLVcZyc4
