## songs
**Blue Suede Shoes (1955)** : The idea for this landmark song originated from Johnny Cash's anecdote about a serviceman referring to his shoes, and was solidified when Perkins overheard an audience member exclaim, "Don't step on my suede!" Perkins wrote the song one night, playing his electric guitar unplugged to avoid disturbing his wife. He recorded it on December 19, 1955, and famously misspelled "suede" as "SWAD-A-D-E" because he did not know the correct spelling. The song became a massive cross-over hit, reaching number two on both Pop and Country charts and number five on the R&B chart. Perkins's version is rhythmically sophisticated, featuring a stop-time introduction that transitions from 6/4 time into 4/4, creating a unique tension and release dynamic. "Perkins found himself one night getting out of bed, playing his electric guitar unplugged, so as not to disturb his wife, and writing a song he called Blue Suede shoes." ← Episode 36： ＂Blue Suede Shoes＂ by Carl Perkins | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=TItMbEioi7Y

**Matchbox (1956)** : The B-side to "Your True Love," this song originated from an idea given to Carl by his father, who recalled an old song line. Carl developed a new song around the line "sitting here wondering, will a matchbox hold my clothes? Ain't got no matches, but I've got a long way to go." Perkins never heard the original "Matchbox Blues" and based his song on his father's description, diverging significantly from Blind Lemon Jefferson's song after the chorus. The song features floating blues lyrics, including a verse from the traditional "Poor Boy, Long Way From Home." It was largely improvised in the studio, and while credited to Carl Perkins, Jerry Lee Lewis provided the boogie piano part. Perkins, impressed by Lewis's musicianship, upped his guitar playing, using a hard-driving riff on the bass strings and executing solos on the top three strings with a "Triple String" technique. He took two solos to outshine Lewis. The song became a rock and roll standard after The Beatles brought it to prominence decades later. "Matchbox shows all the sounds of having been put together in the studio, largely impovervised, as most of Perkins' songs were." ← Episode 51： ＂Matchbox＂ by Carl Perkins | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0Nrjr0AGd3U
