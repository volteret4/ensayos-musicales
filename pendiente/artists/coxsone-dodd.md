## songs
**Easy Snapping** : Produced by Coxsone Dodd, this record is generally regarded as the first true Ska record. The term "Ska" itself is believed by some to have originated from Dodd's instruction to arranger Ernest Ranglin to "play it, Ska, Ska, Ska," emphasizing the off-beats. "The next record in the sequence was produced by Dodd and is generally considered the first real Scar record." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI

## curiosities
**"Gator" Exclusivity (7 years)** : Coxsone Dodd managed to exclusively play Willis Gatortail Jackson's "Gator" at his shows for seven years, a record so tied to him it was known as "Cox and Harp." This exclusivity was eventually broken by Duke Reid. "Cox and Dodd managed to use one record, later for Gator by Willis Gatortail Jackson. At every show for seven years, we name it Cox and Harp." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**"Lollipop Girl" Confrontation (early 1960s)** : After Dodd acquired "Lollipop Girl" and it became highly popular, Duke Reid had one of Dodd's assistants make a copy for him. During a "sound clash," when Reed played his copy, Dodd reacted by pulling a gun on him, with a nearby police station likely preventing fatal violence. "Dodd pulled a gun on Reed, and it was only the fact that the clash was next door to the police station that kept the two men from killing each other." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**Countering Duke Reid's Exclusivity** : Dodd utilized Prince Buster's knowledge of R&B production styles and lyrics to identify records being played by rival Duke Reid. He would then arrange for merchant seamen to acquire copies from America, which would be pressed locally and sold to other soundmen, thus undermining Reid's competitive advantage. "God would then get a merchant seamen to bring your copy of that record back from America, get a local record pressing plant to fess up a bunch of copies of it, and select to the other soundmen, thus destroying Reed's edge." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**Discovery of Millie Small (1962)** : In 1962, Coxsone Dodd became interested in a 14-year-old Millie Small, primarily because her voice bore a striking resemblance to Shirley Goodman of the New Orleans R&B duo Shirley and Lee. This led to her first recordings. "she came to the attention of Cox and Dodd, who was very interested in her, because her voice sounded spookily like that of Shirley, from the duo Shirley and Lee." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**Downbeat Sound System** : Coxsone Dodd ran Downbeat, one of the two biggest sound systems in Kingston, rivaling Duke Reid's Trojan. His system became so popular that he eventually operated five different sound systems simultaneously across the city. "the other being Downbeat, run by Cox and God." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**Pioneering Technology in Sound Systems** : Coxsone Dodd was known for his technological prowess, possessing the most powerful sound system with the best amplifiers during the intense sound system rivalries in Kingston. "Dodd knew about technology and had the most powerful sound system with the best amps." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**Reaction to "Gator" Loss** : When Duke Reid publicly played "Gator," thereby ending Dodd's seven-year exclusivity, the psychological impact was so severe that Dodd collapsed. Prince Buster recounted the scene, emphasizing the profound shock. "The psychological impact had knocked him out, nobody never hit him." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**Starting His Own Label (early 1960s)** : Following Duke Reid's entry into the record business, Coxsone Dodd also established his own record label. He began recording local singers and using a core group of session musicians to produce Jamaican records. "both Cox and Dodd and Prince Buster started up their own labels, and started having local singers, plus people from a small pool of players who became the go-to session musicians." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**Training Assistants (1950s/1960s)** : Dodd's popularity led him to employ assistants to run his multiple sound systems, many of whom, like Prince Buster and Lee "Scratch" Perry, went on to become major figures in Jamaican music themselves. "the ones who didn't perform at himself being run by assistants, who later became big names in the Jamaican music world themselves. Like Prince Buster and Lee Scratch Perry." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**View of Ska (late 1950s/early 1960s)** : In the early stages of Ska's development, Coxsone Dodd, like Duke Reid, viewed the music they produced as largely a substitute for American R&B, rather than an entirely new, distinct Jamaican genre.
