## songs
**Just Between You and Me (1967)** : This song served as Charlie Pride's breakthrough. It led to his Grammy nomination in 1968 and was the song he performed for his television debut on "The Lawrence Welk Show" in 1967. "Charlie Pride's breakthrough song, just between you and me, got him a Grammy nomination in 1968." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 53 - More than Charley Pride - African Americans in 70s Country | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Kiss An Angel Good Morning (1971)** : Released in 1971, this was Charlie Pride's significant pop crossover hit, bringing him to the attention of non-country fans. Written by Ben Peters, the song features Pride's baritone voice, which had "no hint at all of blues" unlike Ray Charles, embodying the country-pop sound popular on AM radio in the 1970s. "That includes Kiss and Angel Good Morning, the pop crossover hit that put him on the radar of people who were not country fans." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 53 - More than Charley Pride - African Americans in 70s Country | https://www.ftr70.com/

## curiosities
**"You Look Like Them But Sound Like Us"** : Charlie Pride was told, "you look like them but sound like us," a comment highlighting his ability to appeal across racial lines in the country music genre. **Addressing His Race in Detroit (1966)** : At a show in Detroit in 1966, Pride confronted an audience that greeted him with silence upon realizing he was African American. He introduced himself, acknowledged his "permanent suntan," stated his Mississippi farming background, and declared his intention to entertain them. "friends, I realize it's a little unique me coming out here with a permanent suntan to sing country and western to you. But that's the way it is." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 53 - More than Charley Pride - African Americans in 70s Country | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Career Focus on Country** : Despite the pop crossover success of "Kiss an Angel Good Morning," Charlie Pride's other hits primarily charted on the country charts. He was content with this, viewing himself primarily as a country artist not seeking a pop career. "His other hits were on the country charts, which I think he was just fine with because he was a country artist who was not looking for a pop career." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 53 - More than Charley Pride - African Americans in 70s Country | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Comparison to Linda Martell** : Charlie Pride was frequently used as the frame of reference for any African American country singer, including Linda Martell, who was often referred to as "the female Charlie Pride." Unlike Martell, displaying his face on album covers eventually became a non-issue, and he made numerous television appearances. "Linda fits the pride pattern. Typical, as she was frequently referred to as the female Charlie Pride." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 53 - More than Charley Pride - African Americans in 70s Country | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Country Music Association Awards (1971, 1972)** : Charlie Pride won the Country Music Association Male Entertainer of the Year award in both 1971 and 1972, marking significant recognition in the country music industry. **Discovery in Montana** : Pride was discovered singing in country clubs in Montana, a state that had the fewest black people in the union at the time. He had lived there for a decade before being encouraged to record a demo. **Early Life and Baseball Career** : Born into poverty in Mississippi, Charlie Pride initially pursued a career in baseball, playing in the Negro leagues. He was paid extra to sing before games to boost attendance, but his baseball career was cut short by an elbow injury. **Experience with Racism (Personal Account)** : Charlie Pride stated that he never experienced anyone yelling a slur at him or telling him to leave the stage. He attributed this to his belief in his identity as "Charlie Pride, the American singing musician," suggesting people might accept you as you believe yourself to be. "Pride said that there was never a time when anyone yelled to slur at him and shouted at him and told him to get off the stage." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 53 - More than Charley Pride - African Americans in 70s Country | https://www.ftr70.com/

**Grand Ole Opry Membership (1967)** : In 1967, Charlie Pride became the second African American to be granted membership to the Grand Ole Opry, and the first since harmonica player DeFord Bailey, who last played at the Opry in 1941. This marked a significant milestone for racial integration in country music's premier institution. "Charlie Pride became the second African American ever to be allowed to be a member of the Grand Ole Opera in the first since the harmonica player to Ford Bailey who had last played at the Opera in 1941." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 53 - More than Charley Pride - African Americans in 70s Country | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Initial Obscuring of Race by RCA** : Early in his career, RCA avoided using Charlie Pride's photo on promotional materials. While his voice created demand, many listeners were unaware he was African American. "up to this point, RCA was not using Pride's photo on anything." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 53 - More than Charley Pride - African Americans in 70s Country | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Inspiration for "Nashville" Character** : The character of Tommy Brown in Robert Altman's 1975 film "Nashville" was widely believed to be based on Charlie Pride due to the actor's resemblance in hairstyle and dress, and Pride's unique status as a prominent black country singer. Pride, however, denied any connection, stating that if it was meant to be him, it was "a lie." "The film director, Robert Altman, made a movie about the country music industry which came out in 1975 and he included a character that many people believed was supposed to be Charlie Pride." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 53 - More than Charley Pride - African Americans in 70s Country | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Major Label Exclusivity (1967-1991)** : Charlie Pride was the only African-American country singer signed to a major record label, RCA, between 1967 and 1991. This highlights his unique position and the industry's limited diversity during that period. "he was the only African-American country singer that was signed to a major record label between 1967 and 1991. RCA, however, adopted a cautious approach, initially booking him for shows in different towns to "temperature check" audience reactions before scheduling further performances.
