## curiosities
**Advocate for Sam Cooke's Secular Career** : Bumps Blackwell was instrumental in pushing Sam Cooke to become a secular solo performer in 1956, recognizing Cooke's "very, very special talent" beyond his gospel work with The Soul Stirrers. "Blackwell knew then that Cook was a very, very special talent and he and the rest of the people at specialty became more and more insistent as 1956 went on that Sam Cook should become a secular solo performer rather than performing in a gospel group." ← Episode Sixty： ＂You Send Me＂ by Sam Cooke | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=92tD5qJR-yY
**Conflict with Art Rupe over "Rip It Up"** : Blackwell had a significant dispute with Specialty head Art Rupe over Little Richard's "Rip It Up" when he changed the song's arrangement to a drum pattern, contrary to Rupe's approved piano triplet arrangement, nearly costing him his job. "When they agreed to record that, black well had worked out an arrangement beforehand the Rube was happy with. One that was based around piano triplets." ← Episode Sixty： ＂You Send Me＂ by Sam Cooke | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=92tD5qJR-yY
**Disguising Sam Cooke's Secular Debut** : Blackwell helped orchestrate the release of Sam Cooke's first secular recordings under the pseudonym "Dale Cook" to avoid jeopardizing Cooke's gospel career. "So the decision was made to put the single out under the name Dale Cook and maintain a small amount of plausible denierability." ← Episode Sixty： ＂You Send Me＂ by Sam Cooke | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=92tD5qJR-yY
**Introducer of Quincy Jones to Ray Charles** : Bumps Blackwell played a pivotal role in Ray Charles's career by introducing him to Quincy Jones, a connection that led to extensive collaborations between Charles and Jones for much of their respective careers. "Around this time, Charles was introduced by Bumps Blackwell to Quincy Jones, with whom he would go on to collaborate in various ways from much of the rest of his career." ← Episode 32： ＂I Got A Woman＂ by Ray Charles | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0oeI9f2x_fo
**Long-Term Association with Ray Charles** : Bumps Blackwell had an enduring association with Ray Charles, playing jazz with him early in his career and continuing their collaboration long into the future, notably introducing Charles to Quincy Jones. "But while his association with Bumps Blackwell would continue long into the future, playing jazz wasn't how Ray Charles was going to make his name." ← Episode 32： ＂I Got A Woman＂ by Ray Charles | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0oeI9f2x_fo
**Producer of "You Send Me" Session** : Blackwell supervised Sam Cooke's "You Send Me" recording session in Los Angeles, which featured New Orleans veteran musicians. "Blackwell supervised it, and while the session was in LA, almost everyone there was a veteran New Orleans player." ← Episode Sixty： ＂You Send Me＂ by Sam Cooke | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=92tD5qJR-yY
**Retaliation Against Art Rupe** : In response to Rupe's attempt to copy "You Send Me" with "I'll Come Running Back to You," Blackwell orchestrated the rush-release of an unreleased Little Richard song on Keen Records, featuring Don and Dewey and The Valiants, forcing Specialty to also rush their own Little Richard release. "In retaliation for that being released, Bump's Blackwell took a song that he'd recorded months earlier with Little Richard, but which still hadn't been released, and got the specialty duo Don and Dui to provide instrumental backing for a vocal group called The Valiant, and put his house on Keen." ← Episode Sixty： ＂You Send Me＂ by Sam Cooke | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=92tD5qJR-yY
**Termination from Specialty and Deal with Rupe** : Rupe fired Blackwell from Specialty Records due to his repeated insubordination regarding recording arrangements and vocal choices. However, they reached a deal where Blackwell could leave and take Sam Cooke and his recordings with him to a new label. "Rube decided it was the last straw. Black well had to go." ← Episode Sixty： ＂You Send Me＂ by Sam Cooke | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=92tD5qJR-yY
