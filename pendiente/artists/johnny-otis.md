## albums
**Cold Shot (Late 1960s)** : This album by "the new Johnny Otis show" was signed by Kent Records, a small blues label owned by the Bihari Brothers, at Frank Zappa's persuasion. It featured a core band of only three people: Otis on piano and drums, Delmar "Mighty Mouth" Evans on vocals, and Otis's 15-year-old son, Sugar, playing all guitar and bass parts. The album marked Otis's first R&B hit since "Willie and the Hand Jive" over a decade earlier. **For Adults Only (Late 1960s)** : Recorded by the same trio (Otis, Evans, and Sugar) as "Cold Shot," this album was released under the name Snatch and the Poon Tangs. The cover, designed by Otis, was a "spot on imitation of the style of Robert Crum." Despite its obscene humor, the album had a serious sociological purpose: Otis wanted to preserve black folklore, including songs like "The Signifying Monkey," which had been performed for years but typically stripped of their sexual insults in other recordings by artists like Chuck Berry and Willie Dixon. "Around the same time, that trio also recorded another album called For Adults Only, under the name Snatch and the Poon Tangs, and with the covered run by Otis in a spot on imitation of the style of Robert Crum." ← REUPLOAD Episode 71： ＂Willie and the Hand Jive＂ by Johnny Otis | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Ez6BCUlI6Rg
**Monterey Jazz Festival (1970) - Live Album** : A live performance by Johnny Otis's band at the Monterey Jazz Festival in 1970 was released as a live album. This performance featured many veteran musicians, including Esther Phillips, Big Joe Turner, Eddie "Cleanhead" Vincent, Roy Milton, Peewee Crayton, Ivory Joe Hunter, and Roy Brown. Footage from this show, specifically the band performing "Willie and the Hand Jive," was later featured in Clint Eastwood's film "Play Misty for Me," showcasing Otis's belief in the commonality and combined power of different strands of black American music. **Willie and the Hand Jive (1958)** : Inspired by the "Hand Jive" dance popularized on the British TV show "6-5 Special" and demonstrated by The Three Tons of Joy, Johnny Otis wrote this song. ## curiosities
**"Hand Jive" Misinterpretation and Bans** : Despite "Willie and the Hand Jive" becoming a major hit in the US, several radio stations banned the song. This was due to the mistaken assumption that the "Hand Jive" dance, which The Three Tons of Joy demonstrated on Otis's TV shows and stage performances, was a reference to masturbation. Otis continued to address these questions about the song's perceived lascivious meaning as late as the 1990s. **Civil Rights Activism (1960s)** : In the 1960s, Johnny Otis became increasingly involved in the Civil Rights Movement. He wrote a weekly newspaper column condemning racism, assisted his friend Mervyn Dymally (who became the first black person elected to state-wide office in the USA since Reconstruction), and worked alongside Malcolm X, facing death threats and having crosses burned on his lawn. Despite being white, his wife was black, which heightened the personal stakes of his activism. **Critique of Systemic Injustice in Listen to the Lambs** : Otis's book, "Listen to the Lambs," expressed fierce anger at systemic racism, colonialism, Anglo-centric beauty standards, police brutality, the Vietnam War, and the inequalities suppressing black people. The book dedicates over 30 pages to witness accounts of the riots, including descriptions of white police officers casually shooting black teenagers. **Literary Works - Listen to the Lambs (1965)** : Following the Watts riot in 1965, Johnny Otis authored "Listen to the Lambs," a multifaceted work combining political essay, autobiography, and eyewitness accounts of the riots.
