## songs
**Back Stabbers (1972)** : This song marked a significant milestone for The O'Jays, becoming their first number one R&B single in 1972. It is characteristic of the "Philadelphia sound" produced by Kenny Gamble and Leon Huff, evident in its prominent use of strings. Before this breakout hit, The O'Jays had a long history performing on the "Chitlin circuit" in the South. Scholars like Alice Eccles suggest that while the song can be interpreted as relationship betrayal, it also carries a deeper political commentary, possibly echoing Dr. King's sentiments about white moderates and reflecting a broader theme of betrayal within the Black Power movement. ## curiosities
**From Chitlin Circuit to Chart-Toppers** : Prior to the success of "Back Stabbers" in 1972, The O'Jays had a long and extensive career performing on the "Chitlin circuit" across the American South. This network of venues was historically vital for black entertainers during segregation, and their eventual mainstream success marked a significant transition from a regional touring act to national prominence. **Philadelphia Sound Pioneers** : The O'Jays' "Back Stabbers" is a prime example of the "Philadelphia sound," characterized by its lush string arrangements, a signature of producers Kenny Gamble and Leon Huff. This sound distinguished their work and became highly influential in soul music.
