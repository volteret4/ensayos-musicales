## curiosities
**Boredom with Elvis Film Routines (c. 1958)** : After contributing to a couple of Elvis's films, Leiber and Stoller grew bored with the repetitive and routine nature of writing songs for them. They perceived the process as merely filling "gaps" with similar types of songs, stifling their creative expression. "They were already, after a couple of films, getting bored with the routine nature of writing for Elvis's films." ← Episode 72： ＂Trouble＂ by Elvis Presley | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=psIgGqkQhZI
**Brief but Defining Collaboration with Elvis (April 1957 - February 1958)** : Jerry Leiber and Mike Stoller's active collaboration with Elvis Presley, which significantly shaped public perception of all involved, lasted a mere 10 months, from April 1957 to February 1958. Despite the end of their direct studio work, Elvis continued to record many of their songs written for other artists. "Their active collaboration with Elvis, a collaboration that would define all of them in the eyes of the public, had lasted only 10 months, from April 1957 through February 1958." ← Episode 72： ＂Trouble＂ by Elvis Presley | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=psIgGqkQhZI
**Elvis Continued to Record Their Songs (Post-1958)** : Even after their direct studio collaboration ceased, Elvis Presley continued to "keep an eye on their careers" and recorded many more of Leiber and Stoller's songs, although they were no longer commissioned to write material specifically for him. These later recordings included songs they had written for other artists like The Clovers and The Coasters. "He'd just never again have them write a song specifically for him." ← Episode 72： ＂Trouble＂ by Elvis Presley | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=psIgGqkQhZI
**Loss of RCA Jobs and Shift to R&B Focus (Post-June 1958)** : Following their definitive split from working with Elvis, Leiber and Stoller soon lost their jobs at RCA. This enabled them to redirect their focus entirely to their work with R&B artists, including notable groups such as The Clovers, The Coasters, and The Drifters. **Obsession with Stop-Time Blues Riff (c. 1954 onwards)** : Jerry Leiber and Mike Stoller developed a strong "obsession" with the "stop time blues riff," which originated in Muddy Waters' "Hoochie Coochie Man" in 1954. They consistently used this riff as a defining "signifier of the blues" in many of their compositions, making it a generic indicator of the genre. **Shared Musical Taste with Elvis (c. 1958)** : Jerry Leiber and Mike Stoller shared a mutual admiration for Elvis Presley's work ethic in the studio and his taste in blues music, which initially formed a strong basis for their creative collaboration. "They liked Elvis himself a lot, and they admired and respected his work ethic in the studio, and shared his taste in blues music." ← Episode 72： ＂Trouble＂ by Elvis Presley | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=psIgGqkQhZI
**Writing Songs as Jokes for Serious Singers (Various)** : Leiber and Stoller had a distinctive habit of writing songs humorously, often as jokes or parodies, and then providing them to singers who would perform them with complete seriousness. This was evident with "Love Me" and "Trouble" for Elvis, with Jerry Leiber noting the irony of Elvis's earnest delivery.
