## curiosities
**"The Big Beat" TV Appearance Controversy** : Lymon's appearance on Alan Freed's TV series, "The Big Beat," resulted in a major controversy that ultimately led to the show's cancellation within weeks. During his performance, Lymon, a Black boy, began dancing with a white girl from the predominantly white studio audience, which was not well-received by Southern network affiliates. This event, likely Lymon's first solo performance, highlighted significant racial tensions in the era. "Frankie Lyman started to dance as well, a black boy dancing with a white girl. This did not go down well at all with the Southern network affiliate, and within a couple of weeks, Fried's show had been taken off the TV." ← Episode 35： ＂Why Do Fools Fall In Love？＂ by Frankie Lymon and the Teenagers | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AtZf9PJEttM
**Complex Estate and Widow Disputes** : Following his death, the rights to "Why Do Fools Fall in Love" became the subject of extensive legal battles in the 1980s, as three separate women claimed to be Lymon's legitimate widow and heir. These included his first wife (married in 1964 while she was still married to someone else), Zola Taylor (who claimed a bigamous marriage a year later but lacked evidence), and a third wife (married bigamously in 1967). The third wife eventually won these legal battles and now controls his legacy. "In the 1980s, three separate women claimed to be Lyman's widow, and thus his legitimate heir." ← Episode 35： ＂Why Do Fools Fall In Love？＂ by Frankie Lymon and the Teenagers | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AtZf9PJEttM
**Early Death** : Frankie Lymon died at the age of 25 in 1968 from a heroin overdose. He had been a husband for almost half of his life at the time of his death. "Frankie Lyman died, aged just 25, and a husband for almost half of his life, of a heroin overdose in 1968." ← Episode 35： ＂Why Do Fools Fall In Love？＂ by Frankie Lymon and the Teenagers | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AtZf9PJEttM
**Falsetto Innovation in Rock and Roll** : Lymon's distinctive falsetto voice, attributed to his voice not yet having broken, introduced a groundbreaking new timbre to rock and roll lead vocals. While falsetto was present in doo-wop, Lymon's use of it for lead parts was a late addition to "Why Do Fools Fall in Love" (originally intended for Herman Santiago) and profoundly influenced subsequent vocal groups such as The Four Seasons, Jan and Dean, and The Beach Boys. "Limemon, by virtue of his voice not yet having broken, introduced a new tamper into rock and roll lead vocals, and he influenced almost every vocal group that followed." ← Episode 35： ＂Why Do Fools Fall In Love？＂ by Frankie Lymon and the Teenagers | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AtZf9PJEttM
**Post-Teenagers Career Decline** : After splitting from The Teenagers, neither Lymon nor the group ever achieved another hit. He continued to record for a couple of years, but his subsequent releases were unsuccessful, especially after his voice broke and he lost his distinctive youthful sound. "Now the Lyman, nor the teenagers, would ever have another hit after they split." ← Episode 35： ＂Why Do Fools Fall In Love？＂ by Frankie Lymon and the Teenagers | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AtZf9PJEttM
**Tragic Later Life and Heroin Addiction** : Lymon's life after The Teenagers was marked by profound tragedy. He married three times, each bigamously, and his only child died two days after birth. He developed a heroin addiction, reportedly stealing from his second wife, Zola Taylor, to support it. Despite a brief reunion with The Teenagers in 1965 and a period in the army where he appeared to get clean, securing a new record deal, he relapsed the night before a scheduled studio session. **Youthful Talent and Charisma** : Frankie Lymon was a very young kid when he joined The Premiers (The Teenagers), initially singing and playing percussion in a Mambo group. Richie Barrett quickly recognized his star potential due to his captivating performance style, which resembled a young James Brown, and his gorgeous falsetto voice, leading to comparisons with Michael Jackson's early career trajectory.
