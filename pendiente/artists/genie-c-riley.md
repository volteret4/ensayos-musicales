## songs
**Harper Valley PTA (1968)** : Genie C. Riley, at 22, recorded this song written by Tom T. Hall, which became a smash hit due to its sassy narrative about small-town hypocrisy. It debuted at number 81 on the Billboard Hot 100 in September 1968, rapidly climbing to number 1 by September 21st, sharing the top 10 with artists like The Beatles, Steppenwolf, and Aretha Franklin. It also spent three weeks at number 1 on the Billboard Country Chart. The song is notable for its modern take on a traditional folk song, with a rebellious "I'm not going to take your bullshit" vibe, and serves as a time capsule with references to mini skirts, Peyton Place, and "Sock It To Me." ## curiosities
**Harper Valley PTA - Movie and TV Show** : The hit song "Harper Valley PTA" inspired a movie starring Barbara Eden, known from "I Dream of Jeannie." In 1980, it was further adapted into a TV show. "Harper Valley PTA also inspired a movie starring Barbara Eden... and then in 1980 it became a TV show." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 23 - Women of Country Music in the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/023-country-women-72820-2-45-pm
**Image vs. Authenticity Struggle** : Despite singing a song about a strong woman, Genie C. Riley faced a cruel irony where her producer, Shelby Singleton, heavily controlled her image. Singleton transformed her into an "over-sexed hillbilly girl" trope, making her wear mini skirts on stage and even shortening a floor-length gown she intended to wear to the 1968 Country Music Association Awards. This exploitation extended to promotional appearances where her husband was forbidden from accompanying her when he tried to intervene. "It is this cruel irony that she was singing a song about a strong woman but she was not permitted her own authenticity." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 23 - Women of Country Music in the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/023-country-women-72820-2-45-pm
**Transformation to Gospel Singer** : After years of struggling for control over her image and career, Genie C. Riley broke her contract with Shelby Singleton's Plantation label and moved to MGM. By the end of the 1970s, she had become a gospel singer, performing in full-length gowns, an image she presumably controlled herself.
