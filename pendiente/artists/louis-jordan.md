## curiosities
**Alternating Pianists with Wild Bill Davis** : While officially Bill Doggett joined Louis Jordan in 1949, interviews suggest he joined much earlier, alternating with Jordan's other piano player, Wild Bill Davis. According to Bill Adams, involved in Jordan's management, Davis would tour for a week while Doggett arranged, and then they would swap roles. "But I've seen interviews with members of Jordan's organisation that suggest he joined much earlier, but he would alternate with Jordan's other piano player, Wild Bill Davis." ← Episode 50： ＂Honky Tonk＂, by Bill Doggett | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H2rYnKmeZF0
**Attempt to Poach Band Members and Firing** : Convinced of his and Ella Fitzgerald's importance to Chick Webb's band, Louis Jordan planned to form his own group, taking Ella and other members with him. Webb discovered the plan and fired Jordan, while most of the band, including Ella, remained loyal. "He thought that he and Ella were the two important ones in the web band, and he planned to form his own band, and take her, and much of the rest of the band, with him, web found out and fired Jordan, and Ella most of the band remained loyal to Webb." ← Episode 4： ＂Choo Choo Ch'Boogie＂ by Louis Jordan | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DFZnwvsXGJM
**Collaborations with Ella Fitzgerald** : Louis Jordan had recorded hits with Ella Fitzgerald, a collaboration that began when they first worked together in Chick Webb's band in the 1930s. "just like the ink spots, he'd had hits to wedding with Ella Fitzgerald, who he'd first worked with back in the 1930s in Chick Webb's band." ← Episode 50： ＂Honky Tonk＂, by Bill Doggett | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H2rYnKmeZF0
**Crossover Appeal and Country Market** : Louis Jordan's music had crossover success, appealing to audiences of both Bob Wills and Big Joe Turner. This led him to record songs, like "Choo Choo Ch'Boogie," originally targeted at the white country market. "to appeal to the same audience that was listening to Bob Will's, just as much as you were to the audience that was listening to Big Joe Turner, and because of this crossover success, Jordan started recording occasional songs that were originally aimed at the white country market." ← Episode 4： ＂Choo Choo Ch'Boogie＂ by Louis Jordan | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DFZnwvsXGJM
**Exceptional Performer and Charisma** : Louis Jordan was an exceptional performer capable of selling songs with wit, humor, and a hip-ness rivaled only by Cab Calloway and Slim Gaylord at the time. He was also a superior musician, charming, funny, tuneful, and attractive. "He was someone who could absolutely sell a song, with wit and humour, and a general sense of hip-ness that could possibly be matched at that time, only by Cap Calaway and Slim Gaylord." ← Episode 4： ＂Choo Choo Ch'Boogie＂ by Louis Jordan | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DFZnwvsXGJM
**First Studio Recordings with Tympany Five** : On December 20, 1938, Louis Jordan and his "Alksrande v. band" (which soon became Louis Jordan and His Tympany Five) made their first studio recordings, laying down "Honey in the Bee Bowl" and "Barnacle Bill the Sailor." "on December 20, 1938, ten days before Roll and Pete was recorded, Louis Jordan and his Alksrande v. band went into the studio for the first time, to record Honey in the Bee Bowl, and Barnacle Bill the Sailor." ← Episode 4： ＂Choo Choo Ch'Boogie＂ by Louis Jordan | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DFZnwvsXGJM

**Frontman Role in Chick Webb's Band** : Louis Jordan was a crucial alto sax player and male lead vocalist for Chick Webb's band, often taking on the frontman role. He was so integral that some people mistakenly thought he was Chick Webb himself. "This sax player did a lot of the frontman job for Webb's band, and he was so important to the band in those years, that allegedly, some people thought he was Chick Webb." ← Episode 4： ＂Choo Choo Ch'Boogie＂ by Louis Jordan | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DFZnwvsXGJM
**Fusion of Genres in "Choo Choo Ch'Boogie"** : Jordan's "Choo Choo Ch'Boogie" exemplified a fusion of genres, resembling Bob Wills' Western Swing (lacking only fiddle or steel guitar) while clearly showcasing the horn-driven pulse and hip, jive vocals characteristic of rhythm and blues, making it a seminal example of early rock and roll. "There's only really the lack of a fiddle or steel guitar to distinguish the styles, but you can also hear the horn driven pulse and the hip vocals that characterize rhythm and blues." ← Episode 4： ＂Choo Choo Ch'Boogie＂ by Louis Jordan | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DFZnwvsXGJM
