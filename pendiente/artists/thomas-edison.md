# artist - Thomas Edison

## curiosities
**Cylindrical Format - First Major Failure** : Thomas Edison initially developed his phonograph using cylindrical tubes, a decision driven by a complex technical challenge with flat discs. While cylinders allowed for a constant angular speed with linear needle movement, they proved impractical for storing sound, leading to their abandonment and marking them as the first major failure in music formats. **First Playable Recording (1877)** : Thomas Edison made the first playable recording in 1877, following his invention of the telephone. He aimed to explore if telephone audio could be preserved for later reproduction. His device consisted of a rotating cylinder, a recording needle, and a diaphragm that translated sound pressure into analog indentations on the cylinder. **Flat Disc Speed Problem (Late 19th Century)** : Thomas Edison's initial attempt to use flat discs for recording faced a critical technical hurdle: he observed that while a vinyl disc spins at a constant angular speed, the needle's reproduction speed is not constant. This complex issue led him to temporarily set aside the flat disc concept in favor of cylindrical recordings. **Phonograph Invention and Analog Voice Recording** : Edison designed a system where he spoke into a diaphragm, which moved a recording needle. As a cylinder was turned, the pressure from his voice was imprinted as analog indentations onto the cylinder's surface, creating a physical record of his speech. This invention marked the beginning of sound recording technology. **Talking Machine - First Demonstration (1877)** : Thomas Edison's talking machine was one of the first devices capable of capturing sound, demonstrating this capability in 1877. It had a recording limit of approximately three minutes, which subsequently influenced the standard length of popular songs.
