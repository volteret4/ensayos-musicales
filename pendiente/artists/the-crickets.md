## curiosities
**"Peggy Sue" Origin Story** : Buddy Holly originally intended to name a song after his niece, Cindy Lou. However, Jerry Allison suggested that the song would be better if it was about his girlfriend, leading to the eventual title "Peggy Sue." "Jamie, on the other hand, thought the song would be better if he was about his girlfriend." ← Episode Sixty-One： ＂That'll Be the Day＂, by the Crickets | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KVJMKLc54O0
**Band Naming - Rejecting "The Beetles"** : Buddy Holly and Jerry Allison, inspired by New Orleans R&B groups like The Spiders, initially considered naming their group "The Beetles" but decided it was "too creepy" because people would want to squish them, ultimately settling on "The Crickets." "At first they considered The Beatles, but decided that that was too creepy. People would want to squish them. So they settled on The Crickets." ← Episode Sixty-One： ＂That'll Be the Day＂, by the Crickets | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KVJMKLc54O0
**Continuation After Holly's Death (1959-2015)** : After Buddy Holly's death, the remaining Crickets picked themselves up and continued as a band. Sonny Curtis joined on guitar, and they went through a succession of Holly sound-alike singers. Joe Mauldin remained a member until his death in 2015. "Meanwhile, the remaining crickets picked themselves up and carried on." ← Episode 74： ＂It Doesn't Matter Any More＂ by Buddy Holly | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6unib5T2mDQ
**Contract for "That'll Be the Day" (1957)** : To avoid issues with Buddy Holly's previous Decca contract, the contract for the release of "That'll Be the Day" on Brunswick was sent to Jerry Allison, intentionally omitting Buddy Holly's name in case record executive Bob Thiele reported it to his superiors. "And the contract was sent to Jerry Allison, not Buddy Holly. His name wasn't mentioned at first, in case Thiel decided to mention it to his buses, and the whole thing was blown." ← Episode Sixty-One： ＂That'll Be the Day＂, by the Crickets | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KVJMKLc54O0
**Extensive Session Work (Early 1957)** : In addition to recording their own material, The Crickets actively engaged in session work for other artists, playing on records by Jim Robinson, Jack Huddl, Hal Goodson, and Fred Crawford, among others, demonstrating their busy schedule and versatility. "They were also doing session work, playing on records by Jim Robinson, Jack Huddl, Hal Goodson, Fred Crawford and more." ← Episode Sixty-One： ＂That'll Be the Day＂, by the Crickets | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KVJMKLc54O0

**Jerry Allison's Drumming Technique on "Peggy Sue" (1957)** : For "Peggy Sue," drummer Jerry Allison played a fast "paviddle" on his snare drum continuously. Due to the loudness, he had to play in a separate, outside room from the main studio. Producer Norman Petty added an echo chamber effect, flicking the switch on and off to create the illusion of two drummers. "Allison started playing a fast paviddle on his snare drum. Holly decided that they were going to change the tempo of the song, and have Allison play that part all the way through." ← Episode 74： ＂It Doesn't Matter Any More＂ by Buddy Holly | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6unib5T2mDQ
**Joe B. Mauldin's Audition and Joining** : The band's permanent lineup was finalized with Joe B. Mauldin on bass after he sat in on a gig with Buddy Holly, Jerry Allison, and Nikki Sullivan, impressing them with his playing. Mauldin had previously played with The Four Teens. "Joe B had sat in on a gig with the other three, and they'd been impressed with his bass playing." ← Episode Sixty-One： ＂That'll Be the Day＂, by the Crickets | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KVJMKLc54O0

**Nikki Sullivan's Departure (Year not specified)** : Nikki Sullivan quit The Crickets after only 90 days of touring and a total of 27 songs recorded during Holly's lifetime, due to feeling side-lined and unhappy with the group's internal dynamics and financial arrangements. He attempted a solo career with "It's All Over" on Dott Records but was unsuccessful, later working in electronics and benefiting from the Buddy Holly nostalgia industry. "Sullivan was becoming more and more sideline in the group, and when they returned to Lubbock at the end of the tour, drawing which he'd ended up breaking down and crying, he decided he was going to quit the group." ← Episode 74： ＂It Doesn't Matter Any More＂ by Buddy Holly | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6unib5T2mDQ
**Nikki Sullivan's Side-lined Role (1957)** : Nikki Sullivan's rhythm guitar was often deemed unnecessary in The Crickets' songs, and on "Peggy Sue," he ended up not playing guitar at all. Instead, he acted as Holly's "foot pedal," kneeling beside him to flick a pickup switch on Holly's guitar to change its tone for the solo, and then back again. "Nikki Sullivan was already starting to regret joining the cricket, because the rarely wasn't room for his rhythm guitar and most of the songs they were playing." ← Episode 74： ＂It Doesn't Matter Any More＂ by Buddy Holly | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6unib5T2mDQ
**Norman Petty's Manipulation Post-Split (1958)** : After Holly's plan to confront Petty and form their own company, Petty manipulated Jerry Allison and Joe Mauldin by telling them they were "small town Texas boys" incapable of living in New York, that they didn't need Holly, and that Maria Elena was controlling him. He also threatened to withhold all their money, leading Mauldin and Allison to side with Petty against Holly. "Pettie started manipulating them. He told them they were small town Texas boys who would never be able to live in the big city." ← Episode 74： ＂It Doesn't Matter Any More＂ by Buddy Holly | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6unib5T2mDQ
**Prolific Recording Pace (Early 1957)** : The Crickets were constantly in Norman Petty's studio, recording a "ludicrous amount of material" and piling up songs much faster than they could be released. This output included dozens upon dozens of songs in the early months of 1957 that continued to be released for years afterward. "And they were recording a ludicrous amount of material." ← Episode Sixty-One： ＂That'll Be the Day＂, by the Crickets | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KVJMKLc54O0
**Studio Personnel and Recording Strategy (Year not specified)** : The Crickets' records were made by Buddy Holly (vocals, lead guitar), Nikki Sullivan (rhythm guitar), Jerry Allison (drums), Joe Mauldin (bass), and producer Norman Petty (with his wife sometimes on keyboards). Straight-ahead rock and roll tracks would have backing vocals overdubbed by The Picks and be released as Crickets records, while more experimental tracks with only Holly's vocal would be solo releases. "The more straight ahead Rock and Roll records would have backing vocals overdubbed on them, usually by a vocal group called The Picks, and would be released as Cricket's records." ← Episode 74： ＂It Doesn't Matter Any More＂ by Buddy Holly | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6unib5T2mDQ
**Sullivan's Financial Settlement (Year not specified)** : Nikki Sullivan engaged in endless battles with Norman Petty over the group's accounts and his promised equal share of income. He eventually settled for just $1,000, 10% of the recording royalties for "That'll Be the Day," and co-writing royalties for "I'm Gonna Love You Too." His church, which Petty had promised 10% of the Crickets' income, received nothing. "Sullivan got into endless battles with Petty, overseeing the group's accounts, which Petty wouldn't show him, and eventually settled for getting just $1,000, 10% of the recording royalties just for the single That'll Be the Day, and co-writing royalties on one song, I'm going to love you too." ← Episode 74： ＂It Doesn't Matter Any More＂ by Buddy Holly | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6unib5T2mDQ
**The Apollo Controversy (1957)** : The Crickets were among the few white acts to play the Harlem Apollo, though not the first (Bobby Darin and possibly Buddy Rich preceded them). They faced a notoriously hostile audience and had to adapt their setlist to include more R&B covers to win over the crowd, as Holly's original material (except "That'll Be the Day") was not well received. "when it reached the Harlem Apollo, which had notoriously hostile audiences, the group had to reconfigure their sets, as the audiences didn't like any of Holly's original material, except that'll be the day." ← Episode 74： ＂It Doesn't Matter Any More＂ by Buddy Holly | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6unib5T2mDQ
**The Name Split Strategy (Year not specified)** : The distinction between "Buddy Holly" solo records and "The Crickets" group records was a deliberate strategy by their management to encourage DJs to play two different records, creating the impression of distinct artists. "the whole idea of the split was that DJs would play two records instead of one, if they appeared to be by different artists." ← Episode 74： ＂It Doesn't Matter Any More＂ by Buddy Holly | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6unib5T2mDQ
