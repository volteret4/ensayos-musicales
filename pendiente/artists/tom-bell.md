## curiosities
**Avoidance of Political/Religious Songs** : Tom Bell intentionally refrained from writing political or religious songs, holding the belief that such themes were excessively personal and carried the risk of alienating a segment of his audience. "He was not going to write political songs, and he was not going to write songs ahead. **Definition of Soulful Music** : In 1972, Tom Bell articulated his perspective that the quality of "soulful" music was not exclusive to Black artists, citing diverse examples such as country singer Charlie Pride, The Beatles, Frank Sinatra, and at times, Andy Williams, to illustrate his point. "There is no such thing as a person being soulful because he's black." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 37 - Philly Soul and the Sound of the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-37-philly-soul-and-the-sound-of-the-70s
**Musical Experimentation** : Bell was notable for his readiness to integrate unconventional instruments into soul music, exemplified by his use of a sitar on The Delfonics' "Didn't I Blow Your Mind This Time" and an oboe on The Stylistics' "Betcha by Golly Wow." "Bell was very willing to try new things musically like he used the Cittar on Didn't I Blow Your Mind This Time by the Delphonics." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 37 - Philly Soul and the Sound of the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-37-philly-soul-and-the-sound-of-the-70s
**Not Part of PIR** : Tom Bell, while a close associate of Kenny Gamble and Leon Huff, consciously chose not to be a permanent fixture at Philadelphia International Records, instead opting for the flexibility to select his own projects and collaborations. "Tom Bell was never part of PIR. He picked and chose who he wanted to work with and when." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 37 - Philly Soul and the Sound of the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-37-philly-soul-and-the-sound-of-the-70s
**Producer's Style - No Improv** : Tom Bell maintained a stringent production methodology, requiring vocalists and musicians to perform songs precisely as he envisioned them, disallowing any improvisation during the recording process. "You just had to sing and play the songs his way, no improv." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 37 - Philly Soul and the Sound of the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-37-philly-soul-and-the-sound-of-the-70s
**Tom Bell's Wager with The Spinners** : To persuade The Spinners to allow him to produce their next record, Tom Bell made a bold wager: he promised each member $1,000 if he failed to secure a number one hit, but in return, they would owe him a custom Cadillac if he succeeded. "If I do not get you a number one record, I will give each one of you $1,000. If I do get you a number one record, you owe me a custom Cadillac." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 37 - Philly Soul and the Sound of the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-37-philly-soul-and-the-sound-of-the-70s
**Wrote Songs for His Children** : Tom Bell composed songs for all of his children, though "Rubberband Man" remains the sole recorded track among them. The song was originally conceived with the lyric "fat man" but was changed to "rubber band man" to uplift his son, Mark, who was overweight. "Tom Bell also wrote songs for all of his kids, but rubber band band.
