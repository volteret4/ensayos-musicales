**Mighty Garvey (1968) - Fourth Album** : This album is considered the closest the group came to a truly great album. Unlike earlier albums dominated by blues covers, "Mighty Garvey" was mostly made up of original material by Mike Hugg or Mike Davo. The highlight was likely the Mellotron-driven "It's So Easy Falling," but the album ultimately did not chart. ## curiosities
**"No A-Sides by Group Members" Rule** : During Mike Davo's tenure as lead singer, the group operated under an implicit "no A-sides by group members" rule, which limited their material choices. Davo himself was a songwriter and wrote two hit singles, "Handbags and Glad Rags" and "Build Me Up Buttercup," that the group never recorded. "Dabo was himself a songwriter, and it shows the limitations of the No A sides by group members rule. The wild Dabo was the lead singer of Manfred Mann, he wrote two hit singles which the group never recorded." ← Episode 118： ＂Do-Wah-Diddy-Diddy＂ by Manfred Mann | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QAP7abAbwwM
**Dave Richmond's Departure and Tom McGuinness's Arrival (1964)** : Dave Richmond was seen as a "great jazz bass player" who wanted to emulate Charles Mingus, playing "strange cross-rhythms." The group needed someone to play "straightforward blues bass lines without complaint," closer to Willie Dixon. Tom McGuinness, a guitarist who bluffed his way into the bass player role, replaced Richmond. McGuinness had never played bass before his first gig and was only asked if he was willing to play simple parts during his interview. "Richmond was a great bass player, but he was a great jazz bass player. He wanted to be Charles Mingus, and play strange cross-vethams, and what the group needed at this point was someone who would just play straightforward blues bass lines without complaint, they needed someone closer to Willie Dixon than to Mingus." ← Episode 118： ＂Do-Wah-Diddy-Diddy＂ by Manfred Mann | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QAP7abAbwwM
**Eclectic Catalogue and Critical Reassessment** : Manfred Mann was a band with too many musical interests for a coherent image, and their reliance on outside songwriters and frequent lineup changes meant they lacked a consistent sound compared to contemporaries. However, this eclecticism created a catalog that "rewards exploration" and may warrant a major critical reassessment in the future.
