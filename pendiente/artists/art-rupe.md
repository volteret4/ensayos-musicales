**Conflict over Sam Cooke's Royalties** : Rupe believed Sam Cooke's demand for a higher royalty share (more than one cent per record) was "greed" and disliked artists pushing for more money. "Rube didn't like that kind of greed from his artists. **Contractual Claim over Little Richard** : Art Rupe maintained a contractual claim over Little Richard's recordings for Specialty Records even after Richard's initial conversion, reflecting the label's continued rights to his work. "Richard was impressed enough by them that he got in touch with Art Roup who still had some contractual claim over Richard's own recordings to tell him about them..." ← Episode 54： Keep A Knockin | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=oA4Y_K9E5Y4
**Disinterest in The Beatles** : When Little Richard attempted to introduce Art Rupe to The Beatles after spending time with them in Hamburg, Rupe dismissed them as "some English group," indicating his lack of interest in signing new talent and his primary focus on getting Little Richard back into the studio. "Roup said he wasn't interested in some English group. **Falling Out with Bumps Blackwell (Little Richard's "Rip It Up")** : Rupe had a major conflict with producer Bumps Blackwell over Little Richard's "Rip It Up." Blackwell, on a plane to the session, changed the arrangement from piano triplets (which Rupe approved) to a drum pattern, leading Rupe to almost fire him, only relenting when the song became a massive hit. "Rube and black well had already had one massive falling out overripped up by little Richard. **Forced Release of Little Richard Material** : Specialty Records was forced to "rush release" a Little Richard song, which they were trying to "drip-feed" to the public, as a direct response to Bumps Blackwell's counter-release of an unreleased Little Richard track on Keen. "Specialty had to rush release Little Richard's version to make sure it became the hit. **Musical Vision and Commercial Acumen** : Rupe, despite loving gospel music, recognized the greater commercial potential in other black music styles after Lloyd Price's "Lordy Miss Claude" became Specialty's biggest hit, prompting him to shift the label's focus towards more secular genres. "Even though Rooop loved gospel music, he realised when Lordy Miss Claude became the biggest hit specialty he had ever had to that point, that maybe he should refocus the label away from gospel and towards more secular styles of music." ← Episode Sixty： ＂You Send Me＂ by Sam Cooke | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=92tD5qJR-yY
**Racial and Gender Objections to Backing Vocals** : Rupe was "horrified" by the use of white female backing vocalists (Lee Gotch Singers) on Sam Cooke's "You Send Me," viewing it as an attempt to make black music sound "white" and explicitly objecting to the presence of women. "Rube was horrified that bumps and bathees had put white backing vocalists behind Cooke's vocals. They were, in Rube's view, trying to make Sam Cooke sound like Billy Ward and his dominoes at best, and like a symphony orchestra at worst." ← Episode Sixty： ＂You Send Me＂ by Sam Cooke | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=92tD5qJR-yY
**Retaliation against Sam Cooke** : In retaliation for Cooke's success on Keen, Rupe took Sam's demo "I'll Come Running Back to You" and had Rene Hall and the Lee Gotch Singers overdub it to mimic the sound of "You Send Me," then released it on Specialty. "Roup started legal action against him, and meanwhile he took a demo Sam had recorded. I'll come running back to you, and got many hall and the Lee got singers. **Settlement with Bumps Blackwell** : Rupe and Bumps Blackwell eventually reached an agreement: Blackwell would leave Specialty, taking Sam Cooke and his existing recordings, while Rupe would retain publishing rights for any songs Sam wrote and an option on future Cooke recordings.
