## songs
**Disco Inferno (1976)** : This song was initially released by The Trammps as a single and became a hit in clubs but struggled on pop radio, failing to reach the Top 40. Its fortunes drastically changed when it was included in the *Saturday Night Fever* soundtrack, playing in a memorable scene where Tony Manero dances with Annette. Inspired by disaster movies of the early 70s like *The Towering Inferno*, the title references a common theme of the era. "First, the title, disaster movies were all the rage in the early 70s, saying something about maybe the vibes of the era perhaps." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 50 - The Travolta Trilogy | https://www.ftr70.com/

## curiosities
**Overwhelming Popularity** : The song's immense popularity following the movie led to significant life changes for the band. Jimmy Ellis's daughter, Erica, recounted having to move due to people randomly showing up at their house and being driven to school in a limousine, illustrating the song's widespread cultural impact. "People just started randomly showing up at their house. The next thing she knew, she's being dropped off to school in a limo." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 50 - The Travolta Trilogy | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Pioneering "Four on the Floor" Beat** : Drummer Earl Young, while working as a session drummer for MFSB at Philadelphia International Records in 1973, developed the iconic "four on the floor" disco sound. He innovated by using his kick drum and hi-hat to create a continuous, driving rhythm for Harold Melvin and the Blue Notes' "The Love I Lost," which became the heartbeat of disco and, by extension, the late 70s. "He used his kick drum and his hi-hat to make the four on the floor sound that really turns out to be the heartbeat of disco and then in many ways, the heartbeat of the late 70s." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 50 - The Travolta Trilogy | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Soundtrack Success and Chart Performance** : Following its inclusion in the *Saturday Night Fever* soundtrack, "Disco Inferno" soared to number one on the Billboard dance chart for 11 consecutive weeks and reached number 11 on the Billboard Hot 100 in 1978. It won a Grammy for Best R&B Vocal by a Group in 1979 and was inducted into the Dance Hall of Fame in 2005.
