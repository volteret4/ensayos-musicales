## curiosities
**Acting Style and Audience Connection** : Roger Ebert analyzed Travolta's unique appeal, noting his ability to play a character while simultaneously winking at the audience, creating loyalty rather than distance. This "easy musical charm" was particularly evident in films like *Grease*, allowing him to make the audience his confederates. "He's making the audience his confederates in the performance, enlisting them, not creating distances by playing to the camera, but creating loyalties by playing through it." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 50 - The Travolta Trilogy | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Career Overexposure and Revival** : Travolta's career experienced a period of overexposure, similar to disco and Gilley's nightclub, which contributed to a decline in his immediate popularity. His significant resurgence in *Pulp Fiction* in 1994, notably his twist dance with Uma Thurman, was widely welcomed, marking a successful return to the cinematic spotlight. "I think that's why we welcomed him back in Pulp Fiction and his dancing of the twist with Uma Thurman." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 50 - The Travolta Trilogy | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Characters Find Tranquility Through Dance** : Across *Saturday Night Fever*, *Grease*, and *Urban Cowboy*, Travolta's characters are depicted as young men who, though frequently challenged by the adult world, find peace and happiness on the dance floor. This consistent theme highlights dance as an escape and a source of calm amidst hectic lives. "On the dance floor however Travolta's characters find tranquility and I think that that's true." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 50 - The Travolta Trilogy | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Comparison to Legendary Stars (1978)** : Film critic Roger Ebert, writing for the *Chicago Sun-Times* in 1978, declared Travolta a star on the level of Elvis Presley, James Dean, and Frank Sinatra, acknowledging his significant popularity in the late 1970s. **Early Acting Career - Vinny Barberino** : Before his film stardom, Travolta played Vinny Barberino, one of the "sweat hogs" on the sitcom *Welcome Back Kotter* from 1975 to 1979. This character was seen as a precursor to Tony Monero and Danny Zuko, being a wise-cracking, lovable pseudo-juvenile delinquent with a signature dance. "Travolta started to back away from that series in year four before he completely left the show to pursue his movie career." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 50 - The Travolta Trilogy | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Grease Audition and Casting (1973/1978)** : In 1973, at age 18, Travolta auditioned for Danny Zuko in the stage production of *Grease* but was considered too young, receiving the role of Doody instead. When the film adaptation was being cast, Henry Winkler, then a big star as Fonzie on *Happy Days*, was the frontrunner for Danny Zuko before Travolta secured the part. **Oscar Nomination - Saturday Night Fever (1978)** : Travolta earned an Academy Award nomination for Best Actor in 1978 for his role as Tony Monero in *Saturday Night Fever*. He did not win, with Richard Dreyfus taking the award for *The Goodbye Girl*, but many argued Travolta created a more memorable role.
