## albums
**Color Me Country (1969)** : Linda Martell's debut album, recorded in a remarkable 12 hours, was signed to Shelby Singleton's Plantation Records. Martell challenged Singleton on the controversial name of his label, asserting its racial connotations. The album featured her breakthrough single, "Color Him Father." **Color Him Father (1969)** : The first single from Martell's debut album "Color Me Country," this song is a country-fried cover of The Winstons' R&B hit. Written by Richard Lewis Spencer, it tells the story of a dedicated stepfather raising seven children after their father's death, presumably in Vietnam. Martell's version reached number 22 on the Billboard Country Charts, earning her an invitation to perform at the Grand Ole Opry. ## curiosities
**Alice Randall's Theory on Visibility** : Alice Randall, author of "My Black Country," theorized in 2009 that Martell's career may have been hindered by her visibility. Randall questioned whether it would have been more beneficial for Martell's career if her voice had been heard without her race being immediately apparent, suggesting that some performances might have been canceled once it was revealed she was not white. "Would it have been better for her career just to let her voice be heard without people thinking that is a black woman singing the song?" ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 53 - More than Charley Pride - African Americans in 70s Country | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Discovery by William Duke Rayner (1969)** : In 1969, William Duke Rayner, a furniture store owner from Nashville with aspirations in music, discovered Martell. He heard her covering country songs at an Air Force Base in South Carolina and was impressed by the audience's reaction, convincing her to let him fund a demo and become her manager. "He liked the reaction that he heard from the audience when Linda was singing at a Air Force Base in South Carolina." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 53 - More than Charley Pride - African Americans in 70s Country | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Early Life and Name Change (1961)** : Born Thelma in South Carolina in 1961, her stage name "Linda" was suggested by a DJ. She began singing at age six and developed roots in singing, hearing country music on the radio alongside her R&B foundations. **Experience with Racism and Advice from Charlie Pride** : Initially, Martell reported minimal racist heckling, primarily from "a couple of drunkards" at a Pennsylvania show. However, "we learned later that there were other incidents." She sought advice from Charlie Pride, who told her to develop a "thick skin" and get used to the racism. Her manager, Shelby Singleton, advised her against addressing the audience directly during heckling. **Grand Ole Opry Debut (1969)** : Linda Martell was the first solo female black artist to perform at the Grand Ole Opry, playing there a total of 12 times. During her performances at the Ryman Auditorium, she would have seen the words "Confederate gallery" hanging in the balcony, a sign that remained until its removal in 2017. **Lawsuit and Career Stalling (1970)** : In May 1970, manager William Duke Rayner filed a lawsuit against Martell, claiming he was owed royalties and asserting she "does not possess business ability" to handle money, suggesting a court should sequester 25% of her earnings. Although Shelby Singleton reportedly made the lawsuit "go away," he then informed Martell he would prioritize promoting white artist Jeannie C. Riley. Singleton subsequently released Martell from her contract but threatened to sue any future label she signed with, effectively blacklisting her. "Singleton let Martell out of her contract, but then threatened to sue the next label she signed with." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 53 - More than Charley Pride - African Americans in 70s Country | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Rapid Career Launch** : Within three days of meeting William Duke Rayner, Linda Martell secured a manager, signed a contract with Plantation Records, and recorded her debut album, "Color Me Country." This rapid progression marked a swift entry into the country music scene. **Views on Black Artists and Country Music (1972)** : In a 1972 interview with the Meican News, Linda Martell discussed the scarcity of African American country artists. She suggested that black artists often dismiss country music without listening to it, thereby missing potential career opportunities. "It's not that the Negro artists aren't interested in country music.
