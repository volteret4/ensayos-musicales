## songs
**Hide Me and Vibosum (Original title of Rock Me)** : The original title of the song later rewritten and recorded by Sister Rosetta Tharp as "Rock Me." **It's Tight Like That (1928)** : A collaboration with Tampa Red under the name Georgia Tom. This song is one of the earliest "hocombe records," responsible for many clichés of the form, and sold seven million copies. It is considered one of the most important records ever made, influencing hocom blues, R&B, and virtually all subsequent black American music. "Their song It's Tight Like That from 1928 is one of the earliest hocombe records, and is largely responsible for a lot of the cliches of the form, and it sold seven million copies." ← Episode 5： ＂This Train＂ by Sister Rosetta Tharpe | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=II-hDnwUgT8
**Peace in the Valley (1937)** : Dorsey wrote this song 14 years before it was first recorded and released by The Soul Stirrers with Sam Cooke in 1951, where it appeared as the B-side to "Jesus Gave Me Water." "That song had been written by Thomas Dorsey, 14 years earlier, but this was the first time it had been released on a record, at least by anyone of any prominence." ← Episode Sixty： ＂You Send Me＂ by Sam Cooke | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=92tD5qJR-yY

## curiosities
**Inventor of Gospel Music** : Thomas Dorsey is largely credited with inventing gospel music as it is known today, around 1930. He combined themes and melodies from traditional Christian hymns with the feel of blues and jazz, incorporating popular music idioms into the genre. "gospel music as we know it basically was invented by Thomas Dorsey." ← Episode 5： ＂This Train＂ by Sister Rosetta Tharpe | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=II-hDnwUgT8
**Personal Struggle and Career Shift** : Dorsey, a blues and jazz musician (also known as Georgia Tom), experienced multiple breakdowns due to the conflict between his faith and his secular music career. At one point, he was unable to play the piano on stage with Marraini. This led him to eventually focus entirely on making religious music, though he maintained parallel careers for a while. "He did this, because he was so torn between his faith and his work as a blues musician, that he had multiple breakdowns, at one point finding himself on stage with Marraini, and completely unable to move his fingers to play the piano." ← Episode 5： ＂This Train＂ by Sister Rosetta Tharpe | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=II-hDnwUgT8
**Pioneer of New Gospel Music** : Thomas Dorsey was a pivotal figure in Chicago, spearheading the creation of a new, revolutionary form of gospel music during the 1940s, influencing young artists like Sam Cooke. "Chicago which...was the city where a whole new form of gospel music was being created at that point, spearheaded by Thomas Dorsey." ← Episode Sixty： ＂You Send Me＂ by Sam Cooke | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=92tD5qJR-yY
**Revolutionary Musician** : Dorsey is described as a man who revolutionized music multiple times over, through his foundational work in hocombe blues with Tampa Red and his invention of modern gospel music. "That's one man, a man who personally revolutionized music multiple times over, writing about his own personal grief, and creating something that stands as great art, without having to be patronised or given special consideration." ← Episode 5： ＂This Train＂ by Sister Rosetta Tharpe | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=II-hDnwUgT8
