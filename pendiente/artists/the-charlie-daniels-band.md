**The Devil Went Down to Georgia (1979)** : This song, from the 1979 album "Million Mile Reflections," was originally intended as instrumental filler with no lyrics, to provide fiddle music to fill gaps on the album. Lyrics were later added, developing a "sell your soul narrative" about a character named Johnny. It was inspired by Stephen Vincent Benét's poem "The Mountain Whippoorwill." Charlie Daniels considered the fiddle a bridge from rock to the band's bluegrass roots. The song became a huge crossover hit, reaching number three on the Billboard Hot 100 in 1979, and was prominently featured in the 1980 movie "Urban Cowboy," introducing its "bad-ass fiddle playing" to new audiences and regions. The Charlie Daniels Band performed it on Austin City Limits in 1981. "The devil went down to Georgia made it to number three on the billboard hot 100 in 1979. Yes, it was a huge crossover hit." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 49 - Austin City Limits and the 70s Country Music Revolution | http://ftr70.com

## curiosities
**Embodiment of Austin City Limits' Mission** : The Charlie Daniels Band, with its diverse sound rooted in country but incorporating jazz, blues, and rock, was considered the epitome of what Austin City Limits aimed to accomplish: showcasing a wide variety of music with country roots. "In many ways the Charlie Daniels band was really the epitome of what Austin City Limits was trying to accomplish, which was to showcase a wide variety of music that had its roots in country." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 49 - Austin City Limits and the 70s Country Music Revolution | http://ftr70.com
**Fiddle Technique for Grittier Sound** : Charlie Daniels intentionally pressed down harder on the fiddle strings to achieve a grittier sound, demonstrating a specific instrumental technique contributing to their distinctive musical style. "Charlie Daniels said that by pressing down harder on the fiddle strings, it made for a grittier sound." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 49 - Austin City Limits and the 70s Country Music Revolution | http://ftr70.com
**First Nashville Band on Austin City Limits (1976)** : The Charlie Daniels Band was the first Nashville-based band to perform on Austin City Limits in 1976. Their inclusion marked a necessary expansion for the show beyond Austin-centric artists, showcasing their blend of jazzy, bluesy rock with country roots. "By that, I mean they had to include some Nashville bands and the first Nashville band to be on Austin City Limits was the Charlie Daniels band in 76." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 49 - Austin City Limits and the 70s Country Music Revolution | http://ftr70.com
**Prominent Feature in "Urban Cowboy" (1980)** : The Charlie Daniels Band, and specifically their song "The Devil Went Down to Georgia," were prominently featured in the final part of the 1980 movie "Urban Cowboy," particularly during the indoor rodeo scenes at Gilley's. This exposed their music and fiddle playing to a massive new audience across the United States. "Kids, if you like that fiddle music, you might just want to go check out Urban Cowboy, especially the last part of the movie that prominently features the Charlie Daniels band at that big indoor rodeo at Gillies." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 49 - Austin City Limits and the 70s Country Music Revolution | http://ftr70.com
**Uncategorizable Music (1980 Interview)** : In a 1980 interview, Charlie Daniels expressed difficulty in categorizing his band's music, stating, "It ain't country. It ain't rock." He criticized radio for categorizing things too closely, emphasizing his preference for variety. "I can't describe my music. It ain't country. It ain't rock. Radio categorizes things too closely. They seem to do, they see too much in black and white and I like variety in life." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 49 - Austin City Limits and the 70s Country Music Revolution | http://ftr70.com
**Versatility of Charlie Daniels** : Charlie Daniels was a well-respected and versatile musician. He played guitar and bass on Bob Dylan's classic "Nashville Skyline" and also "hung out with the Beatles," highlighting his broad musical connections and talents beyond his own band.
