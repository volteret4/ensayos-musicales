# artist - Larry Page

## members
- Larry Page the Teenage Rage (as a singer)

## curiosities
**Failed Singing Career ("The Teenage Rage")** : Before becoming a manager, Larry Page attempted a career as a British teen idol under the stage name "Larry Page the teenage rage." This career was short-lived and "somewhat sabotaged by his inability to sing," along with his producers' insistence on recording a particular song whose original version was deemed too poor to be a UK hit. "he was someone who had had a brief career as an attempt at producing a British teen idol, under the name Larry Page the teenage rage, a career that was somewhat sabotaged by his inability to sing." ← Episode 119： ＂You Really Got Me＂ by the Kinks | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_XNJ6Qwmh_c
**Influence on "You Really Got Me" Lyrics** : Larry Page made a minor but significant change to the lyrics of "You Really Got Me." Ray Davis had initially started the song with "yeah you really got me going," but Page suggested altering it to "girl you really got me going." This change aimed to enhance audience connection, add variety to the repetitive lyrics, and subtly address emerging questions about the group's sexuality. "Paige suggested that instead he sing, girl you really got me going. Partly to increase that sense of connection with the audience again, partly to add a tiny bit of variety to the repetitive lyrics, but also partly because the group's sexuality was already coming in for some question." ← Episode 119： ＂You Really Got Me＂ by the Kinks | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_XNJ6Qwmh_c
**Integrated Music Business Model** : Larry Page, Eddie Casna, Shell Tauhmey, and Decca's Dick Rowe operated in a close, "hand in glove" business model. Page managed artists, Casna published their songs, Tauhmey produced them, and Rowe signed them to his label. This comprehensive structure made contacting Page a virtual guarantee of a record deal for the Ravens (The Kinks). "Paige, Casta, Tauhmey and Rowe all worked hand in glove with each other. With Paige managing artists, Casta publishing the songs they recorded, Tauhmey producing them, and Rowe signing them to his record label." ← Episode 119： ＂You Really Got Me＂ by the Kinks | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_XNJ6Qwmh_c
**Intervention on "You Really Got Me" Original Recording** : After Shell Tauhmey's initial recording of "You Really Got Me" was deemed overproduced and unsuitable by Ray Davis, Larry Page intervened. Leveraging his and Eddie Casna's ownership of the publishing rights, Page "insisted that as he and Casna owned the publishing, the record couldn't come out in the state it was in," effectively forcing a re-recording session to produce the version the band wanted. **Naming of "The Kinks"** : Larry Page was responsible for naming the group "The Kinks." He saw an opportunity in the contemporary "kinky" buzzword, which arose from the Profumo sex scandal and was popularized by "Kinky Boots" in "The Avengers." He believed the name, especially given the "camp demeanor" of Dave Davis and Pete Quayfe, would create "plenty of outrage" but still be "just about broadcastable." "Page decided that this was too good an opportunity to miss... it would make sense to call the group The Kinks, as a name that would generate plenty of outrage, but was still just about broadcastable." ← Episode 119： ＂You Really Got Me＂ by the Kinks | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_XNJ6Qwmh_c

**Partnership with Eddie Casna and Shell Tauhmey** : After his singing career ended, Larry Page ventured into management, forming a "complicated business arrangement" with Austrian songwriter and publisher Eddie Casna and independent producer Shell Tauhmey. This arrangement saw Page manage artists, Casna publish their songs (including those written by Page's acts), and Tauhmey produce them, creating an integrated system to guarantee record deals. "Page and Casna had entered into a complicated business arrangement... **Songwriting Mentorship** : Larry Page actively worked to develop Ray and Dave Davis as songwriters, particularly Ray, teaching them the "craft of songwriting." His philosophy centered on creating a strong connection between the singer and audience, leading him to advise the brothers to use words like "I," "me," and "you" frequently in titles and lyrics.
