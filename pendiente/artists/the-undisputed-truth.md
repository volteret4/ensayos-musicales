## songs
**Smiling Faces Sometimes (1971)** : Written by Norman Whitfield, this song reached number three on the Billboard Hot 100 in 1971. Scholar and former disco DJ Alice Eccles credits "Smiling Faces Sometimes" as the first Black Power song to become a pop hit, indicating its crossover appeal to white audiences who purchased the record and played it on the radio. While ostensibly a song about general betrayal, its meaning depends on perspective; many African Americans interpreted it as a commentary on white people, particularly white liberals, cautioning against false friendships and hidden malice. ## curiosities
**Motown's Evolving Sound** : In 1971, "Smiling Faces Sometimes" signaled a departure for Motown from its established formula, adopting a distinctively funkier sound. This shift occurred at a time when Motown, like James Brown, faced criticism from African Americans for its perceived lack of political engagement, as Berry Gordy had historically been cautious about political statements in his music until the early 1970s.
