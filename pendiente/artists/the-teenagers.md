**Why Do Fools Fall in Love (1956)** : This song was the group's only major hit, but it became one of the biggest hits of the 1950s and remains almost universally known. It was originally meant to be sung by Herman Santiago, but was rearranged in the studio to feature Frankie Lymon's distinctive falsetto. The song reached number 6 on the pop charts, helped by a massive promotional push from Morris Levy's friend, Alan Freed, and outsold a white cover version by The Diamonds. "The Teenagers only had one really big hit, but it was one of the biggest hits of the 50s and it was a song that is almost universally known to this day." ← Episode 35： ＂Why Do Fools Fall In Love？＂ by Frankie Lymon and the Teenagers | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AtZf9PJEttM

## curiosities
**Behind-the-Scenes Production** : Much of the high-quality sound on The Teenagers' records was attributed to session saxophone player Jimmy Wright, not the group members or producer George Goldner. Wright ended up doing the arrangements for all of their records and was responsible for the distinctive bass intros performed by Sherman Garns. "Much of the work to make the record sound that good was done, not by the teenagers or by Goldner, but by the Session Saxophone player Jimmy Wright, who ended up doing the arrangements on all of the teenagers' records." ← Episode 35： ＂Why Do Fools Fall In Love？＂ by Frankie Lymon and the Teenagers | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AtZf9PJEttM
**Commercial Success Despite Cover Versions** : The Teenagers' version of "Why Do Fools Fall in Love" reached number 6 on the pop charts, outperforming an almost immediately released white cover version by the Canadian group The Diamonds, which only went to number 12. This indicated a changing market where the "novelty sound" of a young boy singing in falsetto was more appealing than adult cover artists. "But in a sign of how the times were changing, the Diamond's version of the song only went to number 12, while the teenagers version went to number 6." ← Episode 35： ＂Why Do Fools Fall In Love？＂ by Frankie Lymon and the Teenagers | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AtZf9PJEttM
**Discovery by Richie Barrett** : The group's career changed dramatically when Richie Barrett heard them singing on a street corner. Barrett, known for his 1962 single "Some Other Guy" and then the lead singer of The Valentines, was working as a talent scout and producer for George Goldner at Rama Records, a role akin to Ike Turner for Chess or Jesse Stone for Atlantic. **Exploitation and Lack of Financial Gain** : The story of Frankie Lymon and the Teenagers is a cautionary tale of young people of color achieving massive musical success but making no money from their work due to wealthy businessmen, like Morris Levy, who stole their intellectual property and used mafia connections to prevent legal action. **Formation and Early Names** : The group began when two Black teenagers from New York, Jimmy Merchant and Sherman Garns, left their previous vocal group, The Earth Angels (named after The Penguins' song). They then teamed up with two Latino neighbors, Joe Negroni and Herman Santiago. They initially called themselves The Hermans, later changing to The Coop Devils in honor of The Cadillacs, and then to The Premiers before becoming The Teenagers. "The Teenagers started when two black teenagers from New York, Jimmy Merchant and Sherman Garns, left the vocal group they'd formed... and hooked up with two Latino neighbours, Joe Negroni and Herman Santiago." ← Episode 35： ＂Why Do Fools Fall In Love？＂ by Frankie Lymon and the Teenagers | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AtZf9PJEttM
**Later Reunions and Members' Deaths** : The Teenagers briefly reunited with Frankie Lymon in 1965, but with little success. In the 1970s, they reunited again, briefly including Lymon's brother in the lineup. Sherman Garns passed away in 1977, followed by Joe Negroni in 1978. Herman Santiago and Jimmy Merchant continued performing with various iterations of The Teenagers, with Santiago still active today and Merchant remaining with the band until his retirement a few years prior. **Name Change by George Goldner** : When their debut record was released, it was credited to "The Teenagers featuring Frankie Lymon," rather than The Premiers. George Goldner disliked their prior name and decided to emphasize their youth, particularly that of their new lead singer, Frankie Lymon, as their main selling point. **Post-Lymon Struggles and Lineup Changes** : After Frankie Lymon left, The Teenagers struggled to regain their success. They introduced several new lead singers, including Billy LeBvano, a white kid who sounded like Freddie Houston, and even tried focusing on other original members, performing as "Sherman and the Teenagers." **Record Label's Decision to Separate Lymon** : The record label made the unusual decision to promote Frankie Lymon as a solo star, separating him from The Teenagers, rather than maintaining a successful group. **Songwriting Origin from Love Letters** : The group used to practice in the hallway of Sherman Garns' apartment block. A neighbor, annoyed by their repetitive singing, offered them his girlfriend's love letters, some written as poems, suggesting they turn them into songs. This led to the creation of "Why Do Fools Fall in Love" from the phrase "wider bird sing so gay."
