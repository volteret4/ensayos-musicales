## concerts
- **Carnegie Hall (1955)** : The group reunited after being blacklisted from radio and TV and performed a concert at Carnegie Hall in 1955, which resulted in a hit live album that included their version of "Wimoweh." "The groups split up, though they did get back together again a few years later, and managed to have a hit live album of a concert they performed at Carnegie Hall in 1955, which also included Wimmer Way."

## curiosities
**Blacklisting During McCarthy Era** : Both Pete Seeger and Lee Hayes were named as past Communist Party members and called before the House on American Activities Committee to testify. As a result, The Weavers were blacklisted from radio and TV, causing "Wimoweh" to drop off the chart and their recording catalogue to be deleted. "Wimmer Way went to number 6 on the charts, but then sent it to McCarthy Stepton." ← Episode 92： ＂The Lion Sleeps Tonight＂ by the Tokens | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QMP_DFyktMg
**Commercial Success** : The Weavers achieved far more commercial success than The Almanac Singers, recording hit versions of songs like "Good Night Irene" and "Wimoweh." "The weavers immediately had far more commercial success than the Almanac singers ever had, and recorded such hits as their version of Lead Belly's Good Night Irene, with orchestration by Gordon Jenkins." ← Episode 92： ＂The Lion Sleeps Tonight＂ by the Tokens | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QMP_DFyktMg
**Continuation of The Almanac Singers** : The Weavers were formed by Pete Seeger and Lee Hayes more or less as a continuation of The Almanac Singers, but with a less explicitly political agenda. They performed songs by Woody Guthrie and Lead Belly, and their own material, without being tied to a specific ideological line. "But the core of the group had been Seeger and Lee Hayes, and those two had eventually formed another group, more or less as a continuation of the Almanac singers, but with a less explicitly political agenda." ← Episode 92： ＂The Lion Sleeps Tonight＂ by the Tokens | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QMP_DFyktMg
**Dylan's Acknowledgment of Influence** : Bob Dylan would sometimes introduce his own song "Let Me Die In My Footsteps," a successful early composition about nuclear war, as a Weavers' song. "His first truly successful song, a song about nuclear war, let me die in my footsteps. He would introduce as a weaver's song." ← Episode 97： ＂Song to Woody＂ by Bob Dylan | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3DufLVcZyc4
**Eric Darling Replaces Seeger** : Following Pete Seeger's departure, he suggested Eric Darling from the folk group The Tarriers as his replacement. Darling, an "Ironman fan and a Libertarian," presumably held different views on advertising than Seeger. "At his suggestion, he was replaced by Eric Darling from another folk group that Tarias." ← Episode 92： ＂The Lion Sleeps Tonight＂ by the Tokens | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QMP_DFyktMg
**Pete Seeger's Departure Over Tobacco Commercial** : After reuniting for a hit live album, Pete Seeger permanently left the group a couple of years later when they did a commercial for tobacco. While the group saw it as an opportunity for exposure due to being blacklisted, Seeger disapproved of tobacco and advertising. He did appear in the commercial due to a prior commitment, not wanting to break his word. "Seeger left the group permanently a couple of years after that, when they did a commercial for tobacco." ← Episode 92： ＂The Lion Sleeps Tonight＂ by the Tokens | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QMP_DFyktMg
**Spawned from Armanac House** : The Weavers emerged from the environment of left-wing intellectuals and folk singers centered in Armanac House in Greenwich Village, alongside The Armanac Singers.
