**Suzy Q (1957)** : This song achieved significant commercial success, reaching number 7 on the R&B chart and number 27 on the pop charts. It was recorded in four takes by engineer Bob Sullivan, with Hawkins choosing an early take that captured an audible quick volume adjustment on his vocal. The final version featured James Burton's biting guitar riff and Ronnie Lewis's cowbell-infused drum part, inspired by AJ Tomanello's demo performance. ## curiosities
**"Suzy Q" Musical Synthesis** : The song "Suzy Q" was a blend of various musical sources; its overall sound was influenced by Muddy Waters' "Baby Please Don't Go," its melody by The Clovers, and its guitar riff by Howlin' Wolf, suggesting it evolved from jam sessions despite conflicting authorship claims. "The most obvious influence for its overall sound. Both the melody and the way the melody interacts with the guitar riff. Is Baby Please Don't Go by Marty Waters." ← Episode 63： ＂Susie Q＂, by Dale Hawkins | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4vqj1DcXOfw
**"Suzy Q" Songwriting Credit Discrepancy** : Hawkins claimed that not only were the songwriting credits for "Suzy Q" wrongly attributed (to Stan Lewis and Delna Roadwater), but he also did not receive any royalties until 1985. Crucially, co-writers like James Burton received no credit. "But according to Hawkins, not only did the credit get split with the wrong people, but he didn't receive any of the royalties to which he was entitled until as late as 1985." ← Episode 63： ＂Susie Q＂, by Dale Hawkins | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4vqj1DcXOfw
**Academic Achievement** : In the early 1980s, after getting clean, Hawkins achieved a significant personal milestone by earning his master's degree, an accomplishment he was as proud of as any hit record, especially considering he was a high school dropout. "as well as finally getting his master's degree, which for a high school dropout was a major achievement and something to be as proud of as any hit." ← Episode 63： ＂Susie Q＂, by Dale Hawkins | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4vqj1DcXOfw
**Amphetamine Addiction and Recovery** : Hawkins battled an amphetamine addiction throughout much of the 1970s, having started using speed in the 1950s. He successfully got clean in the early 1980s, subsequently performing on the Rockabilly Revival circuit. "But after a successful few years, he spent much of the 70s suffering from an emphetamine addiction, having started taking speed back in the 50s." ← Episode 63： ＂Susie Q＂, by Dale Hawkins | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4vqj1DcXOfw
**Chess Records' Delayed Release of "Suzy Q"** : Chess Records inexplicably delayed the release of "Suzy Q" for 10 months after receiving the master. Hawkins, frustrated, sent a copy to Jerry Wexler at Atlantic Records, who expressed interest, which then prompted Chess to recognize its commercial potential and finally release it on Checker. **Country Music Family Background** : Hawkins came from a country music family; his father, Dalmar Hawkins Sr, was a country musician and bass player, possibly an original member of the Sons of the Pioneers, and his younger brother Jerry was a rockabilly who released singles in the 1950s. Despite this, Dale felt he lacked the country element necessary for the rockabilly label. "His father, Dalmar Horkin's senior, was a country musician who was, according to some sources, one of the original members of the sons of the pioneers, the group that launched the Come Here." ← Episode 63： ＂Susie Q＂, by Dale Hawkins | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4vqj1DcXOfw

**Name Change to "Del Hawkins"** : During the release of "See You Soon, Baboon," Chess Records' Leonard Chess misheard "Dale Hawkins" over the phone, leading to his first name being misspelled as "Del" on the record. This spelling then became his professional name from that point forward. "Leonard Chess misheard Del Hawkins over the phone, and the record came out as by Del Hawkins, which would be his name from that point on." ← Episode 63： ＂Susie Q＂, by Dale Hawkins | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4vqj1DcXOfw
**Paradoxical Musical Legacy** : Dale Hawkins is considered a paradoxical figure: he foresaw the future of rock and roll where white artists adapted black blues ideas, yet his own biggest hit, "Suzy Q," was meticulously crafted from various musical fragments, despite his reputation for raw, instinctive rock and roll. "He was in many ways a paradoxical figure." ← Episode 63： ＂Susie Q＂, by Dale Hawkins | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4vqj1DcXOfw
**Pioneering Electric Bass Use in Rock and Roll** : For the released version of "Suzy Q," Hawkins consciously chose an electric bass, played by Sonny Tramble (the only person in Shreveport to own one), to emulate the sound of electric blues records, differentiating it from the double bass typically associated with country and rockabilly music. "This was a deliberate choice by Hawkins, who wanted to imitate the sound of electric blues records, rather than using the double bass, which he associated with country music." ← Episode 63： ＂Susie Q＂, by Dale Hawkins | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4vqj1DcXOfw
**Presaging 1960s White Rock Acts** : Dale Hawkins is argued to be a pivotal figure, possibly the first white rock and roller to be directly inspired by black blues musicians like Muddy Waters and Howlin' Wolf. His song "Suzy Q," with its electric bass, Chess-style guitar riffs, and country-inflected vocals, is considered a precursor to 1960s white rock acts like The Rolling Stones and The Animals. "One can make an argument that Dale Hawkins was the first white Wuck and Ruler to be inspired by people like Waters and Wolf, and for Susie Q as the record, more than any other, that presaged the white rock acts of the 60s." ← Episode 63： ＂Susie Q＂, by Dale Hawkins | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4vqj1DcXOfw
**Record Shop Expertise and Friendships** : While beginning his music career, Hawkins worked at Stan's Record Shop, where he became deeply familiar with nearly every blues and R&B record available. This job also led to a lifelong friendship with customers like B.B. King. "It was through doing this job that Hawkins became friendly with customers like Bibi King, who would remain a lifelong friend." ← Episode 63： ＂Susie Q＂, by Dale Hawkins | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4vqj1DcXOfw
**Self-Identified Bluesman** : Dale Hawkins consistently thought of himself as a bluesman, rather than a rockabilly, a description he disliked. While living in Shreveport, Louisiana, he resided in a shack in the black part of town, possessing only a folding camp bed, a record player, and thousands of 78 RPM blues records. "When he was living in Shreveport, Louisiana, he lived in a shack in the black part of town, and inside the shack there was only a folding camp bed, a record player, and thousands of 78 RPM blues records." ← Episode 63： ＂Susie Q＂, by Dale Hawkins | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4vqj1DcXOfw
