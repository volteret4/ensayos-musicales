## songs
**Come and Get These Memories (Year unknown)** : Released five months after "I'll Have to Let Him Go," this song is widely considered the beginning of the "Motown sound" and the start of Motown's true golden age, so much so that Brian and Eddie Holland's autobiography is named after it. It was primarily written by Lamont Dozier, who was inspired by country singers like Loretta Lynn, and features a lyrical style using physical objects as metaphors for emotional states. The song reached number 29 on the pop chart and number 6 on the R&B chart, establishing Martha and the Van Dellers as stars. "Come and get these memories, made number 29 on the pop chart, and number 6 on the Warren B chart." ← Episode 111： ＂Heat Wave＂ by Martha and the Vandellas | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AAiUJhSKjzs
**Dancing in the Street (Year unknown)** : Written by Mickey Stevenson, Marvin Gaye, and Ivy Joe Hunter, this song became the group's most remembered record and their cultural and commercial high point, reaching number two on the charts. Marvin Gaye played drums, and Ivy Joe Hunter provided prominent percussion using either a steel chain or hitting a tire. Martha Reeves' "astonishing" vocal, with an "edge," was reportedly from a second take, as she sounded annoyed after nailing the first take, only to find a recording problem. Two years after its release, in the wake of civil rights protests, the song gained notoriety when it was misinterpreted as an incitement to riot, an idea later referenced by The Rolling Stones in "Street Fighting Man." "That went to number two in the chart, and will be the group's cultural and commercial high point." ← Episode 111： ＂Heat Wave＂ by Martha and the Vandellas | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AAiUJhSKjzs
**Heat Wave (Year unknown)** : The follow-up to "Come and Get These Memories," this Holland-Dozier-Holland song, again stemming from an idea by Lamont Dozier, is described as possibly Motown's most exciting record to that point. While "Come and Get These Memories" established the Motown sound, "Heat Wave" specifically established the Martha and the Van Dellers' sound and style, which Holland-Dozier-Holland would apply to many up-tempo productions. Its energetic rhythm, featuring drums accenting the backbeat and tambourine on every 4/4 beat, is credited as a precursor to Northern Soul, disco, high energy, electro-pop, the 80s hit factory sound, and much dance music. The song highlights Martha Reeves' powerful, blues and gospel-influenced voice, making it a top 10 hit. **I'll Have to Let Him Go (Year unknown)** : This song was originally being produced by Mickey Stevenson for Mary Wells. During a surprise union inspection, Mary Wells was absent, so Stevenson grabbed his secretary, Martha Reeves, to sing lead for the musicians. Martha performed with full effort, impressing Stevenson, who decided to give the song to her. It was then recorded with new vocals by Martha and the Van Dellers (with Gloria on lead for the backing track), and its release marked Martha's definitive leadership in the group. ## curiosities
**Annette Beard's Departure** : Annette Beard left the group after two Holland-Dozier-Holland records ("Heat Wave" and "Quicksand") and two subsequent singles failed to break the top 40. She was replaced by Betty Kelly. "a net left, being replaced by Betty Kelly." ← Episode 111： ＂Heat Wave＂ by Martha and the Vandellas | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AAiUJhSKjzs
**Backing Vocalists for Marvin Gaye** : The Van Dellers became Marvin Gaye's backing vocalists, both in the studio and for shows like the Motortown Revue. This gave them early exposure and allowed them to perform with many other artists. "Van Dellers were now also Marvin Gaye's backing vocalists, and it shows like the Muggeda Town review shows, as well as performing their own set, the Van Dellers would sing with Gaye as well." ← Episode 111： ＂Heat Wave＂ by Martha and the Vandellas | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AAiUJhSKjzs
**Classic Lineup Members Still Alive** : Martha and the Van Dellers are noted as one of the very few 1960s hitmaking groups where all members of their classic lineup (Martha, Rosalind, Betty, Annette, and Lois) are still alive and performing, despite Gloria Williams' death in 2000. "Martha and the Van Della's are one of the very few 60s hitmaking groups, where all the members of their classic lineup are still alive and performing." ← Episode 111： ＂Heat Wave＂ by Martha and the Vandellas | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AAiUJhSKjzs
**Gloria Williams' Departure** : Gloria Williams left the group around the time "I'll Have to Let Him Go" was released, after Martha Reeves became the definitive leader. Reasons suggested include Gloria's disapproval of Martha's leadership or her desire to keep a stable job with the city rather than risk it for a full-time singing career. **Greatest Motown Girl Group** : The Van Dellers are recognized as one of the greatest Motown acts and girl groups, with their biggest hits standing up against any contemporary Motown recordings, still embodying "the Sound of Young America" nearly 60 years later. **Name Origin** : After being signed to Motown, Barry Gordy wanted a new name for The Delphiys. They first went by The Dominettes. Martha Reeves then suggested "Martha and the Van Dellers," combining Van Dyke Street (where she lived) and Della Reese (her favorite singer). Rosalind Ashford disputes Martha's role in naming, stating the group was initially just "The Van Dellers" with Gloria as lead. "Martha suggested a name based on the street she lived on, Van Dyke Street, and Delerie's Martha's favourite singer, who had hits like, what do you know? The group became Martha and the Van Dellers." ← Episode 111： ＂Heat Wave＂ by Martha and the Vandellas | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AAiUJhSKjzs
**Post-Motown Split and Touring Groups** : When Motown moved from Detroit to LA in the early 1970s, Martha Reeves chose not to relocate, leading to the group's split. Currently, there are two touring versions: "Martha Reeves and the Van Dellers" (Martha and her sisters, including Lois Reeves) and "The Original Van Dellers" (Rosalind Ashford and Annette Beard). **Rock and Roll Hall of Fame Induction** : The classic lineup members Martha Reeves, Rosalind Ashford, Betty Kelly, Annette Beard, and Lois Reeves were inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 1995, marking them as only the second all-female group to receive this honor.
