**Stand By Your Man** : This song could have become a joke due to Tammy Wynette's tumultuous marriage to George Jones, but she performed it as a genuine love song, which resonated with audiences. The song faced criticism for seemingly advocating for women to tolerate bad treatment, but it also contains a subtle message about men's inherent weakness, encapsulated in the line, "after all, he's just a man." "When Weinette sang, stand by your man, which could have easily turned into a joke after she married George Jones, it didn't because she sang it like a love song." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 23 - Women of Country Music in the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/023-country-women-72820-2-45-pm
**Till I Can Make It On My Own (1976)** : This song was Tammy Wynette's favorite and became her 15th number one hit on the country charts in 1976. She co-wrote it with producer Billy Cheryl and her then-husband, George Richie. The lyrics express profound sadness and dependency, reflecting aspects of her difficult personal life. "That went to number one on the country charts in 1976. ## curiosities
**Academy of Country Entertainers Co-Founder** : Following Charlie Rich's antics at the 1975 CMA Awards, Tammy Wynette and George Jones hosted a gathering of country musicians to discuss how to address artists deemed "not country" winning awards, leading to the formation of the Academy of Country Entertainers. "George Jones and his wife Tammy Wynette, Country Music royalty, hosted a gathering of other country musicians at their house and discussed what can we do about singers who many decided were not country and they were winning country music awards." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 2 - Countryish Music of the 1970s (Encore) | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Academy's Short Lifespan Linked to Marriage** : The Academy of Country Entertainers, co-founded by Tammy Wynette, did not last long because her marriage to George Jones "didn't last much longer than that." **Domestic Abuse and Drug Problems (1970s)** : Tammy Wynette's personal life was fraught with difficulties. Her daughter claimed that her husband, George Richie, abused her. In 1977, Wynette made claims of being kidnapped and beaten, which many suspected were a cover-up for abuse from Richie, particularly after photographs of her beaten face and burn marks on her neck surfaced. Concurrently, she struggled with pill addiction, similar to Elvis, to the extent that her band often didn't know which version of Tammy would appear for a show. "Her daughter claims what many people suspected that Richie abused Tammy, and when she made these really wild, hard to believe claims that she had been kidnapped and beaten in 1977." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 23 - Women of Country Music in the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/023-country-women-72820-2-45-pm
**Early Dominance (1960s)** : In the 1960s, Tammy Wynette was a dominant female artist in country music, responsible for eight of the 14 number one records by women during that decade. "There were 14 number one records by women in the 60s and eight of those belonged to Tammy Wynette." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 23 - Women of Country Music in the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/023-country-women-72820-2-45-pm
**Grammy Female Country Vocalist Nomination (1974)** : Tammy Wynett was nominated for a Grammy for Female Country Vocalist of the Year in 1974, alongside Olivia Newton-John, Maria Osmond, Barbara Fairchild, and Dottie West. "Maria Osmond, Barbara Fairchild, Tammy Wynett, and Dottie West." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 40 - Honestly Loving Olivia Newton-John | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-40-honestly-loving-olivia-newton-john
**Living Her Songs** : Tammy Wynette stated in her memoir that she believed artists had to "live the songs" they sang, a sentiment reflected in her emotionally charged performances of tracks like "Stand By Your Man" and "D-I-V-O-R-C-E." Given the sadness and difficulties in her own life, this was seen as a deeply personal conviction. "Weinette wrote in her memoir, I believe you have to live the songs." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 23 - Women of Country Music in the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/023-country-women-72820-2-45-pm
**Protest Against Country-Pop Crossover (1974)** : In 1974, Tammy Wynette and George Jones famously left the Country Music Association in protest over Olivia Newton-John's success and her Grammy win, viewing her as not "country enough" due to the blurring lines between country and pop.
