# artist - Norman Petty

## genres
- Jazz (early career)
- Rock and Roll

## instruments
- Echo Chamber
- Keyboards (sometimes played by his wife)

## curiosities
**Assuming "That'll Be the Day" was a Demo (1957)** : When Norman Petty shopped the recordings for "That'll Be the Day" to different labels, he initially assumed they were merely demos, but Bob Thiele of Brunswick Records believed the tracks were already polished enough for immediate release. "Norman Petty had assumed that what they'd recorded so far was just going to be a demo, but Thiel said that no, he thought what they had was fine as it was, and put this out." ← Episode Sixty-One： ＂That'll Be the Day＂, by the Crickets | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KVJMKLc54O0
**Contract Deception (Year not specified)** : Petty secretly structured Buddy Holly's recording contract so that Holly was recording for Petty's company, which then sold the masters to other labels like Brunswick and Coral, allowing Petty to collect all royalties and leave The Crickets' bank account empty. "By the terms of buddy's contract, he hadn't really been recording for Buns Recall Coral, so they didn't tell him a penny. He'd been recording for Petty's company, who then sold the masters onto the other labels and would get all the royalties." ← Episode 74： ＂It Doesn't Matter Any More＂ by Buddy Holly | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6unib5T2mDQ
**Death and Unreleased Project (1984)** : Norman Petty died in 1984. His final project was an unreleased attempt to update Buddy Holly's biggest hits using synthesizers. "Norm and Petty died in 1984. His last project was a still unreleased updating of Buddy's biggest hits with synthesizers." ← Episode 74： ＂It Doesn't Matter Any More＂ by Buddy Holly | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6unib5T2mDQ
**Devising a Contract Solution for Holly (1957)** : Faced with Buddy Holly's Decca contract prohibiting him from re-recording 1956 songs for five years, Norman Petty secretly recorded a conversation with Paul Cohen to understand the specifics. He then proposed releasing Holly's new recordings under a group name, The Crickets, to circumvent the legal issue. "So Norman Petty, who had been secretly recording the conversation, suggested a way around the problem." ← Episode Sixty-One： ＂That'll Be the Day＂, by the Crickets | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KVJMKLc54O0
**Early Overdubbing Experiments (Late 1940s)** : Norman Petty was a former musician who, in the late 1940s, conducted "crude experiments in overdubbing," albeit in a less sophisticated manner than pioneers like Les Paul and Mary Ford. "Petty was a former musician who had performed crude experiments in overdubbing in the late 40s, copying Les Paul and Mary Ford, though in a much less sophisticated manner." ← Episode Sixty-One： ＂That'll Be the Day＂, by the Crickets | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KVJMKLc54O0
**Echo Chamber Construction** : Norman Petty successfully built his own echo chamber, completing it with the assistance of Buddy Holly, Holly's father, and Holly's brother, demonstrating a collaborative effort in developing his studio's technical capabilities. "started to build his own Echo Chamber, something that would eventually be completed with the help of Buddy Holly and Buddy's father and brother." ← Episode Sixty-One： ＂That'll Be the Day＂, by the Crickets | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KVJMKLc54O0
**Financial Exploitation (Year not specified)** : Petty handled all the money for The Crickets, giving them only pocket money and withholding their actual shares. He later manipulated the financial split to give Buddy Holly 50% and Joe Mauldin/Jerry Allison 25% each, while still controlling all funds. He also made false promises of church donations. "Petty was handling all the money and only giving the group pocket money, rather than giving them their actual shares of the money they'd earned." ← Episode 74： ＂It Doesn't Matter Any More＂ by Buddy Holly | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6unib5T2mDQ
**Frequent Songwriting Credit Inclusion** : Norman Petty almost always ended up with his name on the songwriting credits for songs written by Buddy Holly, Jerry Allison, and sometimes Joe B. Mauldin, despite their primary creative input. "almost always Norman Petty would end up with his name on the songwriting credits." ← Episode Sixty-One： ＂That'll Be the Day＂, by the Crickets | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KVJMKLc54O0
**Initial Impression of Holly and Curtis** : Petty was impressed by Buddy Holly during their demo sessions, but he was even more impressed by Sonny Curtis, who was still with Buddy at that time, highlighting Curtis's strong musical presence. "Petty was impressed by Holly, though he was more impressed by Sonny Curtis, who was still with Buddy for those demo sessions." ← Episode Sixty-One： ＂That'll Be the Day＂, by the Crickets | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KVJMKLc54O0
**Interest in Rockabilly and Echo (1950s)** : Petty's interest in rockabilly was sparked after recording Roy Orbison and The Teen Kings' first single, "Ooby Dooby." He became fascinated by the possibilities of echo after hearing Sam Phillips' remake of the song, which inspired him to build his own echo chamber. "When he heard Sam Phillips' remake of the song, he became intrigued by the possibilities that Echo offered and started to build his own Echo Chamber." ← Episode Sixty-One： ＂That'll Be the Day＂, by the Crickets | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KVJMKLc54O0
**Minor Hit with "Mood Indigo"** : Petty achieved a minor hit with his version of Duke Ellington's "Mood Indigo," demonstrating some early success in his musical career before transitioning to studio work. "One of his singles, a version of Duke Ellington's mood indigo, had actually been a minor hit." ← Episode Sixty-One： ＂That'll Be the Day＂, by the Crickets | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KVJMKLc54O0
**Misogyny and Manipulation (Year not specified)** : Petty expressed extreme misogynistic slurs against Buddy Holly's new wife, Maria Elena Santiago, and manipulated Jerry Allison and Joe Mauldin against Holly by claiming Maria Elena was controlling him and that they couldn't survive in New York without Petty. "Petty used some extremely misogynistic slurs I'm not going to repeat here against his new wife." ← Episode 74： ＂It Doesn't Matter Any More＂ by Buddy Holly | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6unib5T2mDQ
**Posthumous Riches (1959-1984)** : After Buddy Holly's death, Norman Petty became very rich by exploiting his stockpile of unreleased Holly recordings and by securing an agreement with Holly's estate to overdub new backing tracks onto Holly's acoustic demos, transforming them into full-band songs. "Suddenly, his stockpile of unreleased Buddy Holly recordings was a gold mine, and not only that, he ended up coming to an agreement with Holly's estate that he could take all those demos Holly had recorded, and overdub new backing tracks on them, turning them into full-blown rock-and-roll songs." ← Episode 74： ＂It Doesn't Matter Any More＂ by Buddy Holly | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6unib5T2mDQ
**Producer and Manager (Year not specified)** : Norman Petty served as the producer for Buddy Holly and The Crickets, and later became their manager. His wife sometimes contributed by adding keyboards to their recordings. "producer Norman Petty and his wife, sometimes adding keyboards." ← Episode 74： ＂It Doesn't Matter Any More＂ by Buddy Holly | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6unib5T2mDQ
**Producing a Number 1 Hit** : Petty recorded another rockabilly group, Buddy Knox and the Rhythm Orchids, on their song "Party Doll," which went to number 1, solidifying his reputation as a successful rockabilly producer. "Petty recorded another rockabilly group, Buddy Knox and the Vithermorkids, on a song, Petty style that went to number 1." ← Episode Sixty-One： ＂That'll Be the Day＂, by the Crickets | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KVJMKLc54O0
**Recording Studio Business Model** : Petty established a recording studio business where he charged bands $60 to record two songs. However, if he deemed the songs commercially viable, he would waive the fee in exchange for publishing rights and a co-writing credit. "He'd gone into the recording studio business, and charged bands $60 to record two songs in his studio, or if he thought the songs had commercial potential, he'd waive the charge if they gave him the publishing and a co-writing credit." ← Episode Sixty-One： ＂That'll Be the Day＂, by the Crickets | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KVJMKLc54O0
