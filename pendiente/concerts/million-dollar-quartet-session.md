# concert - Million Dollar Quartet Session

## curiosities
**Best Moment: Elvis's "That's When Your Heart Aches Again"** : The very best moment of the session is identified as Elvis Presley's solo version of "That's When Your Heart Aches Again." This performance, originally recorded by Elvis three years prior, showcased the significant maturation of his voice and improvement in his performance since his first recording at Sun Studios. "To me, the very best moment in the whole session comes near the end. **Date and Participants (December 4, 1956)** : This legendary jam session took place at Sun Studios on December 4, 1956. It famously involved Carl Perkins, Elvis Presley, and Jerry Lee Lewis, with Johnny Cash's participation disputed but likely present. The event began when Elvis walked in during Perkins' "Matchbox" recording session. "But on December 4, 1956, Perkins was still working for some, and so he was in the studio with his brothers, recording another single that was destined to do very little." ← Episode 51： ＂Matchbox＂ by Carl Perkins | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0Nrjr0AGd3U
**Dominance of Elvis and Jerry Lee** : The recordings of the Million Dollar Quartet session are "completely dominated by Elvis Presley and Jerry Lee Lewis," with Carl Perkins a "distant third." While Perkins' backing band played on early tracks, the majority features Elvis on acoustic guitar or piano, Jerry Lee on piano, and both sharing most lead vocals. **Historical Document vs. Great Music** : The Million Dollar Quartet tape is described as an "interesting historical document" rather than consistently great music, except for specific "electrifying" moments when the musicians, at the height of their powers, played for pure pleasure. **Impact on Jerry Lee Lewis's Career** : The session "might well have been the making of Jerry Lee Lewis." Despite the recordings being released decades later, Sam Phillips used the event for extensive publicity, which positioned Lewis as an equal to Elvis Presley, Carl Perkins, and Johnny Cash, significantly boosting his career. "The million dollar quartet session might well have been the making of Jerry Lee Lewis, even though the recordings weren't released until decades later." ← Episode 51： ＂Matchbox＂ by Carl Perkins | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0Nrjr0AGd3U
**Intense Musical Rivalry and Joy** : The session is characterized by two immense talents, Elvis Presley and Jerry Lee Lewis, "sizing each other up, out to each other," while also sharing joy in making music. This friendly rivalry is evident in Jerry Lee Lewis's swift takeover of lead vocals from Elvis on gospel songs and Carl Perkins' efforts to outplay Lewis on guitar. "But what this really is is two immense talents, both trying to size each other up, out to each other, and also at the same time share in their joy at making music together." ← Episode 51： ＂Matchbox＂ by Carl Perkins | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0Nrjr0AGd3U
**Musical Content** : The music played during the session consisted of surprisingly little pure rock and roll. Instead, a "substantial proportion" was pure country music or Southern Gospel. They did play Chuck Berry's "Brown-Eyed Handsome Man" and discussed "Too Much Monkey Business," and Elvis sang a line of Little Richard's "Rip It Up." "The interesting thing about the music they play is how little of it is actually rockin' roll." ← Episode 51： ＂Matchbox＂ by Carl Perkins | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0Nrjr0AGd3U
