## curiosities
**"Free Form, Country Folk, Rock, Science Fiction, Gospel, Gum, Existential, Bluegrass, Guacamole, Opera Music"** : Steve Fromholz coined this descriptive phrase, which appeared on Bill Arhose's business card, to encapsulate the incredibly diverse and eclectic range of music Austin City Limits aimed to showcase, extending far beyond conventional genre boundaries. "Steve from Holtz came up with this phrase that ended up on the business card of Bill Arhose to describe what Austin City Limits was doing. Its free form, country folk, rock, science fiction, gospel, gum, existential, bluegrass, guacamole, opera music." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 49 - Austin City Limits and the 70s Country Music Revolution | http://ftr70.com
**Early Diverse Musical Scope (Season 2)** : Austin City Limits began expanding its musical horizons significantly in Season 2, featuring artists like Merle Haggard, Jimmy Buffett, the Earl Scruggs Review, Firefall (described as "Yacht Rock"), Gatemouth Brown, and Delbert McClinton. However, producer Terry Lickona noted in 1979 that the show might have broadened its scope too quickly and planned to return to a more traditional country/Western guest list. "Austin City Limits is expanding its horizons a bit in this season. Firefall is not country. I think today the kids would call it Yacht Rock, I think." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 49 - Austin City Limits and the 70s Country Music Revolution | http://ftr70.com
**Early Expansion Beyond Texas (1970s)** : To secure continued funding, Austin City Limits, under Bill Arhose, successfully convinced PBS stations outside of Texas, including in New York, to broadcast the show, demonstrating its early efforts to broaden its reach beyond its initial regional focus. "Of course, now, the key to continue to be funded year after year is to get PBS stations outside of Texas, including in places like New York, to convince them that a show featuring this country-ish music was something that they should have on the air too and Arho's was able to do that." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 49 - Austin City Limits and the 70s Country Music Revolution | http://ftr70.com
**Embracing the Definition of Country Music Debate** : Austin City Limits has historically embraced the ongoing debate about what constitutes country music, taking a stance that values whether the music is liked by its audience rather than adhering to rigid genre definitions. This open-minded approach is exemplified by its diverse programming, even in the face of new artists like Beyoncé releasing country-inspired albums. "What we can appreciate about Austin's Sydney limits is that it seems to have embraced that debate and said something like this. What is the music? Do you like it? Is that good enough?" ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 49 - Austin City Limits and the 70s Country Music Revolution | http://ftr70.com
**Focus on Authenticity and Music Quality** : From its inception, Austin City Limits has prioritized the music itself, with musicians historically paid minimum scale wages, indicating that artistic quality is paramount over commercial spectacle. The show aims to reflect the natural, unlaundered, and sometimes "raunchy" aesthetic of the Austin music scene. "From the beginning, the show has always been about the music. I mean, look at it this way. The musicians who perform for the show then and now are paid scale, they're paid the minimum. The quality is paramount." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 49 - Austin City Limits and the 70s Country Music Revolution | http://ftr70.com
**Founder Bill Arhose** : Bill Arhose, a producer for Austin's public broadcasting station KRLU, was the founder of Austin City Limits. He had prior experience creating the TV show "Sessions," which featured acts like REO Speedwagon and Billy Joel, providing him with the expertise to make Austin City Limits a reality. "The founder of Austin City Limits was Bill Arhose, who started his career as a producer for KRLU, a public broadcasting station in Austin." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 49 - Austin City Limits and the 70s Country Music Revolution | http://ftr70.com
**Longest-Running Music Series (1974-Present)** : Austin City Limits is recognized as the longest-running music series in television history, celebrating its 50th anniversary in the fall of 2024. It debuted in the fall of 1974, ten years after The Beatles' arrival in the US. "Soon to be celebrating its 50th anniversary, this is the 50th anniversary year, but this fall celebrating its 50th anniversary and I'm talking about Austin City Limits." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 49 - Austin City Limits and the 70s Country Music Revolution | http://ftr70.com
**Longevity Attributed to Artists and Audiences** : A panel of long-time associates, including producer Terry Lickona, attributed Austin City Limits' enduring success and longevity to its unwavering focus on the artists and the audiences. This commitment to the core elements of live music has allowed it to adapt and remain relevant for five decades. "Listening to them, the answer that they give seems to be pretty similar here, that it's always been about the artists and the audiences." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 49 - Austin City Limits and the 70s Country Music Revolution | http://ftr70.com
**National Medal of Arts (2003)** : In 2003, Austin City Limits was awarded the National Medal of Arts, a singular achievement as it remains the only television show ever to receive this honor. This recognition speaks to its profound cultural and artistic impact. "In 2003 Austin City Limits was awarded the National Medal of Arts the only television show to ever receive that honor." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 49 - Austin City Limits and the 70s Country Music Revolution | http://ftr70.com
**New Studio and Annual Music Festival (Present)** : Austin City Limits has evolved into a major brand with a new, larger studio located on Willie Nelson Boulevard, featuring a statue of Willie Nelson outside. It also hosts an annual music festival that showcases a wide variety of music, from the Foo Fighters to Kendrick Lamar, Shania Twain, and Tanya Tucker in 2023. "Austin City Limits now is it's a whole brand. They have a new bigger studio on Willie Nelson Boulevard. There's a statue of Willie out front. **Originated as a Fundraiser** : Austin City Limits initially began its life as a fundraiser for Austin's local PBS station. PBS, being a nonprofit network, often relies on concert performances and special programming during fundraising periods. "That's how Austin City Limits began. **Originators Bill Scafe and Paul Bosner** : The initial idea for Austin City Limits was conceived by local musician Bill Scafe and TV producer Paul Bosner. They pitched the concept to Bill Arhose, who then wrote the proposal and became instrumental in bringing the show to life. "In fact, it was an idea that was created by a local musician named Bill Scafe and a TV producer, Paul Bosner, who pitched it to Arhose and then Arhose wrote the proposal." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 49 - Austin City Limits and the 70s Country Music Revolution | http://ftr70.com
**PBS Platform (1974)** : Austin City Limits found its home on PBS, a commercial-free television network established in 1970 as a product of the 1967 Public Broadcasting Act. At the time of its debut in 1974, PBS was in need of programming and open to innovative ideas. **Refuge for Austin Sound (1970s)** : Austin City Limits emerged from a flourishing music scene in Austin, Texas, in the early 1970s, which served as a refuge for country artists seeking to experiment and escape Nashville's polished sound. The show aimed to capture the "Austin Sound," characterized by authenticity, lack of show business antics, and minimal slick electronic techniques.
