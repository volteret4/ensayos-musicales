# concert - The Troubadour

## curiosities
**"Hootnanny" Open Mic Nights** : On Mondays, The Troubadour hosted an open mic night affectionately called "The Hoot," offering an opportunity for anyone who could sing (or thought they could) to perform a three or four-song set to an audience of 10 to 15 acts. Getting on the list required arriving early when the club opened. "On Mondays at the troubadour, they had this kind of an open mic night that they called the Hootnanny. Well, it was a Hootnanny. **Comedy Showcase and Controversy** : The Troubadour also featured comedy acts, serving as a venue before dedicated comedy clubs became widespread. It was the site of controversy when legendary comedian Lenny Bruce was arrested on October 24, 1962, for uttering an "obscene word," "schmuck," making it his third arrest in three weeks. **Community Hub for Musicians** : In the late 1960s and first half of the 1970s, The Troubadour became a vital community hub where both newcomers and established stars would gather. Linda Ronstadt called it a "cafe society," and Bonnie Raitt noted it was "the heart of an entire music scene" where numerous musicians became friends, playing music and continuing their camaraderie in Laurel Canyon. "But so is the community that was created. **Competition from The Roxy** : A major factor in The Troubadour's decline was the opening of The Roxy by Lou Adler and David Geffen in 1973, located not far from The Troubadour, which provided a new, popular alternative venue. **Decline in the Late 70s** : By 1978, The Troubadour was largely empty, described by Ricky Lee Jones as a "club for used to be singers" and a "sad remnant" of its former glory. Although it later became a heavy metal venue in the 1980s, its golden age for folk, rock, and singer-songwriters had passed. "By 1978, the troubadour was as Ricky Lee Jones put it, it used to be club for used to be singers. Occasionally it drew in heavy metal bands. And in the 1980s, heavy metal is going to become the main genre of the troubadour. In fact, David Geffen discovered guns and roses there. **Doug Weston's Role in Decline** : The club's owner, Doug Weston, contributed to its downfall. He reportedly became envious of the success of artists, record executives, and managers, leading him to impose "unreasonable conditions" in artist contracts. These conditions, such as clauses requiring famous artists to return to the small club after gaining stardom, alienated performers. Weston was also known to be an abuser of drugs and alcohol, further impacting the club's environment. "Another problem for the troubadour was Doug Weston himself. It seemed like Weston started envying the success of not only the artists that were making big names for themselves and making money, but he was envious of the record executives and the managers." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 41 - The Impact of The Troubadour on 1970s Popular Culture | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-41-the-impact-of-the-troubadour-on-1970s-popular-culture
**Intimate Venue to Expanded Space** : Initially, The Troubadour had seating for about 65 people, described as "intimate." After moving and expanding into a former Porsche mechanic shop, it could accommodate some 300 music fans, allowing it to host a larger audience for emerging folk and rock legends. "At that time, it had seating for about 65. You know, we like to call that intimate. **Opening and Location** : The Troubadour was opened by Doug Weston in September 1957 as a jazz club on La Cienega Boulevard in Los Angeles. Four years later, it relocated to 9081 Santa Monica Boulevard and expanded into a space previously occupied by a Porsche mechanic shop, which featured a steel-reinforced balcony.
