# concert - Ozark Music Festival (1974)

## curiosities
**"Three Days of Sodom and Gamora"** : A local Missouri Senate committee report, whose phrasing was verified, described the festival as "three days of Sodom and Gamora." A local newspaper echoed this sentiment. "A local Missouri Senate committee report said this was three days of Sodom and Gamora." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 46 - The Chaos and Music of the Ozark Music Festival of 1974 | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Aftermath and Bans** : Following the festival, Chris Fritz was banned from Sedalia for life, and rock bands were prohibited from performing in Sedalia for the next 15 years. Promoters faced grand jury probes and lawsuits, being compelled to pay for damages including lost livestock and destroyed fields. The state fairgrounds required extensive cleanup, with lime sprinkled on the topsoil before a bulldozer removed the entire top layer to clear trash and drug paraphernalia. "Chris Fritz, he was banned from Sedelia for life and rock bands were banned from Sedelia for the next 15 years." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 46 - The Chaos and Music of the Ozark Music Festival of 1974 | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Described as a Disaster** : A local newspaper unequivocally labeled the Ozark Music Festival as "a disaster." A state senate report further concluded that the scene at the festival made "the degradation of Sodom and Gomorrah appear mild," with "Natural and unnatural sex acts" becoming "a spectator sport." "The Ozark Music Festival can only be described as a disaster." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 46 - The Chaos and Music of the Ozark Music Festival of 1974 | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Financial Cost and Legacy** : The artists' fees for the festival amounted to approximately $190,000 in 1974. Chris Fritz estimated in 2015 that reassembling the same lineup would cost around $25 million. Despite the behavioral problems, the festival is recognized for showcasing the diverse range of rock music, particularly Southern rock. "This was a showcase of the diversity of this big tent that we call rock music and hopefully we are approaching the day maybe we've arrived at the day when it's not the drugs and misbehavior but it's the music that was showcased that is the legacy of this festival." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 46 - The Chaos and Music of the Ozark Music Festival of 1974 | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Kent State Comparison** : The production manager for the festival recalled the 1970 Kent State shootings when the National Guard contemplated intervention, and he declined a gun offered by a local factory worker who was protecting equipment. "Now the production manager for the festival said that when the National Guard wanted to come out all he could think about was Kent State the shootings at Kent State in 1970." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 46 - The Chaos and Music of the Ozark Music Festival of 1974 | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Lineup** : The festival featured a lineup of 27 artists, including Aerosmith, Lynyrd Skynyrd, Nitty Gritty Dirt Band, Black Flag, Elvin Bishop, Joe Walsh, The Eagles, Marshall Tucker Band, REO Speedwagon, and Bob Seger. Bruce Springsteen and the E Street Band were scheduled but canceled due to transportation issues. "You would be hard pressed. To find a 70s music festival that had a better line up than this." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 46 - The Chaos and Music of the Ozark Music Festival of 1974 | https://www.ftr70.com/
**MC's Contribution to Disorder** : Wolfman Jack, the legendary master of ceremonies, actively encouraged misbehavior at the festival, including the display of naked breasts, stating his desire to see "a lot of love." "He encouraged a lot of it including the display of naked boobs because he said he wanted to see a lot of love." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 46 - The Chaos and Music of the Ozark Music Festival of 1974 | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Security Failure and Chaos** : Teenagers hired as "security" abandoned their posts to join the crowd. The festival suffered from a severe lack of basic amenities, with only five porta-potties and insufficient food, leading to reports of hungry fans butchering local livestock. Golf carts used to shuttle bands were destroyed, and some performers expressed fear about the unfolding chaos. "The golf carts that were used to shuttle the bands around were destroyed and a security guard said that many of the performers were a bit afraid of everything that was happening." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 46 - The Chaos and Music of the Ozark Music Festival of 1974 | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Unpreparedness for Scale** : The festival, organized by Kansas City concert promoter Chris Fritz, was held on the weekend of July 19, 1974, in Sedalia, Missouri, at the State Fairgrounds. Fritz anticipated approximately 50,000 attendees, but estimates suggest the actual attendance soared to around 300,000, with no accurate count possible once security abandoned posts and gates became irrelevant. The town of Sedalia was profoundly unprepared for the influx. "Who could have predicted that bags of ice would go for 30 bucks and hookers who could be found in nearby trailers would advertise their services for five." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 46 - The Chaos and Music of the Ozark Music Festival of 1974 | https://www.ftr70.com/
