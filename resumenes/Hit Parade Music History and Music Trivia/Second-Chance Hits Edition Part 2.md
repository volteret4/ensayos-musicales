# artist - Gary Guthrie

## curiosities
**WAKY Program Director - "You Don't Bring Me Flowers" Catalyst** : In 1978, as program director for Louisville Pop Radio Station WAKY, Gary Guthrie noticed that Neil Diamond and Barbra Streisand had each recorded separate album versions of "You Don't Bring Me Flowers." He took the initiative to splice these two recordings together, noting their similar arrangements and keys, to create a makeshift duet despite primitive technology. "Guthrie spliced together diamonds and Streisand's separate recordings of you don't bring me flowers. They had similar arrangements, and were even sung in the same key."
**DJ Activism - Creating a Number One Hit** : Gary Guthrie's spliced duet of "You Don't Bring Me Flowers" was tried out on WAKY and quickly inundated the station with listener requests. This phenomenon spread to other radio stations, eventually leading Neil Diamond and Barbra Streisand to officially record the duet, which then became a number one hit. "A DJ's activism had turned two deep cuts into a single, smash second chance hit."

**Days of Future Past (1967) - Stereo Sound Showcase** : Faced with a lack of hits, The Moody Blues were invited by their label in 1967 to record a classical rock hybrid album to showcase stereophonic sound. ## songs
**Go Now (1964) - Early British Invasion Hit** : This "beat combo style single" was a Top 10 hit for The Moody Blues during the early years of the British Invasion, but its sound gave little indication of the band's future musical direction. It stands as one of their three American Top 10 hits across different decades. "In the 60s go now, in the 70s Night's in White Satin and in the 80s your wildest dreams."
**Tuesday Afternoon (1968)** : This song was the only Top 40 hit from "Days of Future Past" at the time of its release, reaching number 24 in 1968. "Tuesday afternoon was the only top 40 hit from Days of Future Past at the time. It was a number 24 hit in 1968."
**Nights in White Satin (1967 / 1972) - Career-Defining Second Chance Hit** : The centerpiece of their 1967 LP "Days of Future Past," this nearly six-minute ballad with a poetry interlude was initially too long for pop radio. A severely edited three-minute version issued as a single in early 1968 flopped on the pop chart at number 103, but the song remained popular on progressive FM rock radio. **Second Chance Hits Defined** : Second chance hits are singles that initially performed poorly or flopped on the charts upon their first release. They later become smash successes due to a subsequent catalyst, such as a movie feature, a boost in the artist's fame, a radio DJ's advocacy, or a viral social media meme, causing them to be "brought back" to prominence. "singles that did nothing or even flopped on the charts on first release, before a catalyst, a movie, a boost in artist fame, a radio DJ, a social meme, turn them into smashers."
**Album Cuts vs. Standalone Singles in Chart History** : Not all second chance hits were originally issued as standalone singles; many began as unpromoted album cuts. In rankings of these hits, songs that were initially released as 45s and genuinely flopped on the Hot 100 often rank higher than those that were never intended as singles in their first iteration. "not all of them were issued the first time as actual standalone singles. Many started out as album cuts."
**DJ Activism as Chart Catalyst** : The actions of a radio DJ, such as Gary Guthrie splicing together separate album cuts of "You Don't Bring Me Flowers" or Scott Shannon playing "I've Never Been to Me" on a loop, have historically served as powerful bottom-up catalysts, transforming obscure tracks or flops into smash second chance hits. "A DJ's activism had turned two deep cuts into a single, smash second chance hit."
**TikTok and Sped-Up Samples Phenomenon** : In the TikTok era, sped-up samples have become a significant trend, driving increased consumption for older songs as video makers prefer tracks with "extra pep." This social phenomenon can push songs like The Weeknd's "Die For You" and Miguel's "Sure Thing" back onto the Hot 100 and into pop radio rotation, often played at their original speed. "In the TikTok era, sped-up samples have become big business. Video makers like their songs with extra pep."
**Billboard Metrics and Holiday Song Eligibility** : The chart eligibility rules, particularly regarding holiday music, have evolved. For example, Mariah Carey's "All I Want for Christmas Is You" was initially ineligible for the Hot 100 in 1994 because it was released only as an album cut, not a single. Its later chart-topping success is seen as Billboard's metrics adapting to reflect its long-standing popularity rather than a traditional "second chance" narrative. "It was an eligible for the hot 100 in 1994. And really the story here is one of Billboard metrics catching up with Carey's song."
**Historical Context of Holiday Music Charting** : In earlier decades, such as the 1960s, holiday music generally did not chart well. This historical trend explains why songs like Brenda Lee's "Rockin' Around the Christmas Tree" struggled to crack the Top 10 even at the peak of her fame, despite its eventual enduring popularity.
