## albums
**Stankonia (2000)** : This album, which arrived in November 2000, featured an album cover photo depicting Big Boi and Andre 3000 in front of an all-black American flag, with Dre flexing his hands at the camera. It debuted to over half a million sales in its first week, landing at number two on the charts; it would have been number one if not for Jay-Z's *The Dynasty* released the same week. *Stankonia* was Outcast's biggest and most sonically expansive album to date, blending elements of funk, psychedelia, gospel, rock, and club music with hip hop. By February 2001, it had already achieved triple platinum status and eventually sold 4 million copies. "The album was Outcast's biggest and most sonically expansive to date, incorporating elements of funk, psychedelia, gospel, rock, and club music, all fused with hip hop."
**Speaker Box, Slash, The Love Below (2003)** : Released in late September 2003, this two-CD set was Outcast's first album not to have a portmanteau word as its title, instead essentially having two titles in one: Big Boi's *Speaker Box* and Andre's *The Love Below*. The album reflected its creation as a pair of solo albums fused together, showcasing the members' diverging musical interests. Arista Records strategically priced the set so retailers could discount it to approximately the cost of a single CD, encouraging the public to view it as an extended Outcast album. This tactic resulted in it becoming Outcast's first and only number one album on the Billboard 200 in October 2003, opening with over half a million first-week sales. The album won the Grammy for Album of the Year at the 46th annual Grammy Awards in 2004 and topped many critics' polls as the best album of 2003. "In fact, the album essentially had two titles in one with a slash in the middle, Speaker Box, Slash, The Love Below."
**Idlewild Soundtrack (2006)** : This companion album, officially Outcast's last CD, was released in late summer 2006 alongside the movie of the same name. It shipped in platinum quantities and debuted at number two on the Billboard chart, driven by pent-up demand for new Outcast music. However, it had the shortest chart run of any Outcast album, falling off the Billboard 200 in less than three months, and critics called it "over long and unfocused." "It shipped in platinum quantities and debuted at number two on the Billboard chart."

## songs
**B.O.B. (Bombs Over Baghdad) (2000)** : Released in 2000 ahead of the *Stankonia* album, this single was acclaimed as one of the most dazzling in rap history, running at a "frenetic 155 beats per minute." The song was Outcast's attempt to synthesize the rhythms of British drum and bass music, which had been popular in UK clubs throughout the late 1990s. Critics deemed it seminal and prophetic, with the Village Voice calling it "just about the damnedest bass track ever, an electro workout," and Pitchfork later naming it the best single of the 2000s for its "obliteration of the boundaries separating hip hop, metal, and electro." Despite critical acclaim, its cutting-edge nature and controversial titular phrase (even before the Second Persian Gulf War) limited its chart success, reaching only number 69 on the R&B songs chart and missing the Hot 100 entirely. "B-O-B, Bombs Over Baghdad, has been acclaimed as one of the most dazzling singles in rap history."
**Ms. Jackson (2000)** : This song became Outcast's first blockbuster pop single, topping both the Hot 100 and the R&B chart in the winter of 2001. Contrary to initial belief, it was not about Janet Jackson, though Outcast would sometimes tease audiences live by having their DJ drop a line from Janet's 1986 hit "Nasty." The song was indirectly about Andre's former partner Erica Badu, with whom he had a child, but was directly an apology to Badu's mother, with whom Andre reportedly had an altercation. The song features a recurring piano line borrowed from opera composer Richard Wagner's famed "Bridal Chorus" (The Wedding March), used cleverly in a song about a man refusing to settle down. "The song is an apology for letting down the mother of your child."
**So Fresh, So Clean (2001)** : This track became a number 30 pop hit and a number 10 R&B hit for Outcast. It featured Outcast rapping over an "irresistible chanting chorus" sung by Organized Noise members Sleepy Brown and Rico Wade. "Outcast wrapped over an irresistible chanting chorus sung by organized noise members Sleepy Brown and Rico Wade."
**The Whole World (2001)** : Released later in 2001, this new single was added to Outcast's first-ever greatest hits album, reaching number 19 on the pop chart and number 8 on the R&B chart. It was built on a "bouncy chorus" sung by Andre and Dungeon Family singer George, and also included "bars" (verses) from fellow Atlanta rapper Killer Mike, serving as "yet another victory lap for hip hop from the ATL." "The Whole World also featured bars from fellow Atlanta rapper, Killer Mike, yet another victory lap for hip hop from the ATL."
**Hey Y'all (2003)** : Composed by Andre 3000 on an acoustic guitar, this "strummy catchy power pop anthem" became Outcast's biggest hit ever, arguably not rap at all. Influenced by bands like The Smiths, The Buzz Cogs, and The Hives, it blended these elements into an "abulliant title complete with an exclamation point" that contrasted with its secretly heartfelt verse lyrics meditating on the meaning of love ("Separates always better when there's feelings involved... nothing lasts forever, then what makes love the exception."). It was issued to radio and music stores in September 2003 as a double-A-sided single alongside Big Boi's "The Way You Move." By Thanksgiving, it had overtaken Big Boi's track on the Hot 100, and three weeks later, two weeks before Christmas 2003, it topped the Hot 100, knocking out Ludacris's "Stand Up." "Hey Y'all" was a multi-genre juggernaut, appearing on Billboard's pop, R&B, adult, alternative rock, and Latin charts, and Slate magazine named it the number one song in the new American songbook of the 21st century. "Its bridge chant alone, the closest thing to pure hip hop in the song is made for celebration."
**The Way You Move (2003)** : This "horn-inflicted funk love song," featuring an "infectious chorus" sung by Sleepy Brown, was Big Boi's half of a double-A-sided single released in September 2003, three weeks before "Hey Y'all." Although officially credited to Outcast, it represented Big Boi's "head-to-head" competition with Andre 3000. It initially scaled the charts faster than "Hey Y'all," reaching the top 10 in early November while "Hey Y'all" was still at number 15. For eight of the nine weeks that "Hey Y'all" was number one, "The Way You Move" held the number two spot. Finally, in February 2004, in its 21st week on the chart, it overtook "Hey Y'all" to reach number one. This made Outcast only the fifth act to replace themselves at number one and set a new record for the longest wait by a song at number two before rising to number one. "Although both tracks were officially credited to outcast, in reality, big boy and Andre were going head-to-head on the charts."
**Roses (2004)** : This funk rock song was a top 10 follow-up single from *Speaker Box, Slash, The Love Below*, showcasing the album's broad musical range. "The top 10 hit of Roussis."
**Ghetto Music (2003)** : An electro-pop jam created by Big Boi, this track was featured on the *Speaker Box, Slash, The Love Below* album, highlighting Big Boi's solo vision within the double album. "To big boys' electro-pop jam, ghetto music."
**Prototype (2003)** : An ethereal guitar-ballad by Andre 3000, this song was featured on the *Speaker Box, Slash, The Love Below* album and included a duet with Grammy Queen Norah Jones. "Or Andres' ethereal guitar-balled prototype."
**Morris Brown (2006)** : This single from the *Idlewild* soundtrack evoked the sounds of a historical black college marching band, reflecting the film's big band music influences from the era of Cab Calloway. "On one track, the single Morris Brown, they even evoked historical black college sounds of the marching band."
**Players Ball (1993)** : This holiday single, dropped by Outcast in 1993, was a nostalgic highlight of their 2014 reunion tour, performed at the Coachella Music Festival where fans "nodded along." "As heads nodded along to players ball, the holiday single Outcast dropped back in 1993."

## curiosities
**1995 Source Awards Upset & Andre's Prophecy** : Outcast's emergence as teenage hitmakers was followed by an upset at the 1995 Source Awards. During this event, Andre Benjamin delivered a prophetic speech "Claiming Rap for the South," which foreshadowed the genre's shift and the South's future dominance. "They're upset when at the 1995 Source Awards, and Andre's prophetic speech at that show, Claiming Rap for the South."
**Andre 3000's Name Change (2000)** : By 2000, Andre Benjamin began referring to himself for the first time as Andre 3000, a change that "signaled that Big Boy and Andre... were on some next level, shit" in their musical ambitions. "Andre, who by the way was now for the first time calling himself Andre 3000, were on some next level, shit."
**Musical Divergence Leading to Solo Albums (Early 2000s)** : Even as Southern Rap gained dominance, Big Boi and Andre Benjamin's musical interests significantly diverged in the early 2000s, with Big Boi exploring "Brassie R&B and Dirty South Funk" and Andre moving toward "jazz, pop standards, even alternative rock." This led to their next album, *Speaker Box, Slash, The Love Below*, being structured as two distinct solo discs under the Outcast name. "Big Boy going deeper into Brassie R&B and Dirty South Funk, and Dre toward jazz, pop standards, even alternative rock."
**Self-Replacement at Number One (2004)** : Outcast became only the fifth act in chart history to replace themselves at number one on the Hot 100 when "The Way You Move" dethroned "Hey Y'all" in February 2004. This rare feat had only previously been achieved by Elvis Presley, The Beatles, Boys to Men, and Nelly. Including two other acts who succeeded themselves in featured roles (Puff Daddy and Ja Rule), Outcast was the seventh act to achieve this feat. "Outcasts became only the fifth act to replace themselves at number one."
**Record for Longest Wait at Number Two (2004)** : "The Way You Move" set a new record for the longest wait by a song at number two before finally rising to number one. Ironically, the song that held it back for a record eight weeks was another Outcast song, "Hey Y'all." "Outcast also set a new record for the longest wait by a song at number two before rising to number one."
**Grammy Album of the Year Win (2004)** : At the 46th annual Grammy Awards in Los Angeles, Outcast achieved a career pinnacle by winning the Grammy for Album of the Year for *Speaker Box, Slash, The Love Below*, entering "rarefied company." It was only the second hip-hop album ever to win the National Academy of Recording Arts and Sciences' top prize, following Lauryn Hill's *The Miss Education of Lauren Hill* five years prior. "By winning the Grammy for Album of the Year, Outcast entered rarefied company."
**Andre's Grammy Acceptance Speech (2004)** : During their acceptance speech for Album of the Year at the 2004 Grammys, Andre 3000's joy was "tinged with just a little resentment" towards "new voe fans who he felt didn't understand outcast." This sentiment reflected underlying tensions even as the group reached an imperial highpoint. "And like his moment at the source awards, his joy was tinged with just a little resentment, this time toward new voe fans who he felt didn't understand outcast."
**Idlewild Movie (2006)** : Outcast fulfilled a long-time dream by creating *Idlewild*, a movie released in late summer 2006, which reimagined Depression-era movie musicals for the hip hop era. Andre and Big Boi starred as Percival and Rooster, respectively, "a pair of Juke joint performers in a fantastical Georgia town called Idol Wilde in 1935." The film also featured acclaimed black actors such as Terrence Howard, Cicily Tyson, and Ben Verine, with dazzling dance sequences and songs that evoked big band music. It earned back its $10 million budget worldwide but received mixed reviews and had a short theatrical run. "Idol Wilde was Outcast's reimagining of great depression era movie musicals for the hip hop era."
**Hiatus and Solo Work (Post-2006)** : After *Idlewild*, which served as their last regular studio album, Outcast never recorded another album together. Both members have, however, produced plenty of new music individually, albeit "at irregular intervals and rarely together." "As for Andre 3000 and Big Boy, even though they never recorded another Outcast album, they have produced plenty of new music, but at irregular intervals and rarely together."
**2014 Reunion Tour** : Outcast briefly reunited in 2014 for a tour to "burnish" their legacy, with a highlight being their headlining performance at the blockbuster Coachella Music Festival in Indio, California. At Coachella, Big Boi echoed Andre 3000's sentiment from the 2004 Grammys by shouting out to "the fans who truly remembered Outcast from back in the day," while playing their 1993 single "Players Ball." "It's a legacy that they sought to burnish in 2014 when they, briefly, reunited to tour as outcast."
**Legacy for Southern Rap** : Andre 3000 and Big Boi's "stronger legacy" is their profound impact on Southern Rap in general, with Atlanta widely acknowledged as a "hotbed of hip hop" that continues to generate new rap movements from artists like Future and meagres. This is a legacy they are "rightfully proud of." "But Dre and Big Boy's stronger legacy is on Southern Rap in general."
