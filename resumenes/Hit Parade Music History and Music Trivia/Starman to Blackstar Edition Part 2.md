## albums
**Aladdin Sane (Early 1970s)** : One of Bowie's peak glam era LPs that topped the British album chart but did not hit number one in America. It was one of his last UK number one LPs until "Scary Monsters." "And though he hadn't had a UK number one LP, since the era of Aladdin, Sain, and Diamond Dogs, even Bowie's quirkier LPs from the Berlin period, consistently made the UK top five."
**Diamond Dogs (Early 1970s)** : One of Bowie's peak glam era LPs that topped the British album chart but did not hit number one in America. It was one of his last UK number one LPs until "Scary Monsters." "And though he hadn't had a UK number one LP, since the era of Aladdin, Sain, and Diamond Dogs, even Bowie's quirkier LPs from the Berlin period, consistently made the UK top five."
**Station to Station (1976)** : A funk-infused album that charted better in the US than in the UK. It reached number 3 on the Billboard album chart in the US, which was his highest charting LP in America until "The Next Day" (2013). "And the funk-infused station to station album charted a bit better in the US as well."
**The Idiot (1977)** : An album by Iggy Pop for which David Bowie co-wrote the song "China Girl." Bowie later covered "China Girl" on his "Let's Dance" album, providing a financial windfall for Iggy Pop. "For the track that became the album's second single, Bowie brought him a song he'd co-written for Iggy Pop's 1977 LP, The Idiot, The aforementioned China Girl."
**Heroes (1977)** : An experimental album from Bowie's Berlin period that, like other Berlin LPs, consistently made the UK Top 5 but fell short of the Top 10 in America. Its famous black and white album cover, depicting Bowie holding his hands around his face in a "proto-vogue pose," was later satirized for "The Next Day" album cover. "Bowie's lowest charter since the experimental heroes a decade earlier."
**Scary Monsters (and Super Creeps) (1980) - UK Pop Comeback** : This album demonstrated a stark difference in reception between the UK and the US, being arguably Bowie's most British pop album. In the UK, it hit number one and was enthusiastically received as a major pop comeback, whereas in America, it was met with a shrug, peaking at a respectable but unremarkable number 12. Its full title was "Scary Monsters (and Supercreeps)." "But the David Bowie album, with perhaps the starkest difference in reception between the UK and the US, was 1980s scary monsters."
**Let's Dance (1983) - Global Pop Star Bid** : Produced by Nile Rogers and Bowie's first under a new contract with the EMI label, this album was his determined bid to become a global pop star again by re-embracing a glossier, R&B-based rock sound. It peaked at number 4 on the Billboard album chart, his highest charting LP since 1976's "Station to Station," and was his first to go platinum in America, ushering in a new mid-80s "imperial phase." "That 1983 album, produced by Rogers, and Bowie's first in a new contract with the EMI label, was his bid to be a global pop star again."
**Tonight (1984)** : Benefiting from the coattails of "Let's Dance," this album shot to number 1 in the UK, reached the top 10 in the US, and became Bowie's second ever platinum LP in America. Despite its commercial success, critics were nearly universal in their scorn for the album, and Bowie himself later apologized for its quality. "Blue Jean was the lead single of Tonight, David Bowie's 1984 album, benefiting from the co-tails of Let's Dance."
**Never Let Me Down (1987)** : Bowie's attempt to rock harder, turning up the guitars and synthesizers to make his beats louder, though not necessarily edgier. However, the album delivered "airless digital rock" that failed to impress critics or fans, peaking at number 34 on the Billboard album chart (his lowest charter since "Heroes") and leaving Bowie with a "bad taste" in his mouth. "And on his next studio album, 1987's Never Let Me Down, he turned up the guitars and especially the syndromes, making his beats louder, if not necessarily edgier."
**Tin Machine (1989)** : The self-titled debut album from Bowie's grungy rock band, featuring a metallic, feedback-laden sound that was noisier than anything he had attempted before, as a response to art punk bands like Pixies and Sonic Youth. While the lead track "Under the God" did well on the modern rock chart, the album only peaked at number 28 and did not go gold, struggling to establish the band beyond Bowie's side project status. "He released a self-titled album filled with noisy rock."
**Tin Machine II (1992)** : The sophomore album from Bowie's band Tin Machine. Despite generating their biggest modern rock hit, "One Shot," which reached number 3, the album commercially underperformed, reaching an anemic number 126 on the charts and failing to move beyond its perception as a David Bowie side project. "And in 1992, the group's sophomore album Tin Machine 2 generated the band's biggest hit, one shot which reached number three on the modern rock chart."
**Black Tie White Noise (1993)** : A reunion album with producer Nile Rogers, intended to remind listeners of David Bowie's debonair 80s image. However, it arrived in a changed pop landscape where R&B was new Jack Swing and top 40 radio favored club music and alt pop, leaving only alternative rock stations hospitable to Bowie. Despite upbeat reviews and its lead single "Jump They Say" reaching number 5 on the modern rock chart, poor distribution and promotion led to the album barely scraping the top 40 and becoming his shortest-lived album on the Billboard 200 to that date. "David and Nile's 1993 reunion was called Black Tie White Noise, an attempt to remind listeners of David Bowie's debonair 80s image."
**Outside (1995)** : A collaboration with Brian Eno that adapted their 70s Berlin techniques to 90s industrial rock, with its lead single "The Hearts Filthy Lesson" making the modern rock top 20. Fans noted its resemblance to Trent Reznor's Nine Inch Nails, solidifying Bowie's exploration of heavier electronic sounds in the grunge era. "Three years later, Brian Eno reunited with Bowie for the 1995 album Outside, which adapted their 70s Berlin techniques to 90s industrial rock."
**Earthling (1997)** : Released just weeks after Bowie's 50th birthday concert, this album featured the single "I'm Afraid of Americans," which gained further attention when Trent Reznor collaborated on a remix. It demonstrated Bowie's determination to keep pace with contemporary sounds, though his 50s ultimately became his quietest artistic period. "On a remix of Bowie's single I'm Afraid of Americans from his 1997 album Earthling."
**Heathen (2002)** : Produced by longtime collaborator Tony Visconti, this melodic album was hailed by critics as David Bowie's strongest since "Scary Monsters" and marked a significant "return to form." It reached number 14 on the Billboard 200, representing his best album chart showing in nearly two decades, and made the top five in the UK, sparking his early 2000s comeback. "The Melodic Heathen in 2002 sparked the comeback."
**Reality (2003)** : A more rock-forward album, also produced by Tony Visconti, recorded with the intention of giving Bowie's touring band more live material for his upcoming tour. While its lead single "New Killer Star" didn't chart anywhere, the album itself did solid business, making the top 30 in America and the top 3 in the UK, leading to his extensive Reality Tour. "Bowie and Visconti kept the momentum going with 2003's more rock-forward reality, an album they recorded with the intention of giving Bowie's touring band more live material."
**Changes One Bowie (1976)** : One of Bowie's many hits compilations, dating back to 1976, that compiled his best-selling tracks. It preceded the more comprehensive "Nothing Has Changed" collection from 2014. "He had released more than a dozen, dating back to 1976's Best Selling Changes One Bowie."
**Nothing Has Changed (2014) - Hits Compilation** : This comprehensive hits compilation, released in 2014, covered David Bowie's entire career across multiple labels. Notably, it concluded with a brand new song, "Sue (Or in a Season of Crime)," showcasing his continued artistic output alongside a retrospective of his discography. "Only this 2014 compilation, nothing has changed. Not only covered Bowie's entire career across multiple labels, it also ended with a new song called Sue, parentheses, or in a season of crime."
**The Next Day (2013) - Surprise Comeback** : This album, secretly recorded with producer Tony Visconti for over a year, was abruptly announced on Bowie's 66th birthday, January 8th, 2013, effectively ending his decade-long unofficial retirement. Despite the lead single's somber tone, the album was largely uptempo and rocking, though moody in its lyrics. It debuted at number one in the UK and a surprising number two on the Billboard 200 in the US, becoming his highest charting LP in America to date, though it was blocked from the top spot by a new Bon Jovi album. "And he announced the arrival of a new album two months later, which would be called the next day."
**Blackstar (2016) - Final Testament** : Released on David Bowie's 69th birthday, January 8th, 2016, this album was secretly recorded with producer Tony Visconti after Bowie's liver cancer diagnosis in mid-2014, becoming his "last will and testament" once he learned his cancer was terminal. The album is suffused with lyrics about death and some of the most "ghostly vocals" Bowie ever recorded. A week after his death, it debuted at number one on the Billboard 200, knocking Adele's "25" from the top spot, and was critically acclaimed as one of his best LPs and a seminal meditation on death. "That title, Blackstar, would also double as the title of the next David Bowie album, which was announced in October 2015, accompanied by a music video for Blackstar."

## songs
**Space Oddity (1969)** : An early Bowie hit from his "70s changes" era that explored themes of space and alienation. The song re-entered the Hot 100 at number 42 in January 2016, the week of Bowie's death, and remains one of his most played catalog songs on Spotify. "from the space oddity to Ziggy Stardust"
**Fame (1975)** : A significant funk and soul excursion that became a number one hit on the US Hot 100, showcasing Bowie's appeal in America. However, its chart performance differed significantly in the UK, where it only reached number 17. "Fame was a number one hit on our Hot 100, only a number 17 hit in the UK."
**Ashes to Ashes (1980)** : The lead single from "Scary Monsters," this song was a cheeky continuation of the story of Bowie's beloved character, Major Tom, picturing him "knocking aimlessly through the far reaches of space while strung out on a kind of galactic heroine," singing "we know Major Tom's a junkie." Promoted with a colorful, high-concept, and very expensive music video (reportedly £250,000) that premiered on "Top of the Pops," it became Bowie's fastest-selling UK single ever, rising to number one in two weeks. In the US, it was a total flop, bubbling under the Hot 100 at number 101, due to its alignment with pre-MTV British New Wave sounds that hadn't yet crossed over. "Ashes to Ashes was a cheeky continuation of the story of the doomed astronaut, picturing him knocking aimlessly through the far reaches of space while strung out on a kind of galactic heroine."
**Fashion (1980)** : The second single from "Scary Monsters," this infectious, synth-driven, and danceable track was infused with R&B energy. It cracked the Hot 100 at a meager number 70 in America, but reached the top five in the UK, serving as a better indicator of Bowie's evolving, rhythmic pop sound for the 80s. "The only scary monster's track that did any business for Bowie in America was its second single, Fashion."
**Under Pressure (1981)** : An anthemic 1981 duet with Queen, widely considered a masterpiece for both artists. It reached number one in the UK and got Bowie back into the US Top 40 (peaking at number 28) for the first time in six years. Featuring explosive vocals by Freddie Mercury, an "immortal baseline" by John Deacon (later sampled by Vanilla Ice for "Ice Ice Baby"), and lyrics primarily written by Bowie, it's described as a song of protest, fellowship, and love. "We've talked about under pressure, David Bowie's Anthemic 1981 duet with the band Queen in several prior hit parade episodes."
**Cat People (Putting Out Fire) (1982)** : The title song to the 1982 Paul Schrader erotic horror film "Cat People," co-written and sung by Bowie and teamed with electro-disco producer Giorgio Moroder, who had largely pre-written the backing track. Critics called it "One of Bowie's best vocal performances," and it was a fairly successful single, making the top ten on Billboard's new rock tracks chart and the top 30 on the UK pop chart. However, Bowie was unhappy with Moroder's production and requested Nile Rogers to re-record it with a "crunchier" backing for his next album. "Its full title was Cat People, parentheses putting out fire, and it teamed Bowie with electro-disco producer George Eo Moroder."
**Let's Dance (1983)** : The lead single and title track from Bowie's 1983 album, produced by Nile Rogers. This "glossy, new romantic new wave" track fused elements from across his career—rocking, funky, glam, new wave, melodic, syncopated, debonair, and danceable. It became a global number one hit, reaching the top spot in both the UK and US, marking Bowie's first number one hit on the Hot 100 in nearly eight years and kickstarting the biggest pop star moment of his career. It also helped launch the career of young guitarist Stevie Ray Vaughan, who played its "searing solo." "Bowie named the LP after its lead single, a track that fused everything he had done in his career to date."
**China Girl (1983)** : Co-written by Bowie for Iggy Pop's 1977 album "The Idiot," Bowie covered this song on "Let's Dance" as a favor to Iggy Pop, whose finances were shaky, knowing the royalties would be a windfall. Bowie and Nile Rogers transformed the proto-punk track into a pop hit by layering in a "stereotypical oriental riff." Fueled by a risque music video that satirized Asian stereotypes and closed with Bowie and actress Geeling Ng recreating a fully nude embrace from "From Here to Eternity," it peaked at number 2 in the UK and number 10 on the Hot 100, making "Let's Dance" Bowie's first album to generate two US Top 10 hits. "For the track that became the album's second single, Bowie brought him a song he'd co-written for Iggy Pop's 1977 LP, The Idiot, The aforementioned China Girl."
**Modern Love (1983)** : The third hit single from the "Let's Dance" album, produced by Nile Rogers as "Debenair Jet Setting Dance Rock." It reached number 14 on the Hot 100, enabling "Let's Dance" to overcome the barrier of having only two hits and becoming Bowie's first album to generate three hits. The video captured Bowie live on stage on his "Serious Moonlight" tour, portraying him as a sleek, "up-ified pop idol" in a yellow suit with a Little Richard-style pompadour. "Let's Dance overcame that barrier too, when Modern Love, produced by Nile Rogers as Debenair Jet Setting Dance Rock, reached number 14 on the Hot 100."
**Blue Jean (1984)** : The lead single from the 1984 album "Tonight," benefiting from the lingering success of "Let's Dance." This bopping love song, a pastiche of Bowie's 80s sound, became another top 10 hit for Bowie on both sides of the Atlantic. It was supported by a 20-minute short film/music video directed by British author Julian Temple, with a shortened version airing on MTV. "Nonetheless, Blue Jean, a pastiche of Bowie's 80s sound in the form of a bopping love song, became another top 10 hit for Bowie on both sides of the Atlantic."
**Dancing in the Street (1985)** : A cover of Martha and the Vandellas' classic Motown hit, recorded as a duet with old friend Mick Jagger in 1985 to benefit Live Aid's Ethiopian Relief charity. Despite good intentions and raising significant money, the single was "instantly dated" and its rushed music video was a "laugh riot," featuring Bowie and Jagger shouting lyrics and performing "hokey dad dancing." Regardless, it was a chart smash, reaching number one in the UK (Jagger's only solo number one) and number 7 on the Hot 100. "To benefit Live Aid, the 1985 Global Famine Relief effort organized by Bob Guildoff, old friends Mick Jagger and David Bowie teamed up to record a cover of Martha and the Van Dela's classic Motown hit, Dancing in the Street, with all proceeds earmarked for the Ethiopian Relief charity."
**This Is Not America (1985)** : An ambient, sophisticated pop single recorded with jazz fusion combo The Pat Metheny Group for the soundtrack to the 1985 Sean Penn spy thriller, "The Falcon and The Snowman." Benefiting from the tail end of Bowie's "imperial phase," this unlikely hit cracked the top 40, peaking at number 32, and marked an early sign of Bowie's pivot away from his mainstream pop persona. "When Bowie released, this is not America. An ambient, sophisticated pop single recorded with Jazz Fusion combo, The Pat Methini Group."
**Day In, Day Out (1987)** : The first single from "Never Let Me Down," which aimed for a harder rock sound with loud drums. It reached number 21 on the Hot 100 and a more impressive number 3 on Billboard's Album Rock Chart, but the album itself was met with critical disinterest. "The LP's first single, Day in, Day out, built up a noisy head of steam."
**Never Let Me Down (1987) - Title Track** : This jazz-influenced title track from the album of the same name reached number 27 on the pop chart. Despite generating one more hit, critics and Bowie himself generally found the album uninspired. "The album did generate one more hit in the Jazz E title track, which reached number 27 on the pop chart."
**Time Will Crawl (1987)** : Considered by critics and Bowie himself as the only "keeper" from the "Never Let Me Down" album, this art-rocking track reached number 7 on the A.O.R. chart but missed the Hot 100 entirely. "But critics and Bowie himself generally agreed the album's only keeper was the art rocking time will crawl, a number 7 A.O.R. hit that missed the Hot 100 entirely."
**Under the God (1989)** : The lead-off track from Tin Machine's self-titled debut album, which reached number four on the modern rock tracks chart in the summer of 1989. It exemplified the band's noisy rock sound, reflecting their art punk influences. "Under the god, the lead-off track from Tin Machine reached number four on modern rock tracks in the summer of 89."
**Heaven's In Here (1989)** : A bluesy follow-up track from Tin Machine's self-titled debut album that just missed the modern rock top ten, peaking at number 11. "A follow-up, the bluesy heavens in here just missed the modern rock top ten at number 11."
**One Shot (1992)** : The biggest hit generated by Tin Machine, from their sophomore album "Tin Machine II," reaching number three on the modern rock chart. This track, however, did not prevent the album from commercially underperforming. "And in 1992, the group's sophomore album Tin Machine 2 generated the band's biggest hit, one shot which reached number three on the modern rock chart."
**Real Cool World (1992)** : A track Bowie recorded for the soundtrack of the 1992 animated film "Cool World." This song, with its electronic club music pulse, scraped the modern rock top 10, demonstrating Bowie's ability to keep pace with younger sounds and his dabblings in techno and industrial music during a decade dominated by grunge and gangsta rap. "A track Bowie recorded for the 1992 animated film Cool World called Real Cool World."
**Jump They Say (1993)** : The lead single from "Black Tie White Noise," which managed to reach number 5 on the modern rock chart. Despite this, the album suffered from poor distribution and promotion in a changed early 90s pop landscape. "Jump they say, the albums lead single did manage to reach number 5 on the modern rock chart"
**The Hearts Filthy Lesson (1995)** : The lead single from Bowie's 1995 album "Outside," a collaboration with Brian Eno. This industrial rock track sported a thunderous beat and made the modern rock top 20, with fans noting its resemblance to Nine Inch Nails. "Its lead single The Hearts Filthy Lesson sported a thunderous beat and made the modern rock top 20."
**I'm Afraid of Americans (1997)** : A single from the 1997 album "Earthling," which gained significant attention when Trent Reznor collaborated with Bowie on a remix. This collaboration excited fans who saw Bowie's mid-90s experiments leaning towards industrial rock. "On a remix of Bowie's single I'm Afraid of Americans from his 1997 album Earthling."
**Everyone Says Hi (2002)** : A solid hit single from the melodic "Heathen" album, which cracked the UK top 20 and contributed to the album's success as a "return to form" for Bowie. "In the UK, Heathen made the top five and spawned a solid hit, the single Everyone Says High, which cracked the UK top 20."
**New Killer Star (2003)** : The lead single from the 2003 album "Reality," which aimed to provide Bowie's touring band with more live material. While the single did not chart anywhere, the album itself did solid business globally. "The reality lead single New Killer Star didn't chart anywhere, but the album did solid business, making the top 30 in America the top 3 in the UK."
**Where Are We Now? (2013)** : A ruminative new single dropped without warning on David Bowie's 66th birthday, January 8th, 2013, ending his decade-long unofficial retirement. It bubbled under the Hot 100, driven mostly by iTunes downloads, and cracked the top 10 in the UK. Musically, it was a gentle rumination unlike any prior Bowie lead single, wistfully looking back on his life and suggesting a somber tone for the upcoming "The Next Day" album. "On January 8th, 2013, David Bowie's 66th birthday, he ended his unofficial retirement. With no prior warning, Bowie dropped a ruminative new single called Where Are We Now?"
**The Stars Are Out Tonight (2013)** : The second single from "The Next Day" album, described as a "banger" and released with a "cheeky music video" co-starring actress Tilda Swinton. This song showcased the album's uptempo and rocking side, contrasting with the more somber tone suggested by the first single. "The second single, The Stars Are Out Tonight, was a banger, released with a cheeky music video co-starring actress Tilda Swinton."
**Sue (Or in a Season of Crime) (2014)** : A new song featured as the closing track on Bowie's 2014 hits compilation "Nothing Has Changed." This track was remarkable for its music, as Bowie played with a jazz combo led by saxophonist Danny McCaslin, marking his first attempt at actual experimental jazz and serving as a preview for his next album. "Only this 2014 compilation, nothing has changed. Not only covered Bowie's entire career across multiple labels, it also ended with a new song called Sue, parentheses, or in a season of crime."
**Blackstar (2015)** : Released a few months ahead of the album of the same name, this song was initially featured on the soundtrack to the TV show "The Last Panthers." Its accompanying surreal 10-minute music video depicted a woman discovering a dead astronaut and featured Bowie himself appearing as both a spirit in the clouds holding a Bible-like book and a "rath" with bandages and buttons over his eyes, setting a profound tone for his final album. "A few months ahead of the album, a song called Blackstar was released via the soundtrack to a TV show called The Last Panthers."
**Lazarus (2015)** : A track worked on during the "Blackstar" album sessions, which also served as the title track to a jukebox musical of Bowie's life's work, co-written with Irish playwright Enda Walsh and premiering off-Broadway in late 2015. This six-minute, mortality-obsessed track debuted at number 40 on the Hot 100 the week Bowie died, making it officially his last American top 40 hit. "One of the tracks they worked on, Lazarus, doubled as the title track to a jukebox musical of Bowie's life's work, that he was co-writing with Irish playwright and a Walsh."
**Changes (1971)** : A classic Bowie hit and one of the Bowie songs that took up more than half of the Rock charts' top 20 the week of his death in January 2016. It also remains one of his most played catalog songs on Spotify. "Bowie songs took up more than half of the Rock charts top 20, including such classics as Let's Dance, Changes and Life on Mars."
**Life on Mars (1971)** : A classic Bowie hit that, along with "Let's Dance" and "Changes," took up more than half of the Rock charts' top 20 the week of his death in January 2016, indicating its enduring popularity. "Bowie songs took up more than half of the Rock charts top 20, including such classics as Let's Dance, Changes and Life on Mars."
**Moonage Daydream (1972)** : One of more than a dozen David Bowie songs that have been streamed over 100 million times each on Spotify since his death, establishing it as a perennial favorite in the digital age. "At Spotify, more than a dozen Bowie songs have been played more than 100 million times each from Moon Age Daydream, to Modern Love."
**Heroes (1977)** : Despite not being a chart hit in its original release year (1977), "Heroes" has been commercially redeemed in the digital age, becoming one of Bowie's most beloved songs. The week he died, it was his fifth most played catalog song on Spotify, and it re-entered the Rock and Alternative Songs chart in January 2026 after its use in Netflix's "Stranger Things." "And one Bowie favorite that has been commercially redeemed in the digital age is 1977's Heroes."

## curiosities
**Chart Performance Disparity (UK vs. US) - 1970s** : During his first decade of hit making, David Bowie's stardom differed considerably between his British homeland and America. In the US, he was a star, but in the UK, he was a mega star; several of his peak glam era LPs topped the British album chart, while none hit number one in America. His quirkier Berlin LPs consistently made the UK top five, but fell short of the top ten in America, which generally preferred his funk and soul excursions. "During his first decade of hit making, David Bowie's stardom in his British homeland, differed considerably from his chart performance in America."
**Major Tom's Return - "Ashes to Ashes" (1980)** : Bowie revived his beloved character Major Tom in the lead single for "Scary Monsters," portraying him as a "doomed astronaut" aimlessly knocking through space while "strung out on a kind of galactic heroine." This cheeky continuation of the character's story was a key element of the single's UK success. "Maybe that was because on its lead single, Bowie was reviving his most beloved character, Major Tom."
**"Ashes to Ashes" Music Video (1980)** : The music video for "Ashes to Ashes" was a colorful, high-concept, and very expensive production, reportedly costing a quarter million pounds. It premiered on the BBC's "Top of the Pops" and contributed significantly to the single becoming David Bowie's fastest-selling UK single ever. "Promoted with a colorful, high concept and very expensive music video that premiered on the BBC's Top of the Pops and was reported to have cost a quarter million pounds."
**New Wave Pioneer Status** : David Bowie was recognized as being "New Wave before New Wave was a thing," and his 1980 hit "Ashes to Ashes" aligned him with cutting-edge British pop artists like Kate Bush and Gary Numan, whose sounds hadn't yet crossed over to America. "Bowie was New Wave before New Wave was a thing. And now he was aligning himself with cutting-edge British pop."
**"The Young Ones" Satirical Gag (1982)** : The widespread cultural phenomenon of "Ashes to Ashes" in the UK led to references in popular media, including a throwaway gag on the satirical British punk comedy show "The Young Ones" a couple of years after its release, highlighting its pervasive recognition. "Ashes to Ashes was a true UK phenomenon, the kind that invited injuks, such as this throwaway gag, a couple of years later, on the satirical British punk comedy show The Young Ones."
**Longest Album Gap and RCA Departure (Early 1980s)** : Bowie experienced the longest gap in his career to date—three years—before releasing a new studio album, largely due to his unhappiness with RCA Records and his efforts to get out of his contract, driven by a desire to try new things and pursue a new sound. "It would be another three years before Bowie returned with a new studio album, the longest gap in his career to date."
**"Cat People" Production Discontent (1982)** : David Bowie was unhappy with Giorgio Moroder's production of "Cat People (Putting Out Fire)," despite critics calling it one of Bowie's best vocal performances. This discontent led him to ask Nile Rogers to re-record the track with a "crunchier" backing for his subsequent album. "However, David Bowie was unhappy with Moroder's production, and he asked the producer of his next album, Nile Rogers, to re-record Cat People with a new, crunchier backing track."
**Nile Rogers Collaboration Origin (1982)** : David Bowie met Nile Rogers, the former mastermind of disco band Chic, at a New York club in 1982 and invited him to write and record in Montreux, Switzerland. Bowie sought Rogers to shake up his sound after leaving RCA, explicitly stating he wanted to record the biggest hits of his career. "David Bowie had met Nile Rogers, former mastermind of disco band Sheik, at a New York club in 1982, and he invited Nile to write and record with him in Montreux, Switzerland."
**"Let's Dance" Birth from Acoustic Guitar (1983)** : The massive hit "Let's Dance" originated from a simple tune Bowie had written on an acoustic guitar. Nile Rogers then transformed it by adding syncopation, turning it into a "bang" and a signature glossy new romantic new wave track. "Rogers took a simple tune Bowie had written on an acoustic guitar, added syncopation, and turned it into a bang."
**Broadway Success - The Elephant Man (Early 1980s)** : Two and a half years before "Let's Dance" hit number one, Bowie played the title role in "The Elephant Man" on Broadway, breaking the record for a non-musical by selling out 125 performances. This demonstrated his versatility and widespread appeal beyond music. "Two and a half years ago, he played the title role in the Elephant Man, and he broke the Broadway record for a non-musical by selling out 125 performances."
**"China Girl" Royalties for Iggy Pop (1983)** : Bowie's decision to cover "China Girl," a song he co-wrote for Iggy Pop's 1977 album "The Idiot," was partly an act of friendship. Iggy Pop's finances were described as "shaky" in the early 80s, and Bowie knew the royalties from the hit cover would provide a significant windfall for him. "Legend has it, Bowie wanted to do his friend Iggy Pop a favor by covering the song. Iggy's finances in the early 80s were shaky, and Bowie knew the royalties from a China Girl cover would be a windfall."
**"China Girl" Music Video Controversy (1983)** : The music video for "China Girl" was considered risque, satirizing Asian stereotypes and concluding with Bowie and actress Geeling Ng recreating the fully nude crashing waves embrace from the 1950s film "From Here to Eternity." The controversy surrounding the video likely contributed to the song's commercial success. "fueled by a risque music video that satirized Asian stereotypes and closed with Bowie recreating the crashing waves embrace from the 50s film from here to eternity, with actress Gilling Eng, both fully nude in the surf."
**"Serious Moonlight" Tour Image (1983)** : During his "Serious Moonlight" tour, captured in the "Modern Love" music video, Bowie appeared as a sleek, "up-ified pop idol," dressed in a yellow suit with blonde hair teased into a Little Richard-style pompadour. This image was part of his mid-80s "imperial phase" persona, reflecting his embrace of mainstream pop stardom.
