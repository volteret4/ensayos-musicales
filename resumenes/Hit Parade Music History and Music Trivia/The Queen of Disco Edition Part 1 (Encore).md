## albums
**Lady of the Night (1974) - Debut Album** : This album was Donna Summer's debut, with all songs produced and written by Giorgio Moroder and Pete Bellotte. It presented a pastiche of styles, showcasing the breadth of Summer's diverse pop influences, including an homage to 60s girl group pop. "All of the songs were produced and written by Moroder and Balotti. The album was a pastiche of styles, showcasing the breadth of Summer's pop influences."
**Love to Love You Baby (1975) - First American Release** : Summer's first American album, released jointly by Moroder's Oasis label and Casablanca Records in late summer 1975. Its 17-minute title track, a re-recorded and expanded version of the single, took up the entire Side A. The album was the first in its genre to feature a full-side suite and sold unusually well for a disco LP, going gold and peaking at number 11 on the Billboard album chart, alongside LPs by artists like Electric Light Orchestra and Peter Frampton. "Love to Love You Baby, Summer's First American Album Release, issued jointly by Moroder's Oasis label and Casablanca Records in the late summer of 1975."
**A Love Trilogy (1976) - Extended Dance Suites** : Released in March 1976, while the *Love to Love You Baby* album was still in the top 40, this album committed to the concept of extended length dance suites. It featured an 18-minute track, "Try Me, I Know We Can Make It," which occupied all of Side A. Tracks from this album reached number one on Billboard's Club Play chart, and the album went gold within weeks, though it did not generate a top 40 pop hit. "A love trilogy released in March 1976 while the love to love you album was still in the top 40, led off with the 18-minute track, Triming I Know We Can Make It, taking up all of Side-Aid."
**Four Seasons of Love (1976) - Concept Album** : Issued just seven months after *A Love Trilogy*, this was a full-blown concept album, composed of a suite of six to eight-minute tracks, each named for a season, such as "Winter Melody" to "Spring Affair." Tracks from this album also reached number one on Billboard's Club Play chart, cementing Summer's status as disco's emerging queen. It went gold within weeks, but, like its predecessor, did not produce a top 40 pop hit. "four seasons of love was a full-blown concept album, composed of a suite of six to eight-minute tracks named for the seasons, from Winter Melody to Spring Affair."
**I Remember Yesterday (1977) - Futuristic Concept** : This album was based on a concept by Pete Bellotte, envisioning Side A with tracks recalling the 1940s, 50s, and 60s, and Side B featuring current-sounding tracks that concluded with a song representing the future. The album's futuristic closing track, "I Feel Love," was almost an afterthought but proved to be revolutionary. "a concept Balotti came up with called I Remember Yesterday. Side A would consist of tracks recalling the 1940s, 50s and 60s. Side B would feature current sounding tracks and close with a song that represented the future."
**Bad Girls (1979) - Chart-topping Double Album** : This was the second of three consecutive chart-topping double albums for Donna Summer, which solidified her status as the Queen of Disco. Released in the final year of the 1970s, it significantly dominated radio airplay. "the second of those three chart-topping double albums, Bad Girls, dominated the radio in the final year of the 1970s."

## songs
**If You Walk In Alone (1969)** : A Northern Soul single released by German label Phillips, notable for being given a badly translated title that Donna Summer never actually sings. Despite the translation error, her young voice on the track was potent. "At least one track was given a badly translated title by German label Phillips, the 1969 Northern Soul single If You Walk In Alone."
**Sally Go Round the Roses (1971)** : Donna Summer's cover of The Jaynetts' 1963 Top 10 pop and R&B hit, recorded in 1971 for MCA Records' UK subsidiary in London. Her version "amped up the soul, and pumped up the funk." "Donna's cover of the hit in 1971, Amped Up the Soul, and Pumped Up the Funk."
**Denver Dream (1974)** : This single was pivotal as Donna Summer's first recording with producers Giorgio Moroder and Pete Bellotte, and marked the first time the spelling "Donna Summer" appeared on a single cover, originating from a typo by Lark Records. The collaboration initiated a partnership that would profoundly change dance music. "That single, Denver Dream in 1974, was pivotal, and not just because the single cover read Donna Sommer for the first time. It was also her first collaboration with a pair of producers who, like her, had relocated to Munich from other countries, an Italian producer, Georgioma Rotor, and a Brit, Pete Belotti."
**The Hostage (1974)** : The first single from Summer's debut album *Lady of the Night*, this was a story song featuring melodramatic spoken word segments. Its music was a hybrid of R&B and Symphonic Rock, achieving a number two hit in the Netherlands. "The Hostage was a number two hit in the Netherlands."
**Lady of the Night (1974)** : The title track from Summer's debut album, this song was an homage to 60s girl group pop. It became a number four Dutch hit and a minor top 40 hit in Germany. "Its title track was an homage to 60s girl group pop, and the album's first single, The Hostage, was a story song with melodramatic spoken word segments, and the music was a hybrid of R&B and Symphonic Rock."
**Love to Love You (1975) - Original Dutch Single** : This song, which began as "Love to Love You," featured a whispery, "sex kitten" vocal, a departure from Summer's powerful mezzo-soprano, and included overdubbed orgasmic moans. Donna initially conceived the lyrical hook and intended it as a demo for another artist, but Giorgio Moroder convinced her to keep and record it herself. This single reached number 13 on the charts in the Netherlands in early 1975. "She came up with the lyrical hook Love to Love You Baby, and when she brought it to Moroder, thinking it might make a good demo for another artist, Georgio convinced her she should keep it, and record it."
**Love to Love You Baby (1975) - Extended American Hit** : Retitled by Neil Bogart of Casablanca Records, this song was fully re-recorded by Summer, Moroder, and Bellotte to be 17 minutes long, five to six times the length of the original Dutch single, featuring a string section and additional backup singers. This extended version filled an entire side of the *Love to Love You Baby* album and a five-minute radio edit peaked at number two on the Hot 100 in February 1976. It is now enshrined in the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame as one of its "Songs That Shaped Rock and Roll," and was a watershed for popular music, signaling disco's coming of age and helping popularize the extended mix. "Love to Love You Baby is now enshrined in the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame as one of its songs that shaped Rock and Roll."
**Try Me, I Know We Can Make It (1976)** : An 18-minute track that led off and filled Side A of the album *A Love Trilogy*. This extended length track contributed to the album's success on Billboard's Club Play chart, where it reached number one. "led off with the 18-minute track, Triming I Know We Can Make It, taking up all of Side-Aid."
**I Feel Love (1977) - EDM Pioneer** : The final track and lead-off single to the album *I Remember Yesterday*, this song reached number six on the Hot 100, becoming Summer's first Top 10 hit and gold single since "Love to Love You Baby," and her first number one hit in England. It is generally agreed to have single-handedly invented electronic dance music, being the first hit single with an entirely synthesized backing track, with only Donna Summer's voice as the organic element. Summer co-wrote its mantra-like lyrics and helped arrange its complex vocal melody, which was difficult to sing. The song was a smash in discos, particularly gay clubs, and influenced artists like Blondie, Human League, David Bowie, and Brian Eno. "I Feel Love is generally agreed to have single-handedly invented electronic dance music."
**Bad Girls (1979)** : This song rose to number one on the charts during the week ending July 14, 1979, becoming one of two of Donna Summer's singles in the top three in America at that time. It was the title track of her chart-topping double album *Bad Girls*. "when the song Bad Girls rose to number one."
**Hot Stuff (1979)** : A former number one hit, this song was positioned just a couple of notches below "Bad Girls" on the charts, contributing to Donna Summer having two of the top three singles in America simultaneously. "While the former number one hot stuff was just a couple of notches below, Donna Summer had two of the top three singles in America."

## curiosities
**Early Life and Musical Roots (1948)** : Born Ladonna Adrienne Gaines in Mission Hill, Boston, on New Year's Eve, 1948, Donna Summer began singing in her church choir, soloing on hymns as young as eight years old, demonstrating her potent vocal talent from an early age. "she was born on New Year's Eve, 1948, and sang in her church choir, soloing on hymns as young as eight years old."
**European Theatrical Career (1967-1971)** : After arriving in New York City in 1967 at age 18 and performing with blues rock combo The Crow, Donna moved to Germany in 1968 to play Sheila in the Munich production of the musical *Hair*, singing the German version "Wassermann." Her relocation to Germany was likened to the impact Hamburg had on The Beatles or Berlin on David Bowie. She quickly became fluent in German and participated in German productions of *Godspell*, *Showboat*, and the rock musical *The Me Nobody Knows* (known as *Ichbien-Ich*), later singing for the Viennese folk opera after moving to Vienna in 1971. "The musical opened in Germany in October 1968. More important to Donna than the show was Germany itself. Relocating there did for the future Donna Summer what gigs in Hamburg had done for the young Beatles at the turn of the 60s, or what Berlin would do for David Bowie in the 1970s."
**Origin of Stage Name "Donna Summer" (1974)** : Donna Gaines married Austrian actor Helmuth Sommer in Vienna in 1973, taking his surname. Her stage name, "Donna Summer" (S-U-M-M-E-R), was an accidental misspelling by German label Lark Records on her 1974 single "Denver Dream," a spelling that stuck and outlasted her three-year marriage. "when the newly married Donna Sommer recorded a track for German label Lark Records in 1974, the single was pressed and labeled as Donna Sommer, S-U-M-M-E-R. The name with that spelling not only stuck, it outlasted Donna's first marriage."
**Formation of Creative Partnership with Moroder and Bellotte (1973)** : Donna Summer met Italian producer Giorgio Moroder and British producer Pete Bellotte at a German recording session for the American band Three Dog Night in 1973. This meeting led to a creative partnership that would, without exaggeration, change the sound of dance music in the 1970s. "The three of them met at a German recording session for American band Three Dog Night in 1973, and they formed a creative partnership that, without exaggeration, would change the sound of dance music in the 1970s."
**"Queen of Disco" Title and Genre Breadth** : While widely and correctly known as "The Queen of Disco" for her chart dominance in the late 1970s, this title is somewhat limiting, as Summer's musical roots spanned rock, soul, and Europop. Her career successfully outlasted disco's infamous implosion in the early 1980s, and critics of classic rock prestige eventually acknowledged her vital role in rock and roll's evolution, though often posthumously. "Donna Summer has been called The Queen of Disco, and that name is both totally correct and somewhat limiting."
**Journalists' Questions about "Love to Love You Baby" Vocals** : For years after the release of "Love to Love You Baby," journalists frequently inquired if Donna Summer had been pleasuring herself while recording the song's orgasmic moans. Summer consistently quipped that she "hadn't been touching anything more than her knee." "For years afterward, Summer would roll her eyes at panting journalists, wondering if she had been pleasuring herself while recording that vocal. She usually quipped that she hadn't been touching anything more than her knee."
**Initial Perception as Club Artist Only (1976)** : Despite the global success of "Love to Love You Baby," Summer's subsequent 1976 albums, *A Love Trilogy* and *Four Seasons of Love*, did not generate top 40 pop hits, leading to the initial perception that she was destined to be a "queen of the clubs" but not a consistent Hot 100 artist. "Donna was perhaps destined to be a queen of the clubs but not the hot 100."
**Meeting Future Husband Bruce Sudano** : Donna Summer co-wrote the mantra-like lyrics of "I Feel Love" on the night she met her future husband, Bruce Sudano, a member of the band Brooklyn Dreams. "Balotti revealed that Summer co-wrote the mantra like lyric of I Feel Love, the night she met her future husband Bruce Sudano of the band Brooklyn Dreams."
**Impact of "I Feel Love" on Gay Clubs (1977)** : "I Feel Love" became a significant smash in discos, particularly within gay clubs, where its innovative sound and ecstatic vocals resonated strongly. "The record was a smash in discos, particularly Reynolds notes, gay clubs, and artists from Blondie to the human league to David Bowie and Brian Eno took inspiration from it."
**Chart Success Amidst Disco's Rise (1977)** : By November 1977, when "I Feel Love" peaked on the Hot 100, disco was rapidly dominating charts across America and Europe, with the film *Saturday Night Fever* opening the following month, solidifying Summer's status as a consistent international hitmaker. "By the time I Feel Love reached its peak on the Hot 100 in November 1977, disco was taking over the charts in both America and Europe. Saturday Night Fever would open in movie theaters the next month, and Summer became a more consistent hitmaker on both sides of the Atlantic."

# curiosity

**Billboard Album Chart First - Three Consecutive Double Albums** : The first artist to achieve the feat of topping the Billboard album chart with three consecutive double albums was Donna Summer, a surprising accomplishment often incorrectly attributed to rock bands like Led Zeppelin or Pink Floyd, or rappers like Tupac. "So which badass dude pulled off this trifecta of double album chart crushers? Actually, it wasn't a dude at all, but she was a badass."
**Hair (Musical) - Last Broadway Cast Recording to Top Billboard Album Chart** : The album for the musical *Hair* was the last Broadway cast recording to reach the top of the Billboard album chart, marking a significant historical achievement in the intersection of theatrical and popular music. "whose album was the last Broadway cast recording to top the Billboard album chart."
**Early Funk and R&B Singles Influencing Disco** : Before Donna Summer's "Love to Love You Baby," the market for sensual disco was primed by raw and ribald funk and R&B singles widely embraced by radio stations and discotheques, such as Sylvia Robinson's "Pillow Talk" and Shikaccia's "Jungle Feeder." "radio stations and disco techs had already embraced more raw and ribbled funk and R&B singles, such as Sylvia Robinson's Pillow Talk and Shikaccia's Jungle Feeder."
**Invention of Extended Club Mix (1973)** : Legendary DJ Tom Moulton is credited with inventing the extended club mix format in 1973. He achieved this by spinning his own acetates of popular club tracks, consciously prolonging their instrumental passages to enhance the club experience. "Legendary DJ Tom Molten is credited with inventing the form in 1973 when he began spinning his own acetates of favorite club tracks with the instrumental passages prolonged."
**12-inch Single Format Evolution** : The 12-inch single format emerged around 1975 as a radio promotional tool, taking another two years to hit record shops for consumer purchase in 1976. Despite "Love to Love You Baby" not being issued as a 12-inch in 1975 due to the format's novelty, its success helped prime the consumer market for the idea of tantric, epic-length club tracks. "It took another two years for the so-called 12-inch single... to emerge as a radio promotional tool. 12-inch singles for consumers didn't hit record shops until 1976."
**"I Feel Love" as the Start of the 1980s** : Music critic Simon Reynolds suggests that "I Feel Love" by Donna Summer is the one song that can be pinpointed as where the 1980s began, due to its profound influence on future genres like high energy, Italo, house, techno, and trance, and its innovative impact on post-punk and new wave groups. "If any one song can be pinpointed as where the 1980s began, its I Feel Love."
