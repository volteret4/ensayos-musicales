## albums
**Cracked Rear View (1994) - Major Label Debut** : This album was packed with songs the band had recorded for their independent EPs, including all three future hit singles. Its production was handled by Don Gehman, known for his work with John Mellencamp and R.E.M. Initially, expectations were modest, with Atlantic Records hoping it would sell as well as The J-Hawks' album. Despite internal skepticism and a desire by Atlantic's head of A&R to shelve it as "irreleasable," it was issued in July 1994. After initial slow sales outside the South, it was almost dropped, but a performance on the *Late Show with David Letterman* dramatically boosted its profile. It went gold by mid-December 1994, platinum by February 1995, rose to number one on the Billboard 200 in May 1995 (its 44th week), and went quadruple platinum. By the end of 1995, it was Billboard's number one album of the year with 12 million certified sales, outperforming records by Garth Brooks, Boyz II Men, TLC, and Pearl Jam. As of 1996, it had sold 16 million copies, and with streaming factored in by the late 2010s, it was recertified at 21 times platinum, making it the ninth highest certified album in chart history according to the RIAA. "Hoody came to regard cracked rear view as a perial butt joke, but it was in fact a quote of a very poignant lyric from a 1987 John Hyatt song, a metaphor about a car's actual rear view mirror."
**Kootchippop (Unknown Year) - Self-Released Debut EP** : This EP was recorded by the band using money from a small settlement after a record deal with J.R.S. Records fell through in Los Angeles. The title, which the band members now admit was "dopey," was inspired by a term comedian Shirley Hemphill coined for female genitalia in a thong bikini, which "seemed funny at the time." Despite its title, *Kootchippop* sold remarkably well regionally, shifting 25,000 copies across the states where Hootie played clubs, fraternities, and sororities, a stunning figure for a self-released CD. "Dopey title and all, Kuchipop sold amazingly well regionally."

## songs
**I'm Going Home (Unknown Year)** : This song was performed by Hootie & the Blowfish live on television from the Shrine Auditorium in Los Angeles in February 1996, during the Grammy Awards. The performance occurred at a time when the band was not only performing a track from their 12-times platinum album but also winning statuettes, including the coveted Best New Artist prize. "The song was called I'm Going Home, but this for some wasn't going home anytime soon."
**Hold My Hand (1995) - Breakthrough Single** : This song was the band's first hit, breaking into the top 10 on Billboard's Hot 100 at number 10 in the week ending February 18, 1995, after a slow, steady climb. It kicked off a year of mega-stardom for the band. The song was brought to the band by drummer Jim Sonefeld, who was its primary songwriter and whose previous bands had turned it down. To Sonefeld, the song was about "compassion in a cruel world," while Darius Rucker interpreted it as a protest song about "love in the face of hatred," given his experiences with racial epithets in college. For the re-recording on *Cracked Rear View*, Atlantic Records secured David Crosby to provide backing vocals on the chorus. "Whatever it was, Hold My Hand was a rousing sing-along, a hit in the making."
**Let Her Cry (1995) - Second Single** : This impassioned ballad was issued as the second single from *Cracked Rear View* in March 1995. It quickly climbed the charts, entering the Top 40 by April and the Top 20 by May. "And it was in the top 40 by April, the top 20 by May."
**Only Want to Be With You (1995) - Biggest Hit** : This song was the third single from *Cracked Rear View*. While "Let Her Cry" was reaching its peak, rock and adult contemporary stations were already jumping on this perky track. It eventually became Hootie & the Blowfish's biggest hit, peaking at number 6 on the charts. "Only wannabe with you became their biggest hit at number 6."
**Time (1995) - Final Single** : This was the final single released from the album *Cracked Rear View*. It eventually reached number 14 on the charts. "and the final single, Time, Eventually reached number 14."
**I Don't Understand (Unknown Year) - Early Composition** : This was one of Hootie & the Blowfish's early original compositions. According to Tim Summer, it was "redolent with their Southern Alt Rock influences," indicating the band was still finding its unique voice while drawing heavily from its foundational sounds. "Early Hoody compositions like I Don't Understand were redolent with their Southern Alt Rock influences."
**Drowning (Unknown Year) - Protest Song** : Included on *Cracked Rear View*, this was a punchy protest song featuring pointed lyrics influenced by the racism Darius Rucker had experienced. Rucker used the song to decry the Confederate flags that were still flying at the South Carolina Statehouse in Columbia at the time. "Drowning, on which Rucker decried the Confederate flags still flying at the South Carolina Statehouse in Columbia."
**Motherless Child (Unknown Year) - Acapella Spiritual** : This song closed the album *Cracked Rear View* and served as a solo vocal showcase for Darius Rucker. It was an a cappella take on a slavery-era spiritual, highlighting Rucker's powerful voice and the band's willingness to include diverse musical influences and historical context on their mainstream debut. "And the album closed with a solo vocal showcase for Rucker, and Acapelitek on the slavery era spiritual, motherless child."

## curiosities
**University Formation in the 1980s** : Hootie & the Blowfish formed at the University of South Carolina in the 1980s. This collegiate origin contributed to their "college bar band" identity and their initial regional popularity on the frat and sorority circuit. "Judy and the Blowfish, a band formed at the University of South Carolina in the 1980s."
**Best New Artist Grammy Win (1996)** : In February 1996, the band won the coveted Best New Artist prize at the Grammy Awards. This achievement marked a significant peak in their early career, cementing their status as a major success, despite initial skepticism from critics and their label. "not only performing a track from their 12 times platinum album, but also winning statuettes, including the coveted best new artist prize."
**Dominant Album Sales of the 1990s** : Hootie & the Blowfish had the top-selling album of 1995 with *Cracked Rear View*, and it became one of the best-sellers of the entire decade. The album remarkably outsold contemporary records by bands like Pearl Jam, Toad the Wet Sprocket, and R.E.M., demonstrating their immense commercial success. "The band not only had the top selling album of 1995, but one of the best sellers of the decade, outselling any album by the bands that had paved the way for Judy, from Pearl Jam to Toad the Wet Sprocket to R&M."
**"Snoozy and the Bland Fish" Nickname** : During the 1990s, many rock critics found Hootie's music to be mild, derivative, and inexplicably popular, leading some to jokingly refer to them as "snoozy and the bland fish." This critical perception contrasted sharply with their massive commercial success and hinted at a disconnect between critical acclaim and popular appeal. "I'll admit, in the 90s, I made my share of snoozy and the bland fish, jokes."
**Omission from 90s CNN Documentary (2017)** : The band members, including Darius Rucker, Mark Bryan, Dean Felbert, and Jim Sonefeld, expressed mystification and hurt that a 2017 CNN documentary about music in the 1990s did not even mention them. This was despite the fact that their album *Cracked Rear View* was one of the decade's ten best-selling albums, suggesting a perceived lack of historical recognition for their massive impact. "said they were mystified and hurt, frankly, that a 2017 CNN documentary about music in the 1990s didn't even mention them, despite the fact that cracked rear view was one of the decades ten best-selling albums."
**"Normcore" Aesthetic and "Beer and Gulf" Image** : Hootie & the Blowfish were never considered "cool," even at the height of their popularity. Their aesthetic was described as "beer and gulf," and they "dressed Normcore before that had a name," suggesting an approachable, unpretentious image that might have contributed to their mainstream appeal but alienated some critics. "They were a band who dressed Norm Kor before that had a name, and their aesthetic was beer and gulf."
**Rehabilitation by John Karamanica** : Recently, some critics, notably John Karamanica of *The New York Times*, have attempted to restore the band's standing in music history. Karamanica has been described as a "one-man Hoody rehabilitation machine," dedicating several podcast episodes to the band and arguing that their significance and musicality were undervalued. "Only fairly recently have some critics tried to restore the band's standing in music history."
**Initial Band Name "The Wolf Brothers"** : When the band first consisted only of Darius Rucker and Mark Bryan, they were briefly known as The Wolf Brothers. This early incarnation pre-dated the formation of Hootie & the Blowfish as a full quartet. "By then, the band that was briefly known as The Wolf Brothers when it was just Rucker and Bryan had finally picked their name."
**Origin of the Band Name** : The name "Hootie and the Blowfish" originated from nicknames of two University of South Carolina students who were friends with Darius Rucker and were always seen together on campus. The band struggled to come up with a better name, even considering the rejected, racially charged option "Black and Blue" to denote their Black lead singer and three white band members, before settling on the now-famous moniker. "Hoody and the Blowfish were nicknames of two U of S C students, Rucker was friends with, who were always seen on campus together."
**Early R.E.M. Cover Band** : For a period, especially when Darius Rucker and Mark Bryan expanded their act, Hootie & the Blowfish were largely an R.E.M. cover band. They incorporated up to 12 R.E.M. songs into their sets, demonstrating the profound influence R.E.M. had on their early sound and repertoire. "Quote, we were basically an REM cover band, Rucker told Summer. At one point, we were playing 12 REM songs in our set."
**Creative Breakthrough with Jim Sonefeld (1989)** : The band experienced a major creative breakthrough in the fall of 1989 when original drummer Brantley Smith left to pursue Christian ministry. He was replaced by Jim Sonefeld, a slightly older U of S.C. graduate who had played in goth-rock and classic rock bands and, crucially, was a budding songwriter. Sonefeld brought with him the song "Hold My Hand," which became a pivotal hit for the band. "The band's major creative breakthrough came in the fall of 1989, when original drummer Brantley Smith, uninterested in life on the road, amicably left the band to pursue Christian ministry. He was replaced by Jim Sony Sonafeld."
**Early Demo EPs with Dick Hodgin (1990-1991)** : In 1990 and 1991, Hootie & the Blowfish recorded two cassette EPs with Raleigh, North Carolina-based producer Dick Hodgin. These EPs served as demo tapes for the band and contained many of the songs that would later appear on their major label albums, including future hits "Hold My Hand" and "Let Her Cry," showcasing their early songwriting development. "In 1990 and 91, Hoody recorded two cassette EPs with Raleigh North Carolina-based producer Dick Hodgian that doubled as demo tapes for the band."
**"Hacky-Sack Music in the Age of Flannel" (1991-1993)** : During the peak of grunge from 1991 through 1993, Hootie & the Blowfish struggled to secure a major label deal because their mellow, melodic sound was considered out of step with the prevailing trend. As Tim Summer eloquently stated, "Hoody and the Blowfish were playing hacky-sack music in the age of flannel," perfectly capturing the perceived incongruity of their style during the grunge era. "Hoody and the Blowfish were playing hacky-sack music in the age of flannel."
**Frustrated Major Label Dealings (Early 1990s)** : The band faced significant frustration in their attempts to sign with a major label. They initially turned down a deal with MCA-affiliated Gasoline Alley due to a handshake agreement with J.R.S. Records (an RCA affiliate). However, after traveling to Los Angeles in 1991 to formalize the J.R.S. deal, one of its lead executives disliked the band and pulled their studio funding, effectively nullifying the agreement. "Hoody's efforts to get signed to a major label were frustrating and almost comically ill-fated."
**Financial Success as an Unsigned Band (1993)** : Despite not being signed to a major label, Hootie & the Blowfish achieved remarkable financial success in 1993. They commanded between $5,000 and $10,000 per show and, combined with the 25,000 copies sold of their self-released *Kootchippop* EP, they earned over $300,000 that year. "Between that revenue and their live gigs, for which Hoody were now commanding between $5,000 and $10,000 a show, the band pulled down more than $300,000 in 1993 alone."
**Discovery by Tim Summer (1993)** : Tim Summer, an A&R representative for Atlantic Records, discovered Hootie & the Blowfish at a gig in Charleston, South Carolina, in mid-September 1993. He was the only A&R person in attendance and was astonished to find a regionally popular band that was packing clubs across the Eastern Seaboard and had independently sold tens of thousands of CDs, realizing the immense potential that major labels had overlooked. "When he finally saw the band in mid-September, 1993 at a gig in Charleston, South Carolina, he was the only A&R guy in the crowd."
**David Letterman's Career-Making Support (1994)** : In the late summer of 1994, legendary late show host David Letterman happened to hear a Hootie & the Blowfish song on the radio and personally requested to book the band for his show, at a time when Atlantic Records was not even promoting them for late-night television. This appearance became the band's first national showcase, and Darius Rucker credits Letterman with making their career, as *Cracked Rear View* significantly climbed the Billboard 200 chart immediately following the performance. "To this day, Darius Rucker says quote, Letterman made our career, and we can never thank him enough."
**Post-Letterman Chart Ascent (1994)** : After their pivotal performance on the *Late Show with David Letterman* in late August 1994, *Cracked Rear View*, which had been languishing on the lower runs of the Billboard 200, leaped 30 places the following week and broke into the top half of the chart. By October, the album was in the top 50, and their single "Hold My Hand" had finally entered the Hot 100, marking the beginning of their gradual but steady ascent to national prominence. "The week after the Letterman performance, the album left 30 places, then broke into the top half of the chart the following week."
**Role in Mainstreaming Jangly Americana (Mid-1990s)** : Hootie & the Blowfish were credited with leading the mainstreaming and mellowing of "jangly Americana" in the mid-1990s. Their success transformed what had been considered cutting-edge and postmodern R.E.M.-esque sounds in the 1980s into centrist rock and pop, effectively making the airwaves safe for a new generation of similar-sounding bands like Dave Matthews Band and Blues Traveler. "Some summers pet project was in the right place at the right time, leading the mainstreaming and mellowing of Djangly Americana."
**Impact on College-Friendly Bands** : By riding the charts in the mid-1990s, Hootie & the Blowfish made the airwaves safe for a new generation of jangly and college-friendly bands. Contemporaries like the Dave Matthews Band, whose major label debut had just gone platinum, and Blues Traveler, who were finally scoring their first-ever top 40 hits, benefited from the sonic landscape Hootie helped create. "Riding the charts alongside Hoody by mid 1995 were the Dave Matthews band whose major label debut had just gone platinum. And Blues Traveler, who, after languishing for several albums, were finally scoring their first ever top 40 hits in 95."
**Celebration at Williams-Brice Stadium (2022)** : In October 2022, Hootie & the Blowfish were still being celebrated at Williams-Brice Stadium in Columbia, South Carolina, during a college football game. The jumbo tron flashed Billboard chart data about *Cracked Rear View* and showed videos of the band, while thousands of students, faculty, and alumni swayed and sang along to Darius Rucker's voice, highlighting their enduring legacy as "favorite sons" of the University of South Carolina. "Throughout Williams' Bryce Stadium, thousands of students, faculty and alumni were swaying and singing along with Darius Ruckers' mighty baritone."
**Pre-Game "Thing" at U of SC** : The celebration of Hootie & the Blowfish's chart feats at University of South Carolina events, such as football games and local library talks, was a known "thing" at the institution, even 27 years after their peak. This indicates a sustained pride and cultural attachment to the band within their alma mater community. "This was, I discovered, a thing at U of S.C."

## curiosities
**Early Musical Eclecticism** : Darius Rucker, born in 1966 outside historic Charleston, consistently defied stereotypes regarding the types of music a young Black man would listen to, developing a truly eclectic array of influences. As a child, he loved The Beatles, his parents' soul records (Al Green, Gladys Knight, Otis Redding), and later, hard rock bands like Kiss. In high school, he sang Kenny Rogers songs and listened to Boston, Journey, and Kansas, stating, "I have always liked what I liked." "Rucker, born in 1966, just outside of historic Charleston, had always defined the stereotype of what types of music a young black man would listen to."
**High School Suspension for Music Taste** : While in high school, Darius Rucker was once suspended for a day for driving into the school's parking lot with his Boston cassette playing at full blast. This incident highlights his early, defiant individuality and commitment to his diverse musical preferences, even when they went against perceived norms. "He was once suspended for a day from high school for driving into the school's parking lot with his Boston cassette at full blast."
**Discovery by Mark Bryan in College** : As a sophomore at the University of South Carolina, Darius Rucker first caught the attention of freshman and budding guitarist Mark Bryan when Bryan overheard Rucker singing Billy Joel's "Honesty" in the dorm shower. This impromptu performance sparked their collaboration and the eventual formation of Hootie & the Blowfish. "Tim Summer... tells the story of how Darius Rucker, a U of SC sophomore, first caught the ear of freshman and budding guitarist Mark Bryan. Mark overheard Darius singing Billy Joel's honesty in the dorm shower."
**Profound Influence of R.E.M.** : Darius Rucker first heard R.E.M.'s "South Central Rain" on MTV while in high school, an event he describes as a "moment" that "blew my mind" and "changed the way I heard music." From that day forward, R.E.M. became everything he wanted to be, shaping his musical aspirations and leading him to learn and play their songs throughout high school and college. "That band instantly became everything I wanted to be. From that moment forward, there was nothing more important in my life than REM."
**Experiences with Racial Epithets in College** : During his college years, Darius Rucker frequently endured racial epithets at the fraternity houses and bars where he and his predominantly white bandmates (Mark Bryan and Dean Felber) played. He relied on his bandmates to support him during these difficult moments, an experience that influenced his interpretation of songs like "Hold My Hand" as a protest against hatred. "Rucker had already spent college enduring racial epithets at the frat houses and bars he and his white bandmates played, and he relied on Mark and Dean to have his back."
**Later Career Genre Shift to Country** : After Hootie & the Blowfish's moment passed at the end of the 1990s, Darius Rucker faced a unique challenge in finding a new genre for his voice. He eventually found a new home as a chart-topper in the 21st century in country music, a form of "Southern Heritage music" that had not incubated a new Black solo singer in decades. This shift successfully brought many of his 90s fans along and exemplifies his ability to defy genre pigeonholes. "Amazingly, in the 21st century, Darius Rucker found himself a chart-topper all over again, in a completely different genre, and he brought many of the fans who loved his 90s work along for the ride."

## curiosities
**Incubators of Southern Jangly Rock** : R.E.M. emerged from college towns like Athens, Georgia, which served as incubators for a distinctive "jangly rock" sound during the 1980s. This style fused the raw economy of punk with the country rock influences of Gram Parsons, defining a new strain of indie music that significantly impacted the alternative scene. "As we discussed in our REM B-52's episode of Hipperade, towns like Athens, Georgia were incubators for a jangly rock that fused the economy of punk with the country rock of Graham Parsons into an indie music that defined the 80s."
**Avatar of Quirky College Rock** : When Darius Rucker first discovered R.E.M., they were still on an independent label and widely regarded as the "avatars of quirky college rock." Their unique sound and independent spirit made them highly influential within the college radio circuit and for bands like Hootie & the Blowfish. "When Darious Rucker got into REM, they were still on an independent label and considered the avatars of quirky college rock."
**Spawned Nirvana and Hootie & the Blowfish** : R.E.M. is credited with helping to spawn the breakthrough of both Nirvana and Hootie & the Blowfish. Their pioneering sound and mainstream success paved the way for a generation of bands, proving that "what had sounded cutting edge and postmodern coming from REM in the 80s was now centrist rock and even pop in the mid 90s." "It is often said that REM, a band Kurt Cobain looked up to, helped spawn the breakthrough of Nirvana. The truth was REM also spawned Hoody and the Blowfish."

## curiosities
**A&R Who Signed Hootie & the Blowfish** : Tim Summer was a musician and A&R man who ultimately signed Hootie & the Blowfish to Atlantic Records. He authored the memoir *Only Want to Be With You, the inside story of Hootie and the Blowfish*, which provides an inside perspective on the band's journey. "Tim Summer, a musician and an R-man who would sign hoody to Atlantic Records tells the story of how Darius Rucker, a U of SC sophomore, first caught the ear of freshman and budding guitarist Mark Bryan."
**Indie Credibility** : Tim Summer brought significant indie credibility to Atlantic Records. He was a rock critic-turned bassist for the 80s Art Rock band Hugo Largo, traveled in New York hipster circles, wrote for *Altrock Bible* and *Trouser Press*, and played with no-wave pioneer Glenn Branca. His band Hugo Largo's debut EP was even produced by R.E.M.'s Michael Stipe. "Summer had serious indie cred."
**Host of Postmodern MTV** : Tim Summer had a brief stint as a host for MTV's late-night alternative video show *Postmodern MTV*. During his time there, he co-hosted with figures like Michael Stipe, showcasing his connections and immersion in the alternative music scene of the era. "There even had a brief stint hosting MTV's late night alternative video show Postmodern MTV."
**Hired by Danny Goldberg to Boost Alternative Roster** : Danny Goldberg recruited Tim Summer to Atlantic Records specifically to "beef up Atlantic's alternative roster." Summer initially championed Seattle-based punk bands like The Gits and Seven-Year Bitch, demonstrating his early focus on the more aggressive sounds of the alternative scene before discovering Hootie & the Blowfish. "Danny Goldberg brought him in to beef up Atlantic's alternative roster. And early on, summer did champion Seattle-based punk bands The Gits and Seven-Year Bitch."
**Recognized Hootie's Potential Through Comparison** : Upon seeing Hootie & the Blowfish perform in 1993, Tim Summer was able to articulate their appeal through a combination of contemporary and antecedent influences. He noted that their songwriting and production evoked Heartland rocker John Mellencamp, their jangly guitars and Southern college rock sensibility were reminiscent of R.E.M., and Darius Rucker's "throaty, growly voice" brought to mind Eddie Vedder of Pearl Jam. This blend convinced him of their commercial viability. "Something in me said, huh, Eddie Vetter plus Life's Rich Pagent Era R.E.M. Summer later wrote, and a bit like college rock Millencamp. Yep, huh, that's kind of interesting."

# artist - Nirvana

## albums
**Nevermind (1991) - Multi-Platinum Debut** : Nirvana's 1991 major label debut, *Nevermind*, was initially expected by Atlantic Records to sell only as well as the last Sonic Youth album. However, it achieved an astounding commercial success, ultimately selling 10 million copies and shattering all expectations, becoming a pivotal album in the grunge movement. "an oft repeated story about Nirvana was that their 1991 major label debut Nevermind was only expected to sell as well as the last Sonic Youth album, a benchmark that came to seem absurd when Nevermind sold 10 million copies."

## curiosities
**Leader of Grunge Movement** : Nirvana was a leading force in the grunge movement, characterized by its fusion of "East Coast dissonance" and "metallic sludge coming from the Pacific Northwest." The band achieved multi-platinum success and played a critical role in shaping the sound of alternative rock at the turn of the 1990s. "These acts, Sonic Youth and Nirvana, have been managed and signed to major label contracts by an executive named Danny Goldberg, who is widely credited with shepherding Nirvana toward their multi-platinum success."
**Quieter Evolution (1993)** : By late 1993, Nirvana, particularly evidenced by their *MTV Unplugged* set, began to explore a quieter, more acoustic sound.
