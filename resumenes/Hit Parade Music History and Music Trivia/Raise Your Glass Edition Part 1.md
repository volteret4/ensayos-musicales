## albums
**Can't Take Me Home (2000) - Debut Album** : Released in April 2000, this album appeared on Billboard's R&B Hip Hop songs chart first, then the Hot 100 Pop Chart. The album cover featured a 20-year-old Pink in an overlit photo with reflective sunglasses and close-cropped hair, designed by LaFace to make her ethnicity hard to discern. It debuted on the Billboard 200 at number 26 and on the R&B Hip Hop album chart at number 23, riding both charts for over a year. By early 2001, it was certified double platinum, establishing Pink as a star. "Can't Take Me Home was an undoubtedly R&B and hip hop influenced vehicle that was an odd preamble to a very different career."
**Missundaztood (2001) - Self-Reinvention Album** : Titled with a creative misspelling (M-I-S-S-U-N-D-A-Z-T-O-O-D), this album is historically significant as "the first great self-reinvention by a pop star of the new millennium." Pink fought her record company president, L.A. Reid, for creative control, feeling her debut album had misrepresented her. Despite Reid's initial concerns that she was committing commercial suicide, Pink pushed for a rock-flavored direction with collaborators like Linda Perry and Dallas Austin, shaping the album to be "top to bottom Pink's vision." It generated three Top 10 hits in the US and became Pink's first UK number one hit with "Just Like a Pill." The album rode the chart for over a year, sold millions, and was eventually certified Quintuple Platinum, becoming her biggest album and still her top seller to this day. "Missundaztood had been riding the album chart for more than a year and had sold millions."
**Try This (2003) - Rock-Focused Album** : Pink's third album, where she "doubled down on her third album" in a deliberate push further into rock music. While Linda Perry contributed to a couple of tracks, the majority of the album was produced and co-written by Tim Armstrong, frontman for the punk band Rancid. The album "rocked pretty hard," moving largely in Armstrong's direction with traces of hip hop and soul. Despite its artistic direction, the pop world was not entirely ready for this shift towards hard rock. It did crack the Top 10 on the Billboard 200 at number 9, thanks to the goodwill from "Missundaztood," and quickly went platinum. However, it was off the chart in just 15 weeks, unlike its predecessor's nearly two-year run. "What would it sound like when Pink recorded a full album with a full-time rock dude?"
## songs
**There You Go (2000)** : This song was Pink's first hit, co-written and produced with Kevin "She'kspere" Briggs and Kandi Burruss, who had worked on TLC's "No Scrubs." Released in January 2000, it first appeared on Billboard's R&B Hip Hop songs chart, reaching number 15, and a week later debuted on the Hot 100 Pop Chart, topping out at number 7. The song itself was described as "a kiss off to a former lover who didn't measure up, a foreshadowing of later pink hits." "Released in January of 2000, there you go, appeared on Billboard's R&B Hip Hop songs chart first, where it eventually reached number 15."
**Most Girls (2000)** : Co-written by Babyface, this track continued Pink's success from her debut album, "Can't Take Me Home." It reached number four on the pop charts in the fall of 2000. "Up a couple of spots on TRL. This debut CD continued spawning hits on both the pop and R&B charts, including most girls co-written by Babyface, which reached number four in the fall of 2000."
**You Make Me Sick (2001)** : Produced by Babyface, this song was another hit from Pink's debut album, "Can't Take Me Home." It reached number 33 in the winter of 2001. "And you make me sick, produced by Babyface. It reached number 33 in the winter of 2001."
**Lady Marmalade (2001)** : Pink's first number one hit, this song marked "the last gasp of her career as a crossover R&B star." It was a cover of a 1970s soul classic by LaBelle for the "Moulin Rouge" soundtrack. Produced by Missy Elliott, the remake featured a "4-Diva pile-up" with Christina Aguilera, Lil' Kim, Mýa, and Pink, who critics generally agreed gave the "all-around best job vocal." The lyric about "Old New Orleans" was swapped for "Moulin Rouge" for promotional synergy. It took less than two months to reach number one on the Hot 100 in spring 2001, spending five weeks there, and its music video won Video of the Year at the MTV Video Music Awards that summer. It was also the last Pink-affiliated song to make Billboard's R&B Hip Hop chart, reaching number 43. "The song would wind up as pink's first number one hit, and the last gasp of her career as a crossover R&B star."
**Get the Party Started (2001)** : This uptempo jam served as the first single from Pink's "Missundaztood" album and was intended as a "bridge" between her R&B debut and her new rock-influenced direction. It was written by Linda Perry, who deliberately aimed for a dance-pop sound featuring funk-rock guitar and hip-hop syncopation. L.A. Reid, Pink's record company president, approved it as the first single due to its commercial appeal and attitude, despite disliking most of the other rock-flavored material on the album. The song arrived in the fall of 2001 and shot to number four on the Hot 100. "The first song Linda Perry presented to Pink in their writing sessions was an uptempo jam that didn't sound much like four non-blans."
**Don't Let Me Get Me (2002)** : A future Top 10 hit from "Missundaztood," this song was co-written by Pink and Dallas Austin, an R&B and pop songwriter known for hits with Boyz II Men, Madonna, and TLC. In its lyrics, Pink included a "teasing homage" to her record company president, L.A. Reid, and "made clear... what or whom she did not want to be." Ironically, despite its rock flavor, it wasn't co-written by Linda Perry. Released in spring 2002, it went to number eight on the Hot 100. "In a kind of teasing homage to LA Reed, Pink wrote him into the lyrics of another track that wound up on Miss Undistute."
**Just Like a Pill (2002)** : Co-written by Pink and Dallas Austin for the "Missundaztood" album, this song followed "Don't Let Me Get Me" as a single in summer 2002 and also reached number eight on the Hot 100. It notably became Pink's first UK number one hit. The song served as a metaphor for Pink's "previous experiments with drugs, thinly disguised as a gripe, about a toxic relationship." "Pill was a metaphor for Pink's previous experiments with drugs, thinly disguised as a gripe, about a toxic relationship."
**Family Portrait (2003)** : The fourth single from "Missundaztood," this song was co-written by Pink with hip-hop producer Scott Storch. It was described as Pink "baring it all," recounting "the violence of her parents' divorce." Even this harrowing and personal song was a hit, reaching number 20 by early 2003. "For the fourth single, Family Portrait, co-written with hip-hop producer Scott Storch, Pink Bared It All, recounting the violence of her parents' divorce."
**Trouble (2003)** : The first single from Pink's third album, "Try This," this song represented her deliberate shift towards hard rock. Critics noted its strong resemblance to Nirvana's cover of "Molly's Lips" by the indie rockers The Vaselines. While it performed well internationally, making the Top 10 in the UK and Australia, it was "kind of a bomb" in America, peaking at number 68 on the Hot 100. Despite its commercial struggles in the US, Grammy voters recognized its rock credibility, giving Pink a Best Female Rock Vocal Performance statue for the song. "Several critics even pointed out that the album's first single, Trouble. Bore a strong resemblance to Nirvana's cover of Molly's Lips by Indy Rockers, The Vaseline."
**Feel Good Time (2003)** : A song given to Pink by folk funk alt rocker Beck, produced by electronic artist William Orbit. It was recorded for the soundtrack to the "Charlie's Angels" sequel, "Full Throttle." The song reached number 60 on the Hot 100 and was remarkable for its existence, essentially being "an electro rock-bex song with Pink's vocals on it." "Only reached number 60 on the Hot 100, but was remarkable just for existing."
**God Is a DJ (2004)** : The second single from Pink's "Try This" album, this track featured a more dance-rock sound, chosen perhaps to "right the ship" after the struggles of "Trouble." Despite appearing on the soundtrack to the hit 2004 movie "Mean Girls," it did not make the Hot 100, instead "bubbling under the chart at number 103." "For the album's second single, Pink went with the more dance rock sound of God is a DJ. But it didn't write the ship."
## curiosities
**Early Life and Nickname** : Born Alicia Beth Moore in 1979 in Doylestown, Pennsylvania, to a Catholic father and Jewish mother. She developed a potent voice at an early age and focused on singing, not gymnastics (which would later become useful in her career). After her parents' divorce, she became a troubled teen who often ran away from home. One plausible origin story for her nickname "Pink" is that she was "the only white girl in her friend group." "Alicia Beth Moore, born in 1979 to a Catholic father and Jewish mother in the Philadelphia suburb of Doyle's Town, Pennsylvania, developed a potent voice at an early age."
**Early Career in Philadelphia Nightclubs** : As a teenager, Pink was already performing in Philadelphia nightclubs. She secured a regular Friday singing slot after impressing the crowd at an open mic night with a Mary J. Blige song. She also served as a dancer and then a vocalist for local R&B acts like Schools of Thought and Basic Instinct, although she never recorded with them. "By her early teenage years, Alicia Moore, a troubled teen who, after her parents divorced, often ran away from home, was already performing at Philly Nightclubs."
**Marketed as Racially Ambiguous** : For her debut album, "Can't Take Me Home," LaFace Records intentionally presented Pink in a way that made her ethnicity hard to discern, marketing her as a "hip hop diva in training" and "racially ambiguous." This strategy "worked on the charts," and cultural critics like Ashley Reese of Jezebel, in an article titled "Soulful Vanilla Child, When Pink Was Black," commented on how Pink's "black adjacent presentation worked on the charts." "On the cover, in an overlit photo, the 20-year-old pink wore reflective sunglasses and very close cropped hair, a look that made her ethnicity hard to discern."
**Individualism and Fighting the Record Label** : Pink established her individualism by fighting her record label, LaFace Records, for creative control over her second album, "Missundaztood." She challenged the direction the label had set for her debut, which she felt misrepresented her true artistic identity. Despite label president L.A. Reid's belief that her new rock-oriented approach would be "commercial suicide," Pink persisted and ultimately was granted "the freedom to fail," an opportunity she appreciated. "This individualism was established two decades ago when Alicia Moore took on her record label, fought them to a draw and was told they would give her the freedom to fail."
**Madonna as an Inspiration and Encounter** : Pink cited Madonna as a significant inspiration for her willingness to take risks and evolve her sound. In a later interview, Pink expressed admiration for Madonna's career longevity and ability to change styles successfully. Recently, Pink discussed meeting Madonna with Howard Stern, clarifying that while she loves Madonna, the narrative that she was "fan-girling" was twisted, as Madonna had actually invited Pink into her dressing room. "That's why Madonna has always been an inspiration for me."
**Influence on Avril Lavigne and Pop-Rock Shift** : Pink was "ahead of a shift in pop music" towards a more rock-oriented direction, anticipating the decline of Britney Spears and Backstreet Boys-style teen pop. Her album "Missundaztood" opened up the market for artists like Avril Lavigne, who was signed by L.A. Reid around the same time Pink was working on her second album. Lavigne's album "Let Go" then blew up months after "Missundaztood" dropped. "In essence, Pink had opened up the market for Avril."
**Lack of Rock Chart Presence** : Despite her strong rock influence, multiple rock-leaning albums, and even winning a Grammy for Best Female Rock Vocal Performance for "Trouble," Pink, like Madonna, has never made Billboard's rock tracks charts. This is "fairly remarkable" as few female pop stars of her generation have integrated rock into popular music more than Pink, and the average fan often describes her as a rocker. "Pink, however, like Madonna, has not. This is fairly remarkable because few female pop stars of her generation have done more to integrate rock back into post-millennial, post-hip hop popular music than Pink."

## songs
**Burning Up (1983)** : One of Madonna's earliest singles, released in 1983, it is described as "hard to pigeonhole" – a club record featuring prominent rock guitar. Inspired by her years in New York City punk clubs, the song possessed a sinister edge that sounded less like disco and more like post-punk or new wave. A quarter-century later, Blender Magazine ranked "Burning Up" as one of its "40 quintessential new wave songs." Due to its dance beats and Madonna being a new female artist, it only charted on the Billboard Club Play chart, failing to reach the Hot 100 or rock charts. Madonna never really recorded anything like it again after achieving mainstream success with poppier hits. "Burning Up is hard to pigeonhole. This a club record with prominent rock guitar."

## curiosities
**Punk Club Inspiration and Early Demos** : Madonna's early musical direction was heavily inspired by her experiences in New York City punk clubs, leading her to record early demos with a punk band. This influence was evident in her 1983 single "Burning Up," which featured prominent rock guitar and a post-punk/new wave edge. "Madonna was inspired by her years in New York City punk clubs. She'd even recorded some early demos with a punk band."
**Chart Performance and Genre Categorization** : Despite songs like "Burning Up" having rock elements and being lauded by magazines like Blender as quintessential new wave, Madonna's early work with dance beats, coupled with her status as a new female artist in 1983, limited its chart success to the Billboard Club Play chart, preventing it from reaching the Hot 100 or rock charts. She was never played by rock stations. This contrasts with contemporaries like Pat Benatar, who maintained rock radio presence. "But because Burning Up had dance beats and because Madonna was not only a new artist in 1983, but a woman, the only Billboard chart it touched was the club play chart."
**Influence and Personal Encounter with Pink** : Madonna has been cited by Pink as a significant inspiration, particularly for her ability to evolve her musical styles and maintain a successful career. Pink recounted on Howard Stern how Madonna had invited her into her dressing room, clarifying a rumored "fan-girling" incident. "That's why Madonna has always been an inspiration for me."
