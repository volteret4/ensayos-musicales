## albums
**Madonna (1983) - Self-Titled Debut LP** : This album, Madonna's first, was released on Sire Records in the summer of 1983. It contained early hits such as "Holiday," "Borderline," and "Lucky Star," with "Lucky Star" being the final hit from this debut. "Her self-titled debut LP on Sire Records arrived in the summer of 1983."
**Like a Virgin (1984) - Second Album** : This album featured the title track which became Madonna's first number one hit, spending six weeks at the top through January 1985. The album was propelled to the number one spot in February 1985, holding for three weeks, largely due to the success and impact of the "Material Girl" video. "Like a Virgin album to number one in February 1985, where it held for three weeks."
**True Blue (1986) - Third Album** : Released a few weeks after "Live to Tell" became a number one hit, this album included "Live to Tell" as its first single. It proved to be a "hit song jukebox," generating five top five hits, which was more than any other Madonna LP. By May 1987, the album had achieved quadruple platinum status and was on its way to seven million in sales. "A few weeks later, Madonna released her third album, True Blue."
**Who's That Girl Soundtrack (1987)** : This soundtrack was released for Madonna's movie bomb "Who's That Girl." Despite the film's poor box office performance, the soundtrack spun off hits for Madonna, including the title track, "Who's That Girl," which went to number one on the Hot 100. "The title track of the Who's That Girl Soundtrack was what you might call a quicky mashup, already made."
**You Can Dance (1987) - Remix Album** : This remix album was released at the end of 1987 to provide fans with new material. It featured a new bonus track titled "Spotlight." "At the end of 1987, to tide over her fans, she released a remix album titled You Can Dance that contained a new bonus track Spotlight."
**Like a Prayer (1989) - Studio Album** : Following her preceding studio albums, "Like a Prayer" was eclectic and packed with future hits. Its title track was conceived as gospel, and its second single, "Express Yourself," was reinvented into a sleek house music anthem. "Like Madonna's preceding studio albums, Like a Prayer was eclectic and packed with future hits."
**The Immaculate Collection (1990) - Greatest Hits Collection** : Issued in late 1990, this compilation became Madonna's first and best-selling greatest hits collection, achieving diamond sales. It controversially included "Into the Groove" (a non-charting US single) but omitted several of her 80s hits, including number ones like "Who's That Girl," "Angel," "Dress You Up," and "True Blue," demonstrating Madonna's confidence in shaping the narrative of her first decade. "In late 1990, Madonna issued her first and ultimately best selling greatest hits collection, the diamond selling the immaculate collection."
**Ray of Light (1998)** : This album marked the culmination of Madonna's pivot to Electronica music in the 1990s, described as a triumphant artistic statement. "culminating in her triumphant 1998 album Ray of Light."

## songs
**Holiday (1983)** : An early hit from her self-titled debut LP, "Holiday" cracked the top 40 and peaked at number 16 by January 1984. "By January 1984, Holiday peaked at number 16."
**Borderline (1984)** : Approximately five months after "Holiday," "Borderline" became Madonna's first top 10 hit, peaking at number 10. "About five months later, Borderline became her first top 10 hit, peaking at number 10."
**Lucky Star (1984)** : As the final hit from her debut album, the video for "Lucky Star" established Madonna as a fashion icon, inspiring many "Madonna wannabes" with its lacy, naval-bearing outfit. It became her first top five hit, reaching number four. "Lucky Star eventually reached number four, Madonna's first top five hit."
**Like a Virgin (1984)** : This title track from her second album was debuted by Madonna at the first MTV Video Music Awards in September 1984, where she appeared in a disheveled bridal gown. It quickly cracked the top five and became her first number one hit a week later, spending six weeks at the top through January 1985. "And a week later, it became Madonna's first number one hit. It would spend six weeks at number one, through January 1985."
**Material Girl (1985)** : The video for "Material Girl," released at the start of 1985, paid homage to Marilyn Monroe's performance in "Gentleman Prefer Blondes," satirizing Madonna's glamorous image while simultaneously making her more so. The catchy phrase "Material Girl" became a lasting nickname for Madonna, and the single reached number two on the Hot 100. "Most people's first exposure to material girl was when its video arrived at the start of 1985."
**Crazy for You (1985)** : This delicate ballad presented an unexpected sound for Madonna and was featured in the movie "Vision Quest," where Madonna made a cameo as a bar singer. Due to bickering between Sire and Geffen, it was issued by Geffen as a 45. The song climbed the charts through late winter and spring of 1985, ultimately reaching number one in May, where it displaced USA for Africa's "We Are the World" in a "giant killing feat." "Crazy for you was another sign of Madonna's growing imperiality."
**Gambler (1985)** : This was a second Madonna track from the "Vision Quest" soundtrack, but it went unreleased as a single in the US due to a compromise reached between Sire and Geffen executives during a dispute over single releases. "A second Madonna track from Vision Quest called Gambler went unreleased as a single."
**Into the Groove (1985)** : Arguably Madonna's "greatest dance floor song," "Into the Groove" was not officially released as a Hot 100 single in America. Madonna's label chose not to issue it as a single, fearing it would divert radio attention from other releases, a decision described as a "miscarriage of chart justice." While it was a number one single in the UK and other countries, in the US, it appeared on the B-side of a 12-inch single, qualifying it only for Billboard's Dance Charts. "Into the groove was not allowed to even chart in America."
**Angel (1985)** : This song, Madonna's take on New Wave synth pop, was produced by Nile Rodgers and served as the A-side of a 12-inch single in the US, with "Into the Groove" on the B-side. "Angel" peaked at number five in June 1985, continuing Madonna's top five streak. It was later omitted from "The Immaculate Collection." "The A-side of that 12-inch was the like a virgin track, Angel."
**Dress You Up (1985)** : The video for "Dress You Up" was filmed during Madonna's first headlining "The Virgin Tour" and epitomized "Madonna Mania," featuring a concert audience filled with "wannabes" replicating her style, including floppy hair bows, lace gloves, and lingerie worn as outerwear. This single reached number five in October 1985. It was later omitted from "The Immaculate Collection." "Dress You Up captured the peak of Madonna Mania."
**Live to Tell (1986)** : This dramatic torch song, from the soundtrack of the Sean Penn movie "At Close Range," featured "heartbearing lyrics about insecurities, secrets, and doubts," making it Madonna's most theatrical single to date. It was perceived as revealing her post-marriage vulnerability. With no dedicated soundtrack album and her next LP unreleased, fans were compelled to buy the single, driving it to number one by June 1986. It was later included on her "True Blue" album. "Live to Tell was Madonna's most theatrical single to date."
**Papa Don't Preach (1986)** : The second single from "True Blue," this story song about a pregnant teenager wanting to keep her baby and seeking her father's approval "scrambled Madonna's public persona." Its controversial message placed it in the Reagan era culture wars, with conservatives defending it and pro-choice progressives decrying it. Despite or because of the controversy, the "Washing Disco Strings" track became a number one hit by August 1986, Madonna's first back-to-back Hot 100 chart-topper, and was named Billboard's 1986 Song of the Summer. "Papa don't preach, scrambled Madonna's public persona."
**True Blue (1986)** : The third single from the album of the same name, "True Blue" was an homage to the girl group sound of the early 1960s. It reached number three on the charts in November 1986. This song was later omitted from "The Immaculate Collection." "Its third single, The Title Track, True Blue, and homage to the girl group sound of the early 60s reached number three in November of 86."
**Open Your Heart (1987)** : The fourth single from "True Blue," this "throbbing banger" was accompanied by a "racy video" set in an "arty peep show" where Madonna performed as the main striptease attraction. It became the album's third number one single in February 1987. "Open Your Heart, a throbbing banger that benefited from a racy video set in an arty peep show with Madonna as the main striptease attraction."
**La Isla Bonita (1987)** : The fifth single from "True Blue," this "tropical breezy" song was a Latin-flavored pop track featuring flamenco guitar and a Spanish title. Madonna portrayed a flamenco dancer in its video. It reached number four in May 1987. "La Isla Bonita" is notably the 1980s Madonna song that most consistently appears on her concert set lists and was included on "The Immaculate Collection" over other hits, suggesting it's a "fond memento" of her imperial phase. "La Isla Bonita reached number four in May of 87."
**Who's That Girl (1987)** : This quicky mashup was the title track from the "Who's That Girl" movie soundtrack, combining the "throbbing baseline of open your heart" with Latin flavor and Spanish chorus lyrics, similar to "La Isla Bonita." It reached number one on the Hot 100 a couple of weeks after the movie had tanked at the box office. Despite being a number one hit, this song was notably omitted from "The Immaculate Collection." "Who's That Girl The Song reached the Hot 100's top 10 in the summer of 1987."
**Causing a Commotion (1987)** : Described as "Madonna by numbers," this dance-pop track was the kind of song that Madonna was "mostly out growing," and one that imitators like Regina or Stacey Q could have scored with. However, for Madonna, it became a number two hit in the fall of 1987 and was her 13th consecutive top five hit on the Hot 100. "Causing a commotion was Madonna by numbers, the kind of dance pop she was mostly out growing."
**Spotlight (1987)** : This was a new bonus track included on Madonna's remix album, "You Can Dance," released at the end of 1987. "You Can Dance that contained a new bonus track Spotlight."
**Like a Prayer (1989)** : Madonna conceived this title track from her 1989 album as gospel, hiring the Andre Crouch Choir, who had previously sung on Michael Jackson's "Man in the Mirror." Madonna's take, however, sounded more like a dance floor where she found spirituality. Its video, mixing religious iconography (Stigmata, burning crosses) with sex with a Black Christ figure, generated the "most controversy of any Madonna production to date," leading Pepsi to drop a $5 million ad campaign (which Madonna still kept the money from). It soared to number one on the Hot 100 in just six weeks, her fastest rising hit since "Like a Virgin" in 1984. "Madonna conceived the song as Gospal, and she even hired the famed Andre Crouch Choir..."
**Express Yourself (1989)** : The second single from "Like a Prayer," this song was famously reinvented by producer Shep Pettibone from a horn-inflected Latin jam into a sleek, throbbing house music anthem. The remixed version, heard in its multi-million dollar music video (directed by David Fincher as an homage to Fritz Lang's "Metropolis"), became the standard radio version. Madonna was "ahead of the curve" by bringing a house beat to Top 40 radio before a wider wave of house singles crossed over later in 1989. The combination of the original and remix pushed the song to number two on the Hot 100 by July. "its second single, Express Yourself. Was reinvented by producer Shep Pettybone from a horn-inflected Latin flavor jam into a sleek, throbbing house music anthem."
**Cherish (1989)** : Following "Express Yourself," this song was seen as a sequel to "True Blue," incorporating girl group energy with elements of Doo-wop and 60s Sunshine Pop. Madonna interpolated a lyric from The Association's 1966 number one hit, also titled "Cherish." This was her most optimistic and openly romantic hit of the period, written to cheer herself up after her divorce from Sean Penn. It became a smash, reaching number two by October 1989, marking her record 16th consecutive Top 5 hit in Hot 100 History. "Madonna's Cherish was her most optimistic and openly romantic hit of this period."
**Oh Father (1989)** : This "beautifully orchestrated, broke and very sad ballad" was Madonna's most personal single, featuring lyrics mourning her mother (who died when Madonna was a child) and expressing resentment towards her father for remarrying. Considered a strange fit for Top 40 radio, its release was Madonna's last imperial move of the 1980s, ending her imperial phase. It peaked at number 20 in the first week of January 1990, her first single since 1984 to miss the Top 10, let alone Top 5. "In the lyrics, Madonna mourned her mother who died when Madonna was a child. And she exercised her feelings of resentment toward her father who had remarried."
**Keep It Together (1990)** : This song was a Top 10 hit in 1990 and helped Madonna revive her status after the end of her initial imperial phase, igniting a new wave of hits. "1990 top 10 hits like Keep It Together and the number one smash Vogue ignited a new wave of hits."
**Vogue (1990)** : A number one smash hit in 1990, "Vogue" helped ignite a new wave of hits for Madonna and is often cited as part of her subsequent imperial phase in the early 90s. "the number one smash Vogue ignited a new wave of hits."
**Justify My Love (1990)** : This song is recognized as a trend-defining and trend-defying hit from the early 1990s, with scholars like Tom Ewing arguing it was part of Madonna's last imperial phase. "trend defining and trend defying hits like Justify My Love."

## curiosities
**Birth Details** : Madonna Louise Ciccone was born in 1958 in Bay City, Michigan. "the woman born Madonna Louise Chaconne in 1958 in Bay City, Michigan."
**Archetypal Imperial Phase (1984-1989)** : Madonna's 5-year chart run from 1984 through 1989 is considered an unprecedented, potentially unrepeatable, and archetypal "Imperial Phase." This period saw her achieve a remarkable streak of top five hits that lasted throughout the 1980s, beginning with "Lucky Star" and "Like a Virgin." "But Madonna's 5-year run of hits from 1984 through 1989, which I submit was one long Imperial phase checks a lot of the boxes."
**Fashion Icon Status Established (1984)** : The music video for her single "Lucky Star" was instrumental in establishing Madonna as a fashion icon, with her lacy, naval-bearing outfit inspiring a multitude of "Madonna wannabes" and marking a pivotal moment in her early career. "First, the video for her next single, Lucky Star, the final hit from the debut album, established Madonna as a fashion icon, with a lacy, naval-bearing outfit that would inspire scores of Madonna wannabes."
**Debut Performance at MTV VMAs (September 1984)** : Madonna delivered the "most talked about performance" at the inaugural MTV Video Music Awards in September 1984, debuting "Like a Virgin" while famously arriving on stage in a disheveled bridal gown. This performance was a key factor in her transformation into "80s pop's new Imperial Queen." "Madonna delivered the most talked about performance at the first MTV Video Music Awards in September of 1984."
**Enduring "Material Girl" Nickname** : The "Material Girl" video, an homage to Marilyn Monroe's "Diamonds Are a Girl's Best Friend," both satirized and amplified Madonna's glamorous public image. The catchy phrase led to "Material Girl" becoming a lasting nickname for Madonna, still used by headline writers today. "To this day, headline writers cannot resist calling Madonna the material girl."
**Record Label Dispute over Singles (1985)** : Executives at Sire and Geffen Records bickered over which Madonna songs could be released as singles from the "Vision Quest" soundtrack, a situation indicative of her rising "imperiality" due to having "too many hits." This dispute resulted in "Crazy for You" being released by Geffen, while "Gambler" was withheld as a single. "Reportedly, executives at two labels, sire and geffen, bickered with each other over which Madonna songs could be released as singles."
**Uninvited to "We Are the World" (1985)** : Madonna was notably not invited to participate in USA for Africa's "We Are the World" project. The irony was highlighted when her song "Crazy for You" later unseated "We Are the World" from the number one spot, a "giant killing feat" that underscored her ascendant status. "The fact that Madonna was not invited to We Are the World only made the irony more delicious."
**"Into the Groove" Sacrificed for Chart Strategy (1985)** : Madonna's label chose not to release "Into the Groove" as a single in America, sacrificing what was arguably her "greatest dance floor song" due to label whims and a fear it would divert radio attention from other releases. This act was seen as a "miscarriage of chart justice" but also an "Imperial flex," demonstrating her power within the major label system. "Again, talk about Imperial, Madonna sacrificed a major hit to the whims of the major label system, and not just any hit, arguably her greatest dance floor song."
**The Virgin Tour (1985)** : This marked Madonna's first headlining tour, during which she filmed the video for her single "Dress You Up." "By then, she was on her first headlining tour, the virgin tour."
**Marriage to Sean Penn (August 1985)** : Madonna married actor Sean Penn in August 1985 in Malibu, an event that became a major tabloid story and a "circus" complete with helicopters flying overhead, significantly contributing to her "center of the culture status." "One other thing that happened to Madonna in her crazy 1985, she married actor Sean Penn."
**Sean Penn's Career Influence (1986)** : Madonna's marriage to Sean Penn began to "bear career fruit" in 1986, starting with her first hit of that year, "Live to Tell," a dramatic torch song from the soundtrack of his film "At Close Range." "the Sean Penn story began to bear career fruit for Madonna in 1986, starting with her first hit of that year."
**First Back-to-Back Hot 100 Number Ones (1986)** : With "Papa Don't Preach" soaring to number one by August 1986, Madonna achieved her first instance of scoring back-to-back Hot 100 chart-toppers, following "Live to Tell." "The first time Madonna had scored back-to-back Hot 100 sharp toppers."
**"Papa Don't Preach" and Reagan Era Culture Wars (1986)** : The controversial message of "Papa Don't Preach" — a pregnant teenager's decision to keep her baby — placed the song squarely in the Reagan era culture wars. It was defended by conservatives and decried by pro-choice progressives, with the controversy ultimately being "good for business." "The song became part of the Reagan era culture wars, but the controversy was good for business..."
**Five Top Five Hits from "True Blue" (1986-1987)** : The "True Blue" album was a "hit song jukebox" that generated an unprecedented five top five hits for Madonna, more than any other of her LPs. This showcased her stylistic breadth, covering Torch Balladry, Neo Disco, Retro R&B, Electro Dance, and Latin Pop. "The True Blue album turned out to be a hit song jukebox, generating five top five hits, the most of any Madonna LP."
**Wave of Imitators (1986-1987)** : As Madonna expanded her stylistic range, a new wave of imitators, including Regina, Stacey Q, and Debbie Gibson, crowded the charts with "the Madonna Sound" (perky dance pop). This phenomenon served as further evidence of her "elite status" and reinforced her position as the "apex predator of pop." "And while Madonna was exploring her stylistic breath, a new wave of Madonna imitators was crowding the charts, further evidence of her elite status."
**Back-to-Back Movie Bombs (1986-1987)** : Madonna starred in two consecutive movie flops: "Shanghai Surprise" (1986) with Sean Penn, which nearly bankrupted George Harrison's production company, and "Who's That Girl" (1987), a screwball comedy that also performed poorly. These cinematic failures occurred at the apex of her musical fame. "In 1986 and 1987, Madonna starred in Back to Back movie bombs."
**Musical Dominance Despite Movie Flops (1987)** : Despite her notorious movie bombs, Madonna's musical imperiality remained strong. She dominated the charts twice with songs from one of these failures, "Who's That Girl," with the title track reaching number one on the Hot 100 even after the film had tanked. This was considered the "ultimate imperial flex," demonstrating her ability to reclaim her sound and top the charts regardless of her film career. "In music, Madonna proved as imperial as ever. She confirmed it by dominating the charts twice with songs from one of these bombs."
**13th Consecutive Top 5 Hit (1987)** : "Causing a Commotion" marked Madonna's 13th consecutive top five hit on the Hot 100, highlighting her sustained and impressive chart performance. "For those keeping score, causing a commotion had been Madonna's 13th consecutive top five hit on the Hot 100."
**Year-Long Sabbatical and Divorce (1988-1989)** : Madonna took 1988 off to address her "crumbling marriage" to Sean Penn, leading to their divorce by 1989, and to plan her next album. This period of inactivity, in hindsight, was not the end of her imperial phase but a strategic pause. "She took 1988 off to deal with her crumbling marriage to Sean Penn. They would divorce by 1989."
**"Like a Prayer" Video Controversy (1989)** : The music video for "Like a Prayer" sparked the "most controversy of any Madonna production to date" due to its combination of religious iconography (Stigmata, burning crosses) with sex featuring a Black Christ figure. This put Madonna back into the "Culture Wars" and led Pepsi to cancel a planned $5 million ad campaign, though Madonna was able to keep the money. "Its video also stirred up the most controversy of any Madonna production to date, as its mix of religious iconography from Stigmata to Burning Crosses, coupled with sex with a black Christ figure, put Madonna right back into the Culture Wars again."
**Fastest Rising Hit Since Debut (1989)** : "Like a Prayer" soared to number one on the Hot 100 in just six weeks, making it her fastest rising hit since "Like a Virgin" in 1984, signaling that her imperial phase was clearly not over yet. "Like a prayer soared to No. 1 on the Hot 100 in just six weeks, her fastest rising hit since Like a Virgin in 1984, the start of her imperial phase."
**Ahead of the House Music Curve (1989)** : Madonna was "ahead of the curve" with her remixed "Express Yourself," which, with its multi-million dollar music video, established a house beat on Top 40 radio. This occurred before a broader wave of house singles began crossing over on the pop charts later in 1989. "Yet again, Madonna was ahead of the curve, getting a house beat on top 40 radio ahead of a wave of house singles that would begin crossing over on the pop charts later in 89."
**"Cherish" as Post-Divorce Uplift (1989)** : Madonna wrote "Cherish" as a "wistful song to cheer herself up" following her divorce from Sean Penn, making it her most optimistic and openly romantic hit of that period. "A wistful song she wrote to cheer herself up after her divorce from Sean Penn."
**Record 16th Consecutive Top 5 Hit (1989)** : When "Cherish" reached number two by October 1989, it marked Madonna's record-setting 16th consecutive Top 5 hit in Hot 100 History, a streak that only The Beatles had previously come close to matching. "Madonna's record 16th consecutive Top 5 hit in Hot 100 History only the Beatles had come close to that streak."
**End of 1980s Imperial Phase (1990)** : The release of "Oh Father" marked the end of Madonna's 1980s imperial phase. Its peak at number 20 in the first week of January 1990 was her first single since 1984 to miss the Top 10, let alone the Top 5. "Oh, father peaked at number 20, the first week of January 1990, her first single since 1984 to miss the Top 10, let alone the Top 5."
**Early 1990s as "Last Imperial Phase"** : Many fans, critics, and scholars, including Imperial phase scholar Tom Ewing, argue that the early 1990s, ignited by hits like "Keep It Together" and "Vogue," constituted Madonna's "last imperial phase," characterized by trend-defining and trend-defying hits such as "Justify My Love." "many Madonna fans, critics and observers, including Imperial phase scholar Tom Ewing argue that the early 90s was Madonna's last imperial phase, during which she scored her last major round of trend defining and trend defying hits like Justify My Love."
**"The Immaculate Collection" as Narrative Reshaper (1990)** : Madonna used "The Immaculate Collection" as a "bookend" to her 1980s imperial phase, confidently shaping the narrative of her first decade by including classics like "Into the Groove" (despite its non-single status in the US) and intentionally omitting several number one and top five hits like "Angel," "Dress You Up," "True Blue," "Who's That Girl," and "Causing a Commotion." "I regard the immaculate collection as the bookend to Madonna's 80s imperial phase, her last flex, with regal confidence she was already shaping the narrative of her first decade, discarding what was expendable and enshrining her class."
**"La Isla Bonita" as a Lasting Favorite/Mementos** : "La Isla Bonita" is the 1980s song Madonna most consistently includes in her concert set lists, even on tours where she largely ignores older hits, and she included it on "The Immaculate Collection" over other hits. This suggests it's a "fond memento" of her imperial period when she "could get away with anything" and her phase seemed endless. "In the decades since Madonna's epic streak, fans have noticed that the 180s song that always makes her concert set lists, even on tours where she is mostly ignoring her old hits, is the 1987 single La Isla Bonita, the final single from her true blue album."

# genre - Imperial Phase

## curiosities
**Definition and Origins** : The "Imperial Phase" is a 21st-century term describing a phenomenon in popular music fandom that dates back to its beginning. It characterizes a moment when an artist seemingly "has the secret of pop" and can achieve a hit with almost any release. "It describes the moment when an artist seems to have the secret of pop and can score a hit with seemingly anything."
**Identifying Characteristics** : An Imperial Phase can be identified by streaks of number one or top 10 hits, as well as "improbable hits" (success with unexpected sounds). A streak of top ten hits is a "sure sign," and the emergence of imitators further confirms an artist's elite status within this phase. "Remember I said one sure sign of an Imperial phase is a streak of top ten hits? Madonna did better than that."
**Conditions for Matching Madonna's Streak** : The current streaming era, where superstars commonly place many album cuts on lower chart rungs, means that the conditions may "never exist again" for any artist, even Taylor Swift, to match Madonna's record of 16 consecutive Top 5 singles. "Nowadays, in the streaming era, when superstars routinely place piles of their album cuts on lower rungs of the charts, the conditions may never exist again for anyone to match Madonna's streak."

Instead, it was placed on the B-side of a 12-inch single, making it eligible only for Billboard's Dance Charts. "In the US, Sire put into the groove on the B-side of a 12-inch single, making it eligible for Billboard's Dance Charts, but not the Hot 100."
