## albums
**Poems, Prayers & Promises (1971)** : This LP, released in 1971, included the song "Take Me Home, Country Roads," which Denver recorded in January 1971 with Fat City providing backing vocals. The album performed well, reaching number six on the Country LPs chart and number 15 on the pop album chart. "added it to his poems, prayers and promises LP."
**Aerie (1971/1972)** : This album, released after 'Poems, Prayers & Promises', peaked at number 75 on the pop album chart and did not appear on country charts. "The album, Aerie, John Denver's LP after poems, prayers and promises peaked at number 75 on the pop album chart."
**Rocky Mountain High (1972)** : Released in the fall of 1972, this was John Denver's sixth studio album and his first to break into the top 10 on the LPs chart, eventually peaking at number four. However, it only reached number 40 on the country albums chart, with its title track failing to appear on the Hot Country Singles chart, possibly due to lyrical controversies. "Rocky Mountain high was the title track to John Denver's sixth studio album and his first to crack the top 10 on the LP's chart."
**Farewell & Andromeda (1973)** : This LP, released in late 1973, did not generate any major hits, but one of its deep cuts, "Please Daddy Don't Get Drunk This Christmas," became a minor holiday hit on country radio. "a deep cut on his next LP, Farewell and Dramada."
**John Denver's Greatest Hits (1973)** : Issued by RCA in late 1973, this compilation featured re-recorded versions of several tracks. Denver utilized improved studio facilities and his enhanced vocal abilities for these new recordings, notably augmenting "Sunshine on My Shoulders" with a string section. This album rose to number one on the album chart in March 1974, coinciding with "Sunshine on My Shoulders" hitting number one on the Hot 100. "My beloved John Denver's greatest hits."
**Back Home Again (1974)** : The album cover featured John Denver and his wife, Annie, on what looked like a farm, visually catering to country audiences, despite the LP being recorded in Los Angeles. This LP topped both the pop and country album charts, spending one week at number one on the pop chart and an impressive 13 weeks at number one on the country chart. In late summer and fall of 1974, it frequently alternated with Olivia Newton-John's 'If You Love Me, Let Me Know' album at the country chart's top spot. "On the cover of his 1974 album, Back Home Again, John Denver was photographed next to his beloved wife, Annie, straddling a fence on what appeared to be a farm yard."

## songs
**Leaving on a Jet Plane (1969)** : John Denver wrote this song, which became a number one hit for Peter, Paul and Mary in December 1969, their first and only number one hit. He demoed it under the working title "Babe I Hate to Go," and The Mitchell Trio later attempted it before producer Milton Okun gave it to Peter, Paul and Mary. Originally written about a lover's regret, the lyrics gained additional resonance by 1969, becoming a parable for soldiers leaving for the Vietnam War. "He'd even written a song that gave folk trio Peter Paul and Mary a number one hit."
**The Bells of Romney (cover)** : John Denver took the lead vocal on The Mitchell Trio's cover of this Pete Seeger song, recorded after Chad Mitchell's departure and the group's renaming. "Here he is taking the lead on the Mitchell trio's cover of The Bells of Romney, a Pete Seager song."
**Take Me Home, Country Roads (1971)** : This song, recorded in the winter of 1971, moved Denver closer to country music, despite being a pop smash that reached number two on Billboard's Hot 100 in the summer of 1971. Its origin traces to December 1970 when Denver met Bill Danoff and Taffy Nivert (Fat City) in D.C., and the trio collaboratively wrote its bridge in an all-night session. They debuted it to a five-minute standing ovation at D.C.'s Cellar Door Club. Released as a single in the spring of 1971, it debuted at number 99 and took over four months to reach number two, prevented from reaching number one by the Bee Gees' "How Can You Mend a Broken Heart." This song established John Denver as a solo artist and defined his persona as earnest, folky, and homespun with a talent for melody. Surprisingly, it only reached number 50 on the Hot Country Singles chart, as the country audience was not initially very interested. "Take me home country roads turned out to be a pop smash eventually climbing to number two on billboards hot 100 in the summer of 71."
**Friends With You (1971/1972)** : The lead single from the album 'Aerie', this song reached number 47 on the Hot 100 but did not chart on the country charts. "Its lead single, Friends With You, got to number 47 on the Hot 100, and neither the LP nor the single touched the Country charts."
**Rocky Mountain High (1972)** : The title track of his sixth studio album, released in the fall of 1972, this song was a slow grower, taking over twelve weeks to reach the Hot 100's Top 10 by early spring 1973. More than "Take Me Home, Country Roads," this song, with its soaring melody and celebration of nature, solidified John Denver's public persona. However, the lyric "and everybody's high" caused controversy, leading to a temporary ban by the Federal Communications Commission (FCC) for perceived drug abuse promotion, despite Denver intending it to mean "high on life." This censorship limited its appeal to conservative country listeners. "Released in the fall of 1972, Rocky Mountain high was the title track to John Denver's sixth studio album and his first to crack the top 10 on the LP's chart."
**Please Daddy Don't Get Drunk This Christmas (1973)** : A deep cut from the album 'Farewell & Andromeda', this drinking song broke through on country radio in late 1973, becoming a minor holiday hit and eventually reaching number 69 on the country songs chart. "please daddy don't get drunk this Christmas, broke through at country radio and became a minor hit that holiday season, eventually reaching number 69."
**Sunshine on My Shoulders (re-recorded 1973)** : This 1971 ballad was re-recorded by Denver in 1973 for his 'Greatest Hits' compilation, featuring a rich string section and benefiting from his improved singing and studio facilities. Its popularity was significantly boosted by its inclusion in the 1973 made-for-TV movie "Sunshine," a rating smash about a young mother battling cancer. This exposure helped the song become a belated chart smash, reaching number one on the Hot 100 by March 1974. "The single that benefited most from this approach was a 1971 ballad that Denver augmented in 73 with a rich string section, the gentle sunshine on my shoulders."
**Annie's Song (1974)** : John Denver wrote this song for his wife, Annie Martell, whom he married in 1967. The inspiration struck him while on a ski lift in Aspen, Colorado, where the mountain's beauty and sensory experience led him to the line "you fill up my senses." He claims to have written the basic melody and lyrics in ten minutes. Though not stylistically closer to country music, its beautiful melody and lyrics about married bliss resonated with both pop and country audiences. By August 1974, it reached number nine on the country chart, marking Denver's first top 10 country hit, and also returned him to number one on the Hot 100. "Denver says, he had written the basic melody and lyrics of a tune that he called Annie's song."
**Back Home Again (1974)** : The title track from his 1974 album, this gentle country two-step served as the follow-up to "Annie's Song." It was Denver's first hit to perform better on the country chart than on the pop chart, reaching number one country and number five pop. "It became John's first hit to do better on the country chart than on the pop chart."
**Fly Away (1976)** : A collaboration featuring Olivia Newton-John, this song peaked at number one on the Easy Listening chart, number 12 on Hot Country Singles, and number 13 on the Hot 100 during the week ending January 31, 1976. It marked a peak in the parallel careers of Denver and Newton-John. "fly away by John Denver featuring Olivia Newton John hit number one on the easy listening chart, number 12 on hot country singles and number 13 on the hot 100."

## curiosities
**Career Trajectory Change (1971)** : In the winter of 1971, John Denver recorded a song that significantly altered the course of his career, moving him closer to a country music sound. "recorded a song that would change the trajectory of his career."
**Parallel Crossover Careers** : John Denver and Olivia Newton-John, despite not knowing each other in 1971 and barely in 1973, began singing together on TV specials and in recording studios by the mid-1970s, building parallel careers as country music crossover stars. Neither grew up near Nashville. "They'd also be building parallel careers as crossover stars of country music, though neither of them grew up anywhere near Nashville."
**Biggest Male Soloist** : For a time, John Denver was considered the biggest male soloist on the charts due to his country-leaning pop hits. "These country leaning pop hits would make Denver and Newton John for a time the biggest male and female soloists on the charts."
**Country Music Establishment Controversy** : Denver's success, alongside Olivia Newton-John's, generated controversy within the country establishment, with some artists bristling at these "outsiders" dominating their charts and winning prizes over long-established country acts. "Some artists in the country establishment bristle that these outsiders coming into the genre, dominating their charts, and even winning prizes over acts that had been recording country music for decades."
**Acting Career** : When his fame exceeded the bounds of pop and country music, John Denver ventured into acting, achieving memorable results. "both Denver and Newton John tried their hands at acting with memorable results."
**Focus on Family Entertainment** : After his initial crossover success, John Denver doubled down on family entertainment. "One of them doubled down on family entertainment."
**Wholesome Persona and Children's Entertainer Image** : John Denver is often remembered for his wholesome image, frequently paired with Jim Henson's Muppets on television, leading some generations to view him as a children's entertainer. However, he was a successful hitmaker for adults, appealing to both urban and rural audiences. "he is now mostly remembered for his wholesomeness."
**Hitmaking Period Context** : Denver's most successful period for hitmaking coincided with the end of the Vietnam War and the Watergate scandal, offering a "country light" and "pacifying reprieve" during a convulsive time when Americans craved earnestness. "Not for nothing, did Denver's biggest hitmaking period coincide with the end of both Vietnam and Watergate."
**Early Musical Interest** : Henry John Deutschendorf Jr. (John Denver) developed an early love for folk music, likely trying to play songs by groups like The Weavers on his first guitar, given to him by his grandmother at age 11. "young Henry John Doichendorf Jr. was likely trying to play it on his first guitar."
**Birth and Upbringing** : Born in 1943 in Roswell, New Mexico, John Denver (Henry John Deutschendorf Jr.) was the son of Henry John Deutschendorf Sr., a decorated Air Force captain. His childhood involved frequent relocations across the South and Southwestern United States due to his father's military transfers. "Born in 1943 in Roswell, New Mexico to Henry John Doichendorf Sr., a decorated captain in the Air Force, Doichendorf Jr. grew up all over the South and Southwestern United States, as his dad was transferred from base to base."
**Teenage Attempt to Run Away** : As an introverted loner, John Denver attempted to run away to Los Angeles at age 16 with his guitar, but was brought back by his strict father. "John tried to run off to Los Angeles at age 16 with his guitar, but was dragged back by his disciplinary and father."
**Path to Folk Singing Career** : After completing high school and attending Texas Tech, John Denver dropped out in his junior year to move permanently to Los Angeles, aiming to establish a career as a folk singer, having already gained experience playing in folk clubs. "He completed high school, enrolled in Texas Tech, began playing folk clubs, and dropped out his junior year, moving to LA permanently to try and make it as a folk singer."
**Adoption of Stage Name** : Upon moving to Los Angeles to pursue his folk singing career, Henry John Deutschendorf Jr. adopted the stage name John Denver, which was chosen in homage to his favorite city. "It was only then that he adopted a stage name aligned with his favorite city, John Denver."
**Multitalented Artist** : John Denver demonstrated multiple talents, including singing in a pure tenor voice, playing instruments, and songwriting. His songwriting abilities, in particular, became his initial claim to fame, often overshadowing his performance skills. "He could sing in a pure tenor, he could play, and he could write songs."
**Milton Okun's Role** : Legendary producer Milton Okun, who produced John Denver, was instrumental in the early success of "Leaving on a Jet Plane" by passing it along to Peter, Paul and Mary. "his producer, the legendary Milton Okan, passed the song along to a much more famous folk trio he was also producing, Peter, Paul, and Mary."
**First Chart Appearance (1971)** : John Denver first appeared on the charts in 1971, marking a significant year for his solo career. "They both got onto the charts for the first time."
**Initial Country Audience Reception** : Country audiences initially struggled to categorize John Denver, who blended folk, pop, and rustic country elements, leading to a lukewarm reception for his music on country charts despite its title. "At the time, Country audiences didn't know what to make of John Denver, who straddled folk, pop and a bit of rustic country."
**"The Great Outdoors" Persona** : John Denver's persona as a pop star was significantly defined by his "uncool" interest in "The Great Outdoors," frequently singing about nature and particularly the majesty of the Colorado mountains, the state from which he adopted his stage name. His brand was characterized by wide-eyed earnestness. "What defined John Denver as a pop star was his most uncool interest, The Great Outdoors."
**FCC Ban of "Rocky Mountain High"** : The lyric "and everybody's high" in "Rocky Mountain High" caused the song to be briefly banned by the Federal Communications Commission (FCC) for allegedly celebrating drug abuse. John Denver intended the lyric to mean "high on life" and the natural wonders of Colorado, and he never forgot this act of censorship, decrying it later in his career. "But everybody's high briefly got the song banned by the Federal Communications Commission as celebrating drug abuse, an act of censorship that Denver never forgot and would decry later in his career."
**Marriage to Annie Martell** : John Denver was married to Annie Martell since 1967, and she was the inspiration for his song "Annie's Song." "John Denver had been married to any Martell since 1967."
**CMA Awards Pushback** : John Denver would face similar "Nashville pushback" at the CMA Awards the year following Olivia Newton-John's controversial wins, indicating ongoing resistance to crossover artists. "at the CMA Awards, Denver would experience some of the same Nashville pushback Newton-John had experienced"

**Birth and Family Background** : Born in 1948 in Cambridge, England, Olivia Newton-John's father, Brindley Newton-John, was a Welsh MI5 officer involved in the Enigma project, while her German Jewish mother escaped the Nazis. At age five, her family emigrated to Melbourne, Australia, where her father became master of a college at the University of Melbourne. Her older sister, Rona Newton-John, was an actress and model, leading to her being described as coming from a "family of overachievers." "Olivia Newton-John was born in 1948 in Cambridge, England."
**Early Performing Career** : In high school, Olivia Newton-John formed a girl group, which helped her gain comfort in performing. She subsequently became a regular on various teen-oriented TV shows in Melbourne, such as The Happy Show, Bumarot, and The Go Show. "she soon became a regular on Melbourne's spate of teen-oriented TV shows, including The Happy Show, Bumarot, and The Go Show."
**Partnership with Pat Carroll and John Farrar** : On TV shows like "The Go Show" and "Time for Terry," Olivia Newton-John frequently performed as a "spunky teen pair" with Pat Carroll, who sang harmonies. Carroll became a lifelong friend and married John Farrar, a singer-songwriter and producer who would later write and produce the majority of Newton-John's hit songs. "Pat soon married John Farar, a singer-songwriter and eventual producer, who would later write and produce the bulk of Olivia Newton-John's hits."
**"Sing Sing Sing" Talent Competition Win (1965)** : In 1965, Olivia Newton-John won a talent competition on the Australian TV show "Sing Sing Sing," performing the torch-ballad "Anyone Who Had a Heart," earning a prize trip to Great Britain. At 18, after her mother's persuasion, she moved to London, where she began singing backup for UK pop acts like The Easy Beats and eventually recorded her own music. "In 1965, Olivia Newton-John won a talent competition on the Australian TV show Sing Sing Sing"
**Discovery by Don Kirshner** : Olivia Newton-John's career gained visibility after she was discovered by American producer Don Kirshner, known for creating prefab TV groups like The Monkees and The Archies. In 1970, Kirshner recruited her for his new co-ed pop group, Two Morrow, envisioned as a transatlantic version of The Monkees. "she was discovered by Don Kirschner, the American producer impresario behind such prefab made for TV groups as The Monkeys. And The Archies."

This was her last major film role for eight years, until "Grease." "The Co-Ed group would record an album and star in a Barbara like sci-fi musical fantasy film called Two Morrow."
**Two Morrow's Lack of Success** : The group Two Morrow, featuring Olivia Newton-John, did not achieve success, with neither the film nor the music connecting with audiences; its singles failed to chart. Newton-John primarily sang harmonies within the group, and it's suggested she should have been given more lead vocals. "Neither the movie nor the music connected. Several singles were released from the soundtrack, but they charted nowhere."
**Exposure to American Music Business** : Despite Two Morrow's failure, the experience provided Olivia Newton-John with crucial exposure to the American music business and allowed her to experiment with various pop sounds, including the country R&B hybrid "Like the River." "it gave Olivia Newton-John exposure to the American music business and allowed her to try out a range of pop sounds."
**Solo Breakthrough (1971)** : A year after the dissolution of Two Morrow, Olivia Newton-John achieved her major solo breakthrough in 1971, marking her first appearance on the charts. "Newton-John's work paid off when she had her major solo breakthrough."
**UK-focused Career (1971-1973)** : For two years after her initial American breakthrough, Olivia Newton-John primarily remained in England, achieving hits there and maintaining visibility through a partnership with British star Cliff Richard, opening his concerts and frequently appearing on his variety show. "For the next two years, Newton-John stayed mostly in England, and only really scored hits there."
**First Major Breakthrough on Country Radio** : Between John Denver and Olivia Newton-John, it was Newton-John who achieved a major breakthrough on country radio first, preceding Denver's return to the country charts in late 1973. "the one who broke through big time on country radio first was actually Newton John."
**Strategic Shift to Americana (1973)** : By 1973, Olivia Newton-John's team deliberately "nudged her more in that direction" towards traditional Americana, aiming to fully establish her in the American market, following her demonstrated adeptness with prior singles like "Banks of the Ohio." "by 1973 her team decided to nudge her more in that direction to break her fully in the American market."
**Grammy Win for Best Country Vocal Performance Female (1974)** : In 1974, Olivia Newton-John's debut country hit, "Let Me Be There," was nominated for and won the Grammy for Best Country Vocal Performance Female, surprising Nashville by beating established veterans like Tammy Wynette, Marie Osmond, and Dottie West. This win, coupled with her album reaching number one on the country charts the same week, momentarily positioned her as the top female country singer in America. "Newton John stunned Nashville when she beat all of these ladies to take home the Grammy."
**Controversial Grammy Acceptance Speech (1974)** : Upon receiving her Grammy, Newton-John innocently remarked, "it's probably the first time an English person won an award over Nashville people," unaware of the controversy it would ignite. "Newton John innocently said, quote, it's probably the first time an English person won an award over Nashville people."
**CMA Award for Female Vocalist of the Year (1974)** : A few months after her Grammy win, Olivia Newton-John also won the Country Music Association (CMA) Award for Female Vocalist of the Year, beating out prominent figures like Tammy Wynette, Loretta Lynn, and Dolly Parton. This further inflamed "factions in Nashville." "when Olivia also won the Country Music Association or CMA Award for Female Vocalist of the Year over Tammy Weinett, Loretta Lynn and Dolly Parton."
**Pop Incursion into Country Music** : The significant success of Olivia Newton-John's music, which served country audiences but was also highly accessible to pop audiences, was perceived as a "pop incursion" into country music. This became a major point of contention and contempt within Nashville, particularly as she was an act from overseas. "This perceived pop incursion into country music was a point of contempt within Nashville, even among American artists, let alone an act from overseas."
**"Country Qualifications" Debate and CMA Split** : A heated debate over Olivia Newton-John's "Country Qualifications" erupted along Nashville's Music Row, causing a split within the Country Music Association. Following her CMA win, a protest group including George Jones, Tammy Wynette, Dolly Parton, Porter Wagoner, Dottie West, Conway Twitty, Barbara Mandrell, and Johnny Paycheck (who stated, "we don't want somebody out of another field coming in here and taking away what we've worked so hard for") formed the short-lived Association of Country Entertainers (ACE) to preserve traditional country music. "A debate now raged along music row in Nashville over Newton John's Country Qualifications."
**Casey Kasem's American Top 40 Introduction** : Casey Kasem, on American Top 40, introduced "I Honestly Love You" by highlighting Olivia Newton-John's origins as a "female pop star from England, raised in Australia" and her four nominations in American country music, while noting her own disclaimer that she wasn't "really a country singer."
