**Fictional Artist Creation (1973)** : C. W. McCall was not a real artist but a fictional recording persona invented by an ad campaign for Old Home Bread in 1973. "an artist who wasn't even real. The number one hit that resulted from this artist wasn't an ad in itself, but without advertising, the whole thing never would have happened in the first place."

W. McCall to provide national distribution, leading to the broader success of "Convoy." "The major label MGM records took notice and signed C. W. McCall to provide national distribution."
**Film Adaptation (1978)** : The immense popularity of the song "Convoy" led to a movie adaptation two years later, starring Chris Christofferson and Ali McGraw and directed by Sam Peckinpah. This film further solidified the song's cultural impact, grossing nearly $50 million at the box office. "It spawned a movie two years later starring Chris Christofferson and Ali McGraw and directed by Sam Peckinpaw."
**Chip Davis's Dislike for Country Music** : Despite "Convoy" becoming a six-week number one hit on Billboard's Hot Country Singles chart, its co-creator Chip Davis paradoxically declared that he didn't even like country music, adding an ironic twist to the song's massive success in the genre. "Which was kind of funny because Chip Davis declared that he didn't even like country music."

# label - American Gramophone

## curiosities
**Founding by Chip Davis (1974)** : American Gramophone was established in 1974 by Chip Davis as an Omaha-based independent label. Its primary purpose was to release Davis's own instrumental music project, Manheim Steamroller, before it also became the initial distributor for C. W. McCall's debut single. "In 1974, Davis founded American Gramophone, an Omaha-based indie label that he launched mostly to release his own instrumental music, a synthesizer classical fusion project that he called Manheim Steam Roller."

# curiosity

**Advertising's Role in Music History** : Over several decades, a significant number of songs have achieved hit status through their association with advertising. This phenomenon includes instances where ad jingles were transformed into legitimate pop hits, as well as cases where existing songs gained chart success primarily due to their placement in commercials. "Over the decades, numerous songs have been made into hits through advertising, whether they were turned from ad jingles into legitimate hits."
**Pop Charts and Happiness** : A legendary, though fictional, ad man's quote suggests that the pop charts, much like effective advertising, are fundamentally "about happiness." This perspective implies that there should be no embarrassment in enjoying catchy "earworms" that simultaneously aim to sell products such as cars, shoes, soda, beer, or gum. "And the pop charts, they're about happiness too."
**"Selling Out" Concept** : The concept of "selling out" became a particularly contentious and significant idea in the second half of the 20th century. Accusations of selling out often arose when artists signed to major labels, licensed their music for commercial use, engaged in corporate sponsorships, or participated in commercials, intertwining with broader discussions about authenticity, purity, and integrity in art. "Selling out becomes particularly contentious, signing to a major label licensing your music corporate sponsorships God forbid doing a commercial will all give rise to accusations of selling out."
**Blurred Lines of Music Publicity and Advertising** : The music business is inherently a commercial enterprise, and while music publicity has always existed, the distinction between traditional promotion and "more craven synergy with Madison Avenue gets pretty blurry." This blurriness arises as artists and brands increasingly collaborate for mutual commercial benefit. "The music business is, after all, a business. And sometimes the line between music publicity, which has been around from the beginning, and more craven synergy with Madison Avenue gets pretty blurry."
**Tin Pan Alley and Jingles** : The historical relationship between hit songwriting and jingle writing was characterized by a "thin and porous" border, even as far back as the Tin Pan Alley era. This indicates a long-standing cross-pollination between the advertising industry and the recording industry's creative output. "As far back as Tin Pan Ali, the border between hit songwriting and jinglewriting was pretty thin and porous."
**The Wrecking Crew** : This group was a celebrated team of rock and pop session musicians whose members were often employed for various recording projects. Their talent was leveraged by jazz band leader Dave Pell, who hired several members to form The T-bones and record an instrumental version of an Alka Seltzer commercial melody. "So, Pell hired a few members of the celebrated rock and pop session musician team known as the wrecking crew."
**Alka Seltzer Commercial (1965)** : This commercial featured a distinctively catchy melody that, in 1965, caught the attention of jazz band leader Dave Pell. Pell believed the tune had the potential to become a hit song in its own right, directly inspiring The T-bones' successful recording of "No Matter What Shape Your Stomach's In." "Alka Seltzer relieves the fliers."
**Diet Pepsi "Music to Watch Girls By" Ad (1966)** : This 1966 Diet Pepsi advertisement featured a melody titled "Music to Watch Girls By," which was later adapted into an instrumental hit by The Bob Crewe Generation. Although the ad's taglines, such as "The kind of girl girl watchers watch," were noted for being "dated to the 60s" and "creepy," the music itself proved popular. "The kind of girl girl watchers watch. The kind of girl who keeps her figure, keeps her cool."
**Benson and Hedges 100s Ad (1966)** : A Benson and Hedges cigarette advertisement in the mid-1960s promoted a new, extra-long "100s" cigarette with a catchy melody. Producer and arranger Phil Bodner's studio group, The Brass Ring, subsequently attempted to reboot this jingle as a pop single, "The Disadvantages of You." "The Disadvantages of a new Benson and Hege's 100s. They're a lot longer than King's size, and that takes them getting used to."
**Crocker Citizens Bank Commercial (1970)** : This poignant one-minute commercial, created by a San Francisco-based advertising agency, aimed to appeal to young people by featuring footage of a young couple getting married and embarking on their new lives. It was accompanied by the tune "We've Only Just Begun," which later became a signature hit for The Carpenters. "It all began when this ad for Crocker Citizens Bank began airing in California."
**"Hilltop" Coca-Cola Commercial (1971)** : The iconic "Hilltop" Coca-Cola commercial, which became a full-blown cultural phenomenon, was conceived when McCann Erickson executives, stranded at an airport in Ireland, observed formerly irate passengers sharing sodas and cheering up. This inspired them to combine the ideas of "buying the world a Coke" with sharing "peace and love" into a jingle featuring a "rainbow coalition of people" on an Italian hillside. "In the TV version, a rainbow coalition of people from around the world sing together on a sunny hillside in Italy while holding bottles of Coca-Cola."
**"Hilltop" in Mad Men Finale** : The legendary status of the Coca-Cola "Hilltop" commercial is so deeply ingrained in advertising history that it was fictionalized in the series finale of TV's "Mad Men." Showrunner Matthew Weiner imagined the deceptive ad man Don Draper conceiving the ad while meditating at a retreat, cementing its place in popular culture. "So much so that in the final moments of the series finale of TV's Mad Men, showrunner Matthew Weiner fictionalized the story of Hilltop, imagining that deceptive ad man Don Draper dreamed it up while meditating at a retreat."
**Product References Removal from "I'd Like to Teach the World to Sing"** : Due to the extraordinary popularity of the Coca-Cola "Hilltop" ad, the team behind the song made the decision in the summer of 1971 to release it as a standalone single. Crucially, all specific product references were removed from the lyrics to enable its broader appeal as a pop song. "Given the coke ad's popularity, the team behind the song decided by the summer of 1971 to release it as a single with product references removed from the lyrics."
**Inspirational Hippie Pop and Jesus Christ Superstar (1971)** : The year 1971 was a peak for "Inspirational Hippie Pop," a genre into which the Coca-Cola ad's song, "I'd Like to Teach the World to Sing," fit perfectly. This period also saw significant popularity for the theatrical album "Jesus Christ Superstar," reflecting a broader cultural trend. "You may recall from past episodes of Hippory That in 1971 that Jesus Christ superstar theatrical album was very popular."
**Kodak's Influence on Pop Songs** : Kodak significantly influenced pop music in the 1970s. After Paul Simon released a hit song titled "Kodachrome" (leading to legal trademark stipulations), crooner Paul Anka later transformed an actual jingle he recorded for Kodak into a top 10 hit, "Times of Your Life," showcasing the company's dual impact on chart success. "What was funny was a couple of years later, Kruner Paul Anka turned an actual jingle 4 Codac into a top 10 hit."
**Old Home Bread Ad (1973)** : This 1973 advertisement for Old Home Bread, a Midwestern brand, was the creative work of Bill Freeze, an Omaha ad agency's creative director. Freeze built the campaign around a trucker character named C. W. McCall, with Chip Davis providing the music, directly leading to the creation of the fictional artist and his subsequent hits. "It all started with this 1973 ad for a Midwestern brand called Old Home Bread."
**Mid-70s CB Radio Fad** : The mid-1970s experienced a widespread fad for Citizens Band (CB) radio, which began with long-haul truckers and quickly spread to general hobbyists. CB enthusiasts viewed themselves as "freedom fighters" challenging regulations and used a unique slang, referring to state troopers as "bears" or "smokies," and driving fast as "putting the hammer down." "They were jumping onto a fat, the mid-70s Citizens Band or CB Radio Fat, that had spread from long haul truckers to regular hobbyists."
**80s Pop Stars and Brand Deals** : By the 1980s, the synergy between advertising and popular music had intensified, or become "supercharged." Brands like Pepsi were paying pop stars such as Michael Jackson, Lionel Richie, David Bowie, Tina Turner, and Madonna millions of dollars to adapt their existing hits for use as TV jingles. "Brands like Pepsi were now paying pop stars like Michael Jackson, Lionel Richie, David Bowie, Tina Turner, and Madonna millions to adapt their hits into TV jingles."
**Pre-existing Hits as Advertising** : The 1980s saw a prevalence of "high-profile uses of pre-existing pop hits, repurposed as advertising," rather than jingles becoming hits. This shift occurred amidst ongoing debates about "baby boomer sellout" and "rock and roll authenticity," which made actual jingles turning into hit songs a rarer phenomenon. "But all of these were high-profile uses of pre-existing pop hits, repurposed as advertising."
**Michelob Beer Commercials (1980s)** : A series of Michelob Beer ads that aired in America in the 1980s garnered significant attention for involving new recordings by "rock and roll Hall of Famers." These campaigns featured guitar virtuoso Eric Clapton and former Traffic frontman Steve Winwood, who either re-recorded hits or created new songs specifically for the beer brand. "Higher profile was a series of ads for Mikalo Beer that did air in America and involved new recordings by rock and roll Hall of Famers."
**Future Ad-Music Connections** : As the advertising industry moved into the 1990s and approached the 21st century, a key "unanswered question" remained: whether advertising possessed the power not just to generate a hit song, but to fundamentally redefine an artist's entire career, hinting at future collaborations with brands like Levi's, Volkswagen (with Nick Drake), and Apple. "The still unanswered question was whether advertising could not only make a hit but redefine an artist's career."
