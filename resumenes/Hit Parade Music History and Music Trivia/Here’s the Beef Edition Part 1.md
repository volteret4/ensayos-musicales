**Sweet Home Alabama (1974)** : This song debuted on Billboard's Hot 100 in July 1974, becoming the band's highest-charting pop hit. **It Wasn't God Who Made Honky Tonk Angels (1952)** : This historic 1952 country single was the first answer record—and arguably the first diss record—to immediately follow and knock the song it was targeting out of the number one spot. It is regarded as an unprecedented proto-feminist smash and foreshadowed the dynamic of rap music's first major 1980s beef war, with a sassy female vocalist challenging a condescending man. "It wasn't God who made honkytunk angels was not only an unprecedented proto-feminist smash."

# curiosity

**History of Beef Records** : The phenomenon of "beef records" is much older than the hip hop era or even rock and roll, with roots tracing back to the 19th-century slang term "beef." Early examples from 1923 show artists "bitching about another person or cultural phenomenon on wax." These records were not initially termed "beef records" or "diss tracks" until the hip hop era. "No, they weren't called beef records or diss tracks until the hip hop era."
**"Yes, We Have No Bananas" Cultural Impact (1923)** : This novelty song was a "mega hit" in 1923, dominating the hit parade multiple times. Its ubiquity was such that it inspired both direct covers and an answer record, "I've Got the Yes, We Have No Bananas Blues," specifically written for those "sick of Yes, we have no bananas." "Yes, we have no bananas was the mega hit of its day."
**Parody as a Song Type** : Parody involves borrowing and interpolating the melody of an original song while changing its lyrics. While typically associated with sweet-natured works like those by Weird Al Yankovic, some diss records also function as parodies, such as Eddie Cantor's "I've Got the Yes, We Have No Bananas Blues." "Parity is a word we normally associate with the likes of Weird Al Yankovic, whose parodies are usually sweet-natured, not disc records per se."
**Answer Records** : Answer records are songs that respond to or ride the coattails of a prior hit. Not all answer records are disses, and not all diss records are answers; many are simply a "wink" rather than a complaint. Historically, listeners in 1923 would have termed Eddie Cantor's anti-banana hit an "answer record," reflecting that the terms "diss" or "beef record" were not yet in common parlance. "Not all answer records are dices and not all disc records are answer records."
**Parodies vs. Diss Records** : Some diss records are direct, note-for-note covers of the songs they are dissing, with new lyrics, as exemplified by Sporty Thievz's "No Pigeons" which "cops the melody" of TLC's "No Scrubs." This contrasts with other diss records, like "Sweet Home Alabama," which do not share a melodic connection with their target. "But some disc records are straight note-for-note covers of the songs they're dissing, such as, for example, Sporty Feeves 1999 hit No Pigeons, a clapback to TLC's number one hit from that same year, No Scrubs."
**The Dozens** : "The dozens" is a form of speak-singing over music in a "proto-rap style," exemplified by Cassius Clay (Muhammad Ali) on his 1963 album "What Else I Am the Greatest." This style was used to call out and humiliate boxing rivals. "On the LP, Clay did the dozens, speak singing over music in a kind of proto-rap style, and he wasn't above calling out his boxing rivals, like Sunny Liston, whom he humiliated on the album's title track, which was released as a single and bubbled under the hot 100."
**Protest Records vs. Diss Records** : Protest records are generally distinct from diss records, only tangentially qualifying as "beef" when they target a specific leader. The key difference is that true diss records are usually "petty and personal," rather than "principled." "Generally, the more trivial the complaint, the more it qualifies as a beef."
**Yankee Doodle Dandy as a Proto-Diss Track** : The song "Yankee Doodle Dandy," dating back to at least the 18th century, is identified as a pre-Revolutionary War diss track. It was sung by the British to mock American colonists, essentially calling them "pansies." "It's basically a pre-revolutionary war diss track sung by the British about us low-bred yanks."
