# artist - Parliament Funkadelic
## genres

## curiosities
**Innovator of 1960s Funk** : Parliament Funkadelic is recognized as a key innovator of funk in the 1960s, whose contributions were taken up by a rising generation of syncopated bands and soulful singers in the first half of the 1970s. "the 60s innovations of... Parliament Funkadelic were taken up by a rising generation of syncopated bands and soulful singers."
**Described as Funk's "Id"** : Parliament Funkadelic was seen as the "id" of funk, in contrast to Earth Wind & Fire's "super-ego." *The Rolling Stone Encyclopedia of Rock and Roll* characterized them as EWF's "ideological rivals," noting their "down and dirty, but equally eclectic" style. "If Earth, Wind, and Fire represented funk's heightened super-eagong, you might say Parliament Funkadelic was the id."
**Focus on Deep Funk over Pop Crossover** : George Clinton's dual bands, Parliament and Funkadelic, never achieved the major pop crossover success of Earth Wind & Fire, as neither group landed a Top 10 hit on the Hot 100. However, away from the pop spotlight, Clinton led an elaborate and "crazy funk revolution" with equally spacey LP covers, making P-Funk the prototype for deep 70s funk fans. "Neither Parliament nor Funkadelic ever landed a top 10 hit on the hot 100."
**Extravagant Live Shows** : P-Funk's stage shows were noted for being even more extravagant than Earth Wind & Fire's, attracting concertgoers across the spectrum who might not otherwise attend a funk show. "If possible, the P funk's stage show was even more extravagant than Earth Wind and Fire's, and it attracted concert goers across the spectrum who might otherwise not have gone to a funk show."
**Most Influential 70s Funk Act** : Parliament Funkadelic, led by George Clinton, is considered one of the most influential 70s funk acts, perhaps more so than Earth Wind & Fire, especially concerning their impact on hip hop and later 80s funk. "Maybe among the 70s class of funk acts, Earth Wind and Fire were not as influential as George Clinton and the P funk All Stars."

## curiosities
**Rising Generation of 70s Funk** : Kool & the Gang was part of a rising generation of syncopated bands and soulful singers in the first half of the 1970s that took up the innovations of 60s funk. "These acts from war to cool in the gang to the Ohio players, saw their funky recordings rise not just on the singles charts, but also the album chart."
**Career Imperative to Adapt to Disco** : By the mid-1970s, Kool & the Gang faced a career imperative to adapt to the rising disco trend, prompting band leader Robert "Kool" Bell to steer the group towards a new sound. "For Cool and the Gang, however, the choice was motivated by a career imperative."
**Initial Failed Disco Attempts** : Kool & the Gang's initial attempts to embrace disco, such as the *Open Sesame* LP and the "Everybody's Dancing" single and album, were largely unsuccessful, with the album bubbling under the chart at number 207 and the single missing both the R&B Top 40 and the Hot 100 entirely. This period led to the perception that "disco had seemingly made Cool and the Gang obsolete."
**Total Overhaul and 80s Pop Sound** : Facing obsolescence after failed disco attempts, band leader Robert Bell initiated a total overhaul, bringing in a new producer, new instrumentalists, and permanent lead singer James "JT" Taylor. This resulted in a shift towards slick balladry and gently funky pop, anticipating the sound of 80s pop and R&B, which proved to be a very wise move. "They would need a total overhaul."
**Kings of Pop and R&B Crossover in the 80s** : Though only medium-level hitmakers in the 70s, Kool & the Gang became "kings of pop and R&B crossover" in the 1980s, scoring 7 more Top 10 hits (10 total out of 13 Top 40 hits in the 80s alone) by mining their smoothed-out, gently funky pop sound. "Cool and the Gang became kings of pop and B crossover."
**Continued Funk Elements** : Despite their shift towards pop and R&B crossover, Kool & the Gang did not entirely abandon funk, retaining funk baselines and syncopated horns in their smoothed-out sound. "And they didn't have to give up funk entirely."

## albums

**Ladies Night (1979)** : This late 1979 LP's title track returned Kool & the Gang to chart success, marking a pivotal moment in their career overhaul. "Ladies Night, the title track of their late 1979 LP returned Cool and the Gang to the top 10 on the Hot 100 and to number 1 on the R&B chart."

## curiosities
**Early Black Exploitation Soundtrack** : Earth Wind & Fire served as the uncredited backing band for Melvin Van Peables' seminal 1971 film *Sweet Sweet Back's Badass Song*, a foundational black exploitation movie. Their contribution to the soundtrack, which All Music describes as "serviceable period Funk Soul," is barely discernible, with the main telltale sign being Maurice White's kalimba playing. "Frankly, you can barely tell Earth Wind and Fire are playing on that soundtrack, which all music calls, quote, serviceable period Funk Soul."
**Early Record Deal and Dissolution** : After signing with Warner Brothers Records, Earth Wind & Fire released two albums and contributed to the *Sweet Back* soundtrack, but these achieved only modest success. Consequently, Maurice White disbanded the initial group, retaining only the band name and his brother, bassist Verdean White, before signing a new deal with Columbia Records in 1973. "White disbanded the group, retaining only the Earth Wind and Fire name, and his brother, bassist Verdean White, signed to a new deal with Columbia Records in 1973..."
**Jazzy Nine-Piece Band** : In 1973, Earth Wind & Fire featured a jazzy nine-piece band, contributing to their expansive and eclectic sound. "played by a jazzy 9-piece band"
**Five-Year Platinum Album Streak** : From 1975 through 1979, Earth Wind & Fire achieved a remarkable run of success, scoring a Top 5 platinum album every single year. "From 1975 through 79, EWF scored a top 5 platinum album every year."
**Sophisticated Album Art and Iconography** : Earth Wind & Fire's albums were known for their elaborate gatefold sleeves, intricate Afrocentric imagery, and slick, artfully arranged band photos. This visual presentation elevated R&B and funk iconography beyond even groups like the Ohio Players, making EWF albums comparable to progressive or hard rock albums and appealing to a broader audience who collected LPs alongside acts like Led Zeppelin. "EWF albums, even more than the sexy covers from the Ohio players, upped the ante for R&B and funk iconography."
**Extravagant Live Shows** : Earth Wind & Fire's live performances were highly elaborate, featuring lavish costumes, special effects, and pyrotechnics. Maurice White sometimes incorporated stunts designed by magician Doug Henning, and the band's stage presence was often augmented by their regular brass combo, the Phoenix Horns. "The elaborate imagery also matched EWF's live show, which Maurice White stacked with lavish costumes, special effects, and pyrotechnics, sometimes even stunts designed by magician Doug Henning."
**Contrast with Parliament Funkadelic** : Earth Wind & Fire represented the "heightened super-ego" of funk, contrasted with Parliament Funkadelic, which was described as the "id." *The Rolling Stone Encyclopedia of Rock and Roll* positioned P-Funk as EWF's "ideological rivals," characterized as "down and dirty, but equally eclectic." "If Earth, Wind, and Fire represented funk's heightened super-eagong, you might say Parliament Funkadelic was the id."
**Compared to P-Funk Stage Shows** : Earth Wind & Fire's stage shows were acknowledged as elaborate but were sometimes considered less extravagant than P-Funk's Mother Ship Tour, which featured a full-scale spaceship. "If possible, the P funk's stage show was even more extravagant than Earth Wind and Fire's..."
**Maurice White's Crossover Instinct** : Maurice White's "killer instinct" and "unerring ear for crossover hits" enabled Earth Wind & Fire to remain relevant and successful through the end of the 1970s and beyond, adapting their sound without straying too far from their funk roots. "Maurice White's Killer Instinct, his unerring ear for crossover hits, kept EWF in the game right through the end of the decade and beyond."
**Versatility in Music** : Earth Wind & Fire's commercial success, marked by numerous LPs and hits, was attributed to the band's versatility, enabling them to blend genres like funk, disco, and yacht soul while maintaining core musical elements. "It was the band's versatility that sold all those LPs and generated all those hits."
**Mid-80s Hiatus and Continued R&B Success** : After "Let's Groove," Earth Wind & Fire experienced a mid-80s hiatus but continued to score hits on the R&B chart through the late 80s and early 90s, demonstrating their enduring appeal. "After a mid 80s hiatus, the group kept scoring on the R&B chart through the late 80s and early 90s..."
**Rock and Roll Hall of Fame Induction** : Earth Wind & Fire was inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 2000, with Maurice White making a brief speech despite his Parkinson's diagnosis. "Earth Wind and Fire were inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 2000."
**Most Successful Product of Funk** : Maurice White's vision for Earth Wind & Fire, which sold approximately 90 million records, led the band to be considered the most successful product of funk, showcasing the music's adaptability, reach, and joyous, versatile, and all-encompassing nature. "But you could say that Mori's White's brainchild, the band he formed half a century ago that sold about 90 million records, was the most successful product of funk."
**Continued Touring in 2022** : As of September 2022, Earth Wind & Fire, fronted by Philip Bailey and Verdean White, continued to tour and perform, including their perennial hit "September." "In fact, just a month ago, in September 2022, they were on stage performing well, of course, September."
**Evolution of Live Shows** : By 2022, EWF's live shows no longer featured onstage pyrotechnics or magic tricks by Doug Henning. Pyramid images and laser lights were projected onto screens, and the band no longer wore elaborate wide collars, sequins, or moon boots, though the distinctive groove and undeniable funk remained. "There are no onstage pyrotechnics anymore at an EWF show, no magic tricks, the pyramid images and laser lights are now just projected onto a screen."

**Session Musician Background** : Maurice White began his career as a session musician, playing drums on numerous recordings for Chess Records before forming his own band. "Maurice White started as a session musician, playing drums on countless recordings for chess records."

**Most Famous Kalimba Player** : Maurice White was likely the most famous kalimba player of all time, an African-style thumb piano he incorporated into Earth Wind & Fire's sound. "White was probably the most famous Calimba player of all time."
**Parkinson's Disease Diagnosis** : In 2000, it was announced that Maurice White was contending with Parkinson's disease, but he still made it to the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame induction ceremony and spoke briefly. He lived another decade and a half, continuing to manage the group's business, though he could no longer perform on stage. "That same year, it was announced that Mori's White was contending with Parkinson's disease."
**Death** : Maurice White died in February 2016 at the age of 74. "Mori's White died in February 2016 at the age of 74."
**Legacy as Funk's Most Successful Product** : White's brainchild, Earth Wind & Fire, formed half a century ago, sold approximately 90 million records, establishing itself as arguably the most successful product of funk music. The band proved the music's adaptability and reach with its all-encompassing, versatile, and joyous sound. "But you could say that Mori's White's brainchild, the band he formed half a century ago that sold about 90 million records, was the most successful product of funk."
**Songwriting Contribution to "September"** : Maurice White co-wrote "September," one of Earth Wind & Fire's most enduring and streamed songs. "Co-written by Maurice White, his guitarist Al McKay, and Journey Woman's songwriter Ali Willis..."
**Random Date Selection for "September"** : Maurice White claimed he picked the 21st of September, the date mentioned in Earth Wind & Fire's hit song "September," entirely at random. "And Maurice White claimed that he picked the 21st of September entirely at random."

## curiosities
**Leader of the P-Funk Revolution** : George Clinton led an elaborate "crazy funk revolution" with his dual bands Parliament and Funkadelic, focusing on a deeply funky and often weird aesthetic rather than aiming for pop crossover. He became the "Prime Minister of Funk" by following his unique muse. "But away from the spotlight of pop listeners, Clinton had been mounting his own elaborate crazy funk revolution, with equally spacey LP covers."
**Star Trek Fan and Album Art** : George Clinton, a huge Star Trek fan, was featured on the cover of Parliament's *Mother Ship Connection* album emerging from a flying saucer, wearing platform Moon Boots. "The cover of Mother Ship Connection featured George Clinton, a huge star trek fan, emerging from a flying saucer, legs first in platform Moon Boots."
**Imperial Year of 1978** : George Clinton experienced his most "imperial year" in 1978, scoring his biggest hits at the height of disco by continuing to champion "uncut funk" while other acts abandoned it. "And 1978 was his most imperial year."
**Hip Hop Influence and Lingo** : Clinton's loopy catchphrases from his hits, such as "Psycho Alpha Disco Beta" and "Bow Wow Wow, Yippee-Yo, Yippee-A," became permanent parts of hip hop lingo, making him a significant influence on the genre and subsequent 80s funk acts.
