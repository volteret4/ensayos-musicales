## albums
**Fleetwood Mac (1975) - Self-titled LP** : This album sold millions by 1976 and gradually climbed the charts, eventually hitting number one in the fall of 1976. Its success laid the groundwork for the subsequent chart explosion of "Rumours." "Their 1975 album had gradually sold millions by 76 and set the stage for whatever came next to explode on the charts."
**Rumours (February 1977) - Breakthrough Album** : This album began taking shape even before the promotional cycle for its 1975 predecessor concluded. Recording was notoriously difficult due to romantic breakups among all five band members, including divorces and relationship splinters. Despite the dramatic backstory not being widely known initially, the LP shot to number one in just six weeks by early April 1977. It spent a staggering 31 weeks at number one, making it the third longest run on top in chart history, behind "West Side Story" soundtrack and Michael Jackson's "Thriller." It is certified for 20 million copies in the US alone and roughly double that worldwide. "When rumors arrived in February 1977, the dramatic backstory of the album wasn't widely disseminated in the press yet, but the LP shot to number one in just six weeks, quite fast in the days before computerized charts."
**Rumours (Original Title) - Yesterday's Gone** : At one point during its development, the album "Rumours" was intended to be titled "Yesterday's Gone," a phrase derived from a lyric in the song "Don't Stop." "At one point, the rumors albums original title was going to be yesterday's gone, after Aliric in Don't Stop."
**Tusk (1979) - Double Experimental Album** : Recorded for over a million dollars, a record sum at the time, "Tusk" was intentionally designed as the opposite of "Rumours," featuring a thorny and experimental sound rather than warm and accessible. Lindsey Buckingham largely dominated the album, aiming to integrate 1970s punk and new wave movements into Fleetwood Mac's music. It reached number four on the album chart but sold only a fraction of "Rumours," and was the only LP of Fleetwood Mac's peak hitmaking period where Christine McVie didn't write either the most or the biggest hits. "They spent more than a million dollars, a record at the time, to record their 1979 double album Tusk."
**Mirage (1982) - Return to Centrist Pop** : This album marked Fleetwood Mac's return to a more "Centrist Pop" sound, shifting away from the experimental nature of "Tusk." Its lead single, "Hold Me," featured a joint vocal between Christine McVie and Lindsey Buckingham, reminiscent of "Don't Stop." While "Hold Me" was in the Hot 100's top five, "Mirage" hit number one on the album chart in mid-August 1982. "In mid-August, while the song was locked in the top five, Marage hit number 1 on the album chart."
**Tango in the Night (1987) - Lindsey Buckingham's Disguised Solo Album** : Initially conceived as Lindsey Buckingham's third solo album, Mick Fleetwood persuaded him to transform it into the next Fleetwood Mac album. Buckingham took full producer credit for the first time on a Mac album, and its creation took 18 months, longer than "Tusk," a process Buckingham later called "the worst recording experience of his life." He compiled songs from fragments contributed by other members, including Mick Fleetwood and Stevie Nicks, both battling cocaine addiction at the time. "The resulting LP, Tango in the Night, was in essence a Lindsey Buckingham album disguised as Fleetwood Mac."
**Greatest Hits (1988) - Multi-Platinum Compilation** : This compilation focused on Fleetwood Mac's post-1975 multi-platinum incarnation and included new songs, one of which was Christine McVie's "As Long As You Follow." The album instantly went platinum upon release and has since been certified octuple platinum. "In 1988, Fleetwood Mac issued a greatest hits album, focused on their post-1975 multi-platinum incarnation."
**Behind the Mask (1990) - Commercially Challenged LP** : This album earned middling reviews and poor sales, but its commercial saving grace was the Christine McVie-penned song "Save Me." This track became Fleetwood Mac's last new top 40 hit. "Though 1990s behind the mask earned middling reviews and poor sales, its commercial saving grace was Save Me, written by McV with husband Eddie Cantella."
**Time (1995) - Failed to Chart** : This album failed to chart entirely, despite featuring "I Do," which Christine McVie co-wrote and sang as its lead single. "Their 1995 album Time failed to chart entirely, although McV co-wrote and sang the album's lead single I Do."
**The Dance (May 1997) - Live Album Blockbuster** : This live album originated from an MTV broadcast special where the group revisited their older hits. It became a blockbuster, topping the Billboard album chart and achieving quintuple platinum certification. "The dance topped the Billboard album chart and went quintuple platinum."
**Say You Will (2003) - Last Studio Album** : Recorded five years after their Rock and Roll Hall of Fame induction, this album remains the last Fleetwood Mac studio album and featured four-fifths of the classic lineup. Christine McVie was the only holdout from fully rejoining, though she contributed backing vocals to a few tracks, such as Lindsey Buckingham's "Believe To Love Her." "Five years later, in 2003, four fifths of the classic Fleetwood Mac lineup actually reformed in the studio to record what remains the last Fleetwood Mac studio album Say You Will."

## songs
**Dreams (June 1977) - Only US Number One Single** : Penned by Stevie Nicks, this song's lyrics conveyed the emotional aftermath of breakups within the band during the "Rumours" recording sessions. It became Fleetwood Mac's only US number one single, topping the Hot 100 by June 1977. "Stevie Nix's resigned sigh of a breakup lament became Fleetwood Max only US number one single."
**Go Your Own Way (1977) - First US Top Ten Hit** : Authored by Lindsey Buckingham, this song explicitly reflected the spite and animosity between former couples during the "Rumours" recording. It peaked at number ten on the Hot 100, becoming the band's first-ever US top ten hit by early April 1977. "After Go Your Own Way peaked at number ten, becoming the band's first ever US top ten hit, Dreams did even better, going all the way to the top of the hot 100 by June."
**Don't Stop (September 1977) - Third Straight Top Ten Hit** : Written solely by Christine McVie after her separation from John McVie, this song expressed hope for her post-married life, despite being a breakup song. Often mistaken for a Lindsey Buckingham song because he sang the first verse, McVie sang the second verse and harmonized with Buckingham on the third. It peaked at number three on the Hot 100 by September 1977. "Don't Stop was written by Christine McVeah alone. She sang the second verse and harmonized with Buckingham on the third."
**You Make Loving Fun (December 1977) - Fourth Top Ten Hit** : Written by Christine McVie about a fling she had with a lighting technician in 1976 during her breakup with John McVie. This groovy and sensual song echoed McVie's earlier "woolly blues rock" for Fleetwood Mac but with a much brighter tone. It peaked at number nine in December 1977, making "Rumours" the first single artist LP in chart history to generate four top ten hits. "Christine McVeah wrote, You Make Loving Fun, about a fling she had with a lighting technician in 1976, while in the midst of breaking up with John."
**The Chain (1977)** : This song became an FM staple on album rock radio by the end of 1977. It is currently ranked fourth among Fleetwood Mac's most popular songs on Spotify when non-hits are included. "on album rock radio almost every song on the album became an FM staple from the chain to second hand news to Gold Dust Woman."
**Second Hand News (1977)** : This song became an FM staple on album rock radio by the end of 1977, widely played on album rock radio stations. "on album rock radio almost every song on the album became an FM staple from the chain to second hand news to Gold Dust Woman."
**Gold Dust Woman (1977)** : This song became an FM staple on album rock radio by the end of 1977, joining other deep cuts from "Rumours" that became fan favorites. "on album rock radio almost every song on the album became an FM staple from the chain to second hand news to Gold Dust Woman."
**Songbird (1977) - Tender Ballad Fan Favorite** : A tender ballad penned by Christine McVie, this song became a deep cut fan favorite from the "Rumours" album, widely embraced by fans beyond the singles. "Deep cuts became fan favorites like Christine McVeah's tender ballad songbird."
**Sentimental Lady (1972/1977) - Bob Welch Re-recording** : Christine McVie co-produced Bob Welch's re-recording of his 1972 Fleetwood Mac song "Sentimental Lady" for his solo career. Lindsey Buckingham played guitar and provided backing vocals, and Mick Fleetwood played drums. Released in the fall of 1977, Welch's solo single reached number eight in January 1978. "Christine even sprinkled her hit making fairy dust on an old friend when she helped former band made Bob Welch re-record his 1972 Fleetwood Mac song, Sentimental Lady, as a solo single."
**Tusk (1979) - Tribal Chant Hit** : The title track of the 1979 album, this tribal chant was partially recorded in Dodger Stadium, backed by the USC Trojan marching band. Despite its unusual nature, it successfully reached number eight on the Hot 100. "Including the title track, a tribal chant that was partially recorded in Dodger Stadium, backed by the USC Trojan marching band."
**Sarah (February 1980) - Stevie Nicks Ballad** : A tender Stevie Nicks ballad, intriguingly written about an unborn child. The song sprawled for over six minutes and featured Mick Fleetwood playing with brushes rather than his usual pounding drumsticks. It reached number seven in February 1980. "And Sarah, a tender Stevie Nicks ballad written about, intriguingly, an unborn child."
**Think About Me (April 1980) - Approachable Rocker** : Christine McVie's chugging rocker reached number 20 in April 1980. On the experimental album "Tusk," McVie's compositions, including this song, notably stood out for their approachability. "As ever, the friendliest hit on the album came from Christine McVeigh, whose chugging rocker Think About Me reached number 20 in April 1980."
**Hold Me (Summer 1982) - Return to Pop Hit** : Co-written with Robbie Patton, a singer-songwriter Christine McVie had been producing, this song featured a joint vocal between McVie and Lindsey Buckingham. Similar in style to 1977's "Don't Stop," it signaled Fleetwood Mac's return to a "Centrist Pop" sound. It rose to number four on the Hot 100 in the summer of 1982, holding that position for seven straight weeks. "Co-written with Robbie Patton, a singer-songwriter Christine McVee had been producing, Hold Me featured a joint vocal between Mick Vee and Lindsay Buckingham."
**Gypsy (1982) - Stevie Nicks Fantasia** : A dreamlike Stevie Nicks composition, this song became a number 12 hit from the "Mirage" album, contributing to its commercial success. "Marage went on to generate more hits, including the Dreamlike Stevie Nicks Fantasia Gypsy, a number 12 hit."
**O Diane (1982) - UK Top 10 Return** : Lindsey Buckingham's song, featuring a "Throwback 50s Rock and Roll style," returned Fleetwood Mac to the UK top 10 for the first time in over three years, demonstrating the album's diverse appeal. "And in England, Lindsay Buckingham's Throwback 50s Rock and Roll style O Diane returned Fleetwood Mac to the UK top 10 for the first time in over three years."
**Love in Store (January 1983) - Mid-Tempo Pop Hit** : Christine McVie's mid-tempo bop reached number 22 in January 1983, giving her a pair of hits from the "Mirage" album. "Her mid tempo-bop Love in Store reached number 22 in January 1983, giving her a pair of hits from Marage."
**Big Love (March 1987) - Quintessential Buckingham Hit** : The first single from "Tango in the Night," this song showcased quintessential Lindsey Buckingham style, with backing vocals from other Mac members and Buckingham himself modifying his voice to sound female. It peaked at number five, indicating the public's excitement for the band after a five-year absence. "When Tango's first single, Big Love, arrived in March 1987, it sounded like quintessential Buckingham, with backing vocals from the other Mac members, and even Lindsey himself, modifying his own voice to sound female."
**Seven Wonders (1987) - Otherworldly Nicks Contribution** : One of the few songs Stevie Nicks contributed to the "Tango in the Night" sessions, this otherworldly track reached number 20. **Little Lies (November 1987) - McVie/Kintella Collaboration** : Released as the third single from "Tango in the Night," this song was co-written by Christine McVie with her then-husband Eddie Kintella. It climbed all the way to number four by November 1987. "Released as Tango's third single, Little Lies, co-written by McVe with her then-husband Eddie Kintella, climbed all the way to number four by November 87..."

To this day, it remains one of Fleetwood Mac's most popular songs, ranked third among all their hits on Spotify (fourth if "The Chain" is included) and receives over 6,000 radio spins a year according to Luminate. Pitchfork magazine ranked it among the 200 greatest songs of the 1980s, with critic Brad Nelson calling it "incandescent" and the best song on "Tango in the Night." "Though it only reached number 14 in early 1988, everywhere has had a remarkably long shelf life."
**Isn't It Midnight (1987) - Album Rock Chart Hit** : Co-written by Christine McVie with Lindsey Buckingham and Eddie Kintella, this song was "Tango in the Night's" biggest non-single. It reached number 14 on Billboard's Album Rock Chart without being issued as a US single. "The rock radio hit isn't it Midnight. Co-written by McVe with Buckingham and Kintella, isn't it Midnight reached number 14 on Billboard's album Rock Chart without being issued as a US single?"

## curiosities
**Transatlantic Band Formation** : Fleetwood Mac formed as a transatlantic band, combining British and American members to forge its unique sound and appeal. "We talked about the formation of transatlantic band Fleetwood Mac..."
**Early British Blues Rock Identity** : In their early days, Fleetwood Mac was recognized as British blues rock hitmakers, a foundation from which Christine McVie's songwriting evolved. "their early days as British blues rock hitmakers..."
**Late 70s Lineup Transformation** : The addition of American members Lindsey Buckingham and Stevie Nicks in the late 1970s, alongside Christine McVie's existing songwriting prowess, significantly powered Fleetwood Mac's biggest LP, "Rumours," and subsequent success. "The Americans, Lindsey Buckingham and Stevie Nicks have joined the band. And their songs and McVees are going to power Fleetwood Mac's biggest LP ever."
**Romantic Breakups During Rumours Recording** : Infamously, all five members of Fleetwood Mac experienced significant romantic breakups during the making of the "Rumours" album. This included John and Christine McVie's divorce in 1976 due to John's alcoholism and the strains of their shared life and work, the permanent splintering of Lindsey Buckingham and Stevie Nicks' relationship due to his controlling nature and her affair, and Mick Fleetwood's discovery of his wife Jenny Boyd's romantic involvement with former Fleetwood Mac guitarist Bob Weston, leading to multiple divorces. "Infamously, every member of Fleetwood Mac literally all five of them underwent some kind of romantic breakup during the making of rumors."
**The Rumours Turmoil Legend** : A widely discussed legend surrounding "Rumours" suggests that the intense emotional turmoil and "torturous" working conditions caused by the band members' romantic breakdowns actually enhanced the quality of the music. "The legend of rumors is that all this turmoil and the band members all agree that working together at this time was often torturous, made the music better."
**Intense Songwriting Competition During Rumours** : During the creation of "Rumours," Nicks, Buckingham, and Christine McVie were all vigorously spurring each other on to achieve "never better songwriting," both melodically and lyrically, which contributed to the album's exceptional quality. "Nicks, Buckingham and Christine McVeigh were all spurring each other to never better songwriting, melodically as well as lyrically."
**Record for Four Top Ten Hits from One Album** : The "Rumours" album set a historical record by becoming the first single artist LP in chart history to generate four top ten hits on the Hot 100. "When you make Loving Fun peaked at number nine in December 1977, rumors became the first single artist LP in chart history to generate four top ten hits."
**Rumours as an Exception in Chart History** : Prior to "Rumours," no album by a single act had ever generated more than three top ten hits on the Hot 100, highlighting the album's unprecedented success. "To that date in Hot 100 history, no album by a single act had ever generated more than three top ten's."
**Album Rock Radio Dominance in 1977** : By the end of 1977, nearly every song from the "Rumours" album, including "The Chain," "Second Hand News," "Gold Dust Woman," and the ballad "Songbird," became an FM staple on album rock radio. "on album rock radio almost every song on the album became an FM staple from the chain to second hand news to Gold Dust Woman."
**Christine McVie's Optimistic Songwriting** : Christine McVie demonstrated an optimistic approach to songwriting about romantic difficulties, famously stating, "I'm just definitely not a pessimist," often transforming heartbreak into uplifting themes in her music. "When it came to writing about romantic travail, Christine later said, quote, I'm just definitely not a pessimist, unquote."
**Tusk Album's Anomaly for Christine McVie's Hits** : "Tusk" stands out as the only LP during Fleetwood Mac's peak hitmaking period where Christine McVie did not write either the most or the biggest hits. "Tusk, which only reached number four on the album chart and sold a fraction of what rumors did, wound up the only LP of Fleetwood Mac's peak hitmaking period where Christine McVeigh didn't write either the most or the biggest hits."
**Band Burnout and Solo Projects Post-Tusk Tour** : After an extensive 11-month tour supporting "Tusk," the band members experienced burnout with each other and eagerly pursued individual projects, leading to three out of the five members releasing solo albums in 1981. "After an 11-month tour supporting Tusk, the band members were burned out on each other and eager to work outside of Fleetwood Mac."
**Christine McVie's Initial Resistance to Solo Work** : While other members of Fleetwood Mac ventured into solo careers after "Tusk," Christine McVie resisted the "solo siren song" for nearly a decade and a half, strategically saving her songs for the next Fleetwood Mac album, a decision that proved "wise." She had, however, been the first to release a solo LP back in 1970 under the name Christine Perfect. "The One Regular Mac songwriter, who resisted the solo siren song, was Christine McVeigh."
**Lindsey Buckingham's First Full Producer Credit** : For "Tango in the Night," Lindsey Buckingham took a full producer credit for the first time on a Fleetwood Mac album, reflecting his significant creative control over the project. "Buckingham even took a full producer credit for the first time on a Mac album."
**Band Members' Addiction During Tango in the Night** : During the 18-month recording of "Tango in the Night," Mick Fleetwood was battling cocaine addiction, and Stevie Nicks went to rehab at the Betty Ford Clinic for her cocaine addiction. "He'd painstakingly compiled songs from fragments contributed by the other band members, including Mick Fleetwood, who was battling cocaine addiction, and especially Stevie Nicks, who actually went to rehab at the Betty Ford Clinic for her cocaine addiction."
**Buckingham and McVie Production Chemistry** : Lindsey Buckingham and Christine McVie consistently worked well together in the studio, and "Tango in the Night" notably contained some of their most polished productions. "Buckingham and McVe had always worked well together, and Tango in the Night contained some of their most polished productions."
**Lindsey Buckingham's Departure in 1987** : Toward the end of 1987, Lindsey Buckingham unexpectedly announced his departure from Fleetwood Mac, just weeks before the launch of a tour to support "Tango in the Night." This decision stemmed from his burnout after carrying the creative burden of the latest LP. "Toward the end of 1987, Buckingham stunned his fellow bandmates when, just weeks before the launch of a tour to support Tango, he announced he was leaving Fleetwood Mac, the result of his burnout after carrying their latest LP."
**End of Famed 5-Person Lineup** : Lindsey Buckingham's departure in late 1987 permanently ended the famed five-person lineup (Buckingham, Mick Fleetwood, Christine McVie, John McVie, and Stevie Nicks) that had generated over a dozen years of hits, as they would never record a full studio album together again. "The famed 5-person Mac lineup, Buckingham, McFleetwood, Christine McVe, John McVe, and Stevie Nicks that had generated more than a dozen years of hits would never record a full studio album together again."
**Fleetwood Mac's Perseverance** : The history of Fleetwood Mac, tracing back to its 1960s founding by Peter Green, is characterized by "Perseverance," with Mick Fleetwood actively working to keep the band alive through various lineup changes. "But the story of Fleetwood Mac dating to the 1960s and its founding by Peter Green was one of Perseverance."
**Christine McVie's Temporary Retirement from Touring** : Christine McVie opted out of touring for the "Behind the Mask" album and temporarily retired from the road. "Christine McV turned down touring for behind the mask, even retiring temporarily from the road."
**Classic Lineup Reunion by Presidential Decree** : In the early 1990s, the classic Fleetwood Mac lineup reunited for a single performance at the request of US presidential candidate Bill Clinton. Clinton had adopted Christine McVie's song "Don't Stop" as his campaign theme, and as the new commander-in-chief, he requested the band, including Lindsey Buckingham, perform at his January 1993 inaugural. "The classic lineup did reunite for one gig in the early 90s, essentially by presidential decree."
**Rock and Roll Hall of Fame Acoustic Set** : In 1998, during their induction into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame, Fleetwood Mac performed an acoustic set that notably began with Christine McVie's classic 1976 hit "Say You Love Me," rather than any of the more prominent "Rumours" hits. "In 1998, when Fleetwood Mac were inducted into the rock and roll hall of fame, they performed an acoustic set that was led off, not by any of the big rumors hits, but rather Christine's classic 1976 hit Say You Love Me."
**Christine McVie's Absence from 2003 Album** : Christine McVie declined to fully rejoin Fleetwood Mac for their 2003 album "Say You Will," although she did contribute backing vocals to a few tracks, such as Lindsey Buckingham's "Believe To Love Her." "The one hold out was Christine McV, who declined to rejoin the band for Say You Will, but she did contribute backing vocals to a few tracks, such as Lindsay Buckingham's Believe To Love Her."
**Christine McVie's Absence from 2000s Touring** : Throughout the entire 2000s, while Fleetwood Mac toured frequently and lucratively, Christine McVie remained off stage. "but McV stayed off stage for all of the 2000's."
**Christine McVie's Return to Live Performance in 2013** : Christine McVie was finally persuaded to return to the stage in 2013 for a single, highly publicized appearance at London's O2 Arena. This event proved to be a "breakthrough" for her, leading to her rejoining the band and touring extensively with Fleetwood Mac in the final decade of her life. "It was only in 2013 that she was finally persuaded to return on stage for a much-touted single appearance at London's O2 Arena."
**Band's Last Tour and Christine McVie's Final Show** : Fleetwood Mac's last tour took place in 2019, prior to the COVID-19 pandemic. Christine McVie's final performance was in late November 2019, almost exactly three years before her passing in late November 2022 due to a brief illness. "The band last toured in 2019, the year before the COVID-19 pandemic."
**Lindsey Buckingham's Absence from 2019 Tour** : Lindsey Buckingham was once again out of the band during Fleetwood Mac's final tour in 2019. "On that final 2019 tour, by the way, Lindsay Buckingham was once again out of the band."
**Christine McVie's Consistent Stage Presence and Anchoring Role** : Christine McVie typically occupied a position behind her keyboards, singing slightly apart from the front of the stage. Her stage presence was described as unflashy, friendly, familiar, and reliable, underscoring her role in quietly anchoring Fleetwood Mac for nearly 50 years, with her songs consistently featured in the setlist. "Christine McV could usually be found behind her keyboards, singing slightly apart from the front of the stage."
