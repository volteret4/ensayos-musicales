**Old Town Road (2018) - First TikTok Incubated #1 Hit** : Released in December 2018, this song quickly became established across social media as the official soundtrack for the "Yeehaw Agenda." Its initial official music video was composed entirely of Red Dead Redemption 2 game footage. TikTok users found the song especially effective for short cowboy-themed videos, particularly keying into the moment after the opening chorus when the trap beat drops and the lyric "I got the horses in the back" is sung. This track changed the landscape of TikTok, becoming the first hit song plus meme incubated on the platform, following examples like I Love Friday's "Mia Khalifa" which took off as a meme but never charted. The song later scaled charts, tangled with Nashville, and set all-time Hot 100 records. "Old Town Road did what no hit had ever done before."
**Old Town Road Remixes (2019) - Multi-Genre Appeal** : Lil Nas X kept the song's virality going with multiple remixes, emphasizing its multi-genre appeal. One remix featured rapper Young Thug, who reprieved his "Yeehaw" vocalization from his 2017 track "Family Don't Matter." The same remix also included country music's online sensation Mason Ramsey, known as "the yodeling kid," who added his "kitty friendly verses." Another remix, subtitled "Soul Town Road," featured South Korean rapper R.M. (Rap Monster) of K-pop sensation BTS, showcasing the song's global conquest. "Nas kept the virality going with additional remixes of Old Town Road that emphasized the song's multi-genre appeal."

## songs
**Lafay Taffy (2005) - Mobile Virality Hit** : This quirky and arguably simple track, originating from the Bankhead projects in Atlanta, exploded in popularity during the 2005 holiday season. In the first week of January 2006, it ejected Mariah Carey's "Don't Forget About Us" from the number one spot on the Hot 100, where it topped the chart for one week. The song was a prototypical example of Snap Music, an Atlanta-based offshoot of Crunk Music characterized by an 808-thump, high-hat, snapping, and chanting vocals, with a focus on simplicity. It sold a record 175,000 downloads in one week, largely fueled by 99-cent downloads from Apple's iTunes Music Store, which began counting for Billboard charts in 2005. Despite not being among radio's most played songs that week, the ubiquity of "Lafay Taffy" as a ringtone on Motorola and Nokia phones contributed to 3 million full-length downloads. It's considered the first Hot 100 number one hit powered by mobile virality, where short ringtone bursts compelled millions to buy the full song. "Lafay Taffy ejected a song by the mighty Mariah Carey from the number one spot. Her smash don't forget about us."

# artist - Chubby Checker
## songs
**The Twist (1960/1962) - First Song to Hit #1 in Two Separate Runs** : This dance craze, released by Chubby Checker, first climbed to the top of the Hot 100 in September 1960, fueled by teenagers learning the dance from TV's American Bandstand. More than a year later, in 1961, as celebrity photos performing the Twist appeared in newspapers and second-wave twist records emerged, the dance became acceptable for adults. This resurgence caused the 1960 hit to re-enter the Hot 100, climbing back to number one by January 1962. It achieved a historical milestone by becoming the first ever single to reach number one in two separate chart runs. "Chubby Checkers, the twist, became the first ever single to reach number one in two separate chart runs."

**Head First (1994) - First Major Label Internet Distribution** : In 1994, Geffen Records issued this B-side track for free on Compuserve, which was the dominant internet service provider at the time. This marked the first ever major label distribution of a song over the internet. Due to dial-up modem speeds of the era, downloading the four-and-a-half-minute track took approximately 90 minutes. It was distributed as a wave file in fairly low fidelity, serving as an early "beta test" for digital music distribution. "Snap Music was also colloquially called Ringtone rap because it was crisp and simple enough to be reproduced by primitive, chirpy mobile phones."

**Music and Technology Interplay** : The interplay between music and technology can be viewed in two main varieties: the medium and the marketing. Technology has profoundly shaped both the physical format and distribution of music, as well as the ways in which it is promoted and goes viral. "You might say the interplay of music and technology comes into varieties, the medium and the marketing."
**Evolution of Song Length (19th-20th Century)** : The common length of popular songs, typically around three minutes, was initially dictated by early recording technologies. In the late 19th century, phonograph cylinders could hold two to four minutes of audio. By the 1920s, the 10-inch 78 RPM shellac record limited songs to three minutes per side. This average length persisted through the introduction of the 1940s 45 RPM vinyl single, though some hits like The Beatles' "Hey Jude" (1968) extended to seven minutes. "The common length of a popular song averaging around three minutes was driven first by the late 19th century technology of phonograph cylinders, which ran for two to four minutes in length."
**Pre-Internet Viral Hits (Early 1960s)** : Before the internet, viral hits existed but propagated at a slower pace. The Twist dance craze, led by Chubby Checker's hit, spread from teenagers learning it on American Bandstand in 1960 to celebrities adopting it in places like New York City's Peppermint Lounge by 1961. This led to the song re-entering the charts and hitting number one again in 1962, demonstrating a slower, yet effective, form of virality over a two-year period. "there was such a thing as a viral hit before the internet. It's just that infections caught on a little slower."
**Dial-up Modem Download Speeds (1994)** : In 1994, downloading a four-and-a-half-minute song over the then-dominant internet service provider Compuserve, using dial-up modem speeds, took approximately 90 minutes. This illustrates the nascent and rudimentary state of internet audio distribution at the time. "On dial-up modem speeds in 94, it took about 90 minutes to download this four and a half minute track."
**Early Digital Audio Formats** : Early digital music releases utilized formats that later became obsolete. Aerosmith's "Head First" (1994) was distributed as a low-fidelity wave file, while Duran Duran's "Electric Barbarella" (1997) was sold in a "long defunct format called Liquid Audio." These early formats highlight the experimental nature of initial digital music offerings. "Erasmith's track was distributed as a wave file in fairly low fidelity. The Durand Durand single was sold in a long defunct format called Liquid Audio."
**Rise of MP3 and Piracy** : Internet song sharing and mass-scale electronic musical piracy began to flourish with the widespread adoption of the MP3 format, particularly on college campuses. This popularization of MP3s predated and ultimately led to the formalization of digital music commerce. "It wasn't until the MP3 began to take hold on college campuses that internet song sharing and mass scale electronic musical piracy took off."
**iTunes Music Store Launch (2003)** : Apple launched its iTunes Music Store in 2003, marking a turning point where music downloads "came of age as e-commerce." This platform played a pivotal role in legitimizing and mainstreaming the digital purchase of music, paving the way for downloads to be integrated into Billboard's chart formulas by 2005. "This eventually led to the 2003 launch of Apple's aforementioned iTunes music store, which was when music downloads came of age as e-commerce."
**Downloads in Billboard Chart Formulas (2005)** : By 2005, digital downloads had become an integral part of Billboard's major chart formulas. This shift allowed downloads, particularly 99-cent purchases from the iTunes Music Store, to significantly influence chart positions and contribute to hits like D4L's "Lafay Taffy." "By 2005, downloads were woven into billboards major chart formulas."
**iPod Popularity and iTunes Gift Cards (2005)** : In 2005, Apple's iPod reached peak popularity as a gift item. Many teenagers receiving iPods for Christmas 2005, along with iTunes gift cards, created a surge in demand for digital music. These 99-cent download buyers were crucial in turning songs like D4L's "Lafay Taffy" into smash hits. "Apple's iPod was at a peak of gifting popularity. A lot of teenagers woke up on Christmas morning 2005 with a new gadget to fill with songs and iTunes gift cards in their stockings."
**Ringtones as Industry Money Makers (Mid-Aughts)** : Ringtones were a significant source of revenue for the recording industry during their short-lived peak in the mid-aughts. Billboard even introduced its own "Hot Ringtones" chart starting in 2004 to track these purchases, highlighting their commercial importance despite not counting directly for the Hot 100. "Ringtones were big money makers for the recording industry during their short-lived peak. Billboard even tracked ringtones on their own Hot Ringtones chart for a few years starting in 2004."
**Ringtone Virality's Knock-on Effect** : Although ringtone purchases never counted directly for the Hot 100, the viral nature of ringtone technology had a "knock-on effect" on chart performance. The ubiquity of songs like D4L's "Lafay Taffy" on Motorola and Nokia phones compelled thousands to purchase the full-length song, which *did* count for the main chart. "While Ringtone purchases never counted for the Hot 100, the viral technology had a knock-on effect."
**Social Media and Video Sharing - The Profound Invention of the Aughts** : In hindsight, the more profound musical invention of the 2000s (Aughts) was not file sharing or even the dollar download, but rather social media and video sharing platforms. These technologies shifted the focus to the "meta narrative" becoming more vital than the medium itself, fueling new forms of musical consumption and virality. "the more profound musical invention of the Aughts wasn't file sharing or even the dollar download. It was social media and video sharing."

Within months of its 2005 launch, music videos became the most viral content on the site, with "The Lonely Island's Lazy Sunday" parody rap video being an early example. "The invention of YouTube, for example, was first inspired by creators who wanted to find a more convenient way to share video of the Justin Timberlake Janet Jackson wardrobe malfunction from the 2004 Super Bowl halftime show."
