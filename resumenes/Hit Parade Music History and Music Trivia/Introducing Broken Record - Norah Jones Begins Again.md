## albums
**Come Away With Me (2002) - Debut Album** : Released on Blue Note Records, this was Nora Jones' "juggernaut debut album" that came out in February 2002. Before its official release, actress Sandra Bullock had an advanced copy and sought to include music from it in her movie "Two Weeks Notice." The album's rise to popularity was described as a "slow study rise." Nora wrote only two or three songs on the album, with the rest contributed by Jesse Harris, Lee Alexander, and various covers. The initial recording session for the album with producer Craig Street involved recording 21 songs in a week and mixing them immediately, but the album was rejected due to perceived issues with the mixes. Ultimately, three "amazing takes" from this session were used on the final record. "Nora released her Juggernaut debut album Come Away With Me on Blue Note Records way back in 2002."
**Visions (2024) - Ninth Album** : Released in 2024, this is Nora Jones' ninth album with Blue Note Records. It was created in collaboration with Leon Michels of El Michels Affair, resulting in a beautiful blend of their two distinctive sounds. The album's release coincides with Blue Note's 85th-anniversary celebrations. "her latest album, Visions, that was put out just this year."
**The Fall (2009) - Album** : This album was released by Nora Jones, though Bruce Lentval, the head of Blue Note Records, candidly admitted that he initially did not like it when she turned it in. He later told her he had come to understand and appreciate the record. "I made records that I made the fall and then I made little broken hearts with the injured mass and we went to lunch one day and he's like, well, I'm going to be honest with you. I didn't like those records when you turned them in."
**Little Broken Hearts (2012) - Album with the injured mass** : Nora Jones created this album in collaboration with "the injured mass." Bruce Lentval, then head of Blue Note Records, confessed years later that he initially disliked this album upon its submission, finding it "not really my thing," but eventually came to "get it" and like it. "I made records that I made the fall and then I made little broken hearts with the injured mass."
**Second Album (Undisclosed Title)** : Following "Come Away With Me," Nora Jones' second album featured a slightly more country sound, influenced by her developing interest in bluegrass that year. Everyone in her band contributed songs to it, and it was produced by Arif Mardin and engineered by the same person who worked on her debut album. "the second album was a little more country, but everyone in the band wrote for it."

## songs
**Come Away With Me (Year not specified, likely 2002)** : Nora Jones wrote this song one night after her mom sent her an old guitar from Texas. She had initially known only two chords on the guitar, then learned five, finding the simpler chords more conducive to songwriting than the complex jazz chords she knew on piano. This song was one of the first original compositions she performed publicly during early gigs with Jesse Harris and Lee Alexander at The Living Room. "I learned five chords and I wrote, come away with me one night."
**Don't Know Why (Year not specified, likely 2002)** : This song was the first take from the demos Nora Jones made for Blue Note Records. Blue Note had provided her with funds to create these demos as part of their evaluation process before deciding to sign her. "Don't know why it was the first take, right? From your demos, right?"
**World of Trouble (Year not specified)** : This song, written by Jesse Harris, was included on Nora Jones' three-song demo that she presented to Bruce Lentval, the head of Blue Note Records. It was characterized as a 6/8 country-style song featuring piano, guitar, and bass. "This was just, I was called World of Trouble. It was piano and guitar. Oh, well, okay. Yeah, and bass."
**Strange Fruit (Year not specified)** : Nora Jones sang this emotionally powerful song during a high school black history program where she portrayed Billie Holiday. She had to audition for the role by singing "Fidenmello" (likely "Fine and Mellow"), but performed "Strange Fruit" for the main program, an experience she described as "crazy" and "heavy" for a high school student. "the actual song for the performance was Strange Fruit, which was also crazy, you know."
**A Natural Woman (You Make Me Feel Like) (1967)** : As a child, Nora Jones attempted to mimic Aretha Franklin's singing, specifically focusing on doing the harmonies for this song. **The Humpty Dance (1990)** : This hip-hop track from Digital Underground was the reason Nora Jones bought her very first record. She heard it on the radio and it immediately motivated her to purchase the album. "Because the Humpty dances. Yeah. The radio."

## curiosities
**Early Musical Upbringing and Exploration** : Nora Jones grew up in Grapevine, Texas, a suburb of Dallas that was small during her childhood but later became a highly developed area. She moved frequently and attended various schools, including a six-month period living in Alaska. She played saxophone in marching band in ninth grade, eventually quitting piano lessons but later reconnecting with music through a saxophone teacher (John Rosina) and a jazz piano teacher (Julie Bonk), which introduced her to the jazz scene in Denton, Texas. She also gained early singing experience in the church choir at Grapevine Methodist Church. "I kind of bounced around though and I bounced around schools a lot and we moved to Alaska for six months."
**Childhood Music Tastes** : As a child, Nora Jones was a fan of pop music from the American Top 40, enjoying artists like Paul Abdul (whose album she considered a "great record" with "really good songs"), Milli Vanilli, and Bobby Brown. Her earliest musical purchases included Digital Underground's "The Humpty Dance" as her first record and MC Hammer as her first concert experience. She fondly recalled listening to K-Earth 101 every weekend. "I loved Paul Abdul. That album, that one album was just, it was huge when I was little."
**Childhood Personality and Self-Awareness** : Nora Jones described herself as a generally shy child, particularly after seventh grade. In sixth grade, she felt "completely unaware of how I was perceived" and "completely open," but an encounter with "mean girls" in school led her to become more self-aware and self-conscious, causing her to "kind of went inside." "I was kind of shy. I wasn't that shy until seventh grade."
**Mixed Race Background and Identity** : Nora Jones' mother is white and her father is black, making her mixed race. She stated she did not experience racism but frequently encountered "nosiness" from people asking "What are you?". In Texas, she was often assumed to be Mexican and addressed in Spanish, which she couldn't speak, leading to awkward moments. In high school, some thought she was half black, and people "just didn't know what to make of me." "I'm mixed as well. I'm black and white. My mom's white, my dad's black."
**High School Performing Arts Experience** : Nora attended a performing arts high school in Dallas, which she described as a great institution for "people of artistic interest." While her major was jazz piano, she also took classical piano (at which she admitted being "horrible") and a synthesis ensemble class where she learned on instruments like the DX7 and Moog. The teacher encouraged songwriting, and she even contributed a song to a cassette tape, though she disliked it due to the synth strings. Notable alumni of the school include Roy Hargrove and Erykah Badu. "I went to performing arts high school in Dallas, which was completely all people of artistic interest."
**College Experience and Dropout** : Nora Jones attended the University of North Texas, known for its strong jazz program, for two years. She initially focused on piano but later joined the vocal ensemble for jazz singers, though singing was not her main academic focus. She dropped out after failing her classical piano jury, primarily because she hadn't practiced, realizing she didn't need a music degree for her career aspirations and was busy playing in two bands and doing gigs. "I ended up going to North Texas. Yeah. And two years from now."
**Early Gigs and Songwriting Inspiration in NYC** : After moving to New York City and dropping out of college, Nora took restaurant gigs, which paid less than her previous job in Texas. She immersed herself in the city's vibrant music scene, regularly attending clubs like Smalls and Tonic (an avant-garde venue), hearing diverse genres from Brazilian bands to jazz. She was inspired by songwriter friends performing at The Living Room, which prompted her to start writing songs again after a long hiatus since a high school synthesizer class. Her mother sent her an old guitar, on which she learned simple chords and wrote her breakthrough song, "Come Away With Me." "I was just, I was going to Smalls every night and watching people play and just so in love with going to hear music every night."
**First Public Performance in NYC and Audience Connection** : Nora Jones' first public performance in a dedicated listening venue like The Living Room was a significant moment. Performing with Jesse Harris and her bassist Lee Alexander, she experienced a profound connection with the audience: "you could hear a pen drop. Nobody was talking. Everybody was listening." This attentive reception, coupled with passing a tip jar, contrasted sharply with her previous restaurant gigs where nobody listened. "it was the first time I'd played in a place like that. And they passed a tip jar around and you couldn't. You could hear a pen drop. Nobody was talking. Everybody was listening."
**Blue Note Record Deal Negotiation** : Nora Jones secured a meeting with Bruce Lentval, the head of Blue Note Records, after an EMI publishing contact heard her play at her 21st birthday jazz brunch gig at The Garage on 7th Avenue. She brought a three-song demo for the meeting. When Lentval asked if she wanted to be a jazz or pop singer, she confidently responded "jazz singer," which she described as the "correct answer" for Blue Note. Her primary aspirations at the time were simply to pay rent and transition from restaurant gigs to club performances, rather than securing a record deal. "Did you aspire to get a record, you know? I was aspiring to pay my rent and to get a gig at a non-brunch venue."
**Mimicking and Finding Her Authentic Voice** : Growing up, Nora Jones learned by mimicking her favorite singers, including Aretha Franklin (especially harmonies for "A Natural Woman") and Billie Holiday (whom she portrayed in a high school program, singing "Strange Fruit" and actively trying to emulate her sound). She was also "obsessed with Saravan" (Sarah Vaughan). A college friend's observation of her "Texas twang" in a jazz standard made her realize she could "kind of be myself" and didn't "have to imitate Ella... or Saravan." She values "imperfect or weird" voices that "embody themselves." "I tried to mimic Aritha and all her, all her background singing, you know, I was always doing the harmonies to Aena Wave."
**Pianist Skills and Live Performance Preference** : Don Was praised Nora Jones as an "incredible pianist" with "incredibly sophisticated" voicings and a deep understanding of theory, scales, and modes, though she admits to not having a lot of "chops" or playing very fast. She feels she has gotten "a lot better in the last 10 years" due to playing more piano live, often in a trio format. She finds it challenging to sing without accompanying herself, stating she's "not great at it" and "don't know what to do with my hands," feeling more connected to her performance when playing and singing simultaneously. When recording, she gets a better vocal when singing and playing live together, as overdubbing tends to lead to overthinking and overplaying. "I don't know if people understand what a great pianist you are. I hear it in the, your voicings are incredibly sophisticated."
**Bruce Lentval's Candid Feedback** : Years after her early releases, Bruce Lentval, then head of Blue Note Records, candidly admitted to Nora Jones that he initially disliked her albums "The Fall" and "Little Broken Hearts" (with the injured mass). He told her, "I'm going to be honest with you. I didn't like those records when you turned them in. They're not really my thing, but I think now I like them and I get it. Now I get it." Nora appreciated his honesty, noting he "never kissed my butt." "He never kissed my butt. He never told me he liked them. So I never really thought about him not saying anything but like it was fine."
**Artistic Evolution and Avoiding Pigeonholing** : Nora Jones expressed excitement about continuously "finding stuff" and getting "into songwriting" after her debut album, where she only wrote a few songs. She was eager to avoid being "pigeonholed" into a specific sound like a piano trio and deliberately explored new genres, such as bluegrass, which influenced her second album to be "a little more country." Don Was compared her artistic evolution to Miles Davis, noting how she, like Miles, always sounded like herself but kept it interesting by "changing the textures." "Did the success of come away with me? Were you trying to avoid getting pigeonholed into piano, trio? I was just excited to keep finding stuff."

# label - Blue Note Records

## curiosities
**Founding and Legacy** : Blue Note Records is described as "one of the first and longest standing institutions of jazz music," founded in 1939. It is celebrating its 85th anniversary in 2024. The label is known for its "cooler branding," attributed to art director Reed Miles and photographer-turned-executive Francis Wolfe. Over its history, it has released albums by numerous prominent artists including Herbie Hancock, Art Blakey and the Jazz Messengers, Robert Glasper, Lee Morgan, John Coltrane, Wayne Shorter, Bobby McFerrin, Greg Porter, Ron Carter, Michelle Andegeo Cello, Charles Lloyd, and Julian Lodge. "Blue Note is one of the first and longest standing institutions of jazz music."
**Presidency and Vision** : Don Was is the current president of Blue Note Records, leading the label's 85th-anniversary celebrations in 2024. He expressed his intention to speak with artists representing the label's past, present, and future, including Ron Carter, Michelle Andegeo Cello, Charles Lloyd, Julian Lodge, and Nora Jones. "Blue Note's current president, Don Was, to speak with the label's past, present, and future."
**Signing Nora Jones** : Bruce Lentval, the head of Blue Note Records at the time, met Nora Jones after an EMI publishing contact set up a meeting. Nora presented a three-song demo, and despite Lentval remarking that some of it "isn't jazz... This is country," he decided to sign her, stating, "I don't care. Let's do it. Let's make a record." This decision allowed Nora to embark on her entire career with the label. "Bruce listened and he liked it. And then after the Jesse song came on, he goes, well, what do you want to be a jazz singer or a pop singer? I was like, jazz singer. I'm here in Boone Records, Jesse. Correct answer, right? Yeah."

**Sandra Bullock's Influence on Early Career** : Actress Sandra Bullock played a role in Nora Jones' early career recognition. "gosh, you really hear your Texas twang no matter what you do. And I was like, really? I didn't, I didn't know that. And he's like, yes, it's really cool. And I was like, that's cool, okay."
**Music as Unified Expression** : Don Was described Willie Nelson's view of music as a single, unified expression where his singing and guitar playing were inseparable. This concept of inextricable musical elements resonated with Nora Jones' own experience of singing and playing simultaneously for a more "special" and connected performance. "To him, it was one form, one solitary expression when it was coming from his vocal chords or his fingers."

## albums
**Come Away With Me (2002)** : Norah Jones initially found the 6 PM daily departure rule imposed by producer Arif Mardin during its recording to be "ridiculous" as she was used to recording late, but it ultimately worked out well. The album's massive success led to her photo on the New York Post next to Saddam Hussein's and details about her apartment's rent in the daily news, making it impossible for her to go home due to the intense scrutiny. She describes the fame from this record as "bananas" and doesn't wish to experience that level of overwhelming attention again. "I don't think I would want it to be like my first record ever again. That was just too much."
**Second Record (Year Unknown)** : This album was produced by Arif Mardin again, with Norah Jones expressing a strong desire to continue working with him and Bruce Lundvall also wanting Arif to be involved. They recorded it upstate at Alar, where Arif Mardin, known for his early departures, came up with the band and they all had fun, drinking martinis. Norah recounts a humorous moment of Arif coming downstairs in his pajamas with a martini glass for water. "I think Bruce really wanted a reef to do it. And a reef really wanted to do it. And we loved a reef. So it just, there was never any question of changing it up."
**Christmas Album (Year Unknown)** : Norah Jones made this album with producer Leon Michels after working on a single together. It involved creating a lot of tracks and was a fun collaboration that eventually led to them deciding to make a "real album" without specific parameters. "Yeah and then when it was done, I said, I miss working together. Should we try to make a real album where there's no like roles and no Christmas parameters and so that's what we decided to do."
**New Album (Year Unknown) - Untitled** : This album's songs originated from ideas Norah Jones had while falling asleep, which she would then quietly record as voice memos. It was made by piecing things together "dribs and drabs," whenever she and Leon Michels could meet for short days, typically from 11 AM to 2 PM, due to their kids being in school. She described the process as "so fun," with Leon playing drums and her playing piano. "This album was different for me and that it was sort of pieced together whenever we were around."

## songs
**The Nearness of You (Year Unknown)** : This classic song, written by Hoagy Carmichael and Ed Washington, was initially phrased a little loosely by Norah Jones when she played it solo. Producer Arif Mardin suggested she hit the actual melody for at least the first time, an advice she still remembers every time she sings the song. A string arrangement for the album version was later created by Arif Mardin at the request of Sandra Bullock for a movie, making Arif very excited. "Every time I sing that song, now I think about him."
**I Don't Miss You at All (Year Unknown)** : Norah Jones wrote the lyrics for this song over Duke Ellington's composition "Melancholia." The idea came by chance when her boyfriend was playing the album, and she started singing along, which led to her trying to record it. She successfully obtained permission to use the composition. "I don't know if we're going to get permission to do it like this. Because I'm not trying to take advantage of this song or anything. I just really loved it."
**Don't Know Why (Year Unknown)** : The record label wanted Norah Jones to do a remix of this song to get it onto pop radio. She refused, stating it wasn't for a musical reason and she disliked the remix they provided. Despite her refusal, the song somehow found its way onto pop radio. "And guess what? It somehow made it on pop radio anyway."
**Come Away With Me (Year Unknown)** : Norah Jones wrote this song in high school, after her college days. She composed it in her tiny bedroom after seeing her friends play, using the five chords she knew. Being a "theory nerd" from second grade piano lessons, she notated the chord changes (e.g., "one, two, three, one, six, five, three, one") in her notebook, but without a voice recorder or cell phone, she forgot the original version by the next morning. # curiosity

**Long Studio Hours and Ruined Records** : Norah Jones recounts instances where staying up for 36 hours in the studio led to "ruined records" or, at the very least, a lot of wasted time. She notes that while some good takes can come late at night after drinks, excessive hours often lead to poor decisions in the recording process. "No, I've ruined records by staying up for 36 hours. Really? Oh, yeah. Yeah. I mean, thankfully you get some sleep and then you catch it the next day. I run and say, I ruined records. I would say I wasted a lot of time going down."
**The Value of Short Studio Days** : The speaker now prefers shorter studio days, ideally finishing by 5 PM, to allow for dinner and turning off her brain. This approach contrasts with earlier experiences of working long, late hours, and she notes that Arif Mardin's 6 PM departure rule, initially seen as restrictive, proved effective. "I just want to eat dinner and like turn my brain off after five. I don't want to. I don't want to."
**The Philosophy of Playing Only When Something to Say** : Both Wayne Shorter and Brian Blade share a profound approach to performance, where they only play when they genuinely have "something to say." This philosophy results in precise, impactful contributions, as demonstrated by Wayne Shorter waiting until the solo section of "Peace" and Brian Blade sometimes sitting out half a tune. "They don't really play until they have something to say."
**Music as a Source of Joy** : The feeling of spontaneous musical creation, similar to what one experiences in high school or college, is identified as a deeply joyful and fulfilling aspect of music. This feeling is pursued through collaborative improvisation and a "beginner's mind" approach to performance. "Well, it's, it's where it's where you get that feeling that from high school where you're just like, oh my god, music is so fun."

# curiosity

**Songwriting and Chord Structure** : The song "Heart Ake" is highlighted for its unique and unconventional chord structure. It is noted for changing keys, starting in one place, moving to the minor five, staying there to become the new center, and then returning to a "bridgy chorus" and finally to E-flat. This structure is seen as organic, not "overthought," and contributes to the song's distinctiveness. "It starts there and then goes to the minor five And then it kind of stays there And then that becomes the center And then it goes back to the sort of bridgy chorus And it goes back to E-flat."
