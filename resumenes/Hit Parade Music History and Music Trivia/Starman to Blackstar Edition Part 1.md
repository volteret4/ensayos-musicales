## albums
**David Bowie (1967) - Deram Debut** : This self-titled debut LP was released by the Deram label in 1967, but it ultimately failed to make the charts. It was issued after Bowie had found his name and voice but before he had found his distinctive sound or image, leading to him being dropped by the label in 1968. "Neither the single nor the self-titled David Bowie album made the charts."
**David Bowie (1969) - Space Oddity Album** : This self-titled album, released in 1969, was Bowie's second, although marketed as if his flop 1967 LP had never happened. It was rushed by Mercury Records to capitalize on the Apollo 11 moon landing. Later, RCA repackaged this LP as 'Space Oddity, the album', adding a newer Ziggy-era photo on the cover, which helped it break into the top 20 on the album chart in 1973. "After Bowie signed to Major Label Mercury Records, they rushed release the single to capitalize on that summer's excitement over the Apollo 11 moon landing, as well as a second self-titled David Bowie album, as if his flop 1967 LP had never happened."
**The Man Who Sold the World (1970) - Harder Rock Sound** : Released in 1970 and produced by Tony Visconti, this album saw Bowie refining his sound and rocking harder. Despite this, the album was initially a flop on both sides of the Atlantic, failing to chart in either the UK or US upon its original release. "The following year, on his 1970 LP The Man Who Sold the World, Bowie refined his sound with producer Tony Visconti and rocked harder, but the album was a flop on both sides of the Atlantic, failing to chart in either the UK or US."
**Hunky Dory (1971) - RCA Debut & Commercial Potential** : This album, released in late 1971, marked Bowie's debut on the RCA label and showcased the first real evidence of his commercial potential. It won favor on US rock stations and cracked the Billboard album chart in early 1972, peaking at number 176. The album also featured tracks like "Oh! You Pretty Things" and "Queen Bitch" which played with queer iconography, complementing Bowie's emerging androgynous identity. "These songs, including Life on Mars and Changes, would appear on his late 1971 album, Hunky Dory. Bowie's debut on the RCA label and the first real evidence of his commercial potential."
**The Rise and Fall of Ziggy Stardust and The Spiders from Mars (1972) - Image-Making Breakthrough** : Bowie's fifth LP, landing in the spring of 1972, gradually became a sensation by early 1973, fueled by his new alter ego, the orange-haired glam rock alien Ziggy Stardust. This album proved to be Bowie's image-making breakthrough, cracking the UK top 10 and becoming his first album to break into the US Top 100 on the album chart, peaking at number 75 by early 1973. "The Ziggy Stardust LP proved to be David Bowie's image-making breakthrough."
**Images (Compilation, 1973) - Charting Retrospective** : In the spring and summer of 1973, this compilation of Bowie's early work was simultaneously charting on Billboard's top LP charts alongside 'Space Oddity' (album) and 'Ziggy Stardust'. Its presence highlighted the surge in Bowie's popularity, as RCA capitalized on his breakthrough. "By the spring and summer of 1973, David Bowie was all over the album chart. Space Audity, Ziggy Stardust, and a compilation of his early work called Images were all writing Billboard's top LP charts simultaneously."
**Aladdin Sane (1973) - Iconic Persona Evolution** : Released in 1973, this album further twisted Bowie's alien persona and made his look even more iconic, notably with its album cover featuring a bear-chested, flame-haired David with lightning bolt face makeup and a teardrop. Although its lead single "The Jean Genie" wasn't a big US pop hit, the potency of Bowie's persona ensured the album sold well, leaping into the top 20 and hitting number 17 by June 1973. "And they were joined by a new album that put a twist on Bowie's alien persona and made his look even more iconic, Aladdin's scene."
**Diamond Dogs (1974) - Glam Rock Peak & R&B Hints** : This 1974 studio album broke into the top 10 on the Billboard album chart in under a month, peaking at number 5 that summer, cementing Bowie as a leading light of British glam rock in America. Despite the glam styling on the cover, critics noticed R&B allusions in tracks like "1984," hinting at his future musical direction. "Boey had amassed enough notoriety that his 74 studio album, Diamond Dogs, broke into the top 10 on the Billboard album chart in under a month, peaking at number 5 that summer."
**David Live (1974) - Soulman Persona** : Released in 1974, this concert album showcased Bowie's R&B allusions, cracking the Billboard Top 10. Both the album cover photo, depicting Bowie in a silky suit, and the live arrangements, with songs like "Rock and Roll Suicide" rearranged to sound like Otis Redding, presented Bowie fully in his "Soulman" persona, crooning like an R&B artist. "These R&B allusions persisted on Boey's other major LP release of 1974, the concert album David Live. It too cracked the Billboard Top 10, and from the photo on the album cover to the live arrangements, Boey was giving Soulman, a screening in a silky suit and vocalizing like an R&B crooner."
**Young Americans (1975) - Plastic Soul Transformation** : Recorded in Philadelphia and New York City, this 1975 album was a significant stylistic pivot, with Bowie fully committing to what he called "Plastic Soul," delving deep into American R&B. The album immediately cracked the top 10, reaching number 9, and featured collaborators like Carlos Alomar, David Sanborn, and Luther Vandross. "Young Americans remains one of the great stylistic pivots by any artist in rock history. Boey fully committed to what he called Plastic Soul, going deep on his love of American R&B for the first time since the mid-60s."
**Station to Station (1976) - Imperial Peak & Thin White Duke** : Anchored by the hit "Golden Years," this album, released in 1976, shot to number three on the Billboard LP's chart in just three weeks, holding the benchmark as Bowie's highest charting LP for the next four decades. Despite the slickness of its single, the album's lyrics reflected Bowie's cocaine-fueled paranoia, and its cover was a still from 'The Man Who Fell to Earth', introducing his gaunt "Thin White Duke" character. "Golden Years, a number 10 hit in early 76, anchored the album Station to Station, arguably the 70s Imperial Peak for David Boey."
**Low (1977) - Berlin Trilogy Part 1, Electronic & Ambient** : The first album in Bowie's Berlin trilogy, released in January 1977, contained music originally intended for 'The Man Who Fell to Earth' soundtrack. It was heavily influenced by electronic music, Brian Eno's ambient sound, and post-punk guitars, peaking at number 11 on Billboard's top LPs despite spawning no major hits. "Low, the first album in the trilogy contained music Boey originally intended for the soundtrack to the man who fell to earth."
**"Heroes" (1977) - Berlin Trilogy Part 2, Masterpiece of Isolation** : The second album in Bowie's Berlin trilogy, arriving nine months after 'Low' in 1977, is best remembered for its title track, now considered one of Bowie's most cherished songs and called his "masterpiece." Though initially underrated and a modest performer (peaking at #35 on Billboard LPs), it depicted lovers by the Berlin Wall during the Cold War with innovative production by Tony Visconti, oscillating electronics by Brian Eno, and guitar drone by Robert Fripp. "Heroes has been called David Boey's masterpiece."
**Blackstar (2016) - Final Recording & Epitaph** : This album, released in the week ending January 30th, 2016, was David Bowie's final recording and his first and last number one album, reaching the top spot on the Billboard 200 album chart. It was described as an act of self-sacrificing genius, a combination of calculation, happenstance, inspiration, and fluke, serving as Bowie's self-written epitaph. "The week ending January 30th, 2016, when Blackstar, David Bowie's final recording, reached number one on the Billboard 200 album chart."

## songs
**Liza Jane (1964)** : This was the only single released under the moniker Davy Jones with The King Bees, marking the first recording to feature David Robert Jones. Despite being promoted by major label Decca Records, the single sold poorly, leading Jones to leave the band after a few weeks. "This is Liza Jane, the only single released under the moniker, Davy Jones with the King Bees."
**I Pity the Fool (1965)** : This song was a cover of bluesman Bobby Bland, released as a single by The Manish Boys with Davy Jones fronting them. A notable footnote is that the guitar solo for this single was performed by a young session guitarist named Jimmy Page. "Their single with Davy Fronting them was a cover of Blue's Man Bobby Blanz' I Pity the Fool."
**You've Got A Habit of Leaving (1965)** : Issued in the summer of 1965 by Davy Jones and The Lower Third, this was their debut single and was written by Jones himself. It was around this time that David Jones decided to change his name to David Bowie due to the rising fame of another British pop singer named Davy Jones. "Davy Jones and The Lower Third issued their debut single You've Got A Habit of Leaving, a song written by Jones himself in the summer of 1965."
**Can't Help Thinking About Me (1966)** : This was David Bowie's first song released under that name, featuring his distinct "alien tone of voice" that would become a signature, albeit over a track by The Lower Third that still sounded like The Who. Bowie's manager attempted to force it onto the charts by buying copies, leading it to miss the official British chart but reach number 34 on the Melody Maker chart. "I can't help thinking about me was not only David Bowie's first song released under that name."
**The Laughing Gnome (1967) - Kitschy Novelty Single** : Released by the Deram label in 1967, this kitschy novelty single, along with his self-titled debut LP, failed to make the charts. It represented a period where Bowie had found his name and voice but not yet his definitive sound or image. "That helped get Bowie signed to a solo contract with the DRAM label, where Bowie issued a self-titled 1967 debut LP, as well as a kitschy novelty single called No Kidding, The Laughing Known."
**Space Oddity (1969/1973) - Major Tom's Journey** : Brainstormed after watching Stanley Kubrick's "2001: A Space Odyssey," this song tells the story of Major Tom, an isolated astronaut. It captured the 1969 zeitgeist, was rushed by Mercury Records for the Apollo 11 moon landing, and became a UK number five hit. In 1973, it belatedly became Bowie's first US Top 40 hit, peaking at number 15/16 on the Hot 100, years after its initial release. "He called the song Space Odyssey and it would change everything, ushering Bowie into his next phase."
**Changes (1971/1975) - Statement of Purpose** : Originally released on the 1971 album 'Hunky Dory', "Changes" first climbed to number 66 in 1972. RCA reissued it in January 1975, promoting it as Bowie's ultimate statement of purpose, embodying his lyrical declaration of needing to be a "different man." However, this reissue stalled at number 41, just missing the American Top 40 again. "It was the second attempt to turn that classic song into a hit, originally released on Bowie's 1971 album, Hunky Dory, Changes had climbed as high as number 66, the first time in 1972."
**Life on Mars? (1971)** : Composed on piano after Bowie's 1971 US tour, this song, alongside "Changes," featured the pomp of glam rock and the poignancy of traditional American pop. It appeared on his late 1971 album 'Hunky Dory', which was his debut on the RCA label. "These songs, including Life on Mars and Changes, would appear on his late 1971 album, Hunky Dory."
**Oh! You Pretty Things (1971)** : Appearing on the 1971 album 'Hunky Dory', this song saw Bowie playing with queer iconography, which complemented his androgynous presentation at the time. This was part of a broader shift in his public image, leading to him coming out as bisexual in early 1972. "And on songs like Hunky Dory's, Oh, You Pretty Things, Bowie played with queer iconography."
**Queen Bitch (1971)** : This track from the 'Hunky Dory' album served to complement Bowie's developing androgynous identity and persona. The album, released in late 1971, marked Bowie's debut on the RCA label and showcased his emerging commercial potential. "Bowie's androgynous identity complemented his persona on Hunky Dory cuts like Queen Bitch."
**Starman (1972) - Ziggy Stardust Anthem** : This song, released in conjunction with 'The Rise and Fall of Ziggy Stardust and The Spiders from Mars' LP, made the singles top 10 in the UK. It was a key track in Bowie's image-making breakthrough, solidifying his orange-haired glam rock alien alter ego, Ziggy Stardust, in the public consciousness. "In the UK, the album cracked the top 10, and the song Starman made the singles top 10 as well."
**Suffragette City (1972) - US Rock Airplay Staple** : A twin rocker from 'The Rise and Fall of Ziggy Stardust and The Spiders from Mars' album, "Suffragette City" became a staple on US rock radio airplay, along with the album's title track. This helped the album become Bowie's first to break into the Top 100 on the Billboard album chart in early 1973. "On US Radio, the album's title track and its twin rocker Suffragette City became rock-airplay stapes."
**The Jean Genie (1973)** : This "glambop" song was the lead single from the 'Aladdin Sane' album. It topped out at number 71 on the Hot 100, indicating it wasn't a big US pop hit, but the strength of Bowie's potent persona at the time ensured the album itself still sold well regardless of the single's chart performance. "Though Aladdin's scene's lead single, The Glambop Jean-Geney, wasn't a big US pop hit, topping out at number 71 on the Hot 100, the David Bowie persona was now so potent, his album sold well regardless."
**Rebel Rebel (1974) - AOR Favorite** : This "glammy stomper" was the lead single from the 1974 'Diamond Dogs' album. While it only reached number 64 on the Hot 100, it quickly became a favorite on AOR (Album Oriented Rock) radio, and it's suggested it would have been a rock smash if Billboard had an album rock chart in 1974. "The Diamond Dogs album was led by one of Boey's catchiest singles, The Glammy Stomper Rebel Rebel. It only reached number 64 on the Hot 100, but quickly emerged as a favorite on AOR radio."
**1984 (1974) - Proto-R&B Experimentation** : A track from the 'Diamond Dogs' album, "1984" was part of a planned George Orwell stage musical that Bowie never fully executed. Critics noted its R&B sound, particularly its funky wah-wah guitar, which was compared to Isaac Hayes' "Theme from Shaft," and Bowie admitted its string arrangement emulated Barry White's Love Unlimited Orchestra, showcasing his proto-disco soul influences. "But critics noticed that deep on the LP's second side, the track 1984, part of a planned George Orwell stage musical, Boey never quite executed, sounded a lot like R&B."
**Young Americans (1975) - Stylistic Pivot to Plastic Soul** : The title track and lead single from Bowie's 1975 album, "Young Americans" was a seminal stylistic pivot, where Bowie fully embraced "Plastic Soul" and American R&B. It featured collaborators like Carlos Alomar and Luther Vandross, peaked at number 28 on the Hot 100 in the spring of 1975, and included a lyrical reference to The Beatles' "A Day in the Life" sung by the Vandross-led chorus. "Young Americans remains one of the great stylistic pivots by any artist in rock history. Boey fully committed to what he called Plastic Soul, going deep on his love of American R&B for the first time since the mid-60s."
**Fame (1975) - First US Number One Hit** : Co-written in the studio by Bowie, John Lennon, and Carlos Alomar, this biting, spiteful song was a kiss-off to Bowie's former manager, Tony DeFries. Released as the second single from 'Young Americans' in summer 1975, it became David Bowie's first single to reach number one in America, also crossing over to number 23 on Billboard's Soul Singles Chart and being perceived as de facto disco on Top 40 radio. "Released as the second single from Young Americans in the summer of 75, Fame did something no David Boey single had ever done. It went all the way in America."
**Golden Years (1975) - Soul Train Debut** : Performed on Soul Train in November 1975, this track was the first single from Bowie's next album, 'Station to Station', and continued his funk-infused sound. It became a number 10 hit in early 1976 and anchored the 'Station to Station' album, which marked an "Imperial Peak" for Bowie in the 70s. "While on Soul Train, Boey took the opportunity to perform the first single from his next album, a track that kept the funk going called Golden Years."
**TVC 15 (1976) - Cocaine-Fueled Fever Dream** : This song, featured on the 'Station to Station' album, was a number 64 hit and represented the album's lyrical themes of growing cocaine-fueled paranoia. The lyrics themselves described a fever dream about a girlfriend being eaten by a television set, reflecting the haze Bowie experienced during the mid-70s. "The TVC 15, for example, a number 64 hit was a fever dream about a girlfriend being eaten by a television set."
**Station to Station (1976) - Thin White Duke Introduction** : The title track of the 1976 album, its lyrics introduced the "Thin White Duke" character. This persona was associated with Bowie's gaunt appearance at the time, heavily influenced by his cocaine addiction, which he later claimed made him unable to remember anything about the album's creation. "This was the LP where he introduced the aforementioned Thin White Duke character in the lyrics of Station to Station's title track."
**Speed of Life (1977)** : A track from the 1977 album 'Low', this song featured post-punk guitars and bore a heavy influence from electronic music and Brian Eno's ambient sound. 'Low' was the first album in Bowie's Berlin trilogy, partly conceived from music originally intended for 'The Man Who Fell to Earth' soundtrack. "It bore a heavy influence from electronic music and Brian Eno's ambient sound, as well as post-punk guitars on tracks like Speed of Life and Be My Wife."
**Be My Wife (1977)** : Appearing on the album 'Low' (1977), this track highlighted the album's use of post-punk guitars, electronic music, and Brian Eno's ambient sound. 'Low' was a departure from Bowie's previous pop sound, reflecting his experimental turn towards krautrock and electro influences during his Berlin period. "It bore a heavy influence from electronic music and Brian Eno's ambient sound, as well as post-punk guitars on tracks like Speed of Life and Be My Wife."
**Sound and Vision (1977)** : Despite 'Low' not spawning major hits, this song was considered the album's catchiest track. It reached number 69 on the Hot 100, performing modestly on the charts. "Sound and Vision" demonstrated the album's experimental sound, integrating electronic music and ambient textures. "The album's catchiest song, The Groovy Sound and Vision, only got as high as number 69 on the Hot 100."
**"Heroes" (1977) - Cold War Anthem** : The title track of Bowie's 1977 album, "Heroes" is now one of his most cherished songs and considered a masterpiece, though it was underrated at the time (peaking at #24 in the UK, missing the US Hot 100). Its lyrics depict two lovers by the Berlin Wall during the Cold War. The recording features Brian Eno's oscillating electronic pulse, Robert Fripp's guitar drone, and Tony Visconti's innovative gated microphone system for Bowie's vocal, making it thunderous and anthemic. "Majestic, histrionic, soaring, ruminative, enigmatic, yet regal. Heroes has been called David Boey's masterpiece."
**Lazarus (2016) - Last Top 40 Hit** : Released the same week as his final album 'Blackstar' (ending January 30th, 2016), "Lazarus" became David Bowie's last top 40 hit. Its release alongside 'Blackstar' was part of what was described as an act of "self-sacrificing genius," forming Bowie's musical epitaph. "The same week this song, Lazarus, became his last top 40 hit."

## curiosities
**Birth & Early Life** : Born David Robert Jones in Brixton in 1947, and largely raised in the London borough of Bromley, he showed early ambition, telling his parents he intended to become a pop star. He began performing at age 13, learned the saxophone while at Bromley Technical High School, and formed his first band at 15. "The young man born David Robert Jones in Brixton in 1947. Largely raised in the London borough of Bromley, the ambitious Jones, who told his parents he intended to become a pop star, began performing at age 13, learned the saxophone while at Bromley Technical High School, and formed his first band at 15."
**Early Name Change - Davy Jones to David Bowie** : David Jones decided to change his name in 1965-1966 because another British pop singer named Davy Jones was becoming semi-famous, and by 1966, would achieve "galactically famous" status as a member of The Monkees. He chose "Bowie" after American pioneer Jim Bowie, popularizer of the Bowie Knife, and the name eventually settled on "Bowie" by the press and himself. "It was around this time that David or Davy Jones decided to change his name. You see, there was already a semi-famous soon-to-be-very famous British pop singer named Davy Jones."
**"Chameleon" & "Godfather of Change" Nicknames** : David Bowie was frequently called a "chameleon" and a "restless artist" for his constant reinvention and willingness to try anything for his art, switching between styles from glam to disco, synth-pop, and industrial rock. He was also known by other monikers such as "The Starman," "Aladdin Sane," "The Thin White Duke," and "The Godfather of Change." "They called David Bowie a chameleon, a restless artist who never stayed in one place very long, always changing to satisfy his muse."
**Pop Success as a "Costume"** : Bowie approached pop success as "one more cloak to try on and then discard," treating pop stardom like a costume. This contrasted with artists like Lou Reed or Joni Mitchell, who found that experimentation often led to commercial toxicity, while Bowie successfully experimented and scored hits before, during, and after his artistic explorations, setting a new template for shape-shifters with pop aspirations. "For Bowie, pop success was one more cloak to try on and then discard."
**Androgynous Presentation & Bisexual Identity** : Bowie attracted attention for his androgynous presentation, frequently appearing in dresses, including on the cover of the British version of 'The Man Who Sold the World' LP. In an early 1972 interview with England's Melody Maker magazine, he came out as bisexual, instantly becoming an LGBTQ icon and glam rock's gender-bending avatar. "Also drawing attention was Bowie's androgynous presentation. He was often seen in dresses."
**Fame Exceeded Sales (Early 70s)** : In late 1972, Bowie was making headlines everywhere and had been featured in Time and Newsweek, yet his fame significantly outpaced his record sales, as he had not yet scored a major US hit of his own. His first Top 40 breakthrough came not with his own song, but by writing and producing "All The Young Dudes" for Mott The Hoople. "An artist whose fame so far is way ahead of his record sales, but if the critics are right, that'll come too."
**The Ziggy Stardust Persona** : Four years after "Space Oddity," Bowie transformed himself into the orange-haired glam rock alien character, Ziggy Stardust, for the album 'The Rise and Fall of Ziggy Stardust and The Spiders from Mars'. This alter ego became his image-making breakthrough, solidifying his quirky space cadet image. "Basically, four years after Space Audity Bowie had turned himself into the glam rock alien."
**The Aladdin Sane Persona** : This persona represented a twist on Bowie's alien image, leading to an even more iconic look, famously depicted on the 'Aladdin Sane' album cover. The cover featured a bear-chested, flame-haired Bowie with distinctive lightning bolt face makeup and a teardrop pooling in his collarbone. "If you know one Bowie album cover, it's probably this one. Bear Chested, Flame-haired David, with lightning bolt face makeup, a teardrop pooling in his collarbone."
**"Soulman" Persona** : By 1974, Bowie began adopting a "Soulman" persona, evident in his concert album 'David Live', where he appeared in a silky suit and vocalized like an R&B crooner. This transition culminated in his "Plastic Soul" commitment on the 1975 'Young Americans' album, marking a deep dive into American R&B. "From the photo on the album cover to the live arrangements, Boey was giving Soulman, a screening in a silky suit and vocalizing like an R&B crooner."
**Cocaine-Induced Haze of Mid-70s** : The mid-1970s became a blur for Bowie due to a "cocaine-induced haze." This period heavily influenced his lyrics on the 'Station to Station' album, reflecting growing paranoia, and he later claimed not to remember anything about the album's creation due to his raging addiction. "This would become a theme for Boey over the next year, a cocaine-induced haze that made the mid-70s one long blur for him."
**The Thin White Duke Persona** : Introduced in the lyrics of the title track from the 'Station to Station' album (1976), this character was associated with Bowie's especially gaunt appearance at the time. Bowie later claimed not to remember anything about the album's creation due to his raging addiction during this period. "This was the LP where he introduced the aforementioned Thin White Duke character in the lyrics of Station to Station's title track."
**Personal and Career Reset in Berlin (1977)** : Prompted by his raging addiction and a need to change his trajectory, Bowie left America and England for West Berlin in 1977. There, with friend Iggy Pop, he aimed to kick drugs and record new music, leading to his prolific "Berlin period" with Brian Eno and Tony Visconti. "This finally prompted a personal and career reset for Boey, and he felt compelled to leave America and even England to change his trajectory."
**Ironic Quotation Marks for "Heroes"** : David Bowie treated the song and album "Heroes" with detachment, intentionally putting ironic quotation marks around the title. This "arch gesture" meant both the song and the album were officially called, "quote, heroes, unquote," even though the song is now unironically beloved and recognized as a global anthem. "Even Boey treated the song with detachment, putting ironic quotation marks around the title. Officially, both the song and the album are called, quote, heroes, unquote."

# artist - Lou Reed

## albums
**Transformer (1972)** : This entire album was produced by David Bowie and his guitarist Mick Ronson in 1972. It included the song "Walk on the Wild Side," which was climbing the Hot 100 concurrently with Bowie's "Space Oddity" at one point. "Bowie and his guitarist, Mick Ronson, produced Lou Reed's entire 1972 album Transformer."
**"Darker and More Alienating" Albums** : Reed had a pattern of releasing darker and more alienating LPs after scoring a hit album. This reflected his desire not to remain too accessible for too long, distinguishing his career path from David Bowie's, who sought out more hits. "Any time Lou had a hit album, his next LP tended to be darker and more alienating. He didn't want to remain too accessible for too long."

## songs
**Walk on the Wild Side** : This song technically made Lou Reed a one-hit wonder on the Hot 100. It was produced by David Bowie and was climbing the Hot 100 concurrently with Bowie's "Space Oddity," with the two songs at one point just two positions away from each other (Reed at #19, Bowie at #21). "The man who produced walk on the wild side was in the top 40 with his first major US hit. At the same time, walk on the wild side was in the top 40. That man was David Bowie."

## curiosities
**Artistic Peer of Bowie** : Lou Reed is considered an artistic peer of David Bowie, not only as a friend and contemporary but also for consistently following his muse. Both as a solo artist and with The Velvet Underground, Reed was influential, admired, and popular, though his chart success was less extensive than Bowie's. "Reed certainly qualifies as a man who was not only friends with Bowie and a contemporary, but who always followed his muse."
**One-Hit Wonder Status** : Despite his influence, Lou Reed technically achieved "one-hit wonder" status on the Hot 100 with his song "Walk on the Wild Side." This commercial pattern, where he didn't seek sustained pop accessibility, contrasts with David Bowie's career. "One of my favorite pieces of Chart Trivia about this song by Lou Reed walk on the wild side. Well, besides the fact that it technically made Reed a one-hit wonder on the hot 100."

# artist - Neil Young
