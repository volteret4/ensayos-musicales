## albums
**Autoamerican (1980)** : This album was recorded in 1980 with producer Mike Chapman, building on the band's prior success with "Call Me." Like Talking Heads with "Remain in Light," Blondie explored sounds from beyond American borders, showcasing a wide array of genre experiments including reggae and early hip-hop. "Blondie in 1980 were building on their success with Colmy as they recorded their next album, Auto American, with producer Mike Chapman."
**The Hunter (1982)** : This LP was recorded by Blondie upon Debbie Harry's return to the group in 1982 to fulfill their outstanding contract with Chrysalis Records. The album was described as a "pale shadow" of their earlier sound, and its single "Island of Lost Souls" only reached number 37 on the Hot 100, while the album itself peaked lower on the chart than Debbie Harry's solo debut, "KooKoo," and failed to achieve gold status after a string of platinum albums. "That LP, The Hunter, was a pale shadow of the prior Blondie sound."

## songs
**Atomic (1980)** : Pulled as a single from their 1979 album, *Parallel Lines*, in early 1980, "Atomic" barely reached the Top 40 in America, peaking at number 39. However, it was a smash hit in the UK, spending a fortnight at number one. The song was crucial for Blondie's direction in the new wave era, incorporating elements of surf rock and a cowboy-like twang, while being danceable without being disco, marking a commercial yet credibly rock sound for the band. "What was most important about Atomic was how it pointed away forward for Blondie in the new wave era."
**Call Me (1980)** : Debbie Harry was invited by Italian producer Giorgio Moroder to contribute to the soundtrack for Paul Schrader's 1980 film *American Gigolo*. Moroder had originally offered the song to Stevie Nicks, but she was unavailable, leading him to invite Harry to finish the lyrics and record it. Despite minor contributions from other Blondie members, the song was released as a Blondie single and exploded onto the charts, reaching number one on the Hot 100 for six straight weeks after two months, and was named the top song of 1980 by Billboard. "Call me, a song quite literally about a Jigalo's lifestyle, was full of sleaze and strut."
**The Tide Is High (1980)** : A cover of a 1967 rocksteady song by Jamaican vocal group The Paragons, "The Tide Is High" was released in the fall of 1980. Debbie Harry discovered the song from a compilation tape during a late 70s visit to London and initially wanted to record it with British ska band The Specials. After The Specials declined, Blondie recorded it themselves for their *Autoamerican* album. It took twelve weeks to reach number one on the Hot 100, affirming Blondie's "could do no wrong" status and becoming their third number one hit in different genres (disco, electro rock, reggae). "The Tide Is High is arguably the record that affirmed Blondie's imperial could do no wrong status."
**Youll Tide Throw Down (unreleased)** : This was Blondie's first experiment with rap, conceived after Debbie Harry and Chris Stein were introduced to the Bronx hip-hop scene by Fab 5 Freddy in 1978. It was intended as a novelty Christmas record with Freddie rapping, serving as a blueprint for future work but remaining unreleased. "Their first experiment was a novelty Christmas record called Youll Tide Throw Down with Freddie rapping."
**Rapture (1981)** : A seminal moment for hip-hop culture in the mainstream, "Rapture" began climbing the charts while "The Tide Is High" was still at number one. It featured a bizarre rap break penned by Chris Stein and Debbie Harry, shouting out Fab 5 Freddy and Grandmaster Flash, and telling a "loopy tale of a man from Mars eating cars." Keyboardist Jimmy Destry added tubular bells, and the rhythm section contributed a post-disco groove. It quickly reached number one just eight weeks after its debut, completing Blondie's "quad-fecta" of number one songs in four different styles between 1979 and 1981, and also peaked at number 33 on Billboard's Soul Singles chart. "Just eight weeks after it debuted, Rapture was number one."
**For Your Eyes Only (1981)** : Chris Stein penned this song in 1981 for the James Bond film *For Your Eyes Only*. The film's producers and score composer Bill Conti ultimately preferred Conti's own version of the song. Debbie Harry was invited to sing Conti's version but declined, choosing instead to support the song Chris Stein had written and Blondie had recorded. Consequently, Sheena Easton recorded Conti's version, which became a top five hit. "So in 1981, Chris Stein penned this song for the Roger Moore as Bond film For Your Eyes Only."
**Island of Lost Souls (1982)** : This single from Blondie's 1982 album *The Hunter* attempted to adapt calypso music in a similar fashion to how "The Tide Is High" had utilized rocksteady. However, it "petered out" at number 37 on the Hot 100, indicating a decline in the band's chart performance. "Iland of Lost Souls, which attempted to do for colipso music what the tide is high had done for Rocksteady, peedered out at number 37 on the hot 100."
**Go Lil Camaro Go (1987)** : This was a duet between Blondie's Debbie Harry and The Ramones, seen as a "CBGB veterans reunion." Had Billboard's modern rock chart existed in 1987, the song "likely would have been a smash," indicating its potential for mainstream success and a strong reception. "If the chart had existed in 1987, the remones Debbie Harry duet likely would have been a smash."
**Maria (1999)** : Following Blondie's reformation in 1997, "Maria" became a significant comeback hit for the band in 1999. It topped the UK chart and reached the top 20 on Billboard's Adult Top 40 Radio Chart in the United States, demonstrating renewed success for the group after a prolonged hiatus. "a 1999 Blondie single called Maria topped the UK chart and made the top 20 on Billboard's Adult Top 40 Radio Chart."

## curiosities
**Early Career - Genre-Hopping CBGB Band** : Blondie was recognized as one of the "genre hopping" bands emerging from the New York City nightclub CBGB in the 1970s. This early period was characterized by their willingness to experiment with diverse musical styles, a trait that would continue into the 1980s. "From the pure punk of the remones to the free verse of Patty Smith, the quirky funk rock of talking heads to the genre hopping Blondie."
**Transition to New Wave Era (1980)** : At the start of the 1980s, Blondie was among several bands attempting to expand the definition of punk to achieve pop chart success. Their single "Atomic" in early 1980 was key in pointing a way forward for them in the new wave era, blending surf rock, cowboy twang, and danceable rock elements. "What was most important about Atomic was how it pointed away forward for Blondie in the new wave era."
**Collaborations with Giorgio Moroder (1980)** : Debbie Harry was invited to contribute to the soundtrack of Paul Schrader's 1980 film *American Gigolo*. The film's score was overseen by Italian producer Giorgio Moroder, known for his work with Donna Summer and his Oscar-winning score for *Midnight Express*. Moroder was seen as an ideal producer to steer Blondie towards electronic music and danceable rock, leading to the creation of "Call Me." "This made Moroder, perhaps the ideal producer to push Blondie further in the direction of electronic music and danceable rock."
**Quad-fecta of Number One Hits (1979-1981)** : Blondie achieved four number one songs between the spring of 1979 and the winter of 1981, each in a distinctly different style or genre: disco ("Heart of Glass"), electro rock ("Call Me"), reggae ("The Tide Is High"), and hip-hop ("Rapture"). This demonstrated their remarkable versatility and commercial appeal during their "imperial era." "Completing Blondie's quad-fecta of number one songs all scored between the spring of 1979 and the winter of 1981 and in four different styles."
**Debbie Harry's Celebrity Status (1980-1981)** : During the recording of *Autoamerican*, Debbie Harry's prominence grew significantly. She was invited as a showcase guest on an episode of "The Muppet Show" and Blondie was approached to contribute a theme song for the James Bond film *For Your Eyes Only*. Harry also recorded a solo album during this period, indicating her rising individual stardom. "Debbie Harry was invited to be the showcase guest on an episode of The Muppet Show."
**Exploration of Hip-Hop with Fab 5 Freddy (1978)** : In 1978, Debbie Harry and Chris Stein were introduced to the emerging hip-hop scene in the Bronx by Fred Brathwaite, also known as Fab 5 Freddy, whom they met at CBGB. Freddy saw a synergy between the downtown punk and uptown hip-hop scenes, leading Harry and Stein to experiment with rap, starting with an unreleased Christmas record and culminating in "Rapture." "The story goes that in 1978, Debbie Harry and Chris Stein were taken by Fred Brathwaite, better known in hip-hop circles as Fab5 Freddie. Up to the Bronx, where they witnessed their first rap show..."
**"Debbie Blondie" Nickname** : In an effort to distinguish her solo career from the band, Debbie Harry jokingly noted that fans sometimes called her "Debbie Blondie" or mistakenly assumed the group's name referred solely to her. This illustrates her strong identification with the band's image. "Debbie Harry joked at the time that fans sometimes called her Debbie Blondie or just assumed the group's name meant her."
**First Solo Album - KooKoo (1981)** : Debbie Harry recorded her solo debut album, *KooKoo*, in the first half of 1981 with Nile Rodgers and Bernard Edwards of the disco supergroup Chic. The album moved her further into "Black informed pop," featuring cutting-edge music like the dance hit "Backfire" and the breakbeat track "The Jam Was Moving." The album cover, an image by sci-fi/horror artist H.R. Giger depicting her face pierced with swords, was noted as "freaky." Despite its artistic ambition, *KooKoo* was not a commercial success, peaking at number 25 on the album chart, producing no Top 40 hits, and exiting the chart within three months. "It was the first sign that Blondie's imperial era was coming to a close."
**Prolonged Hiatus (1983-1997)** : Following the commercial disappointment of *The Hunter* in 1982 and the end of their Chrysalis Records contract, Blondie went on a prolonged hiatus by 1983. Debbie Harry then resumed her solo career, even recording a single for the Al Pacino film *Scarface*, and did not return to the group for over a decade and a half. "By 1983, Blondie had gone on a prolonged hiatus."
**Reformation (1997)** : Debbie Harry and Chris Stein reformed Blondie in 1997 after a hiatus of over a decade and a half. This reunion led to renewed chart success with their 1999 single "Maria." "Debbie Harry and Chris Stein not only reformed the group in 1997..."

## albums
**End of the Century (1980)** : This album was produced by Phil Spector, a legendary but volatile producer, in a bid to expand the Ramones' fanbase. The recording sessions were notoriously difficult, with Spector's perfectionism and temper leading to extreme situations, including allegedly holding Joey and Dee Dee Ramone at gunpoint. The album, which took nearly a year to record and months to remix, was released in winter 1980, earned respectable reviews and better-than-average sales for the band, reaching their highest placement ever on the Billboard album chart at number 44. It also featured a cover of The Ronettes' "Baby I Love You" sung by Joey Ramone and Dee Dee Ramone's "Chinese Rock," an ode to his heroin addiction. "When the cursed album finally came out in the winter of 1980... end of the century earned respectable reviews and better than average sales for the Ramones."
**Pleasant Dreams (1981)** : Produced by Graham Gouldman of 10cc, this album saw The Ramones experimenting with a more conventional AOR (Album Oriented Rock) sound. It featured the track "We Want the Airwaves," a single reflecting the band's desire for mainstream attention, and "The KKK Took My Baby Away," written by Joey Ramone as a diatribe against racism, possibly alluding to a personal conflict with Johnny Ramone over a former girlfriend. "That track came from 1981's Pleasant Dreams, which was produced by Graham Goldman of British Rock Band 10CC."
**Subterranean Jungle (1983)** : On this album, The Ramones attempted a return to their punk roots with tracks like "Psychotherapy" and "My My Kind of Girl," which recaptured a "girl group gone punk" sound. The album received their best reviews since *Rocket to Russia* but only achieved modest sales, indicating critical but not widespread commercial success. "The album earned the Ramones their best reviews since Rocket to Russia, but only modest sales."
**Too Tough to Die (1984)** : This album saw The Ramones branching out musically, working with Dave Stewart of Eurythmics. The collaboration resulted in "Howling at the Moon," a clever synthesized punk track. Despite the experimental approach, the album confused Ramones fans and peaked at a low number 171 on the album chart. "The result of the Dave Stewart collaboration, Howling at the Moon, was a clever synthesized punk track, but Too Tough to Die Confused Ramones fans, peaking at number 171 on the album chart."
**Animal Boy (1985)** : This album included the single "Bonzo Goes to Bitburg" and "Something to Believe In." On *Animal Boy*, the Ramones explored themes of political protest and satire, marking a period where they recast themselves as a satirical protest band. "On that same album called Animal Boy, the Ramones also took a swipe at the spectacle of the charity mega-single."
**Adios Amigos (1995)** : This was The Ramones' final album, aptly titled "Adios Amigos." The band joked that they would continue recording only if the album performed well on the charts, reflecting their ongoing resentment of historically poor chart fortunes. It spent only two weeks on the Billboard 200 album chart, peaking at number 148, but yielded a minor hit single with their cover of Tom Waits' "I Don't Want to Grow Up," which cracked the modern rock chart at number 30. "They recorded one last album, the aptly titled Adios Amigos, in 1995."

**My My Kind of Girl (1983)** : Also from *Subterranean Jungle*, this song showcased a "girl group gone punk" sound, contributing to the album earning The Ramones their best reviews since *Rocket to Russia*. "And to the girl group Gone Punk Sound of My My Kind of Girl."
**Howling at the Moon (1984)** : A "clever synthesized punk track" resulting from The Ramones' collaboration with Dave Stewart of Eurythmics on the album *Too Tough to Die*. Despite its innovative sound, it contributed to fan confusion and the album's low chart performance. "The result of the Dave Stewart collaboration, Howling at the Moon, was a clever synthesized punk track..."
**Bonzo Goes to Bitburg (1985)** : Co-written by Joey and Dee Dee Ramone, this 1985 single was a satirical protest against President Ronald Reagan ("the titular Bonzo") for his ill-advised visit to Germany's Bitburg Cemetery, a site containing many Nazi graves. Despite Johnny Ramone being a staunch conservative, he agreed to record the "fierce rocker," which gained The Ramones significant college radio airplay and a high placement (fifth) on critics' lists in the 1985 Pazz & Jop Poll. "Their 1985 single Bonzo Goes to Bitberg, co-written by Joey and DD Ramone, protested President Ronald Reagan, the titular Bonzo..."
**Something to Believe In (1985)** : Also from the *Animal Boy* album, this song was The Ramones' "platitudinous sardonic" take on the charity mega-single phenomenon, specifically targeting "We Are the World." Its "hilarious music video," a spoof of Hands Across America, earned solid rotation on MTV. "The Ramones take on the phenomenon, the platitudinous sardonic something to believe in, which won solid rotation on MTV for its hilarious music video, A Spoof of Hands Across America."
**Go Lil Camaro Go (1987)** : This duet with Blondie's Debbie Harry was a "CBGB veterans reunion" and was seen as a potential smash hit if the modern rock chart had existed in 1987, indicating its strong commercial appeal. "The chart launched a bit too late to catch their duet with blondies Debbie Harry, a kind of CBGB veterans reunion called Go Lil Camaro Go."
**Pet Cemetery (1989)** : This song served as the title theme for the 1989 film adaptation of Stephen King's *Pet Sematary*. It became a modern rock smash, peaking at number four in June 1989, making it The Ramones' only top five hit on any Billboard chart ever. "Peaking at number four in June of 89, the remones Pet Cemetery single became their only top five hit on any Billboard chart ever."
**I Don't Want to Grow Up (1995)** : A cover of a Tom Waits song, this was the final minor hit single from The Ramones' last album, *Adios Amigos*. It cracked the modern rock chart at number 30, providing some consolation for the band's historically poor chart fortunes. "Their cover of I Don't Want to Grow Up by Tom Waits cracked the modern rock chart at number 30."

## curiosities
**Early Career - "Pure Punk" from CBGB** : The Ramones were identified as exemplifying "pure punk" emerging from the New York City nightclub CBGB in the 1970s. This classification highlights their foundational role in the genre. "From the pure punk of the remones..."
**Attempted Crossover with "Rock 'n' Roll High School" (1979)** : In 1979, The Ramones appeared in the B-movie *Rock 'n' Roll High School*, produced by Roger Corman. The film capitalized on the late 70s musical movie craze and starred P.J. Soles as Riff Randall. The Ramones, though not the first choice (Cheap Trick and Todd Rundgren were considered), provided a "very punchy theme song" and looked the part of youth rebels. The movie was a critical success and modest box office earner, intended as a mainstream crossover vehicle for the band into the 1980s. "A claimed by critics and a modest box office success upon its release in 1979, Rock and Roll High School should have been the ticket for the Ramones too at last make a mainstream crossover into the 1980s."
**Volatile Recording Sessions with Phil Spector (1980)** : For their *End of the Century* album, The Ramones worked with legendary producer Phil Spector, known for his "Wall of Sound" but also his perfectionism and violent temper. Spector had pursued the band for years, seeing their "punk melodicism" as a replica of his classic sound. The band agreed to work with him to expand their fanbase but regretted the decision due to Spector locking them in the studio, demanding endless takes, and allegedly holding Joey and Dee Dee Ramone hostage at gunpoint. "A decision the Ramones came to regret when Specter locked them in the studio and made them do take after take."
**Joey and Johnny Ramone's Feud** : A long-standing feud between Joey and Johnny Ramone was hinted at in the song "The KKK Took My Baby Away." Joey's apparent diatribe against racism may have obliquely referred to a former girlfriend, Linda, who had a dalliance with and eventually married Johnny, causing an enduring rift between the bandmates. "Joey may have been obliquely referring to a former girlfriend whom Johnny Ramone had had a dalliance with."
**Satirical Protest Band Era (Mid-80s)** : In the mid-1980s, The Ramones reinvented themselves as a "satirical protest band" to gain mainstream attention. This era included their single "Bonzo Goes to Bitburg," which protested President Ronald Reagan's visit to a Nazi cemetery, and "Something to Believe In," which spoofed charity mega-singles like "We Are the World." "What finally got the Ramones some mainstream attention in the mid-80s was recasting themselves as a satirical protest band."
**"Pop's Class Clowns" Identity (Mid-80s)** : Unable to become pop stars, The Ramones embraced the role of "pop's class clowns," exemplified by their sardonic take on the charity single phenomenon and the humorous music video for "Something to Believe In." "In short, if the Ramones couldn't make themselves into pop stars, they were going to be pop's class clowns."
**Struggles with Commercial Success in the 80s** : Despite various crossover attempts, including film appearances, working with high-profile producers, and genre experiments, The Ramones struggled to achieve significant mainstream chart success throughout the 1980s. Their albums often had modest sales, and their chart peaks were relatively low compared to their CBGB peers. "Nonetheless end of the century didn't make the Ramones a big enough chart success to justify the awful recording experience."
**Life Expectancy of Members** : The Ramones experienced a series of tragic early deaths. Joey Ramone was diagnosed with lymphoma in 1995 and died in 2001 at age 49. Dee Dee Ramone died of a heroin overdose in 2002 at age 50. Johnny Ramone died of prostate cancer in 2004 at age 55. Original drummer Tommy Ramone survived the longest, succumbing to bile duct cancer in 2014 at age 65. "Life expectancy in the Ramones turned out to be short."

## albums
**Wave (1979)** : Produced by Todd Rundgren, *Wave* was Patty Smith's highest-charting LP on Billboard, peaking at number 18 on the album chart. Its most famous single, "Dancing Barefoot," became her most covered song after "Because the Night." "Patty Smith finished the 70s with her WAVE album produced by Todd Rungren."
**Dream of Life (1988)** : This album marked Patty Smith's return to music after a nine-year semi-retirement, co-produced with her husband Fred "Sonic" Smith. It was their only musical project together. The album was warmly received by critics and rock radio, with the anthem "People Have the Power" becoming a number 19 album rock hit. Another track, "Up There Down There," charted at number six on Billboard's Modern Rock chart, becoming her only Top 10 hit on any Billboard list. "Dream of life was co-produced by her husband, Fred Sonic Smith."

## albums
**Remain in Light (1980)** : Talking Heads' fourth album, co-produced with Brian Eno, deepened their sonic explorations. Inspired by African music, particularly Fela Kuti's Afrobeat, and incorporating African-style polyrhythms from Chris Frantz and Tina Weymouth, the band developed the album by recording jams and isolating the best parts. Its magnum opus, "Once in a Lifetime," blended funk and Afrobeat with David Byrne's elliptical lyrics. "As talking heads went deeper into their fourth album, which they would title Remain in Light, they took inspiration from African music, most especially the work of Afrobeat legend, Fella Cooty."
**Speaking in Tongues (1983)** : This album was self-produced by Talking Heads, with David Byrne steering the band towards a "commercially funky direction" after reportedly envying Tom Tom Club's success. The lineup was beefed up with seasoned black players including guitarist Alex Weir, percussionist Steve Scales, and synth player Bernie Worrell. Released in summer 1983, it was steeped in funk, R&B, and gospel, featuring bumping keyboards, stomping basslines, and call-and-response vocals on "Slippery People" with Nona Hendryx. It became Talking Heads' most commercially successful music to date, reaching number 15, going platinum, and generating their first and only Top 10 pop hit, "Burning Down the House." "The result was the most commercially successful talking heads music to date."
**Stop Making Sense (1984) - Live Soundtrack LP** : The live soundtrack album for the concert film of the same name, released in 1984. It initially saw modest chart success, peaking at number 41, lower than the band's five previous LPs. However, it became a "long-distance runner," staying on the Billboard album chart for nearly two years—longer than any other Talking Heads album—and eventually went double platinum, serving as a de facto greatest hits compilation before the band released any official one. "But Stop Making Sense was the long-distance runner of their albums, serving as a de facto Talking Heads greatest hits, years before the group put out any such compilation."
**Little Creatures (1985)** : This album further explored Talking Heads' "nationwide status" and was "awash in kitschy Americana." It was powered by a string of radio singles, none of which cracked the pop Top 40, but all became staples on album rock radio, and their "cutting-edge videos" were popular on MTV. Hits included "Road to Nowhere" (number 25 rock radio), "The Perky and She Was" (number 11 rock chart, number 54 Hot 100 with a 20-week run), and "Stay Up Late" (number 24 rock radio). "1985's Little Creatures was powered by a string of radio singles."
**True Stories (1986)** : This LP served as a pseudo-soundtrack to David Byrne's directorial debut film of the same name and doubled as a Talking Heads album. Though the film was a box office flop, the album went gold and produced the group's last Top 40 pop hit, "Wild Wild Life," which reached number 25 on the Hot 100 in fall 1986. "But the True Stories LP, which served as a pseudo soundtrack and doubled as a Talking Heads album went gold and generated the group's last top 40 pop hit, Wild Wild Life..."
**Naked (1988)** : Talking Heads' final studio album. It was released just before the launch of Billboard's Modern Rock chart, so it missed its opportunity to chart there immediately. "In 1988, the band just missed the chart launch with their final studio album Naked."
