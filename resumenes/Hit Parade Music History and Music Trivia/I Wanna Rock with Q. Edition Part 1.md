## albums
**This Is How I Feel About Jazz (1957)** : Quincy Jones's first album released under his own name, recorded in Paris, France, after he moved there in 1957 to study music theory and work for Mercury Records' French subsidiary. On tracks such as "Stockholm Sweetening," which Jones both wrote and arranged, he conducted varying lineups of jazz players, virtually all of whom were older than him. "With the ambitious title, this is how I feel about jazz."
**The Great Wide World of Quincy Jones** : An album released under Quincy Jones's own name on Mercury Records, reflecting his early branding efforts while also serving as an executive and arranger for other artists. "Say this for Q, he knew a thing or two about branding."
**Quincy Jones Plays Hip Hits** : Another album released under Quincy Jones's own name on Mercury Records during his tenure as a recording artist for the label, showcasing his diverse musical interests. "Say this for Q, he knew a thing or two about branding."
**Big Band Bossa Nova (1962)** : An album where Quincy Jones hybridized his jazz sound with trendy Latin exotica, successfully charting onto the Billboard Top LPs. It reached number 112 in early 1963, propelled by the lead-off track "Soul Bossa Nova." "Jones tried hybridizing his jazz with a little trendy Latin exotica on an album he titled Big Band Bossan Nova."
**Walking in Space (1969)** : An album that rebooted Quincy Jones's discography, described by All Music as the album that "kickstarted Jones's career as a recording artist." It recast his big band influences within the framework of the budding fusion movement, incorporating elements of contemporary rock, pop, and R&B. The LP rode the charts deep into 1970, peaking at number 56. "All music calls Walking in Space the album that, quote, kickstarted Jones's career as a recording artist, recasting his big band influences within the framework of the budding fusion movement. And the influences of contemporary rock, pop, and r&b, unquote."
**Smackwater Jack (1971)** : Quincy Jones's first LP to top Billboard's soul albums chart, featuring his cover of Carole King's "Smackwater Jack," a gently funky pop and beat track. His version amped up the funk and prominently featured harmonica, with its sound serving as a dress rehearsal for the *Sanford & Son* theme. "It was his first LP to top Billboard's soul albums chart."
**Body Heat (1974)** : Quincy Jones's highest charting album to date, it cracked the Billboard Top 10 in the fall of 1974 and peaked at number 6. The LP featured stellar guest players and singers, including Leon Ware, Minnie Riperton, and Al Jarreau on its centerpiece single, "If I Ever Lose This Heaven." "The Body Heat LP was Quincy's highest charting album, cracking the Billboard Top 10 in the fall of 74 and peaking at number 6."
**Mellow Madness (1975)** : An album by Quincy Jones where he hired the Brothers Johnson to play, who were still commercially unknown at the time. The Brothers Johnson ended up contributing four songs to the LP, including "Is It Love That We're Missing," which reached number 18 on the R&B chart, marking Quincy's highest charting hit to date under his own name. "The Brothers wound up contributing four songs to the LP, including Is It Love That Were Missing, which reached number 18 on the R&B chart, Quincy's highest charting hit to date under his own name."

## songs
**Stockholm Sweetening (1957)** : A track from Quincy Jones's debut album *This Is How I Feel About Jazz*, which he both wrote and arranged, showcasing his early talents as a composer and conductor of jazz players. "On tracks like Stockholm Sweetening, which Jones both wrote and arranged, he conducted varying lineups of jazz players who were virtually all older than him."
**Soul Bossa Nova (1962)** : The lead-off track from the album *Big Band Bossa Nova*, which featured legitimate jazz players like Rahsaan Roland Kirk and Lalo Schifrin but leaned into camp with its use of toodling flutes, brash horns, and a Brazilian chattering drum called the Queca, described as sounding like giggling. Within a year of its release, it became popular on TV shows and movies, and decades later, it became Quincy Jones's most-streamed track on Spotify after comedian Mike Myers adopted it as the theme song for his character Austin Powers. "The album made it onto the chart, reaching number 112 in early 63, fueled in large part by the lead-off track, Soul Bossan Nova, a kitschy reinvention of Quincy's sound."
**Killer Joe (1969)** : Quincy Jones's first hit single as a front-line artist, released from his album *Walking in Space*. It reached the middle rungs of both the Hot 100 and Hot Soul Singles charts, marking a new phase in his recording career. "Killer Joe hit the middle rungs of both the hot 100 and hot soul singles charts."
**Smackwater Jack (1971)** : Quincy Jones's cover of a deep cut from Carole King's blockbuster LP *Tapestry*, which was originally a gently funky pop and beat track. Jones's version amplified the funk, built around prominent harmonica, and its distinctive sound served as a "dress rehearsal" for "The Street Beater," the theme to the TV show *Sanford & Son*. "Jones, not only covered the Carol King song, he named his entire 1971 album, Smackwater Jack."
**The Street Beater (1972)** : The opening credits melody and theme song to the NBC sitcom *Sanford & Son*, which premiered in 1972. This piece of music, written by Quincy Jones, featured a sound developed during the recording of his version of Carole King's "Smackwater Jack," notably utilizing prominent harmonica. "The Red Fox junkyard sitcom premiered on NBC in 1972, punctuated by the song The Street Beater, an opening credits melody that Quincy wrote for the show."
**If I Ever Lose This Heaven (1974)** : The centerpiece single from Quincy Jones's 1974 album *Body Heat*, featuring vocals from prominent black luminaries Leon Ware, Minnie Riperton, and Al Jarreau. This track contributed to *Body Heat* becoming Jones's highest-charting album at the time. "For example, the centerpiece of 1974's Body Heat album, the single If I Ever Lose This Heaven, featured vocals from Leon Ware, Mini-Rypertin, and Al Jaron."
**Is It Love That We're Missing (1975)** : A song from Quincy Jones's album *Mellow Madness*, co-written and performed by the Brothers Johnson, who were hired by Jones for the LP. This track reached number 18 on the R&B chart, marking Quincy's highest charting hit to date under his own name. "The Brothers wound up contributing four songs to the LP, including Is It Love That Were Missing, which reached number 18 on the R&B chart, Quincy's highest charting hit to date under his own name."
**Roots Medley (1977)** : A medley of Quincy Jones's themes from the televised adaptation of Alex Haley's novel *Roots*. This blend of African rhythms with Western orchestration made both the Hot 100 and R&B charts in the summer of 1977, coinciding with the miniseries' record-breaking TV ratings. "A medley of Quincy's themes from Roots, blending African rhythms with Western orchestration, made the hot 100 and R&B charts in the summer of 1977."
**Stuff Like That (1978)** : Quincy Jones's first ever R&B number one and top 40 pop hit as a front-line artist, created through a collaboration with Chaka Khan, Nick Ashford, and Valerie Simpson. The song originated as a jam session with studio musicians like Steve Gadd and Richard Tee, with Ashford and Simpson later adding lyrics and melodic hooks to the "buttery smooth" result. It topped the R&B chart in the summer of 1978 and peaked at number 21 pop, helping to establish Jones as a pivotal figure in "Yacht Soul." "The buttery smooth result was stuff like that, which topped the R&B chart in the summer of 78 and got Q on American top 40 for the first time."

## curiosities
**Early Musical Education** : Born Quincy Delight Jones Jr. in Chicago, Illinois in 1933, Jones was taught the rudiments of stride or jazz piano by a neighbor during his childhood on the city's south side. "Quincy Jones was born on Chicago's south side and as a child, he was taught the rudiments of stride or jazz piano by a neighbor."
**Seattle & Musical Development** : In his teens, Quincy Jones's father moved the family to Seattle, Washington, where Quincy continued to pursue his passion for music. He sang with a local gospel troop and learned to play the trumpet in high school, showing enough promise to earn a scholarship to Boston's Schillinger House (later known as the Berklee School of Music) just one year after graduating. "By his teens, Quincy's father had moved the family to Seattle where Quincy pursued his passion for music."
**Arranger for Jazz Legends** : After relocating to New York City and finding immediate work, Quincy Jones quickly demonstrated his great skill in not just playing, but also the ability to transcribe horn parts or charts. While barely out of his teens, he served as an arranger, writing charts for esteemed musicians such as Count Basie, Tommy Dorsey, and Dinah Washington. "He wrote charts for the likes of Count Basie, Tommy Dorsey and Dina Washington, serving as an arranger while barely out of his teens."
**Leadership in Jazz Orchestras** : Around his 20th birthday, Quincy Jones secured a performing break by joining the brass section of vibraphonist and band leader Lionel Hampton, playing trumpet alongside "Ace Players" Art Farmer and Clifford Brown. Three years later, in 1956, he was playing in the band of legendary trumpeter Dizzy Gillespie, who then chose Quincy to lead a big band of outstanding musicians for a special ensemble. "Q could play very well, but from a very young age he was seen as a leader, straddling the worlds of artist and svengali, the guys who made the music and the ones who made the music happen."
**First Black Major Label Executive** : In 1961, impressed with his work for their French subsidiary, Mercury Records promoted Quincy Jones to the title of Vice President at Mercury U.S. This historic appointment made him the first black upper-level executive at a major U.S. recording company. "Mercury Records, impressed with his work for their French subsidiary, promoted Quincy to the title of Vice President at Mercury U.S., making him the first black upper-level executive at a major U.S. recording company."
**Relationship with Ray Charles** : Quincy Jones and Ray Charles met in the 1940s when Jones was 14 and Charles was 16. Ray Charles served as a formative influence on the younger Quincy, and by the 1950s, with Charles established as an R&B chart-topper, Jones began arranging his albums, including *The Great Ray Charles* (1956) and *The Genius of Ray Charles* (1957), which helped establish Jones as a hitmaker. "16-year-old Ray was a formative influence on 14-year-old Quincy, and by the 50s, with Charles established as an R&B chart-topper, Jones began arranging his albums."
**Hollywood Scoring Career** : Starting with the 1965 Sydney Lumet film *The Pawnbroker*, Quincy Jones embarked on a prolific sideline career as a Hollywood score composer. Between 1965 and 1972, he composed more than two dozen soundtracks, averaging two to four scores per year, several of which were acclaimed. "Jones was also pursuing a sideline as a Hollywood score composer."
**Oscar Nominations (1967)** : At the 1967 Academy Awards, Quincy Jones's music was tied to multiple films. His score for *In Cold Blood*, based on Truman Capote's novel, was nominated for Best Original Score, and a song he composed for the Robert Wagner film *Banning*, titled "The Eyes of Love," was nominated for Best Original Song. "At the 1967 Oscars, in fact, Q's music was tied to multiple films."
**Collaborator on Best Picture** : In 1967, Quincy Jones also provided the music for the film *In The Heat of the Night*, which starred Sidney Poitier and won the Academy Award for Best Picture that year, although Jones's score itself was not nominated. For the film's title track, he teamed up with his long-time friend Ray Charles. "And though its score was not nominated, Jones also provided the music for the film that won 1967's Best Picture, the Sydney Pauâtié starring in The Heat of the Night."
**Brain Aneurysm and Career Shift (1974)** : In 1974, Quincy Jones suffered a brain aneurysm, a life-threatening event that caused him to re-evaluate his furious work schedule. After making a full recovery, he decided to cut back on his commitments and focus on fewer projects where he felt he could have the most impact. This period of recovery and re-focus is seen as giving his work greater direction and making him an even bigger presence in the music industry. "After he made a full recovery, he decided to cut back on his schedule and focus on fewer projects where he could have the most impact."
**Roots Miniseries Score (1977)** : Quincy Jones was invited by author Alex Haley to score the televised adaptation of Haley's acclaimed novel of African American history, *Roots*. The epic 1977 miniseries garnered the highest TV ratings ever recorded to that date, and the project earned Quincy Jones his first Emmy award for his musical contribution. "As for his own recordings, Quincy was invited by the author Alex Haley to score the televised adaptation of Roots, his acclaimed novel of African American History."
**Pivotal Figure in Yacht Soul** : Quincy Jones's 1978 hit single "Stuff Like That" played a key role in establishing him as a pivotal figure in what would later be termed "Yacht Soul." This R&B offshoot of Yacht Rock utilized many of the same nexus of California session musicians favored by smooth bands like Steely Dan, Toto, and The Doobie Brothers, and this distinct sound would prove pivotal to Jones's career by the 1980s. "Stuff like that peaked at number 21 pop and helped establish Jones as the pivotal figure in what would later be termed Yacht Soul."
**Meeting Michael Jackson on The Wiz Set (Late 1970s)** : The production of music for the film adaptation of the Broadway musical *The Wiz* was a project Quincy Jones undertook somewhat begrudgingly as a favor to his friend, film director Sidney Lumet. It was on this film set that Jones met a 20-year-old singer, dancer, and former child star named Michael Jackson, who was playing the Scarecrow. Jackson boldly asked Jones if he would consider producing his first adult solo album, a meeting that would profoundly change both their lives. "And it was on the set of The Wiz that Q met a 20 year old who would change his life, a singer, dancer and former child star who was playing the scarecrow."
**Michael Jackson's Adult Solo Album** : After meeting Michael Jackson on the set of *The Wiz*, Quincy Jones was approached by the young singer, who boldly asked him to produce his first adult solo album. This encounter led to a legendary collaboration that would help Michael Jackson transform into the biggest star pop music had ever seen. "This young man boldly asked Quincy Jones if he might consider producing his first adult solo album."

"Michael looms large in Quincy's legend."
**Allegations and Public Perception** : Michael Jackson's legacy is attached to sexual abuse allegations, including those raised in the 2019 documentary *Leaving Neverland*. Despite this, his music remains inescapable in 2024, forming the backbone of a Tony-winning Broadway musical, being the subject of a forthcoming authorized biopic, and maintaining omnipresence on classic hits radio. His music is described as vital, influential, and beautifully crafted, leading to him being "uncancellable" for many reasons, including the quality of his work with Quincy Jones. "But Michael Jackson is a very different story, a purveyor of truly superb music who has been accused of horrific things."

## songs
**It's My Party (1963)** : Leslie Gore's debut single, produced by Quincy Jones. As a "one woman girl group," Gore delivered the sass and melodrama reminiscent of the Shangri-Las in this tale of a teen who finds her boy Johnny with a "hussey named Judy" at her own celebration. Gore, then a junior in high school, liked the lyric, and Jones threw all his musical savvy at the recording, hiring Jazz Arranger Klaus Ogerman to conduct the backing track and speckling it with "spunky horns." Upon its release in the spring of 1963, it hurtled from number 60 on the Hot 100 to number one in just four weeks, marking Quincy Jones's first number one hit as a producer. "On its my party, Leslie Gore was a one woman girl group, with all the sass and mellow drama of Ashangra Law."
**Judy's Turn to Cry (1963)** : A deliberate sequel to "It's My Party," quickly recorded by Quincy Jones after the success of her debut single. Its lyrics detail Gore's party girl character getting her revenge by luring back Johnny, and it peaked at number five on the charts in the summer of 1963. "Judy's turn to cry was a deliberate sequel down to the lyrics in which Gore's party girl got her revenge by luring back Johnny."
**She's a Fool** : A jazzy R&B style shuffle that became another top five hit for Leslie Gore during her collaboration with Quincy Jones. "Another top five hit."
**Sunshine, Lollipops and Rainbows** : A number 13 hit for Leslie Gore, which she performed in the Frankie Avalon teen exploitation movie *Ski Party*. "The spunky sunshine Lollipops and rainbows, a number 13 hit that Gore performed in the Frankie Avalon teen exploitation movie, Ski Party."
**You Don't Own Me (1964)** : Considered by critics and Leslie Gore herself as her signature hit, this proto-feminist anthem was brought to Quincy Jones by Gore. Jones provided a stately arrangement and a major promotional push, convinced it would return Gore to number one. It peaked at number two in the winter of 1964, behind The Beatles' number one smash "I Want to Hold Your Hand," and was Gore's final top ten hit. "The spunky sunshine Lollipops and rainbows, a number 13 hit that Gore performed in the Frankie Avalon teen exploitation movie, Ski Party."

## curiosities
**Quincy Jones's Discovery** : Quincy Jones personally signed Leslie Gore (birth name Leslie Sue Goldstein), a white Jewish teenager from Tenafly, New Jersey, to Mercury Records. Jones noted her "mellow distinctive voice" and her ability to "sing in tune," a quality he found lacking in many adult rock and roll singers of the time. "Of this discovery, a white Jewish teenager from Tenefly, New Jersey, Jones later wrote, quote, she had a mellow distinctive voice and sang in tune, which a lot of grown-up rock and roll singers couldn't do. So I signed her, unquote."
**Feminist and LGBTQ Icon** : Decades after her hits, Leslie Gore not only became a feminist icon due to songs like "You Don't Own Me," but also an LGBTQ advocate after she publicly came out. She spoke highly of her producer, Quincy Jones, in an interview with Rolling Stone. "Decades later, Gore, who not only became a feminist icon but an LGBTQ advocate after she came out, told Rolling Stone, quote, Quincy was a great mentor and a wonderful teacher. You can't match a genius like Quincy Jones, unquote."

# genre - Disco

## curiosities
**Disco Backlash of 1980** : In the early summer of 1980, the music business was actively contending with a significant disco backlash. Despite this, a string of hits emerged that seemed to defy conventional wisdom, being danceable and bathed in disco strings, yet also sophisticated, meticulous, brassy, and even jazzy, such as "Stomp" by The Brothers Johnson and "Off the Wall" by Michael Jackson. "44 years ago in the early summer of 1980 as the music business was contending with the disco backlash."

# genre - Yacht Soul

## curiosities
**Emergence from Yacht Rock** : Yacht Soul is described as an R&B offshoot of Yacht Rock that became pivotal to Quincy Jones's career by the 1980s. It notably employed many of the same nexus of California session musicians favored by smooth bands like Steely Dan, Toto, and The Doobie Brothers. "Yacht Soul. The R&B offshoot of Yacht Rock that employed many of the same nexus of California session musicians as smooth bands like Steely Dan, Toto and the Dubie Brothers."

# label - Mercury Records

## curiosities
**Quincy Jones's Executive Role** : In 1961, Mercury Records, impressed with Quincy Jones's work for their French subsidiary, promoted him to the title of Vice President at Mercury U.S., a historic move making him the first black upper-level executive at a major U.S. recording company. "Mercury Records, impressed with his work for their French subsidiary, promoted Quincy to the title of Vice President at Mercury U.S., making him the first black upper-level executive at a major U.S. recording company."
**Early Recording Home for Quincy Jones** : After moving to Paris in 1957 to study music theory and work for Mercury Records' French subsidiary, Quincy Jones released his first album, *This Is How I Feel About Jazz*, under his own name on the label that same year. "In 1957, Jones moved to Paris to study music theory and work for the French subsidiary of Mercury Records, and he released his first album under his own name that year."

# concert - Sinatra at the Sands (1966)

## curiosities

# instrument - Harmonica

## curiosities
**Prominence in Quincy Jones's Sound** : The prominent use of harmonica in Quincy Jones's 1971 version of Carole King's "Smackwater Jack" played a crucial role in developing a sound that served as a "dress rehearsal" for one of his most recognized pieces of music: the theme to TV's *Sanford & Son*, "The Street Beater." "And the sound of that track, built around prominent harmonica, turned out to be a dress rehearsal for one of Q's most heard pieces of music, the theme to TV's Sanford & Sun."

# curiosity

**Post-Disco Sound of 1980** : In the early summer of 1980, amid the disco backlash, a series of hits defied conventional wisdom by being danceable and infused with disco strings, yet also distinguished by sophistication, meticulous production, brassiness, and even jazzy elements. Notable examples included The Brothers Johnson's "Stomp," Michael Jackson's "Off the Wall," and George Benson's "Give Me the Night," all sharing a unified sound primarily due to their producer, Quincy Jones. "They were danceable and bathed in disco strings, yet they were sophisticated, meticulous, brassy, even jazzy."
**"We Are The World"** : Quincy Jones played a significant role in "We Are The World," referring to himself as having heard some "pretty big cats" on the so-called "greatest night in pop." This charity mega-single corralled dozens of superstars, making Quincy a totemic figure with the authority to lead such a massive collaborative effort. "He even heard some pretty big cats on the so-called greatest night in pop. We are the world, we are the children, we are the ones to make a brighter day, so let's talk in there."
**Producers as Front-Line Artists** : Decades before hit-making front-line producers like Dr. Dre, Pharrell Williams, Timbaland, Mark Ronson, or Metro Boomin scaled the album charts with their own names above the title, Quincy Jones was already achieving this, even scoring hit records credited solely to himself. "Decades before such hit-making front-line producers as Dr. Dre, Ferelle Williams, Tim Beland, Mark Ronson, or Metro Boomin, Quincy Jones was scaling the album charts on LPs with his own name above the title."
***The Pawnbroker* (1965)** : The Sydney Lumet film that marked the beginning of Quincy Jones's prolific career as a Hollywood score composer, kicking off a streak of more than two dozen soundtracks between 1965 and 1972. "Starting with the 1965 Sydney Lumet film The Pond Broker, Quincy kicked off a streak of more than two dozen soundtracks."
***In Cold Blood* (1967)** : The film adaptation of Truman Capote's novel, for which Quincy Jones composed the score. His work earned him a nomination for Best Original Score at the 1967 Academy Awards, highlighting his early acclaim in Hollywood. "His score for the film of Truman Capote's In Cold Blood was nominated for Best Original Score."
**"The Eyes of Love" (1967)** : A song composed by Quincy Jones for the Robert Wagner film *Banning*. This composition was nominated for Best Original Song at the 1967 Academy Awards, alongside Jones's nomination for Best Original Score for *In Cold Blood*. "A song Quincy composed for the Robert Wagner film Banning called The Eyes of Love was nominated for Best Original Song that year."
***In The Heat of the Night* (1967)** : A Sidney Poitier-starring film that won the 1967 Best Picture Oscar. Quincy Jones provided the music for this film, and although his score was not nominated, he teamed with Ray Charles for the title track, which became a Hot 100 hit. "Jones also provided the music for the film that won 1967's Best Picture, the Sydney Pauâtié starring in The Heat of the Night."
**Rock Musical *Hair*** : The source of the song "Walking in Space," which Quincy Jones covered for the title track of his 1969 album *Walking in Space*. Jones's cover earned him a Grammy for Best Instrumental Jazz Performance. "The album's title track, a cover of a song from the rock musical Hare, one a Grammy for Best Instrumental Jazz Performance."
***Sanford & Son* (1972)** : An NBC television series starring Red Foxx, a junkyard sitcom that premiered in 1972. Its opening credits were punctuated by the song "The Street Beater," a melody Quincy Jones wrote for the show, which developed its sound from his earlier work on "Smackwater Jack." "The Red Fox junkyard sitcom premiered on NBC in 1972, punctuated by the song The Street Beater, an opening credits melody that Quincy wrote for the show."
***Roots* (1977)** : The televised adaptation of Alex Haley's acclaimed novel of African American history. This epic miniseries scored the highest TV ratings ever to that date and earned Quincy Jones his first Emmy award for his score, which blended African rhythms with Western orchestration. "The epic 1977 mini series scored the highest TV ratings ever to that date, and the project won Quincy Jones his first Emmy award."
**Casey Kasem's "American Top 40"** : Quincy Jones's "Stuff Like That" (1978) was his first song to be counted down on Casey Kasem's "American Top 40" radio show, a milestone that showcased his increasing pop chart presence as a front-line artist. "Casey Kason counted it down."
***The Wiz* Film Adaptation** : The film adaptation of the Broadway musical *The Wiz*, for which Quincy Jones produced the music. He did so "almost begrudgingly" as a favor to his friend, film director Sidney Lumet. This project became a pivotal point in Jones's career as it was on its set that he met a 20-year-old Michael Jackson. "But you might say the most pivotal Quincy project of the late 70s, more even than Roots, the Brothers Johnson or Shaka Khan, was producing the music for the film adaptation of the Broadway musical The Wiz."
