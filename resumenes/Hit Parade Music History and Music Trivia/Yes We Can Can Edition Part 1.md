## albums
**The Pointer Sisters (1973)** : This self-titled debut album on Blue Thumb Records featured the four sisters posing in 1920s flapper outfits, reflecting the classic pop harmonies and jazz style present on many tracks, including their cover of Lambert, Hendricks & Ross's "Cloud Burst" and their original jazzy "Jada." It gradually climbed the Billboard chart in summer 1973, reaching just outside the top 40, and eventually peaked at number 13 on the album chart following the success of "Yes We Can Can." "On the cover of their self-titled debut album, the four pointer sisters Anita, Bonnie, June and Ruth posed seated around a table in 20 style flapper outfits with stylish hats and wrap dresses. The cover matched much of the music on the album, which leaned into their classic pop harmonies as on their cover of Lambert Hendrix and Ross's Cloud Burst."
**That's a Plenty (1974)** : Their second LP, which opened with a swing-era style medley centered around "Steam Heat" and contained their surprising country hit "Fairy Tale," which was also its biggest success. "They led off their own 1974 LP, That's a Plenty, with a swing-era style medley centered around the 1950s Broadway standard Steam Heat from The Pajama Game."
**Steppin' (1975)** : The sisters' third album, from which the bold and funky first single "How Long (Betcha' Gotta Chick on the Side)" became their first number one hit on a Billboard chart (R&B). "The sassy first single from the sister's third album, Steppen. Reached number one on the R&B chart and number 20 on the Hot 100 in the fall of 75."
**Energy (1978)** : The Pointer Sisters' debut on Richard Perry's Planet Records, this album saw a reinvention of their sound, leaning more rock-adjacent while maintaining soulful and danceable elements. It included covers of Fleetwood Mac's "Hypnotized" and Stephen Stills' "As I Come of Age," and its single "Fire" became a huge hit, pushing the album to number 13 and earning it gold certification. "On their Planet Records debut energy, Richard Perry made The Pointer Sisters more rock adjacent, while still keeping them soulful, even danceable."
**Priority (1979)** : A quickly issued follow-up album to *Energy*, produced by Richard Perry, featuring further covers of rock tracks by artists like Mott the Hoople ("Who Do You Love") and Stealers Wheel ("Blind Faith"), though it did not achieve the same sales success as its predecessor. "On a quickly issued follow-up album 1979's Priority, Perry had The Sisters cover another round of rock tracks, including Who Do You Love by Moth the Hoople and Blind Faith by Steelers Wheel."

## curiosities
**Oakland, California Origins** : The Pointer Sisters were a family vocal group from Oakland, California, where sisters Ruth (b. 1946), Anita (b. 1948), Bonnie (b. 1950), and June (b. 1953) were born. "a family vocal group from Oakland, California, dropped four straight top ten hits all in 1984."
**Strict Religious Upbringing** : Raised by Sarah and Elton Pointer in a strict religious household, their father, Elton Pointer, was a pastor at the West Oakland Church of God, and the girls were initially only encouraged to listen to and sing gospel music. "They were all born in Oakland, California, Ruth in 1946, Anita in 48, Bonnie in 1950, and Baby June in 1953, to Sarah and Elton pointer who raised them in a strict religious household."
**Formative Bay Area Gospel Scene** : The Bay Area's gospel music scene was highly influential for the sisters, with June and Bonnie participating in the Northern California youth choir, the same ensemble that produced the Edwin Hawkins Singers' "Oh Happy Day." "by the late 1960s Kernodal writes, The West Coast had become the epicenter of a new wave of music experimentation that would shift the sound and cultural context of black, sacred music during the latter part of the 20th century."
**Arkansas Country Music Influence** : During yearly visits to Prescott, Arkansas, the Pointer Sisters were exposed to country music through a local radio station, with Anita Pointer recalling listening to artists like Hank Williams and Willie Nelson and appreciating the "short story format" of country songs. "There, the pointer sisters were exposed to country music. Anita pointer later recalled, quote, I only remember listening to one Arkansas radio station. And all they played was country music."
**Andrews Sisters Harmony Inspiration** : The intricate harmony arrangements of the Andrews Sisters, particularly on songs like "Boogie Woogie Bugle Boy" and "Don't Sit Under the Apple Tree (With Anyone Else But Me)," significantly inspired The Pointer Sisters, who found they could imitate this pre-rock style. "The intricate harmony arrangements of the Andrews sisters who turned songs like the Boogie Woogie Bugleboy and don't sit under the apple tree with anyone else but me into standards."
**Lambert, Hendricks & Ross Vocalese and Interracial Influence** : The Pointer Sisters were particularly inspired by the 1950s vocal trio Lambert, Hendricks & Ross, who pioneered the intricate jazz singing style known as vocalese and were notable for being a co-ed and interracial group. "Who not only helped pioneer the intricate jazz singing style known as vocal ease, they were also coed and interracial joining together the white Dave Lambert and Annie Ross with the black singer John Hendrix."
**Evolution of Group Lineup (Late 60s - 70s)** : Bonnie and June were the primary singers in the late 1960s, then second-oldest sister Anita joined to form a trio, and by the 1970s, oldest sister Ruth made them a quartet. "At first, Bonnie and June, the two youngest sisters, were the primary singers. By the late 60s, they were joined by second oldest sister Anita to form a trio. And by the 70s, oldest sister Ruth made them a quartet."
**West Coast Backing Vocal Troupe** : Around the early 1970s, the Pointer Sisters became a highly sought-after backing vocal troupe, described as a "west coast version of the Sweet Inspirations," providing vocals for a diverse mix of rock and R&B acts. "It was around this time that the pointers became a well-regarded backing vocal troupe, a kind of west coast version of the sweet inspirations."
**First African American Vocal Group at Grand Ole Opry (1974)** : In October 1974, The Pointer Sisters made history by performing "Fairy Tale" at the Grand Ole Opry in Nashville, becoming the first ever African American vocal group to grace its stage. "culminating in an October 1974 performance of Fairy Tale at the Grand Ole Opera, making the pointer sisters the first ever African American vocal group to perform at the Opera."
**Billboard's Fourth Biggest Girl Group in Chart History** : Billboard ranks The Pointer Sisters as the fourth biggest girl group in chart history, following Destiny's Child (third), TLC (second), and The Supremes (first). "Billboard ranks the pointer sisters as the fourth biggest girl group in chart history, after in third place Destiny's Child, in second place TLC, and leading the pack unsurprisingly the Supremes."
**Unique Multi-Genre Top 40 Success** : According to a recent obituary of Anita Pointer, the group holds the distinction of being the only act to have landed top 40 hits on all five of Billboard's country, R&B, pop, dance, and adult contemporary charts, a rare combination for any act, particularly a Black vocal group. "According to a recent obituary of Anita pointer, the pointer sisters are the only group to have landed top 40 hits on all five of Billboard's country, R&B, pop, dance, and adult contemporary charts."
**No Rock and Roll Hall of Fame Nomination** : Despite their indelible hits and broad musical versatility, The Pointer Sisters have never been nominated for the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame, possibly because their widespread pop chart success prevented their fandom from being "owned" by any single genre audience. "Despite recording some truly indelible hits, the pointer sisters do not enjoy the same status as such rock and roll hall of fame inducted girl groups as Martha and the Vandela's, The Ronets, or The Go-Gos. They have never even been nominated for the rock hall."
**Audience Dilution due to Wide Range (1977)** : One potential drawback of The Pointer Sisters' diverse vocal projects and genre-crossing was that it arguably diluted their audience, leading to underperforming singles on the charts by 1977. "If there was any downside to the pointer sisters wide range of vocal projects, it's that it arguably diluted their audience."
**Transition to Trio (1978)** : Following Bonnie Pointer's departure for a solo career in 1978 and a temporary break by June, the group was reduced to a duo of Ruth and Anita, until June was persuaded to return, reconstituting The Pointer Sisters as a trio for their subsequent period of greatest success. "For a brief period, the pointer sisters were reduced to a duo of Ruth and Anita. Youngest sister June took a break from recording entirely."
