## albums
**Aretha (1961) - Major Label Debut** : This was Franklin's major label debut album for Columbia Records, recorded with a jazz trio. It was primarily a collection of standards and compositions by proven songwriters, serving as a studio experiment by Columbia to determine the best approach for Aretha's voice, including a cover of "Over the Rainbow" from The Wizard of Oz. "These singles were found on Franklin's major label debut album, simply titled Aretha."
**I Never Loved a Man the Way I Love You (1967) - Atlantic Debut** : This LP marked Franklin's debut with Atlantic Records and was recorded following her pivotal session at FAME Studios in Muscle Shoals, Alabama, and continued at Atlantic's studios in New York City. The album climbed to number two on the Billboard album chart, where it remained for three weeks, positioned between LPs by The Monkees, The Mamas and the Poppas, and Jefferson Airplane. It spent a year and a half on the chart and became her first million-seller, solidifying her status as an album artist at the peak of 60s rock. "Her LP, I Never Loved a Man the Way I Love You, climbed to number two on the Billboard album chart and stayed there three weeks."
**Amazing Grace (1972) - Gospel Double Album** : This double album became Aretha Franklin's highest-selling LP, representing her boldest display of cultural power and music business clout. Despite generating no top 40 pop hits, it was a full-on gospel album and served as the soundtrack for a film that would not be released until decades after her death. "She released what would become her highest selling LP, a double album that generated no top 40 hits and soundtracked a film that wouldn't see the light of day until decades later."

## songs
**Today I Sing The Blues (1960) - Columbia Debut Single** : This was Aretha Franklin's debut single for Columbia Records, released in the closing weeks of 1960. It cracked the R&B charts, peaking at number 10 just before Christmas, though it did not chart on the Hot 100. "Though it didn't touch the hot 100, it cracked the R&B charts top 10. Peaking at number 10, R&B just before Christmas 1960."
**Won't Be Long (1961) - First Hot 100 Hit** : Released two months after her debut single, this song reached number 7 on the R&B chart. It marked Franklin's first entry on the Hot 100, where it peaked at number 76. "Reach number 7 on the R&B chart and became Franklin's first hot 100 hit. Peaking at number 76."
**Rock-a-Bye Your Baby with a Dixie Melody (1961) - First Pop Top 40** : A show tune previously made famous by Al Jolson and covered by Judy Garland and Dean Martin, Aretha's version brought her gospel-trained soul to the shop-worn classic. It became her first-ever top 40 pop hit, reaching number 37 on the Hot 100 in November 1961, and was her last pop top 40 hit for over six years. "Aretha's version of Rock Abai Your Baby reached number 37 on the hot 100 in November 61."
**Running Out of Fools (1964)** : This single was a number 57 hit for Aretha Franklin during a period when she struggled to find her chart footing. Columbia Records' attempts to position her as a traditional pop star left her out of step with the burgeoning girl group era, and the song lacked the ebullience of other R&B soloists like Sam Cooke or rising Motown stars. "For singles, however poised, like, say, running out of fools, a number 57 hit in 1964, were out of step with the girl group era."
**One Step Ahead (1965)** : As Columbia Records began pivoting Aretha Franklin back toward the R&B chart, this song became a mid-tier hit, reaching number 18 on the R&B chart in the spring of 1965. It signaled a shift in her label's strategy to better align with her gospel-rooted sound. "Columbia got the message and started pivoting Aretha Franklin back toward the R&B chart, scoring some mid-tier hits with one step ahead, a number 18 R&B hit in the spring of 65."
**Cry Like a Baby (1966)** : This song reached number 27 on the R&B chart in late 1966. Its peak coincided with the expiration of Franklin's contract with Columbia, a period during which she actually owed the label money due to a string of low-charting and unprofitable singles and albums. "And cry like a baby, which reached number 27 on the R&B chart in late 1966."
**I Never Loved a Man the Way I Love You (1967) - Atlantic Debut Single** : This song, written by Detroit journeyman Ronnie Shannon and brought to the Muscle Shoals session by Franklin, featured Spooner Oldham on electric piano and Franklin's own sultry piano playing. It became her debut Atlantic single and a smash hit, reaching number nine on the Hot 100 (her first pop top 10) and number one on the R&B chart in the spring of 1967. "I never loved a man the way I loved you would become both the title track of her first Atlantic LP and her debut Atlantic single and it was a smash."
**Do Right Woman, Do Right Man (1967)** : A slow-burning country soul waltz, this R&B classic was written by famed songwriters Chips Moman and Dan Penn. It was one of the tracks recorded for Franklin's Atlantic debut LP, "I Never Loved a Man the Way I Love You," continuing the recording sessions at Atlantic's studios in New York City with members of the Muscle Shoals Rhythm section. "Including a slow-burning country soul waltz written by themed songwriters Chips Momen and Dan Penn called Do Right Woman, Do Right Man."
**Dr. Feelgood (1967)** : Co-written by Aretha Franklin herself, this track was recorded for her Atlantic debut LP. It showcased her songwriting ability alongside her powerful vocal performance on the album that redefined her career. "And a couple of tracks co-written by Franklin herself. The aforementioned Dr. Feelgood."
**Save Me (1967)** : Co-written by Aretha Franklin, this chugging, horn-inflicted jam was one of the tracks featured on her Atlantic debut LP, "I Never Loved a Man the Way I Love You." It contributed to the album's collection of world-beating R&B classics, demonstrating Franklin's artistic contributions beyond interpretation. "And a chugging horn-inflicted jam called Save Me."
**Respect (1967) - Chart-Topping Anthem** : Originally written and recorded by Otis Redding in 1965, Franklin's cover is widely considered the greatest pop cover of all time due to its transformative power. She flipped the gender, added new sections like the R-E-S-P-E-C-T spelling and the "Socket To Me" chant, and remade it into a feminist anthem about a woman demanding her own money and proper deference. Released as the second single from her Atlantic debut album in April 1967, it climbed to number one on both the Hot 100 and the R&B chart by June. "Just by singing about earning her own money and demanding her proper from her man, Franklin remade respect into a feminist anthem and a chart topping smash."
**Baby I Love You (1967)** : This song was one of Aretha Franklin's nine straight top 40 hits that cracked the top 10 between 1967 and 1968, reaching number four in 1967. It contributed to her reign as the Queen of Soul during her imperial phase with Atlantic Records. "Between 1967 and 1968, she racked up a streak of nine straight top 40 hits that cracked the top 10, including such classics as Baby I Love You, number four in 1967."
**You Make Me Feel Like A Natural Woman (1967) - Anthem of Self-Actualization** : This standout hit was specially ordered by Atlantic producer Jerry Wexler from famed Brill Building songwriters Carole King and Gerry Goffin, based on a title Wexler conceived for Aretha. Featuring gospel-style piano by Spooner Oldham, backing vocals from The Sweet Inspirations and Aretha's sister Irma Franklin, and strings arranged by Ralph Burns, the song became an anthem of female self-actualization. It reached number eight on the Hot 100 and number two on the R&B chart in November 1967. "You Make Me Feel You Make Me Feel Like A Natural Woman, a natural woman reached number eight on the hot 100 and number two on the R&B chart in November 67."
**Chain of Fools (1968)** : An early 1968 hit, this song reached number two on the Hot 100. It was part of Aretha Franklin's impressive streak of nine consecutive top 40 hits that broke into the top 10 between 1967 and 1968, further solidifying her status as a dominant pop and R&B artist. "And in early 1968, the number two hit, Chain of Fools."
**Sweet Sweet Baby (Since You've Been Gone) (1968)** : This song achieved a number five hit status in the spring of 1968. It was another success in Aretha Franklin's remarkable streak of nine straight top 10 pop hits, showcasing her consistent chart presence during her imperial phase. "Or Sweet Sweet Baby, since you've been gone, a number five hit in the spring of 1968."
**The House That Jack Built (1968)** : This summer 1968 hit reached number six on the charts, adding to Franklin's impressive run of consecutive top 10 singles. It further cemented her position as a leading artist in both R&B and pop music. "For the summer, 68 hit, The House that Jack built, which reached number six."
**(I Can't Get No) Satisfaction (1967) - Rolling Stones Cover** : Aretha Franklin's 1967 take on The Rolling Stones' classic was described by critic Jack Hamilton as "exciting and new." By singing it, she changed and broadened the meaning of Mick Jagger's original lyrics, demonstrating her power as a song interpreter. "In his book Just Around Midnight, my slate colleague Jack Hamilton calls Franklin's 1967 take on the Rolling Stones satisfaction quote, exciting and new."
**The Weight (1969) - The Band Cover** : Franklin's cover of The Band's classic "The Weight" charted significantly better than the original. Six months after The Band's version reached number 63 on the Hot 100, Aretha's rendition climbed to number 19. "Six months after the band took their classic The Weight to number 63 on the Hot 100, Aritha took her cover to number 19."
**Eleanor Rigby (1969) - Beatles Cover** : Franklin's "utterly transformative" cover of The Beatles' "Eleanor Rigby" reimagined Paul McCartney's morose meditation on lonely people with "Aretha sassily calling herself Eleanor Rigby." Her version nearly matched the original's peak, reaching number 17 on the Hot 100 (compared to The Beatles' #11 in 1966) and achieved a new peak of number 5 on the R&B chart. "Later in 69, Franklin's overhaul of the Beatles Eleanor Rigby and utterly transformative cover that took Paul McCartney's Marose meditation on all the lonely people and rethought it with Aritha sassally calling herself Eleanor Rigby."
**Border Song (1970) - Elton John Cover** : Franklin's cover of Elton John's "Border Song" entered the top 40, reaching number 37 on the Hot 100. This rendition was particularly significant as Elton John's original had stalled at number 92, and Aretha's version effectively gave him his first top 40 hit as a songwriter. "In 1970, Franklin gave Elton John his first top 40 hit as a songwriter when she took her cover of his border song into the top 40."
**Call Me (1970)** : This song reliably topped Billboard's Soul Singles chart in 1970, even when her hits might have missed the top 10 on the pop side. It demonstrated her continued dominance within the R&B genre. "Even when her hits missed the top 10 on the pop side, they reliably topped Billboard's Soul Singles chart, including 1970s call me."
**Spanish Harlem (1971)** : In 1971, this song reliably topped Billboard's Soul Singles chart, showcasing Aretha Franklin's consistent appeal and success within the R&B market, even as her pop chart performance varied. "1971's Spanish Harlem."
**Bridge Over Troubled Water (1971) - Simon & Garfunkel Cover** : A year after Simon & Garfunkel's original reached number one, Aretha Franklin covered this song as a full-on gospel hymn, fulfilling its destiny. Her version impressively reached a respectable number 6 pop peak on the Hot 100 and brought Paul Simon's composition to number one on the R&B chart. "Impressively, her version not only reached a very respectable number 6 pop peak on the Hot 100, she brought Paul Simon s composition to number 1 on the R&B chart."
**Daydreaming (1972)** : This song reliably topped Billboard's Soul Singles chart in 1972, further confirming Franklin's premier artist status in R&B during the early 1970s, a period marked by her consistent Grammy wins. "And 1972's Daydreaming."
**Nessun Dorma (1998) - Opera Aria** : Aretha Franklin performed this challenging tenor aria from Puccini's opera "Turandó" at the Grammy Awards on February 25, 1998, stepping in for Luciano Pavarotti with less than an hour's notice. She had only previously performed it at a Music Care's event a few nights prior, but her live telecast rendition was a vocal masterclass, restrained and subtle before blasting into the aria's infamously high top note. "It was a vocal masterclass restrained and subtle when needed before blasting into space on the arias infamously high top note at the climax."
**I Knew You Were Waiting (For Me) (1987) - George Michael Duet** : This duet with Wham! frontman George Michael reached number one on the Hot 100 in the week ending April 18, 1987. It marked Franklin's second Hot 100 chart-topper, coming two decades after "Respect," and signaled a significant comeback in her career. "The week ending April 18, 1987, when I knew you were waiting for me, Aretha Franklin's duet with WAM frontman George Michael reached number one on the Hot 100."
**(Unnamed 1998 Single) (1998) - Lauren Hill Collaboration** : Released in the same month as her 1998 Grammys performance, this single cracked the pop top 30 and went top 5 on the R&B chart, featuring a collaboration with rapper Lauren Hill. ## curiosities
**Early Career Misdirection - Columbia Records** : The great irony of Aretha Franklin's early career was that Columbia Records, which signed her at age 18, primarily wanted her to be a pop singer in the mold of Ella Fitzgerald or Dinah Washington, largely ignoring the R&B market. Even legendary talent scout John Hammond, who signed her, later expressed disappointment with how the label presented her gifts, leading to mixed results and financial debt at the end of her contract. "Even John Hammond later said he was disappointed with how the label presented Franklin's gifts to the world."
**Columbia Contract Debt (1966)** : By the time her Columbia contract expired in late 1966, Aretha Franklin actually owed the label money. This was a result of her string of low-charting, unprofitable singles and albums; she had recorded nine LPs for the label in six years, with more than half failing to chart and none peaking higher than number 69 on the Billboard album chart. "By the time that song peaked, not only had Franklin's Columbia contract expired, she actually owed the label money due to her string of low charting, unprofitable singles and albums."
**FAME Studios Session Incident (January 1967)** : During her initial session at FAME Studios in Muscle Shoals, Alabama, which yielded "I Never Loved a Man the Way I Love You," the recording environment became chaotic. Franklin's then-husband and manager, Ted White, became intoxicated and had two brass players fired, purportedly for making advances toward Aretha, leading to a physical altercation between White and the studio owner. As a result, Jerry Wexler pulled Aretha out, making it the only time she ever recorded at FAME studios, though they completed one pivotal song. "The fame session was immortal but also kind of a mess. Franklin's then husband and manager Ted White got drunk and had two brass players fired supposedly for coming on to Aretha."
**Queen of Soul Coronation (1967)** : The moniker "Queen of Soul" was bestowed upon Aretha Franklin at a 1967 concert at Chicago's Regal Theater. During a multi-act bill headlined by Franklin, local disc jockey Purvis Span, who considered himself an arbiter of blues and soul, held a theatrical coronation ceremony at the end of the show, placing an actual crown on her head and proclaiming her the Queen of Soul. "He placed an actual crown on Franklin's head, pronouncing her the Queen of Soul. The name stuck."
**Time Magazine Cover (1968)** : By 1968, Aretha Franklin's cultural impact was undeniable, leading to her being featured on the cover of Time Magazine. The headline, "The Sound of Soul," underscored her widely accepted status as the ruler of soul music and a symbol of Black America. "By 1968, Franklin was on the cover of Time Magazine under the headline The Sound of Soul."
**Southern Christian Leadership Conference Award (1968)** : Aretha Franklin was honored by her friend Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. with a Southern Christian Leadership Conference Award two months before his assassination. This award acknowledged her status as a black icon and a symbol of increased confidence and pride within the African American community during the civil rights movement. "Many also saw her as a symbol of black America itself, reflecting the increased confidence and pride of African Americans in the decade of the civil rights movement and other triumphs for the black community."
**Grammy Awards Dominance (1968-1975)** : Aretha Franklin held the longest single-award Grammy streak in history, winning the prize for Best Female R&B Vocal Performance for eight consecutive years, from 1968 through 1975. This unparalleled achievement solidified her position as the premier artist of R&B during the early 1970s. "For the first 8 years that the Grammy Awards gave out a prize for best female R&B vocal performance, from 1968 through 1975, Aritha won that Grammy every year. Still, the longest single award Grammy streak in history."
**Opera Performance at Grammys (February 25, 1998)** : At the 1998 Grammy Awards at Radio City Music Hall in New York City, Aretha Franklin famously stepped in for opera great Luciano Pavarotti to perform "Nessun Dorma." Approached less than an hour before taking the stage, and with only a prior performance at a Music Care's event as rehearsal, Franklin delivered a "vocal masterclass," hitting the aria's infamously high top note at the climax. "The story goes that the Grammy producers approached her to fill in for Pavarotti less than an hour before she took the stage."
**Chart Success More Exciting Than Opera (1998)** : Despite her lauded, impromptu Grammy performance of "Nessun Dorma," Aretha Franklin later revealed to biographer Mark Bego that she was more excited about the chart performance of a single she released that same month. This single, a collaboration with Lauren Hill, cracked the pop Top 30 and went Top 5 on the R&B chart. "Franklin later told Biographer Mark Bego that in the winter of 98, she was more excited about how this single charted than she was filling in for Luciano Pavarotti."
**Vocal Influence on Generations** : Aretha Franklin's impact on generations of vocalists is incalculable, as she widened the lane for mainstream pop for numerous gospel-rooted singers. Her influence extended to her R&B contemporaries like Gladys Knight, Roberta Flack, and Patti LaBelle, and younger performers known by first name alone, such as Whitney and Ariana, as well as male contemporaries like Elton John, Rod Stewart, and Michael McDonald who incorporated R&B style vocals into rock. "Her impact on the trajectory of popular music, especially in the vocal arena, is simply incalculable. She widened the lane for what was considered mainstream pop for generations of gospel-rooted singers."
**Critiques of Franklin's Voice and Ad-Libs** : Franklin's vocal power has been extensively praised, with Rolling Stone calling it "the greatest voice pop music has ever produced," and Beyoncé describing it as "one of God's blessings." Her ad-libs were also considered iconic, with critic Emily Lordy arguing in her book "The Meaning of Soul" that Franklin's spontaneous vocal runs "generated effective networks between women," serving as conversations with fans about everyday situations. "Even Aretha's ad-libs were considered iconic. In her book The Meaning of Soul, critic and author Emily Lordy argues that Franklin's spontaneous vocal runs, the random stuff she just invented in the moment, were like conversations that quote, generated effective networks between women."

## curiosities
**Famous Baptist Minister** : Reverend C.L. Franklin was one of the most famous Baptist ministers in the American Midwest, known for his compelling sermons and powerful voice. He relocated his family to Detroit in 1946 when Aretha was four, and was frequently in demand at pulpits across the country on the church revival circuit. "Reverend Clarence Levant or C.L. Franklin, one of the most famous Baptist ministers in the American Midwest."
**"Million Dollar Voice" and Recording Star** : C.L. Franklin was renowned for his "million dollar voice" and was a recording star in his own right, with his sermons captured for posterity. His sermon, "The Eagle Stireth Her Nest," is even preserved in the U.S. Library of Congress. "On the church revival circuit, C.L. was known for his so-called million dollar voice, and he was something of a recording star himself as his sermons were captured for posterity."
**Family Life and Infidelities** : Due to Reverend Franklin's infidelities, he and his wife, Barbara Franklin, separated in 1948 when Aretha was six. His frequent travels also meant that Aretha and her four siblings were often raised by extended family and friends, including gospel legend Mahalia Jackson. "Due to Reverend Franklin's infidelities, he and Barbara Franklin separated in 1948 when Aretha was six."
**Manager and Music Mentor to Aretha** : Reverend Franklin began managing his daughter Aretha in 1954 when she was 12, taking her along on his gospel caravan tours where she would perform songs like "Jesus Be a Fence Around Me" or "Precious Lord." He also arranged for her to record tracks for a small independent label in Detroit in 1956 and gave his blessing for her to pursue pop music around her 18th birthday. "When she turned 12 in 1954, C.L. began managing his daughter, even taking her along on his gospel caravan tours, where she would perform at various churches, songs like Jesus be a fence around me, or precious Lord."
