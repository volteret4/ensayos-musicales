## albums
**My Aim Is True (1977)** : This was his Nick Lowe-produced debut album. During its recording, Costello was still holding down a mundane day job, commuting between sessions and his data entry clerk position at Elizabeth Arden offices. He had not yet formed a backing band, so for this LP, he was backed by members of the California band Clover. Stiff Records marketed the album as punk adjacent, but its sound ranged widely. It initially reached number 86 on the Billboard album chart, but after his infamous Saturday Night Live appearance, it soared into the top 40, peaking at number 32. "My Aim is True."
**This Year's Model (1978)** : Costello's second album, which saw him move deeper into New Wave. This album was ranked as the top Paz and Jop album of 1978. "This Year's model."
**Armed Forces (1979)** : The American version of this album included The Attractions' take on Nick Lowe's "What's So Funny About Peace, Love and Understanding," which, though never issued as an A-side, was played extensively on album rock radio. This exposure, along with the single "Accidents Will Happen," fueled *Armed Forces* to become Costello's biggest US album, reaching number 10 on the Billboard album chart by March 1979. However, radio stations later pulled the LP from rotation after details of his drunken incident in Columbus, Ohio, were leaked. "Armed Forces."
**Get Happy (1980)** : This album was produced by his mentor, Nick Lowe, and was entirely devoted to classic American soul music. "Get Happy."

## songs
**What's So Funny About Peace, Love, and Understanding (1978)** : While originally written and recorded by Nick Lowe with Brinsley Schwarz, The Attractions' version of this "peace-nick anthem" became the definitive one. Despite never being issued as an A-side and therefore not charting, it received extensive play on album rock radio and was included on the American version of Costello's 1979 album *Armed Forces*, contributing significantly to that album's top 10 chart peak. "what's so funny about peace, love, and understanding."
**Less Than Zero (1977)** : Costello's debut single, released on Stiff Records and produced by Nick Lowe. It defined his early sound as melodic but bashing, acerbic but catchy. The song was a diatribe against former British fascist leader Oswald Mosley, whom Costello had seen on TV attempting to distance himself from his racist past. Costello famously began to perform this song live on Saturday Night Live before abruptly switching to another track. "Less than zero was the debut single by the 22-year-old born Declan Patrick McManus known to the world by the moniker Elvis Costello."
**Alison (1977)** : This was his second single, a slow dance "anti-love love song" with lyrics expressing bitterness, such as "I understand that you are not impressed," or "I wish that I could stop you from talking when I hear the silly things you say." Despite Costello's often embittered and unromantic stage persona, the song is considered one of the "most bio-filled beautiful songs ever." A lyric from this song, "I know this world is killing you, Oh, Alison. My best true," provided the title for his debut album, *My Aim Is True*. Linda Ronstadt's cover of "Alison" reached number 30 on the Billboard Adult Contemporary Chart in May 1979. "Alison."
**Watching the Detectives (1977)** : This single bubbled under the Hot 100, reaching number 108. It was also on trend due to its reggae beat, a connection Costello made overtly, which helped normalize reggae in white rock and contributed to his breakthrough in America. The song was also a number 15 hit in his UK homeland. "Watching the detectives was also on trend in another way. It's Reggae Beat."
**I Don't Want to Go to Chelsea (1978)** : A British hit from his album *This Year's Model*, which reached number 16 in the UK. "I don't want to go to Chelsea."
**Pump It Up (1978)** : A power pop party rocker featured on *This Year's Model*, which became a number 24 UK hit. "Pump it up."
**Accidents Will Happen (1979)** : This single narrowly missed the Hot 100, peaking at number 101. It played a role in fueling the commercial success of his album *Armed Forces*. "Accidents will happen."
**Oliver's Army (1979)** : Costello's biggest ever UK single, reaching number two. The song, however, also drew attention for an "unfortunate use of the N-word." Although it passed cultural gatekeepers at the time, the lyric later seemed even more regrettable in light of his Columbus, Ohio, incident. "Oliver's Army."
**I Can't Stand Up for Falling Down (1980)** : The lead single from *Get Happy*, this was a cover of a previously obscure B-side by 60s R&B duo Sam and Dave. Elvis and The Attractions reinvented it as an ebullient rave-up, turning it into a number four UK hit and generating royalties for its black songwriters, Homer Banks and Alan Jones. "I Can't Stand Up for Falling Down."
**High Fidelity (1980)** : One of the soulful original songs featured on the *Get Happy* album. "High Fidelity."
**New Amsterdam (1980)** : The final single from *Get Happy*, this waltz-time number was a number 36 UK hit. It also signaled the more baroque approach Costello would begin to pursue in his music as he entered the 1980s. "New Amsterdam."

## curiosities
**Self-Naming after "The King"** : He had the nerve to name himself after Elvis Presley, the king of rock and roll. "named himself after the king of rock and roll."
**Showbiz Family Background** : Costello grew up in a showbiz family, as the son of Ross McManus, a British Irish band leader and vocalist. His father was reportedly keen to achieve success on the hit parade, exemplified by a "delightfully cheesy" televised performance in 1963 of Pete Seeger's folk-standard "If I Had a Hammer," and he also recorded jingles for TV adverts, with Elvis singing backup on a White's Lemonade ad in 1973. "He'd grown up in a showbiz family, the son of British Irish band leader and vocalist Ross McManus."
**"Angry Young Men" Archetype Founder** : With his acerbic rock nerd persona, Costello was the first to establish the archetype of the "Angry Young Man" among his peers. "No one would even think to call Graham Parker an angry young man before this other, a Serbic rock nerd built that archetype first."
**Punk Identity through Lyrics** : What was most authentically punk about Elvis Costello was primarily his lyrical content rather than his musical style. He possessed a more pronounced rebellious streak compared to Nick Lowe. "what was most punk about Elvis Costello were his words more than his music."
**Distinctive Image and Stage Presence** : Costello presented himself as a different kind of male rock star, characterized by his Buddy Holly glasses, narrow suits, and jittery, akimbo-limbed singing stances. He often came across as too fitful and embittered to be seen as romantic, yet his songs, like "Alison," still managed to convey romance. "Costello was a different kind of male rock star with buddy Holly Glasses, narrow suits, and jittery, a Kimbo-limbed singing stances."
**Elizabeth Arden Day Job** : During the recording of his debut album, *My Aim Is True*, Costello was still holding down a mundane day job as a data entry clerk at Elizabeth Arden offices, commuting between his work and the recording sessions. "Elvis had to commute between the recording sessions and the offices of Elizabeth Arden, where he worked as a data entry clerk."
**Saturday Night Live Stunt (1977)** : Costello and The Attractions secured their appearance on NBC TV's *Saturday Night Live* only because the original guests, The Sex Pistols, were unable to perform due to visa issues. Columbia Records insisted Costello perform his single "Less Than Zero," but believing Americans wouldn't understand its subject matter (British fascist Oswald Mosley), Costello abruptly stopped the song after just seven seconds live on the air and switched to the then-unreleased "Radio, Radio." This act enraged SNL producer Lorne Michaels and NBC brass, leading to Costello being banned from the show for a dozen years. However, Columbia Records viewed it as excellent publicity, solidifying Costello's image as a "punk fellow traveler" and an "actual angry young man." "What Costello pulled live on the air that night became the stuff of legend."
**Columbus, Ohio Incident (1979)** : In the spring of 1979, during a drunken exchange at a hotel bar in Columbus, Ohio, Costello used racial slurs to insult several African American musical artists, including James Brown and Ray Charles, in front of members of Stephen Stills' band. When details of this incident were leaked to the press, his American reputation was tarnished, leading radio stations to pull the *Armed Forces* LP from rotation and causing later dates on his US tour to sell poorly. Costello, who had previously been involved in England's Rock Against Racism movement, felt foolish and expressed deep regret for the incident in later interviews, stating in 1982, "what could I do, hold up a sign that read, I really like black people?" "After Word got around that spring of an incident at a hotel bar in Columbus, Ohio between Costello and members of Stephen Stills band."
**Musical Reinvention in the 80s** : Costello's later work, particularly signaled by "New Amsterdam," indicated a move towards a more baroque approach. In the 1980s, he, along with other "angry young men," would reinvent the New Wave template, reaching new pop heights and expanding their musical scope beyond punk, post punk, or even power pop, by following their own muse. "the more baroque approach Costello would pursue as he entered the 80s."

## albums
**Nervous On the Road (1972)** : An album by Brinsley Schwarz where Lowe wrote almost every song that was not a cover, showcasing his early gift for melody. "Nervous On the Road."
**The New Favorites of Brinsley Schwarz (1974)** : The final album by Brinsley Schwarz, produced by Dave Edmonds. On this album, Nick Lowe experienced a significant songwriting breakthrough with the original version of "What's So Funny About Peace, Love and Understanding." "1974's The New Favorites of Brindsley Schwartz."

## songs
**What's So Funny About Peace, Love and Understanding (1974)** : The original version of this song was recorded with Brinsley Schwarz for their final album. It was a remarkably meta song, which, despite the pub rock genre's rejection of 60s hippie rock, saw Lowe embracing the hippie ideal of peace and love and delivering a cynically phrased but earnestly delivered plea for that ideal over a sparkling power pop melody. The Brinsley's version, however, never saw release. "The original version of what's so funny about peace, love and understanding."
**I Knew the Bride (When She Used to Rock and Roll)** : A retro rock single that Lowe wrote for Dave Edmonds, which became a number 26 UK hit. "I New the Bride when she used to rock and roll."
**So It Goes (1976)** : Lowe's debut solo single, released on Stiff Records. This track was described as straight-up Power Pop and featured guitar backing from his friend, Dave Edmonds. "So it goes."
**I Love the Sound of Breaking Glass (unknown year)** : This sleek, New Wave-oriented track became Nick Lowe's biggest ever British single, reaching number seven in the UK. "I Love the Sound of Breaking Glass."
**Girls Talk (1978)** : A song written by Elvis Costello for Dave Edmonds that saw Edmonds shift in a New Wave direction and eventually reached number four in the UK. "Girls Talk."
**Cruel to Be Kind (1979)** : This song was co-written by Lowe and his Brinsley Schwarz bandmate Ian Gomm during their time together. Lowe modeled it after "The Love I Lost," an early disco hit by Harold Melvin and the Blue Notes, which he admired and sought to adapt into a power pop song. The Brinsley's version was never released, but Lowe later re-recorded it with his Rockpile bandmate Dave Edmonds. What might have been an "odd fit for the pub rock era" proved "ideal for the New Wave era." Released in the summer of 1979, "Cruel to Be Kind" reached number 12 in both the US and the UK. It remains Lowe's only top 40 hit in America, making him, by definition, a one-hit wonder there. Lowe has stated that he still loves the song and continues to play it live. "Crull to be kind."
**American Squirm (1979)** : A single for which Lowe was backed by Elvis Costello and The Attractions. Today, this track is primarily remembered as the A-side of a single whose B-side, the classic "What's So Funny About Peace, Love and Understanding," is now far better known. "American Squirm."

## curiosities
**Musical Versatility and Definition Challenges** : Like his friend Elvis Costello, Lowe's musical style was difficult to define, having been described as everything from pub rock to power pop to post punk. "Low was hard to define. These men had been called everything from pub rock to power pop to post punk."
**Producer and Talent Incubator** : Equally significant to his singing and songwriting careers was his role as a producer and an incubator of new talent. He filled a similar role in the mid-70s for emerging British New Wave acts as Dave Edmonds had for the pub rock scene in the early 70s. "Equally important as Nick Lo's singing and songwriting careers were his other role as a producer and incubator of New Talent."
**Mentorship of Graham Parker** : Around 1975, Lowe began mentoring Graham Parker, eventually producing Parker's 1976 debut album with The Rumor, *Howlin' Wind*. "Graham Parker and The Rumor, Howlin' Wind, was produced by former Bringsley leader Nick Lo."
**Mentorship of Elvis Costello** : In 1977, Lowe went on to mentor another emerging singer-songwriter from the pub rock scene, Elvis Costello, a creative partnership that ultimately proved to be a much greater success. "Low would go on to mentor another newly emerging singer-songwriter who'd made his bones on the pub rock scene."

## albums
**Look Sharp (1979)** : His debut album, produced by American producer David Kirchenbaum, which positioned Jackson as a sardonic rocker akin to Elvis Costello, featuring cutting lyrics and New Wave pop hooks. His band provided a "bashing energy" even to his most melodic compositions. The album cracked the top 30 and achieved gold status. "Look Sharp."

## songs
**Steppin' Out (1982)** : This song broke into the top 10 on Billboard's Hot 100 during the week ending November 20th, 1982, eventually peaking at number 6. It was characterized as "urban sophisticated." "Steppen Out by Joe Jackson broke into the top 10 on billboards hot 100 on its way to a number 6 peak."
**Is She Really Going Out with Him? (1978)** : Jackson's first major pop hit. It is a bitter lament sung by a solitary man pining for a woman he feels he has no chance with. Despite its caustic facade, the song is melodically graceful and romantic. It was first released in late 1978, finally cracking the Hot 100 in June 1979, and by mid-July, it had entered the top 40 on its way to a number 21 peak. "Is she really going out with him."
**Got the Time (unknown year)** : On this track, the Joe Jackson band veered completely towards pure punk, demonstrating their versatility. The song was later covered by the speed metal band Anthrax. "On Got the Time, which would later be covered by Speed Metal Band and Thrax, the Joe Jackson band veered all the way toward pure punk."
**It's Different for Girls (1979)** : A significant hit single from the album *I'm the Man* in his homeland, reaching number five in the UK and becoming Joe Jackson's biggest ever British hit. It is described as an insightful, empathetic song presented as a conversation between a man and a woman, with the woman taking on the role of the hardened cynic. Notably, Jackson played no instruments on the track, including his signature piano, to allow the melody to resonate primarily on guitar. "It's different for girls."

## curiosities
**"Least Likely Punk" Background** : Jackson was described as the "least likely punk of all" due to his profound love of jazz and classical music. He earned a scholarship to London's Royal Academy of Music and developed his skills as a multi-instrumentalist on the cabaret circuit rather than as a pub rocker. "Joe Jackson was, in a way, the least likely punk of all, a lover of jazz and classical music who'd earned a scholarship to London's Royal Academy of Music."
**Name Change Inspired by TV** : He did not adopt the name Joe Jackson until the mid-1970s, choosing it because of his resemblance to the title character of a British TV puppet show called Joe 90. "would not change his name to Joe Jackson until the mid 70s after his resemblance to the title character on a British TV puppet show called Joe 90."
**Admiration for Punk and New Wave** : Despite his classical education and diverse musical background from playing in clubs, Jackson admired the punk and New Wave movements, specifically appreciating their energy, simplicity, and outspoken lyrical tone. He found no difficulty in incorporating these elements into his own music. "despite his classical education and background playing many types of music in clubs, Jackson admired the punk and New Wave movements, especially the energy and simplicity of the music and the outspoken tone of the lyrics."
**"Angry Young Men" Tag** : Upon the release of *Look Sharp*, Jackson was immediately compared to Elvis Costello, as music magazines labeled both snide but melodic rockers as "angry young men." This term, initially coined for British playwrights and novelists, also soon included Graham Parker. He was perceived as possessing a "gimmelied, distinctly British take on rock." "Jackson was immediately compared to Costello, as magazines tagged both snide but melodic rockers as angry young men."
**Musical Evolution in the 80s** : In the 1980s, Jackson would transition to become the "jazz bow's favorite rocker," indicating his move beyond the confines of punk, post punk, or power pop. "Joe becomes the jazz bow's favorite rocker."

## albums
**Howlin' Wind (1976)** : His debut album with his backing band, The Rumor, produced by Nick Lowe, who presented Parker's songs with a "ragged frugality." The album was met with significant critical acclaim, with Rolling Stone praising The Rumors' "raw efficiency" and reserving "special praise for Parker's songwriting." Although it did not chart in the UK or US, it was ranked number two on the Paz and Jop critics pole at the end of 1976. "Howlin' Wind."
**Heat Treatment (1976)** : This album was released just six months after his debut, *Howlin' Wind*. It marked Parker's first appearance on the charts, reaching number 52 on the UK album chart and number 169 on Billboard's Top LPs in America. It also ranked number four on the Paz and Jop critics pole at the end of 1976. Most of the album was overseen by Robert John "Mutt" Lang, a future hard rock, pop, and country producer, an "odd pairing" Parker later sought to undo. Nick Lowe produced the album's track "Back Door Love." "heat treatment."
**The Parkerilla (1978)** : A "quicky live album" recorded with his band, The Rumor, which Parker undertook to fulfill his contract with Mercury Records, as he believed the label was "botching his career." His contempt for the project was evident when he remade an old track, "Don't Ask Me Questions," as a disco song specifically to fill side 4 of the double LP, a creative decision described as "not great." "The Parker Rilla."
**Squeezing Out Sparks (1979)** : Parker's most celebrated album, which Arista Records chief Clive Davis supported with a "big advance and an ample recording budget." All Music's Stephen Thomas Erlewine later described it as a "masterful fusion of pub rock classicism, new wave pop and pure vitriol." Released in March 1979, the album finally pushed Parker into the top 40 on the album chart, peaking at number 40 and remaining on the chart for half a year. Critically, *Sparks* performed even better than his previous albums, with Paz and Jop ranking it first in its 1979 poll. "Squeezing Out sparks."

## songs
**Silly Thing (1976)** : His horn-inflected debut single. This track showcased Parker's distinctive sound, described as "white soul crossed with pub rock," while his voice and vibe also carried the edgy influence of punk, creating an "unusual collision of styles." "The Horn Inflected Silly Thing."
**Hold Back the Night (1977)** : A single from the *Heat Treatment* album that managed to crack Billboard's Hot 100, peaking at number 58 in 1977. "Hold Back the Night."
**Back Door Love (1977)** : A "cheeky track" featured on the *Heat Treatment* album, which was produced by Nick Lowe. "Back Door Love."
**Sweet On You (unknown year)** : A single that failed to reach the Hot 100, illustrating Mercury Records' difficulties in effectively promoting Graham Parker. "Sweet On You."
**Don't Ask Me Questions (1978)** : An older track that Parker controversially remade as a disco song to fill side 4 of his live double LP, *The Parkerilla*. This was a deliberate act to show his contempt for the project and his dissatisfaction with Mercury Records. "don't ask me questions."
**Mercury Poisoning (1978)** : A "thinly veiled screed" against Mercury Records, which Parker issued as a "final kiss off" to the label. Released as a one-off by Stiff Records in the UK and Arista Records in the US, critics lauded it as one of Parker's best songs, further solidifying his reputation as one of rock's most acerbic scribes, drawing comparisons to The Sex Pistols' "EMI." "Mercury Poisoning."
**I Want You Back (Jackson 5 cover) (1979)** : Following the critical success of *Squeezing Out Sparks*, Parker and The Rumor issued a live cover of this Jackson 5 song as a single, and it briefly appeared on the pop chart. "I Want You Back."

## curiosities
**"Angry Young Men" Figure** : Parker gained acclaim from critics and tastemakers, becoming known as one of the "Angry Young Men." This was an ironic title, considering he was often as "tender and soulful as much as they were angry," but the name nonetheless stuck. He became a central figure, or "locus," of what New Wave evolved into, emerging from England's pub rock and punk rock scenes and the transatlantic sound of Power Pop. "Graham Parker."
**Early Life and Work Experience** : Born in Hackney, East London in 1950, Parker spent the early 1970s working various jobs such as factory worker, dock worker, and gas station attendant, while simultaneously performing in obscure pub rock bands around London. "Graham Thomas Parker, born in Hackney, East London in 1950, spent the early 70s working on jobs, factory worker, dock worker, gas station attendant, and gigging around London in obscure pub rock bands."
**Musical Influences** : His distinctive sound drew inspiration from artists like Van Morrison and 1960s R&B, leading it to be characterized as "white soul crossed with pub rock." "Parker's sound drew inspiration from the likes of Van Morrison and 60s R&B."
**Disputes with Mercury Records** : Parker grew convinced that his US label, Mercury Records, was "botching his career" due to their struggles to promote him effectively, resulting in singles failing to reach the Hot 100 and albums languishing at the bottom of the charts. This frustration led him to fulfill his contract with the contemptuous live album *The Parkerilla* and record the outspoken diss track "Mercury Poisoning." "Parker came to believe Mercury was botching his career."
**Musical Evolution in the 80s** : In the 1980s, Parker would evolve to become "wistful," reinventing the New Wave template and achieving new pop heights. His music would no longer be confined to punk, post punk, or power pop. "Graham turns wistful."
