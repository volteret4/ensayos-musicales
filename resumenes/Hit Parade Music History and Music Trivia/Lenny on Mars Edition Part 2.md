# artist - Lenny Kravitz

## curiosities
**Early Career Emulation and Criticism** : Lenny Kravitz's career in the 90s and 2000s involved emulating rock heroes from earlier eras, which often drew criticism for his stylistic borrowings. This approach was later contrasted with Bruno Mars's reception in the digital age, where eclectic genre-crossing was more readily accepted by millennial audiences. "We walked through Kravitz's career in the 90s and Outs, the rock heroes of Yesteryear he emulated, and the criticism he received for his stylistic borrowings."
**Romeo Blue Phase** : Similar to Bruno Mars, Lenny Kravitz played in cover bands during an early phase of his career, which was referred to as his "Romeo blue face." This period highlights his formative experiences performing and developing his musical style. "Like Lenny Kravitz in his Romeo blue face, he had played in cover bands."
**Band Member Replacement** : At some point in his career, a former member of the Puerto Rican boy band Menudo replaced Lenny Kravitz in one of his bands. This detail offers a glimpse into the interconnectedness of various musical acts across different genres and eras. "A former Menudo member replaced Lenny Kravitz in one of his bands."
**Artistic Control in Contract Negotiations** : Lenny Kravitz was known for holding out for a contract that granted him significant artistic control over his music. This steadfast approach to creative independence served as a precedent and was later mirrored by Bruno Mars in his own career development, allowing him to explore his diverse musical interests. "like Lenny Kravitz, who held out for a contract with artistic control, Mars had earned the right to explore the full range of his musical interests."
**Precursor to Bruno Mars' Jukebox Approach** : Lenny Kravitz's method of "rebooting sounds and styles of other artists" from previous generations, as exemplified by hits like "It Ain't Over 'til It's Over" or "Are You Gonna Go My Way," established a template for Bruno Mars's similar "jukebox" approach to pop music a generation later. This demonstrates a shared strategy of drawing from diverse musical influences. "Bruno Mars queued up a different pop song template with the same knowing wink Lenny Kravitz brought a generation earlier to such hits as it ain't over till it's over."
**"Just to Find My Love" Background Vocals** : Early in his career, Lenny Kravitz contributed "barely noticeable background vocals" to a hit song by Madonna. This collaboration represents an initial step in leveraging his songwriting and musical talents, before he fully emerged as a prominent frontline pop star. "On just to find my love, Kravitz had sung some barely noticeable background vocals."
**Criticism in Baby Boomer Rock Era** : Unlike Bruno Mars, who rose to fame in the digital era, Lenny Kravitz "endured criticism from the start of his career" for emulating his 60s and 70s heroes. This negative reception stemmed from an era dominated by baby boomer-rock critics who were less receptive to eclectic, genre-crossing music compared to millennial audiences. "Unlike Lenny Kravitz, who endured criticism from the start of his career for emulating his 60s and 70s heroes in an era of baby boomer-rock critics, Mars came up in the digital era of pop, when eclectic genre-crossing music was considered normal by a millennial audience."
**Shared Performance Instinct with Bruno Mars** : Lenny Kravitz, like Bruno Mars, is characterized as a "born performer" who possesses an inherent ability to engage and "rock a crowd." This shared trait highlights their natural stage presence and capacity to connect with audiences. "it doesn't hurt that both men are born performers who know how to rock a crowd."

## albums
**Do-Wops & Hooligans (2010)** : Bruno Mars's official debut album, released days after its lead-off single "Just the Way You Are" topped the Hot 100 in October 2010. It entered the album chart at number three and eventually became a perennial seller, spending more than 600 weeks on the Billboard 200 and achieving Septuple Platinum certification from the RIAA. "Do Wops and Hooligans arrived just days after the single hit number one, and it entered the album chart at number three."
**Unorthodox Jukebox (2012)** : Bruno Mars's sophomore album, released in late 2012, which quickly became his first number one album on the Billboard 200. Critics noted its highly descriptive title, reflecting Mars's eclectic approach of emulating sounds and styles from various artists, ranging from The Police to Adele, creating a diverse "jukebox" of pop song templates. "That, by the way, was the name of Bruno Mars' second album, and rarely has an album title been so descriptive."
**24K Magic (2016)** : Bruno Mars's third album, released a year after "Uptown Funk" completed its chart run, deepened his exploration of 80s electro-R&B tropes and featured boastful lyrics reminiscent of West Coast hip-hop. Despite its lead single charting slightly lower than previous debuts, the album proved to be a mainstay, lodged in the top 10 for months, and ultimately won Album of the Year at the 2018 Grammys, seen by some as a comprehensive homage to the 80s black boy band New Edition. "One year after Uptown Funk completed its chart run, when Mars dropped his third album, 24-karat magic, this controversy would be kicked up a notch."
**An Evening with Silk Sonic (2021)** : The critically acclaimed debut album by Silk Sonic, the duo formed by Bruno Mars and Anderson .Paak, presented Mars's continued mining of classic black music tropes within a "musical costume" where they "play acted the roles of 70s style soulman." The album, bathed in the influences of Barry White, The Delfonics, Teddy Pendergrass, and Aretha Franklin, earned Mars some of the best reviews of his career, praised for its "cheeky but also sincere" music and the duo's undeniable chemistry, spawning multiple hits on the Hot 100 and R&B/Hip-Hop charts. "The duo's debut album, an evening with Silk Sonic, earned Mars some of the best reviews of his career."

## songs
**Lost (Unknown Year)** : A song Bruno Mars originally intended to record for himself, co-written with Philip Lawrence, during a period where Mars had spent over four years in LA with little to show for his efforts. The duo, contemplating moving back home, were persuaded to sell it for $20,000 to the Puerto Rican boy band Menudo, which became a Top 40 hit on Billboard's pop radio chart and subsequently established Mars and Lawrence as in-demand hit songwriters. "Lost was a song Bruno Mars originally intended to record for himself."
**Get Sexy (2009)** : A song co-written by Bruno Mars and Philip Lawrence for the British girl group Sugar Babes, which achieved a peak position of number two in the UK in 2009. The track was notably an "unabashed interpolation" of Right Said Fred's 1991 hit, "I'm Too Sexy," leveraging familiarity for its commercial success. "For the British girl group Sugar Babes, Mars and Lawrence co-wrote Get Sexy, which hit number two in the UK in 2009."
**Right Round (2009)** : A smash hit for rapper Flo Rida, topping the Hot 100 in February 2009, which originated from Bruno Mars and Philip Lawrence's idea to adapt Dead or Alive's 1985 hit "You Spin Me Round (Like a Record)" into a new club pop track. They brought this concept to hit-making producer Dr. Luke, and The Smeezingtons (Mars, Lawrence, and Ari Levine) received writing credits, leading to a significant increase in demand for their songwriting services. "His dead or alive reboot was called Right Round. And it topped the Hot 100 in February 2009."
**Billionaire (2010)** : Travie McCoy's solo single, co-written by The Smeezingtons, prominently featuring Bruno Mars singing the chorus hook. The song peaked at number four in 2010, serving as a key example of Mars's strategy to position himself as a full-blown hook singer for hit-making rappers, thereby boosting his own career as a frontline pop star. "For example, for rapper and Jim Class Heroes frontman Traville McCoy, the Smezing Tens not only wrote his solo single, Billionaire, Bruno sang the chorus hook."
**Nothing On You (2010)** : An R&B ballad originally written by Bruno Mars and The Smeezingtons for rapper Lupe Fiasco, but Atlantic Records swiftly secured the song to give it to their rising rapper B.o.B, with Mars featured as the hook singer. Bruno Mars's "sweeping crooning on the chorus" was the song's most memorable part, driving it to number one on the Hot 100 in May 2010, ultimately showcasing Mars more prominently than B.o.B and prompting Atlantic to sign Mars to his own artist contract. "The love-learn ballad was called Nothing On You."
**Rocketeer (Unknown Year)** : A number nine hit for the EDM Club Collective Far East Movement, written by The Smeezingtons while Bruno Mars and his songwriting partners were simultaneously working on material for Mars's official debut album. "including the EDM Club Collective Far East Movement, for whom the Smeezington's wrote the number nine hit, Rocketeer."
**Forget You (2010)** : A "comically foul-mouthed retro Motown-style pastiche" penned by The Smeezingtons for former Goodie Mob and Gnarls Barkley singer CeeLo Green, famously known on radio as "Forget You." This song became a number two Hot 100 smash, further showcasing the impressive stylistic breadth and versatility of The Smeezingtons as songwriters. "Silo Green, for whom the Smeezington's penned the comically foul mouthed retro Motown-style pastiche, and fuck you, better known on the radio as Forget You."
**Just the Way You Are (2010) - Lead Single** : The lead-off single from Bruno Mars's debut album "Doo-Wops & Hooligans," which was subtitled "Amazing" in certain countries to prevent confusion with the older Billy Joel hit. Mars and his co-writers transformed the gentle sentiment of self-acceptance into a soaring anthem, with Mars describing his intent for a direct, heartfelt message akin to Joe Cocker's "You Are So Beautiful" and Eric Clapton's "Wonderful Tonight." It reached atop the Hot 100 in October 2010, marking his first number one hit under his own name. "Just the way you are was the lead-off single to Bruno Mars debut album Do Wops and Hooligans."
**Grenade (2010)** : A "heart-rending mid-tempo ballad" from "Doo-Wops & Hooligans" detailing the extreme lengths a desperate suitor would go to win affection, which became Bruno Mars's second consecutive number one song. Critics highlighted an "uncanny resemblance" in its tension and pleading vocals to his childhood hero Michael Jackson's 1988 number one hit "Dirty Diana." "Grenade" lightly embraced retro 80s Jackson-style vibes while rebuilding the sound with a 2010s digital feel, holding the top of the Hot 100 for four weeks in January and February of 2011. "Grenade. A heart-rending mid-tempo ballad about the lengths a desperate suitor would go to win a lover's affection became Bruno Mars's second straight number one song."
**The Lazy Song (2011)** : A "kitschy ode to sloth" that successfully reached the top five on the charts, demonstrating Mars's versatile ability to perform "Joayan surf pop" similar to the reggae-inflected style popularized by Jason Mraz at the time. "including Rapper's Snoop Dogg and Wiz Khalifa, who teamed with Bruno on the 2011 number seven hit, Young, Wild, and Free."
**Never Close Our Eyes (Unknown Year)** : A song written by The Smeezingtons that American Idol alumnus Adam Lambert took to number six on Billboard's Dance Club Play chart. This track further solidified The Smeezingtons' reputation for crafting hits for a diverse range of artists. "Adam Lambert, who took the Smeezington's Never Close Our Eyes to number six on Billboard's Dance Club Play chart."
**It Will Rain (2011)** : A pop soul ballad composed by Bruno Mars and his mates for the "Twilight" movie franchise, specifically the "Breaking Dawn Part One" soundtrack, which reached number three in December 2011. Sharp-eared critics observed that the verses of "It Will Rain" shared the same chord progression as The Rolling Stones' classic "Wild Horses," though the similarity was subtle. "Their composition for the Breaking Dawn Part One soundtrack, The Pop Soul Ballad It Will Rain reached number three in December 2011."
**Locked Out of Heaven (2012)** : The lead single from Bruno Mars's second album, "Unorthodox Jukebox," which heavily leaned into the influence of The Police and Sting, featuring lustful lyrics with a pre-chorus refrain "Your Sex Takes Me to Paradise" and a reggae-tinged, economical post-punk groove. It quickly rose to the top of the Hot 100 in December 2012 and held the position through January 2013 for six weeks, leading to an iconic Grammy performance where Sting himself joined Mars on stage. "Locked out of heaven was as lustful as Roxanne."
**When I Was Your Man (2013)** : Bruno Mars's poignant reflection on a love gone wrong, which made history as the second ever piano and vocal-only number one hit when it topped the Hot 100 in April 2013, following Adele's "Someone Like You." This song played a significant role in pushing the "Unorthodox Jukebox" album to the top of the Billboard 200, and its lyrics gained renewed familiarity in 2023 when Miley Cyrus's "Flowers" was released as an answer record, a "clapback" to her ex-husband Liam Hemsworth who had dedicated Mars's song to her. "When I was your man, Mars' own reflection on a love gone wrong became the second ever piano and vocal only number one hit when it topped the hot 100 in April 2013."
**Treasure (2013)** : A summer 2013 single for Bruno Mars, an electro disco pop and R&B track that became a number five hit. The song adapted the stylings of Michael Jackson from his Quincy Jones-era "Rock With You" sound, demonstrating Mars's ability to draw from vintage influences to create contemporary hits. "Into the electro disco pop and B track Treasure, a number five hit for Bruno Mars."
**Gorilla (2013)** : A chest-thumping pop rocker that reached number 22 on the charts, in which Bruno Mars adopted the "anthemic sound of late 80s hair metal, à la Def Leppard." This track showcased his continued experimentation with different retro styles. "And turned that into his chest thumping pop rocker Gorilla, a number 22 hit."
**Uptown Funk (2014)** : A mega-blockbuster hit officially credited to Mark Ronson featuring Bruno Mars, who was acknowledged as the song's "animating force." The song began with Mars's improvisation in an LA studio, involved extensive sessions across studios in six cities (Memphis, New York, London, Vancouver, etc.), featured three different horn sections (including Mars's band The Hooligans and Mark Ronson collaborators The Dap-Kings), required over 100 takes, and famously had Philip Lawrence singing the bass line due to recording difficulties. Released in November 2014, critics lauded its recreation of turn-of-the-80s electro-funk and R&B, noting numerous references to artists like James Brown, The Sequence, Kool & The Gang, George Kranz, Rick James, George Clinton, Zapp (featuring Roger Troutman), and The Gap Band, who successfully sued for a belated songwriting credit due to similarities with their 1979 hit "Oops Up Side Your Head." It spent 14 weeks at number one on the Hot 100 in early 2015, becoming the second longest run in chart history at the time, and earned the Grammy for Record of the Year. "Uptown Funk was credited officially to Mark Ronson, the British born American-raised producer who had already won Grammys for Helming Hits by Amy Winehouse and Adele."
**24K Magic (2016)** : The title track and lead single from Bruno Mars's third album, explicitly conceived by Mars as a sequel to "Uptown Funk," burrowing deeper into 80s electro-R&B tropes and featuring boastful lyrics that echoed West Coast hip-hop. Although it peaked at number four on the Hot 100, a slightly smaller debut than his previous number one lead singles, it set the tone for the album's success. "By his own admission, Bruno Mars conceived 24-karat magic, the song, as a sequel to Uptown Funk, burrowing deeper into 80s electro-r&b tropes and amping up the boastful approach with lyrics that echoed West Coast hip-hop."
**That's What I Like (2017)** : The second single from the "24K Magic" album, which became a number one hit in May 2017. The song was noted for echoing the distinctive New Jack Swing sound popularized by New Edition vocalist turned soloist Bobby Brown, further cementing the album's homage to 80s R&B. "the second single, that's what I like, a number 1 hit in May 2017."
**Finesse (Unknown Year)** : A number three hit from the "24K Magic" album, whose remix famously featured rapping from Cardi B. In this track, Bruno Mars emulated the "slamming hip-hop club sound" characteristic of New Edition spin-off group Bell Biv DeVoe, showcasing his versatility in channeling different 80s R&B styles. "On the number 3 hit, Phinesse, whose remix featured rapping from Cardi B. Mars emulated the slamming hip-hop club sound of new addition spin-off group Belle Biv de Vaux."
**Versace on the Floor (Unknown Year)** : A ballad from the "24K Magic" album that reached number 33 on the charts. "And on the ballad Versace on the floor, a number 33 hit, Mars channel the silky sound of new addition's Hay Day."
**Blow (2019)** : A single released in 2019 where Bruno Mars teamed up with pop singer-songwriter Ed Sheeran and country star Chris Stapleton. "In 2019, Mars teamed with pop singer-songwriter Ed Sheeran and country star Chris Stapleton on Blow, a single that got Mars on Billboard's Rock charts for the first time."
**Leave the Door Open (2021)** : The first single from Silk Sonic's debut album, "An Evening with Silk Sonic," a quiet storm ballad whose title was a "knowing wink" to Teddy Pendergrass's classic slow jam "Close the Door." "The first single, a quiet storm ballad called Leave the Door Open."
**Smoking Out the Window (Unknown Year)** : A hit song from Silk Sonic's debut album, "An Evening with Silk Sonic," which achieved notable chart success, reaching number five on the pop charts and number two on the R&B/Hip-Hop charts. "including the number five pop, number two R&B hit, Smoking Out the Window."

## curiosities
**Early LA Struggles and Cover Bands** : Bruno Mars spent over four years in Los Angeles with limited success, during which he played in cover bands, including one named Sex Panther. He initially rejected a label's offer to sign him as a Latin act and sing in Spanish, preferring to identify himself by his birth name, Peter Jean Hernandez, and holding out for the right kind of deal. "He had already been in LA for more than four years, with little to show for it."
**Motown Records Deal** : Bruno Mars signed a deal with Motown Records for a year, but this period ultimately did not result in any recorded material being released. The most significant outcome of this brief association was an introduction to Philip Lawrence, which led to their prolific and successful songwriting partnership. "Bruno even signed with Motown records for a year, but no recordings resulted from that deal."
**The Smeezingtons Formation** : Following his introduction to Philip Lawrence at Motown, Bruno Mars began writing songs with him. They later added Ari Levine to their team, forming the songwriting collective "The Smeezingtons" by 2009. This trio quickly gained significant industry attention and hit-making success under their distinctive, "goofy name." "Together, the trio of Bruno Mars, Philip Lawrence and Ari Levine began marketing their songwriting collective under the goofy name The Smezing Tens."
**Leveraging Songwriting for Solo Career** : Similar to Lenny Kravitz's early career strategy, Bruno Mars adeptly leveraged his songwriting success for other artists to significantly boost his own career as a frontline pop star. This involved strategically positioning himself as a "full-blown hook singer" for hit-making rappers, a role that was not as readily available to artists like Lenny Kravitz in the early 90s. "Not unlike Lenny Kravitz's Madonna hit early in his career, Bruno Mars was going to leverage his songwriting to boost his own career as a frontline pop star."
**"Unorthodox Jukebox" Criticism** : Critics often described Mars's albums, including "Unorthodox Jukebox," as "Trend Hopping" and "Show Offie," with one critic specifically calling "Unorthodox Jukebox" "a reasonably-listenable exercise in genre fetishization." This critique reflected a growing apprehension towards his "jukebox approach" to songwriting and stylistic borrowing. "One critic called Unorthodox Jukebox, quote, a reasonably-listenable exercise in genre fetishization, unquote."
**Cultural Appropriation Controversy** : Following his 2018 Grammy win for "24K Magic," Bruno Mars faced significant accusations of cultural appropriation. Cultural critic Seren Sensei argued that Mars, who is multiracial but not Black, exploited his racial ambiguity to cross genres and re-create existing Black music "word for word" without genuine originality or improvement, characterizing him as a "karaoke singer." Black artists such as Stevie Wonder and Charlie Wilson, however, came to Mars's defense, emphasizing the universal nature of music and Mars's authentic inspiration. "One month later, on The Grapevine, an online video series covering African American issues, participants debated whether Bruno Mars was a cultural appropriator in the wake of his Grammy win."
**Grammy Acceptance Speech - 24K Magic** : At the 2018 Grammys, while accepting the Album of the Year award for "24K Magic," Bruno Mars graciously acknowledged and praised his fellow nominees in the category, including "Lord Kung Fu Kenny" (Kendrick Lamar), Jay-Z, and Childish Gambino. He stated that their "top shelf artistry and music" pushed him to excel in his own work. "First off, to the other nominees in this category, Lord Kung Fu Kenny, Jay Z, Gambino. You guys are the reason why I'm in the studio pulling my hair out, man, because I know you guys are going to only come with the top shelf artistry and music, and thank you guys for blessing the world with your music."
**Cited Influences - James Brown, Prince, Michael Jackson** : In response to criticisms, Bruno Mars explicitly stated in a radio interview that his musical presence is entirely attributable to the foundational influences of James Brown, Prince, and Michael Jackson. He emphasized that his music originates from "love," challenging critics to recognize this core motivation. "Years later, Bruno Mars himself told a radio interviewer, quote, the only reason why I'm here is because of James Brown, is because of Prince, Michael. That's it."
**Silk Sonic as Musical Costume** : In 2021, Bruno Mars and West Coast rapper Anderson .Paak announced their collaboration as the duo Silk Sonic, releasing the album "An Evening with Silk Sonic." This project was described as a "musical costume" where Mars and .Paak "play acted the roles of 70s style soulman," continuing Mars's practice of mining classic Black music tropes within a distinct persona. "Silk Sonic was, in essence, a musical costume in which Mars and Pock play acted the roles of 70s style soulman, with songs bathed in the tropes of Barry White, the Delphanix, Teddy Pendergrass and Aritha Franklin."
**Born Performer** : Bruno Mars, much like Lenny Kravitz, is described as a "born performer" who possesses an innate ability to captivate audiences and "rock a crowd." This characteristic highlights his natural stage presence and charismatic appeal. "it doesn't hurt that both men are born performers who know how to rock a crowd."
**Showman Upbringing** : Bruno Mars was "raised to be a showman," a trait evident from his early years performing as "Little Elvis" and his subsequent experiences in cover bands in Honolulu and Los Angeles. This upbringing instilled in him a "killer instinct for what audiences wanted" and a willingness to borrow sounds, genres, hooks, or even song titles to achieve popular appeal. "Mars had been raised to be a showman, from his years as little Elvis, to his covers performances in Honolulu and LA."
**Uptown Funk Bassline Recording** : The recording of the bass line for "Uptown Funk" proved to be exceptionally challenging, requiring extensive effort. Ultimately, the final recorded version features Philip Lawrence performing the bass line vocally, singing it with his mouth, due to the difficulty in capturing the desired sound. "The bass line was so hard to get right, the final recording is just Philip Lawrence singing the bass line with his mouth."
**"Uptown Funk" Grammy Acceptance Speech** : During their Grammy acceptance speech for Record of the Year for "Uptown Funk" in 2016, Mark Ronson and Bruno Mars explicitly credited and thanked numerous funk and soul pioneers, including James Brown, George Clinton, Jamal Lewis, Prince, The Meters, and Earth, Wind & Fire. This gesture acknowledged their profound influence on the song and the broader funk genre. "So I want to thank James Brown, George Clinton, Jamal Lewis, Prince The Metas, Earth Wind and Fire of course, and I'm sure these guys want to say something really quick."
