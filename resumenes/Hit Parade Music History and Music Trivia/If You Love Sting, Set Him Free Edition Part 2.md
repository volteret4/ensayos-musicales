## albums
**The Dream of the Blue Turtles (1985) - Solo Debut Album** : Released in 1985, this album marked a significant departure from The Police's sound for many fans, but for Sting, it was a return to his jazz roots in Newcastle. He assembled a band of seasoned jazz players for the project. The album rose to number two on the album chart, solidifying Sting as a solo act, and eventually went double platinum and then triple platinum. Its title was inspired by a dream Sting had about blue turtles. "To Sting himself, it was a return to his roots as a jazz player in Newcastle."
**Nothing Like the Sun (1987) - Sophisticated Second Album** : Released in the fall of 1987, this album was named after the opening line of a Shakespeare sonnet. It was designed to fit the length of a single CD (an hour-long), while also being released as a double vinyl album, targeting the upscale 80s-yuppie compact disc market. Digitally recorded and sonically pristine, it was positioned to define Sting as "pop's Prince of Sophistication, a world-beat icon and rocks social conscience." The album was rated one of the 100 best albums of the 1980s by Rolling Stone, despite some critical dissent. "Musically, nothing like the sun positioned Sting as pop's Prince of Sophistication, a world-beat icon and rocks social conscience."
**The Soul Cages (1991) - Transitional and Personal Album** : Released in 1991, this album marked a transitional period for Sting, representing "the last gasp of 80s sting and his jazzy moody melancholy rock." The entire album is deeply personal, abounding with references to death and Sting's childhood, particularly the shipyards he grew up around, following the passing of his father since his previous album. "Indeed, the entire Soul Cages album abounded with references to death and Sting's childhood, including the shipyards he grew up around."
**Ten Summoners Tales (1993) - Romantic and Accessible Album** : Released in the winter of 1993 as Sting's fourth solo album, its title is a scholarly pun on "The Summoner" character from Chaucer's "The Canterbury Tales" and Sting's real last name, Sumner. Despite its academic title, the album was Sting's most "gushy, most VH1 friendly" to date, filled with unabashed love songs. Sting intended to write sunnier pop music after the darkness of "The Soul Cages." It became one of his highest-certified LPs, reaching triple platinum. "Sting admitted he was trying to write sunnier pop music after the darkness of the Soul Cages."
**Mercury Falling (1996) - Genre Experimentation Album** : This album, released in 1996, featured Sting's first major attempt at writing country music with the track "I'm So Happy I Can't Stop Crying." "For example, on his 1996 album Mercury Falling, Sting included the track, I'm so happy I can't stop crying. His first major attempt at writing country music."
**Brand New Day (1999) - World-Beat Collaboration Album** : Released in 1999, this album showcased Sting's continued genre dabbling, notably including a collaboration with Algerian Rai singer Cheb Mami on the song "Desert Rose." The album entered the top 10 for the first time after "Desert Rose" gained popularity and eventually went double platinum. "On his 1999 album Brand New Day, Sting included a collaboration with Algerian singer Cheb Mami, a vocalist of the Arabic folk music known as Rai."
**Symphonicities (2010) - Orchestral Reworking Album** : Released in 2010, this album featured Sting rearranging his Police hits and solo songs for a symphony, performed with the Royal Philharmonic Concert Orchestra. "whether that's rearranging his police hits for a symphony, as on his 2010 album, Symphonicities, with the Royal Philharmonic Concert Orchestra."
## songs
**Long, Long Way to Go (1985) - Guest Vocal for Phil Collins** : Sting provided counterpoint vocals on this haunting ballad from Phil Collins' 1985 blockbuster LP "No Jacket Required." "For Phil Collins, who was recording his soon-to-be 1985 blockbuster LP No Jacket Required, Sting provided counterpoint vocals on Phil's haunting ballad long, long way to go."
**Do They Know It's Christmas (1984) - Band Aid Charity Single** : Sting was given a showcase slot on the 1984 All-Star British charity single by Band Aid, harmonizing with Duran Duran's Simon LeBon. Band Aid organizer Bob Geldof humorously made Sting sing the line "The Bitter Sting of Tears." The song aimed to provide relief for starvation in Ethiopia. "Sting was given a showcase slot, harmonizing with Durandoran's Simon LeBon."
**The Promise (Arcadia Track) (1985) - Guest Vocal with Duran Duran Side Project** : A few months after harmonizing on "Do They Know It's Christmas," Simon LeBon invited Sting to add vocals to this track by Duran Duran's side project, Arcadia. "That's Sting chiming in on Arcadia's The Promise."
**Money for Nothing (1985) - Dire Straits Collaboration** : Sting contributed guest harmony vocals to this number one smash by Dire Straits, led by Mark Knopfler, on their fifth studio album "Brothers in Arms." Knopfler wrote the song after observing workmen making snide remarks about musicians while watching MTV. Sting, vacationing on Montserrat where Dire Straits were recording, was invited to sing on the track. His additional vocal refrain, a riff on the "I want my MTV" slogan sung to the tune of The Police's "Don't Stand So Close To Me," earned him a co-songwriting and publishing credit, generating millions. "Seriously, Sting probably could have retired off of his contribution to this number one smash alone."
**If You Love Somebody (Set Them Free) (1985) - Solo Debut Single** : This song was the first single from Sting's solo debut album "The Dream of the Blue Turtles." It featured Sting playing with all Black performers in the video, including R&B backing vocalists Dolet McDonald and Janice Pendarvus. Intended as an antidote to "Every Breath You Take," it focused on letting go rather than control. It rose to number three on the Hot 100, number one on the album Rock Chart, and number 17 on the R&B chart. "If you love somebody, set them free, was Jazzy R&B."
**Fortress Around Your Heart (1985) - Second Single** : The second single from "The Dream of the Blue Turtles," this airy track reached number eight on the Hot 100. Critics later categorized its style as "Sophistopop," drawing comparisons to artists like Roxy Music, The Style Council, or Sade. "A number eight hit that leaned toward what critics would later call Sephistopop, akin to the Roxy Music, the Style Council, or Shade."
**Love Is the Seventh Wave (1985) - Reggae-Infused Single** : The third single from "The Dream of the Blue Turtles," this song saw Sting returning to a reggae sound similar to his work with The Police, but with a more overtly tropical flavor. Described as sunny and bright, almost a children's nursery rhyme, it reached number 17 on the Hot 100. Cheekily, Sting repeated lyrics from "Every Breath You Take" just before the song's fade-out. "Other than blending reggae with post punk, Love is the seventh wave, was sunny and bright, essentially a children's nursery rhyme."
**Russians (1985) - Anti-Cold War Dirge** : The fourth and most ambitious single from "The Dream of the Blue Turtles," this cold war dirge decried the specter of nuclear war between the United States and the Soviet Union. Its melody interpolated the Romance theme from Sergei Prokofiev's "Lieutenant Kyzhya" suite. The lyrics referenced Nikita Khrushchev, Ronald Reagan, and J. Robert Oppenheimer, inspiring Christopher Nolan's film "Oppenheimer" decades later. The song, centered on the line "if the Russians love their children too," reached number 16 on the Hot 100 in early 1986. "Sting's anti-nuclear lament centered around the rhetorical line if the Russians love their children too."
**We'll Be Together (1987) - Pop Funk Lead Single** : This pop funk jam was the accessible lead single from "Nothing Like the Sun," released at the urging of Sting's record label. It originated as a jingle Sting wrote for a Japanese beer commercial. The song reached number seven on the pop chart and cracked the top 40 on the R&B chart. "Sting wrote for a Japanese beer commercial."
**Be Still My Beating Heart (1987) - Ethereal Second Single** : The second single from "Nothing Like the Sun," this track was closer to the album's overall ethereal, high-toned Sophistapop style. It performed well on the charts, reaching number 15 on the Hot 100 and number two on the album Rock Chart. "The album's second single, Be Still My Beating Heart, was closer to the ethereal, high-toned Sophistatop on the rest of the CD."
**Englishman in New York (1987) - Cult Classic and LGBTQ+ Anthem** : This jazzy, jaunty third single from "Nothing Like the Sun" reached number 84 on the Hot 100 at the time but has since become a Spotify standard from Sting's 80s period, accumulating nearly half a billion streams. Sting wrote it as a loving profile of gender non-conforming gay icon Quentin Crisp, who also appears in the music video. Its centerpiece lyric is "Be Yourself, no matter what they say." "Sting wrote Englishman in New York as a loving profile of gender non-conforming gay icon Quentin Crisp, who appears in the music video."
**History Will Teach Us Nothing (1987) - Socially Conscious Track** : A song included on the "Nothing Like the Sun" album, reflecting Sting's incorporation of progressive politics and socially conscious lyrics into his work during the late 80s. "He slipped socially conscious lyrics into songs across the album, including History Will Teach Us Nothing..."
**Fragile (1987) - Anti-War Anthem** : A gentle anti-war anthem from the "Nothing Like the Sun" album, showcasing Sting's socially conscious lyrics. This song was later sampled by Tupac for his posthumous single "When Thugs Cry." "the gentle anti-war anthem Fragil..."
**They Dance Alone (Cueca Solo) (1987) - Protest Song** : A protest song from the "Nothing Like the Sun" album, specifically against Chilean dictator Augusto Pinochet, demonstrating Sting's progressive political stance in his music. "They Dance Alone, Guéque La Solo, a protest song against Chilean dictator Augusto Pinochet."
**All This Time (1991) - Personal Pop Song** : The first single from "The Soul Cages," this was a jaunty pop song and one of Sting's friendliest hits, featuring a lead melody line played by a mandolin. It reached number five on the Hot 100 in March 1991 and number one on the modern rock chart. A close listen to the lyrics reveals it is about Sting's deceased father, describing a funeral and imagining a burial at sea, culminating in the question, "Father, if Jesus exists, then how come he never lived here?" "However, a close listen to the lyrics revealed that the song was actually about Sting's deceased father who had passed since his previous album."
**The Soul Cages (1991) - Brooding Title Track** : The title track from Sting's 1991 album, this brooding rocker features guitar work from his long-time backing player Dominic Miller. The song tells a tale of a "child locked in the fisherman's yard" and asks, "where is the child with his father's eyes?", tying into the album's themes of death and childhood memories of shipyards. It reached number seven on Billboard's album rock chart in April 1991. "The album's title track, for example, a brooding rocker with guitar from Sting's long-time backing player Dominic Miller, told a tale of a quote, child locked in the fisherman's yard..."
**It's Probably Me (1992) - Movie Soundtrack Collaboration** : Collaborating with Eric Clapton, Sting contributed this "No Frills guitar and sax rocker" to the soundtrack of the 1992 summer blockbuster "Lethal Weapon 3." It became a top 20 album rock hit and was noted as the closest Sting had come to a basic blues sound. "Key teamed up with Eric Clapton for the No Frills guitar and sax rocker it's probably me, a top 20 album rock hit."
**If I Ever Lose My Faith in You (1993) - Adult Contemporary Hit** : The first single from "Ten Summoners Tales," this love song reached number 17 on the Hot 100 in May 1993. It was characterized as "adult contemporary radio pop in Renaissance Fair clothing" and hit the top 10 on Billboard's Adult Contemporary Chart. "Basically this was adult contemporary radio pop in Renaissance Fair clothing."
**Fields of Gold (1993) - Enduring Romantic Ballad** : This song from "Ten Summoners Tales" paired courtly lyrics about undying love amidst rolling hills of barley with some of Sting's smoothest music. While it peaked at number 23 on the Hot 100 in summer 1993, it spent a month at number two on Billboard's Adult Contemporary Chart. As of 2025, it is Sting's most played solo single ever on both radio and Spotify. "Since of 2025, Fields of Gold is Sting's most played solo single ever, both on the radio and Spotify."
**All for Love (1993) - Movie Hit Collaboration** : This song from the 1993 Disney live-action movie "The Three Musketeers" became Sting's only post-Police number one hit, reaching the top of the Hot 100 in January 1994. It was a collaboration with Bryan Adams and Rod Stewart, orchestrated by Adams to repeat the success of "Everything I Do (I Do It for You)." Critics, like Stereo Gum's Tom Bryan, called it "pure filler" that "begs to be forgotten." "Sting was one of three singers, all with husky voices and an ear for corn. All for love."
**I'm So Happy I Can't Stop Crying (1996) - Country Music Attempt** : Included on Sting's 1996 album "Mercury Falling," this track was his first major attempt at writing country music. Sting's original version was a minor hit, peaking at number 94 on the Hot 100. A year later, Toby Keith covered it, inviting Sting to join him for a version recorded in Nashville with country instrumentalists, which became an actual country hit, peaking at number two on Billboard's Hot Country Songs chart. "His first major attempt at writing country music."
**Roxanne 97, Puff Daddy Remix (1997) - Hip Hop Crossover** : Sting invited Sean Combs (Puff Daddy) to remix The Police's first hit, "Roxanne," for a compilation CD. The remix featured DJ scratching, samples from UTFO's "Roxanne Roxanne," and rap bars from Praz of The Fugees, with Sting also making a cameo in the music video. Credited to Sting and The Police, it reached number 20 on the R&B chart (Sting's first since "We'll Be Together") and number five on the Hot Rap Singles chart. "The quirky result featured DJ scratching samples from UTF-Os 80s rap classic Roxanne Roxanne and rap bars from Praz of the Fuji's."
**Desert Rose (1999) - World-Beat Sleeper Hit** : Featured on his 1999 album "Brand New Day," this song was a collaboration with Algerian singer Cheb Mami, showcasing Mami's vocals in Arabic alongside Sting's mix of English and Arabic lyrics, set against electronic production. Co-written by Sting to highlight Mami's voice, it took months to catch on. Car maker Jaguar licensed it for commercials, which spurred American radio play. It debuted on the Hot 100 in May 2000, becoming a sleeper hit, spending 26 weeks on the chart and rising to number 17. "Sting had grown enamored of Mami's voice and co-wrote Desert Rose specifically to showcase Mami's vocals alongside Sting's own."
**Shape of My Heart (1993) - Sample Source** : A track from Sting's 1993 album "Ten Summoners Tales," this song was not a big hit upon its release, with no chart appearances in America and peaking below the top 50 in the UK. However, it has had a remarkable afterlife as an enduring source of samples for artists like Nas, Sugar Babes, Craig David, and most notably, Juice Wrld's "Lucid Dreams" in 2018. It is the only track on the album where Sting shares songwriting credit, as his long-time guitarist Dominic Miller created the "entrancing guitar riff" to which Sting added lyrics. "Shape of my heart has had a remarkable afterlife. It was a single back in 1993, but not a big hit, no chart appearances in America, and even in the UK, it peaked below the top 50."
## curiosities
**Solo Career Launch (1984)** : After The Police went on hiatus in 1984, Sting began work on his solo album while also making guest appearances on other superstar records. He was about to embark on a solo career that was "even more wide-ranging and genre-unbound." "After the police went on hiatus in 1984, Sting started work on a solo album while making guest appearances on other superstars records."
**MTV Slogan Co-Writing Credit (1985)** : Sting earned a co-songwriting credit on Dire Straits' "Money for Nothing" not just for his harmony vocals on the chorus, but for an additional vocal refrain he added to the front and back of the track. This refrain was a riff on "I want my MTV," a slogan he and The Police had used in an MTV ad years earlier, sung to the tune of The Police's "Don't Stand So Close To Me" as an inside joke. "He rift on the slogan that he and the police had yelled in that MTV ad a couple of years earlier, I want my MTV."
**Amnesty International Activism (1986)** : In 1986, Sting co-headlined Amnesty International's Conspiracy of Hope Tour, performing alongside socially conscious stars like U2 and Peter Gabriel. For this tour, he briefly reunited The Police for some dates for a good cause. "In 1986, he co-headlined Amnesty International's Conspiracy of Hope Tour alongside such socially conscious stars as U2 and Peter Gabriel."
**Rainforest Foundation Fund (1987)** : In 1987, Sting and his wife, Trudy Styler, co-founded the Rainforest Foundation Fund. The organization's mission is to help protect the Amazon rainforest and defend the rights of its indigenous peoples, a cause Sting remains associated with to this day. "Then, in 1987, Sting and Trudy Styler founded the Rainforest Foundation Fund to help protect the Amazon and defend the rights of its indigenous peoples."
**Influence on Christopher Nolan's Oppenheimer (Decades Later)** : Filmmaker Christopher Nolan stated that he was partially inspired to make his Oscar-winning film "Oppenheimer" by the lyrics to Sting's 1985 song "Russians." "Decades later, filmmaker Christopher Nolan said he was partially inspired to make his Oscar-winning film Oppenheimer by the lyrics to Sting's Russians."
**"Russians" Lyric Controversy (1986)** : The core lyric of Sting's anti-nuclear lament "Russians," "if the Russians love their children too," was pilloried by some critics for its perceived glibness. Sting countered in interviews that he understood Russians loved their children, and the lyric was intended to be hyperbolic to emphasize the absurdity of nuclear war. "A lyric that some critics pilloried for its glibness in interviews Sting countered that of course he knew the Russians loved their children, and the lyric was meant to be hyperbolic to emphasize the absurdity of nuclear war."
**Sting's Audience Shift (1990s)** : After the "Ten Summoners Tales" album in 1993, Sting's audience increasingly shifted to adult contemporary. While college radio, alternative, and even some mainstream rock stations largely stopped playing his music, this "AC Sting" mode proved to be his most popular long-term, with his songs becoming staples on adult contemporary radio. "Sting's audience was always adult. Now it was primarily adult contemporary, with all the unhipped signifiers that indicated."
**Sting Endorses Bruno Mars' "Police" Imitation (2013)** : Despite not receiving payment for Bruno Mars' 2012 hit "Locked Out of Heaven," which was a style imitation of The Police rather than a sample or interpolation, Sting endorsed the song. He even joined Mars on stage at the 2013 Grammy Awards to perform "Locked Out of Heaven" during a medley of reggae-inspired hits. "And as far as anyone knows, Sting wasn't paid a penny for Bruno Mars' hit, but Sting did endorse it."
**Financial Impact of "Shape of My Heart" Samples (Post-1993)** : Although "Shape of My Heart" was not a significant hit when released in 1993, its enduring appeal as a sample source, particularly for Juice Wrld's "Lucid Dreams," has generated millions of dollars. Sting shares the publishing on the song with his long-time guitarist Dominic Miller, who created the core guitar riff. "Sting has quipped in interviews that Lucid Dreams will put his grandkids through college, because it is built out of a sample of shape of my heart, a track from his lushly romantic 1993 album, Ten Summoners Tales."
**Tuscan Villa Concerts (Annual)** : Sting hosts annual intimate acoustic concerts for select fans at his Tuscan Villa, where he performs his biggest hits, including "Every Breath You Take." "Sting are inviting select fans to his Tuscan Villa annually for a series of intimate acoustic concerts."

"Although the band's biggest hit, every breath you take, was not as romantic as it seemed."

## curiosities
**Breakup and Hiatus (1984)** : The Police quietly broke up in 1984 after becoming "Rock's biggest band," going on hiatus before Sting fully embarked on his solo career. "The police have quietly broken up after becoming Rock's biggest band."
**Brief Reunion for Amnesty International (1986)** : The Police briefly reunited for some dates in 1986 to perform as part of Sting's co-headlining role on Amnesty International's Conspiracy of Hope Tour, which was described as a "short-lived reunion for a good cause." "For the tour, he even got the police to briefly reunite for some dates."
**Global Reunion Tour (2007-2008)** : Sting, Andy Summers, and Stewart Copeland put together a full global reunion tour that played over 150 dates around the world through 2007 and 2008. The two-year trek was the third highest-grossing tour in history at the time and remains within the top 20 all-time. Despite some interpersonal dynamics, the three members "mostly got along" during the tour, even with Copeland critiquing their onstage mistakes online. "Then in 2007, Sting, Summers and Stuart Copeland put together a full global reunion tour that played more than 150 dates around the world through 2008."
**Interpersonal Relations & Lawsuit (Ongoing)** : The interpersonal relations within The Police "have waxed and waned over the years." While Sting played guitar on some of Summers' solo projects in the 80s and Summers played bass and sang on some of Sting's projects in the 90s, relations were "frostier 17 years later" (from 2007/2008) due to a lawsuit concerning "Every Breath You Take." "The police's interpersonal relations have waxed and waned over the years."
**Groove-Oriented Style for Sampling** : The Police were described as a "very groove oriented group" due to Sting's prominent baselines and Andy Summers' hypnotic guitar riffs, which made their music a popular source for hip hop samples. "The police in particular were a very grove oriented group thanks to Sting's prominent baselines and Andy Summers' hypnotic guitar riffs."
