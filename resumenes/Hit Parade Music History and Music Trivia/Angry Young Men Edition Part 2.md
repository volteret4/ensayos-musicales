## albums
**Trust (1981) - Fifth Album with Nick Lowe** : This album was the final of a five-LP streak with producer Nick Lowe, and saw Costello and Lowe ranging even more widely in style, incorporating elements alluding to jazz, rockabilly, and country. Despite the effort and stylistic breadth, the album was only a moderate success, charting lower than its recent predecessors in both the US and UK, and, like its precursor "Get Happy," failed to go gold. The album's reception signaled to Costello that it was time to try a different production arrangement and that his musical interests were expanding beyond new wave. "The album signaled two things to Costello. First, that it was probably time to try a different production arrangement...and second, that his interests were veering far beyond new wave."
**Almost Blue (1981/1982) - Country Covers Album** : For this LP, Costello immersed himself fully in country music, recording in Nashville with producer Billy Sherrill, known for his work with legends like George Jones and Tammy Wynette. The album consisted entirely of country cover songs, including the lead single, Costello's rendition of George Jones's hit "A Good Year for the Roses." Despite the artistic departure, the album charted even lower than "Trust," and Costello and The Attractions found the experience of working with Sherrill unenjoyable, though it fulfilled a creative urge and allowed Costello to "hit the reset button" on his career, opening the door for future experiments. "it scratched an itch for Elvis and hit the reset button on his career, freeing him to try other experiments."
**Imperial Bedroom (1982) - Critically Acclaimed Masterpiece** : This was Elvis Costello's seventh studio album and his first LP of original material not produced by Nick Lowe, instead helmed by renowned Beatles engineer Geoff Emerick. The album reflected Emerick's historical influence and Costello's ambition, featuring a sound that ranged from psychedelia to Baroque art pop in a post-Sgt. Pepper style. Upon its release in the summer of 1982, many critics hailed it as Costello's masterpiece, topping the Pazz & Jop critics poll for that year. However, despite critical acclaim, the LP performed modestly on the charts and produced no major hits, indicating a commercial disconnect from the mainstream. "many critics hailed it as Costello's masterpiece."
**Punch the Clock (1983) - Commercial Shift** : On this album, Costello collaborated with 80s hitmakers Clive Langer and Alan Winstanley, known for their work with MTV staples Madness and Dexys Midnight Runners, signaling his readiness to record more commercially viable songs. The album was influenced by Nick Lowe's "When I Write The Book" and featured the single "Every Day I Write The Book," which significantly boosted Costello's album sales, becoming his best in over three years. The album helped Costello become "conversant" with MTV and re-upholstered his image for the synth pop era. "it fueled punch the clock to Elvis Costello's best album sales in more than three years."
**Goodbye Cruel World (1984) - Critically Savaged Follow-Up** : This album saw Costello again working with producers Clive Langer and Alan Winstanley. Despite its efforts to align with the synth-pop sound of the era, the album was severely criticized by critics. Nonetheless, it did manage to get Costello back on MTV with synth-heavy singles, including "I Wanna Be Loved" and the Darryl Hall duet "The Only Flame in Town." "Though the album was savaged by critics, it got Costello back on MTV, with such synth heavy singles as I Wanna Be Loved."
**King of America (1986) - Roots Rock Re-invention** : Produced by T-Bone Burnett, this album was a collection of roots rock and country folk songs, with Burnett notably honing his Americana sound through this collaboration with Costello. The album's sound was described as ahead of its time, resembling music that would emerge in the 1990s, and included a cover of The Animals' classic "Don't Let Me Be Misunderstood," which helped Costello get back on rock radio. The album reached number 38 on the album rock chart. "King of America was a collection of roots rock and country folk gems."
**Blood and Chocolate (1986) - Proto-Grunge Sound** : Later in the same year as "King of America," Costello reformed The Attractions for this album, which was recorded near live in the studio, loud, and with as few takes as possible. The album's bruising and raucous sound was later identified by critics as a kind of proto-grunge, predating the coining of the term itself. The most remembered song from this album, however, was the slower, sinister "I Want You." "the album was later tagged by critics as a kind of proto-grunge before that term had even been coined."
**Spike (1989) - Warner Bros. Debut with Pop Success** : This album was Costello's debut for Warner Bros. and featured the lead single "Veronica," co-written with Paul McCartney. The album marked a significant commercial breakthrough for Costello, producing his first and only US Top 20 pop hit with "Veronica," which climbed to number 19 on the Hot 100 after first topping the Modern Rock Chart. "Released in the winter of 1989 as the lead single from Costello's new Warner Bros. album, Spike, Veronica first rose to number 1 on the Modern Rock Chart for 2 weeks."
**The Juliet Letters (Implied 1993) - Hybrid Classical Pop** : This album was a collaboration with the string players of The Brodsky Quartet, showcasing a hybrid of classical and pop music. It represented Costello's continued refusal to be limited to any single genre or medium, exploring new artistic territories. "He has recorded a hybrid classical pop album with string players, The Brodsky Quartet."
**The Convincer (2001) - Acclaimed Release** : This album featured "She's Got Soul" as its lead single, an acclaimed track that demonstrated Costello's ongoing commitment to releasing quality music and touring, even after reaching financial independence through royalties from the "Bodyguard" soundtrack. "She's Got Soul, the lead single from his acclaimed 2001 album The Convinces."

## songs
**From a Whisper to a Scream (1981)** : A hard-rocking track from the album "Trust," this song marked Elvis Costello's first appearance on Billboard's Rock Tracks chart, peaking at number 46. The track notably featured counterpoint vocals from Squeeze frontman Glenn Tilbrook, highlighting inter-artist collaborations of the period. "The Hard Rocking, from a whisper to a scream, got Costello on billboards rock tracks chart for the first time, where it peaked at number 46."
**Oliver's Army (1981)** : The music video for this song was one of the first 40 videos played on MTV on its inaugural day in 1981. However, Costello's videos at the time were generally described as relatively low-tech affairs compared to the slicker, glossier, and more expensive productions that would soon dominate the channel and influence the sound of new wave. "on the video channel's first day, his Oliver's Army was one of the first 40 videos MTV played."
**Beyond Belief (1982)** : This song was the lead-off track on "Imperial Bedroom," and Elvis Costello had intended for it to be the album's lead single. Its ambitious scope reflected the post-Sgt. Pepper style of the album, ranging from psychedelia to baroque art pop. "If it had been up to Costello, beyond Belief would also have been the lead single for Imperial Bedroom."
**Man Out of Time (1982)** : A stately and lush recording from "Imperial Bedroom," Costello considered this song to be "The Heart of the Record" due to its lyrical depth and sophisticated arrangement. He had hoped for it to be a single, but the record label opted for a lighter pop track instead. "He called it The Heart of the Record."
**You Little Fool (1982)** : Against Elvis Costello's wishes, this lighter pop number was issued as the first single from "Imperial Bedroom" by the record label, which also commissioned a cinematic music video for it. In the video, Costello played a wordless school headmaster, with his Attractions bandmates as supporting players in a British teen drama, but both the video and single failed to capture the unique essence of the album, only reaching number 52 in the UK. "But the record label overruled Costello on both tracks, issuing instead the lighter pop number, you little fool as the first single."
**Every Day I Write The Book (1983)** : Inspired by a ditty from his friend Nick Lowe titled "When I Write The Book," Costello adapted the title into his own song featuring a wickedly catchy piano hook and chanting female backing vocals. Accompanied by a cheeky music video depicting celebrity lookalikes of Prince Charles and Princess Diana navigating domestic life, the song took off on MTV and the charts, becoming Costello's first single to crack the Hot 100 (debuting at #38, peaking at #36) and the Top 40, as well as reaching number 33 on the Rock Tracks chart. "Every day, I write the book, finally changed that."
**I Wanna Be Loved (1984)** : A synth-heavy single from the album "Goodbye Cruel World," this track contributed to Elvis Costello's return to MTV. Despite the album itself being savaged by critics, this song represented Costello's embrace of the synth pop sound prevalent in the mid-80s. "I Wanna Be Loved."
**The Only Flame in Town (1984)** : This song was a duet with Darryl Hall, featured on Costello's "Goodbye Cruel World" album. It was a synth-heavy single that managed to get Costello back on MTV, and notably just missed the pop Top 40 chart. "And the Darryl Hall duet, the only flame in town, which just missed the pop top 40."
**Don't Let Me Be Misunderstood (1986)** : A cover of The Animals' classic, this song was featured on Elvis Costello's "King of America" album. It gained significant traction on rock radio, helping Costello re-establish his presence on the airwaves, and peaked on the album rock chart at number 38. "including a cover of the animal's classic Don't Let Me Be Misunderstood, which got Costello back on rock radio."
**I Want You (1986)** : This rather slow song from the album "Blood and Chocolate" became its best-remembered track, despite the album's overall bruising and raucous nature. It is a sinister piece exploring romantic obsession, often mistaken for a traditional love song, with Attractions bassist Bruce Thomas describing it as "neurotic." The song remains one of Costello's most covered compositions, notably performed by Fiona Apple live with Costello on stage at a VH1 event. "The sinister I Want You. Appeared to romantic obsession that many mistook for a love song."
**Veronica (1989)** : Co-written by Elvis Costello with former Beatle Paul McCartney, this song is a character study of an elderly woman suffering from memory loss. It features numerous Beatlesque touches, including its bass line, which McCartney himself played on his trademark Hofner bass. Released in the winter of 1989 as the lead single from Costello's Warner Bros. album "Spike," "Veronica" first rose to number 1 on the Modern Rock Chart for two weeks before crossing over to the Hot 100, where it reached number 19, becoming Costello's first and only US Top 20 pop hit. "Veronica was co-written by Costello with former Beatle Paul McCartney, a character study of an elderly woman suffering from memory loss."
**This Town (1989)** : This song served as Elvis Costello's follow-up single to "Veronica." Paul McCartney, who co-wrote and played bass on "Veronica," also played bass on this track. "This Town" achieved success on the Modern Rock Chart, reaching number 4. "McCartney also played bass on Costello's follow-up single, the number 4 Modern Rock hit This Town."
**The Other Side of Summer (1991)** : Described as a frothy single, this song held the number 1 spot on the Modern Rock Chart for three weeks in the summer of 1991. Its chart performance was a significant moment for Costello, though it proved to be the last time either he or Joe Jackson would place that high on any Billboard chart. "for 3 weeks while Elvis Costello held down the number 1 spot with his frothy single The Other Side of Summer."
**God Give Me Strength (Late 1990s)** : This piece of music was co-written with songwriting legend Burt Bacharach for the soundtrack of the musical film "Grace of My Heart." It showcased Costello's versatility and willingness to collaborate across different musical mediums and styles. "Written with songwriting legend, Bert Bakerak, for the soundtrack of the musical film Grace of My Heart."

"Imperial Bedroom topped the Paz and Job critics poll for 1982."

Marcus compared Costello's artistry to a unique combination of various legends, noting his brains (Randy Newman), implacability (Bob Dylan), everyman pathos (Buddy Holly), and uniqueness (John Lennon). "In Rolling Stone, critic Greal Marcus wrote one of the unquestioned originals of modern pop music."
**Re-upholstering Image for Synth Pop Age** : The success of "Every Day I Write The Book" on MTV and the charts made Elvis Costello conversant with the visual demands of the music industry in the mid-80s, effectively re-upholstering his image for the burgeoning synth pop age. This adaptation allowed him to remain relevant during a period of significant change in popular music. "It also made Costello conversant with MTV, re-apholstering his image for the synth pop age."
**Collaboration with Paul McCartney** : Elvis Costello co-wrote and collaborated with former Beatle Paul McCartney on several tracks, including "Veronica" and "My Brave Face." McCartney notably played bass on "Veronica" with his Hofner bass and on Costello's follow-up single "This Town," highlighting a significant partnership between two prominent songwriters. "Veronica was co-written by Costello with former Beatle Paul McCartney."
**Alt Rock Radio Fixture at 35** : By the age of 35, Elvis Costello had emerged as a consistent presence on alt rock radio, particularly with the success of singles like "Veronica" and "This Town." This positioned him as an influential figure within the developing alternative rock scene. "At age 35 Costello was emerging as an alt rock radio fixture."
**Continued Musical Exploration** : Since the 1990s, Elvis Costello has released more than a dozen albums, consistently refusing to be confined to any single genre or medium. His post-90s work includes a hybrid classical pop album with The Brodsky Quartet, collaborations with Burt Bacharach, and continued touring and recording of new material. "he has released more than a dozen albums since the 90s, refusing to be limited to any one genre or medium."
**Touring with Nick Lowe** : Elvis Costello has toured with his friend and long-time collaborator Nick Lowe, performing songs that made them both "pseudo pop stars." Their joint performances include "What's So Funny About Peace, Love, and Understanding," which, despite its punk origins, now conveys a message appropriate for "happy old men." "You can hear it in the voices of Elvis Costello and Nick Lowe when they tour together and perform the song that made them both pseudo pop stars."

## albums
**Beat Crazy (Late 1980) - Deeper into Dub Reggae** : This album saw Joe Jackson exploring Dub Reggae influences more deeply than ever before, with the title track exemplifying this direction. Notably, Jackson allowed his bassist, Graham Maby, to trade off lead vocals with him, indicating a shift in the band's dynamics and sound. The album sold modestly and generated no hits, leading Jackson to further artistic changes. "On his late 1980 album Beat Crazy, the title track went deeper into Dub Reggae than Jackson had ever attempted before."
**Jumpin' Jive (1981) - 1940s Big Band Jazz and Jump Blues Homage** : After the modest commercial performance of "Beat Crazy" and the dissolution of his band, Joe Jackson devoted his fourth album entirely to the sounds of 1940s Big Band Jazz and Jump Blues. This artistic pivot saw his cover of the Cab Calloway classic "Jumpin' Jive" become a minor hit in the UK, and the album performed comparably to "Beat Crazy" on the charts, emboldening Jackson to pursue an even more eclectic mix on his subsequent LPs. "And he devoted his fourth album, 1981's Jump and Drive, to the sound of 40's Big Band Jazz and Jump Blues."
**Night and Day (1982) - New York City-Inspired Breakthrough** : Joe Jackson's fifth studio album, titled in homage to Cole Porter, was conceived as an LP entirely about New York City, where he had relocated in late 1981. Recorded in a small SoHo studio, it integrated the urban sounds of jazz and Latin clubs he absorbed in the city, alongside experiments with synthesizers and drum machines, influenced by Kraftwerk. The album became Jackson's biggest selling and most hit-packed LP, reaching number 4 on the Billboard album chart and reinventing his career by establishing him as an MTV staple and a critically acclaimed artist. "These sounds and styles would all wind up on Joe Jackson's fifth studio album. An LP all about New York City that he would title in homage to Cole Porter, Night and Day."
**Body and Soul (1984) - Sophisticop and Latin/Jazz Infusion** : This album marked a deeper dive into "Sophisticop" for Joe Jackson, again recorded in New York City. It was infused with an even greater influence from Latin pop and jazz, further refining the stylistic direction he had begun with "Night and Day." The album broke into the top 20 on the album chart and produced the lead single "You Can't Get What You Want (Till You Know What You Want)," which reached number 15. "Joe Jackson went deeper into Sophisticop on his next album Body and Soul, which was also recorded in New York City, and infused with even greater influence from Latin pop and jazz."
**Big World (1986) - Live Album of New Material** : Joe Jackson's "Big World" was a unique live album featuring all new material, recorded for the first time in front of an audience specifically instructed not to cheer or applaud. This unconventional approach to a live recording produced an album rock radio hit with "Right and Wrong," which reached number 11 on that chart. "His 1986 LP Big World, for example, was a live album of all new material recorded for the first time in front of an audience who were instructed not to cheer or applaud."
**Will Power (1987) - Vocal-Free Classical LP** : This vocal-free LP represented Joe Jackson's attempt to fulfill his original teenage dream of being a classical composer. While some critics called it pretentious, many were impressed by his ambitious foray into the classical genre, showcasing his wide-ranging musical talents and determination to follow his muse. "Jackson even tried fulfilling his original teenage dream of being a classical composer with 1987's Vocal Free LP Will Power."
**Live 1980/86 (1988) - Diverse Live Renditions** : This live album captured multiple versions of Joe Jackson's hit song "Is She Really Going Out With Him?" presented in three distinct styles: Jazz Rock, Acoustic, and even A Cappella. This showcased Jackson's penchant for reinterpreting his old material in new ways during his concert tours, often "confounding and delighting his audiences." "His 1988 live album captured three versions of Ishi really going out with him in three styles, including Jazz Rock, Acoustic, and even Acapilla."
**Blaze of Glory (1989) - Modern Rock Appearance** : This album featured the single "19 Forever," which made an early appearance on the Modern Rock Chart, climbing all the way to number 4. This demonstrated Jackson's continued relevance and ability to connect with emerging musical trends, even as he pursued diverse artistic directions. "taking his single, 19 Forever, a track from his 1989 album, Blaze of Glory, all the way to number 4."
**Fool (2019) - 20th Album with Vintage Sound** : Joe Jackson's 20th album, "Fool," was released in 2019 and was noted for its sound, which critics described as reminiscent of "vintage Joe" Jackson. This indicated his enduring style and continued recording output well into the 21st century. "His 20th album, Fool, was released in 2019 and it sounds like vintage Joe."

**"Angry Young Men" Moniker** : Along with Elvis Costello and Graham Parker, Joe Jackson was among a group of British singer-songwriters who emerged from Pub Rock and Powerpop, adopted a punk attitude, helped shape new wave, and were collectively tagged with the moniker "Angry Young Men." "In the fall of 1981, with his band broken up, and his latest tour ended, Joe Jackson took up residence in the East Village and started recording in a small SoHo studio."

"On his concert tours, when Jackson played old material, he refused to play the songs as they were originally arranged on record, alternately confounding and delighting his audiences."

## albums
**The Up Escalator (1980) - Mainstream Album Rock Sound** : Graham Parker and The Rumour collaborated with producer Jimmy Iovine on this album, a pairing initiated by Arista Records' desire to position Parker as "the next Bruce Springsteen." Iovine combined Parker's typically sardonic lyrics with a mainstream album rock sound, as heard on the lead single "Stupefaction." While the LP sold decently, matching the peak of "Squeezing Out Sparks" at number 40, critics often complained that Iovine's production "neutered The Rumour's raucous sound." "On the up escalator, Iaveen paired Parker's typically sardonic lyrics with a mainstream album rock sound."
**The Mona Lisa's Sister (1988) - Independent Production** : This album represented Graham Parker's determination to retain artistic control, as he insisted on writing and producing it himself. After signing with Atlantic Records (his fourth label in a decade) and being rejected when he offered to record demos himself due to their insistence on outside producers and studio sheen, Parker left Atlantic. He then signed with RCA Records and produced the entire album himself with members of The Rumour for less than $60,000. Released in 1988, it generated two airplay hits across two radio formats. "A record Parker wanted to write and produce himself."

# curiosity

**Angry Young Men Moniker** : In the late 1970s, three British singer-songwriters—Elvis Costello, Joe Jackson, and Graham Parker—who emerged from Pub Rock and Powerpop scenes, adopted a punk attitude, and helped shape New Wave, were collectively tagged with the moniker "Angry Young Men." This label encapsulated their initial rebellious and sardonic image, though they would later evolve their sounds in diverse directions. "and were tagged with the moniker, Angry Young Men."
**1982: A Contrasting Year for Costello and Jackson** : 1982 was a year of contrasting commercial fortunes for Elvis Costello and Joe Jackson. Both artists released their most adventurous and widest-ranging albums that year—Costello with "Imperial Bedroom" and Jackson with "Night and Day." While Costello's album, despite critical acclaim, performed modestly, Jackson's became his biggest selling and most hit-packed LP, marking a significant divergence in their career trajectories. "You might say Joe Jackson's 1982 was the opposite of Elvis Costello's 1982."
**MTV Launch and its Visual Impact (1981)** : The launch of MTV in 1981 fundamentally transformed the music business, making it much more visual. This change favored acts from the 70s that could translate their sound into the video medium, putting them in heavier rotation, and also influenced the evolution of musical genres, pushing New Wave towards synthpop through slicker, glossier, and more expensive videos. "By 1982, the music business had become much more visual. Thanks, of course, to the 1981 launch of MTV."
**Pazz & Jop Critics Poll (1982)** : The Pazz & Jop critics poll, an annual survey of music critics, recognized Elvis Costello's "Imperial Bedroom" as the top album of 1982. This critical acclaim underscored the album's artistic significance and sophisticated nature, even though its commercial performance was modest. "Imperial Bedroom topped the Paz and Job critics poll for 1982."
**Casey Kasem's American Top 40** : Radio personality Casey Kasem, known for counting down the American Top 40, played a significant role in mainstream music promotion. He notably announced Joe Jackson's return to the Top 40 with "Steppin' Out" in 1982 and Graham Parker's first Top 40 hit "Wake Up Next to You" in 1985, lending crucial exposure to these artists. "Casey Kasim counted it down."
**Greal Marcus's Rolling Stone Critic of Elvis Costello** : In Rolling Stone, critic Greal Marcus offered profound praise for Elvis Costello, calling him "one of the unquestioned originals of modern pop music." Marcus described Costello as combining the brains of Randy Newman, the implacability of Bob Dylan, the everyman pathos of Buddy Holly, and the uniqueness of John Lennon, cementing his reputation as a formidable songwriter. "In Rolling Stone, critic Greal Marcus wrote one of the unquestioned originals of modern pop music."
**Music Video Adaptation Struggles (Early 80s)** : In the early 80s, the "Angry Young Men" (Costello, Jackson, Parker) developed "fit-full relationships" with the music video era, at times questioning the worth of these "mini-movies." Elvis Costello's early videos were low-tech, and Joe Jackson eventually "detested making them" because he felt they "overinterpreted and limited his songs." "For the next few years, they would develop fit-full relationships with the music video era, at times questioning whether the mini-movies were even worth it."
**Alt Rock "Elder Statesmen" in Late 80s** : By the late 1980s, as new wave transitioned into alternative rock, the "formerly young men," now in their 30s, began to resemble "Elder Statesmen" of the genre. They unwittingly seemed "a little ahead of the curve," largely because they were pursuing their individual musical muses and doing "whatever they wanted." "the formerly young men, all in their 30s, began to resemble Elder Statesmen. And, however unwittingly, they again seemed a little ahead of the curve, mostly because they were doing whatever they wanted."
**Billboard's Modern Rock Tracks Chart Launch (1988)** : In September 1988, Billboard magazine launched its Modern Rock Tracks chart, which was the publication's first Alt Rock Chart. This new chart provided a significant platform for alternative artists and saw early appearances from Graham Parker, Joe Jackson, and Elvis Costello. "Billboard launched Modern Rock Tracks, the magazine's first Alt Rock Chart. In September of 88."
