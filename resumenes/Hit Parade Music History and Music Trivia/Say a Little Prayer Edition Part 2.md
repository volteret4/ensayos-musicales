## albums
**Amazing Grace (1972) - Live Gospel LP** : This ambitious multimedia project, recorded live over two nights in January 1972 at the New Temple Missionary Baptist Church in Los Angeles, was a critical and commercial triumph. It blended elements of her gospel and pop worlds, featuring rock accompanists like bassist Chuck Rainey and drummer Bernard Purdy, along with the Southern California Community Choir and James Cleveland. Marvin Gaye praised it as "Aritha's singular masterpiece," and it remarkably cracked the top 10 on the Billboard pop album chart, reaching number seven by July of '72. Selling a million copies initially and later certified double platinum by the RIAA, it remains Franklin's top-selling album and the best-selling live gospel LP of all time. "Amazing Grace cracked the top 10 on the Billboard pop album chart, extraordinarily rare for a gospel LP, and reached number seven by July of 72."
**Hay Now Hay, the other side of the sky (1973) - Quincy Jones Produced LP** : This album was produced by Quincy Jones and, while it underperformed on the pop album chart, it successfully generated an R&B chart number one hit with the song "Angel." "her Quincy Jones produced 1973 LP, Hay Now Hay, the other side of the sky, underperformed on the pop album chart, but it did produce an R&B chart number one with Angel."
**Sparkle (1976) - Studio LP** : This studio album featured music written by Soul Legend Curtis Mayfield for the soundtrack to the Black film musical *Sparkle*. Although not a direct soundtrack, the music was a deliberate throwback to the 1960s soul sound of both Mayfield and Franklin, and it was embraced by Black audiences, becoming Franklin's only gold album in the second half of the 1970s. "Black audiences embraced both the film and Aritha's album, a studio LP titled Sparkle, and though it was technically not a direct soundtrack to the movie, Sparkle became Franklin's only gold album in the second half of the 70s."
**LaDiva (1979) - Disco Era Album** : This album was Franklin's attempt to engage with the disco sound, produced by Van McCoy, known for "The Hustle." Universally panned by critics, it charted lower than any of her Atlantic LPs, peaking at number 146, and its lead single "Ladies Only" only reached number 33 on the R&B chart, failing to chart on the Hot 100. Rolling Stone critic Jimmy Guterman cited it as Franklin's last album on Atlantic Records, marking the end of her 12-year tenure with the label. "The LaDiva album was universally panned by critics and charted lower than any of her Atlantic LP's, peaking at number 146."
**Jump to It (1982) - Comeback Album** : Produced by Luther Vandross, this album sealed Franklin's 1980s comeback, bringing in a new sound of synthesized funk and kicking up the tempo. The title track returned her to the top of the R&B chart for the first time in six years and brought her back into the pop top 40. "under Vandros' guidance, the album they produced titled Jump to It, Sealed Franklin's 80s comeback, kicked up the tempo, and brought in a new brand of synthesized funk."
**Get It Right (1983) - Synth Funk Follow-up** : This follow-up album to *Jump to It* was another collaboration with Luther Vandross, delving deeper into synth funk. While its title track topped the R&B chart, the album as a whole sold less well and the single did not make the pop top 40. "The title track from that LP which went deeper into synth funk also topped the R&B chart. But it didn't make the pop top 40 and the LP sold less well."
**Who's Zuman Who (1985) - Blockbuster Studio Album** : Produced by Narada Michael Walden, this blockbuster studio album became Franklin's first-ever platinum album and her most hit-packed LP since her late 1960s heyday. It included the successful singles "Freeway of Love" and the title track "Who's Zuman Who," solidifying her return as a current pop star. "Produced by Narada Michael Walden, Zuman became Franklin's first ever platinum album, and her most hit-packed LP since her late 60s heyday."
**Aretha (1986) - Warhol Cover LP** : This was Franklin's 31st studio LP and the third time she used "Aritha" as an album title. Its cover was famously painted by pop art legend Andy Warhol, marking the last work he completed before his death in 1987. Narada Michael Walden produced most tracks, with Franklin producing some herself, and Keith Richards contributing to the single "Jumpin' Jack Flash." "In a flex, Arista hired pop art legend Andy Warhol to paint the Aritha album cover. It would be the last work he completed before his 1987 death."
**Through the Storm (1989) - Duet Album** : This album generated multiple hits featuring guest vocals from other megastars, including the title track, a duet with Elton John, and "It Isn't, It Wasn't, It Ain't Never Gonna Be," a duet with Whitney Houston. "In 1989, her album Through the Storm generated a pair of hits featuring guest vocals from fellow megastars."
**What You See Is What You Sweat (Early 1990s) - New Jack Swing/House Foray** : This album represented Franklin's deeper dive into New Jack Swing and House music during the early 1990s. Despite containing a successful house cover of Sly and the Family Stone's "Everyday People" on the R&B and Dance Club charts, the album itself was considered a flop and was her lowest charting album since *LaDiva* in 1979. "In the early 90s, Franklin went deeper into New Jack's Swing and House music on the CD What You See is What You Sweat. Bluntly, the disc was a flop."
**A Rose Is Still A Rose (1998) - Lauryn Hill Collaboration Album** : This album featured the acclaimed title track written and produced by Lauryn Hill, which expertly blended Franklin's classic sound with a contemporary hip hop beat. Both the single and the album achieved gold status, marking Franklin's last recordings to do so. "Both the single and the A Rose Is Still A Rose album went gold. Franklin's last recordings to do so."

## songs
**(You Make Me Feel Like) A Natural Woman (1967)** : This classic hit was one of Franklin's greatest ever. She famously performed it live at the 2015 Kennedy Center Honors, where she was celebrating Carole King (who co-wrote the song), captivating the audience and moving President Obama to tears, dropping her fur coat on stage as a gesture of honor from gospel music. "writer of one of Franklin's greatest ever hits, which the Queen of Seoul stepped out on stage, clad in a fur coat to perform live."
**Respect (1967)** : This iconic song was Franklin's last number one pop chart hit before "I Knew You Were Waiting (For Me)" in 1987, almost exactly 20 years later. "Almost exactly 20 years after Franklin had last topped the big pop chart with respect."
**Think (1968)** : This classic song was performed by Franklin with light choreography in the 1980 musical film *The Blues Brothers*, providing her with much-needed positive exposure following her struggles in the disco era and reminding audiences of her enduring talent. "Her performance of her 1968 classic Think, complete with light choreography, reminded audiences of what they loved about her."
**Holy Holy (1972)** : This was the only single released from the *Amazing Grace* album. Despite the album's massive success, "Holy Holy" missed the top 40 on both the Hot 100 and R&B charts, though it did manage to reach number 49 on soul singles. "Though the album's only single, Holy Holy, missed the top 40 on both the Hot 100 and R&B charts, it did manage a number 49 peak on soul singles."
**Until You Come Back to Me (1973)** : Franklin's cover of this song, originally recorded and co-written by Stevie Wonder in 1967, became a smash hit in late 1973. It reached number three on the Hot 100 and number one on the R&B chart in the winter of 1974, marking her last pop top 10 hit for over a decade. "So Aritha Franklin's cover of Until You Come Back to Me was itself a smash, reaching number three on the Hot 100 and number one on the R&B chart in the winter of 74."
**Angel (1973)** : This song, from her Quincy Jones-produced album *Hay Now Hay, the other side of the sky*, achieved a number one position on the R&B chart, despite the album's underperformance on the pop charts. "but it did produce an R&B chart number one with Angel."
**I'm in Love (1974)** : Written by veteran singer-songwriter Bobby Womack, this song brought Franklin back to number one on the Soul Singles Chart a year after the success of "Angel." "One year later, Franklin was back at number one on the Soul Singles Chart with I'm in Love, a song written by veteran singer-songwriter Bobby Womack."
**Something He Can Feel (1976)** : This single from the *Sparkle* album went to number one on the R&B chart and number 28 on the Hot 100 in the summer of 1976. The song, a 1960s soul throwback written by Curtis Mayfield, was later remade in the 1990s by the girl group En Vogue. "the single, something he can feel, which, by the way, would later be remade in the 90s by Girl Group Envogue, went to number one on the R&B chart and number 28 on the Hot 100 in the summer of 76."
**Ladies Only (1979)** : The first single from Franklin's *LaDiva* album, produced by disco artist Van McCoy, this song peaked at number 33 on the R&B chart and failed to make an impression on the Hot 100, symbolizing Franklin's struggle to adapt to the disco era. "Even on the R&B chart, the Ladies Only single could only manage a number 33 peak. It didn't touch the Hot 100."
**United Together (1981)** : This melodramatic song became a number three R&B hit in early 1981, marking Franklin's first R&B top 10 hit in nearly four years and helping her get back on Black Radio. "United Together, a number three R&B hit in early 1981. It was Franklin's first R&B top 10 hit in nearly four years."
**Love All the Hurt Away (1981)** : A torch song that became a number six R&B hit later in 1981, helping Franklin regain airplay on Black Radio, though such tracks still contributed to her perception as a "legacy act." "Torch songs like United Together and Love All the Hurt Away, a number six R&B hit later that year, got Franklin back on Black Radio."
**Jump to It (1982)** : The title track from her album produced by Luther Vandross, this song marked Franklin's return to the top of the R&B chart for the first time in six years and brought her back into the pop top 40, reaching number 25. It broke the tie with James Brown for most number one hits on the Soul Chart, making it her 18th number one soul song. "The title track to Franklin's Jump to It album not only brought Aritha back to the top of the R&B chart for the first time in six years, it returned her to the pop top 40."
**Freeway of Love (1985)** : Released in early summer 1985, this song was a commercial triumph, climbing to number three on the Hot 100 by August (her first pop top 10 hit since 1974's "Until You Come Back to Me") and number one on the R&B chart. Its video gave her first MTV appearance, and it was the second most acclaimed single of 1985 in the Pazz & Jop Critics Poll. The song, written by Narada Michael Walden, cleverly updated the Motown sound with a Bruce Springsteen influence, including a "pink Cadillac" lyric and a saxophone solo by Clarence Clemons. "It's difficult to overstate what a commercial triumph Freeway of Love was for Aritha Franklin."
**Who's Zuman Who (1985)** : The title track from her blockbuster 1985 album *Who's Zuman Who*, this song followed "Freeway of Love" into the pop top 10, reaching number seven in November 1985 and number one on the R&B chart. "The LP's title track followed Freeway of Love into the pop top ten. Who's Zuman Who reached number seven in November 85."
**Sisters Are Doin' It for Themselves (1985)** : This electro-R&B feminist anthem was a duet with British synth-pop duo Eurythmics, serving as a vocal showcase for both Franklin and Annie Lennox. It reached number 18 on the charts, helping Franklin maintain her momentum in the MTV era. "Their electro-R&B feminist anthem Sisters are doing it for themselves, a vocal showcase for both Franklin and Eurythmics frontwoman Annie Lennox reached number eighteen."
**Another Night (1986)** : A synth pop jam and another single from *Who's Zuman Who*, this song's video received strong rotation on MTV, where Franklin appeared with an angular haircut and glittery outfit, projecting a new wave rocker image. "He dropped yet another single from Who's Zuman Who in early 1986. Another synth pop jam, this time without Eurythmics called Another Night."
**Jumpin' Jack Flash (1986)** : A cover of The Rolling Stones' classic, this song was the first single from her 1986 *Aretha* album. Produced by Keith Richards, who also played guitar, it reached number 21 on the pop charts and appeared on the soundtrack to the Whoopi Goldberg spy comedy of the same name. "a cover of the Rolling Stones classic Jumpin' Jack Flash, that also appeared on the soundtrack to the 1986 Wuppie Goldberg Spy Comedy of the same name."
**Jimmy Lee (1986)** : This percolating soul pop ditty was a follow-up single from the *Aretha* album, reaching number 28 on the pop charts and extending Franklin's best hit-making streak since 1968 to six consecutive Pop Top 40 hits. "a percolating soul pop diddy called Jimmy Lee, reached number 28."
**I Knew You Were Waiting (For Me) (1987)** : This duet with George Michael, co-written by Dennis Morgan and Simon Climie, was a "pop gospel" song not originally conceived as a duet but heard as such by Clive Davis. Released in January 1987 as the third single from the *Aretha* LP, it took nine weeks to reach number one on the Hot 100, almost exactly 20 years after Franklin's last pop chart topper "Respect," completing her remarkable comeback. "I knew you were waiting took nine weeks to reach number one on the Hot 100. Almost exactly 20 years after Franklin had last topped the big pop chart with respect."
**Through the Storm (1989)** : The title track from her 1989 album, this was a duet with Elton John and became the bigger pop hit from the album, reaching number 16 on the Hot 100. "Through the Storm's title track, A Do Wet with Elton John was the bigger pop hit, reaching number 16 on the Hot 100."
**It Isn't, It Wasn't, It Ain't Never Gonna Be (1989)** : This duet with Whitney Houston, whom Franklin considered her de facto vocal godchild, just missed the pop top 40 but impressively hit the top five on the R&B chart. This collaboration also served to introduce Franklin to the rhythms of New Jack Swing. "The follow-up, A Do Wet with Franklin's de facto vocal God Child Whitney Houston, called It Isn't It Wasn't It Ain't Never Gonna Be. Just missed the pop top 40, but hit the top five on the R&B chart."
**Everyday People (Early 1990s)** : Franklin's house cover of this Sly and the Family Stone song appeared on her *What You See Is What You Sweat* album. Despite the album's overall poor performance, this particular track managed to make the top 40 on both the R&B and Dance Club charts. "But her house cover of Sly and the Family Stones Everyday People did make the top 40 on both the R&B and Dance Club charts."
**A Deeper Love (1994)** : Originally an original song by C+C Music Factory that served as a B-side to their "Pride" cover, it topped Billboard's Club Play Chart and just missed the pop top 40. Franklin's 1994 remake with new vocals became the definitive version, topping the Club Play Chart all over again. Nominated for a Grammy, it is still recognized as one of the best house and LGBTQ anthems. "Finally, in 1994, Clavilles and Cole produced a remake of their own A Deeper Love with new vocals by Yes, Aritha Franklin and Franklin's version became the definitive version, topping the Club Play Chart all over again."
**Willing To Forgive (1994)** : This slow jam ballad, included on Franklin's 1994 greatest hits CD, successfully cracked the top 40, peaking at number 26 on the Hot 100, demonstrating her continued presence on the hit parade at age 52. "Also on that album was a slow jam ballad called Willing To Forgive that actually cracked the top 40, peaking at number 26 on the Hot 100."
**Nessun Dorma (1998)** : Franklin delivered a triumphant performance of this aria at the Grammy Awards, which preceded the chart success of her song "A Rose Is Still A Rose" by just a month. "this song broke onto the charts just a month after Aritha Franklin's triumphant Grammys performance of Nessun Dorma."
**A Rose Is Still A Rose (1998)** : Written and produced by Lauryn Hill, who also sang on the chorus, this song was bequeathed to Franklin and expertly blended her classic sound, echoing hits like "(You Make Me Feel Like) A Natural Woman" and "Spanish Harlem," with a loping hip hop beat characteristic of 1998. It subtly interpolated Edie Brickell & New Bohemians' "What I Am," and peaked at number 26 on the Hot 100 and number 5 on the R&B Hip Hop Songs Chart, becoming Franklin's last top 40 hit of her lifetime. "The song deftly pulled off several tricks at once. A Rose is still a Rose sounded like Aritha, echoing prior hits of hers like a natural woman or Spanish Harlem. But it also sounded like 1998 with a loping hip hop beat."
**My Country, 'Tis of Thee (2009)** : Franklin famously sang this song outdoors in the cold at President Barack Obama's inauguration in 2009, wearing a "truly fascinating hat," in a moment that became a memorable part of her final decade. "at President Barack Obama's 2009 inauguration, Franklin sang my country Tizavi, outdoors in the cold while wearing a truly fascinating hat."
**Rolling in the Deep, The Aritha Version (2014)** : Franklin covered Adele's signature torch song in 2014, three years after Adele's original hit number one, cheekily titling her rendition "Rolling in the Deep, The Aritha Version." While it made a brief appearance on the R&B chart at number 47, a remix of the track went all the way to number one on Billboard's Dance Club Play Chart in early 2015, showing Franklin topping charts in her 70s. "Franklin covered it in 2014, just three years after Adels' original hit number 1. In a cheeky move, Franklin titled her cover of the song, Rolling in the Deep, The Aritha Version."

## curiosities
**Early Career at Columbia Records (Early 1960s)** : Despite her background as an acclaimed gospel singer, Franklin spent the first half of the 1960s recording jazzy pop songs for Columbia Records, a period that yielded no big hits for her. "she spent the first half of the 1960s recording jazzy pop songs for Columbia records, with no big hits."
**Switch to Atlantic Records (1967)** : Her move to the R&B-rooted label Atlantic Records in 1967 was pivotal, bringing respect to her name and establishing her as "America's premiere vocal dynamo" after a period of no major hits at Columbia. "But a switch to R&B rooted label Atlantic records in 1967 brought respect to Arita's name, and made her America's premiere vocal dynamo."
**Gospel-Infused Secular Singing** : Even during her secular pop recordings, Franklin's singing style was heavily infused with gospel, to the extent that her love songs often sounded like she was "bearing witness to the Lord," leading fans to yearn for her return to gospel music. "so much of her secular pop recording was already infused with gospel-style singing that, even when she was delivering love songs, it sounded like she was bearing witness to the Lord."
**Amazing Grace the Film Technical Glitch (1972)** : The documentary filmed during the *Amazing Grace* sessions by Sidney Pollock suffered a major technical glitch: the filmed footage and audio were not synced, and no clapperboards were used to fix it in post-production. This led to the film being shelved for over four decades, only seeing release in 2018 after both Pollock and Franklin had passed away, thanks to 21st-century technology resolving the misalignment. "The filmed footage and the audio were not synced up, and Pollock had not used clapperboards to make it fixable in post-production. Amazing Grace the Film would wind up sitting on a shelf for more than four decades, until after both Pollock and Aritha Franklin had died, and 21st century technology made it possible to fix the audio video misalignment."
**Falling Out of Pop Singles Alignment (1970s)** : Following the release of *Amazing Grace*, Franklin's alignment with the pop singles chart seemed to break, leading to a decade where she had only one more pop top 10 hit, "Until You Come Back to Me," for over ten years. "If there was any downside to Amazing Grace, it was how it seemed to break Aritha's alignment with the pop singles chart."
**Regarded as a Luminary of the Past (Late 1970s)** : By the late 1970s, as R&B and Pop shifted towards disco, the nostalgic sound of her *Sparkle* album inadvertently reinforced the perception that Franklin was regarded as a musical luminary belonging to a past era. "Still, the nostalgic Sparkle only reinforced that Franklin was now regarded as a music luminary of the past."
**Disco Era Belly Flop (Late 1970s)** : Franklin experienced a notable struggle in adapting to the disco era, with her 1979 album *LaDiva* and its single "Ladies Only" largely failing commercially and critically, marking her as one of the 1960s veterans who "belly flopped harder" in the genre. "But no 60s veteran seemed to belly flop harder in the disco era than Aritha Franklin."
**Departure from Atlantic Records (Late 1970s)** : After 12 years and over a dozen LPs, Franklin parted ways with Atlantic Records following the critical and commercial failure of *LaDiva*. By then, Atlantic had become more known for rock acts like Led Zeppelin than for R&B, indicating a growing disconnect. "LaDiva turned out to be Aritha Franklin's last album on Atlantic Records. After 12 years and more than a dozen LPs, the Queen of Soul and the Story label had grown apart."
**The Blues Brothers Cameo (1980)** : Film director John Landis was reportedly surprised when Franklin accepted a small acting cameo as a waitress in his 1980 musical film *The Blues Brothers*, remarking that "she didn't have anything else going on." Her performance of "Think" in the film provided much-needed positive exposure after her disco struggles. "When film director John Landis invited her to do a small acting cameo as a waitress in his 1980 musical film The Blues Brothers, he was almost shocked when she said yes, quote, she didn't have anything else going on, he later said."
**Signing with Arista Records (1980)** : Franklin signed a new recording contract with Arista Records in 1980, a label launched by Clive Davis, who aimed to reintroduce her as a current pop star and help her modernize her sound, echoing Jerry Wexler's role at Atlantic in 1967. "The same year Franklin was on screen in the Blues Brothers. She signed a new recording contract with Arista Records."
**Luther Vandross Studio Dynamic (Early 1980s)** : Collaborating with Luther Vandross on albums like *Jump to It* and *Get It Right* led to a "combustible" studio dynamic, as both were headstrong performers with strong opinions. Vandross pushed Franklin to try new vocal techniques like scat-like singing, while Franklin, "ever the diva," pushed back, asserting her legendary status. "Luther and Aritha proved combustible in the studio, both headstrong performers with strong opinions about singing."
**Most Number One Soul Hits (1982)** : With the success of "Jump to It," Franklin broke a tie with James Brown and achieved the record for the most number one hits in the history of the Soul Chart, securing her 18th number one soul song. "That gave the record for the most soul number ones to the lady known as the Queen of Soul."
**Father's Passing and Career Crossroads (1984)** : Franklin spent two years in Detroit caring for her seriously ill father, Reverend C. L. Franklin, who passed away in 1984. This personal loss marked a career crossroads for her, prompting her decision to return to music and push her crossover ambitions even further. "Franklin had spent the prior two years largely in her hometown of Detroit, looking after her seriously ill father. The Reverend C. L. Franklin finally passed in 1984, after which Aritha began thinking about returning to music."
**First MTV Video (1985)** : The music video for her hit "Freeway of Love" marked Franklin's first appearance on MTV, a significant step in her 1980s comeback and her embrace of the MTV era. "Its video got her on MTV for the first time."
**New Wave Rocker Image (1986)** : In the video for "Another Night," Franklin presented a dramatically updated image, performing with an angular haircut and a glittery outfit, looking "less like a sole veteran and more like a new wave rocker," signifying her successful reinvention as a current pop star. "Aritha performed in front of a nightclub with an angular haircut and a glittery outfit. Looking less like a sole veteran and more like a new wave rocker."
**Boosting Whitney Houston (1985-1986)** : Franklin and her "honorary auntie" Whitney Houston, whose mother Cissy sang backup for Franklin for years, mutually boosted each other's chart success. Houston's video for "How Well I Know" even featured a clip of Franklin, providing an implicit seal of approval as Houston brought gospel-trained R&B vocals back to the forefront. "In a way, 22-year-old Whitney and her honorary auntie, Aritha Franklin, boosted each other up the charts that year."
**Rock and Roll Hall of Fame Induction Absence (1987)** : On January 3, 1987, Franklin became the first woman inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame, but she was not present at the Waldorf Astoria ceremony in New York City. This was partly due to her dislike of leaving Detroit and, more significantly, to her being busy as a "current hitmaker," distinguishing her from many of her fellow inductees who were long past their prime. "But Franklin herself wasn't there for one thing she didn't much like leaving Detroit. But for another, Aritha was busy, compared with virtually all of her fellow inductees that night..."
**Outrunning Her Legacy (Final Decades)** : A recurring theme in the final three decades of Franklin's life was her continuous effort to "outrun her own legacy," striving to uphold her "Queen of Soul" status while also avoiding being constrained by it, a goal in which she "intermitably succeeded." "She was trying to outrun her own legacy, to both live up to her status as Queen of Soul, and not get bogged down under the weight of that title."
**Album Flop for Full Club Sound (Early 1990s)** : Franklin's attempt to go "full club" on albums like *What You See Is What You Sweat* in the early 1990s generally did not work, resulting in one of her lowest-charting albums since *LaDiva*, despite individual dance track successes. "There was always something about Aritha trying to go full club on an album that never quite worked."
**Last Gold Recordings and Top 40 Hit (1998)** : Both the single and album for *A Rose Is Still A Rose* went gold in 1998, which were Franklin's last recordings to achieve this certification. The single also proved to be the final top 40 hit of her lifetime. "Both the single and the A Rose Is Still A Rose album went gold. Franklin's last recordings to do so. Rose would also be the last top 40 hit of her lifetime."
**2015 Kennedy Center Honors Performance (2015)** : While honoring Carole King at the Kennedy Center Honors, Franklin, who had previously received the honor in 1994, delivered a powerful performance of "(You Make Me Feel Like) A Natural Woman." She moved King and President Obama to tears and famously dropped her fur coat mid-performance, a gesture from gospel tradition, symbolizing one "Queen recognizing another" and encapsulating her regal, culturally black, universally appreciated, and diva-like persona. "Then, more than two minutes into the performance, Franklin got up from the piano, walked to the front of the stage, dropped her fur coat to the floor, and kept on singing. The media later pointed out that this was a gesture of honor borrowed from gospel music and the black church, one Queen recognizing another."
**Passing Away (August 16, 2018)** : Aretha Franklin passed away on August 16, 2018, receiving national attention typically reserved for heads of state. Her memorial service at her Detroit Church and a massive homegoing service at Detroit's Greater Grace Temple were attended by thousands and included tributes from politicians and celebrities. "When Aritha Franklin passed away on August 16, 2018, it received the kind of attention usually reserved for heads of state."
