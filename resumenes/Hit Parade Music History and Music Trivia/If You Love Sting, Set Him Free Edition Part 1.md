## albums
**Outlandos d'Amour (1978) - Debut Album** : Released in 1978, this album's title was a "comically bastardized French for Outlaws of Love." It was considered the closest The Police came to a "straight-up punk album," featuring aggressive three-piece rock topped by Sting's keening falsetto voice. Initially slow to break in England due to a BBC ban on "Roxanne," it ultimately reached number 23 on the Billboard LP's chart in May 1979 and number 23 in the UK after re-release. "Roxanne would be the lead single of the police's 1978 debut album Outlandos de Moore, comically bastardized French for Outlaws of Love. It was the closest the police came to a straight-up punk album, aggressive three-piece rock topped by Sting's Keening falsetto voice."
**Regatta de Blanc (1979) - Sophomore LP** : Released in October 1979, this album's faux French title, roughly translated to "White Reggae," was a self-conscious reference to white artists experimenting with black music. The album continued The Police's trend of blending reggae into new wave rock and became a solid US hit, reaching number 25 on the album chart and spending 100 weeks charting, twice as long as its predecessor. "Their sophomore LP, which arrived in October 79, was titled Regatta de Blanc, another faux French title that roughly translated to White Reggae."
**Zenyatta Mondatta (1980) - Major US Breakthrough** : Released in the fall of 1980, this album's title "meant literally nothing," according to Stuart Copeland, who said the words just rhymed and rolled off the tongue. The LP solidified The Police's status as New Wave's top band and Sting as its premiere hit-making songwriter, reaching number 5 on the Billboard album chart and going platinum, both firsts for the band. "Released in the fall of 1980, the LP ratified the police as New Wave's top band and Sting as its premiere hit-making songwriter."
**Ghost in the Machine (1981) - Fourth Album** : This album marked the first Police songs built around synthesizer melodies and saw the band collaborate with new producer Hugh Padgham, who brought a "more pop sensibility." It became their third consecutive number one album in the UK, climbed to number two in America, and was their first LP to achieve double platinum status. "Ghost in the Machine was the police's third straight number one album in the UK and in America, it climbed to number two and eventually became the first of their LP's to go double platinum."
**Synchronicity (1983) - Fifth Album** : Released a month after its lead single "Every Breath You Take" in June 1983, this album became The Police's only number one album in America. It spent a remarkable 17 weeks atop the album chart, notably interrupting Michael Jackson's "Thriller" for over four months in 1983. "A month after the single, the police dropped Synchronicity, which would turn out to be their only number one album in America. On American Top 40, as the single and the album settled in for long runs on top of the charts, Casey Kasim counted them down."
