As a music historian and critic, this document compiles salient information regarding musical trends, revivals, and cultural shifts observed within the 2010s, drawing insights particularly from the perspective of alt-rock and its various subgenres. The analysis focuses on the cyclical nature of music, specific genre comebacks, influential artists, and broader industry impacts.

---

### Overall Music Cycles and Trends

*   **Cyclical Nature of Music:** Music inherently follows cycles where sounds and styles achieve prominence, peak, and then gradually recede. However, once established, a style rarely disappears entirely, instead often lying dormant.
*   **The "Sonic Update" Phenomenon:** When a style is rediscovered and reactivated, often by a new generation, it typically receives a "sonic update" to resonate with contemporary tastes, allowing it a new period of popularity before the cycle repeats.
*   **The Alt-Rock Rockabilly Anomaly:** One notable exception to the cyclical return is alt-rock style rockabilly, which was significant in the early 1980s (e.g., The Stray Cats) but largely faded without experiencing a full-blown revival in its original scope, despite existing in niche corners.
*   **The 2010s as a Decade of Rediscovery:** The 2010s were characterized by a significant number of musical revivals, rediscoveries, and comebacks, all rooted in the "musical DNA" of previous eras, demonstrating the persistent influence of past sounds.

### The 2010s: A Decade of Comebacks and Revivals

*   **Rock's Continued Commercial Health:** While Hip Hop emerged as a dominant cultural force globally, rock music maintained strong overall consumption figures throughout the 2010s, particularly when concert ticket sales are factored in. Rock remained the most consumed genre in many countries, including Canada, the US, and the UK, underscoring its enduring live appeal.
*   **Importance for the Industry:** The numerous comebacks and revivals witnessed in the 2010s were crucial for the health of both the recorded music industry and the touring industry, as well as for music as a whole, injecting vitality and revenue.

### The Enduring Power of Rock Giants

*   **Pre-Internet Era Dominance:** The largest rock bands globally in the 2010s were predominantly those who had established successful careers and garnered numerous hits *before* the internet significantly disrupted the music industry.
*   **Arena Fillers:** These established acts retained the ability to consistently fill arenas, amphitheaters, and even stadiums, a feat few new rock acts have managed since 2000, relatively speaking.
*   **Generational Support:** The continued success of these massive tours and significant album sales was largely fueled by Baby Boomer and Generation X audiences, who remained loyal consumers.
*   **Notable Comebacks:** Many "monsters of rock" from previous decades released new albums in the 2010s after various hiatuses, including Pearl Jam, Soundgarden, Radiohead, Green Day, Alice in Chains, AC/DC, Metallica, Nine Inch Nails, and David Bowie. Several of these, alongside others like U2, Linkin Park, Foo Fighters, Slipknot, Queens of the Stone Age, Fall Out Boy, Evanescence, Coldplay, Jack White, and Red Hot Chili Peppers, achieved number one records during the decade.

### Case Study: Tool's Epic Return

*   **Thirteen-Year Hiatus:** Tool's return was arguably the most anticipated comeback of the decade, marked by a 13-year, 5-month, and 3-day gap between their 2006 album "10,000 Days" and their 2019 release "Fear Inoculum."
*   **Pre-iPhone Era Context:** The significance of this delay can be understood by noting that when "10,000 Days" was released, the iPhone did not exist, Google had not yet acquired YouTube, and MySpace was still a dominant social media platform.
*   **Reasons for Delay:** The extended hiatus was attributed to a combination of factors: legal issues, band members' distractions with other projects, and a considerable amount of internal second-guessing within the band.
*   **"Fear Inoculum" Details:** The album, released on August 20, 2019, featured seven songs with a total runtime exceeding 79 minutes, achieving a number one album status. Its title track alone surpassed 10 minutes in length, exemplifying their progressive and intricate style.

### Psychedelic Rock Renaissance

*   **Historical Context:** Psychedelic Rock, originating in the 1960s, experienced a decline in the 1970s but never truly disappeared. Its elements surfaced in the Paisley Underground and Madchester scenes of the early 80s, the rave rock of the 1990s, and in the work of bands like The Flaming Lips, remaining a niche genre for a considerable period.
*   **2010s Resurgence:** The 2010s saw a significant resurgence of psych-rock, with artists like Ariel Pink, King Gizzard and the Lizard Wizard, Temples, The Black Angels, Unknown Mortal Orchestra, Animal Collective, War on Drugs, and Mac DeMarco leading the charge.
*   **Tame Impala's Breakthrough:** Kevin Parker's project, Tame Impala, became one of the most successful acts in this new wave. Their breakthrough album, "Lonerism," was entirely recorded in Parker's home studio and earned a nomination for Best Alternative Album at the 2012 Grammy Awards.

### Shoegaze: From Niche to Resurgence

*   **1990s Origins and Decline:** Shoegaze, characterized by its loud, fuzzy sound, had its primary moment in the early 1990s and appeared to be on the wane by the end of that decade.
*   **Thriving Underground:** Despite its decline in mainstream visibility, a dedicated underground of shoegaze adherents persisted. By the 2010s, this scene was thriving, evidenced by club nights, extensive social media activity, and the emergence of new, primarily British, record labels formed specifically to support the genre.
*   **Cross-Pollination and Evolution:** Shoegaze techniques were absorbed by other genres, leading to hybrid sounds such as "Blackgaze" (integrating Black Metal), "Art School Shoegaze" (from Japan), and elements incorporated into emo music.
*   **Key Artists and Works:** British band Engineers had been working with these sounds since 2003. Other notable acts contributing to this "semi-revival" included M83 (French group), The Pains of Being Pure at Heart, A Place to Bury Strangers, and School of Seven Bells. Montreal's BizNard Lakes released the album "Until Inaccess Impreceptible UFO" in April 2013, which was shortlisted for the Polaris Music Prize.

### Emo's Enduring Heartbeat

*   **Early 2000s Peak and Decline:** Emo had a significant moment in the early 2000s with bands like Dashboard Confessional, Saves the Day, Thursday, Story of the Year, Taking Back Sunday, and My Chemical Romance. However, by the early 2010s, it began to wane as fans matured and bands either broke up (Alexis on Fire, My Chemical Romance) or evolved away from their original emo sound (Fall Out Boy, Paramore, Panic! At The Disco).
*   **The Pause and the Revival:** This apparent decline proved to be a pause. By the mid-2010s, signs of a strong emo revival emerged. Alexis on Fire returned, and My Chemical Romance reunited by the end of the decade.
*   **New Generation and 90s Influence:** A new generation of groups began exploring the genre, potentially with a stronger 90s emo bent. The Connecticut band The World Is A Beautiful Place & I Am No Longer Afraid To Die exemplified this revival with their 2015 album "Harmlessness," featuring the song "Mental Health."

### Progressive Rock's Technical Evolution

*   **70s Origins and Punk Reaction:** Progressive Rock, born in the late 1960s and early 1970s, flourished with increasing complexity, virtuosity, and sophisticated recording techniques. It was later challenged by punk, which viewed it as bloated, inaccessible, elitist, and overly complicated.
*   **Math Rock Precursor (2000s):** The 2000s saw the rise of Math Rock, characterized by its complex, technically demanding, and difficult-to-dance-to nature, drawing inspiration from masters like Rush, King Crimson, and Yes.
*   **2010s Comeback and Djent:** The 2010s marked a progressive rock comeback, including the emergence of "Djent" (a previously discussed subgenre). This period saw bands pushing boundaries, with the use of eight or nine-string guitars becoming a signature of this "post-progressive" movement.
*   **Beyond a Defined Sound:** This new wave of progressive rock, while encompassing Tool's Math Rock approach, also integrated ideas from Indian metal and other diverse sources. It was less about a single defined sound and more about an attitude and a sophisticated approach to instrumentation and arrangements.
*   **Diverse Artists:** The "big tent" of 2010s progressive rock included acts like Biffy Clyro, Dance Gavin Dance, The Mars Volta, and Radiohead, who continued their experimental ways with albums like "King of Limbs" (2011) and "A Moon Shaped Pool" (2016).

### Heavy Metal's Reign: From Reunions to New Waves

*   **Classic Heavy Metal Success:** Established heavy bands like Soundgarden, Metallica, Iron Maiden, and AC/DC continued to enjoy renewed success.
*   **Guns N' Roses' Historic Reunion:** The reunion of "60% of Guns N' Roses" for the "Not In This Lifetime..." tour (2016-2019) was the biggest rock comeback of the decade. It grossed nearly $600 million USD over 158 shows, making it the fourth-most profitable tour of all time at that point.
*   **Thrash Metal's Persistent Presence:** While not reaching the heights of the 80s and 90s, thrash metal performed well. Metallica led the way with their album "Hardwired...to Self-Destruct" and a tour that generated almost $450 million USD. The other "Big Four" bands (Slayer, Megadeth, Anthrax) also released albums that reached the top 10. A thrash metal revival was observed with bands like Municipal Waste, Power Trip, and Warbringer, though they did not achieve the same level of success as the Big Four.
*   **Alternative Metal/Hard Rock's Evolution:** Alternative Metal, prominent in the 90s (Soundgarden, Alice in Chains), saw comebacks from older groups like Jane's Addiction, Biohazard, and Faith No More. Additionally, new, young bands emerged, clearly inspired by classic rock, such as Greta Van Fleet (known for drawing heavily from their parents' classic rock collections), Baltimore's Turnstile, and The Struts (a British band influenced by Queen, Aerosmith, Oasis, The Killers, and The Rolling Stones).

### Pop-Punk's Persistent Pulse

*   **Waves of Popularity:** Pop-punk experienced a brief surge in the 2010s, though it didn't match the popularity of its "first wave" in the 90s or "second wave" in the 2000s.
*   **Heritage Acts and Cult Following:** The genre never fully disappeared, kept alive by heritage acts like The Offspring, Linkin Park, Sum 41, and Simple Plan. A growing number of "culty bands" explored pop-punk styles, often incorporating harder edge or garage rock elements.
*   **Examples of New Talent:** Bands like Wavves, Bass Drum of Death, Ty Segall, and Toronto's PUP (who released three albums in the decade, including "Morbid Stuff" in 2019 with the song "Kids") contributed to this persistent scene.

### Punk: An Unkillable Ethic and Aesthetic

*   **Perpetual Existence:** Punk is characterized as a genre that "NEVER goes away," maintaining its attitude and aesthetic for nearly 50 years. Every generation since the 1970s has rediscovered and reinterpreted punk, making it their own.
*   **Enhanced DIY Ethic:** The "do-it-yourself" ethic central to punk has reportedly become even stronger due to technology democratizing and decentralizing music, compelling artists to work harder to be noticed amidst increased competition. There's also a perception that punk needs to be "torn down and rebuilt," reclaimed from "corporate punk" acts like Blink-182, Sum 41, and The Offspring.
*   **Mid-Decade Resurgence:** After a bleak start to the decade where pop-punk and emo had faltered, punk began to pick up in the mid-2010s. This resurgence may have been influenced by political situations like Donald Trump's presidency in the US and the Tories/Brexit in the UK.
*   **Garage Punk and Post-Punk Focus:** The mid-decade revival leaned towards Garage Punk and Post-Punk, building upon previous punk eras (70s style, 80s hardcore, 90s revival) rather than being a pure replication.
*   **Diverse Manifestations:** This broad movement included diverse approaches, from Laura Jane Grace and Against Me! to the riot grrrl sensibilities of the reformed Sleater-Kinney, the grinding sound of Japandroids, the intensity of Fucked Up, the stoner feel of Parquet Courts, and the working-class rage of Idles and Sleaford Mods.
*   **Beyond Sound:** Punk is highlighted as more than just a sound; it's an attitude, an outlook, an aesthetic, and a way of life. Fontaines D.C.'s album "Dogrel" is cited as a notable punk-ish record from the decade, praised for its presentation.

### Ska: The Four-Wave Phenomenon

*   **Constant Evolution:** Ska is another genre described as one that "never, ever goes away," instead ebbing, flowing, mutating, and evolving across decades.
*   **Four Distinct Waves:**
    1.  **First Wave (1950s Jamaica):** The originators, including Desmond Dekker, Toots and the Maytals, and The Skatalites.
    2.  **Second Wave (Late 1970s/Early 1980s England):** Characterized by its cross-breeding with punk music.
    3.  **Third Wave (1990s):** Featured bands like The Mighty Mighty Bosstones, Sublime, Reel Big Fish, Goldfinger, and Dance Hall Crashers.
    4.  **Fourth Wave (Late 2010s):** Emerged in the late 2010s and continued into the new decade. While not as explosive as past waves "yet," it is actively happening.
*   **Contemporary Examples:** Captain Ska achieved a top 5 single in the UK in 2017. In North America, the scene bubbled underground in cities like Los Angeles and Mexico City, with bands such as The Steady 45s, Lost Aggrotones, and The Interrupters. The Interrupters are particularly notable for becoming the first female-fronted ska band since No Doubt to have a major hit.

### Cross-Generational Appeal in Music

*   **Blurring Age Gaps:** A significant observation from the 2010s is that music has become "far more cross-generational than it's ever been."
*   **Shared Musical Love:** While generation gaps still exist, it has become increasingly common for parents and their children to share a love for the same music and artists, leading to diverse age ranges at concerts for bands like Metallica or Soundgarden.
