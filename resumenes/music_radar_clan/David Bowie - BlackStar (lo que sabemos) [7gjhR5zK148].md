## albums
**Black Star (2016) - Last Legacy Album** : This album is musically, lyrically, and structurally complex, seen by Bowie as a final, independent legacy, distinct from his previous work. It incorporates influences from electronic jazz, rap, hip hop, drum and bass, and other electronic music. The recording process was complicated, involving many musicians including a New York jazz group, and only Tony Visconti was aware of Bowie's terminal illness during this period. The album achieved massive commercial success, becoming the best-selling vinyl album of 2016, more than doubling the sales of Adel's top-selling album from the previous year. "No tiene nada que ver con lo que había hecho de David Bowie anteriormente, musicalmente, liricamente, estructuralmente es algo complicadísimo y yo creo que él quería dejarnos algo completamente independiente de lo que había hecho como último legado."

"Ahí ya teníamos las primeras pistas de que estaba trabajando en nuevo material, había alguna cosa nueva."

## curiosities
**Black Star Production Difficulties** : The production of Black Star was initially planned with three producers: David Bowie, Tony Visconti, and James Murphy. However, James Murphy, overwhelmed by other projects and unaware of Bowie's terminal illness, was often late or absent, didn't understand the material, and ultimately declined a full producer credit, contributing only specific rhythmic and sound elements. "Por desgracia la cosa salió muy mal."
**Terminal Illness Kept Secret** : During the recording of Black Star, David Bowie was suffering from a terminal illness, a fact known only to his long-time producer and friend, Tony Visconti. The other musicians and collaborators involved in the project were not aware of his condition, which contributed to difficulties in the recording process. "Nadie durante la grabación era consciente del estado de enfermedad terminal que tenía David Bowie, el único que lo sabía era Donnie Visconti porque era un gran amigo personal de David Bowie pero el resto no lo sabía."
**Bowie's Listening Habits During Black Star Recording** : David Bowie had a compulsive listening habit during the recording of Black Star. He became a huge follower of Börtshoff Canadá in his final years and listened to their music almost compulsively. He was also deeply fascinated by the hip hop trio Dead Grips and considered Kendrick Lamar's album *Tu Pimp a Butterfly* an "authentic masterpiece," which he listened to continuously as a major influence. "él se convierte un grandísimo seguidor de Börtshoff Canadá en los últimos años y escuchaba Börtshoff Canadá de manera casi compulsiva durante la grabación del Black Star."
**Black Star Posthumous Success and Initial Edition** : Black Star was released three days before David Bowie's death. The initial edition sold out almost instantly following his passing, leading to a period where the album was unavailable before a massive re-release. Copies from these first three or four days, available in transparent or black vinyl, are considered valuable collector's items. "una es los primeros días esos tres días que hubo entre el lanzamiento de la muerte de David Bowie, en el cual sale la primera edición del Black Star y la cual se agota casi instantáneamente tras su muerte."
**Physical Vinyl Artwork of Black Star** : The physical vinyl edition of Black Star is considered an artwork in itself, designed by a "great talent" in physical musical production. It hides secrets, such as reacting to ultraviolet rays and revealing a starry sky when exposed to sunlight, with the possibility of more undiscovered details. "el vinilo como pieza física también es una auténtica obra de arte en el igual que el disco porque nos deja un montón de secretos que hemos ido descubriendo lo largo de estos meses y de este año, sabemos que reacciona los rayos otra violeta, también sabemos que si lo pones a la luz del sol les aloncile estrellado y posiblemente nos quede algún detalle más por descubrir el largo de este tiempo."
**Posthumous Critical Re-evaluation** : The posthumous success of Black Star, reaching No. 1 on charts and receiving awards, significantly altered the perception of David Bowie's later career. Previously, albums like *Hitin*, *Hours*, and *Reality* had faced criticism regarding their musical quality, but Black Star's impact led to a re-evaluation, solidifying his final work as a significant artistic legacy. "Resulta triste que a David Bowie le añandado el número 1 en todas las listas y le añandado todos estos premios postumos cuando los últimos albumes de la carrera de David Bowie sobre todo los últimos tres, cuatro albumes, el hitin, el hours, el reality, habían sido un tanto cuestionado por su calidad musical."
**Black Star as an Unheard Artwork** : Despite its massive sales, especially posthumously, many buyers of Black Star may not actively listen to the album because of its complex art-jazz nature. It's suggested that many purchased it out of affection or as a farewell to the artist, rather than for regular listening, perhaps only engaging with a song like "Lazarus." "Resulta un tanto triste que mucha gente se ha comprado y no lo vaya a escuchar operativamente, no le escucha, al final es un disco de Arjas muy complejo que contrasta mucho con las ventas masivas que tiene."
**Scarcity of Information on Black Star** : The details surrounding Black Star's creation are sparse, largely due to David Bowie's death just three days after its release, his reclusive nature in his final period, and the fact that most collaborators were unaware of his terminal illness, limiting shared information. "la muerte de David Bowie fue tres días después de su lanzamiento, Tony Bizkonti tampoco nos dice mucho, fue una etapa en la que le estaba bastante recluido, no compartía mucha información."

## curiosities
**Rejected Producer Role for Black Star** : James Murphy was initially slated to be a co-producer for David Bowie's Black Star alongside Bowie and Tony Visconti. However, due to his intense involvement in numerous other projects—including his own record label, collaborations, productions, and event organization—he was unable to dedicate sufficient time, often being late or absent from sessions and struggling to understand Bowie's experimental material. He ultimately declined full producer credit, contributing only specific rhythmic and sound elements. "Finalmente, James Murphy terminó rechazando el trabajo como productor colaborador para el Black Star, él sí que trabajo en el álbum, haciendo algunas bases de sonidos de ritmos, poniendo algunas ideas, ayudando a producir alguna cosa pero su trabajo no dejo de ser un montón de cosas puntuales que se entremezclan en el disco."
**Regret Over Black Star Production** : James Murphy, unaware of David Bowie's terminal illness at the time of Black Star's recording, later expressed profound regret for not fully committing to the album's production. He has stated in interviews that it was the "biggest mistake of his life" not to have paid more attention to David Bowie's request. "es algo que hoy en día en sus entrevistas dice que se revendirá toda su vida, dice que había sido el mayor error de su vida no haber hecho más caso a David Bowie."
