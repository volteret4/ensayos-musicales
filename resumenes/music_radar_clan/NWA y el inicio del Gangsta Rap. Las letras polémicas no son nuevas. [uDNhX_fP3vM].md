## albums
**Straight, Abra Kampton (1988) - Defining Rap Album** : This album, released in 1988, marked a pivotal moment in the timeline of rap, leaving American society quite disoriented. Formed out of necessity in the mid-80s due to the extensive Afro-American hip hop scene, the band broke a generational gap and established themselves as absolute leaders of the "gangsta movement." The album is characterized by its rage, aggression, and violence, serving as a therapeutic reflection of the harsh realities lived in Afro-American suburbs. It explicitly discusses racist police behavior, indiscriminate arrests, and police violence, with some band members having personally suffered arbitrary detentions. It also explores the crude and direct relationship of Afro-American suburbs with drugs, detailing both those who profited and those who were devastated and victimized. The album created a "convulsion" in the United States, resonating deeply with many Afro-American youths who identified more with its discourse than with the more restrained messages of older generations. Its release caused significant discomfort, even for the police, due to its direct attacks on law enforcement and calls for popular uprising. It is considered an important, politically complex album and a faithful representation of Afro-American society in the late 80s. "Y aparecieron en 1988 con el grandísimo album Straight, Abra Kampton, que marcaba un antes y un después en la línea temporal del rap y que dejaba la sociedad a América nabastante descolocada."
**nigas por life (1991) - Controversial Second Album** : Released in 1991, this album was a commercial success and had significant repercussions, but it was also highly questionable. It escalated its lyrical content beyond their first album, moving past police brutality and drug dealing to include highly problematic discourses such as sexual violence, mistreatment of women, homophobic attitudes, and even racist sentiments among the band members themselves. The band actively contributed to the banalization of the words "nigga" and "bitch" on this album. Following its release, the band eventually dissolved due to numerous internal conflicts and significant questioning from within Afro-American culture regarding the controversial nature of its content. "tendríamos un segundo albún de la banda en 1991, un segundo albún que sería nigas por life, que por desgracia a pesar de que también fue un buen buen éxito, a pesar de que tuvo mucha repercusión, fue un disco más que cuestionado y sin realmente más que cuestionable."

## curiosities
**Band Formation and Instability** : Ni Ganswizatitude was characterized as a highly unstable band that never truly achieved cohesion, primarily due to poor relationships among its members. Their formation in the mid-80s was driven by necessity, as many rappers were vying for attention in the fervent and extensive Afro-American hip hop scene. Group formation was a common strategy for artists to stand out and increase their collective strength. "Los miembros de Ni Ganswizatitude se juntaron un poco por necesidad porque de aquella es bueno, a mitad de los 80, cuando empiezan a trabajar ellos, había un montón de raperos intentando destacarles."
**Gangsta Rap Pioneers** : Ni Ganswizatitude is widely considered the absolute fathers of the "gangster movement" and "gangster rap," which emerged in the late 1980s. While there's a debate about whether they were the very first, they are unequivocally recognized as the progenitors of "hardcore gangsta rap." They effectively broke a generational gap, establishing themselves as the absolute leaders of this movement and marking a significant "before and after" in the timeline of rap lyrics. "Si nos queremos ir a los orígenes de todo esto, muy posiblemente nos tengamos que ir a Ni Ganswizatitude, padres absolutos, el movimiento gangster, del gangster rap que nace a finales de los 80."
**Ephemeral Myth and Suburban Pride** : Ni Ganswizatitude achieved a mythical status, though it was an ephemeral one given the band's relatively short lifespan. They championed a philosophy of pride associated with belonging to suburbs that were characterized by illegal, macabre, or otherwise questionable attitudes, effectively transforming this affiliation into a matter of honor and self-respect. "Ellos se convirtieron en un mito, aunque un mito tanto fímero porque la banda duraría poco tiempo, y empezaron esa filosofía de sentirse orgullosos, don suburbio lleno de actitudes ilegales o de actitudes macabras o de actitudes cuestionables."
**Post-Album Controversies and Dissolution** : Immediately after the release of their first album, Ni Ganswizatitude encountered substantial problems, including scrutiny from the FBI and organized campaigns by Republican politicians who vocally opposed the album's message, characteristic of controversial works. Internally, the band suffered severe disputes over financial rights, particularly concerning the main lyricists' compensation, which ultimately led to Sirren's departure. The band eventually dissolved after their second album due to these unresolved internal conflicts and significant questioning from within the Afro-American community regarding their controversial lyrical content. "Justo después de este disco, bueno, tuvieron un montón de problemas desde el FBI que entonces juraron el disco movimiento, es político republicano, en políticos que hicieron campaña contra el disco, bueno, estas peleas que siempre hay cuando algo es polémico."
**Banalization of Words** : Ni Ganswizatitude played an active role in the banalization of two significant words: "nigga" and "bitch." The trivialization of "nigga," despite its consistently negative historical connotations, is suggested to have possibly originated from their song "niga niga." The word "bitch" was used with such extensive frequency in their second album, "nigas por life," that the band is likely a primary contributor to its widespread, casual usage. "Nigas Wizzatitude participaron activamente en la banalización de todos grandes palabras, por un lado la palabra niga, aunque la palabra niga siempre se ha usado con connotaciones muy negativas, posiblemente la banalización de la palabra niga venga, de la canción niga niga, de los nigas Wizzatitude, y la segunda es la banalización de la palabra pitch, o sea la palabra pitch en el segundo álbum de niga Wizzatitude sale tantas veces que posiblemente sean los protagonistas y culpables."

# genre - Rap
## curiosities
**Problematic Lyrical Content** : A recurring and much-discussed theme in contemporary rap, frequently highlighted in musical journalism, television, and magazines, is the presence of problematic lyrics that address racism, drugs, illegal dealings, violence, and misogyny. This content, particularly in current rap, is not viewed as a new phenomenon but rather as a continuation of themes that have existed throughout the history of the genre. "un tema recurrente que tenemos siempre calamos en la música actual, o que veis las revistas o que salen la televisión que salen el periodismo musical, entonces estas cosas, son las letras, ciertas letras, racistas que hablan de drogas, que hablan de chanchullos, que hablan de violencia, que hablan de misoginia, en el rap actual, pero sobre todo en el rap actual."
**Generational Shift and Identity** : The emergence of Ni Ganswizatitude marked a significant generational shift within rap music. While earlier rap generations may have presented more restrained narratives, this new generation aimed to directly reflect the harsh realities in Afro-American suburbs. Many Afro-American youths felt a growing disconnection from millionaire rappers, whom they perceived as having lost touch with their community, prompting them to embrace more combative and realistic expressions of rap. "muchas juventudes afroamericanas se sentían mucho más identificados con el discurso de nigas huidatitude, como el discurso que tenían unas generaciones más mayores, que eran mucho más comedias en su discurso."
**White American Society's Reaction** : The rise of a more violent and combative rap generation, exemplified by artists directly attacking the police and advocating for popular uprising, intensified the existing tensions between white American society and rap music. This escalation occurred even as dominant American society appeared to be beginning to assimilate and show a degree of respect for rap music, generating significant discomfort, including within law enforcement. "Cuando parecía que la sociedad americana dominante empezaba a similar y hasta cierto modo respetar la música rap apareció una nueva generación mucho más violenta, mucho más combativa, que atacaba directamente contra la policía, que atacaba directamente llamando a la sublevación popular generó bastante incómodida, incluso en la propia policía."
