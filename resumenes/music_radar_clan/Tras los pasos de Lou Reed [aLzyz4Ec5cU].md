## albums
**Lou Reed (1972) - Debut Solo Album** : This album was released very quickly after the Velvet Underground's separation, heavily utilizing material that had been discarded by the Velvet Underground. Many of its themes also appear in unreleased Velvet Underground editions, with both VU versions and Lou Reed's solo versions of the exact same songs existing. While it received a relatively acceptable critical reception, it wasn't considered incredible and did not sell well, having little impact except for catching the attention of David Bowie. "Ese albún en solitario sale muy rápido porque si usaría muchísimo del material que había quedado desechado de la Belbet Underground."

**Transformer (1972) - Second Solo Album** : Released merely five months after his debut album, this is considered the most important album of Lou Reed's career. It was recorded very quickly, partly because it contained at least four songs that had already been composed and recorded during the Velvet Underground era. The album was funded by David Bowie and produced by Bowie himself, alongside Mick Ronson, Bowie's guitarist. "El Transformer, con diferencia de algún más importante de su carrera."

**Transformer (1972) - Thematic Elements** : The album's themes encompass not only discarded Velvet Underground recordings but also Lou Reed's personal experiences at Andy Warhol's Factory, recounting "macabre stories" from that period. Despite maintaining the same controversial, explicit, and censorable themes as his first album, it achieved "incredible success," largely attributed to the boost provided by David Bowie's involvement. This success officially launched Lou Reed's solo career. "Todo el disco nos viene a contar esas macabras historias que tenía Luric sobre lo que se cocian en la factoria."

**Berlin (1973) - Third Solo Album** : This album was released just a few months after the "Transformer" tour concluded and was initially met with widespread critical condemnation, marking a "great fall" for Lou Reed. It was a highly complex conceptual album, narrating the harrowing story of a drug-addicted couple living through their miseries in New York City, blending song and narrative. The swift composition period (less than a year after "Transformer," including an 8-month tour) likely contributed to its initial failure, as it was the first time Lou Reed had to create 100% new material without relying on Velvet Underground leftovers. "Realmente Berling era un disco muy complejo porque era una especie de algún conceptual que venía como anarrar una historia, venía a contarnos la historia de una pareja de droga dictos que vivían toda su vida o un poco todas sus penurias por la ciudad de Nueva York."

**Berlin (1973) - Critical Re-evaluation** : Lou Reed lacked strong instrumental knowledge for such a complex album and refused external help from other musicians or professional producers, which could have compensated for his deficiencies. While the album was "totally destroyed by critics" upon release, it has since been re-evaluated, with modern reviews from publications like Rolling Stone and Pitchfork being significantly more positive than their original scathing assessments. The album has gained "much context" and "charisma" within music culture over time. "Al final el disco acababa totalmente destrozado por la crítica."

**Berlin (1973) - Storytelling Style** : The album exemplifies Lou Reed's unique method of storytelling in his songs, where each track functions like a "small novel" or a "small, self-contained, everyday story" presented with novelistic depth. "Berlin" specifically defines his lyrical approach, concentrating a concise, novel-like narrative into a three-minute song. "Y es que al final yo creo que en el álbum Berling de Lurry, tenemos esa manera de contar historias que tenían Lurry en sus canciones, que es casi como que cada canción de Lurry es como una pequeña novela o una pequeña historia cerrada, como muy cotidiana, pero contada como muy de novela."

## songs
**Kill Your Sons (Unknown Year)** : This song openly addresses Lou Reed's childhood experiences with psychiatric treatment involving electroshock therapy. It conveys the deep resentment he harbored towards his family, specifically his parents, for subjecting him to this treatment. "De hecho tenemos esa canción de Kee-Jer-Sons en la que habla abiertamente de esa etapa en la que se le trató en psiquiátrico y el rencor que estiene hacia toda su familia por eso, no a su hermana pero sea sus padres."

**Walk on the Wild Side (1972)** : Featured on the album "Transformer," this significant song serves as a list of notable characters from Andy Warhol's Factory. It delves into their "bad influence" or "bad lives," referencing them by their specific names and alluding to their misfortunes or misbehavior. The song may also reflect Lou Reed's post-Velvet Underground resentment and "true hatred" towards Andy Warhol. "De hecho, tenemos esa gran canción que era Walk on the White Side, de Luric que viene a hablar es una lista de todos esos grandes personajes de la factoria y un poco de la mala influencia o de la mala vida que llevaban todos ellos."

## curiosities
**Rock and Roll Hall of Fame Induction** : Lou Reed holds the distinction of being one of the few individuals inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame twice. He was recognized once as a member of the Velvet Underground and a second time for his solo career. "A pesar de que es una de las pocas personas que tenemos que estar en el hall de la fama dos veces, una como velván de Raúm y otra como él miembro de soligario."

**Childhood Psychiatric Treatment** : As a child, Lou Reed suffered from panic attacks, depressive episodes, and certain "asexual" behaviors. These issues led to him being treated in a psychiatric institution with electroshock therapy, an experience that left a lasting impact. He harbored significant resentment towards his family, particularly his parents, for their decision to subject him to this treatment. "Y de niño pues bueno hablábamos el otro día en el canal de Facebook un poco sobre su infancia y es que el de niño tenía pequeños ataques hepánicos, ataques depresivos, ciertas conductas. A unos sexuales por las que acabo siendo tratado en un psiquiátrico con terapia de electro-shox, algo verdaderamente impactante."

**Post-Velvet Underground Financial Struggles** : Following the breakup of the Velvet Underground, Lou Reed, like the other members, earned no money and sold very few records. This financial hardship led him to move back in with his parents and take a job as a typist or transcriber in his father's accounting firm. It was from this challenging situation, without many resources, that he re-signed with RCA to pursue his solo career. "Y en esa situación en la que Luric vuelve a casa de sus padres sin dinero y trabaja cómod, casi transcriptor o mecanógrafo, en la agencia de contabilidad de su padre, Luric vuelve a firmar por la RCA para seguir su carrera en solitario, sin ningún tipo casi de recursos."

**Estrangement and Reconciliation with David Bowie** : Immediately after the recording of "Transformer," Lou Reed became intensely estranged from David Bowie, and they ceased speaking. Although they had been close friends with a great relationship, the precise reason for their sudden animosity remains unknown to this day, as neither offered a clear explanation. Years later, they did reconcile and established a cordial relationship, even collaborating on some projects, but their friendship never returned to its original state. "Lo curioso es que justo tras la grabación pues Luric se enemistaba enormemente con David Bowie y dejaban de hablarse."

**Controversial "Transformer" Tour** : The tour supporting the album "Transformer" was inherently controversial because Lou Reed, while a creative individual and a great composer, was not considered a skilled musician. He hired an orchestra for his concerts that he barely knew and had not rehearsed with, which generated significant criticism for the poor quality of his performances. Mid-tour, he dismissed these musicians and hired new ones, resulting in the second part of the "Transformer" tour achieving considerable success and featuring "great concerts." "La gira del Transformer ya fue polémica de por sí porque al final Luric no era un gran músico, era una persona muy creativa, un gran compositor pero no era un gran músico."

**Career Peak and Decline** : While Lou Reed's career would subsequently experience both highs and lows, it never again reached the peak of popularity that it achieved with the album "Transformer." "Luego en la carrera de Lurry tendría sus altos y sus bajos, pero yo creo que ya no volvería a ser la misma como el gran pico de popularidad que tuvo con el Transformer."

"En su día por ejemplo, la Rollin' Stone le dio unos sobre 5 al Berling. Hoy ha re hecho su crítica hace unos años y hoy tiene 4 de 5 o 4,5 sobre 5, si no os recuerdo mal."
