# artist - Héroes del Silencio

## curiosities
**Early Discrepancies (1993)** : The band's eventual separation began earlier than generally perceived, with issues surfacing around their 1993 album, "El Espíritu del Vino." Interviews from that period already revealed stylistic discrepancies among the members, indicating internal problems. "Porque ya con el espíritu del vino, al que el disco que había sacado en 1993, la cosa ya no iba bien del todo."
**Exhausting "El Espíritu del Vino" Tour** : The tour supporting "El Espíritu del Vino" was exceptionally long and contentious, causing the band members to become thoroughly jaded and leading to numerous disputes, especially during their months-long touring in South America. "La gira del espíritu del vino fue como muy, muy larga y como muy con bússa."
**Post-Tour Crisis** : Following the extensive "El Espíritu del Vino" tour, the band entered a period of crisis marked by significant disagreements among members regarding the future creative direction of Héroes del Silencio. "Y después justo de esa larguísima gira, la banda tiene ahí como ese momento de crisis en el que..."
**Immense Spanish Pressure** : Héroes del Silencio faced an "insane" level of pressure in Spain, and the Spanish-speaking world generally, to maintain and surpass their quality. They were so important and acclaimed in Spain that this pressure sometimes became unhealthy. "Sí que es verdad que posiblemente tenía una presión insana dentro de España, en el mundo en español es general, pero sobre todo en España hacía la calidad y la superación."
**National Commotion from Separation (1995)** : The band's separation in 1995, which occurred mid-tour for their "Avalancha" album, caused a national commotion in Spain. The news was widely reported, and people openly cried, treating it as a public spectacle. "Pero la verdad es que fue como una comocción nacional aquí en España cuando se paran los seres del silencio."
**Lingering Public Grief (1997)** : Even by 1997, when Bunbury released his first solo album, the public was still mourning the separation of Héroes del Silencio and had not fully come to terms with it. Their previous album, "Avalancha," was still on sales charts in Spain at the time. "Porque salía en 1997, la gente estuvo mucho tiempo llorando como la separación del silencio, como que como que aún no lo había sumido."
**South American Legacy (1996)** : In the mid-90s, the band's legacy remained active in South America, with a release (referred to ambiguously as "entibian plaque") coming out in 1996, indicating that fans were still processing their breakup and the band's output was still relevant. "la mitad de suroamérica había sacado el entibian plaque en el 96."

## albums
**El Espíritu del Vino (1993)** : This album marked the beginning of internal strife within the band, with interviews from the time already hinting at stylistic disagreements among members, setting the stage for their eventual separation. "Porque ya con el espíritu del vino, al que el disco que había sacado en 1993, la cosa ya no iba bien del todo."
**Avalancha (1995)** : Released after a period of crisis following the "El Espíritu del Vino" tour, "Avalancha" is considered a great album by the speaker, although some purists of Héroes del Silencio reportedly disliked it. The band famously separated in the middle of this album's tour. "Avalancha, la verdad es que es un discazo."

## albums
**Radical Sonora (1997)** : Bunbury's first solo album, released just two years after Héroes del Silencio's breakup. It was recorded "by all accounts" in London with expensive production and sound engineers, incorporating strong electronic influences and "canned bases," marking a significant stylistic departure from his previous work. "El disco además, se había hecho como por todo lo alto, sabía ir a grabarlo a Londres, sabía coger a gente de producción y a gente para el trabajo, como ingenieros de su unido muy caro, así todo como muy grandioso, tenía como muchas influencias electrónicas, si de bases enlatadas, tenía como muchas cosas ahí metidas, era como muy distinto."
**Pequeño (1999)** : Described as Bunbury's "best solo album" by the speaker, this album was a significant success both critically and commercially, marking a turning point in his career. It was created from a place of sadness and a feeling of abandonment by his fans after the rejection of his previous work. It was recorded humbly in a small Spanish studio, departing from the grand productions of his previous work, and incorporated strong Mediterranean and Spanish influences. "dentro de esa tristeza y dentro de ese sentimiento de incompreensión y de abandono, por la comunidad de fans que tiene Bumbur y, en hace el grandísimo disco de pequeño."
**Pequeño Cabaret Ambulante (Year after Pequeño, post-1999)** : This live album was recorded in Mexico during the extensive and successful tour supporting Bunbury's "Pequeño" album. Its recording in Mexico further solidified Bunbury's special relationship with the country and Latin America. "no solo se vendió un montón, sino que además, si hizo la arquísima girada que acabó grabada, tenemos una grabación que se salió en disco con un factor año, siguiente el pequeño cabaretambulante, que ha grabado en México."

## curiosities
**Solo Material Development (Pre-1997)** : Bunbury had already been working on his own musical material and compositions while still a member of Héroes del Silencio, which ultimately did not fit the band's sound and would later form the basis for his solo career. "Bumbury ya sabemos, había de hoy que ya había empezado a trabajar en material propio por su cuenta cuando aún estaban a los del silencio."
**Post-Héroes del Silencio Transition** : The period immediately following the breakup of Héroes del Silencio was a very difficult time for Bunbury as he transitioned into his solo career, marked by a personal catarsis. "Empezó un poco la carrera separado de Bumbury y fue como una etapa muy difícil."
**"Radical Sonora" as a "Disaster" (1997)** : His debut solo album, "Radical Sonora," was largely considered a "disaster" upon release, primarily due to poor timing and the public's lingering grief over Héroes del Silencio's separation, rather than its inherent quality. "Y ese disco fue el grande desastre de la carrera de Bumbury y creo que desgraciadamente no por su calidad."
**Abrupt Image and Sound Change (1997)** : For "Radical Sonora," Bunbury made a drastic change to his image, cutting his iconic long hair short and adopting a new "electro-ónico" clothing style, which contributed to the perceived abruptness of his new musical direction after years of classic rock success. "Había cambiado como su forma de vestir, como ese especie de electro-ónico raro, era un poco curiosa su imagen."
**Public Rejection of New Sound (1997)** : The audience struggled to connect with "Radical Sonora" due to its stark difference from Héroes del Silencio's sound and the ongoing public mourning for the band. The album was seen as "too combative" for the emotional state of many listeners at the time. "entre la gente que estaba todavía llorando la muerte es el silencio, y que era como demasiado diferente, como creo que la gente no supon cajar bien ese disco."
**Frustration and Thoughts of Quitting (Late 1990s)** : Bunbury experienced significant anger, frustration, and overwhelming pressure as his solo creative vision was rejected by the public, to the point where he seriously considered abandoning his music career entirely. "En las entrevistas Bumbur y Recuerda esa tapa como muy amarga, fue entre una cura de humildad, una comprensión, hasta tal punto que en algunos momentos nos ha dicho que se planteó muy seriamente ya al dejar su carrera, visto que no podía hacer lo que quería, o que el público no le acompañaba lo que quería, que posiblemente ya iba a acabar de alejando su carrera creativa."
**Fan Demands for Héroes del Silencio Songs** : During his early solo concerts supporting "Radical Sonora," audiences would sometimes shout out demands for Héroes del Silencio songs, even specific albums like "Avalancha," creating an embarrassing situation for Bunbury as he tried to establish his new identity. "a la gente grita, va a ser el silencio, cantas, acciones y avalanchas, era honestamente un poco bochornoso por un artista que estaba empezando una nueva tapa de su carrera."
**Emotional Exhaustion and Introspection (Post-1997)** : After the difficult reception of "Radical Sonora," Bunbury felt emotionally fatigued, sad, and deeply disappointed with the world, leading him to channel these feelings into highly intimate and personal lyrics for his subsequent work, particularly "Pequeño." "Yo creo que la palabra que mejor lo define posiblemente se ha decepcionado y empieza a trabajar, pues, como en letras como muy intimistas, como muy propias."
**Humble Recording of "Pequeño"** : For the album "Pequeño," Bunbury deliberately chose a humble recording approach, abandoning expensive London studios and high-profile engineers in favor of a small studio in Spain, focusing on the music without grand pretenses. "Dejas esos estudios del hondre, dejas esas cosas, hay que tan grandilo puentes, deja todas esas gente tan importante para centrarse a un pequeño estudio de grabación en España y hacerlos en grandes pretensiones."
**"Pequeño" as a Career Turning Point** : The success of "Pequeño" marked a crucial inflection point in Bunbury's career, both professionally and emotionally. It helped him regain self-confidence and artistic freedom, and from then on, his audience consistently supported his diverse and often unexpected projects. "Yo creo que este al mundo pequeño marca un punto de inflexión en la carrera de Bumbury, pero también un punto de inflexión emocional dentro de la carrera de Bumbury, porque yo creo que gracias a ese album, él como que recupera toda su confianza en sí mismo, recupera esa libertad de poder hacer lo que quiera."
**Special Relationship with Mexico** : Bunbury has fostered a profound and special relationship with Latin America, especially Mexico, which was solidified during the tour for "Pequeño." He subsequently spent significant time in Mexico, absorbing influences from Mexican artists and culture, which he proudly incorporated into his later work and which distinguished his artistic growth from many other Spanish artists. "Creo que en pequeño es cuando, en esa girada del pequeño, creo que es cuando más se consuma, esa relación de amor que tiene Bumbury con su ramérica, pero en especial con México."
