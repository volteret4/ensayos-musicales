# instrument - Vinyl Record

## curiosities
**Standard Playback Speeds** : Vinyl records are commonly played at various standard speeds, with 33 revolutions per minute (RPM) being the most prevalent. Other standard speeds include 45 RPM and the historical 78 RPM, each offering different characteristics concerning sound quality and storage capacity. "Tenemos varios estandas de reproducción, el más común es el de 33 revoluciones por minuto, pero tenemos varios más."

**Sound Quality and Groove Length at 33 RPM** : At 33 revolutions per minute, a vinyl record turns slightly more than half a rotation (approximately 0.55 turns) in one second. This means that one second of sound occupies a groove length of just over half a meter (more than 50 centimeters) on the record. This speed is chosen to balance sound quality with storage capacity. "En un segundo el plato va a girar poco más de media vuelta, una acero como a 55 vueltas... un segundo de sonido ocupa algo más de medio metro."

**Sound Quality and Groove Length at 45 RPM** : When played at 45 revolutions per minute, a vinyl record completes three-quarters of a turn (approximately 0.75 turns) in one second. This increased rotational speed results in a longer groove segment for each second of sound, specifically 71 centimeters. The greater physical space allocated to the sound wave at 45 RPM allows for higher precision and, consequently, better sound quality compared to 33 RPM. "A 45 revoluciones por minuto va a hacer 3 cuartas partes de vuelta en un segundo... recorre 71 centímetros. Precisión tiene la onda de sonido por lo que tenemos más calidad."

**Sound Quality and Groove Length at 78 RPM** : Historically, some records were played at 78 revolutions per minute. At this speed, a single second of music occupies more than 1.20 meters of the groove. This significant amount of physical space dedicated to the sound wave at 78 RPM translates to an "incredible quality of sound." However, this high quality comes at the expense of storage capacity, as such a long groove per second limits the total amount of music that can be stored on the record. "Un disco que suena a 78 revoluciones por minuto un solo segundo de música ocupa más de un metro con 20... tenemos una increíble calidad de sonido."

**LPs and 33 RPM** : The majority of Long Play (LP) records are recorded at 33 revolutions per minute. This speed is favored for LPs because it allows for a greater storage capacity, enabling more than an hour of sound to be recorded across both sides of the disc. This format prioritizes the quantity of music over the maximum possible sound fidelity, making it suitable for full-length albums. "La mayoría de LP de la regalación está en 33 revoluciones por minuto, pudiendo grabar más de una hora de sonido en las dos caras."

**Singles and Maxi-Singles at 45 RPM** : Singles and maxi-singles are generally produced to be played at 45 revolutions per minute. Although they offer less total sound content compared to LPs, the faster playback speed means each second of sound is allocated more physical groove space, resulting in higher fidelity. This format prioritizes enhanced sound quality for a shorter duration of music. "Los singles o maxis singles generalmente a 45 revoluciones por minuto, tenemos la misma capacidad para menos cantidad de sonido puede reproducirse más de prisa."

**Varying Sound Quality Across a Record** : A peculiar physical reality of vinyl records is that the sound quality is not constant from beginning to end. The first minute of sound, which is represented by the outermost and largest groove on the record, inherently possesses higher quality than the last minute of sound, which corresponds to the innermost and smallest groove. This phenomenon is a direct consequence of the physical characteristics of the spiral groove and the playback mechanism. "El primer sonido o el primer minuto de sonido que tiene un disco de vinilo tiene más calidad que el último segundo que el último minuto que tiene el mismo disco de vinilo?"

**Spiral Groove Structure** : A vinyl record's groove is a continuous spiral that progressively becomes smaller in diameter as it approaches the center of the disc. This fundamental physical design dictates how the information is encoded and retrieved, leading to variations in the linear speed at which the needle traverses the groove. "El disco de vinilo no es más que un sur con espiral que cada vez se hace más pequeño."

**Groove Surface Area Variation** : Due to the diminishing diameter of the spiral groove, the physical space occupied by a minute of sound varies significantly across a vinyl record. The first minute of sound, located in the outer part of the record, occupies a surface area roughly twice as large as the last minute of sound, which is packed into the inner part of the record. This difference in physical space directly correlates to the amount of detail and fidelity that can be preserved for the audio signal. "El primer minuto de sonido vemos que ocupa toda esta superficie. Sin embargo si cogemos el último minuto de sonido que tiene el mismo disco de vinilo, vemos que ocupa toda esta superficie que es exactamente la mitad."

**Progressive Compression Towards Record End** : As a vinyl record plays from the outer edge towards the center, it undergoes a progressive compression of the audio information. This occurs because the diminishing linear speed of the needle, coupled with the reduction in available groove surface area, means that the same amount of audio information must be packed into half the physical space towards the end of the record compared to the beginning. This physical compression inevitably affects the sound quality. "El disco de vinilo, según basonando va generando una progresiva compresión que hace que cuando llega al final del disco, el disco este sonando a la mitad de velocidad y por tanto recorriendo en la mitad de superficie que cuando empezaba a sonar."

**Perceptibility of Quality Variation** : While it is a physical reality that the first minute of sound on any vinyl record has higher quality than the last minute, this difference is often "hardly perceptible or directly not perceptible" to the human ear. Despite the scientific explanation behind this phenomenon, its practical impact on the listening experience might be minimal for most listeners. "Aunque esto, la verdad es que apenas es percibible o directamente no es perceptible."

# instrument - Turntable

## curiosities
**Constant Angular Velocity Playback** : A turntable operates by rotating the vinyl record at a constant angular velocity, meaning it maintains a fixed number of revolutions per minute (RPM) regardless of the needle's position on the record. This constant rotational speed is fundamental to the playback process, but it introduces variations in other physical parameters during sound reproduction. "El plato se mueve a una velocidad de vueltas constantes."

**Needle Linear Velocity Variation** : While a turntable maintains a constant angular velocity, the linear speed at which the playback needle travels along the record groove is not constant. When the needle is on the outermost groove (e.g., approximately 30 cm in diameter), its linear speed can be around 30 meters per minute. As it moves towards the innermost groove (e.g., approximately 15 cm in diameter) near the end of the record, the linear speed decreases significantly, potentially to about 15 meters per minute. This reduction in linear speed affects the amount of groove length available to encode audio information per unit of time, impacting sound quality. "La aguja se está moviendo una velocidad inicial de unos 30 metros por minuto mientras que en el de la derecha a punto de acabar el disco de vinilo, el aguja se está moviendo solo a 15 metros por minuto."

# curiosity

**Trade-off: Storage Capacity vs. Sound Quality** : The selection of a playback speed for vinyl records represents a fundamental trade-off between the amount of music that can be stored and the fidelity of the sound. Higher playback speeds (like 45 RPM or 78 RPM) allocate more physical groove space per second of audio, leading to superior sound quality due to increased precision in the sound wave. However, this higher quality comes at the cost of reduced storage capacity on the disc. Conversely, lower speeds (like 33 RPM) allow for significantly more music to be stored, sacrificing some potential sound quality for extended playback time. "La velocidad de reproducción es una compleja lucha entre la capacidad de almacenamiento y la calidad del sonido que almacenamos, de manera que a más velocidad y más calidad de sonido tendremos, pero por desgracia menos capacidad de almacenamiento tendremos."
