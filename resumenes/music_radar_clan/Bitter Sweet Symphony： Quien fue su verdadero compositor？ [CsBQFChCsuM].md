# artist - The Verve

## members
Richard Ashcroft

## songs
**Bitter Sweet Symphony (1997)** : This song is a very complex composition, featuring 50 distinct tracks. One track incorporates the famous five notes from an instrumental version of The Rolling Stones' "The Last Time" by Andrew Loog Oldham, for which The Verve had sought and received permission. The remaining 49 tracks were composed entirely by The Verve, and its lyrics were written by Richard Ashcroft. Despite this extensive original composition, the song became the subject of a highly complicated and controversial legal battle regarding its authorship and rights, eventually leading to The Verve losing all control over it. It achieved intergenerational success and generated significant income. The song was nominated for a Grammy for "Best Song of the Year," leading to Richard Ashcroft, Mick Jagger, and Keith Richards being nominated as composers, despite the complex legal situation. "Vitex y simple, es una canción muy compleja, tiene las 50 pistas de música de las cuales por un lado tenemos una pista con los 5 notas famosas de la canción instrumental de los Rolling Stones pero tenemos otras 49 pistas de sonido que fueron compuestas por de ver y luego tenemos la letra que es completamente escrita por Richard Ascro."

## curiosities
**Permission for Instrumental Sample** : The Verve had originally requested and received permission from Andrew Loog Oldham to use his instrumental verse, specifically "5 notas de manera repetitiva," as a base for their song "Bitter Sweet Symphony." They then developed a completely new song around this element. This initial agreement was later overshadowed by further legal disputes as the song gained immense popularity and profitability. "ellos originalmente ya habían pedido por miso a Adam Coleman para usar su verso instrumental y él es aviado el visto bueno, había solicitado a 5 notas de manera repetitiva de como base de la canción y realmente ellos hicieron una canción completamente nueva."
**Loss of Song Rights** : The Verve faced immense legal pressure and high costs due to lawsuits initiated by Allen Klein regarding "Bitter Sweet Symphony." Despite having initially secured permission for the sampled portion, the escalating legal battles and their financial strain led The Verve to agree to an extrajudicial settlement, resulting in Allen Klein acquiring 100% of the song's rights. This agreement, while surrendering all control, allowed them to avoid potentially ruinous indemnification costs. "perderían todo derecho y control sobre la canción pero al menos no tenía que indemnizarlo y no tenía que pasar años gastando injecentes candidades de dinero en la bogados."
**Financial Strain and Legal History** : The band faced significant financial hardship and lacked the resources to continue extensive legal battles over "Bitter Sweet Symphony." They had previously experienced very negative outcomes with legal processes, which likely influenced their decision to agree to unfavorable settlements to avoid further, potentially endless, and costly litigation. "estos procesos legales se te antojaban carísimos e interminables y el grupo de ver tampoco tenía este si vos recursos para afrontar nuevas legales ya había tenido muy malas experiencias con nuevas legales anteriormente."

## albums
**The Last Time (1965)** : This song by The Rolling Stones served as the original source for the melody sampled in The Verve's "Bitter Sweet Symphony." However, "The Last Time" itself was not entirely original; its core was based on a classic gospel song from the late 1950s called "Dismay Vide Last Time" (This May Be The Last Time). While the lyrics for "The Last Time" were largely derived from the gospel original, the melody was re-written by Keith Richards. Rights to this song (and other pre-1970 Rolling Stones material) were acquired by Allen Klein. "Esa rección evidentemente viene de un próximo original de los Rolling Stones en 1965 que es de Last Time y ahí hasta donde nos remontamos a la origen de la melodía de David점 Suits Symphony."

# artist - Andrew Loog Oldham

## member of
The Rolling Stones

## curiosities
**Symphonic Versions of Rolling Stones Songs** : Andrew Loog Oldham was responsible for creating symphonic versions of Rolling Stones songs in the late 1960s. One of these instrumental versions, specifically featuring five repetitive notes from "The Last Time," was later sampled by The Verve for their song "Bitter Sweet Symphony." Oldham initially granted permission for its use, but he subsequently filed a lawsuit, feeling he deserved more prominence than agreed upon as the song became hugely successful, although his initial demand did not succeed. "Esa rifa, al finalmente, pertenecía a una grabación de finales de los 60 de Alan Luke que hacía versiones sinfónicas de Rolling Stones."
**Producer and First Manager of The Rolling Stones** : Andrew Loog Oldham held significant roles with The Rolling Stones, serving as their first professional manager and also as their producer. This professional relationship places him in a key position regarding the origins of the music later sampled by The Verve. "Alan Luke, aparte de grabar estas versiones sinfónicas, fue el primer banal de que tuvieron los Rolling Stones profesional. Era el más de manal y era el productor del grupo."
**Loss of Rights to Instrumental Versions** : Andrew Loog Oldham had previously been sued by Allen Klein over the rights to the instrumental versions of The Rolling Stones' songs he had created in the late 1960s. As a result of this earlier legal dispute, Oldham lost all rights he held over these instrumental tracks, which subsequently fell under Allen Klein's ownership. "Alinclain ya en su día habían mandado a Alinclain por los derechos de estas versiones instrumentales que había hecho a finales de los 60 y por el cual había acabado perdiendo todos los derechos que tenía suelas canciones."
**Composer of Key Sample, No Remuneration** : Despite being the original composer of the "cinco famos anotas" (five famous notes) that formed the core instrumental sample in "Bitter Sweet Symphony," Andrew Loog Oldham ultimately did not receive any financial compensation for their use after the complex legal battles concluded. This situation left him feeling deeply frustrated regarding the outcome. "Alando Conban en el fondo ha sido el compositor de esas cinco famos anotas no veo un duro y el pobre también lo ve muy frustro ante el grupo de ver pues yo es podéis imaginar cómo quedarlo."

# artist - Allen Klein

## member of
The Rolling Stones (manager)

## curiosities
**Acquisition of Rolling Stones' Pre-1970 Rights** : Allen Klein served as a manager for The Rolling Stones, and through a series of complex legal maneuvers and contract disputes, he managed to acquire complete ownership of all rights to the band's songs recorded before 1970. This significant legal victory provided him with a powerful position in subsequent disputes involving these songs, including "The Last Time." "Alinclain era el verdadero propietario de los derechos de las canciones de los Rolling Stones antes de 1970. Alinclain para el que no lo conoce fue un manayer posturte tuvieron los Rolling Stones que por una serie de detalles legales cuando se acabó rompiendo el contrato, consiguió quedarse completamente todos los derechos de las canciones de los Rolling Stones a 100770."
**Lawsuit Against Andrew Loog Oldham** : Prior to his involvement with The Verve, Allen Klein successfully sued Andrew Loog Oldham over the rights to the instrumental versions of The Rolling Stones' songs that Oldham had created in the late 1960s. This legal victory transferred the ownership of these instrumental rights, including the specific five notes, to Klein. "Alinclain ya en su día habían mandado a Alinclain por los derechos de estas versiones instrumentales que había hecho a finales de los 60 y por el cual había acabado perdiendo todos los derechos que tenía suelas canciones."
**Initial Claim to "Bitter Sweet Symphony" Rights** : Allen Klein initiated a lawsuit against The Verve for their use of the "The Last Time" sample in "Bitter Sweet Symphony." Leveraging his ownership of The Rolling Stones' pre-1970 catalog, he successfully argued for a share of the new song's rights, initially securing 50% based on the understanding that The Verve had exceeded the granted usage. His legal action succeeded over Andrew Loog Oldham's similar claim due to Klein's established ownership of the Rolling Stones' rights. "el que consiguió reclamar del 50% de los derechos evitarse sin poner al entender que había abusado de los derechos que harían permitido."
**Acquisition of 100% of "Bitter Sweet Symphony" Rights** : As "Bitter Sweet Symphony" became an extremely lucrative song, Allen Klein was not satisfied with the initial 50% share of its rights. He pursued further legal action, leading to an extrajudicial settlement where he acquired 100% of all rights to the song from The Verve. His success was attributed to having very expensive lawyers and his irrefutable ownership of the original Rolling Stones' material. "Alinclain no está con la forma de los 50% y vuelve a demandar a ver aparte de la polo extrajudicia que habían llegado."

This detail highlights his creative contribution to the track amidst the complex legal debates surrounding its musical composition. "la letra que es completamente escrita por Richard Ascro."
**Grammy Nomination as Composer** : Despite the intricate legal situation that saw Allen Klein acquire 100% of "Bitter Sweet Symphony"'s rights, Richard Ashcroft, alongside Mick Jagger and Keith Richards, was nominated for a Grammy for "Best Song of the Year" as a composer of the track. This situation, where he was nominated for a song he didn't entirely compose and no longer owned, is a notable curiosity. "el Grammy en mejor canción del año y en ese momento acabaron nominados que Dricha sin minchakers por una canción que ni siquiera habían compuesto."
**Discomfort with Legal Process** : Richard Ashcroft, along with Mick Jagger and Keith Richards, publicly expressed their discomfort and lack of intention to be involved in the complex and controversial legal processes surrounding "Bitter Sweet Symphony," indicating a desire to distance themselves from the disputes. "Tato Mink Jaer como Chris Riches en el pasado han hecho varias declaraciones respecto a todo este proceso legal tan complejo del que se han visto en versos y ellos no tenían ninguna intención de ver si en estos rollos si no lo consideran correcto."

While their recording is the earliest registered, the group is not listed as the authors, suggesting it was a popular folk or church song of unknown origin at the time. "La grabación más antigua que tenemos de esta canción antigua de gospel corresponde a 1.156 grabadas por un grupo de gospel llamado Distance Inverse."

# curiosity

**Origin of "Dismay Vide Last Time"** : The gospel song "Dismay Vide Last Time" (This May Be The Last Time), which served as the basis for The Rolling Stones' "The Last Time" and subsequently influenced "Bitter Sweet Symphony," is a classic from the late 1950s with an unclear authorship. Its earliest recorded version dates back to 1956 by the gospel group Distance to Invers, but they are not identified as the original authors, indicating it was likely a popular song commonly sung in churches. "El problema es que de toda la canción original desde el principio que es Dismay Vide Last Time no tenemos claro quién es el autor."
**Complex Music Copyright Disputes** : The case of "Bitter Sweet Symphony" exemplifies one of the most complicated copyright struggles in the music industry, characterized by conflicting claims over melody and instrumental verses. This intricate legal history involves multiple artists, managers, and original compositions, making it difficult to ascertain the true authorship in the public eye. "uno de los casos más complicados que tenemos en la industria de la música, que es el caso que tuvo de ver con David점 Suits Symphony, por como hoy en día hay mucha controversia y mucha información falsa sobre quién es el verdadero autor de la canción."
**Legal Fees and Resources in Copyright Cases** : The legal battle over "Bitter Sweet Symphony" highlighted how expensive and protracted copyright disputes can be. The Verve, lacking the financial resources of Allen Klein and having previous negative experiences with legal challenges, ultimately ceded all rights to the song. This demonstrates that access to significant financial resources for legal representation can be a deciding factor in copyright outcomes, regardless of initial artistic contribution or permission. "la primera y el más importante es que tenía los ahorrados más caros."
**Grammy Nomination as a Legal Consequence** : The nomination of Mick Jagger, Keith Richards, and Richard Ashcroft for a Grammy for "Best Song of the Year" for "Bitter Sweet Symphony" was a direct result of the complex legal agreements rather than their direct compositional input to The Verve's final song. This outcome highlights how legal settlements can influence official credits and recognition in the music industry, often misleading public perception about a song's true creative origins. "Es el origen por el que mucha gente cree que ellos fueron el compositorales de la canción pero realmente detrás no es más que un proceso legal."
