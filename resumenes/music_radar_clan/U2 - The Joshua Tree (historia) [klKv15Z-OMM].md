## albums
**The Joshua Tree (1987) - Landmark Album** : This album marks its 30th anniversary this week since its publication in 1987. It was the third consecutive album for U2 to receive maximum critical acclaim and remains the band's best-selling album to date, with approximately 28 million copies sold. It holds the 26th position on Rolling Stone magazine's list of the greatest albums in history. In 1987, it earned U2 their first Grammy for Best Album of the Year and quickly rose to number one on sales charts. Curiously, it also positioned at number 36 on the list of best Christian albums in 1987. The album was recorded rapidly, taking only three months despite its high quality. During its creation, U2 began refining their composition methods, moving towards a more structured approach where Bono would work on lyrics as poems, and the band would then compose the music with almost finished texts. The band's positive mood following the success of their previous three albums also contributed to this process. "Al día de hoy, ha vendido unos 28 millones de copias y sigue siendo el álbum más vendido de la banda U2."
**The Joshua Tree (1987) - Original Titles and Title Change** : The album was not originally intended to be called "The Joshua Tree". Its initial proposed titles were "The Desert Songs" and later "Two Americas". The final name change occurred when the band went to the Mojave Desert in California, specifically Joshua Tree National Park, for the album's cover photography. The iconic Joshua Tree photographed for the cover still stands there today, marked with a commemorative plaque. The discarded original titles, "The Desert Songs" and "Two Americas," better hint at the album's thematic content. "Finalmente se terminó cambiando del Yosu Atri cuando la banda fue a hacerse las fotografías para la portada y para todo el árbol del disco, eso fueron al desierto de Mojave, en California, al Parque Nacional de Yosu."
**The Joshua Tree (1987) - Production by Brian Eno and Daniel Lanois** : The album was produced by the highly influential Brian Eno and his personal friend Daniel Lanois. Brian Eno had previously produced several U2 albums and continued to work with them afterwards, with Bono attributing much of U2's success to Eno's significant production work. Both producers took an unconventional approach, not only handling classic production duties but also playing instruments and contributing backing vocals on some songs during the recording of "The Joshua Tree." "tanto, Brian Nino como Daniel Lanois, a trabajar durante el álbum no solo en trabajos de producción, sino que tocaron instrumentos durante la grabación del álbum, pero incluso a sus voces figuran en los coros algunas de las canciones."
**The Joshua Tree (1987) - American Influences** : A key theme for "The Joshua Tree," hinted at by the original title "Two Americas," arose from the band's extensive touring in the United States in the years prior to the album. During this period, U2 connected with American artists like Bob Dylan and deepened their relationship with European artists such as The Rolling Stones, who were deeply enamored with American culture and music. This exposure led Bono to feel U2 lacked musical roots, prompting the band to study and integrate classic American music, including Country, Blues, and classic American Rock, drawing inspiration from early electric guitars influenced by Chuck Berry. Beyond music, Bono immersed himself in classic American literature and observed the social and cultural aspects, particularly the "breakdown of the American Dream," which became a significant source of inspiration for the album's themes. "tenemos muchas influencias del Country, tenemos muchas influencias del blues, tenemos muchas influencias del rock clásico americano, incluso de las primeras guitarras eléctricas en Chuck Berry."
**The Joshua Tree (1987) - Spiritual and Religious Themes** : The album's working title "The Desert Songs" alludes to its spiritual journey theme, which sparked ongoing debate about its Christian or religious connotations. During this period, Bono embarked on a significant spiritual process, blending humanitarian efforts (such as his trips to Africa to raise money) with mystical and religious experiences. This led to themes of inner exploration, self-discovery, and human morality throughout the album, with numerous references to God, faith, and spiritual seeking. These elements, combined with the "Joshua Tree" imagery, contribute to the album's ambiguous but evident spiritual and potentially Christian undertones. "Bono comenzó un enorme fase de proceso espiritual durante esta época, de hecho, cuando le empezó a hacer sus viajes a África, a conocer las abrones de África, lluchar por este tipo de causas, a recalar dinero."
**The Joshua Tree (1987) - Political Themes** : The album marked a turning point for U2, with its political content becoming increasingly explicit. The band was deeply affected by various late 1980s events, including the Civil War in El Salvador, the plight of mothers in South America who had lost children in civil wars and dictatorships, mining strikes in the United Kingdom, and the persistent religious terrorism in Northern Ireland. These events provided a rich tapestry for the album's direct political commentary, which became a defining feature of "The Joshua Tree" and subsequent U2 albums. "tenemos la Guerra Civil del Salvador, tenemos las famosas comadronas en su América, que habían perdido a sus hijos en las trasitadulas y las guerras civiles, tenemos las vulgas de la minería en el Reconido, tenemos las continuas duchas todavía el terrorismo religioso que había en Irlanda del Norte, y eso viene a ser un poco la guinda del pastel."
**The Joshua Tree (1987) - Composition Process Evolution** : For "The Joshua Tree" and subsequent albums, U2 significantly refined their composition methods. Previously, the band would enter recording sessions with songs still creatively "raw" and sometimes with unfinished lyrics, completing the composition process during recording. With "The Joshua Tree," Bono began to treat lyric writing almost like poetry, working on them in isolation with a highly literary approach. The rest of the band would then compose the music based on these nearly completed lyrics. This more complex and professional method differed greatly from the approach U2 used in their earlier albums. "Bono empieza a trabajar las letras casi como poemas, a escribir las ensolitario, a trabajar las de manera completamente literaria, mientras que la banda después con unas letras casi terminadas empieza a trabajar en la composición de la música."

## songs
**buen de Suís Hathno Name** : This famous song is directly inspired by the religious segregation prevalent in Belfast. The city of Belfast and its religious divisions have consistently been important political themes for U2, and in this period, the political-religious-spiritual content of their music became increasingly evident. "la famosa canción, buen de Suís Hathno Name, que realmente está inspirada en Belfast en la segregación religiosa que hay en la ciudad."

## curiosities
**Early Working Methods** : In their early years, U2 was a group that tended to work long, intensive recording sessions with little time in between, often entering the studio with creatively "raw" songs and sometimes unfinished lyrics, completing composition during the recording process. This approach was refined and changed for "The Joshua Tree" and subsequent albums. "Ludo es el primer año, es en un grupo que tendría mucho trabajar la grabación durante largas jornadas sin mucho tiempo."
**Bono's Spiritual Process** : During the period leading up to "The Joshua Tree," Bono embarked on a significant spiritual journey. This phase included his trips to Africa to learn about the continent's issues and advocate for various causes, intertwining humanitarian efforts with mystical and religious experiences. "Bono comenzó un enorme fase de proceso espiritual durante esta época, de hecho, cuando le empezó a hacer sus viajes a África, a conocer las abrones de África, lluchar por este tipo de causas, a recalar dinero."
**Relationship with Bob Dylan** : U2 established a good friendship with American artist Bob Dylan during their extensive touring in the United States, as part of their cultural encounter with the nation. "conocen artistas americanos con los que establece una buena amistad, como es el ejemplo de Bob Dylan."
**Relationship with The Rolling Stones** : U2 deepened their relationship with European artists like The Rolling Stones, who were noted for their strong affection for the United States. The Rolling Stones' significant American musical influences during the 1980s greatly intrigued U2, leading them to explore the roots of classic American music. "también profundizan su relación con otros artistas europeos, o pueden ser los Rolling Stones, que siempre han estado muy enamorados de los Estados Unidos."
**Quest for Musical Roots** : Observing how many European and American artists explored the roots of their music, Bono began to feel that U2, by comparison, lacked distinct musical roots. This realization prompted the band to delve into and establish roots in classic American music. "a Bono un poco tiene la sensación de que U2 es un grupo un poco sin raíz musicales, y es cuando empiezan a un poco estudiar y a raizarse de toda la música americana clásica."
**Inspiration from American Literature** : During U2's tours and cultural immersion in the United States, Bono began to absorb and draw inspiration from classic American literature, which contributed to the lyrical themes of "The Joshua Tree." "Bono también en los Estados Unidos empiezan pregnarse de la literatura clásica americana."
**Observation of the "Broken American Dream"** : While touring the United States, U2 became immersed in American social culture, finding a rich source of inspiration in what they perceived as the "ruptura del sueño americano" (breakdown of the American Dream) within the society around them. This observation during a controversial era for the U.S. profoundly influenced the album's themes. "ven un poco la ruptura de sueñamericano en la sociedad que le rodea cuando estante gira, y lo ve como una importante fuente de inspiración también para sus temáticas."

# curiosity

**Mojave Desert, California - The Joshua Tree Album Photography** : The Mojave Desert in California served as the location for U2's album photography for "The Joshua Tree." The band visited the area, specifically Joshua Tree National Park, which ultimately inspired the album's title change. "eso fueron al desierto de Mojave, en California, al Parque Nacional de Yosu."
**Joshua Tree National Park - Commemorative Plaque** : The specific Joshua Tree photographed for the cover of U2's "The Joshua Tree" album is still located in Joshua Tree National Park. It features a commemorative plaque, acknowledging its significance. "Hoy en día ese árbol fotografiado, si estando allí, de hecho tiene una placa conmemorativa diciendo, este es el árbol de Yosu Atri de U2."
**El Salvador Civil War - Inspiration for U2** : The Civil War in El Salvador was one of several late 1980s global events that deeply impacted U2 and served as a significant source of political content for their album "The Joshua Tree." "tenemos la Guerra Civil del Salvador."
**Las Madres in South America - Inspiration for U2** : The plight of "Las Madres" (mothers) in South America, who had lost their children in dictatorships and civil wars, was a poignant issue that influenced U2's political themes in "The Joshua Tree." "tenemos las famosas comadronas en su América, que habían perdido a sus hijos en las trasitadulas y las guerras civiles."
**Mining Strikes in the United Kingdom - Inspiration for U2** : The mining strikes in the United Kingdom were among the significant socio-political events of the late 1980s that captivated U2 and contributed to the political narrative explored in their album "The Joshua Tree." "tenemos las vulgas de la minería en el Reconido."
**Religious Terrorism in Northern Ireland - Inspiration for U2** : The ongoing religious terrorism in Northern Ireland was a continuous struggle that deeply affected U2. This issue served as a crucial element in the increasingly evident political and religious content of their music, particularly in "The Joshua Tree." "tenemos las continuas duchas todavía el terrorismo religioso que había en Irlanda del Norte."
**The Breakdown of the American Dream - Inspiration for U2** : U2, during their tours in the United States, observed and were inspired by what they perceived as the "ruptura del sueño americano" (breakdown of the American Dream) within society. This controversial aspect of American culture provided a rich thematic source for their album "The Joshua Tree." "ven un poco la ruptura de sueñamericano en la sociedad que le rodea cuando estante gira, y lo ve como una importante fuente de inspiración también para sus temáticas."
