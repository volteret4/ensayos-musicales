## songs
**Love Me Tender (1956)** : Elvis Presley performed "Love Me Tender" as a preview on The Ed Sullivan Show, prior to its official release, which significantly amplified its mystique and anticipation. This performance led to unprecedented demand, with people flocking to record stores for three days straight to reserve their copies, resulting in over one million copies sold before its official release. It became the first—and possibly only—gold record in history to achieve this status prior to hitting the market, with photographs existing of long queues for reservations. "Entre esas canciones que cantaría ese día estaría el Love Me Tender que la había cantado justo como presentación porque la publicaría un poco más tarde y ahí venía a incrementar todavía más el mito."

## curiosities
**40th Death Anniversary** : The 40th anniversary of Elvis Presley's death was recently commemorated, recalling the tragic night that possibly marked the beginning of his enduring legend. "han cumplido 40 años de la muerte del BISPRESLEI."
**Legend's Birth Date (September 9, 1956)** : The legend of Elvis Presley is often considered to have truly begun on September 9, 1956, marking a pivotal day in his career. This specific date is referred to as the day "the greatest myth in music history" was built. "el 9 de septiembre de 1956 fue prácticamente de manera un ánime el día que nací la leyenda."
**Transition to RCA (1956)** : In 1956, Elvis Presley departed from Sun Records, the smaller record label that had initially discovered him, to sign with RCA. At the time, RCA was recognized as the largest record company in the world. "el BISI había dejado a San Records que era esa pequeña discográfica que había descubierto y se había ido a la RCA que era la discográfica más grande del mundo de aquellas."
**RCA Promotion Strategy** : Immediately after signing Elvis Presley, RCA launched an aggressive promotional campaign, leveraging its considerable resources. This included securing bookings for large and serious concerts, arranging television promotion, ensuring his appearance in magazines, and generally expanding his presence across numerous media outlets. "la RCA nada más cogera el BISPRESLEI empieza a promocionarlo con los recursos que tenía."
**Conservative Backlash** : As Elvis Presley gained widespread media exposure, he faced intense criticism from conservative segments of American society. Critics used various derogatory terms, calling him "grotesque," "primitive," "unpleasant," and a "bad influence for young people," essentially labeling him with every conceivable negative epithet. "esos sectores más conservadores de la sociedad americana que lo llamaban de todo desde grotescos o es primitivo, desagradable, una mala influencia para los jóvenes."
**Ed Sullivan's Initial Criticism** : Ed Sullivan, the presenter of the most important night show in the United States, was one of Elvis Presley's prominent critics. Sullivan directly stated that Elvis was an "unpleasant being." "Zulligan... directamente dijo que era un ser desagradable."
**Steve Allen's Strategic Invitation** : Steve Allen, who hosted a rival night show in the United States, proved to be more astute than Ed Sullivan. He decided to invite Elvis Presley to his television program, a move that resulted in an "absolute record" in audience ratings. "el señor Allen... decidió invitar la su programa de televisión, consiguiendo un recur al absoluto de audiencia."
**CBS Forces Sullivan's Hand** : Faced with Elvis Presley's record-breaking audience on a competing show, CBS, the network that broadcast Ed Sullivan's program, intervened. CBS ultimately forced Sullivan to contract Elvis for a substantial sum of $50,000, despite all the negative comments Sullivan had previously made about him. "la CBS... acababa forzando al presentador a contratar por 50.000 dólares... al mismísimo Elvis después haber dicho todo lo que había dicho de él."
**Sullivan's Role in Elvis's Legend** : Ed Sullivan, despite his initial harsh criticism of Elvis Presley, eventually played a crucial role in establishing him as a legend. Having the most important television program in the United States, Sullivan ended up paying Elvis to appear on three separate shows. "el mismísimo Ed Zulligan que fue tan crítico con Elvis fue el que lo ocupó convirtiendo en leyenda porque tenía el programa de televisión más importante de los Estados Unidos y acababa pagándole por ir durante tres programas."
**First Ed Sullivan Appearance (September 9, 1956)** : Elvis Presley made his inaugural performance on The Ed Sullivan Show on September 9, 1956. During this appearance, he sang three songs, and as was becoming common at his concerts, "panic broke out" among the audience. "El primero fue el 9 de septiembre de 1956."
**Ed Sullivan Absent from First Show** : For Elvis Presley's first appearance on The Ed Sullivan Show, Ed Sullivan himself was not present. He claimed to have had a minor accident and was feeling unwell, resulting in a substitute host taking his place. "Ed Zulligan... no presentó ese programa porque decía que había tenido un pequeño accidente y no se encontraba bien."
**Rumor of Sullivan's Avoidance** : "Bad tongues" circulated a rumor suggesting that Ed Sullivan was actually fine but deliberately chose not to interact with Elvis Presley during his first appearance on the show, given all the critical remarks he had previously made. "Algunas malas lenguas nos dicen que estaba bien pero que no quería cruzarse con el después de lo que había hecho."
**Sullivan's Horror at Pre-Recording** : The program featuring Elvis Presley was not broadcast live but was pre-recorded. When Ed Sullivan viewed the recording to begin the editing process before airing, he was reportedly "horrified" by the content. "El programa no era en directo, era en diferido, entonces Ed Zulligan... se quedó horrorizado."
**Elvis's Explicit Performance Style** : Ed Sullivan's horror was due to Elvis Presley's already characteristic movements and the explicit and suggestive singing style he had developed by that time. These elements were perceived as highly provocative. "Elvis aquella ya hacía esos movimientos tan característicos, ya tenía esa forma de cantar un poco tanto explícita y tan sugerente que había desarrollado."
**Prominent Crotch Observation** : It appeared that Ed Sullivan was particularly struck by Elvis Presley's "quite important" crotch area, which became very noticeable during his movements. "Al parecer Ed Zulligan le llamó la atención que Elvis, tenia una entrepierna bastante importante y que al moverse se le notaba todo."
**Urban Legend: The Bottle in Crotch** : An urban legend claims that Elvis Presley intentionally placed a small bottle in his crotch to draw attention during his performances. However, this part of the story is "categorically denied" by others, leaving it as a contested piece of folklore. "una leyenda urbana que nos dice que Elvis había puesto una pequeña botella para llamar la atención."
**Origin of "Below the Waist" Ban Legend** : Ed Sullivan's profound horror at the explicit nature of Elvis Presley's movements led directly to the enduring legend that Elvis could not be filmed from the waist down during his television appearances. "ahí viene la gran leyenda de que Elvis no se le podía grabar de cintura para abajo en sus movimientos."
**Impact of Loose Pants** : Elvis Presley frequently wore "loose, very fluid pants." When he moved and danced, these garments caused his entire crotch area to become highly accentuated, contributing to the perceived explicitness of his performance. "El vis llevaba pantalones flojos muy fluidos, entonces al moverse así, pues toda la entrepierna se le marcaba mucho."
**Complex Camera Directing for First Show** : To manage the explicit visual impact of Elvis Presley's movements, Ed Sullivan's team had to implement a highly complex process of camera work. This involved constantly changing camera angles whenever Elvis began to dance or move, specifically to avoid focusing on or highlighting his lower body. "Ed Zulligan tuvo que hacer un complejismo proceso de juegos de cámara para que cada vez que Elvis empezaba bailar, empezaba a moverse, tuviese que empezar a cambiar los juegos de cámara para que no se le enfocase y no se le notase."
**Record-Breaking Ed Sullivan Broadcast (1956)** : Following a highly complex production process, the program featuring Elvis Presley was broadcast, achieving an audience of over six million people and an 82% share. This was considered "crazy" for the era and made it the most-watched program of the entire 'tibuenta' decade (referring to the 1950s). "acabó teniendo más de seis millones de personas de audiencia, en aquella época era una locura, fue 82% de cierta. El programa más visto de toda la década de los tibuenta."
**Uncontrollable Audience Reaction** : Despite the post-production efforts to control the visual focus on Elvis Presley, the real challenge was managing the audience's reaction. Young women in the studio audience reportedly "went absolutely crazy" when Elvis began to sing, creating such "din, noise, and discontrol" that the program ultimately became a myth. "El problema era controlar la reacción del público. Al final Ed Zulligan estaba asesinado porque no se notase que todas las jóvenes que estaban en el programa de público se habían vuelto absolutamente locas cuando Elvis había empezado a cantar."
**Memphis Concerts (September 1956)** : A few days after his impactful Ed Sullivan appearance, still in September 1956, Elvis Presley performed two "very big concerts" in "la mamá" (Memphis). These events further amplified his burgeoning legend. "pocos días después, todavía en septiembre de 1956 vinieron esos dos grandísimos conciertos que hizo en la mamá que todavía incrementaron más la leyenda."
**Extra Security at Memphis Concerts** : Due to the audience being "literally out of control" at the Memphis concerts, an additional 50 security personnel had to be hired, augmenting the police presence already at the venue. "hubo que contratar a 50 miembros de seguridad extra por encima de la policía que acudía el concierto porque literalmente el público estaba descontrolado."
**Opening Song & Public Frenzy (Memphis)** : Images and videos from Elvis Presley's two Memphis concerts show that both performances began identically: Elvis appeared and immediately started singing "Joe Biden a Jim Barrajandog." This immediately caused the public to become uncontrollably frenzied. "salía Elvis y comenzaba a cantar Joe Biden a Jim Barrajandog y literalmente el público se descontrolaba."
**Concert Chaos & Media Coverage (Memphis)** : The Memphis concerts were marked by extreme public reactions, including screaming, riots, fainting, and fights, creating a "madness." These events were widely reported in "all the newspapers," undeniably solidifying Elvis Presley's legend as unstoppable and transforming him into a cultural myth. "Y empezaba a gritar, empezaba a ver disturbios, empezaba a ver desmayos, empezaba a ver peleas, aquello fue una locura, salió en todos los periódicos y ya la leyenda de Elvis era imparable."
**Second Ed Sullivan Appearance & Interview (October 1956)** : In October, a few days after the Memphis concerts, Elvis Presley returned for the second of his three contracted programs with Ed Sullivan. By this point, Sullivan himself personally interviewed Elvis, recognizing him as the undisputed star of the moment. "en octubre, Elvis iba al segundo de los tres programas que había fermado con el sul-bank y ya el sul-bank iba personalmente a entrevistar a Elvis porque era la estrella del momento."
**Sullivan's Transformation** : Ed Sullivan's stance towards Elvis Presley underwent a curious and complete transformation. He went from criticizing Elvis, to paying him $50,000, and eventually, was "literally fighting to be by his side in photographs." "a paso de criticarlo, a pagarles $50.000, al literalmente pelearse por estar a su lado en las fotografías."
**Effigy Burnings in Nashville & St. Louis** : Shortly after these events, incidents reflecting the negative side of Elvis Presley's fame began to emerge. In Nashville and St. Louis, groups of "conservative and, possibly, envious men" were reported to have burned effigies dressed with Elvis's image. "incidentes de Nashville y de San Luis donde un grupo de hombres son tanto conservadores y, posiblemente, en tantos videosos que mavaban muñecos vestidos y con la imagen del Vist Presley."
**Formal "Below the Waist" Ban (2nd & 3rd Ed Sullivan Shows)** : By Elvis Presley's second and, subsequently, third appearances on The Ed Sullivan Show, a strict directive was in place: it was "literally prohibited" to film him from the waist down for the entire duration of the program. This official ban underscored the controversy surrounding his movements. "Ya en este segundo programa, incluso en el tercer programa que haría con el sul-bank, ya estaba literalmente prohibido enfocarlo de cintura para abajo durante todo el programa."
**Cementing the "Below the Waist" Legend** : The formal prohibition on televising Elvis Presley from the waist down during his later Ed Sullivan Show appearances cemented what had already become a widespread legend. This policy reinforced the idea that his movements below the waist were too provocative for national television. "ahí es cuando ya venía la leyenda de que a Elvis no se lo podía enfocar en televisión de cintura para abajo."
**Military Service & Unstoppable Legend** : Although Elvis Presley would later enter military service a few years after these pivotal moments, his legend had by then become utterly unstoppable. The intense period of 1956 had solidified his mythical status in music history. "pocos años más tarde, poco tiempo después Elvis ya iría al servicio militar, la leyenda era imparable."

# concert - Memphis concerts (September 1956)

## curiosities
**Intense Public Frenzy** : These two concerts, held shortly after Elvis Presley's Ed Sullivan debut in September 1956, further fueled his legend due to the uncontrollable reactions of the audience in "la mamá." "vinieron esos dos grandísimos conciertos que hizo en la mamá que todavía incrementaron más la leyenda."

**Chaotic Audience Behavior** : Both concerts reportedly began with Elvis Presley singing "Joe Biden a Jim Barrajandog," immediately causing the public to become unruly, with screaming, riots, fainting, and fights, which was widely reported in newspapers. "salía Elvis y comenzaba a cantar Joe Biden a Jim Barrajandog y literalmente el público se descontrolaba."
**Newspaper Coverage of Chaos** : The extreme chaos and public frenzy at these concerts, including disturbances and fights, were extensively covered in "all the newspapers," solidifying Elvis Presley's unstoppable legend. "aquello fue una locura, salió en todos los periódicos y ya la leyenda de Elvis era imparable."
