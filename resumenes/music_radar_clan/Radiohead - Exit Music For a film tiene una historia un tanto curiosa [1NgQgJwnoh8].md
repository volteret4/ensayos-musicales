# artist - Radiohead

## albums
**OK Computer (1997) - Recording & Theme** : The album *OK Computer* was predominantly recorded in a unique setting described as a blend of a barn and an old mansion. Its central theme explores the sadness, depression, and loneliness experienced by individuals due to the impact of new technologies and the modern world. Notably, *Exit Music (For a Film)* is one of two tracks on the album whose thematic content deviates from this overarching concept. "The album is dedicated to how people are sad, depressed, and feel alone because of new technologies and the modern world."

## songs
**Exit Music (For a Film) (1997) - Origin & Thematic Deviation** : This song predates the recording sessions for *OK Computer* by a year and was not recorded during those sessions, instead being laid down in a standard recording studio. It originated as a commission from the director of the modern *Romeo + Juliet* film (which featured Leonardo DiCaprio) for its end credits. Composed by Thom Yorke, the song is a poetic interpretation of one of the play's most tragic scenes, focusing on Romeo and Juliet's escape to elude death and remain together, a theme distinct from broader narratives of escape from war or general suffering. Thom Yorke was so satisfied with the song's tragic and "pompous" nature that he initially refused its inclusion in the film. The "discography" (likely the record label or producers) also deemed it too tragic and pompous, nearly excluding it from *OK Computer*, but Thom Yorke's persistence ensured its placement midway through the album. Its cinematic theme is a notable departure from the album's focus on modern technology and isolation. "It is a song that already existed before recording OK Computer and was not even recorded during OK Computer."
**Exit Music (For a Film) (1997) - Soundtrack Presence** : Over the years, Thom Yorke's interest in *Exit Music (For a Film)* waned considerably, which subsequently led to its frequent inclusion in the soundtracks of numerous films and television series. The song has been featured in an estimated five to six films and recently appeared in the soundtrack for *Black Mirror*, establishing itself as a recurring and prominent piece in cinematic contexts. "Today we have it in five, six films, apart from having recently appeared in several soundtracks like Black Mirror and starting to become quite a recurrent song in cinema."

# artist - Thom Yorke

## member of
Radiohead

## curiosities
**Satisfaction and Refusal for Romeo + Juliet** : After composing *Exit Music (For a Film)* for the *Romeo + Juliet* film commission, Thom Yorke found himself immensely satisfied with the song's tragic and "pompous" outcome. His profound satisfaction was such that, ironically, he ultimately refused to allow the song to be included in the film's final credits, despite having been specifically commissioned for that purpose. "The song is so tragic and pompous that Thom Yorke was very satisfied with the result and the problem is that he was so satisfied with the result that in the end he refused to include it in the film."
**Later Loss of Interest in Exit Music (For a Film)** : With the passage of time, Thom Yorke significantly lost interest in *Exit Music (For a Film)*. This diminished interest was the reason he permitted the song to be used in numerous soundtracks for various television series and films many years after its initial release. "Curiously, Thom Yorke has lost a lot of interest in it over the years and in the end he has allowed it to appear in many soundtracks of many television series and films years later because he has lost quite a bit of interest in it."

# artist - Mary Limmanson

## curiosities
**Life Saved by Exit Music (For a Film)** : Mary Limmanson, who was institutionalized for severe depression and a mental disorder, recounted in a *Rolling Stone* interview how Radiohead's *Exit Music (For a Film)* saved his life. During an escape from the institution, he planned to commit suicide by jumping from a cliff. However, just as he was on the verge of jumping, he heard the song, which prompted him to reflect for hours, ultimately preventing him from carrying out the act and preserving his life. He explicitly states that he is alive thanks to this song. "He heard 'Exit Music (For a Film)' by Radiohead, which made him reflect for hours, ultimately not jumping and saving his life."

# artist - David Bowie

## curiosities
**Wish to Compose Exit Music (For a Film)** : David Bowie once confessed that *Exit Music (For a Film)* was the song he had always wished he could have composed himself. However, he also admitted that he felt he was not talented enough to have written such a piece. This remark stands as his most explicit acknowledgment of his admiration for Radiohead found in his interviews. "He referred to it on one occasion as the song he would have loved to have composed but wasn't good enough to have made."

# curiosity

**Romeo + Juliet (1996) Film Commission** : The director of the modern film *Romeo + Juliet*, which featured Leonardo DiCaprio, specifically commissioned Radiohead to create a song for its end credits. This commission directly led to Thom Yorke's composition of *Exit Music (For a Film)*, which was inspired by a tragic scene from Shakespeare's play. "A commission made by the director of the modern Romeo and Juliet film that Leonardo DiCaprio made, who commissioned Radiohead for a song for the final credits."
**Rolling Stone Interview with Mary Limmanson** : In an interview conducted by *Rolling Stone*, Mary Limmanson vividly shared the story of how Radiohead's song *Exit Music (For a Film)* played a direct role in saving his life during a profound personal crisis. He stated unequivocally that he owes his continued existence to this particular song. "Mary Limmanson once in an interview for Rolling Stone how it saved his life."
**Black Mirror Soundtrack Inclusion** : Radiohead's *Exit Music (For a Film)* was recently featured on the soundtrack for the television series *Black Mirror*. This appearance contributes to the song's growing status as a recurrent track in cinematic and television contexts, having been included in approximately five to six films in addition to various other series soundtracks. "It has recently appeared in several soundtracks such as Black Mirror and is starting to become a quite recurrent song in cinema."
