## albums
**Leisure (1991)** : Released a few months after their first single "She's So High", this debut album was complicated. It sold reasonably well and sounded good for its era, but received very poor critical reviews. Critics largely dismissed it as an attempt to prolong the dying Manchester movement, seeing it as a record label's pressure to stretch a failing trend, and if classified, it belonged more to the Madchester sound than to Britpop, with influences from bands like The Stone Roses.
"fond disco que no se vendió mal, eso es verdad, fond disco que son bastante en la época, pero cuyas críticas no fueron muy buenas."

**Modern Life Is Rubbish (1993)** : This album was a turning point for Blur and the Britpop movement. Following EMI's investment in Food Records, Blur gained significantly more resources, promotion, and development, including more interesting music videos. Some publications consider it the first official album of the Britpop movement, or at least it contained two key elements that would define the Britpop sound by 1995: Graham Coxon's guitars and Damon Albarn's lyrics, which were rich in British content and exalted the United Kingdom. The album's promotion also heavily featured classic British iconography like the Union Jack, Doc Martens, and Fred Perry polos. Critically, it was much better received than "Leisure," sounding fresh and distinct from the Manchester movement, though not yet considered a masterpiece.
"las guitarras de Graham Coxon en Modern Life is Ravi's definen una gran parte del sonido de guitarras del movimiento Britpop y el otro que es otro punto clave son las letras de gran contenido británico o de gran exaltación del Reino Unido que tenía el propio Damon Albarn."

**Parklife (1994)** : Released in April 1994, this album is considered one of the first great masterpieces of the Britpop movement. Building on the success and direction established by "Modern Life Is Rubbish," "Parklife" featured a deeper sound, the distinctive guitars of Graham Coxon, and even more pronounced British lyrics and iconography, to the point it was nearly named "London." Its success was so significant that EMI fully acquired Food Records, ensuring they had one of the UK's leading bands under their complete control. The album received exceptional critical acclaim, with some British publications hailing it as an "instant British classic" and a staple of British culture, which was notable given that critics at the time were also focusing on American grunge, shoegaze, and Suede.
"publicaciones británicas que lo llamaron un clásico británico instantáneo, ya era un clásico de la cultura británica."

**The Great Escape (1995)** : This album is recognized as one of the most important works of the Britpop era in the 1990s. It solidified Blur's position as leaders and exponents of the movement, despite the band feeling increasingly fatigued and disillusioned by the pressures of the industry and public attention at this point.
"The Great Escape es uno de los mejores discos de los 90 de Bribbop."

**Blur (1997)** : Recorded outside the United Kingdom, this album marked Blur's definitive departure from the Britpop movement at its peak. It is considered a masterpiece with a lo-fi sound, and through it, Blur expressed a desire to completely distance themselves from anything related to Britpop. It's a curious case where the band that spearheaded the transition and entered the golden age of Britpop was also the first to abandon it.
"el disco Blur ya es casi yo creo un disco de lo-fi y Blur ya no querría volver a saber nada más del Britpop nunca más."

## songs
**She's So High (1990)** : Released in October 1990 as Blur's first official single, it is now considered an icon and a myth, one of their great songs. However, at the time of its release, it passed largely unnoticed, achieving only acceptable success for an independent band just starting out.
"Hoy es un icono, hoy es un mito, es una de las grandes canciones de Blur, pero en su época pasó un poco sin pena ni gloria."

**Boys & Girls (1994)** : Released in March 1994, this single catapulted Blur to immense success both within and outside the United Kingdom, becoming an anthem across Europe. Its release shortly before the "Parklife" album was instrumental in establishing Blur as a leading force in Britpop.
"Boy Sanca es en todo Europa, fue un himno."

## curiosities
**Early Financial Struggles (1992)** : Despite releasing a well-selling debut album, touring extensively, and playing successful concerts, Blur found themselves completely ruined and deeply in debt by mid-1992. This situation, now seemingly unthinkable, was a common problem due to "shameful" record contracts of the era, which also affected bands like The Smiths, who despite many albums and concerts, earned very little.
"Hoy nos parece impensable, pero Dios es que había unos contratos discográficos a aquellas que parecen una vergüenza."

**Early Entry into Britpop (Late 1980s)** : Blur began working very early, almost in 1989, and quickly started releasing music. Their early presence in London, a city not then a major hub for new bands like Manchester or Scotland, positioned them perfectly as labels in the city were actively seeking new talent.
"Blurio empiezan muy pronto, empiezan a trabajar ya prácticamente en el 89 y muy rápido consiguen empezar a publicar."

**Iconography of Britpop** : Blur, particularly with their album "Modern Life Is Rubbish," heavily incorporated classic British iconography into their promotions and album art. This included images of the Union Jack, Doc Martens boots, and Fred Perry polos, reflecting a growing obsession with British symbols that became characteristic of Britpop.
"Usaban muchísima iconografía clásica británica dentro incluso de las fotografías del libreto y de la promoción del disco."

**Fatigue and Disillusionment (1995)** : By 1995, as Britpop reached its peak, Blur, despite their success, arrived with a different attitude compared to fresh, younger bands like Oasis. They were fatigued from extensive touring, tired of record label pressure (specifically from EMI), disillusioned with the music industry, and experiencing internal problems, feeling like "old dogs" in the movement.
"Blur llegaban un tanto fatigado ya de tantas giras y tanta presión discográfica, de tanta presión de Emmy. Llegaban un tanto desencantados por el movimiento discográfico con algunos problemas internos."

Oasis, being quite young, entered the scene with an unstoppable and fresh energy that contrasted with the more seasoned Blur.

"para 1995 bandras como Asis entraban imparables movimientos britop porque eran bastante jóvenes."

# artist - Pulp

## curiosities
**Unusual Entry into Britpop** : Among the four main pillars of Britpop, Pulp stood out as a peculiar case because they were a much older band than the others, despite being identified as a defining act of the movement. Their long history before the Britpop era made their entry unique.
"palp es muchísimo más antiguo, pero es un caso muy raro."

# genre - Britpop

## curiosities
**Heterogeneous Nature** : Britpop, as a musical movement, was very heterogeneous, encompassing many bands with widely dispersed styles. Despite this diversity, four major pillars (Blur, Oasis, Suede, and Pulp) were identified as defining and leading the movement.
"El Britopo movimiento muy heterogeno tuvo muchísimas bandas, son tanto dispersas en estilo."

**Emergence as a Record Product** : The Britpop movement is often viewed and analyzed as a "record product," highlighting the strategic and commercial aspects of its development and popularization within the music industry.
"el Britpop como producto discográfico."

**Golden Age (1995)** : The Britpop movement reached its full peak and golden age around 1995, marked by significant album releases like Blur's "Parklife" (April 1994) and Oasis's "(What's the Story) Morning Glory?" (August 1995), which propelled the movement to its highest point of popularity and influence.
"yo creo que ya con Parly's estamos ya en plena época de la britop porque en Abril salía Parly's de Blur en agosto del mismo año de 1995 salía de Phil Klima y Videoasis."

**Defining Elements** : Two key elements that defined the sound of Britpop, particularly as solidified by Blur's "Modern Life Is Rubbish," were the distinctive guitar work (exemplified by Graham Coxon) and lyrical content rich in British themes and exaltation of the United Kingdom (as written by Damon Albarn).
"sonido de guitarras y las letras de argumentos típicos británicos que tanto le gustaron a Imogalba en cuando era joven."

**Decline and End (Circa 1997)** : The Britpop movement began to die around 1997. This period saw key bands like Blur starting to abandon the genre and pursue entirely different musical directions, signaling the end of Britpop's dominant era.
"el Bribbop ya se moría como miemiento."

# genre - Manchester sound

## curiosities
**Dominance in UK Music (1989-1990)** : At the end of the 1980s and early 1990s, a significant portion of the UK's musical movement was heavily concentrated in Manchester, experiencing the full explosion of the Manchester sound. This sound was still commercially resistant to dying by 1991, with Blur's debut album "Leisure" being criticized for still sounding like a remnant of this fading movement.
"casi todo el movimiento musical del Reino Unido de Aquella en el 89 y 90 estaba muy focalizado en dos sitios, estaba muy focalizado en Manchester donde teníamos toda la aclosión del movimiento Manchester."

# genre - Shoegaze

## curiosities
**Scottish Scene (Late 1980s)** : Scotland was a significant contributor to the shoegaze movement and other similar sounds in the late 1980s, producing influential bands like Cocteau Twins and The Jesus and Mary Chain. This entire artistic current was often referred to as "the sound of Scotland."
"Descocia había venido una parte importante y sumado de movimiento su guise y incluso el del hipópico. Los cocto Twins eran de eran de Escocia, de Jesus a Merichén eran de Escocia, en muchos sitios, toda esta vertiente se le conoce como el sonido Escocia."

# label - Food Records

## curiosities
**Early Blur Contract** : Food Records, a small independent record label based in London, signed Blur early in their career, offering them a contract for one album and some singles. They quickly began to back the band, releasing "She's So High" in October 1990.
"Una de las que es interesa por las que acaban fichando es por una pequeña discográfica independiente llamada Foot Records de Londres."

**Acquisition by EMI** : By mid-1992, EMI became a shareholder in Food Records, significantly boosting resources and promotion for Blur's second album. Following Blur's immense success with "Parklife," EMI fully acquired 100% of Food Records, eager to secure one of the UK's biggest bands under its complete ownership.
"la discográfica M y la gran multinacional M y una de las discográficas más importantes del mundo se convierten a accionista de fruit records."

# label - EMI

## curiosities
**Investment in Food Records (1992)** : EMI, a major multinational record label, became a shareholder in Food Records in mid-1992. This investment immediately translated into much greater resources and promotion for Blur's second album, "Modern Life Is Rubbish," including significant marketing push and interesting music videos.
"la discográfica M y la gran multinacional M y una de las discográficas más importantes del mundo se convierten a accionista de fruit records."

**Full Acquisition of Food Records (1994)** : Due to the overwhelming success of Blur, particularly with their album "Parklife," EMI decided to fully purchase Food Records, acquiring 100% ownership. This strategic move allowed EMI to have complete control over one of the UK's most successful and prominent bands at the time.
"Emmy acaba comprando 100% de fútbol de corzante el éxito de Blur, o sea, en ella quería tener enganchado una de las grandes bandas que estaban sello en el reino unido."

# curiosity

**Record Contracts of the Early 1990s** : The music industry in the early 1990s featured what were described as "shameful" record contracts. These agreements could lead to successful bands, like Blur and The Smiths, performing numerous concerts and selling many records, yet still finding themselves deeply in debt and earning very little money.
"Dios es que había unos contratos discográficos a aquellas que parecen a vergüenza."

**London's Music Scene (Late 1980s)** : In the late 1980s, London was not a significant hub for contributing new bands to the UK music scene, unlike Manchester with its explosion of the Madchester sound or Scotland with its shoegaze and "sound of Scotland" movements. However, its concentration of record labels meant it was a place where new talent could still be discovered.
"Londres era una ciudad que no estaba jugando demasiado papel aportando bandas nuevas a finales de los 80."

**UK Critical Landscape (Mid-1990s)** : In the mid-1990s, British music critics had diverse interests, not solely focusing on Britpop. While they recognized bands like Blur, they also gave strong reviews to grunge albums from the United States, various shoegaze artists, and notably, Suede, indicating a broader appreciation for different musical trends.
"la crítica en el reino unido de aquellas estaba yendo por otros caminos, me refiero y estaban dando grandes críticas a los grandes iscos de gran, si que venían de Estados Unidos, les estaban dando grandísimas críticas, todavía algunos iscos de Shugay's y les estaban dando muchísimo buen reconocimiento a sué."
