## albums
**Experience (1992) - Debut Album** : This was the band's first album, made exclusively by Liam Howlett, and was deeply rooted in hardcore techno. It was one of the very few, possibly the only, fast hardcore techno albums to achieve mainstream success and album sales, as most hardcore techno was single-driven. It sold millions of copies and was highly successful in European capitals where hardcore techno was established, such as the Netherlands, Berlin, and London, and to some extent Paris. It offered a slightly softened, more sample-heavy, and less aggressive version of fast hardcore techno, but was largely ignored or dismissed by electronic music critics who preferred more "intellectual" IDM, considering it sacrilegious. "It sold millions of copies and gave us an idea of what such fast hardcore techno could become if it wasn't so aggressive and was more focused on sampling."
**Music for the Jilted Generation (1994) - Breakthrough Album** : This second album, released two years after their debut, achieved immense success, sweeping across all of Europe, including Spain, and selling millions of copies. It emerged as hardcore techno was fading into niche circles and the Big Beat generation was rising, acting as a perfect blend of hardcore techno remnants and Big Beat culture. Unlike their instrumental debut, it featured more samples, less speed, and more spoken parts, making it a surprisingly complex album. Critics who had been resistant to their previous work could not ignore its relevance and were compelled to review it. The album incorporated a wide range of influences, including Rave, Dark Techno, German Industrial Music, Rock, and Hip Hop, and produced true anthems of 90s electronic music. "It was a truly relevant album, many people bought this album."
**The Fat of the Land (1997) - Masterpiece Album** : Released in 1997, this album is considered The Prodigy's masterpiece, one of the best electronic music albums ever, and one of the most relevant albums of the 1990s. With this record, The Prodigy took a significant leap forward, creating much more complex music. A crucial factor was Keith Flint's creative involvement; previously a mere figurehead, he contributed to the album's creative process, introducing a much more violent, punk and rock-influenced sound, with heavily distorted guitars and even some ska elements. This brought a very interesting renewal to The Prodigy's sound and a step forward in the iconography of their music videos. It became the quintessential "electronic punk album" and the album of an entire angry generation, selling millions worldwide despite many radio stations refusing to play it and its music videos being censored. "The Fat of the Land is the electronic punk album par excellence and it was the album of a generation."
**Always Outnumbered, Never Outgunned (2004) - Controversial Evolution** : Released in 2004 after a long period of silence and extensive touring, this album was primarily made by Liam Howlett alone. It received very mixed and polarizing reviews from fans and critics, with opinions ranging from enthusiastic approval to strong dislike. It was considered a controversial album for many Prodigy fans. The album represented a "sad but necessary evolution," as the hardcore techno culture in which The Prodigy originated had died out, and electronic music had moved in very different directions by the new millennium. Despite the initial controversy, the album's sound is considered "really current" even today, as recreating an album like "The Fat of the Land" in 2004 would not have made sense in the changed musical landscape. "It was a rather controversial album for Prodigy fans."

## curiosities
**Keith Flint's Creative Involvement (1997)** : Prior to 1997, Keith Flint was primarily a "figurehead" in The Prodigy, not involved in the creative process, a common arrangement in some bands. However, for "The Fat of the Land" (1997), he began working creatively with Liam Howlett, introducing a more punk and rock-influenced sound with distorted guitars and ska influences, which significantly evolved the band's music and visual iconography. "Keith Flint until 1997 was within The Prodigy but was mostly a figurehead... but precisely on this album, The Fat of the Land, Keith Flint began to work within the creative process of the album together with Liam Howlett."
**Genre Categorization Complexity** : The Prodigy is often categorized within Big Beat culture alongside bands like The Chemical Brothers and Fatboy Slim, but the band actually evolved from hardcore techno, mixing Big Beat elements while always remaining "a bit apart" from the core Big Beat culture. Classifying an album like "The Fat of the Land" solely as Big Beat is considered difficult due to differences in style, visuals, aesthetics, influences, and philosophy, as The Prodigy originated from hardcore techno, industrial, sampling, and punk. "It is frankly complicated to put an album like The Fat of the Land into Big Beat culture."
**Critical Reception of "Experience" (1992)** : The Prodigy's debut album was largely dismissed by electronic music critics of the time, who favored more "intellectual" IDM movements. They found the fast hardcore techno approach of "Experience" sacrilegious and often chose to ignore it. "Much of the electronic criticism didn't even want to pay attention to it."
**Cultural Impact of "Music for the Jilted Generation" (1994)** : Despite some radio stations refusing to play it and magazines declining to review it, "Music for the Jilted Generation" became immensely popular among young people across Europe. Its t-shirts were a common sight, indicating its widespread cultural impact. The album's "furious air," inherited from punk, and its industrial sound resonated deeply. "You'd be on the street and you'd see Music for the Jilted Generation t-shirts, that is, it was something that was on the lips of many young people."
**Cultural Impact of "The Fat of the Land" (1997)** : "The Fat of the Land" was described as the album of an "angry generation" who had not experienced punk and found grunge a bit outdated. It became a global phenomenon, selling millions, with its t-shirts ubiquitous despite widespread radio censorship and controversial music videos. The album caused a "true commotion" and was embraced by many. "The Fat of the Land is the electronic punk album par excellence and it was the album of a generation."
**Post-"The Fat of the Land" Career Challenges** : After the enormous success and a multi-year tour for "The Fat of the Land," The Prodigy entered a period of long silence. Their subsequent career became more challenging to categorize and was often questioned by critics and fans. This was largely due to the evolution of electronic music in the new millennium; the hardcore techno and punk sounds they championed, as well as Big Beat itself, had either died out or moved in different directions, forcing the band to evolve in ways that were not universally appreciated. "And from then on, a rather complicated career began for The Prodigy, who would never be what they once were."
**The Difficulty of Style Evolution**: The Prodigy faced the significant challenge of evolving their sound and maintaining relevance when the specific electronic styles they were associated with, like hardcore techno and Big Beat, had either "died" or shifted dramatically. Later attempts to revisit their classic hardcore techno/punk sound were considered illogical given the 15-20 year gap and the changed musical landscape. "It's difficult to survive the death of a style."

# genre - Hardcore Techno

## curiosities
**Origins and Characteristics in Europe** : Hardcore Techno emerged in Europe as an extreme variant of techno, heavily influenced by a severe drug problem that "wiped out entire generations." People under the influence accelerated already rapid techno (120-140 BPM) to extreme tempos, reaching 170-180 BPM, and sometimes exceeding 200 BPM. This high-speed, intense sound was often considered "unbearable to listen to" by those not altered by drugs. "Europe had a very serious drug problem. Here drugs wiped out entire generations. And one of the effects it had was hardcore techno."
**Focus on Singles Over Albums** : Nearly all hardcore techno was released and consumed via singles. The Prodigy's debut album, "Experience" (1992), was noted as one of the very few, possibly the only, hardcore techno album of its kind to successfully cross into the mainstream and achieve significant album sales and impact. "Almost all hardcore techno was worked through singles. I think it was almost the first time this album was made as a hardcore techno album and had quite an impact."
**Decline by Mid-1990s** : By 1994, hardcore techno was in decline, receding into much smaller, niche circles of enthusiasts. This decline coincided with a general shift in techno towards softer, slower, and less aggressive sounds, and the rise of the Big Beat genre. "Hardcore techno was dying and was going into much more minority circles of people who continued to embrace that sound."

# genre - Techno

## curiosities
**European Explosion in the Late 1980s** : Originating in Detroit, techno arrived in Europe, particularly London, in the late 1980s. This migration led to a period described as "the great day for techno," where the genre rapidly diversified and multiplied into dozens, almost hundreds, of sub-styles in less than a decade. "Techno leaves Detroit and arrives in Europe and arrives in London... the great day for techno."
**Shift Towards Softer Sounds by Mid-1990s** : By 1994, while hardcore techno was dying out, the broader techno genre itself was also evolving. It began moving towards slightly softer, slower, and less aggressive sounds, departing from the extreme, rapid paths that characterized its earlier, more intense forms. "Techno was going back to slightly softer, slightly slower paths, it was not going to such aggressive paths."

# genre - Big Beat

## curiosities
**Emergence and Characteristics in the Mid-1990s** : The Big Beat generation, including artists like The Chemical Brothers and Fatboy Slim, began to emerge around 1994. These artists were typically DJs who created a symbiosis of electronic music by blending it with rock and British indie rock culture. "Just in those years the Big Beat generation of The Chemical Brothers and Fatboy Slim was already appearing."
**Stylistic Contrast with The Prodigy** : Despite often being grouped together, The Prodigy's "The Fat of the Land" (1997) is considered difficult to classify within Big Beat culture. Unlike Big Beat artists who mixed DJ electronics with classic rock and indie, The Prodigy's sound originated from hardcore techno, industrial music, sampling, and punk, exhibiting distinct stylistic, visual, aesthetic, and philosophical differences. "Big Beat artists like Groove Armada or The Chemical Brothers or Fatboy Slim, they were DJs who had mixed with rock and with indie culture, with British classic rock bands and had made that symbiosis of electronic DJ and rock."
**Decline in the Late 1990s** : The Big Beat genre itself began to die out towards the end of the 1990s, necessitating evolution for artists associated with the style as the musical landscape shifted into the new millennium. "Even Big Beat itself began to die out by the late 90s."

# genre - IDM (Intelligent Dance Music)

## curiosities
**Critical Preference in Early 1990s** : In the early 1990s, electronic music critics showed a strong preference for IDM, which they regarded as a more "intellectual" and sophisticated version of techno. This preference led them to dismiss or ignore hardcore techno albums, such as The Prodigy's debut "Experience," often viewing them as sacrilegious or not worthy of serious critical attention. "They were doing movements like IDM, with which they were making very intellectual versions of techno."

# curiosity

**Genre Classification Fluidity** : The speaker emphasizes that the categorization of musical genres is often highly interpretive. This is highlighted in the discussion of whether albums like The Prodigy's "The Fat of the Land" fit into Big Beat culture, acknowledging that while some might categorize it as such, valid arguments exist for different classifications due to diverse stylistic and philosophical elements. "In the end all these things are very interpretive."
**Impact of European Drug Problem on Music** : A severe drug problem in Europe during the late 1980s and early 1990s had a profound impact on the music scene, contributing directly to the emergence of hardcore techno. Drug users, seeking to match their altered states, would accelerate already fast techno music to extremely high tempos, creating an intense sound that shaped a generation, even as it "wiped out entire generations" through its societal impact. "Europe had a very serious drug problem. Here drugs wiped out entire generations. And one of the effects it had was hardcore techno."
**Challenge of Surviving a Musical Style's Demise** : For bands like The Prodigy, navigating their career after the specific musical styles they championed (such as hardcore techno and Big Beat) either "died out" or evolved significantly became a major challenge. The speaker notes the difficulty in adapting and the impracticality of attempting to revert to a sound 15-20 years after its peak, underscoring the constant pressure on artists to evolve or fade. "It's difficult to survive the death of a style."
