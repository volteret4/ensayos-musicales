# artist - Thomas Edison

## curiosities
**Phonograph Invention (1876)** : Around 1876, Thomas Edison invented the phonograph, a device for recording and reproducing sound using cylindrical tubes. This invention marked a foundational moment in audio history, though his initial cylindrical format would later be deemed a significant failure in the evolution of music formats. "Thomas Edison inventaba el phonógrafo."
**Cylindrical Format - First Major Failure** : Thomas Edison initially developed his phonograph using cylindrical tubes, a decision driven by a complex technical challenge with flat discs. While cylinders allowed for a constant angular speed with linear needle movement, they proved impractical for storing sound, leading to their abandonment and marking them as the first major failure in music formats. "Esos cilindros, evidentemente no lo usamos por lo que el primer grandísimo fracaso que tuvo el formato de la música."
**Flat Disc Speed Problem (Late 19th Century)** : Thomas Edison's initial attempt to use flat discs for recording faced a critical technical hurdle: he observed that while a vinyl disc spins at a constant angular speed, the needle's reproduction speed is not constant. This complex issue led him to temporarily set aside the flat disc concept in favor of cylindrical recordings. "Thomas Edison se dio cuenta de que el problema del disco plano era que la velocidad angular no era la misma que la velocidad de reproducción de la aguja."

## curiosities
**Sound Recording Innovations (Late 19th Century)** : Towards the end of the 19th century, around 1895, Bernliner significantly advanced sound recording technology by discovering a superior method for engraving sound into grooves. He is recognized as the inventor of sound formats, the process of sound recording, and the first gramophones, effectively laying the groundwork for subsequent physical music formats. "Bernliner descubrió una mejor manera de grabar el sonido en los surcos."
**Shellac Disc Invention (Late 19th Century)** : Bernliner revived Thomas Edison's original idea of a flat disc, inventing the first "vinyl" disc made from shellac and mineral, often pizarra (slate), meaning early records were literally stone. These discs were remarkably resistant to the erosion from rudimentary steel needles and recording methods, but the nascent understanding of plastic materials made them costly and heavy precursors to modern vinyl. "Bernliner inventó el disco de goma lacacom mineral, que casi siempre solía ser pizarra."
**Early Flat Disc Commercial Failure** : Despite Bernliner's innovations, his early flat discs, composed of shellac and mineral, suffered a significant commercial failure. They were prohibitively expensive, extremely heavy, and highly susceptible to breaking when dropped, eventually being superseded by plastic vinyl discs as advancements in plastic material knowledge and casting techniques emerged in the early 20th century. "acabó siendo un enorme fracasso, eran carísimos, eran pesadísimos, se rompían al caral suelo."

# label - Victor

## curiosities
**Founding and Physical Format Hegemony** : Founded by Bernliner, the Victor company quickly established itself as a "mother absolute" in the realm of physical music formats, making pivotal contributions to the development of sound recording and early gramophones. Victor's influence grew further after its merger with RCA, collectively driving significant disc improvements until the mid-1970s. "luego fundaría la casa Victor, madre absoluta de los formatos físicos prácticamente."

# label - RCA

## curiosities
**Merger and Disc Technology Innovation** : RCA fused with Bernliner's Victor company, forming a powerful entity that became instrumental in the discovery and investment in disc technology improvements. This collaboration led to significant advancements in recording quality and format, maintaining a leadership role in the industry until approximately the mid-1970s. "luego se fusionaría con la RCA, y hasta prácticamente mitad de los 70 serían los grandes descubridores y inversores de las mejoras que teníamos en el disco."

# curiosity

**Evolution of Vinyl Disc Standards (Pre-1970s)** : The journey to the universally accepted vinyl disc standard was protracted and intricate, marked by numerous format variations and commercial failures before the "dinaflase" standard emerged around the mid-1970s. This standard, which effectively ended the complex transition period, became the benchmark for vinyl's long reign and remains in use today in some capacity. "para llegar al disco de vinilo hubo una enorme complejísima transición de muchísimas versiones de formatos de discos de vinilo hasta casi mitad de los 70, cuando conseguimos el dinaflase."
**War of Speeds (Early 20th Century)** : The advent of plastic flat discs sparked a "war of speeds" among recording companies, each adopting different reproduction rates. The 78 revolutions per minute (RPM) speed initially dominated, offering superior sound quality due to a larger surface area for audio waves, but this came at the cost of severely limited playback duration, typically allowing only 3-5 minutes or a single song per disc. "empezaba la guerra de las velocidades, y es que según las disintas empresas grababan la música distintas velocidades de reproducción."
**78 RPM Decline and Failed Revival Attempts** : The 78 RPM format, despite its superior sound fidelity, was eventually phased out in favor of slower speeds like 45 and 33 RPM, which offered significantly longer playback times on a single disc. Subsequent attempts to reintroduce 78 RPMs during the 1960s, 70s, and even 80s, leveraging improved recording methodologies, ultimately failed because most existing turntables were not equipped to handle that speed. "se acabó abandonando el formato de 78 revoluciones por minuto, fue un enorme paso atrás en la calidad musical, pero por primera vez podíamos grabar más de una canción en un disco."
**In-Car Vinyl Players (1950s-1970s)** : From the 1960s through the 1970s, an ambitious but ultimately failed commercial endeavor saw the integration of vinyl record players into cars, a concept that originated in the 1950s. While technical solutions, such as a spring system, addressed the issue of needle skipping caused by vehicle movement, the players were prohibitively expensive and prone to damaging the soft vinyl discs over time due to the strong needle pressure, rendering them a commercial disaster and an automotive eccentricity. "Durante los 60 y 70 tuvimos otro grandísimo fracaso comercial que fue el reproductor de Vinilos dentro de los coches."
**Flexi-Disc - Portable Audio Failure** : The flexi-disc, a thin and flexible plastic sheet capable of recording sound, represented the closest attempt at a truly portable vinyl experience but was a significant commercial failure despite its widespread promotional use. Developed through advancements in recording and pressing, these economical discs were often distributed free in magazines or by companies like McDonald's for advertising. However, their critical flaws—poor sound quality due to delicate grooves and extremely low durability—prevented any major company from adopting them as a viable music format. "Fue lo más parecido al primer sistema de audio portátil que podías tener pero que no tuvo ningún éxito la verdad."
**Flexi-Disc Promotional Hegemony** : Despite its resounding commercial failure as a standalone music format, the flexi-disc achieved a "grandísima hegemonía" in the realm of audio advertising and promotion. Billions of these discs were produced and widely distributed for free in magazines (much like CDs were given away in magazines like NME in the 1990s, but earlier) and by brands such as McDonald's, who famously used them for promotions like the "million-dollar record," making them a notable collectible today. "si fue una grandísima gemonía porque tuvieron muchísima popularidad haciendo anuncios de audio."
