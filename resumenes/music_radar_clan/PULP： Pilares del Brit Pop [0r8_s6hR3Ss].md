## albums
**It (1983)** : This was one of Pulp's early albums released on the independent label Fall Records. During this period, Pulp was described as undergoing many stylistic changes and their early records often received very poor or no critical reviews. "The first records of Palt are quite turbulent, they make many changes, they are on very bad record labels with very little repercussion and had very bad reviews."
**Separations (1992)** : This album marked a small achievement for Pulp as it was one of the first of their early works to actually receive reviews in British music magazines, an improvement over many of their even earlier unreviewed releases. The reviews for "Separations" singles, however, were mostly negative. At this point, the band members were older than their Brit-Pop contemporaries like Suede or Blur, despite their young start. "after these first reviews on separations they weren't that old although they had been many years because they started very young."
**His 'n' Hers (1994)** : This album achieved significant success and garnered considerable critical attention, though opinions on Jarvis Cocker's singing style were notably divided. While some critics found his voice "terribly bad," it contributed to Pulp's growing iconic status within the 90s UK music scene. "Palt does not have bad reviews but there are some that are very bad and in some there are some comments that are somewhat hurtful as some critics refer to how terribly bad the voice is, how terribly bad they considered that Jarvis Cocker sang."
**Different Class (1995)** : This album is widely considered a masterpiece and a fantastic record, released during a prolific six-month period that also saw the release of other significant albums like Oasis's "(What's the Story) Morning Glory?". Its commercial success was heavily influenced by the single "Common People," solidifying its status as an "icon" within the UK. "the reviews considered it a masterpiece, it was a fantastic album."
**Countdown (1996)** : This compilation album was released by Pulp's former independent label, Fall Records, in March-April 1996, approximately six months after the highly successful "Different Class." It collected early, "dark" material from 1981 to 1992, an era Jarvis Cocker was deeply ashamed of, finding some tracks like "Doctors of the Universe" embarrassing. He actively tried to prevent its release, and publicly urged fans not to buy it in interviews, but Fall Records' contract allowed them to proceed. The album received horrible reviews, sold poorly, and was eventually withdrawn from the market. Today, due to its scarcity, it is a highly sought-after collector's item for Pulp fans. "the poor Jarvis Cocker was bitter about it, he explicitly asked people in interviews not to buy the compilation because he was quite ashamed of some songs like Doctors."
**This Is Hardcore (1998)** : This album marked a significant departure from the Brit-Pop sound for Pulp, as Jarvis Cocker embraced a "modern experimental" approach, influenced by artists like Scott Walker. It is personally regarded by the speaker as the best album of Pulp's career, featuring passionate songs and moments where Jarvis Cocker's vocals evoke a James Bond-esque style. "Jarvis Cocker passes completely the Brit-Pop and already gets into almost a modern experimental singer full of charisma that have passionate songs within this album."
**We Love Life (2001)** : Following "This Is Hardcore," this album continued Pulp's evolution away from Brit-Pop, aligning with Jarvis Cocker's personal artistic path, though it shared some stylistic similarities with its predecessor. "it also has nothing to do with Brit-Pop although it is more similar to This Is Hardcore but it follows Jarvis Cocker's personal path."
**Pulpations (2006)** : This compilation album officially released two of Pulp's earliest professional recordings from 1981, which are considered the oldest official recordings of the band. "In that album we have two songs from 1981 which is the oldest official recording we have of the band."

## songs
**Doctors of the Universe (from It)** : This early song from Pulp's "It" album was explicitly cited by Jarvis Cocker as one he found "horrible" and deeply embarrassing, leading to his strong opposition to its inclusion on the "Countdown" compilation. "you listen to the first albums of Palt and you listen to songs like Doxar Every One which are horrible."
**Babies (early 90s)** : This was one of Pulp's first significant hits after signing with Gift Records and working with producer Ed Buller. It received a music video, generated considerable buzz, and successfully entered the UK charts, marking a turning point in the band's career by achieving a Top 40 position. "the first singles that come out with Gif Records come out Babies, Razzmatazz, Lips, Duy, a imembracéstein, those great first hits of Palt begin to come out."
**Razzmatazz (early 90s)** : As one of the initial successful singles released with Gift Records, "Razzmatazz" contributed to Pulp's burgeoning popularity. It was accompanied by a music video and began to achieve chart success, typically entering the Top 40, which helped establish their presence in the UK music scene. "the first singles that come out with Gif Records come out Babies, Razzmatazz, Lips, Duy, a imembracéstein, those great first hits of Palt begin to come out."
**Lipgloss (early 90s)** : This track was among the early hits released by Pulp after their signing with Gift Records, signifying their breakthrough into mainstream recognition. It featured a music video and managed to chart within the Top 40, illustrating their growing influence and commercial viability. "the first singles that come out with Gif Records come out Babies, Razzmatazz, Lips, Duy, a imembracéstein, those great first hits of Palt begin to come out."
**Do You Remember the First Time? (early 90s)** : Released as one of Pulp's initial successes with Gift Records, this song played a crucial role in expanding their audience and chart presence. It gained traction through a music video and entered the Top 40, becoming an early indicator of their progressive rise to fame. "the first singles that come out with Gif Records come out Babies, Razzmatazz, Lips, Duy, a imembracéstein, those great first hits of Palt begin to come out."
**Common People (1995)** : This song, released as a single from "Different Class," became an immense phenomenon in the UK, described as "a madness." Despite not reaching number one on the charts, it achieved iconic status and is now an anthem frequently played in British clubs and bars, where it makes people "go crazy." Jarvis Cocker's performance in its music video was particularly memorable. "Common People was a madness within the United Kingdom."
**Help the Aged (1997)** : Following the success of "Different Class," Jarvis Cocker faced a difficult period marked by creative block and a cocaine addiction. During this time, "Help the Aged" was reportedly the only song he managed to complete over several months, highlighting his struggles before he improved his health and drug problems. "the only thing Jarvis Cocker managed to release was the song Help the Aged."

## curiosities
**Early Formation and Struggles (1981-1992)** : Pulp formed very young, around ages 15-16, in Sheffield, at roughly the same time as bands like Joy Division and Siouxsie and the Banshees, predating even New Order. Despite their early start, they struggled for approximately 15 years to achieve success, spending over a decade experimenting with various styles like New Wave, synthesizers, and Post-Punk, and releasing early albums on small, ineffective labels with poor or no critical reception. "Palt is a very curious group because although it was one of the pillars of Brit-Pop Palt was forming almost at the same time as groups like Joy Division or like Suixion de Bansis then."
**Jarvis Cocker's Dream of Stardom** : The entire history of Pulp is deeply intertwined with Jarvis Cocker's personal quest to become a major star, driven not primarily by exceptional musical talent or ambition, but by a profound desire for superstardom against significant odds. This ambition defined Pulp's early, often difficult, years. "all the history of the band is summarized in the personal history of Jarvis Cocker fighting to pursue a dream that is to be a great star."
**Jarvis Cocker's Iconic Voice** : While Jarvis Cocker's vocal style received mixed and sometimes "terribly bad" criticism upon the release of "His 'n' Hers," particularly from a significant portion of critics who were "horrified" by his singing, his voice eventually became an iconic and distinctive element of 90s UK music. "the way Jarvis Cocker sings is already an icon of the 90s in the United Kingdom."
**Jarvis Cocker's Charismatic Persona** : Jarvis Cocker was a uniquely British figure, embodying an "old school" Britishness with an eccentric and strange yet highly charismatic and attractive personality. His interviews from 1993-94 showed him to be engaging and humorous, contributing significantly to Pulp's appeal. "Jarvis Cocker was a character, there are few people more British than Jarvis Cocker."
**Pulp's Brit-Pop Identity** : Within the Brit-Pop movement, Pulp carved out a distinct niche, representing a more "kitsch" sensibility, sometimes drawing comparisons to acts like The B-52s or bizarre New Wave. This unique stylistic path allowed them to stand apart from other bands like Blur (more agile, fun) or Oasis (more classic British rock). "if Blur was that more agile, more movement, more agile, more fun British, and Oasis was that more classic British movement of Paul Weller and the Rolling Stones, if The Jam, then Pulp was that more kitsch movement, which sometimes reminded of The B-52s, sometimes reminded of that somewhat bizarre New Wave."
**Post-Brit-Pop Evolution** : Pulp, unlike some of their Brit-Pop contemporaries, successfully transitioned out of the genre, refusing to be confined by a dying movement. Their long history of stylistic experimentation prepared them for this shift, leading to a new musical direction for Jarvis Cocker inspired by Scott Walker. "Palp was one of those bands that managed to escape Brit-Pop quite quickly and not look back and not get stuck in a dying movement."
**Jarvis Cocker's Cocaine Addiction (1997)** : In 1997, Jarvis Cocker struggled with a severe cocaine addiction, which significantly impacted the band's performances and creative output. Recordings from their concerts that year show some to be "truly pathetic," with anecdotes like Jarvis Cocker vomiting on stage in Spain. This addiction also contributed to a creative block, where he struggled to write new material after "Different Class." "a serious problem was the addition to cocaine by Jarvis Cocker that caused during a year, well, there are recordings of the concerts in 1997 of Palt that some are truly pathetic."
**The Truth and Beauty, The True Story of Pulp** : This 700-page book is considered the only reliable and accurate biography of Pulp. Unlike many bands, Pulp does not have an official biography, largely due to Jarvis Cocker's deliberate effort to not recall or provide much information about the band's first ten to twelve years. This book is the primary source for all serious articles and documentation about the band. "the only correct biography we have of Palt which has 700 pages which is called The Truth and Beauty, The Truth Story of Palt."

## curiosities
**Early Ambition for Stardom** : Jarvis Cocker's primary motivation for starting Pulp was not necessarily musical talent or grand musical aspirations, but rather an intense personal dream of becoming a "superstar." This ambition fueled his persistence through the band's challenging first 15 years, despite having "everything against him." "Jarvis Cocker is not someone who had great musical concerns or great musical talent, but rather the dream of a person who wanted to be a superstar and who had everything against him."
**Iconic 90s UK Persona** : Jarvis Cocker became an iconic figure in 90s Britain, known for his distinct "old school" Britishness. He possessed an eccentric and somewhat strange, yet highly charismatic and attractive personality, making him a captivating presence in interviews during the mid-90s. "Jarvis Cocker was one of those great British characters in the 90s."
**Impact of Drug Addiction on Creativity** : Following the success of "Different Class," Jarvis Cocker developed a severe cocaine addiction in 1997. This addiction, along with other internal band issues, led to a creative paralysis where he struggled to write new material, managing to produce only the song "Help the Aged" over several months. This period significantly impacted Pulp's ability to record their next album. "during a year, well, there are recordings of the concerts in 1997 of Palt that some are truly pathetic, that Jarvis Cocker ended up vomiting on stage here in Spain in one of the concerts."
**Artistic Evolution Beyond Brit-Pop** : As the Brit-Pop movement declined, Jarvis Cocker deliberately steered his artistic direction away from its confines, exploring new musical territories. He delved into an earlier interest in artists like Scott Walker, ultimately evolving into a "modern experimental singer," a style evident in albums like "This Is Hardcore." "Jarvis Cocker began to work in a totally different way, delving into that stage that he had liked so much at the beginning of being Scott Walker of those great charismatic classics."
**Unsuccessful James Bond Theme Song Submission** : Jarvis Cocker was approached to write a theme song for a James Bond film and did record a version, but it was ultimately rejected. This anecdote highlights a lesser-known aspect of his creative output and his willingness to explore diverse projects. "Jarvis Cocker was about to make one of the songs for one of the James Bond films but was finally rejected but he did record it."
**Successful Solo Career** : After Pulp, Jarvis Cocker embarked on an "incredible solo career," characterized by his continued charisma and unique artistic vision. "after this already Pulp is on a completely different path from Brit-Pop and would not look back after This Is Hardcore would come out We Love Life that also has nothing to do with Brit-Pop although it is more similar to This Is Hardcore but it follows Jarvis Cocker's personal path and then we would have the incredible solo career of Jarvis Cocker in which he is full of charisma."

# genre - Brit-Pop

## curiosities
**Golden Age and Pillars** : The mid-1990s are described as a "golden age" for Brit-Pop as a record label movement, characterized by four main "pillars" or leading bands, of which Pulp was one. "this small special on that golden age of Brit-Pop, of Brit-Pop as a record movement and these four small specials of the four pillars of Brit-Pop."
**Movement Decline (1996-1997)** : The Brit-Pop movement began to decline around 1996-1997, with its sound "making water" as public interest shifted to other things. Bands like Pulp and Blur were notable for successfully escaping the movement and not getting "stuck" in its dying sound. "the Brit-Pop movement was falling apart, people were already talking about other things."

# label - Fall Records

## curiosities
**Early Pulp Management and Exploitation** : Fall Records was an independent label that managed Pulp for nearly ten years, releasing their first three albums (roughly 1981-1992). Despite this long association, the label never supported the band, providing no resources or backing. Upon seeing Pulp's later success, Fall Records controversially released the "Countdown" compilation in 1996 without the band's consent, purely for profit, much to Jarvis Cocker's dismay. "Fall Records, which is an independent record company that was the one that carried the almost first 10 years of Palt's career with which it released the first three albums and which never paid any attention to it and never gave it resources and never bet on them."

# label - Gift Records

## curiosities
**Catalyst for Pulp's Success** : Gift Records was an independent label that signed Pulp, marking the definitive beginning of the band's progressive success. This signing allowed Pulp to start working with producer Ed Buller, who had previously produced Suede's successful early albums, leading to their first significant hits. "the success of Palt begins when they manage to sign with Gif Records, which is an independent record label, but that would mark the beginning of Palt's progressive success."
