## albums
**Mellon Collie and the Infinite Sadness (1995) - Conceptual Shift** : This album is considered a key piece for understanding 90s music in the United States. For its production, the band decided to replace their previous producer, Butch Vig, with two European producers, Alan Moulder and Flood (Mark Ellis), which was a significant risk and a major change for the album. The European producers revolutionized the recording process, making the band start from scratch due to fundamental differences in production techniques between Europe and the United States in the 90s. Mark Ellis advised the band to abandon their previous process, focusing instead on a more track-divided recording approach that prioritized well-recorded individual tracks. He also suggested dedicating a couple of hours daily to improvisation, creating new material without sheet music, which directly led to the creation of significant instrumental pieces like the album's title track. Alan Moulder, from day one, challenged Billy Corgan to define a guiding theme or narrative for the album, an idea that deeply resonated with Corgan but was complex given that most of the album was already composed. This pursuit of a conceptual thread led them to change some songs and discard over half of the existing material. The chosen theme for the album became "the sadness and personal frustration a person suffers throughout their life." The final guitar arrangements, especially the use of pedals and echo effects, aligned much more with 90s European rock production styles than with those prevalent in the US, a direction Billy Corgan greatly appreciated. "el meloncoli de los esmas simpánquis es una pizza clave para entender la música que se hizo en los 90 dentro de los Estados Unidos."
**Early Albums Production by Butch Vig** : The Smashing Pumpkins achieved significant early success by collaborating with Butch Vig as a producer for their initial albums. Butch Vig was considered one of the most sought-after producers in the United States at the time. "Una de los grandes secretos que tuvieron los esmas simpánquis para tener tanto éxito desde el miicio fue que tuvieron la increíble suerte de contar con Batch Pick como productor para sus primeros álbumes que eran los hombres más cotizados en la producción en Estados Unidos."
**Democratic Recording Process for Mellon Collie** : Thanks to the intervention of Mark Ellis (Flood), the recording of "Mellon Collie and the Infinite Sadness" became much more democratic. James Iha had significant input into guitar arrangements and even had a song composed by him included on the album. Other band members also contributed ideas to the production work, even if not officially credited as producers. "Marquelys, que hizo mucho desicólogo dentro de la grabación, fue una persona que intervino mucho para ayudar a que la grabación del Melon Collir fue mucho más democrática."
**Multi-Track Recording for Mellon Collie** : Mark Ellis (Flood) instructed Billy Corgan to significantly increase their recording resources for the complex multi-track process they desired. This involved using multiple recording rooms simultaneously, allowing for separate tracking of elements like Billy Corgan's vocals and James Iha's guitar arrangements. Furthermore, even within a single song, guitar tracks had to be subdivided into multiple sub-tracks for meticulous layering and differentiation. "necesitaban varias salas de grabación al mismo tiempo, donde una persona pudiese estar ganando la voz de Bili Corgan mientras al Jens Hyatt, por ejemplo, podía estar ganando a arreglar los editarra y, sobre todo, incluso las pistas de editarra dentro de una misma canción tenían que serán subes divididas en pistas de las pistas de editarra dentro de la misma canción."

## songs
**Mellon Collie and the Infinite Sadness (1995) - Instrumental Result of Improvisation** : The instrumental title track of the album, primarily composed by Billy Corgan on piano, emerged directly from the daily improvisation sessions that the band conducted for a couple of hours each day as suggested by producer Mark Ellis (Flood). This piece is said to represent Billy Corgan's personal sadness and frustration. "la piza instrumental con la gran caldisco que se llama igual que el disco y que más compuso Bili Corgan con el piano, que además representa a la tristeza y la frustración personal que siente Bili Corgan dentro del mundo, fue objeto de estas olas de improvisación que se hacían durante todos los días."

## curiosities
**Producer Change Risk for Mellon Collie** : For the production of "Mellon Collie and the Infinite Sadness," The Smashing Pumpkins made the bold decision to change their producer, letting go of Butch Vig, who was considered the most important producer in the country at the time. They instead chose two European producers, Alan Moulder and Flood (Mark Ellis). "Se regaron un montón porque prestindieron del productor más importante que había en su país para trabajar con un nuevo productor."
**Billy Corgan's Despotic Work Style** : Billy Corgan was known for his "incredibly despotic" way of working with the band members. "la increíblemente despota forma de trabajar que tenía Bili Corgan con el grupo."
**Mark Ellis's (Flood) Psychological Role** : During the recording of "Mellon Collie and the Infinite Sadness," producer Mark Ellis (Flood) acted as a "psychologist" within the band, intervening significantly to help make the recording process more democratic. "Marquelys, que hizo mucho desicólogo dentro de la grabación, fue una persona que intervino mucho para ayudar a que la grabación del Melon Collir fue mucho más democrática."
**Mellon Collie's Distinctive Sound** : One of Mark Ellis's (Flood) significant contributions was making "Mellon Collie and the Infinite Sadness" sound so distinct from the rest of The Smashing Pumpkins' career. This distinctiveness, including the difference from typical 90s US guitar sounds, is entirely attributable to the production work. "porque es una tan distinto el Melon Collir del resto de la carrera los es más impantes."

# instrument - Guitar

## curiosities
**90s US Guitar Production Style** : In the early 90s, guitar production prevalent in the United States typically involved heavily blended guitars, strong, intertwined echo sounds, and often excessively distorted pedal effects. This resulted in guitar tracks that were hard to distinguish, often layering over each other with a huge, distorting echo effect. "la producción sobre todo de guitarras que gustaban Estados Unidos en antes los 90 era muy basada en guitarras que se entre mezclan, ecosonidos muy fuertes, muy entre mezclados e incluso efectos de pedal demasiado distorsionados."
**90s European Guitar Production Style** : In contrast to the US, 90s European guitar production favored much drier and more differentiated sounds, where individual guitars were easily distinguishable. This style avoided the heavy echo or intertwined track effects common in US production. "Mientras que en Europa gustaban los nidos mucho más secos, mucho más diferenciados donde las guitarras son fáciles de distinguirlas unas de las otras."

# curiosity

**90s US vs. European Production Processes** : In the 90s, the production processes in Europe and the United States were very distinct, to the point that producers would often have bands restart recordings from scratch when transitioning between continents. This contrasts with today's globalized music industry, where production methods are much more similar worldwide. "los procesos de producción que se hacían en Europa y en Estados Unidos eran muy distintos en los 90."
**Album Conceptualization Differences (90s US vs. Europe)** : In the 90s, UK albums, and European productions generally, were often characterized by a strong conceptual thread or narrative idea that developed throughout the songs. Pink Floyd's "The Wall" is cited as a clear example of this approach. In contrast, US productions tended to focus simply on creating good individual songs and compiling them into a solid album. "en el rinonido tiene un perfecto hilo conductor, de hecho, Andy Wall de Pink Floyd es un claro ejemplo de cómo se grababan el rinonido y en toda Europa en general todo el proceso de grabación con un hilo argumental, una idea sólida que se va desarrollando las canciones mientras que en Estados Unidos se limitaban a hacer canciones buenas a ponerlas en un buen álbum y a sacarlo."
