## albums
**Low (1977) - First Berlin Trilogy Album** : Recorded in 1976 and released in 1977, this was the initial album of Bowie's Berlin Trilogy, though most of its recording did not take place in Berlin, nor with "Dayan." The album came together from numerous unfinished or rejected projects, including outtakes from "Station to Station," an abandoned soundtrack for "The Man Who Fell to Earth," and material from "Honky Dory." Recording sessions occurred in Los Angeles, his personal studio in Geneva, and Paris, with Berlin primarily serving as a place for idea development. Musically, it incorporated influences from German Krautrock artists like Kraftwerk, Neu!, and Cluster, despite lacking direct lyrical references to Berlin.
"Este sería el primer disco que formaría la trilogía de Berlín de David Bowie, aunque hay muchas discrepancias y mucha gente que discrepa de esto, porque curiosamente low no se grabó prácticamente nada en Berlín ni siquiera con Dayan."

**Low (1977) - Credits Controversy** : The album's credits were minimal, with Bowie appearing almost in solo. Brian Eno was credited for some songs and production, but not as a composer for many of his contributions. This led to controversy, as many uncredited artists who were part of the creative and recording process claimed involvement. This lack of clear attribution was likely due to the dispersed recording across multiple locations over a long period, changing personnel, and Bowie's struggles with addiction at the time, making it difficult for him to track who participated.
"el disco apareció muy poco agreditado y sale David Bowie casi en solitario."

**Low (1977) - Instrumental Second Side** : The second part of the album is largely instrumental and bears a strong resemblance to Brian Eno's solo work from the late 1970s rather than typical David Bowie material.
"la segunda parte del album low que es prácticamente instrumental, parece más un disco de Braian enno de la etapa de final de los 70 que es un disco de David Bowie."

**Low (1977) - Release Challenges and RCA's Reluctance** : The album's release was delayed until 1977 due to problems Bowie encountered with both his current and former managers who still had financial interests. RCA initially refused to release the album, not considering it a guaranteed success despite Bowie's track record of producing chart-topping hits. The label suggested Bowie rework the album with more commercially appealing tracks.
"La decisión inicial de la RCA fue no lanzar este disco porque no lo consideraba un éxito, no lo consideraba una puesta segura."

**Low (1977) - Critical and Commercial Success** : Despite Bowie's refusal to change the album and RCA's initial reservations, "Low" was released as he intended and achieved significant critical and commercial success. It was considered an "instant classic" upon release and performed surprisingly well in sales, exceeding expectations for its experimental and risky nature, although it did not reach the monumental chart positions of his previous "grand hits." Bowie himself was reportedly surprised by its success.
"el disco es un éxito muy importante, es un grandísimo éxito de crítica, se considera una casi un clásico instantáneo en el día que sale y sorprendentemente también es un importante éxito de ventas y llega a posicionarse bastante bien"

**Heroes (1977) - Berlin Trilogy Core** : Work on "Heroes" began almost immediately after "Low" was published in 1977. It is widely considered the true "Berlin album" of the trilogy, being the only one recorded entirely in Berlin. The recording process was very rapid, completed in just a few weeks, indicative of the strong working relationship between Bowie and Brian Eno, who had a clear vision.
"posiblemente sea el disco de la trilogía de Berlín que es realmente de Berlín porque fue el único disco de la trilogía de Berlín que fue grabado en Berlín."

**Heroes (1977) - Berlin Thematic Content** : Unlike "Low," "Heroes" is rich with continuous lyrical and thematic references to Germany and Berlin. These include allusions to the Berlin Wall, the divided city, the communist era, and the lack of freedom in the communist sector. This strong thematic connection to the city solidifies its reputation as the "heart of the Berlin Trilogy."
"el giros tiene muchas referencias a la situación del muro de Berlín a la situación que tenía a una ciudad dividida a lo que era la antigüeta pa comunista a lo que era la falta de libertad en la parte comunista"

**Heroes (1977) - Extensive Credits** : In stark contrast to the controversy surrounding the uncredited contributions on "Low," "Heroes" featured a much more detailed and extensive list of credits for everyone involved in the project. Brian Eno, for instance, received credit for almost the entire album.
"este disco sí ya aparece mucho más acreditado que lo este disco en contra de polémica de la falta acreditación de lo en el giro se aprece acreditada muchísimo gente que trabajó en el proyecto"

**Heroes (1977) - Instrumental Second Side** : Similar to "Low," "Heroes" also features a second part that is largely instrumental. This side heavily recalls Brian Eno's concurrent solo projects and his collaborations with artists like Cluster from the late 1970s, although Bowie also significantly contributed to these instrumental sections.
"tenía una segunda parte prácticamente instrumental que bien podría haber sido casi un disco en solitario de Brian y no"

**Heroes (1977) - Seamless Release and RCA Promotion** : The release of "Heroes" was significantly smoother than "Low," largely due to the unexpected success of its predecessor, which made RCA more receptive to Bowie's experimental projects in Germany. The album was seen as having more commercial potential, and RCA heavily invested in its promotion, using the famous advertising slogan: "There's Old Wave, there's New Wave, and there's David Bowie," positioning him as a pioneer of fresh, new music.
"la RCA puesto mucho por el y hizo muchísima publicidad muchísima prensa uso una de las frases yo creo que más famosas de la cara de iBee Bowie cuando lanzaron el giro que la frase de publicidad que usaba la RCA es de old wave y de new wave"

**Heroes (1977) - Post-Release Tour** : Following the release of "Heroes," David Bowie embarked on a tour that generated considerable excitement and anticipation among his audience.
"con este disco si quiso una gira y una gira más que se ha dado mucha expectación"

## curiosities
**Personal Transformation (1976)** : David Bowie underwent a detoxification process in 1976, transforming from an eccentric, paranoid, and vain individual into a much more humble and discreet person, experiencing a profound catharsis and internal purification.
"se pasa de suena persona esténtrica, paranoica y completamente vanidosa, convertirse a una persona completamente humilde, mucho más humilde, mucho más discreta"

**Geographic Mobility (1976)** : During the period of the Berlin Trilogy, Bowie did not live exclusively in Berlin. He traveled continuously between New York, Los Angeles, Geneva, and Paris. This constant movement contributed to the fragmented recording process of "Low."
"David Bowie no vive a Zina de Berlín. Está continuamente viajando a Nueva York, continuamente viajando a Los Ángeles a Ginebra y a París."

**Legal and Personal Challenges (1976)** : Bowie's frequent travels to places like Paris were partly due to legal issues related to changes in his management and the handling of his finances. He was also undergoing separation proceedings from his wife during this time.
"estaba metido en varios problemas legales con sus cambios de Manager y con cambios que estaba teniendo dentro de la gente gestionaba su dinero, también estaba teniendo ya varias procesos de separación de su mujer"

**Discovery of Devo (1976)** : In the summer of 1976, while in New York, David Bowie discovered the band Devo, a pivotal moment that would lead to his later involvement in their career.
"en ese verano de 1976 es cuando David Bowie en Nueva York vea los divos y descubre a los divos."

**Addiction Issues (Late 1970s)** : During the recording period of "Low" in the late 1970s, David Bowie was battling addiction problems. These issues contributed to the disorganized and dispersed nature of the recording process, making it difficult to properly track and credit all contributors.
"David Bowie había estado en esa etapa de tantos problemas de adicción que posiblemente ni siquiera David Bowie supiese bien quien, ya participado dentro de las grabaciones que había usado dentro del low."

**Artistic Integrity vs. Commercial Pressure (1976)** : Faced with RCA's reluctance to release "Low" as he intended, Bowie refused to compromise his artistic vision by adding more commercial hits. The opposition from his label and managers ironically solidified his belief that he was heading in the right artistic direction, seeking to create music he loved rather than prioritizing assured commercial success.
"el hecho de que nadie de la discográfica de esos manayers estuviesen de acordar lanzar el low justo reforzo su idea de que estaba yendo por el buen camino. David Bowie ya no quería ser esa estrella grandiosa que llenaba las portadas y que hacía polémicas todo el día, sino que quería ser una persona mucho más humilde que quería trabajar cosas que le gustas en mucho más allá del éxito asegurado."

**Mobility During Heroes Recording (1977)** : Even during the recording of "Heroes" in Berlin, David Bowie maintained his highly mobile lifestyle, frequently entering and leaving the city. This transient presence contributed to the rapid completion of the album.
"David Bowie vive muy poco en Berlín está saliendo y entrando de la ciudad continuamente entonces es una razón es por las que se graba tan rápido"

**Return of Vain Persona (1977)** : Following the critical and commercial success of "Heroes," Bowie's self-confidence surged. He began to revert to a more grandiose and sometimes vain persona, contrasting sharply with the humbler demeanor he exhibited in 1976 after his detoxification. This shift led to growing discrepancies in his collaborations.
"ya volvía ese ese de iBee Bowie grandioso un tanto vanidos a veces entonces ya empezaba a haber discrepancias importantes"

## curiosities
**Collaboration with Bowie on Low (1976)** : Brian Eno was instrumental in reworking David Bowie's existing ideas and rejected material from previous albums for "Low." He helped incorporate strong German influences from Krautrock bands like Kraftwerk, Neu!, and Cluster into the album's musical fabric. He was credited for his contributions to some songs and for production, though not as a composer for much of his work.
"David Bowie, Brian e Noco gerían estas ideas exechadas de los discos anteriores de David Bowie y las re trabajarían por completo usando todas las influencias que habían a Alemania, Zebrautro, Crafwerk, Neu, Cluster y todo."

**Productive Partnership with Bowie (1977)** : The unexpected success of "Low" solidified a fantastic and highly productive partnership between David Bowie and Brian Eno throughout 1977, leading to a period of intense creativity and projection for the Berlin Trilogy. However, this close relationship began to wane after "Heroes," with less harmony in subsequent projects.
"con un tandem fantástico que estaba haciendo David Bowie y Brian en los durante el resto en 1977 sería la etapa más apasionante, más intensa y de más proyección dentro de la trilogía de darling."

**Role on Heroes (1977)** : Brian Eno was heavily credited for his work on "Heroes," appearing on almost the entire album. His influence was particularly evident in the second, largely instrumental, side of the album, which strongly resembled his solo projects and collaborations with artists like Cluster from the late 1970s.
"Brian y no ya parece acreditado prácticamente en casi todo el disco y es que también tenía una segunda parte prácticamente instrumental que bien podría haber sido casi un disco en solitario de Brian y no"

**Invitation to Robert Fripp (1977)** : Brian Eno personally invited Robert Fripp to Berlin to record guitar parts for David Bowie's "Heroes." Fripp was hesitant, noting he hadn't played guitar in years, but Eno's insistence persuaded him.
"el mismisimo roberfripe de King Crimson lo llamaría Brian y no para ir a Berlín a grabar con él"

# artist - Iggy Pop

# curiosity

**The Berlin Trilogy - Creative Explosion (1976-1977)** : The Berlin Trilogy, beginning with the recording of "Low" in 1976, was not merely a part of David Bowie's discography but a complex creative explosion that involved many individuals. Although "Low" was not primarily recorded in Berlin, the trilogy's later phases, particularly "Heroes," would deeply engage with the city's themes and locations.
"parte de una compleja explosión creativa que involucró a mucha gente."

**Berlin as a Creative Hub (Late 1970s)** : The late 1970s marked an incredibly exciting period in Berlin, where not only were the final demos for David Bowie's "Low" being worked on, but several other "masterpiece" albums were being recorded simultaneously in the same studio. This included Iggy Pop's "The Idiot" and Brian Eno's solo compositions for "Music For Airports" and "Music For Films."
"esta es una etapa apasionante en Berlin porque no solo se estaban trabajando en las maquetas de Low en las maquetas finales de Low, sino que se estaban grabando otros discos que serían obras maestras."

**End of Berlin Trilogy's Core Period (Late 1977)** : For many, the true "heart" of David Bowie's Berlin Trilogy concluded at the end of 1977 with the release of "Heroes." The subsequent album, which would complete the trilogy, was not recorded in Berlin, and the close collaborative relationship between Bowie and Brian Eno began to deteriorate.
"para mucha gente ya aquí termine el corazón de la trilogía de Berlin porque ya el siguiente disco ya no se grabaría en Berlin de iBee Bowie y Brian y no ya no tendrían esa relación tan cercana"
