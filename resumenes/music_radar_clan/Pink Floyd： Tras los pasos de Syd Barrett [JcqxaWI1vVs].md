## albums
**Solo Albums (Date Unspecified)** : Sid Baret released two solo albums, described as "a bit strange," particularly due to their production. The recording process involved Sid Baret playing only fragments, never a complete song, and he couldn't wait for other artists or create song introductions or endings. This necessitated a complex production process of overdubbing, cutting, and pasting different pieces, and sometimes having others create intros. The albums are seen more as a showcase of his improvisational talent than his compositional skills, as they were largely the result of improvisation. "Yo creo que esos dos discos de Sid Baret son un poco raros, sobre todo por la producción."

## curiosities
**Comprehensive Biography** : An extensive biography, considered one of the most interesting books on music, provides a very human vision of Sid Baret's time in Pin Floyd. It includes narratives from Roger Waters, David Gilmore, and crucially, his sister, who was his closest relative. This biography offers a less sensationalist perspective on his departure from Pin Floyd, presenting a nuanced view that helps understand the early years of the band. "nos da una visión como muy humana de esa parte, una visión como muy personal que además no solo está contada con Roger Waters."
**The Man, The Myth, The Genius** : Sid Baret is described as a "man myth" and the "great genius" behind the early success of Pin Floyd, yet he suffered from severe mental health issues. His exact condition remains unknown; possibilities include schizophrenia, a manic problem, Asperger's syndrome, drug abuse, or even abusive medication. His family never wished to publish his medical history, and he was never adequately medicated, leading to his continued struggles. "Sid Baret es un hombre mito, eso es así, era el gran genio que estuvo detrás del éxito de Pin Floyd pero el problema es que estaba loco."
**Catatonic States and Disappearances** : Even during Pin Floyd's rapid ascent and the recording of their first album in 1967, Sid Baret frequently entered catatonic states. He also had episodes where he literally disappeared for weeks, highlighting the early signs of his deteriorating condition. "un Sid Baret que a veces se entraba en verdadero estado catatónico y en conciertos de 1967 incluso en la agravación del primer álbum."
**Summer 1967 - Point of No Return** : A pivotal weekend in the summer of 1967, when Sid Baret went on a five-day excursion with friends involving drugs, is identified as his point of no return. After this event, his sister noted he no longer recognized some people, couldn't remember lyrics, and often fell into catatonic states, remaining motionless for 8-10 hours without blinking. This is believed to be due to severe LSD abuse, marking a permanent change in him. "En ese momento Sid Baret ya cambia para siempre."
**Ejection from Pin Floyd** : Sid Baret was eventually forced out of Pin Floyd, not due to Roger Waters' ego or jealousy among band members, but because his deteriorating mental state made his involvement unsustainable. By late 1967, his condition was lamentable, evident in concert recordings and catatonic appearances in US television interviews. His erratic and aggressive behavior, including not attending recordings, made it impossible for him to continue. "Hubo que echarlo porque no iba las grabaciones. El tenia un comportamiento estétrico."
**Roger Waters' Guilt** : Roger Waters expressed significant guilt and culpability regarding Sid Baret's departure from Pin Floyd, particularly the fact that Sid Baret was already effectively out of the band before he himself was aware of it. This highlights the emotional toll of the situation on his former bandmates. "Roger Waters habla con mucha culpabilidad, con muchos sentimientos de culpabilidad porque hablaban de que llegó un punto en que Sid Baret ya estaba fuera de la banda y él no lo sabía."
**Compulsive Pursuit of Pin Floyd** : After his expulsion, Sid Baret exhibited compulsive behavior, pursuing Pin Floyd by appearing at their concerts and recording sessions. He would show up at EMI studios, attempting to enter through fire exits, even after knowing he was no longer a member. In one instance, he chased the band to Ibiza, appearing at the airport in a frantic state. These episodes were tragic, as he remained friends with Roger Waters and David Gilmore. "Sid Baret acabó haciendo un comportamiento durante años compulsivo persiguiendo a Pin Floyd en conciertos y van la estudio de grabación."
**Sordid Behavior in the 1970s** : Sid Baret's sister recounted "sordid" stories about his behavior in the 1970s, indicating a continued decline in his mental health and personal conduct during this period. "Su hermana ya en la época de 1970 nos cuenta también historias como muy sordidas del comportamiento de Sid Baret."
**Reluctance of M.Y. for Solo Work** : M.Y. Records was highly reluctant to support Sid Baret's solo career, despite recognizing his talent. They viewed him as "crazy" and, more importantly, unpredictable, making him a difficult artist to work with for recording and production. "Emmy no le gustaba nada en la idea de que Sid Baret volviese a trabajar porque a ver el vice-bale era un talento pero estaba como una cabra y sobre todo era impredecible."
**Challenges in Solo Recording** : Recording Sid Baret's solo material was extremely difficult due to his mental state. Collaborating musicians from Sob Machine and other bands were reportedly obligated rather than voluntary participants. Roger Waters and David Gilmore, who produced his solo work, found it "very complicated." The sound engineer deemed it "absolutely unbearable, impossible," as Sid Baret would play erratically, sometimes brilliantly, sometimes just banging or breaking his guitar, and could not remember what he had played. "decía que llora absolutamente insoportable imposible."
**Disappearance and Psychiatric Internment** : During his solo recording period, Sid Baret once disappeared for several months, driving his Mini around the United Kingdom in a sudden fit. He was later found interned in a psychiatric hospital, having been admitted after causing a scandal in a lamentable state, without anyone knowing he was Sid Baret. The band had to rescue him from the institution. "varios meses más tarde se lo encontraron en un entrenado, un psiquiátrico que alguien no había cogido, un estado lamentable montándome escándalo y la habían acabado internando sin saber que era Sid Baret."
**Erosion of Relationships with Pin Floyd Members** : By the summer of 1971, the intense difficulties encountered during Sid Baret's solo recordings severely eroded his personal relationships with Roger Waters and David Gilmore. They became very distant and exhausted, and their relationship never fully recovered, marking a definitive end to their close bond. "la relación entre Sid Baret y Roger Waters y David Gilmore quedó muy erosionada de esta grabación, quedaron como muy tocados y un tanto distanciado durante un tiempo."
**Formation and Demise of Stars** : In 1972, a final attempt was made to involve Sid Baret in a new group called Stars, but it lasted only a few months. Live recordings from Stars concerts are described as even "more impactful and heavy" than Pin Floyd's 1967 recordings, showcasing his severely deteriorated state. His sister ultimately advised him to abandon his musical career altogether. "De hecho, tenemos algunas grabaciones de conciertos stars que son todavía más impactantes y más heavis que esas grabaciones en 1967 que teníamos de Pin Floyd."
**Dramatic Appearance During "Wish You Were Here" Recordings (1975)** : In 1975, while Pin Floyd was in the studio completing "Wish You Were Here," Sid Baret unexpectedly appeared, completely shaven, pale, without eyelashes or eyebrows, and in a "horrible state." This moment, captured in two or three historical photos, deeply impacted Roger Waters, who broke down crying upon seeing his childhood friend. This event profoundly changed the lives of the band members and the band's history. "Aparece completamente rapado, muy pálido, sin pestañas, sin cejas, en un estado horrible y según nos cuenta un ayudante ingeniero de sonido que estaba allí, Roger Waters rompía llorar totalmente desgarrado por el estado en el que estaba Sid Baret."
**Financial Struggles and Pin Floyd's Support (1978)** : By 1978, Sid Baret, despite having earned substantial money from Pin Floyd and ongoing royalties from their first two albums, ran out of money while living in London. He moved back to Cambridge to live with his family. Pin Floyd made several gestures, including re-editions and securing rights, to ensure he continued to receive royalties as a full member (even for works where he contributed minimally, like the second album). This financial support allowed him and his family to live comfortably without money worries for the rest of his life. "La banda tenía un último gesto hacia el artista para ayudar a generar un poco más de dinero y que pudiese acabar en paz y sin problemas económicos al resto de sus días."

His physical and mental state profoundly shocked the band members, particularly Roger Waters, and marked a historical turning point for the band. "estaban arreglando y estaban en fase de producción y pues producían de l'algun de Wissley Wart Gear, aparece Sid Baret en el estudio de grabación sin decir nada."

## curiosities
**Early Success Driven by Sid Baret** : Pin Floyd achieved rapid success in its early years, primarily due to the immense talent of Sid Baret. However, even during this initial period, Sid Baret was already experiencing severe mental health issues. "todo el éxito de Pin Floyd al principio estaba detrás del talento de Sid Baret pero ya de aquella Sid Baret estaba muy mal."
**Lamentable State of Sid Baret in Late 1967 Concerts** : By late 1967, during the height of Pin Floyd's activity, Sid Baret's condition in live performances was described as "very lamentable." Recordings from these concerts are said to be shocking, revealing his poor state, which was also evident in catatonic appearances during television interviews in the United States. "Tenemos grabaciones de conciertos que te dejan con la boca abierta del mal estado en el que estaba."
**David Gilmore's Recruitment** : David Gilmore was chosen to replace Sid Baret, not by chance, but because he was a childhood friend of both Roger Waters and Sid Baret. Although other names like "Jetpack" (likely a mispronunciation of Jethro Tull's Ian Anderson) were initially considered, Gilmore's existing relationship with the band members provided a stable environment. The initial idea was for Sid Baret to remain a stable member for composition and recording only, similar to "the Beatles," but his condition made it impossible. "Realmente se optó por David Gilmore porque era un amigo de la infancia de Roger Waters y Sid Baret."
**Sid Baret's Post-Ejection Pursuit** : After his expulsion from the band, Sid Baret compulsively pursued Pin Floyd, attending their concerts and even showing up at EMI recording studios, attempting to enter through fire exits. There's an anecdote of him chasing the band to Ibiza, appearing at the airport "almost in tatters running," which highlighted the tragic nature of his continued connection to his former friends. "Tenemos un episodio en el que Sid Baret fue a perseguir a la banda. Pin Floyd hasta Ibiza y apareció en el aeropuerto casi en Carlson Cillos corriendo, intentando coger a la banda."
**Emotional Impact of Sid Baret's 1975 Appearance** : Sid Baret's shocking appearance in the studio during the recording of "Wish You Were Here" in 1975, where he was completely shaven, pale, and lacked eyebrows and eyelashes, had a profound and lasting impact on the band. Roger Waters was reportedly brought to tears, and the event changed the band's life and history forever. "Roger Waters rompía llorar totalmente desgarrado por el estado en el que estaba Sid Baret. Se quedaron tan impaltados que su vida y la historia de la banda cambiaría para siempre."
**Continued Contact and Financial Support for Sid Baret** : Following Sid Baret's 1975 studio appearance, Roger Waters re-established contact with his family, maintaining it until Sid Baret's death. In 1978, when Sid Baret ran out of money, Pin Floyd made gestures, including re-editions and securing rights, to ensure he received royalties as a full member. This allowed Sid Baret and his entire family to live comfortably without financial worries for the rest of his life. "Estos gestos económicos de Pin Floyd a Sid Baret permitieran que Sid Baret pasaría al resto de sus vidas sin tener que preocuparse del dinero y además que toda su familia entera pudiese para que haya de vivir sin preocuparse del dinero."
