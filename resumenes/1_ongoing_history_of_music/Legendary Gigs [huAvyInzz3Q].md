# artist - David Bowie

## genres
British rock

## concerts
Hammersmith Odeon (July 3, 1973)

## albums
**Ziggy Stardust and the Spiders from Mars (1972)** : This album was a huge seller in England through late 1972 and into 1973, complete with hit singles. It was central to Bowie's immense popularity and the widespread fascination with his Ziggy Stardust alter-ego during an extraordinary 18 months in British rock history. "His album Ziggy Stardust and the Spiders for Mars was a huge seller, complete with all the required hits singles."

## curiosities
**Ziggy Stardust Alter-Ego** : Ziggy Stardust was one of David Bowie's first and most enduring alter-egos and stage characters, described as a "bisexual alien dressed up all in glitter." This persona was immensely popular in England in late 1972 and early 1973, with the whole country talking about him. "Ziggy Stardust, one of Bowie's first and most enduring of all his alter-egos and stage characters, had been the ticket."
**"Bowie Quits" Concert (July 3, 1973)** : At the Hammersmith Odeon in London, Bowie staged a theatrical "retirement" for his Ziggy Stardust character, announcing that it was "the last show that we'll ever do." This event was captured by documentary filmmaker D.A. Pennebaker. Despite the dramatic announcement, Bowie did not actually quit, returning to the stage for a TV special less than two months later and continuing his recording and touring career. "Of all the shows on this tour, this particular show will remain with us the longest because not only is it the last show of the tour, but it's the last show that we'll ever do."

## concerts

## albums
**Ramones (1976) – Debut Album** : Released a few months before their influential London gigs in July 1976, this album introduced their unique "US style punk rock" to a select few in the UK who were already "in the loop." "Their debut album had come out a few months earlier and a select few in the UK were in the loop about this new thing that had been dubbed punk rock."

## curiosities
**Conquest of England (July 4 & 6, 1976)** : The Ramones played two pivotal shows in London, first at the Roundhouse (capacity 2,000) on America's bicentennial, and then at Dingwalls two nights later. Despite their European record company trying to cancel gigs, the band delivered explosive performances. These concerts were England's "first proper exposure" to US-style punk rock and are credited with transforming nearly every major player in the burgeoning British punk rock scene, including members of the Sex Pistols, The Clash, The Pretenders, The Damned, and Generation X. "And over the course of those two nights, July 4th and July 6th, 1976, just about every single person who would become a major player in the British punk rock explosion were taught a lesson by the Ramones."
**Backstage Window Encounter (July 4, 1976)** : Joe Strummer of The Clash recounted how he, Johnny Rotten, and members of the Sex Pistols met The Ramones backstage at the Roundhouse by throwing a rock at a window. Johnny Ramone stuck his head out, and the British punks introduced themselves, being subsequently pulled up through the window in a "human chain" to get in. "And so they kind of formed a sort of human chain and pulled us up through this window. And that was the first time we met him and that was just a really great punk rock moment."

## curiosities
**Ian Curtis's Struggles (Spring 1980)** : By spring 1980, Ian Curtis was battling drugs and alcohol, having an affair, and contemplating divorcing his wife. He suffered from severe epilepsy, with seizures becoming more frequent and medication having little effect. Despite the band's rising success and a booked American tour, Curtis was terribly depressed and considering quitting the band to open a bookshop on the continent. "Singer Ian Curtis was battling drugs in alcohol. He was having an affair with a Belgian woman while his wife went to work and cared for their baby daughter. And Ian had a severe case of epilepsy which seemed to be getting worse and worse."
**Final Concert and Seizure (May 2, 1980)** : Joy Division's last concert was held at Birmingham University on May 2, 1980, and was recorded. During the performance of "Decades," Ian Curtis fainted on stage due to an epileptic seizure. The audience initially mistook his collapse for an intense dance move and cheered. Curtis was helped off stage, recovered, and returned for an encore, marking his final stage appearance. He died by suicide less than two weeks later. "The final joy division gig was held on May 2nd, 1980 at Birmingham University. The show was recorded."
**Ian Curtis's Dance Style** : Ian Curtis was known for his "unique, herky, jerky way of dancing on stage." This distinctive style led the audience at Joy Division's final concert to initially mistake his epileptic seizure for an energetic performance collapse. "The audience thought it was all part of the act because Ian had this unique, herky, jerky way of dancing on stage and many people in the audience just looked like he had collapsed from dancing so hard."

# artist - The Jesus and Mary Chain

## members
William Reed
Jim Reed

## concerts
North London Polytechnic College (March 15, 1985)

## songs
**Never Understand (1985)** : This single, released in March 1985, generated excitement among "the cool kids" and contributed to the anticipation surrounding The Jesus and Mary Chain's concert at North London Polytechnic College. "In March 1985, the Jesus and Marychain had a single called Never Understand, which had the cool kids pretty excited."

## curiosities
**Unconventional Live Performances** : The Jesus and Mary Chain were known for their departure from traditional concert staging. Their sets typically lasted only 10 to 20 minutes, primarily featuring "squalling feedback" rather than clearly defined songs, and they often played with their backs to the audience. Their performances were considered "not so much a gig as it was a provocation." "They sort of played songs, but spent most of the time immersed in squalling feedback."
**North London Polytechnic Riot (March 15, 1985)** : This infamous gig became known as "the great Jesus and Marychain riot." The band, having recently moved from Scotland to London, had a hit single "Never Understand." Promoters oversold tickets, leading to an impatient, cramped, and surly crowd. After an opening band member threw a bottle at the audience, the crowd erupted. The Jesus and Mary Chain then kept the audience waiting an hour before playing a 20-minute, feedback-laden set and leaving, prompting the crowd to throw beer cans, invade the stage, and smash all the band's gear. The event received significant media attention due to the presence of the British music press. "The Jesus and Marychain provocateurs of the Great Riots at North London Polytechnic on March 15th, 1985."
