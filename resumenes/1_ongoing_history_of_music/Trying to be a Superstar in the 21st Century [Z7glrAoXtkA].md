**Persistent Decline in Album Sales** : Album sales have been consistently dropping at a double-digit rate for years, indicating a long-term downward trend. "Things have been dropping at a double digit rate for years."
**Failure of Digital Album Sales** : Digital albums, once expected to compensate for the decline in physical product sales, have become the worst-performing section of the market, dropping at a rate of 25% per year for the past few years. "Digital albums have been dropping at a rate of 25% per year for the last couple of years."
**Consensus as a Popularity Metric** : Evaluating an act's popularity based on its place in the public's consciousness and the concept of "consensus" may be a more appropriate metric than sales in the current music landscape. "Well, maybe a better way to evaluate an act's popularity is its place in the public's consciousness. And this is where we need to examine the concept of consensus."
**Historical "Manufactured Consensus"** : In the 1970s, access to music was tightly controlled by traditional gatekeepers like record labels, radio, and printed media, who curated music for the public. This facilitated the "manufacturing of consensus" around certain acts by limiting choices and reinforcing messaging, making it easier for fans to pick a favorite band. "But that was in an era when access to music was tightly controlled by record labels and radio and printed media."
**Empowerment of Individual Music Fans** : With the advent of the internet, the traditional gatekeepers of music are gone, and power now lies with individual music fans. They are free to pursue any musical interests, acting as their own A&R persons, music directors, editors, and curators, and no longer need external sources to dictate what they listen to. "With the internet, the power lies with the individual music fan."
**Unfettered Consumer Choice and Spread Attention** : The current era of unlimited and unfettered consumer choice means that the public's attention is spread much thinner, leading to less agreement on which acts should receive time, attention, and money, making it harder for any single act to achieve widespread consensus like in the past. "Instead, the public's attention has been spread thinner and thinner and thinner."
**Continued Presence of Heritage Acts** : A significant factor explaining why 21st-century acts struggle to reach the same heights as older ones is the continued activity of first and second-generation rock bands (e.g., The Stones, Pink Floyd members, U2, Eagles, The Who, Dylan, Paul McCartney) who still make records and tour, competing for audience attention and money. "Another issue that could explain why 21st century acts haven't been able to scale the same height as older ones is because those old acts are still with us."
**Erosion of Royalty Income for Older Acts** : The past reliable system where acts could generate substantial, consistent royalty checks from decades-old album sales (e.g., The Doors going platinum annually, Beastie Boys selling 2 million *Licensed to Ill* annually, Eagles/Fleetwood Mac retiring on royalties) has ceased. These "retirement annuities" have shrunk, forcing even established acts to tour to maintain their lifestyle and cash flow. "That's not happening anymore. These royalty checks, which were like retirement annuities, have shrunk and shrunk and shrunk."
**Necessity of Touring for Modern Artists** : With royalty checks from record sales drastically diminished, touring has become the primary means for artists to maintain cash flow and lifestyle, leading to a crowded touring market. "The only way to maintain cashflow in lifestyle is to get back out on the road and tour with everyone else."
**Cross-Generational Fanbase for Heritage Acts** : Audiences for heritage acts now include both original fans seeking to relive their youth and younger people who discovered the music through streaming, parents, or older siblings' collections. "But if you look around at any one of these shows, there's a lot of younger people too."
**Increased Musical Commonality Across Generations** : Unlike in the past when kids avoided their parents' music, contemporary generations of parents and children share much more common musical taste, particularly within the rock genre. "In the old days, kids wouldn't have anything to do with their parents music. Today though, parents and their kids have much more in common musically than ever before, especially on the rock side of things."
**Decline in Musical Tribalism** : Younger generations of music fans, those who came of age in the new millennium, are far more "ecumenical" in their tastes and less tribal than previous generations. Technology allows them to freely sample music across all genres and eras, frequently switching between rock, hip hop, pop, classic rock, dance, and EDM. "People who came of age musically in the new millennium aren't nearly as tribal as previous generations."
**The Death of the Album Concept** : The traditional rock album, conceived as a coherent collection of carefully recorded and sequenced songs to be consumed as a whole (a concept that solidified in the mid-1960s with *Sgt. Pepper*), is undergoing a "long, slow death." "The concept of the rock album, this collection of songs carefully recorded and sequenced and meant to be consumed as a whole, really began to take form in the middles 60s."
**Shift from Singles to Albums in the 1960s** : Following the 1967 release of The Beatles' *Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band*, the music industry's primary focus shifted from low-margin singles to high-margin albums, making the album the central product and generating significant revenue. "And from then on, the music industry's main focus was no longer on low margin singles, but on high margin albums."
**Record Labels Phasing Out Singles** : Record labels, enjoying fantastic album profits, gradually phased out singles, coercing fans to buy full albums even if they only desired one song, a move described as "arrogant and cynical." "In fact, they were enjoying such fantastic profits that they gradually phased out singles."
**Napster and the Rise of A La Carte Music** : The advent of the internet and illegal file-sharing programs like Napster transformed music into an "a la carte buffet," where fans could download individual MP3s instead of buying expensive CDs for a single track, marking the beginning of the end for the album era. "With Napster and all the other original illegal file sharing programs, music became one big allocard buffet."
**iTunes Music Store and Legal Single Purchases** : The introduction of the iTunes Music Store on January 9th, 2001, dealt another "big stake through the heart" of the album era, as it allowed consumers to legitimately and legally purchase individual tracks instead of full albums, leading to a decline in album sales. "Another big stake through the heart was the introduction of the iTunes music store on January 9th, 2001."
**Streaming's Impact on Albums and Playlists** : Streaming services further diminished the album concept, with people primarily streaming playlists rather than full albums, and album tracks receiving almost no traction in the streaming world. "Then came streaming. Instead of albums, people are streaming playlists. Album tracks get almost zero traction in the streaming world."
**Streaming as Primary Revenue for Labels** : Streaming music services have become the largest source of revenue for record labels, accounting for over half of their earnings. Physical products like CDs contribute progressively less each quarter. "Streaming is now the greatest source of revenue for record labels. Over half the money they make comes from streaming, with physical product like CDs offering up less and less and less every quarter."
**Record Label Streaming Revenue (Mid-2017)** : In mid-2017, record labels were collectively earning $150,000 USD every minute from streaming. "In mid-2017, we learned that the labels were making a hundred and fifty thousand dollars US per minute from streaming."
**Unprofitable Streaming Services** : Despite being the primary revenue source for labels, streaming services like Spotify, Apple Music, and Google Play are unprofitable because licensing deals compel them to pay out over half of their income before any other expenses. However, streaming is now "too big to fail" for the music industry. "And even though Spotify, Apple Music, Google Play and all the rest of them are unprofitable, thanks to the licensing deals that they've been forced to take, which sees them pay out more than half of all they take in."
**Diminished Artist Payouts from Streaming** : Artists receive only a fraction of a cent per stream, comparable to radio play, which is significantly less than the income from selling a digital file or CD. This, combined with declining CD sales, has led to a shrinking flow of money to artists and composers. "A single stream pays a fraction of a cent, much in the same way that a single play on the radio pays."
**Decimation of the Musician Middle Class** : The dramatic reduction in artist income from streaming and declining sales has "decimated the middle class of musicians," which historically served as the breeding ground for superstars, creating a gap between huge top acts and a large underclass of struggling performers. "This has decimated the middle class of musicians, the traditional breeding ground for superstars."
**Challenges for Struggling Musicians** : Struggling performers, who form a vast underclass, are forced to spend much of their time on non-creative activities to make ends meet, making it difficult for them to develop, experiment, and mature creatively into something great. "They spend so much of their time doing things other than creating music that it's a challenge to be creative and to experiment and to develop and mature into something great."
**Live Gigs as Primary Revenue for Artists** : For most artists, especially those struggling, recorded songs serve as "loss leaders" or "calling cards" to secure live gigs, which have become one of the few steady revenue streams, leading to a constant cycle of recording and touring that can lead to creative and physical exhaustion. "And playing live is one of the few steady revenue streams. Artists end up on a treadmill of recording and touring and recording and touring and before long they're creatively and physically and mentally spent."
**Marketing Prioritization Based on Streaming Revenue** : The recorded music industry directs its marketing dollars towards genres that generate the most revenue. Since streaming provides over half of total revenues and is dominated by pop, R&B, and hip-hop, labels primarily focus their marketing money and attention on these genres, leaving less for rock. "So streaming, generating more than half your revenues. And if pop, R&B, and hip-hop songs are behind those streams, where do you think the labels are going to focus their money and attention?"
**Overwhelming Preference for Singles in Streaming** : Listeners overwhelmingly prefer singles on streaming services, with album tracks receiving almost zero attention. This questions the purpose of recording non-single tracks for albums in the current environment. "If you look at what people are listening to, it's overwhelmingly to singles. Film tracks on streaming services get almost zero attention."
**Streaming's Impact on Attention Spans and Song Structure** : Streaming, with its vast supply of music, contributes to shrinking attention spans. Listeners are quick to skip songs they dislike (25% in 5 seconds, 35% before 30 seconds), fundamentally changing the nature of music as artists focus on retaining attention within the crucial 30-second Spotify payout window. "It is changing the very nature of music. And it has to do with our ever shrinking attention spans."
**Spotify's 30-Second Payout Threshold** : Spotify only pays artists for a stream if 30 seconds have passed. This critical threshold influences artists, composers, and producers to prioritize capturing and retaining listener attention within the first 30 seconds of a song at all costs. "Because Spotify does not pay out for a stream until 30 seconds have passed."
**Changes in Song Intros** : The average instrumental lead-up to vocals in a pop song has drastically shortened from about 17 seconds in the 1980s to less than five seconds today, a direct consequence of the need to immediately engage listeners in the streaming era. "Now in the 80s, the average instrumental lead up to the vocals of a pop song was around 17 seconds. Now it's less than five."
**Front-Heavy Songwriting** : Songwriting has adapted to short attention spans by becoming "front heavy," with more songs starting directly with the chorus, jamming more hooks into the first half-minute, and incorporating familiar samples to immediately grab listeners. "More songs are starting with the chorus. More hooks are jammed into that first half minute. Songs are front heavy with familiar samples."
**Historical Shifts in Cultural Dominance of Music Genres** : The 20th century saw jazz dominate its first half, followed by rock and roll taking over in the 1950s and leading popular music for 50-60 years. Now, rock has been eclipsed by hip hop, which currently serves as the driving force in popular culture, although this does not imply the death of jazz or rock. "The first half of the 20th century was dominated by jazz in all its forms. That was the music that pushed Western culture forward. But then in the 1950s, it was pushed aside by this new thing called rock and roll."
