# genre - Skate Punk

## curiosities
**Definition and Origins** : Skate punk is a subgenre of Alt Rock built on skateboarding culture, with its roots tracing back to the shared music of surfers and skaters, and later evolving with punk rock in the late 1970s. Its "ground zero" is likely Oxnard, California. "My main interest with skateboarding is the music that evolved along with it."
**Nardcore Scene** : The Nardcore scene, named after Oxnard, California, emerged when local skaters formed bands like Aggression and Ill Repute, specializing in music for the skate crowd. "And the scene even had a name, Nardcore, after Oxnard."
**Original Name: Skate Thrash** : The genre's original name was "Skate Thrash," which is related to the name of Thrasher Magazine. "The magazine's name is also related to the genre's original name, Skate Thrash."
**Coined by Thrasher Magazine** : The term "Skate Punk" itself appears to have been coined by an employee of Thrasher Magazine. "The term Skate Punk also seems to have been coined by an employee of Thrasher Magazine."
**Early Sound and Issues** : The early days of skate punk were characterized by sharp, fast, angry songs filled with adrenaline. While cathartic, this sound was also associated with problems like violence, including LA gang violence, which made playing gigs difficult for the skate crowd for a time. "As you heard, the early days of the genre were dominated by sharp, fast, angry songs, containing the maximum amount of adrenaline."
**Mutation with Heavy Metal (1985)** : By 1985, the original skate punk sound began incorporating heavy metal elements, particularly guitar solos. This evolution fractured the community; some bands transitioned to metal genres, while others remained dedicated to hardcore punk. "By 1985, the original Skate Punk sound had acquired a new thing, elements of heavy metal, including guitar solos."
**Shift to Melodic Sound** : Skate punk bands began to integrate more melodic elements, retaining their fast and intense qualities but adding "singable melodies." Bad Religion's 1988 album "Suffer" is seen as a key inflection point for this shift towards "pop sensibilities." "Some Skate Punk bands started to get more melodic."
**Mainstream Breakthrough (1990s)** : Skate punk experienced a significant mainstream breakthrough in the 1990s, especially after the rise of grunge and alt-rock. This was exemplified by bands like Pennywise with "Unknown Road" (1993) and No FX with "Punk in Drublic" (early 90s), culminating in The Offspring's "Smash" (1994) which brought the genre widespread recognition. "Skate Punk did have to wait for its turn for a mainstream breakthrough, but when it happened, it happened big."
**Glory Years (Late 1994-2004)** : The "glory years" of skate punk began in late 1994 and continued for approximately ten years, with The Offspring's success leading to wider discovery of bands like Goldfinger, Lagwagon, Face to Face, MXPX, and Unwritten Law. "Skate Punk's glory years began in late 1994 and continued for a good 10 years."
**New Millennium Growth** : By the new millennium, skate punk had become massive, with established bands like Blink-182, The Offspring, MXPX, Pennywise, and No FX thriving, and newer bands like Sum 41 emerging. "By the new millennium, skate punk was massive."
**Early 2010s Resurgence** : The early 2010s saw another surge in skateboarding's popularity, which in turn inspired a new generation of bands drawing from older skate punk groups. "In the early 2010s, there was another spike in popularity of skateboarding. And that resulted in a new crop of bands all inspired by older skate punk groups."

# instrument - Skateboard

## curiosities
**Early Design (Late 1940s/Early 1950s)** : The very earliest skateboards were rudimentary, consisting of nothing more than a plank of wood with roller skate wheels bolted to the bottom. "The very earliest skateboards were nothing more than a plank of wood with some roller skate wheels bolted to the bottom."
**Bill Richard's Innovation** : A surf shop owner named Bill Richard in Los Angeles made a deal with a Chicago roller skate company to produce special wheels for skateboards, which were initially attached to carved wood decks. "We do know that a surf shop in Los Angeles run by a guy named Bill Richard made a deal with the Chicago roller skate company to produce a special set of wheels that could be attached to the bottom of a deck."
**"Death Traps" (1963)** : By 1963, despite dedicated shops, early skateboards were considered "death traps" due to rudimentary construction, poor design of decks, wheels, and trucks, leading to thousands of hospitalizations each year. "And they were death traps. Construction was very rudimentary and we still had a long, long way to go with the design of the deck and especially the wheels and trucks."
**Resurrection and Material Improvements (1975)** : The resurrection of skateboarding around 1975 brought significant design improvements. Steel roller skate wheels were replaced with polyurethane wheels, offering much better traction and fewer wipeouts. Decks, previously only wood, became available in aluminum, fiberglass, and various composites. "No more steel roller skate wheels. The new wheels were made of polyurethane and offered much bit of traction leading to many fewer wipeouts."
**Dry Swimming Pools as Bowls (1976)** : A severe drought in California in 1976 led to many swimming pools remaining dry, which were subsequently used by skaters as "bowls," providing new surfaces that facilitated new tricks and advanced freestyle skateboarding. "California suffered from a terrible drought causing a lot of swimming pools to remain dry. And a lot of those pools became bowls for skaters."
**Global Reach and Barefoot Designs** : Skateboarding has seen a global explosion in popularity, particularly in Africa and South America, where many boards are specifically designed to be ridden barefoot. "Many of the boards used there are designed to be written barefoot."

# curiosity

**First Skaters** : While the exact identity of the first skateboarder is unknown, it is speculated to have been a California surfer seeking land-based fun when waves were absent or for similar thrills without getting wet. "We don't know who the first skateboarder was but it was probably some California surfer who wanted the same kind of fun without getting wet or wanted to have fun when the waves just weren't there."
**"Sidewalk Surfing"** : Early skateboarding was also commonly referred to as "sidewalk surfing," reflecting its origins among surfers who adapted their ocean tricks to dry land, particularly in Southern California. "Skateboarding or sidewalk surfing caught on with some people in Southern California, usually surfers who tried the same tricks on dry land as they did in the ocean."
**Mid-1960s Competitions and Sponsorships** : By the mid-1960s, skateboarding competitions emerged, primarily focused in Southern California. A select few skaters achieved sufficient skill to attract sponsors and tour the US, showcasing their tricks. "A few skaters were good enough to get sponsors and tour the US showing off their tricks."
**Fad Branding (1967)** : By 1967, skateboards lost public appeal and were branded as a fleeting fad, similar to a hula hoop, and consequently became largely irrelevant for about a decade. "But by 1967 skateboards lost any cache they had. They were branded as a fad, kind of like a hula hoop, that means anything to you."
**First Purpose-Built Skate Parks** : The mid-1970s saw the emergence of the first purpose-built skate parks, a development coinciding with the resurrection of skateboarding and improvements in equipment. "Competition's resumed and the first purpose-built skate parks started showing up."
**Oxnard Skate Park (1975)** : In 1975, the city of Oxnard, California, built a skate park that became a significant magnet for many kids, establishing the location as a "ground zero" for skate punk. "If there is a ground zero for skate punk, it's probably an Oxnard California. And that's because in 1975, the city built a skate park."
**Thrasher Magazine** : This magazine played a crucial role in connecting skaters with the associated bands, covering both skateboarding and the new breed of punk acts from regions like Oxnard, Orange County, and San Francisco. "They often connected through a magazine called Thrasher, which not only covered skating, but the band's associated with skateboarding."
**Snowboarding Videos as Music Platform** : Songs from skate punk bands, such as The Offspring, started to appear in snowboarding videos, which served as an early platform for broader exposure before their mainstream breakthrough. "Plus some of these songs started showing up in snowboarding videos."
**Kurt Cobain's Death and Alt-Rock Vacuum (April 8, 1994)** : Kurt Cobain was found dead on April 8, 1994, the exact same day The Offspring released their album "Smash." This event created a "creative and spiritual vacuum" in alt-rock as grunge's influence waned, which inadvertently paved the way for skate punk's rise. "On April 8th, 1994, the exact same day Kurt Cobain was found dead in a room above a greenhouse in his backyard. The offspring quietly released an album called Smash."
**Nirvana's Sound and Skate Punk** : Nirvana's musical style, characterized by being hard, fast, intense, yet always featuring singable melodies and big choruses, was considered "adjacent to the Skate Punk sound," making it a natural transition for fans of albums like "Nevermind" to appreciate bands like The Offspring. "When you get right down to it, Nirvana was adjacent to the Skate Punk sound."
**Skateboarding's Mainstream Takeover (New Millennium)** : By the new millennium, skateboarding culture achieved unprecedented mainstream interest, leading to millions of sales for Tony Hawk's video games (featuring skate punk music) and a statistic showing more Americans were into skateboarding than baseball in 2001. "Skateboarding culture had grabbed mainstream interest like never before."
**Academic Respectability** : Skateboarding gained respectability as a form of physical activity within education circles, leading to the creation of a skateboarding Hall of Fame and even an event in conjunction with the prestigious John F. Kennedy Center for the Performing Arts. "Skateboarding became a respectable form of physical activity in education circles. There's now a skateboarding home of fame."
**Olympic Sport (2020)** : As of 2020, skateboarding officially became an Olympic sport, further solidifying its global recognition and status. "And as of 2020, skateboarding isn't a Olympic sport."
**Global Skate Park Expansion** : More cities and towns worldwide have been building skateboard parks, exemplified by a massive 12,000 square foot park with a 5,000 seat stadium dedicated to skateboarding in China. "In China, there's a 12,000 square foot park complete with a 5,000 seat stadium just for skateboarding."
