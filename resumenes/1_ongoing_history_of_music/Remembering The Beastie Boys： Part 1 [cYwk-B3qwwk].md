## albums
**Check Your Head** : This was referred to as the band's third album and their "comeback album." Its full context was promised for later in the podcast, but its title and status were noted early. "Called Check Your Head."
**Polywag Stew (1982)** : This LP was recorded by the Beastie Boys in two days at Studio 171A, which was about to go out of business, with the landlord threatening to sell off gear for back rent. Engineer Scott Jarvis rescued a tape machine and mixed the tracks in his bedroom. It was released on Rat Cage on November 20th, 1982. "The Beastie Boys came out on Rat Cage, and the date was November 20th, 1982."
**Licensed to Ill (1986)** : Released on November 19th, 1986, this album was a groundbreaking fusion of rap, hip-hop, and rock, appealing to rap, alternative, and mainstream rock fans. It featured Kerry King of Slayer on guitar, who played during the recording of Slayer's "Reign in Blood." The album bridged the gap between black and white music fans and became one of the fastest-selling debut albums in history, being the first rap record to hit number one on the American album charts. It has sold over 10 million copies in the US and remained a top-selling back catalog item for years, moving at least 500,000 copies annually 10-15 years later. The band initially wanted to title it "Don't Be a Faggot." "Licensed to Ill was the first rap record to ever hit number one on the American album charts."
**Paul's Boutique (1989)** : Released on July 25th, 1989, this album was a conscious effort by the Beastie Boys to create a highly creative rather than commercial record, avoiding being pigeonholed after "Licensed to Ill." Produced with The Dust Brothers over nearly two years, it meticulously sampled no fewer than 105 different songs, which were expertly woven into the tracks. Despite its artistic and technical advancement, it was a commercial disaster at the time, leading Capitol Records to stop promoting it after two singles and even resulting in the firing of the label's president. It cost the band about $250,000 to clear all the samples, a sum considered reasonable compared to today's costs. The album is now recognized as a masterpiece and a landmark in hip-hop, sampling, producing, and recording engineering. "Today we recognize Paul's boutique as an absolute masterpiece."

## songs
**So What'cha Want** : This song is from the Beastie Boys' third album, "Check Your Head," which was considered their comeback album. "The Beastie Boys and so what you want from the third album, their comeback album."
**A Grade on Mojo** : This is one of the Beastie Boys' earliest recordings, dating from late 1982, featuring John Berry on guitar and Kate Shellenbach on drums, before Adam Horowitz joined. It is notable as one of the few songs from their hardcore era that remained part of the band's career. "The song is called A Grade on Mojo, and it's one of their earliest recordings."
**Cookie-puss (1983)** : Released in August 1983 on the Rat Cage label, this four-song EP's title track originated from a prank phone call made to a local Carvel Ice Cream store, incorporating samples from Steve Martin's comedy album "A Wild and Crazy Guy" over beats. It was a distinctive departure from the New York Hardcore scene and gained college radio airplay. The song also became the subject of a lawsuit against British Airways for unauthorized sampling, which the Beastie Boys won, earning them their first significant money from music. "The Beastie Boys with Cookie-puss, another release on The Ratchet Cage label, this time from August 1983."
**Beastie Revolution** : A track from the Beastie Boys' early work, released as a white label around 1984. This song was inadvertently sampled by British Film Director Jeremy Healy for a worldwide British Airways commercial without the Beastie Boys' knowledge or permission. When the band heard it in a New York cinema, they sued British Airways, eventually winning a $40,000 judgment which they used to secure an apartment and rehearsal space at 99 Christie Street. "I put this bit of this Beastie Boys record in it."
**Rock Hard (1985)** : This track, released in 1985, was the second single from Def Jam but was quickly withdrawn due to containing unauthorized samples from ACDC's "Back in Black." ACDC was famously against sampling and had not given permission. The single became a collector's item but was eventually reissued in Europe in 2007 with ACDC's blessing, as the Beastie Boys had become successful enough to negotiate directly with ACDC's members. "The Beastie boys with Rock Hard, with the original illegal ACDC samples intact."
**She's on it (1985)** : Released on September 12, 1985, this single performed much better than "Rock Hard" and was featured on the soundtrack of the film "Krush Groove." Around this time, band members adopted their well-known stage names: Adam Yauch as MCA, Adam Horowitz as Ad Rock, and Mike Diamond as Mike D. "She's on it, originally from the soundtrack of a film called Crush Groove."
**(You Gotta) Fight For Your Right (To Party!)** : This song was widely perceived as a "fratboy novelty song," particularly after its video release, which featured cameos from LL Cool J, Rick Rubin, and MTV's Tabitha Soren, and used rancid, dumpster-scrounged whipped cream due to a low budget. Written by Adam Yauch and his friend Tom, it was intended as a parody of rock anthems like Twisted Sister's "I Wanna Rock" and Quiet Riot's "Cum On Feel the Noize," but 95% of its audience missed the satirical intent. Musically, it distinguished itself by featuring big guitars and heavy drums alongside rapping instead of singing. "You gotta fight! For your right! To party!"
**Brass Monkey** : This song was the fourth single from "Licensed to Ill" and played a significant role in introducing millions of non-rap and non-house music fans to the distinctive sounds of the Roland TR-808 Drum Machine. "This was the fourth, and it introduced millions of non-rappers and those not into house music to the sounds of the legendary Roland TR-808 Drum Machine."
**No Sleep Till Brooklyn** : This song intentionally features a riff structure similar to ACDC's "TNT." Its title is also a deliberate homage to Motorhead's "No Sleep 'til Hammersmith," making the similarities purely intentional. "The Beastie Boys and No Sleep till Brooklyn."
**Hey Ladies (1989)** : The first single from "Paul's Boutique," released on July 25th, 1989. This track showcased the album's advanced sampling techniques, incorporating snippets from 16 different songs. The song's most obvious sample is a line from The Sweet's "Ballroom Blitz," and its title is derived from Curtis Blow's "Party Time." Other notable samples include material from James Brown, The Commodores, Kool & the Gang, and Edwin Starr, demonstrating a staggering complexity of mixing samples with original music. "Sixteen different songs are rated for this track."
**Paul's Boutique Second Single** : The second single released from "Paul's Boutique" contained eight distinct samples. These samples included material from James Brown, Sly & The Family Stone, and the Sugar Hill Gang. It also featured drum samples from a song by the Southern rock band Black Oak Arkansas. "There were eight samples contained therein, including material from James Brown, Slime the Family Stone, Sugar Hill Gang, and even some drums from a song by a southern rock band called Black O'Garkinson."

## curiosities
**Early Ambitions** : The band started as a "goof" by three guys from "well to do New York families," with their initial aspirations being to "drink beer and meat chicks." "All they wanted to do in the beginning was drink beer and meat chicks."
**Name Origin (Legend)** : According to legend, the name "Beastie Boys" stood for "Boys Entering Anarchistic States Towards Internal Excellence." "Now officially, according to legend, that stood for Boys Entering Anarchistic States Towards Internal Excellence."
**First Gig Anecdote** : The Beastie Boys' first official gig, held on Adam Yauch's 17th birthday, was described by one band member as the first time he ever got drunk. "It's the first time I ever got drunk."
**Early Breakups** : The band broke up multiple times in its early stages due to getting "bored" or after recording sessions. "Then they got bored and broke up."
**John Berry's Departure** : Guitarist John Berry quit the band near the end of 1982, reportedly due to a "meth problem." "John Barry quit near the end of 1982, apparently there was a bit of a meth problem."
**Evolution of Sound and Image** : Following Adam Horowitz's entry, the Beastie Boys began to fuse punk with rap and breakdancing, a popular genre in New York at the time. Rick Rubin, who joined as their DJ, further introduced hip-hop elements into their sound and influenced their image, encouraging them to dress like street rappers in matching Adidas track outfits, backward baseball caps, and chunky gold chains. "This is when the Beasties began to explore the possibilities of fusing punk with rap and break dancing, which was getting big in New York at the time."
**Kate Shellenbach's Departure** : Drummer Kate Shellenbach was "forced out" of the band by Rick Rubin. She eventually left because she found Rubin to be "childish and really, really sexist." "She considered him to be childish and really, really sexist and very soon she wanted out."
**"Three White MCs" Novelty** : At the time when hip hop was becoming more central to their sound, the concept of "three white MCs" was considered "pretty weird" in the industry. "I mean, three white MCs?"
**Fratboy Image and Public Reaction** : The Beastie Boys cultivated a "drunken party, Fratboy attitude" that caused "most people to absolutely hate them" prior to the release of "Licensed to Ill." "In fact, their drunken party, Fratboy attitude caused most people to absolutely hate them."
**"Licensed to Ill" Tour Difficulties** : The tour supporting "Licensed to Ill" was "very rough" on the band, leading to significant internal friction and a period where they "wanted to kill each other" by the time they returned home. "But the License to Eale tour was very rough on the Beastie Boys."
**Post-"Licensed to Ill" Hiatus** : After the grueling "Licensed to Ill" tour, the band decided to take a break to "cool down." Adam Horowitz used this time to act in films in Hollywood and relax with his then-girlfriend, Molly Ringwald, while the other members pursued various musical projects. "Adam went off to Hollywood to act in a couple of films and then relax with his then girlfriend Molly Ringwald."
**Def Jam Royalty Dispute** : Following the "Licensed to Ill" tour, Def Jam records demanded a new album immediately, which the Beastie Boys, feeling "burned out," refused. In response, Def Jam began withholding royalties that rightfully belonged to the band, leading to a legal dispute that lasted for months before the Beastie Boys broke free. "So Def Jam started holding back royalties, money that should have rightfully gone to the band."
**British Controversy and Ban Attempts** : After the success of "Licensed to Ill," a group of British politicians sought to ban the Beastie Boys from the UK. This was due to their controversial stage antics, which included inviting female audience members to dance in cages and deploying a "giant motorized inflatable penis" during their shows. "A group of politicians in Britain wanted them banned from the UK."
**British Tabloid False Story** : British tabloids targeted the Beastie Boys, with one paper running a fabricated story claiming the band laughed at and taunted children with terminal leukemia. Despite the story being entirely false and the children themselves coming to the band's defense, the damage to their reputation was done. "One went so far as to carry a false story about the band laughing and taunting a group of dying children, kids with terminal leukemia."
**Liverpool Riot (1987)** : During a concert in Liverpool on May 10, 1987, a group of thugs threw cans and bottles at the band. The Beastie Boys retaliated by throwing the missiles back with baseball bats, resulting in a girl being hit in the eye and Ad Rock's arrest for assault. The show lasted only 10 minutes before escalating into a riot. "The whole show lasted 10 minutes before it devolved into a riot."
**End of Career** : The band's career eventually concluded with the death of Adam Yauch. "through to the end of their career with the death of Adam Yock."

# genre - Rap

## curiosities
**Early Perception as a Fad** : In the late 1970s and early 1980s, rap and hip hop struggled to gain acceptance outside of New York urban areas. A significant majority of the music market, including the industry and fans, viewed rap as a passing "fad" that would quickly die out, dismissing it with phrases like "rap, crap." They believed "real music" involved traditional singing and instruments, not scratching and sampling. "As hard as it is to believe today, a sizeable majority of the music market, the industry and fans, looked at Rap as a fad, something that would quickly die out."
**Mainstream Dismissal of Early Stars** : Despite the presence of genuine stars and musical geniuses like Run DMC, Curtis Blow, Afrika Bambaataa, Grandmaster Flash, and LL Cool J, the rap scene and its sounds were largely not taken seriously by the mainstream music industry and public in the early to mid-1980s. "Run DMC, Curtis Blow, Africa Bambada, Grandmaster Flash, LL Cool J, The Scene and the Sounds were just not taken seriously."

# curiosity

**Early 80s Music Culture Divide** : In the early 1980s, the music landscape was marked by a significant cultural divide, separating urban areas from suburbs, people of color from white audiences, and rockers from rappers. This polarization underscored a broader societal split that impacted how new music genres like rap and hip-hop were received. "There was a huge divide. Urban areas versus the suburbs. People of color versus white. Rockers versus rappers."
**Sampling Legal Costs Evolution** : The cost of clearing music samples dramatically increased after "Paul's Boutique" was released, due to a series of major lawsuits involving sampling. The $250,000 the Beastie Boys paid to clear 105 samples for "Paul's Boutique" was considered reasonable for its time, but today, a similar album would cost millions of dollars in sample clearances, making such complex, sample-heavy albums nearly impossible to produce. "Paul Sputie came out in the time before a bunch of big lawsuits involving sampling pushed the cost of clearing samples way, way up."
