## albums
**Is This It (2001) – Debut Album** : The Strokes' debut album was recorded after Gil Norton was replaced by Gordon Raphael, taking about six weeks in a "crappy studio" that nevertheless had its charms. Guitar teacher JP Bauerstock was brought in to help with guitar lessons, influencing the album's solos. Most tracks were recorded once to maintain rawness, a technique supported by their extensive rehearsal to a click track. This raw approach initially concerned RCA, their US label, who found it unprofessional. The band used only three microphones for a gritty sound, and Julian sang his vocals through a tiny practice amp for a low-fi effect. RCA only understood the vision after hearing the songs played on an old boombox. The album was released in Australia in July 2001, the UK in August, and North America in October. It sold over 4 million copies worldwide and significantly influenced many subsequent bands, including Kings of Leon and The Killers. "The whole thing was recorded using just three microphones to get that gritty raw sound."
**Room on Fire (2003)** : The second Strokes album began with producer Nigel Godrich but eventually reverted to Gordon Raphael. Released in October 2003, it featured the single "1251" with a distinctive guitar tone that became a signature sound for many Strokes songs, originating from a studio accident. While it didn't match "Is This It" commercially, it was a critical success and solidified the band's position in the Indy Rock Revival. "Room on Fire didn't do as well commercially as Is This It. But it was certainly a hit with the Quittix."
**First Impressions of Earth (2006)** : This album, released approximately two years after "Room on Fire," was the band's third studio effort. It again faced producer issues, with Gordon Raphael tapping out in favor of David Khan, who had previously worked with Paul McCartney. This album was less well-received by critics and even garnered a lukewarm response from fans. Sales were anemic despite three singles receiving radio play. "This was the difficult third album, not a lot of love from the Quittix."
**Angles (2011)** : The band's fourth album was a slow process, taking two years for writing, demoing, and recording, marked by more producer problems before the band decided to handle production themselves. Julian Casablancas was increasingly absent, sending vague instructions via email, aiming to give other members a chance at songwriting, as he had written about 95% of previous material. This shift altered band dynamics and sound. Albert Hammond Jr. also suffered a drug problem from a breakup, requiring rehab. Out of 18 initial demos, 17 were discarded or reworked. The album was released on February 9, 2011, and was warmly received, described as "a return to form" and "back to basics." "Overall, the fourth album was warmly received."
**Come Down Machine (2013)** : The fifth album was conceived as another collaborative effort, with all members contributing to songwriting. Upon its release in March 2013, the band imposed a full media blackout, with no interviews, gigs, tours, photo shoots, or videos. Reviews for the album were positive, which was fortunate given the lack of promotion. However, it was not a sales monster, reflecting the industry's transition from physical sales to streaming, where counting physical copies became less important. "Reviews were good, fortunate, given the media block out, but the album wasn't exactly a sales monster."
**Future Present Past (2016) – EP** : This four-track EP was released digitally and on two colors of vinyl, and unlike "Come Down Machine," it received promotion. The promotional efforts included an Instagram campaign, Julian Casablancas hosting a satellite radio show to discuss it, some live shows, and a pop-up shop in New York City. It marked the first Strokes release on Cult, Julian's own imprint label. "This one did get promoted."
**The New Abnormal (2020)** : The sixth Strokes record was released on April 10, 2020, amidst the COVID-19 pandemic, a timing that made its title particularly fitting. Produced by Rick Rubin in California and Hawaii, this change in producer and venue was exactly what the band needed. The album garnered great reviews and earned a Grammy Award for Best Rock Album. "This record was made with producer Rick Rubin in both California and Hawaii. And the change of producer and venue was exactly what the band needed."

## songs
**Bad Decisions (2020)** : This song was the first single from "The New Abnormal" album. Billy Idol and Tony James of Generation X received songwriting credits due to melodic similarities to their song "Dancing With Myself." "Billy Eidle and Tony James of Generation X got songwriting credits because of the melodic similarities to the song Dancing With Myself."
**Last Night (2001)** : An original version of this song was given away as a free MP3 download by NME, which helped prime its pump. It was later re-recorded for "Is This It," becoming the second single from the debut album. "The first place found that version of last night was an MP3 given away as a free download by the NME, one of the British weekly music papers."
**Someday (2001)** : This was the third single released from The Strokes' debut album, "Is This It." By the time of its release, The Strokes had become critical darlings globally, and discussions around a garage rock revival, a return of Indy Rock, and a fresh perspective on an old sound were widespread. The video for "Someday" famously featured Slash, Duff McKagan, and Matt Sorum from Guns N' Roses and Velvet Revolver. "The strokes with Someday, the third single from their first album."
**New York City Cops (2001)** : This track was originally featured on "Is This It." However, for the album's US release in October 2001, it was replaced with "When It Started." This decision was made because the release date was too close to 9/11, and a song critical of first responders was deemed inappropriate. "The album featured a track called New York City Cops. But, October 2001 was too close to 9-11 to release a song critical of any first responders."
**When It Started (2001)** : This song replaced "New York City Cops" on the US version of "Is This It." The swap was made for the North American market because the original track was considered critical of first responders, and its release in October 2001 was too close to the 9/11 events. "So, American copies feature another track called When It Started. Again, probably wise."
**1251 (2003)** : The first single from "Room on Fire," this song was noted for reminding listeners of Sonic Youth's "Bull in the Heather," though the resemblance was completely unintentional. The track also featured a distinctive, "weird guitar tone" from Nick Valensi, which originated from an accident in the studio and became a signature sound in many of The Strokes' songs. "The first single was called 1251, which reminded people of a sonic youth song called Bull in the Heather."
**Juice Box (2006)** : This was the first single from "First Impressions of Earth." The Strokes had to give the famous American composer Henry Mancini a songwriting credit for the song due to its melodic similarities to the theme from the 1950s TV series "Peter Gunn." Henry Mancini himself, however, did not benefit from this credit, as he had passed away approximately ten years before the song's release. "The strokes had to give Henry Man Sini, the famous American composer, a songwriting credit, because this sounded a little too much, like the theme from an American TV series from the 50s called Peter Gunn."
**Undercover of Darkness (2011)** : This song was the first single released from The Strokes' fourth album, "Angles." Its warm reception and description as a "return to form" helped the album be warmly received. "Undercover of Darkness" became the third best-performing song in the band's career on the charts. "Undercover of Darkness was the third best performer in the band's career."
**One Way Trigger (2013)** : This track was the first single from The Strokes' fifth album, "Come Down Machine." Despite the band's complete media blackout for the album's promotion, which included no interviews, gigs, tours, photo shoots, or videos, the single was released. "The most important thing was that the fans dug it, and the first single was one way trigger."
**Drag Queen (2016)** : This song is featured on the four-track EP "Future Present Past." It was released digitally and on two colors of vinyl, and was part of a promotional campaign for the EP, which included an Instagram campaign, a satellite radio show hosted by Julian Casablancas, live shows, and a pop-up shop in New York City. "It's called Drag Queen."
**Eternal Sunday (2020)** : This song from "The New Abnormal" album led to songwriting credits for Richard and Tom Butler of The Psychedelic Furs. The credit was given because "Eternal Sunday" was deemed to sound too similar to The Psychedelic Furs' song "The Ghost In You." "There's another song on here called Eternal Sunday, which sounded a little too much like The Ghost In You from The Psychedelic Furs, which is why Richard and Tom Butler of the Furs get a songwriting credit on that."
**At The Door (2020)** : Another single from "The New Abnormal" album, this song is notable for including The Strokes' guitar technician in its songwriting credits. This is an unusual occurrence for a roadie to receive such recognition. "Part of the songwriting credit went to the Strokes Guitar Tech. How many times do you see a roadie getting credit?"

## curiosities
**Band Formation and Early Members** : Julian Casablancas and Nikolai Fraiture met at age six while attending a prestigious French school in New York City. Julian met Albert Hammond Jr. at the Institute Le Rose, a highly expensive private boarding school in Switzerland. By 1997, Julian and Nick Valensi were at Dwight School, where they met Fabrizio Moretti and started a band. Albert moved from Switzerland to New York for film school, became Julian's roommate, and joined the band, with Nikolai joining shortly after as the bassist. "By 1997, Julian and Nick were attending a college prep school called the Dwight School. Again, not cheap."
**Julian Casablancas' Background** : Julian is the son of John Casablancas, founder of the Elite Model Management Agency, which managed stars like Cindy Crawford, Stephanie Seymour, Gisele Bündchen, Linda Evangelista, Naomi Campbell, Kendall Jenner, Pauline Apport's Gova, Claudia Schiffer, and Heidi Klum. His mother, a former Miss Denmark, encouraged his interest in bands like The Doors, moving him away from Phil Collins records. Julian had a teenage drinking problem, leading him to rehab in high school, which he attributed to his environment, rebelling to be closer to his father. The connection to the modeling agency proved useful, as models from Elite would attend early Strokes gigs, helping with attendance. "He's the son of John Casablanca's, the founder of the famous Elite Model Management Agency."
**Albert Hammond Jr.'s Background and Role** : Albert Hammond Jr. is the son of Albert Hammond Sr., a successful songwriter who wrote songs for artists like Celine Dion, Aretha Franklin, Whitney Houston, Willie Nelson, Chicago, and The Carpenters, and had a writing credit on Radiohead's "Creep." In the early days of The Strokes, Albert was the band's business guy, booking gigs and pitching record companies under the pseudonym "Paul Spencer" (from his fake ID). He collected rejection letters by posting them on a wall. Crucially, he had a credit card funded by his father, which paid for essential band expenses like guitar strings, cables, drumsticks, and rehearsal PA. "Albert was the business guy of the band in the early days. He was the guy who booked gigs and pitch record companies, usually under the pseudonym Paul Spencer, which was the name he used on his old fake ID."
**Nicholas Valensi's Background** : Nicholas Valensi, another member from the Upper East Side of New York, spent his summers on his grandfather's estate in Bordeaux. "Guitarist Nicholas Valenci was another kid from the Upper East Side of New York, who spent his summers on his grandfather's estate in Bordeaux."
**Fabrizio Moretti's Background** : Fabrizio Moretti was born in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil. His father was a nuclear engineer who relocated the family to New York City. "And drummer for Brizio Mareti was born in Rio de Janeiro. His dad was a nuclear engineer who relocated the family to New York."
**Nikolai Fraiture's Background** : Nikolai was the only member who grew up poor. His father worked as a security guard at Macy's, and his family of five lived in a two-room apartment. "It should be noted that out of all the members, Nikolai was the only one who grew up poor."
**Early Struggles and Financial Support** : In the late 90s, the New York rock scene was considered dead, with few venues beyond private parties in Brooklyn warehouses. The Strokes worked through these challenges with Albert Hammond Jr.'s father's credit card and part-time jobs. Julian Casablancas was a bartender at Max's Kansas City, Nikolai Fraiture worked at a video store, Fabrizio Moretti made smoothies, and Albert Hammond Jr. worked at Kim's video. "Along with Albert's dad's credit card, they also had part-time jobs."
**Band Name Origin** : The band played their first gig under the name The Strokes on September 14, 1999, at The Spiral in New York. The name "The Strokes" has no special story; Julian Casablancas blurted it out during rehearsal, and the group adopted it because it was the name everyone hated the least. "There's no special story behind the name, The Strokes. It's just one that everybody hated the least."
**Nikolai Fraiture Backstage Incident** : During an early show where The Strokes opened for Girl Harbor at Don Hills, Nikolai Fraiture was caught having sex backstage in the basement and photographed. Girl Harbor then used this photo for a flyer for the next Strokes gig, with the caption, "this is what happened at our last show." "Nik was caught having sex backstage down on the basement and was photographed."
**Distinct Personalities and Spin Magazine Covers** : The Strokes' lineup, which has never changed, was noted for its five members having five distinct personalities and images. This individuality was so strong that Spin Magazine eventually featured each member on their own cover. "We had five guys with five distinct personalities and five distinct images. This individuality was so strong that Spin Magazine would eventually give each guy their own cover."
**Booker Becomes Manager** : The band's performances at small venues, including the Mercury Lounge (capacity 250), greatly impressed the venue's booker. He was so taken with their set of "a little more than a dozen songs" that he quit his job to become The Strokes' manager. "They had a set of a little more than a dozen songs, which so impressed the booker at the Mercury Lounge that he quit and became their manager."
**The Modern Age EP and Bidding War** : The Strokes recorded a three-track demo in 2000, which was unusually tight for a young group due to their insistence on rehearsing to a click track. This led to "The Modern Age" EP, which sold well and ignited one of the fiercest bidding wars among record labels in a long time. Rough Trade Records (UK) won the bid, prompting label head Geoff Travis to resurrect the label to sign The Strokes. They also secured a deal with RCA, which was amenable to the band's desire to make a music video. "The subsequent EP, the three track, the modern age, not only sold respectively well, but also sparked a bidding war amongst record labels."
**Is This It Album Title and Covers** : The band's debut album was titled "Is This It," intentionally without a question mark because it was felt that "it looked wrong." The album featured two different covers. The original cover depicted the photographer's naked girlfriend, fresh out of the shower, with a black glove as a prop, creating a "spinal tapish" effect. Julian Casablancas later suggested a second cover, an image of subatomic particles from a physics experiment at CERN in Switzerland, which was adopted due to label concerns about displaying the naked woman in stores, particularly in the "puritanical US." Releases in July and August 2001 featured the original cover, while all subsequent releases used the second. "No question mark because it was felt that it looked wrong."
**First Impressions of Earth Hiatus** : The relatively weak commercial and critical performance of the album "First Impressions of Earth" prompted the band to rethink their approach, leading to a three-year break. The tour for this album concluded in 2007, and no new music from The Strokes was released until 2010, marking a five-year gap between albums. "The relatively weak performance of First Impressions of Earth prompted a rethink that lasted three years."
**Albert Hammond Jr.'s Drug Problem** : During the period leading up to the "Angles" album, Albert Hammond Jr. developed a severe drug problem stemming from a breakup. He used heroin, cocaine, and ketamine, described as "a mess," and required time in rehab to address his addiction. "Another thing, Albert Hammond Jr. had developed a nasty drug problem from a breakup and required time in rehab."
**Julian Casablancas' Folding Electric Bicycle Patent** : In the fall of 2015, Julian Casablancas filed for a patent for a folding electric bicycle that did not have pedals. The patent listed Richard Priest, The Strokes' tour manager, and Warren Foo, their video director, as co-inventors alongside Casablancas. "In the fall of 2015, Julian filed for a patent for a folding electric bicycle. No pedals."
**Albert Hammond Jr.'s Fashion Line** : Albert Hammond Jr. developed a significant interest in fashion. He launched his own line of men's suits and ties, with a particular enjoyment for designing ties. "He even has his own line of men's suits and ties. He really likes designing ties."
**Fabrizio Moretti's Art and Relationships** : Fabrizio Moretti works in sculpture and performance art. He was in a relationship with Drew Barrymore for several years, which ended in 2007. "Fabio Moretti works with sculpture and performance arts. He was the one with Drew Barrymore for years, with that ended in 2007."
**Julian Casablancas Solo Projects** : Julian Casablancas has released solo records under his own name. Additionally, he formed and released two albums with a band called The Voids. "Julian has won under his own name and two with a band he called The Voids."
**Nikolai Fraiture Solo Projects** : Nikolai Fraiture has pursued solo musical endeavors, including a band named Nicolai. He also fronted another band called Summer Moon, which featured Stephen Perkins, the drummer from Jane's Addiction. Fraiture also collaborates with his brother on a project that merges music with performance art painting. "Nicolai had a band called Nicolai and another called Summer Moon, which featured Jane's addiction drummer Stephen Perkins."
**Nicholas Valensi Solo Projects** : Nicholas Valensi has a side project known as CRX, under which he has released two albums. "And Nic has a sign project called CRX, which has two albums."
**Albert Hammond Jr. Solo Projects** : Albert Hammond Jr. has released a substantial amount of solo material, including four solo records and an EP. "And then we have Albert Hammond Jr., who released four solo records at an EP."
**Fabrizio Moretti Solo Projects** : Fabrizio Moretti has been involved with several other bands, including Little Joy, Megapus, and Machine Gum. He has also contributed as a helper or session musician with various other artists. "And finally, for Brizio, his worked in bands called Little Joy, Megapus, and Machine Gum, as well as helping out or sitting in with other artists."
**Influence on Other Bands** : The Strokes, particularly with their debut album "Is This It," profoundly influenced many bands that followed, making them one of the most important bands of the 2000s. Kings of Leon learned to play by emulating "Is This It." The Killers, after hearing the album, reportedly scrapped all their existing material except for "Mr. Brightside" and restarted, with elements of The Strokes' construction and recording audible in their music. Other bands like Arctic Monkeys, Franz Ferdinand, and LCD Sound System also credit The Strokes as an influence. "If it wasn't for the Strokes, it's possible that the Indy Rock Garage Rock Revival that took hold in New York City in the early 2000s wouldn't have grown the way it did."

# genre - Indy Rock Revival

## curiosities
**Rise in Early 2000s** : The Indy Rock Revival began at the very tail end of the 1990s and gained significant momentum over the next couple of years, with The Strokes identified as one of its very first new bands. By late 2003/early 2004, The Strokes, along with bands like The White Stripes, the Yeah Yeah Yeahs, Interpol, The Vines, The Hives, and Arcade Fire, were firmly established within this movement, leading to cries of "Rock is Back" after a period dominated by boy bands and other pop stars. **State of Music in 2001** : In the summer of 2001, the mainstream music scene was dominated by pop acts like Spice Girls, Backstreet Boys, Britney Spears, and NSYNC. Alt-rock had lost direction after grunge faded, and new metal, while popular, was highly polarizing. A new genre, "Electronic Hour," was siphoning off rock fans, making traditional rock music with guitars, bass, drums, and vocals seem "old-fashioned" and "played out." "The mainstream was a wash in pop music, spice girls, backstreet boys, Britney Spears, in sync, and alt-rock had kind of lost its way after Grunge burned out."
**"Rock is Dead" Trope and Revival** : Historically, every time rock music has been declared "dead," something or someone has emerged to revitalize it, typically through a "back-to-basics" approach. This pattern was observed again with the rise of bands like The Strokes and the Indy Rock Revival. "In the past, every time rock was declared dead, someone or something came along and breathed new life into everything, usually with a back-to-basics approach."
**New York Rock Scene in the Late 1990s** : Unlike the mid-1970s when the original punk scene spawned numerous bands in New York, the late 1990s saw a dormant rock scene, with hip-hop thriving but rock struggling. There were few venues available for rock bands, leading many to play at private parties in Brooklyn warehouses, often in areas inaccessible by cabs. "The sentence amongst music fans was that the New York rock scene was dead. The best anyone could do was play private parties."
**Melodic Appropriation in Songwriting** : The practice of giving songwriting credits to other artists due to melodic similarities in new songs was highlighted by several instances involving The Strokes. Billy Idol and Tony James of Generation X received credit for "Bad Decisions" due to its resemblance to "Dancing With Myself," and Richard and Tom Butler of The Psychedelic Furs received credit for "Eternal Sunday" due to its similarity to "The Ghost In You." Henry Mancini also received a credit for "Juice Box" because of its likeness to the "Peter Gunn Theme." "We'll revisit that topic of melodic appropriation a little bit later on."
**"Rock is Back" Cry in Early 2000s** : By late 2003 and early 2004, with the success of bands like The Strokes, The White Stripes, and Arcade Fire, a sentiment of "Rock is Back" emerged. This marked a shift after rock music had played "second fiddle to Boy Bands and other pop stars" for a period. "If you were around back then, you might remember cries of Rock is Back after playing Second Fiddled to Boy Bands and other pop stars."
**Music Industry Transition (Physical to Streaming)** : By the time The Strokes released "Come Down Machine" in 2013, the music industry was in a "forced transition" from an era focused on selling physical copies ("pieces of plastic") to the modern era of streaming. In this new landscape, the number of physical copies sold became less important, with fan engagement and reception taking precedence. "They were any forced transition from the days of selling pieces of plastic to the era of streaming. Counting the number of physical copies sold was less important."
