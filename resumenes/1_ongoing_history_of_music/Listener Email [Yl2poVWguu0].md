# artist - Metallica

## albums
**Death Magnetic (2008)** : This album, released in 2008, became one of the most controversial albums of the year, not for its content, but for its sound quality. It is cited as a prime example of the "Loudness Wars" due to excessive compression, which resulted in a lack of dynamic range and perceived distortion, even at low volumes. Fans launched online petitions demanding the band re-do the album. "Not for what it says or what the band has done, but for the way the album sounds."

## curiosities
**Loudness Wars Controversy with "Death Magnetic"** : Metallica's 2008 album "Death Magnetic" is considered a prime example of the negative effects of the Loudness Wars, where excessive compression eliminated dynamic range. This resulted in parts of songs that should have been quieter sounding just as loud as the louder parts, and distortion was audible on many stereo systems, even at low volumes. Despite hundreds or thousands of fans disagreeing, Metallica and producer Rick Rubin stated that the album's sound was "all fine." "Death Magnetic has so much compression on it that people say that there's distortion all over it, even at the lowest volumes."

**The Loudness Wars Explained** : The "Loudness Wars" refer to a trend where artists, producers, and record labels aim to make their music sound louder by applying more compression to recordings, squishing the natural dynamic range of songs. This practice is driven by the belief that louder music stands out more, but it often leads to a lack of dynamic range, distortion even at low volumes, and listener fatigue. Key concepts include 'level' (technical sound pressure), 'volume' (subjective device-related setting), and 'loudness' (perceived difference between soft and loud parts, which increases with compression but not necessarily with level or volume). "artists, producers and record labels have gotten to this arms war over loudness. They believe that if something sounds louder it will stand out."
**Music Video Evolution and Decline** : The traditional music video, popular in the 1970s and 80s when seeing a favorite band was a novelty, is considered "dead" in its original form. Once a valuable marketing tool that could generate significant album sales for a half-million dollar investment, videos are now often "sinkholes with no return" due to the constant availability of content on platforms like YouTube via smartphones. The music video is evolving, with some creators utilizing mobile phone video capabilities for production. "At least in the form in which it was born back in the 70s and 80s."
**Impact of Illegal Downloading on Music Industry** : Illegal downloading of music directly reduces artists' income, particularly affecting niche artists for whom "every penny means something." This loss of revenue forces bands, even successful ones like U2, The Rolling Stones, or Oasis, to seek alternative income streams, leading to higher concert ticket prices, expensive band merchandise, and increased instances of bands licensing their songs for TV commercials. "Every free illegal download is money out of an artist's pocket."
