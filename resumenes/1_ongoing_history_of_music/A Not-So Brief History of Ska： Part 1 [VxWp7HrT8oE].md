# artist - Prince Buster

## albums
**Al Capone (1967) – First Wave Ska; Prince Buster Shifted the Beat to Create Ska** : Cecil Bustamante Campbell — known by his stage name Prince Buster — was a pivotal figure in the creation of ska. Together with guitarist Jerry Johnson, he moved the beat emphasis from the 1 and 3 to the 2 and 4, stressing the offbeat. This change in syncopation, made in 1962, was the spark that created ska as a distinct genre. Prince Buster was also the source of the name for Madness — their name came from an old Prince Buster song. "Cecil moved the beat emphasis from the 1 and the 3 to the 2 and the 4. In other words, the off beat was stressed. This change in syncopation was the spark and SCA was born."


# artist - The Skatalites

## members
Don Drummond

## curiosities
**The Skatalites (Jamaica, 1963–1967) – Trombone-Led Studio Band; Played on Virtually Every Jamaican Record; Don Drummond Murdered His Girlfriend; Died in Asylum 1969** : Don Drummond was a trombone player who gave Jamaican ska a slightly darker feel. He first called his group The Satellites, then changed the name to The Skatalites. The Skatalites played on virtually every record made in Jamaica between 1963 and 1967, functioning as the equivalent of the studio bands at Motown and Stax in America. Their choppy sound became hugely influential and may have given ska its name. Unfortunately, Don Drummond never saw what became of his invention — in 1964 he murdered his girlfriend and died in an insane asylum in 1969, some say of natural causes, others say he was murdered by gangsters avenging his girlfriend's death, and some claim it was a government plot to suppress the powerful Jamaican music scene. "The SCA-TILites played on virtually every record made in Jamaica between 1963 and 1967."


# artist - Desmond Dekker

## curiosities
**Desmond Dekker – Jamaica's Top Singer of the 1960s; Ska's First Worldwide Hit (1969); Worked in Welding Shop with Bob Marley** : Desmond Dekker used to work in a Jamaican welding shop alongside a kid from St. Anne's parish named Bob Marley. Dekker became Jamaica's top singer of the 1960s and, thanks to Island Records (run by Englishman Chris Blackwell, who was in love with Caribbean music), scored ska's first worldwide hit in 1969. "He used to work in a Jamaican welding shop along a kid from St. Anne's parish named Bob Marley."


# artist - The Specials

## members
Jerry Dammers

## albums
**Gangsters (1979) – First Two-Tone Single; Band Borrowed £700 from Family; B-Side Created The Selector** : In March 1979, the Specials borrowed 700 pounds from family and friends to record "Gangsters," the first Two-Tone single, based on a sample from a Prince Buster song. Having only enough money to record the A-side, Jerry Dammers offered the B-side to a friend named Noel Davis, who contributed an instrumental he had recorded with his brother-in-law in his living room in 1977. Dammers remixed the track and included it as the B-side; the song was called "The Selector" — and that became the name of the (then non-existent) band. This shared 7-inch was the beginning of the post-punk ska revival, which was faster and tighter than traditional ska with a greater reliance on horn sections. "Gangsters was the first two-tone single and a fine example of cooperation."


## curiosities
**Two-Tone Records Founded 1979 – Black-and-White Logo Based on Peter Tosh Photo; Anti-Racist Mission** : Jerry Dammers founded Two-Tone Records, naming it after the black-and-white suits worn by mods and skinheads of the 1960s and after the peaceful multiracial makeup of the band. The label's logo — a man in a black and white suit, white shirt, black tie, sunglasses, pork pie hat, white socks, and black loafers — was drawn by Dammers based on a photo of Peter Tosh when he was a member of the Wailers. The character was named Walt Jabsco (Walt after Walt Disney). Two-Tone had an explicit anti-racism message at a time when racial tensions in the UK were getting out of control. "Two-tone became the core of the second wave of Scott."


# artist - Madness

## curiosities
**Madness – Named After Old Prince Buster Song; North London Group; "Nutty Sound"** : Madness was a North London group with what was described as a "nutty sound" — a loose blend of punk and traditional Jamaican ska. Two-Tone signed them and issued their first singles. Their name came from an old Prince Buster song called "Madness." "Two-tone also issued the first singles from a group that featured Prince Buster's old saxophone player, and they were called Mbiet." [Madness]


# artist - The English Beat

## members
Dave Wakeling

## curiosities
**The English Beat – Mirror in the Bathroom (1980); Held Out Against Violence and Racism Through Music; Second Wave Ended ~1985** : The English Beat, led by Dave Wakeling, were formed in the late 1970s and had their first hit with "Mirror in the Bathroom" (1980), a classic of the great ska revival. The second wave of ska — spanning roughly 1979 to 1985 — was vibrant and carried a hopeful message as a positive alternative to hard economic times and racial riots in Britain. By the mid-1980s, Jerry Dammers was broke and Two-Tone was falling apart under huge debt; despite efforts by the Clash, the Beat, and Bad Manners, the second wave ended around 1985. "Ska was a positive alternative to the hard economic times and the racial riots that were plaguing Britain in the early 1980s."


# genre - Ska

## curiosities
**Origins of Ska – Jamaica 1950s; African Rhythms + Jazz/R&B + Calypso + Mento = Shuffle, Then Ska** : Ska was born in Jamaica in the years following World War II. Big Band, Dixieland, Jazz, Blues, and R&B were heard over shortwave radio from places like New Orleans and from American military personnel in the Caribbean. These were mixed with Caribbean rhythms including Calypso (from Trinidad and Tobago, descended from Biguine, a form of French music co-opted by African slaves) and Mento (Jamaican folk music based on traditional sounds from Africa). This musical stew became known as Shuffle and was heard on Jamaican national radio through the late 1950s and early 1960s. In 1962, Cecil Bustamante Campbell (Prince Buster) changed the beat emphasis to the 2 and 4, and ska was born. "So we have Dixieland, we have Jazz, Blues, R&B, Calypso and Minto. This musical stew became known as Schuffle."

**Definition of Ska – 4/4 Time, Beat on 2 and 4, Guitar on 1 and 3, Bass Quarter Notes, Brass Arrangements** : Ska is a choppy staccato form of pop music in 4/4 time where the beat is emphasized on the second and fourth beats of each bar, while the guitar hits on the first, third, and sometimes fourth beats. Arrangements often feature brass instruments like trombones and saxophones, and the bass line plays steady quarter notes. "Skye is a choppy staccato form of pop music it's in 4x4 time where the beat is emphasized on the second and fourth beats of each bar."

**Why "Ska"? – Murky Origin: Guitar Sound, Clue J. Johnson's Made-Up Word, Byron Lee, or The Skatalites** : The origin of the word "ska" is murky. Theories include: it was taken from the sound of the choppy guitar click (ska-ska-ska); a Jamaican musician named Clue J. Johnson liked to say "ska-vuvie" all the time; Byron Lee, leader of a band called the Dragonaires, coined it; or The Skatalites invented it when they changed their name from The Satellites. "The origins of the word SCA are a little murky."

**Ska Became Jamaica's National Dance – Skanking; Then Reggae Took Over by 1969** : Ska was the dominant music of Jamaica through the mid-1960s and was essentially the national dance of the new country. The dance was called skanking — bend the knees, run in place, make fists, bend the elbows, punch and step to the beat. By the late 1960s, poverty and political unrest in Kingston and Montego Bay's ghettos produced the Rude Boys — alienated, unemployed youth who ran dances with big sound systems. The music slowed down and became more menacing, becoming reggae. This ended the first wave of ska by 1969. "SCA became the semi-official sound of this new country."

**Jamaican Independence and Bauxite Mining – Why Jamaicans Moved to UK; Why Ska Arrived in Britain** : Jamaica's desire and ability to stop being a British colony was aided by foreign investment in bauxite mining (Jamaica became the world's number one bauxite producer by 1957 thanks to investor Sir Alfred DeCosta). Young people sold or were removed from rural land by the bauxite miners and moved to cities, settling in ghettos like Trench Town. Starting in 1948, Britain offered all colonials the right to live in the UK due to a post-war labor shortage, and many Jamaicans emigrated to UK cities like Brixton and Coventry, bringing ska (locally called "Bluebeat") with them. In Coventry, bluebeat existed alongside Northern Soul, R&B, reggae, and the mod youth movement — mods were huge fans of all these sounds. "Starting in 1948, Britain said, hey, all you colonials, you have the right to live with us."

**Don Letts DJ'd Reggae at the Roxy (1976–77) – Punk Fans Embraced the Anti-Establishment Bass Lines and "Musical Reportage"** : Don Letts, later an associate of the Clash and a member of Big Audio Dynamite, was DJing only reggae at the Roxy club in 1976–77 to punk audiences. The punk crowd connected with reggae's "musical reportage quality" — the fact that songs were about something — the obvious anti-establishment stance, the bass lines, and the weed. This cross-pollination of punk and ska created the second wave. "Like minded spirits, you know, they were kind of reveled certainly on the streets of London that were thrown together as outsiders."
