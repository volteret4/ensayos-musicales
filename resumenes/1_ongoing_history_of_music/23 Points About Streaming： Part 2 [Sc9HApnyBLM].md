**Concept of the Library** : The idea of collecting all available knowledge in one central place originated many centuries ago, providing a single location for people to find answers to their questions. "thus the concept of the library was born."
**Archiving Recorded Music** : The concept of a central repository for knowledge was later extended to recorded music, leading governments, public broadcasters, and companies to systematically collect and archive humanity's audio recordings. "governments, public broadcasters and companies began collecting together as much of human kinds recorded audio as they could."
**BBC's Physical Media Archive** : The British Broadcasting Corporation (BBC) is renowned for its extensive archive of physical media, boasting hundreds of kilometers of shelving dedicated to storing recorded audio. "The BBC famously has hundreds of kilometers of shelving for physical media."
**Zero Freitas's Record Collection** : Zero Freitas, a collector from Brazil, embarked on a mission to amass a private collection encompassing every record ever made, accumulating at least 8 million records and over 100,000 compact discs, though even this vast collection doesn't cover all music. "He has at least 8 million records and more than 100,000 compact discs, which is nice, but it still doesn't cover everything."
**1980s Vision of Digital Music Archive** : During the 1980s, individuals began envisioning a massive centralized computer capable of storing all of humanity's music in digital format, making any song instantly accessible upon demand. "In the 1980s, some people started to conceive of a giant computer somewhere that could hold humanity's music in digital form."
**"Celestial Jukebox" Concept (1994)** : In 1994, law professor Paul Goldstein coined and popularized the term "Celestial Jukebox" to describe his vision of a networked database, accessible to anyone with an internet connection, that would contain all music. "In 1994, a law professor named Paul Goldstein popularized the term Celestial Jukebox."
**Napster's Launch and Impact (1999)** : Napster launched online five years after the "Celestial Jukebox" concept, creating a sensation by allowing users to download virtually any song they desired, despite the legal ambiguities surrounding its operation. "Five years later, Napster went online and suddenly it seemed that you could download any song you wanted."
**iTunes Music Store Launch (2003)** : The iTunes Music Store debuted in 2003, initially offering hundreds of thousands of songs for sale, and has since grown to include approximately 60 million purchasable tracks. "Then in 2003 came the iTunes music star."
**Limitation of iTunes Music Store** : Despite its vast catalog, the iTunes Music Store still required users to purchase individual tracks, which was seen as a limitation compared to emerging models. "But that still doesn't quite cut it because it still involves buying this music."
**Modern Streaming Music (Present)** : Current streaming platforms provide access to a digital music library of at least 90 million songs, with new additions daily, allowing users to access virtually any recorded song instantly from any device, often at negligible cost or even for free, thus realizing the "theoretical Celestial Jukebox." "Today, of course, we have streaming. All the platforms draw from a digital music library that contains at least 90 million songs and more are being added every day."
**Misconception of Direct Artist Payment** : There is a common assumption among users that subscription fees directly pay the specific artists whose songs they stream, but this is not how streaming payment systems are structured. "If you stream a song from an artist, you would expect that your subscription money goes to paying that artist. You know, you're listening to their song, so they should get paid with your money. Not necessarily."
**Criticism of Streaming Payment Model** : A major criticism of the current streaming payment system is that it disproportionately benefits established and famous artists, effectively marginalizing smaller artists who struggle to earn revenue. "The big criticism here is that the system favors the already rich and the already famous, and the smaller artists get squeezed out of any revenues."
**Decline of CD Profits & Rise of Streaming** : The significant profit margins once associated with CD sales are irrecoverable, as streaming has fundamentally replaced traditional methods of music consumption, leading to a drastically different and less lucrative revenue model for artists compared to physical sales. "Those margins and profits on CDs are not coming back. Streaming has supplanted the old ways when it comes to the way people consume music."
**Advocacy for "Penny Per Stream" Payments** : The diminished earnings for artists in the streaming era have led to calls for a standardized payment of one penny per stream, aiming to provide a more substantial, though still potentially insufficient, income compared to past physical sales. "So you can see why there were calls for artists to be paid a penny per stream."
**30-Second Minimum for Streaming Payouts** : A universal rule across all streaming music services dictates that a song must be listened to for a minimum of 30 seconds to qualify as an official play, and anything less than this duration results in no payment to the artist. "In order to qualify for a song as being officially played, you have to listen for at least 30 seconds."
**Tracking and Payouts of Official Plays** : Streaming platforms meticulously log every official song play, compiling these statistics monthly to calculate and distribute payments to artists based on their previously discussed percentage share formula. "The streaming platforms are able to log each and every song with an official play."
**Challenge of Song Identification in Streaming** : A critical component for the streaming payment system is the precise identification of each played song, which presents an extremely complex challenge to eliminate all potential errors in attributing plays. "How do you identify a specific song so that you've eliminated all possibility of error?"
**Importance of Metadata in Streaming** : The accurate and consistent identification of songs for streaming payouts relies heavily on metadata, which is embedded information within digital music files. "This is where we enter the universe of metadata."
**Definition and Components of Metadata** : Metadata refers to the descriptive information embedded within a digital audio file, encompassing details such as song title, artist, album title, composer, genre, release year, song length, track number, artwork, lyrics, bit rate, and sampling rate, all of which are crucial for identification. "Metadata is the information about a song embedded within the digital file."
**Absence of a Universal Music Metadata Database** : Contrary to popular belief, a comprehensive worldwide database containing the complete metadata for every single song does not exist, primarily due to the immense volume of music that would need to be processed and entered. "You would think that there's a worldwide database somewhere featuring the complete metadata of every single song, but there isn't."
**International Standard Recording Code (ISRC)** : The International Standard Recording Code (ISRC) is a method developed to assign a unique identifier number to individual audio recordings, representing one attempt at standardizing music identification. "One method, which is pretty good, is the International Standard Recording Code, or ISRC Number."
**ISRC System Complexity (Post-1986)** : Since 1986, efforts have been made to assign an ISRC number to every audio recording, but this process is significantly more complex than it appears due to the multitude of versions and releases. "Since 1986, there have been efforts to assign a number to each and every audio recording out there."
**ISRC Number Specificity and Regional Variations** : ISRC numbers are highly specific, with each unique release, remix, live recording, or cover version requiring its own number, further compounded by the need for distinct codes across different geographical territories like Canada, the US, UK, and France. "Each release requires a new ISRC number."
**Disbursement Error due to Generic Song Titles** : When a generic title like "I Love You" is streamed, services must identify the correct artist, and if metadata is faulty, they often default to the most popular song with that title, potentially misdirecting royalties to an unrelated artist. "The streaming music services have to figure out which I love you it is, which artists it really belongs to."
**Orphaned Royalties and Performing Rights Organizations** : Revenues from songs with insufficient metadata, like "I Love You," may be held in an account while performing rights collectives globally attempt to identify and compensate the rightful author. "And any number of performing rights collectives around the planet will try to track down the rightful author of that particular song called, I love you and pay them."
**Issue of "Orphan Songs"** : Hundreds of thousands of "orphan songs" exist because they lack proper metadata, meaning they are unable to earn money they are due, with the associated cash accumulating in accounts while identification efforts proceed. "What we've ended up with is hundreds of thousands of orphan songs orphan because they don't contain the proper metadata."

**Single-Driven Album Releases (Mid-Late 1950s)** : During the mid to late 1950s, even with the existence of notable rock and roll albums, the single reigned supreme; albums were typically released only after an artist had achieved success with a series of singles, which were then compiled with additional tracks, reflecting how consumers purchased individual radio hits. **Enduring Success of Pre-2000 Artists** : Artists who achieved success before the year 2000, such as Pearl Jam, Red Hot Chili Peppers, Green Day, The Offspring, The Rolling Stones, Paul McCartney, and U2, often continue to thrive today, sustained by the career momentum built in the pre-internet era. "The acts that thrived pre-2000 are in many cases still thriving today."
**Challenges for Post-2000 Artists** : Artists emerging after 2000 face significant struggles in building careers due to an overwhelming volume of music, intense competition, and excessive choice for music fans, making it exceptionally difficult to gain recognition in the post-internet era. "But artists that have risen after 2000, let's generally be in a struggle, largely because it's almost impossible to make a name for yourself in the post-internet era."
**Song-Centric Consumption in Streaming Era** : In the streaming era, many music fans prioritize individual songs over artists, often being unable to name the artist behind a viral track on platforms like Spotify, Instagram, or TikTok, leading to fleeting relationships where interest ends with the song. "People love the song, not you, as the artist. And once they're done with the song, they're done with you."
**Rise of One-Hit Wonders in Streaming Era** : The song-centric nature of streaming makes it exceptionally challenging for new artists to forge lasting careers, resulting in a noticeable increase in one-hit wonders over the past decade. "That makes it extremely difficult for any new artist to build a career. The result? A growing number of one-hit wonders in the last decade."
**Data Analytics for Artist Careers** : Artists are encouraged to leverage streaming data analytics to advance their careers, as the industry has become a "moneyball" environment where insights from platforms like Spotify are crucial for identifying trends and opportunities. "Spotify and the rest of them have turned into moneyball."
**Real-Time Data Analytics in Music Industry** : Streaming platforms generate vast amounts of data, enabling unprecedented real-time identification of music trends and career opportunities; record labels now have internal analytics departments, and specialized companies assist musicians in tracking global song performance and consumption. "The insane amount of data that can be harvested from the streaming platforms makes it possible to identify trends and opportunities in ways never before possible."
**Data-Driven Music Creation and Promotion** : Streaming data now dictates music culture, allowing for real-time identification of traction by genre, length, key, and chord progressions, and promoting a paradigm where engagement data, rather than inherent "goodness," drives music creation and promotion optimized for maximum revenue. "This data is also dictating the course of music culture. We can see what sorts of songs are getting traction at any given second."
**Formulaic Music Creation** : The reliance on streaming data has fostered a formulaic approach to music creation, akin to the movie industry's superhero genre, where artists and labels employ data-driven formulas to produce similar-sounding songs designed for maximum revenue, explaining why much new music sounds alike. "With music, everyone is swinging for the fences using the same data to create the same formulas. And ultimately, the same sorts of songs."
**Collaborative, Data-Driven Songwriting** : Modern songwriting, driven by audience engagement data, often requires the combined efforts of multiple musicians, beatmakers, producers, and lyricists to craft a single song optimized for streaming success. "The middies of musicians and beatmakers and producers and lyricists are needed to write a single song using audience engagement data."
**Repetitive Listening by Young Streamers** : Young users, who are the heaviest consumers of streaming services, frequently listen to their favorite songs on repeat, a behavior historically observed in youth (dating back to portable turntables and 45s) but now quantifiable through data analytics to inform the creation of new music designed to encourage similar repetition. "The heaviest users of streaming music services tend to skew young. They also tend to listen to their favorite songs over and over and over and over and over and over and over and over and over again."
**Repetitive Listening's Impact on Charts and Payouts** : Repetitive listening by users propels songs up streaming charts and through algorithms, increasing their exposure and leading to more repeat listens, ultimately resulting in higher payouts for creators and greater profits for record labels. "Repetition then pushes the song up the streaming charts and up through the algorithms, so it's exposed to more people who also put it on repeat."
**Genre Prioritization in Music Promotion** : Hip Hop, Rap, R&B, and Pop genres receive the bulk of label promotional efforts and resources due to their demonstrated streaming success in the "musical moneyball" environment, leaving other genres like Rock, Country, or Jazz with residual attention. "So the label sped all their time and money promoting that music. And if there's anything left over, it goes to rock or country or jazz or whatever."
**Mandate for Artists to Use Streaming and YouTube** : For contemporary artists, having music on streaming platforms is essential, as is maintaining a presence on YouTube, which remains the single largest global source for music discovery; absence from YouTube effectively means non-existence to a significant portion of the audience. "If you are an artist, you have to stream your music. And you have to get something on YouTube too, because that's still the biggest source of music discovery on the planet."
**Strategic Use of Data Analytics by Artists** : Artists are advised to utilize available streaming data analytics to inform career decisions such as tour planning (where and when), merchandise sales management, and optimal new music release timing, mirroring strategies employed by major stars to enhance their success. "You need to get into data analytics. That's what all the big stars are doing."
