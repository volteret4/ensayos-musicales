Apple was considered "mostly a corporate basket case." "Even Google was less than 18 months old at the time."
**Compact Disc Dominance in 2000** : In 2000, compact discs were extremely popular, selling by hundreds of millions, which led to the music industry being highly profitable. "They were still selling by hundreds of millions, ensuring that the music industry was drowning in money."
**Vinyl's Status in 2000** : At the start of the 21st century, vinyl was considered "dead," with only club DJs keeping the format on life support due to their preference for records over CDs in the booth. "Vinyl, dead, dead, dead."

"We had MP3s, and we had begun to trade music files online, but that was still a fairly clunky and frustrating experience for most people..."
**Technological Landscape in 2009** : By December 31, 2009, smartphones were becoming ubiquitous, digital music files were widely used, and streaming services were gaining traction, with Apple, Google, and Facebook becoming some of the world's most powerful companies. "Everyone was getting smartphones."
**CD Sales Decline (2000-2009)** : Global CD sales plummeted from a peak of $26 billion USD in 2000 to approximately $9 billion in 2009, with further declines anticipated, indicating a significant crisis for the recorded music industry. "Global CD sales had dropped from a high of $26 billion US dollars in 2000 to around 9 billion in 2009 with no bottom in sight."
**Vinyl's Resurgence (2009)** : By 2009, vinyl records, previously considered dead, were beginning to experience a comeback. "Meanwhile, vinyl was starting to come back."
**Music Industry Disarray (2009)** : The recorded music industry was in a state of "complete disarray" by 2009, characterized by financial losses, layoffs, dropping artists, and legal battles in an attempt to recover its former prosperity. "The recorded music industry was in complete disarray, bleeding money, laying people off, dropping artists, and still trying to litigate their way back to their former glory."
**Technological Disruption in the 2000s** : The first decade of the 21st century was defined by "massive technological disruption" that profoundly impacted the music industry and how music adapted. "The first decade of the 21st century was an era of massive technological disruption."
**Traditional Cultural Gatekeepers (Early 2000s)** : At the turn of the millennium, traditional cultural gatekeepers, primarily record labels, controlled what music reached the public. Labels filtered new music, signed artists deemed financially or artistically viable, and managed marketing and global distribution, creating an "artificial scarcity" that also maintained quality by excluding amateurs. "At the beginning of the 2000s, the traditional cultural gatekeepers ruled."
**Role of Record Labels (Traditional Model)** : Record labels functioned as filters, signing acts they believed would be profitable, then nurturing, marketing, and distributing their music globally, thereby generating revenue. They controlled distribution, leading to a "controlled supply of new music." "Their job was to sort and filter through all the new music being made, sign the acts that they thought could help make them a buck, and then nurture and market and distribute that music to the rest of the planet."
**Traditional Music Industry Ecosystem (Pre-Internet)** : The traditional music industry operated as a symbiotic ecosystem where record labels, radio stations, record stores, music magazines, and music video channels mutually supported each other. These institutions, operating at their own expense, sold and promoted label-offered products, leading to a system that effectively discovered and distributed music to the masses and generated substantial profit for approximately 100 years. "And the result was an ecosystem designed to discover and disseminate music to the masses, all while making oodles of money along the way."
**Internet's Disruption of Music Gatekeepers** : The advent of the internet allowed music fans to bypass traditional cultural gatekeepers (labels, radio, stores, magazines, video channels) through a process called "disintermediation," effectively ending "iron fisted control" over music distribution, pricing, and accessibility. "For the first time ever, any music fan could easily bypass these traditional cultural gatekeepers."
**Compact Disc as a Seed of Industry Disruption** : The compact disc, released in 1982 by Sony (Japan) and Philips (Netherlands), stored music as digital files but lacked copy protection, allowing for "perfectly and infinitely copied" songs, which unknowingly planted the seeds for the eventual decline of the traditional music industry. "The seeds for the death of the traditional music industry had been sewn back in 1982 with the release of the compact disk."
**Rise of Personal Computers and CD-ROMs** : Personal computers became widespread in the 1990s, driven by Microsoft's MS-DOS and Windows operating systems. By 1995, Windows installations moved from floppy disks to CD-ROMs, which are data-containing compact discs, leading to more PCs being equipped with CD-ROM drives. "personal computers had started to take off thanks largely to Microsoft, first with its MS-DOS operating system, and then with Windows."
**CD Ripping and MP3 Sharing** : The combination of CD-ROM drives in personal computers and MP3 compression software enabled users to "rip" digital music files from compact discs, converting them into smaller, more portable MP3s, which computer-literate music fans then began sharing online, often via platforms like IRC. "It wasn't long before software appeared that allowed anyone to take a compact disk, place it into a CD-ROM drive and a typical personal computer, and ripped those digital music files they contained to a much smaller and much more portable size, the MP3."
**Napster's Launch and Impact** : Launched on June 1st, 1999, by Sean Fanning, Napster was a software that offered users free access to "tens and tens of millions of recordings" from their homes, enabling music to "flow like water everywhere" and profoundly impacting music accessibility. "One came June 1st, 1999, when Sean Fannie introduced Napster, a very clever piece of software that allowed anyone anywhere in the world to reach out at any time from the comfort of their own home and find all the music they had ever hoped to have."

**Post-Napster File Sharing Landscape** : After Napster was sued out of existence, numerous other file-sharing programs like Audio Galaxy, Gnutella, LimeWire, Morpheus, Kazaa, and BearShare emerged, ensuring that music became universally available, never out of print, no longer rare, and free of charge. "And while Napster was soon sued out of existence, other file sharing programs popped up."
**Early MP3 Adoption (2001)** : By 2001, millions of people had grown comfortable sharing music using MP3s, and early, albeit "clunky," portable devices were emerging to allow listening to MP3s on the go. "By 2001, millions of people were very comfortable sharing music via MP3s."
**Record Labels' Panic and iTunes Adoption** : Faced with panic over Napster's digital future, record labels felt compelled to accept Steve Jobs' iTunes online music store proposal, as they were in a vulnerable position. "The record labels began to panic."
**Steve Jobs and iTunes' Individual Song Sales Policy** : Steve Jobs, leveraging the record labels' desperation, controversially insisted that all songs on iTunes be sold individually, ending the traditional practice of forcing consumers to purchase entire albums to get a single track. "He demanded that all songs be made available individually."
**iTunes Evolution from Management Software to Store** : Initially, iTunes, introduced for Mac users, served solely as music management software to organize ripped music. It evolved into an online music store in April 2003, with its popularity significantly expanding after becoming available for Windows in late 2003. "It wasn't until April of 2003 that iTunes became an online music store."
**iPod Launch and Impact (2001)** : Introduced on October 23rd, 2001, following the 9/11 events, the iPod became a significant MP3 player and a status symbol, credited with saving Apple from bankruptcy. It sold nearly 400 million units worldwide, revolutionized music portability, allowed for custom playlists and song skipping, and, combined with iTunes, shifted control from record labels and stores to consumers. "That's when Apple introduced the iPod, this little slab of metal glass and plastic."
**iPod Sales Figures** : From its launch until Apple ceased reporting separate sales, nearly 400 million iPod units were sold worldwide, underscoring its massive impact and commercial success. "nearly 400 million units were sold worldwide."
**Disintermediation's Continuing Impact** : As the 2000s progressed, the process of "disintermediation," which involved breaking down the traditional arrangements between cultural gatekeepers and the music industry, continued to integrate the music experience with online activities, following the initial disruptions caused by file sharing, iTunes, and iPods. "this disintermediation, this breaking of the older arrangement between the traditional cultural gatekeepers in the new world just kept going."
**MySpace as a Music Discovery Platform** : Launched in the summer of 2003, MySpace became a significant platform for discovering new music, allowing artists and fans to post music for sampling, and serving as an essential promotional tool for bands who found it easier to maintain than a traditional website. "But for a while, my space was a great place to discover new music."
**MySpace and iTunes vs. Traditional Record Stores** : MySpace and iTunes transformed music consumption by allowing users to listen to music before purchasing, a stark contrast to traditional record stores which typically stocked only about 100,000 titles and offered limited listening opportunities, often forcing consumers to buy music without prior audition. "MySpace and iTunes changed all that. You could listen before you could buy."
**Facebook's Rise and MySpace's Decline** : Facebook, launched on February 4, 2004, rapidly eclipsed MySpace in the social networking arena, marginalizing it despite not being as music-focused, thereby shifting the landscape of online social interaction. "Facebook, which first appeared on February 4, 2004."
**YouTube's Launch and Origin** : YouTube went online on February 14, 2005, initially envisioned as a video posting site. Its musical inspiration stemmed from founder Jawed Karim's frustration over the lack of online clips of Janet Jackson and Justin Timberlake's Super Bowl halftime show wardrobe malfunction (February 1, 2004), and the massive Indian Ocean tsunami at the end of that year, leading him and partners Chad Hurley and Steve Chen to create a searchable video repository. "On February 14, 2005, YouTube went online for the very first time."

**Music Industry Panic and iTunes Dominance** : The recorded music industry experienced panic due to falling CD sales and rampant piracy, compounded by the realization that iTunes had gained excessive control over digital distribution, with Apple holding at least 70% of the online music sales market at one point. "The labels had begun to realize that they had given too much power and control to iTunes when it came to distribution."
**Artists Bypassing Labels** : With the rise of social media and online platforms, artists began to directly connect with fans, bypassing traditional record labels and cultural gatekeepers, fostering stronger fan bonds, gaining global distribution access, and rendering record stores obsolete for downloads, thereby breaking the "yoke of the cultural gatekeepers." "Meanwhile, artists were figuring out ways to go around labels and write to the fans."
**Negative Impacts of Digital Disruption on Artists** : The digital disruption led to plummeting incomes for artists reliant on record sales, the disappearance of the "middle class of artists," increased touring demands to compensate for lost revenue, and rising concert ticket prices, forcing older acts to "unretire" due to shrinking back catalog royalties. "Artists who depended on selling records saw their incomes plummet."
**Decline of Record Stores and Music Overload** : Record stores faced widespread closures due to the collapse of physical product sales, while the internet's unfiltered proliferation of music led to an overwhelming "too much music out there" phenomenon and a sense of "fatigue" among listeners. "Record stores started going out of business."
**Early Resistance to Streaming** : At the dawn of the 21st century, music fans, accustomed to collecting and possessing music, were generally reluctant to embrace streaming due to the perception that it amounted to "renting" music that would vanish upon subscription cancellation. "The knock again streaming was that you just rented it."
**Failed Early Industry Streaming Services** : Industry-backed streaming attempts like Pressplay and MusicNet failed largely due to their complexity, confusing user experience, and inability for labels to cross-license music between competing services, compounded by issues with digital rights management and CD burning rules, ultimately rendering them "doomed from the start." "There were a couple of industry-sponsored disasters in the form of press play and music net."
**Failed Custom CD Service - Music Maker** : Music Maker was a company that raised millions to launch a service offering custom-burnt CDs, where customers could select individual songs from a database to be burned onto a CD, with aspirations of placing these machines in every record store, but the concept ultimately "didn't happen." "You might remember another attempted modernization, a company called Music Maker."
**Rhapsody's Launch and Rebranding** : Rhapsody, the first non-label streaming music service, launched in December 2001 and continues to exist today, having been rebranded as Napster after acquiring the assets of the original file-sharing program. "The first non-label streaming music service was Rhapsody, which went online in December of 2001."
**Spotify's Emergence and Features** : Founded in 2006 and launched online in 2008, Spotify has become the largest streaming music service, offering convenience by allowing immediate sampling of new music, offline storage with paid subscriptions, and a free tier supported by commercials. "The one that has emerged as the biggest, at least so far, is Spotify, which was founded in 2006 and went online in 2008 and slowly rolled out in territories around the planet."
**Inversion of Power in Music Industry** : The power dynamic in the recorded music industry has shifted, with fans now holding all the power and choice, becoming "their own music director" and no longer relying on traditional gatekeepers like radio or record stores. "All the power and all the choice is now in the hands of the fans."
**Tyranny of Endless Choice in Music** : While Napster initially offered an "endless buffet of music," the abundance eventually led to confusion and the "tyranny of endless choice," causing listeners to constantly wonder what they were missing and to "skip, skip, skip, skip, skip" through songs. "It was the tyranny of endless choice."
**Changing Relationship with Music in the Streaming Era** : The shift from purchasing physical albums to streaming has altered the listener's relationship with music; the financial investment in an album once fostered a deeper appreciation and bond with the artist, an obligation that has diminished with the "infinite jukebox" where music is easily accessible for little to no cost, leading to shrunk attention spans, frequent skipping of unfamiliar songs, and a lack of contextualization for artists, songs, or genres, ultimately making music feel more "disposable" and less valued. "The financial relationship we had to that piece of plastic strengthened the bond you had with that artist."
**Loss of Context and Value in Streaming** : In the streaming era, the absence of traditional gatekeepers like radio DJs or record store clerks means there is "no one to give context to the artist, the song, the album, or the genre," contributing to music becoming "organized noise" and perceived as disposable, with its value diminishing as accessibility increases. "Without a radio DJ or a TV VJ, a writer or a trusted record store clerk, there's no one to give context to the artist, the song, the album, or the genre."
**Vinyl's Near Demise (Early 2000s)** : At the beginning of the 21st century, vinyl records were considered "almost stone-cold dead," with pressing plants closing and remaining ones struggling to find parts for old machines, leading to the format being "relegated to the bin of history." "At the beginning of the decade, vinyl was almost stone-cold dead."
**Three Body Problem (Music Theory)** : The "Three Body Problem" in physics is referenced to explain the increased complexity in predicting music trends due to the rise of Rap, Hip Hop, and R&B as powerful genres, alongside Rock and Pop, disrupting the traditional two-genre cycle. "This adds a third element to our music cycle theory, and that makes things more complicated. Just look up a physics principle called the Three Body Problem."
