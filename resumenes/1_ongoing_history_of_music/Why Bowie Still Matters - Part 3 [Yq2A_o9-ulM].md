## albums
**Ziggy Stardust and the Spiders from Mars (1972) – Groundbreaking Album** : This record, released in 1972, is consistently held in higher and higher esteem with each passing year, and is cited as a groundbreaking and influential work in music history. It introduced themes of dystopianism and dread that would be revisited later in his career. "Damn, that's an influential record."
**Earthling (1997) – 21st Studio Album** : Released in February 1997, about a month after his 50th birthday celebration at Madison Square Garden. Bowie was interested in new dance genres like industrial, techno, jungle, and drum and bass, incorporating them into the album. It sparked debate, with some calling it recycled, while others saw it as Bowie's official recognition of these genres, becoming a fine representation of his later work. "Honestly, Earthling is a good record."
**Hours (1999)** : This album was released in 1999 and featured lyrics from Alex Grant, the 20-year-old American winner of a Bowie Net song contest that received 80,000 submissions. The album's release marked a period where Bowie's output began to slow down. "This was the winner, which appeared on Bowie's 1999 album, Hours."
**Heathen (2002) – Return to Form** : Released in June 2002, this album marked Bowie's first collaboration with producer Tony Visconti since 1980, following a 20-year silence between them. It adopted a 70s-era art rock/art pop feel, incorporating dystopianism and dread reminiscent of albums like *Ziggy Stardust*, *Aladdin Sane*, *Diamond Dogs*, and *Station to Station*. The album was deeply affected by the 9/11 attacks, the birth of his daughter Alexandria, and the death of his beloved mother. Critics hailed it as his best work since *Scary Monsters* 22 years prior, reaching number 14 in the US (his best since 1984's *Tonight*), number 9 in Canada, and number 5 in the UK. "The whole thing was a welcome return to form."
**Reality (2003)** : Released in 2003, this album was supported by what would become Bowie's last large-scale road trip. The tour ended early due to his health problems. "There was a tour for his next album, Reality, which appeared in 2003. But it would be his last large-scale road trip."
**The Next Day (2013) – Secret Comeback Album** : Recorded secretly between 2011 and 2012, featuring 29 brand new songs, this album was released on March 8, 2013, two months after the surprise single "Where Are We Now?". Its artwork was a cheeky takeoff of his 1977 *Heroes* album cover. It debuted at number one in the UK and number two in both the US and Canada. Critics considered it his best work since *Scary Monsters* (1980) and it was nominated for two Grammys, preceding Beyoncé as the first of a series of surprise albums released by superstar acts in the 2010s. "The greatest trick Bowie ever pulled was convincing the world he'd retired."
**Heroes (1977)** : This album, released in 1977, had its iconic cover artwork playfully reinterpreted for Bowie's 2013 comeback album, *The Next Day*. "The artwork was a cheeky takeoff of his heroes album from 1977."
**Scary Monsters (and Super Creeps) (1980)** : This album, released in 1980, served as a benchmark for critical acclaim in Bowie's discography, with his 2002 album *Heathen* and 2013 album *The Next Day* both being described as his best work since this record. "Critics called it the best thing he'd done since Scary Monsters 22 years earlier."
**Tonight (1984)** : Released in 1984, this album's US chart performance was surpassed by *Heathen* in 2002, which reached number 14 in the US. "It had number 14 in the US, the best since the tonight album in 1984."
**Blackstar (2016) – Farewell Album** : This album, Bowie's final studio recording, was heavy on themes of mortality and death, and retrospectively seen as his farewell and a parting gift to fans. It incorporated elements of art rock, jazz, and various experimental approaches. Announced in November 2015, it was released on his 69th birthday, January 8, 2016, three months after he learned his cancer was terminal. Following his death, it sold incredibly well, becoming his only album to reach number one on the Billboard 200. "Blackstar became his only album to ever reach number one on the Billboard 200."
**Blackstar (2016) – Easter Eggs** : The album's cover features a large Blackstar on a white background, with a star cut into five pieces below it that spell out "Bowie" upon close inspection. Exposing the vinyl to light produces a star-like reflection, or possibly a bird, while exposing the cover to sunlight causes the star to fade, revealing a starry sky; under black light, it shines like a star at night. The CD reportedly smells like black licorice when first opened. One font used on the back sleeve is called "Terminal" and belongs to a design suite named "Lazarus." The gatefold liner reveals Bowie's image on the right reflected eerily onto a star field on the left, where connecting the biggest stars forms a stick figure, reminiscent of Starman. "There are other things lurking, just waiting to be found."

## songs
**I'm Afraid of Americans (1997)** : This single was a collaboration between David Bowie and Trent Reznor and was featured on Bowie's 1997 album, *Earthling*. The song was part of Bowie's exploration into new genres of dance music during that period. "We touched on this record last time with his collaboration with Trent Rezner on the single I'm Afraid of Americans."
**Telling Lies (1996) – First Downloadable Song** : Released in September 1996, this song was Bowie's first offering available for download, with support from Virgin Records. It was revealed during an online chat where fans could interact in real-time. Three versions of the song were available, and despite a 7-megabyte file size requiring approximately 50 minutes to download on a fast dial-up connection, over 300,000 people downloaded it, exceeding all expectations for uptake. "The song is telling lies. It was revealed during an online chat, still a mind blowing thing back then."
**Slow Burn (2002)** : This song was the first single released from Bowie's 2002 album, *Heathen*, and featured a performance by Pete Townshend of The Who. It marked a significant return to form for Bowie. "Pete Townsend of The Who is on a song called Slow Burn, which was the first single."
**I've Been Waiting for You (2002)** : This track, featuring guitar by Dave Grohl, was released as a Canada-only single from Bowie's 2002 album, *Heathen*, and reached number 11 on the singles charts there. Its inclusion contributed to the album's critical acclaim as a welcome return to Bowie's earlier style. "Dave Grohl plays guitar on this Canada-only single entitled, I've been waiting for you, which reached number 11 on the singles charts."
**Little Fat Man (2007) – Improvised Song** : This song was improvised by Bowie during a May 19, 2007, performance at Madison Square Garden, where he unexpectedly joined his friend Ricky Gervais on stage. The song, which humorously humiliated Gervais's character in the series *Extras*, marked Bowie's final appearance singing on stage. "For this Madison Square Garden's appearance, he just walked out on stage unannounced and started singing before introducing Ricky Gervais."
**Where Are We Now? (2013) – Surprise Comeback Single** : This song casually appeared online on January 8, 2013, coinciding with Bowie's 66th birthday, and immediately caused a global sensation as the world realized Bowie was making a comeback. Its release preceded the album *The Next Day* by two months, signaling his return in "fine, fine form." "A song called Where They Now Casually Appeared Online. And the whole world screamed at once. What? Bowie is back?"
**The Man Who Sold the World (2016)** : This song was performed by Tony Visconti and Spiders from Mars drummer Woody Woodmansey as the band Holy Holy at a club in New York on January 8, 2016, Bowie's 69th birthday and the release day of *Blackstar*. Bowie was reportedly scheduled to appear but canceled at the last minute due to health, instead taking a phone call from the stage while the crowd sang "Happy Birthday" to him. "Tony Visconti and Spiders from Mars drummer Woody Woodman Z performed the man who sold the world at a club in New York under the name Holy Holy."

## curiosities
**Auto-didact and Explorer** : Bowie was described as an old-school explorer who sailed to distant lands and brought back treasures, and a great auto-didact. He absorbed everything—music, art, painting, books, film, fashion, technology—and spun it together for his audience, always looking cool while doing it. "He was a great auto-didect. He absorbed everything, music, art, painting, books, film, fashion, technology, and then spun it together for us."
**Early Internet Adoption** : Bowie became interested in the internet around 1996-1997, realizing it would change everything, including music, long before it was mainstream. He had been using email since the late 1980s, before the public internet was widely available. He prophetically described the internet in a 1999 BBC interview as an "alien life form" that would establish "total fragmentation" and crush existing ideas of media through the interplay between users and providers. "No, it's an alien life form."
**Online Chat Stunt (1996)** : During an online chat in September 1996 for the release of "Telling Lies," Bowie engaged in a playful deception where three people pretended to be him. Fans were asked to vote on the real Bowie, and according to one account, the actual Bowie finished third. "At the end of it, fans were asked to vote on which of the three Bowies was the real one."
**Bowie Net (1997-2006) – Pioneering ISP** : Over a couple of years, Bowie and a small team created Bowie Net, launched around 1997-1998, which functioned as an internet service provider. Subscribers paid $20 a month for internet access and a @DavidBowie.com email address, or $6 for access to exclusive content like songs, music videos, chat rooms, images of his paintings, and journal writings. Bowie was known to lurk anonymously in chat rooms. In 1999, it held a song contest with 80,000 lyric submissions, won by Alex Grant whose lyrics appeared on the *Hours* album. It featured LiveAndWell.com for live tracks and Bowie World, a 3D virtual environment where users could navigate a Bowie-themed city as avatars and communicate with other fans. At its peak in 1999, Bowie Net had 100,000 customers but ceased operations in 2006 as larger companies copied his innovations. "Bowie Net was incredibly ahead of its time."
**Bowie Bonds (1997) – Financial Innovation** : In 1997, Bowie, with banker David Pullman, pioneered "Bowie Bonds," a financial instrument where he securitized his future music royalties as collateral to issue bonds. He sold these bonds, raising $55 million, which he then used to repurchase the rights to his music that he had lost through previous bad management deals with Tony DeFries. This marked the first time a performer used their cash flow potential in this manner. Issued a few years before widespread music piracy, the bonds initially had a high A3 rating and paid 7.9% interest over 10 years, attracting Prudential Insurance. However, within 18 months, the music industry tanked due to Napster and declining physical sales, causing the bonds' rating to drop to BAA3 (near junk status). In 2007, the bonds were liquidated, and all music rights reverted to Bowie, meaning by his death in 2016, he owned almost everything he had produced. "This was the first time in financial history that a performer had used their cash flow potential to make money."
**Heart Attack and Withdrawal (2004)** : In June 2004, while performing at the Hurricane Festival in Germany, Bowie suffered a heart attack caused by a minor coronary artery occlusion, initially mistaken for a pinched nerve. He underwent an emergency angioplasty and, for the first time in his career, canceled a major tour due to health issues. Following his recovery, he largely withdrew from public life. "It was a heart attack right there on stage, caused by a minor coronary artery occlusion, a blocked artery."
**New York City Residence** : After his 2004 heart attack, Bowie lived a reclusive life in a luxurious penthouse at 285 Lafayette Street in New York City's Soho Nolita area. The 5,100-square-foot residence featured direct elevator access, 11-foot ceilings, a large library, four bedrooms, four bathrooms, and three terraces. He occasionally went out, sometimes with his wife and daughter, and enjoyed Washington Square Park. He used disguises like sunglasses, a hat, and carrying a Greek language newspaper to deter recognition. "As you might expect, it was a lovely place, filled with fine, expensive art, tastefully decorated, and with an interior crafted by architects and designers from Europe."
**Secret Comeback (2011-2013)** : Bowie orchestrated his 2013 comeback album, *The Next Day*, in absolute secrecy. He worked with trusted producer Tony Visconti and a small coterie of musicians, all of whom signed non-disclosure agreements. His family and the skeleton staff at The Magic Shop studio (near his apartment) kept the secret. He had minimal staff, only his fiercely loyal personal assistant Coco Schwab and business person Bill Zysblat, with no pressure from his record label, Sony. His understanding of the internet meant he knew he could announce his return without a traditional marketing campaign, simply by dropping a hint online. "How did Bowie, one of the most famous musicians in the world that ever seen, manage that? He was working with Tony Visconti again. Tony can keep a secret."
**Liver Cancer Diagnosis (2014)** : Sometime in 2014, Bowie was diagnosed with liver cancer, likely a consequence of his alcoholic phase in the 1970s. He kept this diagnosis secret from all but his closest circle, and knowing his time was limited, he intensified his creative output. "Sometime in 2014 Bowie was diagnosed with liver cancer, probably stemming from all the abuse it took when he was in his alcoholic face back in the 1970s."
**Lazarus Musical (2015)** : Bowie wrote a musical titled *Lazarus*, and he attended its Broadway premiere on December 7, 2015, which marked his final public appearance. At this point, he was already planning a sequel to the musical. "In between was the Broadway premiere for the Lazarus musical, which he attended on December the 7th. And this would be the last time he would be seen in public."
**Death and Posthumous Arrangements (2016)** : David Bowie died peacefully in his sleep at his New York home on Sunday, January 10, 2016, at the age of 69, after an 18-month battle with cancer, having released his final album *Blackstar* just two days prior on his birthday. His death was confirmed by his publicist and son, Duncan Jones, leading to a massive global outpouring of tributes, including 20,000 tweets a minute within an hour. As per his instructions, there was no funeral; he was cremated, and his ashes were taken to Bali for Buddhist rituals, though the exact location where they were spread (possibly Alma Pura or the Sidemen fields, places he loved) remains unknown. "He hung on until this last album was released and his birthday. And then after that, his job was done."
**Estate and Will** : At the time of his death, Bowie's estate was valued at approximately $100 million. Most of this was left to his wife and two children, with specific bequests of $2 million to his faithful personal assistant Coco Schwab and $1 million to his friend Mary Oncins, who had been his son Duncan's nanny. "His estate at the time of his death was worth about $100 million."
**Unwritten Autobiography** : Bowie had promised to write his autobiography one day, but he sadly ran out of time before he could fulfill this promise. "One of the saddest things is that Bowie never got around to writing his autobiography."
**Profound Influence** : David Bowie is considered one of the most influential musicians ever, arguably influencing more musical genres than even The Beatles due to his constant reinvention. His artistic DNA can be found across glam, punk, art rock, folk, hard rock, grunge, industrial music, electronic music, indie rock, and hip-hop. He brought an unprecedented level of sophistication to rock music, and his work continues to be studied, finding new fans across generations. "His DNA can be found in glam, punk, art rock folk, hard rock, grunge, industrial music, electronic music of all flavors, indie rock, and hip-hop."
**Role in Fall of Berlin Wall** : The German Federal Foreign Office publicly thanked Bowie for his role in the eventual fall of the Berlin Wall, acknowledging his cultural impact beyond music. "When he died, the German federal foreign office thanked Bowie for his role in the eventual fall of the Berlin Wall."
**Numerous Honors and Legacy** : Bowie is enshrined in the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame, the Songwriter's Hall of Fame, and the Science Fiction and Fantasy Hall of Fame. The BBC once named him the best-dressed Briton in history. A species of spider, an asteroid (342843 David Bowie), and British postal stamps have been named in his honor. A statue of him stands in Ailsbury, UK, where he first introduced Ziggy Stardust, and a street in Paris bears his name. "I think I could go on, but I probably have made my point."

# curiosity

**Early Influential Figures** : Beyond religious figures and scientists, music has its own highly influential figures such as Elvis, Chuck Berry, The Beatles, Dylan, Marley, Hendrix, Prince, and David Bowie, who profoundly shaped the evolution of life on Earth. "Elvis, Chuck Berry, The Beatles, Dylan, Marley, Hendrix, Prince, and David Bowie."
**Early Email and Internet Access (1980s)** : Before the public internet became widespread, email was already in use, with David Bowie engaging with it as early as the late 1980s. This early exposure informed his understanding of the internet's future potential. "He'd been going online since the late 1980s. That's when he first started using email and that's before the public Internet opened up."
**Slow Dial-up Downloads (1990s)** : In the mid-1990s, the online world was characterized by slow dial-up modems, with a 7-megabyte song like Bowie's "Telling Lies" taking approximately 50 minutes to download even with a fast connection, making it a "mind blowing thing" to interact with artists online. "If you had a fast dial-up, you could expect to have this song in your computer in about 50 minutes."
**Post-Bowie Bonds Catalog Acquisition Trend** : Modern music business is dominated by well-capitalized companies like Roundhill, Primary Wave, and Concord, which permanently buy up the catalogs and publishing rights of major artists for hundreds of millions or even billions of dollars, a long-play strategy that Bowie foresaw with his Bowie Bonds in 1997. "Today, the biggest deals in the music business involve well capitalized companies who are buying up the catalogs and publishing rights of major artists."
**Riskiness of Bowie Bonds** : While Bowie Bonds were an innovative financial instrument, the practice is no longer used today because they proved to be too risky, especially after the music industry was ravaged by piracy and declining physical sales shortly after their issuance. "The practice of Bowie bonds is no longer used because they're too risky."
**The Magic Shop Studio** : The Magic Shop was a studio located literally steps from David Bowie's New York apartment, where he secretly recorded his 2013 comeback album *The Next Day* with a skeleton staff to maintain absolute secrecy. "No hangers on, no unnecessary people in the building at the magic shop. Name of the studio, which was literally steps from his apartment."
**Surprise Album Release Trend (2010s)** : David Bowie's 2013 album, *The Next Day*, which was released without prior announcement, was the first in a series of "surprise albums" by superstar acts in the 2010s, predating Beyoncé's well-known use of the strategy. "It was the first of a series of surprise albums recorded and released by superstar acts in the 2010s."
**SpongeBob Squarepants Musical Contribution** : In his final creative burst before his death, David Bowie wrote songs for a Broadway musical based on SpongeBob Squarepants, a choice that seemed "a very Bowie thing to do" given his eclectic nature. "There were songs for a Broadway musical based on SpongeBob Squarepants, which somehow seems like a very Bowie thing to do."
**BBC and ITV Reporting of Bowie's Death (2016)** : Within moments of David Bowie's death on January 10, 2016, major news outlets like the BBC and ITV in Britain broke the news, confirming his passing at age 69 after an 18-month battle with cancer and noting his profound influence on music, fashion, and acting. "It's been confirmed that David Bowie, singer and musician, has died at the age of 69 this morning."
**Bowie Shrines (2016)** : Following David Bowie's death, the building on Lafayette Street where he lived in New York became a shrine, as did a mural of him in Brixton, his birthplace. Other tributes and shrines also appeared in cities such as Berlin and Los Angeles. "The building on Lafayette Street where he lived became a shrine."
