# curiosity

**Canadian Music Industry Pre-1971** : Prior to 1971, Canada's music industry was considered a "backwater," lacking the infrastructure of a "first world industry." Major labels maintained only branch offices, and aspiring artists typically had to move to the United States to achieve significant success. "Before 1971, there really wasn't much of a Canadian music industry. Canada was basically a backwater, a place where the big labels had branch offices."
**Canadian Content Laws (January 1971)** : Introduced in January 1971, these laws mandated that Canadian radio stations allocate 30% of their playlists to Canadian artists. This created an artificial demand for domestic music, which, despite initial opposition, stimulated the growth of a modern Canadian music industry with expanded infrastructure, allowing Canadian artists to build successful careers within the country. "But then came the Canadian content laws in January 1971, and overnight it became law that Canadian radio stations had to devote 30% of their playlists to Canadian artists."
**Canadian Music Industry Maturation (1971-1990s)** : The Canadian music industry underwent a roughly 20-year maturation process, evolving into a world-class entity by the 1990s, at which point Canadian artists were perceived as capable of competing globally. "It took about 20 years for our music industry to mature into something truly world class. And by the time we got to the 1990s, there was this sense that our best could compete with anyone or anything in the world."
