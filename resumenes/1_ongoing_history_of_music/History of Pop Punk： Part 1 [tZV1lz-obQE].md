# artist - The Ramones

## genres
Punk
Pop

## curiosities
**Image and Sound** : The Ramones were known for their quintessential punk image, characterized by leather jackets and torn jeans, and for playing music that was really fast and really loud. However, beneath this surface, their musical core was deeply influenced by the pop radio of the 1960s, essentially playing their unique version of pop songs. "The pop radio of the 1960s was a huge influence of the Ramones."
**Openness to Pop Influences** : Unlike some early punk bands that professed disdain for previous generations, The Ramones were open about their pop sensibilities and expressed no issues with iconic popular acts like The Beatles, The Beach Boys, Motown, or 60s girl groups. They frequently covered 60s pop songs, overtly demonstrating their appreciation for the genre. "The Ramones definitely a punk band, but under the surface, they were also very much a pop band."

## songs
**Needles and Pins** : A 60s pop song covered by The Ramones, originally co-written by Sonny Bono of Sonny & Cher, showcasing the band's appreciation for classic pop material. "Needles and Pins, which was co-written by Sunny Bono, a Sunny In Share."

**California Sun (1960)** : Originally a 1960 R&B song by Joe Jones, this track was covered by The Ramones, highlighting their eclectic taste in pre-punk popular music. "California Sun, a 1960 song by an R&B singer named Joe Jones."
**Do You Want to Dance (1958)** : A 1958 hit by Bobby Freeman that The Ramones included in their repertoire of covers, demonstrating their affinity for early rock and roll and pop. "There was Do You Want A Dance, a 1958 hit by Bobby Freeman."
**Let's Dance (1962)** : A big hit in 1962 by Chris Montez, also covered by The Ramones, reinforcing their deep connection to the pop songs of the late 50s and early 60s. "a song called Let's Dance by Chris Montez was a big hit in 1962."
**Baby I Love You (1963)** : The Ramones covered this 1963 hit by the great girl group The Ronettes in 1980, explicitly showcasing their enduring connection to and influence from 60s pop music. "It's the Ramones covering the great girl group The Ronets. And there in 1963 hit Baby I Love You."

# genre - Punk Rock

## curiosities
**Numerous Subgenres** : Punk rock encompasses a vast array of subgenres, which are compared to the numerous ways one can prepare shrimp. These varieties include hardcore punk, sky punk, cyber punk, synth punk, anarco punk, cow punk, gypsy punk, Christian punk, Celtic punk, art punk, garage punk, glam punk, crust punk, horror punk, street punk, melodic punk, afro punk, skate punk, chicano punk, folk punk, and troll punk, among others. "Punk rock comes in as many different varieties as shrimp does."
**Original Mission and Context (1970s)** : Born between 1974 and 1976, punk rock was conceived as the adversary and destroyer of the prevalent pop music on AM Top 40 radio. It emerged from challenging urban environments like New York and London, marked by high youth unemployment, with a clear mission "to obliterate that schmultzy, wimpy, sand metal crap" that dominated the airwaves. "When punk was born in the 1970s, it was designed to be the enemy, the destroyer of pop music."
**Original Characteristics** : Early punk bands were typically characterized by their fast, loud performances and often expressed highly political and socially aware themes. They commonly professed a disdain for the preceding generation of bands, although some, like The Sex Pistols and The Ramones, subtly or overtly demonstrated popular music influences. "The original punks were fast and loud and often very political and socially aware. And they professed to hate much about the previous generation of bands."

# genre - Pop Punk

## genres
Alternative Rock

## curiosities
**Worldwide Phenomenon** : In contrast to many niche punk derivatives, pop punk has evolved into a worldwide phenomenon, becoming one of the most popular forms of punk rock of all time and a leading form of alternative rock over the last 25 years. "one of the most popular forms of punk rock of all time."
**Paradoxical Nature** : The term "pop punk" is often seen as a paradoxical juxtaposition or an oxymoron, especially given that punk rock was initially conceived in the 1970s as the direct enemy and destroyer of pop music. "pop punk should be a paradoxical juxtaposition, an oxymoron, just like, well, jumbo shrimp."
**Coexistence of Punk and Pop** : The emergence and success of pop punk demonstrated that the raw energy of punk music and the melodic accessibility of pop could not only coexist but also flourish together, despite the initial ideological opposition between the two genres. "So we're starting to see how punk and pop could easily coexist."
**California Origin and Naming** : In California, punk bands began experimenting with melody, harmonies, and arrangements, adopting a less serious approach to punk rock. This evolving style started gaining coverage in the San Francisco-based publication *Maximum Rock and Roll*, which subsequently began using the term "pop punk" to describe melodic punk bands like Social Distortion and The Vandals. "California bands decided not to be so serious when it came to punk rock after a while."
**1990s Explosion** : Pop punk experienced a significant and widespread surge in popularity throughout the 1990s, marking a major shift in the musical landscape and cementing its status as a major genre. "Pop punk exploded in popularity throughout the 1990s."

**American Invention and Evolution** : Hardcore emerged as an American invention, serving as a direct descendant of the hard, fast, and loud punk music of the 1970s. It sought to amplify these characteristics, making the music even harder, faster, and louder than its predecessor, though bands still occasionally yielded to the temptation to sing "fun songs" amidst the genre's intensity. # genre - Pop Music

## curiosities
**Pop Sensibilities Definition** : Writing with "pop sensibilities" is understood as a descriptive term, not an insult, referring to songs that incorporate clear elements such as melody, defined song structure, and sing-along choruses. Such songs are often short and punchy, typically under four or even three minutes, and commonly constructed in the typical verse-chorus-verse pattern using a 32-bar structure, similar to mainstream pop hits. However, the distinctiveness from mainstream pop lies in the musical arrangements, lyrics, and image, which may be "well left of center for mainstream audiences." "You can write songs with pop elements, melody, song structure, sing along choruses, but still keep your subculture image and appeal."

# curiosity

**AM Top 40 Radio Dominance (1970s)** : Between 1974 and 1976, AM Top 40 radio was the predominant source of music for the vast majority of people, significantly more popular than FM stations that played rock and album cuts. This widespread exposure to what was often perceived as "awful music" on AM radio played a role in fueling the rise of punk. "the vast majority of people got almost all their music from AM top 40 radio."
**Pop Punk Turning Point (1992)** : The year 1992 marked a crucial turning point for pop punk, as the emerging genre began to gain broader attention and wider retention. This was notably spurred by articles in national publications, such as *Spin Magazine*'s "California Dreaming." "In 1992, some kind of a turning point, a year when this emerging pop punk thing started to gain some wider retention."
**Spin Magazine's "California Dreaming" (Summer 1992)** : In the summer of 1992, *Spin Magazine* published an influential article titled "California Dreaming." This piece focused on a number of melodic punk bands, including those signed to the Bay Area indie label Lookout Records such as Green Day, and contributed significantly to the wider recognition of the emerging pop punk scene. "That summer, Spid Magazine published an article called California Dreaming, that mentioned a bunch of these melodic punk bands."
**Maximum Rock and Roll Publication** : This new publication, based out of San Francisco, played a key role in the early identification and naming of pop punk. When writing about melodic punk bands like Social Distortion or The Vandals, *Maximum Rock and Roll* began to frequently use the term "pop punk." "This music started to get coverage in a new publication based out of San Francisco, called Maximum Rock and Roll."
