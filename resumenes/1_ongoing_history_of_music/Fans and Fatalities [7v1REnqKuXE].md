## curiosities
**Elton Mont Speedway Free Festival Incident (1969)** : The Rolling Stones hired the Hell's Angels motorcycle gang for $500 and all the beer they could drink to provide security for their set at the Elton Mont Speedway Free Festival on Saturday, December 6, 1969. While the band was performing "Under My Thumb," an 18-year-old named Meredith Hunter allegedly pointed a gun at the stage. In response, the Hell's Angels stabbed Hunter five times and kicked him to death, an event captured by three cameras filming the concert. This tragic incident, and the subsequent acquittal of biker Alan Pacerro on self-defense grounds (though the case wasn't formally closed until May 2005), is said to have "formally killed the peace and love vibe of the 1960s."

**Cincinnati Concert Tragedy (1979)** : On December 3, 1979, almost exactly ten years after the Elton Mont incident, 11 people were trampled to death at a Who concert in Cincinnati. Seventeen-year-old Bernadette O'Brien rushed into the mosh pit and was crushed. He was in the mosh pit when security pulled him out after noticing something was seriously wrong; he was unconscious but showed no obvious external injuries. An autopsy could not definitively link his death to crowdsurfing or moshing, leading investigators to conclude that "Something just went wrong while he was up front."

# artist - Pearl Jam

## concerts

## curiosities
**Roskilde Festival Tragedy (2000)** : On June 30, 2000, Pearl Jam performed at the Roskilde Festival in Denmark during its second day, following heavy rains that left 100,000 attendees soaked and safety concerns that led other bands like The Verve and Pet Shop Boys to cancel. During Pearl Jam's 11:30 PM set, a crowd surge pushed attendees against a steel barrier down a slight, muddy incline. Vocalist Eddie Vedder repeatedly stopped the band, imploring the crowd to move back as the crush became a "slow-motion avalanche of bodies." Ultimately, 26 people were injured, and 8 bodies were pulled from the pile, with another person dying later in hospital. The victims were from Denmark, Germany, the Netherlands, and Sweden. "Our lives will never be the same, but we know that this is nothing compared to the grief of the families and friends of those involved."
**Band's Devastation and Absolution (2000)** : The tragedy at Roskilde left Pearl Jam devastated, prompting them to consider breaking up permanently. Despite the band's initial anguish, multiple investigations in the following months absolved Pearl Jam of all wrongdoing, negligence, and culpability for the incident. "In the end, though, Pearl Jam was absolved of all wrongdoing, all negligence and all culpability."
**Eddie Vedder's Return to Copenhagen (2007)** : On June 27, 2007, seven years after the Roskilde tragedy, Pearl Jam played at the Forum in Copenhagen. Eddie Vedder addressed the crowd from the stage, speaking about the impossibility of "closure" but the importance of connecting and learning from the loss. He specifically acknowledged the family of Frank Noens, one of the men who died at Roskilde, noting his mother Antoinette, sister Kathy, and friends were in attendance to celebrate his life, as a request from Kathy. "And I said, no, there's no closure. Is that the word in English? There's no closure. There's no end of the street."

# concert - Woodstock 99

## curiosities
**Site Selection and Conditions (1999)** : The 30th-anniversary Woodstock concert, held on July 23-25, 1999, in Rome, New York, took place at an old air force base that had been controversially declared a hazardous waste site. The location featured miles of hot concrete and asphalt under high 30s temperatures, with a distinct lack of trees or shade. Its selection was primarily due to its "defensible perimeter" to prevent gate crashers, despite the unsuitable environmental conditions. "Why was the show held there in the first place? Because air force bases tend to have a defensible perimeter. No gate crashes."
**Organizational Failures and Exploitation (1999)** : The festival was plagued by organizational failures from the start, including high ticket prices, hours-long waits for water fountains, and overflowing portable toilets, with some hired security staff walking off the job due to unbearable conditions. Attendees were forbidden from bringing their own food, and once inside, faced exorbitant prices, such as $4 for a bottle of water and $15 for a bag of ice. Organizers explicitly refused vendors permission to lower prices, stating it "cut into our profits." "You couldn't bring in your own food. And when you got on site, you found that a bottle of water was four bucks. And if you wanted a bag of ice, it was fifteen bucks."
**Escalation to Violence and Arson (1999)** : The extreme frustration among the 200,000 attendees at Woodstock 99 rapidly escalated into hatred and then widespread violence, especially as the weekend progressed. The mob mentality grew, culminating in fires started with candles distributed by a peace group and exacerbated by the Red Hot Chili Peppers' performance. Vendor booths were looted and burned, bonfires made from water bottles, plywood perimeter walls torched, ATMs savaged, merchandise areas raided and burned, and even a speaker tower was set alight and toppled. "The frustration of the 200,000 people in attendance quickly grew into hatred. And the hatred turned into violence."
**Aftermath and Casualties (1999)** : Following the chaos at Woodstock 99, initial reports indicated 6 injuries and 7 arrests. However, investigations revealed at least 5 rapes, primarily occurring in the main stage mosh pit, including one reported gang rape. Subsequently, 39 additional arrests were made for charges ranging from disorderly conduct to sodomy. One attendee, a 31-year-old man from St. Petersburg, Florida, died from heat exhaustion complicated by diabetes. The festival is widely remembered as "one of the worst events of its kind ever." "After all the dust cleared it, Woodstock 99. Only six people were injured and seven were arrested. But at least five rapes were reported, most of which apparently occurred in the mosh pit in front of the main stage."

# concert - Beere Festival (Minsk)

## curiosities
**Worst Rock Concert Tragedy (1999)** : The Beere Festival, a rock concert in Minsk, Belarus, on May 30, 1999, is recorded as the worst rock concert tragedy of all time. Under sunny skies, up to 10,000 people gathered, but a sudden downpour of rain and hail caused hundreds to rush for shelter in a nearby subway station. A person, believed to be one of the young women leading the way in high heels, slipped on the wet concrete stairs, leading to a pile-up where people were trampled and suffocated. The disaster resulted in 50 concert-goers and 3 policemen dying, with 42 of the victims being teenage girls. "This remains the worst rock concert tragedy of all time."

**Suicide Bombings (2003)** : The Krila Festival, an annual event held on July 4, 2003, at Toshino airfield north of Moscow with an attendance of around 20,000 people, was targeted by suicide bombers. Two women detonated explosives belts at separate entrance gates after being denied entry, approximately 15 minutes apart. The attacks resulted in 14 deaths and dozens of injuries. Russian authorities attributed the bombings to Chechen rebels, noting the casualties could have been far worse had the bombers infiltrated the main crowd. "In total, 14 people died, dozens were injured."

# curiosity

**Concert Costs and Insurance Risks** : Staging a concert or a tour is not only expensive but also risky, with liability being a significant concern. The money from a single ticket is divided among the artist, promoter, venue, ticket seller, and an increasing portion goes to insurance companies to cover potential lawsuits from fan injuries or fatalities. Actuarial tables have been rewritten to reflect increased risk, leading to higher insurance premiums that are then passed on to fans through increased ticket prices. "Insurance premiums go up, and these costs are passed along to the fans in the form of increased ticket prices."
**Global Rock Concert Fatalities (1974-2003)** : According to a study, 136 people died at rock concerts around the world between 1974 and 2003, with many more suffering injuries. This highlights the inherent risks associated with large gatherings at music events, despite safety precautions. "According to a study on the subject, 136 people died at rock concerts around the world between 1974 and 2003, and many, many, many others have been injured."
**Front-of-Stage Crush Dynamics** : "Front of stage crush" is a common and dangerous phenomenon at concerts where the crowd surges towards the stage barrier, crushing individuals against the barricade or each other. This often leads to people falling and being trampled. In severe cases, a "crowd collapse" can occur, where a large number of people fall rapidly, often with tragic consequences. "Some fall and are trampled. And in some cases, there's what security people call a crowd collapse, where a lot of people suddenly go down very quickly."
