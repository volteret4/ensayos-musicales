# artist - Lou Reed

## albums
**Metal Machine Music (1975) – 64 Minutes of Guitar Feedback as Contract Retaliation; Later Reissued and Covered** : Lou Reed was contractually obligated to deliver one more album to RCA and wanted out. He set up guitars with unusual tunings in front of amplifiers in his New York City apartment, let them feed back on themselves, and recorded everything on a small 4-track. The resulting double album ran 64 minutes; side four of the vinyl ended in a locked groove that played forever. RCA released it as a counterattack hoping to end his career — it worked for nearly a decade. However, the record was later re-evaluated as an important forerunner of industrial, ambient, drone, and doom music. It has been reissued on CD (2000, Buddha Records) and has sold more than 100,000 copies total. A 10-person German ensemble called Zeichkratzer began performing it live around 2000 and released their own recording of it in 2014, arranged for strings, wind instruments, piano, and accordion. Sonic Youth used samples from it in their own work. "Since its release in 1975, Metal Machine Music has sold more than 100,000 copies."


# artist - David Bowie

## songs
**The Laughing Gnome (1967) – Giant Flop, Then Embarrassing Hit in 1973** : Recorded on January 26, 1967, during a period when Bowie was struggling and desperate for any commercial breakthrough, this novelty song featuring cartoonish gnome voices was released as a single on April 14, 1967, and flopped entirely. After Bowie became a global star, old labels kept reissuing it. A 1973 reissue became a major UK hit — to Bowie's horror and enduring embarrassment. "An embarrassment for all time. Nice little skeleton in David Bowie's closet."


# artist - Paul Young

## curiosities
**Toast (1978) – Novelty B-Side That Became a Hit During the UK Baker's Strike, Launched by Street Band** : Released as the B-side to "Hold On" by Street Band, a straight-ahead UK rock group, "Toast" was a song about the joys of making toast, recorded when the band needed filler. During the Baker's Strike of the late 1970s, someone at the BBC discovered the track and put it on air; it became an unexpected quirky hit, but branded the band as novelty artists, leading to their breakup. One member of Street Band was Paul Young, who went on to have major multi-platinum success as a blue-eyed soul singer in the 1980s. "Street Band, with a song called Toast from 1978. Naturally, they were soon branded as a novelty act and they broke up shortly thereafter."

## genres
Krautrock

## albums
**Rome Remains Rome (1987) – Featured Unauthorized Pope John Paul II Easter Mass Sample** : Solo album by Can's key member Holger Czukay, featuring a track called "Blessed Easter" in which Czukay recorded Pope John Paul II's Easter Mass and Easter address via shortwave radio and set it to music. Sampling the Pope in 1987 was considered extremely controversial. The Pope was credited in the liner notes as "Pope Star Wojtyla and his swing nuns." The sample was completely unauthorized. "Sampling the Pope was considered to be Uber controversial in 1987."


# artist - Jandek

## labels
Corwood Industries

## albums
**Living in a Moon So Blue (1982) – One of 50+ Albums by Houston's Most Mysterious Outsider Artist** : Jandek (believed to be Sterling Richard Smith of Houston, Texas) has been releasing albums since 1978 on his own Corwood Industries label — more than 51 records on vinyl, CD, and DVD — communicating only through an anonymous Houston P.O. box. His music features wacky tunings, stream-of-consciousness lyrics, off-key singing, and unusual chord structures, delivered with absolute earnestness. A documentary, *Jandek on Corwood*, was made about him; he began making live appearances for the first time in 2004, including a sold-out Toronto show. "Since 1978, Jandek has released no fewer than 51 indie albums on his own label called Corwood Industries."


# artist - Nirvana

## songs
**Marigold (1993) – Only Nirvana Song Written and Sung by Dave Grohl; Found on Heart-Shaped Box B-Side** : Dave Grohl originally wrote and recorded this song solo in 1990, a year before joining the band. At Pachyderm Studios in 1993, he appears to have recorded it essentially alone after the others left, playing drums, singing, and likely guitar. It exists only on the CD single for "Heart-Shaped Box." "The only Nirvana song written and sung by Dave Grohl, that's our seventh weird recording."

## curiosities
**Kurt Cobain Possibly Contributed Guitar and Vocals to Hole's "Asking For It" (1993, Rio)** : An unofficial version of Hole's "Asking For It" exists featuring a male voice on background vocals widely attributed to Kurt Cobain. It is believed to have been recorded at Ariola Studios in Rio de Janeiro in January 1993 when Nirvana was there for four days and Courtney Love was traveling with the band. The version did not appear on *Live Through This*. An official outtake from the same Rio sessions, a demo of "Miss World," appears on *My Body the Hand Grenade*. "It's not that big a leap to assume that the version of asking for it was also recorded during the Rio sessions."

# curiosity

**1896 Pornographic Cylinder Recording – Gonorrhea Soliloquy; Creator Jailed in Government Sting** : Among the earliest surviving examples of censored recorded material, three wax cylinders from a collection called "The Stag Party" feature dirty jokes and bawdy verses, sold at the outrageous price of $1.50. One is a parody of Hamlet's "To be or not to be" soliloquy in which the speaker laments a case of gonorrhea, titled "To Pee or Not to Pee." The person who made and distributed these recordings was caught in a government sting operation on June 24, 1896, fined, and jailed. These recordings are among the oldest surviving examples demonstrating that censorship of recorded material has existed since the very beginning of the medium. "The person was caught in a government sting operation on June 24, 1896. He was fined and sent to jail for months."

**IBM 1401 Singing Computer (1964–1971) – Icelandic Engineer Made Machine Sing by Programming Its Memory; Funeral Given on Decommissioning** : Gunnar Jóhannsson, a musician and computer engineer who operated Iceland's first computer (an IBM 1401 data processing unit from 1964), discovered that by programming the machine's memory in specific ways, its strong electromagnetic emissions could be picked up by a nearby radio receiver and translated into notes and melodies, which he then recorded on tape. When the machine was returned in 1971, it was given a funeral at which it "sang" one last time. In 2006, Gunnar's son Jóhann Jóhannsson (an Icelandic avant-garde composer) took these recordings and rearranged them into an album called *IBM 1401: A User's Manual*, described as "hauntingly beautiful." "When the machine was returned in 1971, it was actually given a funeral where it got to sing one last time."

**Nash the Slash – EP Playable at Any Speed (33⅓, 45, or 78 RPM)** : Toronto multi-instrumentalist Nash the Slash (real name possibly Jeff, never confirmed; known for always appearing completely wrapped in surgical bandages) released a 1981 instrumental EP called *Decomposing* in which all five tracks were written, arranged, and recorded to sound coherent at any turntable speed — 33⅓, 45, or 78 RPM. He had been part of Toronto prog-rock band FM in the late 1970s before going solo with film soundtrack work and albums. "This EP is written, arranged, and recorded so that each of the five tracks sounded good no matter what speed you played it on."
