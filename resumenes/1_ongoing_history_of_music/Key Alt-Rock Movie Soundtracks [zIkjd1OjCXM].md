# artist - Bill Haley and the Comets

## songs
**Rock Around the Clock (1955)** : This song was used in the opening titles of the 1955 MGM film *Blackboard Jungle*, marking the first time a rock and roll song was ever featured in a Hollywood feature film. The movie was intended to warn against juvenile delinquency, but the song's inclusion famously led to kids dancing in theaters, sometimes resulting in violence and vandalism. The film was subsequently banned in many cities and throughout the UK. "This was the first time a rock and roll song was ever used in a Hollywood feature film."

## curiosities
**A Clockwork Orange (1972) Score** : Wendy Carlos, a classical musician who had been collaborating with Bob Moog on the development and application of his new modular synthesizers, was hired by director Stanley Kubrick to compose the score for his 1971 futuristic dystopian film, *A Clockwork Orange*. The entirely electronic score, released as a soundtrack in 1972 (with Carlos identifying as Walter Carlos at the time), introduced sounds no one had heard before and became a landmark in the widespread development of electronic music, influencing many rock musicians seeking novel sounds. "No one had ever heard such sounds. It was completely electronic."

# artist - Giorgio Moroder

## labels
Casablanca Records (implied by Donna Summer's I Feel Love)

## curiosities
**Synth Experimentation and Production** : Giorgio Moroder, an Italian musician, was a pioneer in experimenting with new synthesizers as they emerged. His work, particularly his production of Donna Summer's "I Feel Love" in 1977, was highly influential, shaping the sound of disco with robotic, futuristic elements and inspiring countless electronic and technopop bands. Moroder's innovations drew a direct line to groups like Depeche Mode, OMD, and the Human League, and even inspired David Bowie, serving as a gateway to electronic music and Kraftwerk for many fans. "He had an extraordinarily big hit in 1977 with this song."

**Midnight Express Theme: Chase (1978)** : Moroder composed "Chase," the instrumental theme for the 1978 movie *Midnight Express*. This track, released on the film's soundtrack, was highly regarded and won the Academy Award for Best Original Score in 1979, further showcasing Moroder's groundbreaking electronic sound. "It was called Chase."

Movie studios readily adopted this new format, leading to a flood of movie soundtracks and original cast recordings of Broadway shows throughout the late 1940s, 1950s, and into the 1960s. "But all that changed in the summer of 1948, when the 33 and a third RPM LP allowed up to 22 minutes of audio per side."

**Movie Soundtracks as Industry Lifeblood** : At one point in history, movie soundtracks were considered the primary driving force behind the recorded music industry. The introduction of the LP record was partly influenced by movie studios and Broadway producers seeking a superior listening experience, highlighting the intrinsic link between film and record sales. "There was a time when movie soundtracks were the lifeblood of the recorded music industry."

**First Movie Soundtrack Record Release (1938)** : The first movie soundtrack believed to be released as a record was for *Snow White and the Seven Dwarfs* in 1938. However, this early release faced the technical challenge of being divided across multiple 10-inch, 78 RPM records, requiring constant listener interaction every four minutes. "The first movie soundtrack to be released as a record seems to have been snow white in the Seven Dwarfs in 1938."

**Soundtrack Market Dominance (Mid-1960s)** : As late as the mid-1960s, movie soundtracks frequently achieved the highest overall sales in the music industry. This period predated the full dominance of rock and roll albums, with soundtracks being regarded as "serious music for adults," while rock was more associated with youth and 7-inch singles. "Even as late as the middle 1960s, movie soundtracks often did the biggest business overall."

**The Sound of Music Soundtrack Success (1965-1968)** : *The Sound of Music* soundtrack was an immense commercial success, spending 109 weeks in the US Top 10 between May 1, 1965, and July 16, 1967. It was the best-selling album in the UK in 1965, 1966, and 1968, and for years, the Guinness Book of World Records listed it as the best-selling album of all time, with an estimated 20 million copies sold. "It was a top 10 record in the US for 109 weeks between May 1st, 1965 and July 16th, 1967."

**Standard Practice of Soundtrack Releases** : Over time, it became standard industry practice to release a soundtrack album alongside a movie. These albums could consist of either the film's instrumental score, incidental music for various scenes, or a collection of songs used in the movie, which might be original compositions or licensed tracks. "As the years passed, it became standard practice to release a soundtrack album with your movie."

**High-Selling Movie Soundtracks (Sales Figures)** : Several movie soundtracks achieved extraordinary sales figures.
