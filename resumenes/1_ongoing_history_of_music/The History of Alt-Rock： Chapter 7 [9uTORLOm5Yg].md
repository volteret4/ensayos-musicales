## genres

## labels

## curiosities
**Inspiration from Sex Pistols** : The band formed after its members, along with their future manager and label boss, attended the infamous Sex Pistols concert at Lesser Free Trade Hall in Manchester on June 4th, 1976, as part of a tiny audience. This show inspired them to pursue music. "Jordy Vision only came together because they and their future manager and their future label boss were part of a tiny audience who saw the sex pistols play in Manchester earlier in their career. It was the infamous Lesser Free Trade Hall Show, June 4th, 1976."
**Early Rehearsal Technique** : After the inspiring Sex Pistols show, the band members purchased a guitar for 35 pounds. Lacking an amplifier, they devised a method to run a wire through the turntable pickup of a grandmother's old console stereo to conduct their first rehearsal. "After that show, the Jordy Vision guys bought a guitar for 35 pounds. They didn't have enough for an amp, so they figured a way to run a wire through the turntable pickup of grandma's old console stereo. That was how they conducted their first rehearsal."
**Singer's Health and Performance** : Lead singer Ian Curtis's jerky dancing style was attributed to his suffering from drug abuse and epilepsy, leading to paranoid and desperate lyrics that perfectly matched the band's minimalist music. "In the old days, the village shaman would have said that singer Ian Curtis was possessed, judging by his jerky ways of dancing. And in fact, he was possessed. He was suffering from the twin demons of drug abuse and epilepsy."
**Production Style** : With the addition of producer Martin Hannett, described as a "crazy dude," Joy Division was able to carve out a unique niche within the post-punk scene with their dark, cold, and threatening sound. "And with the addition of producer Martin Hannet, who was a crazy dude all on his own, Jordy Vision created a niche all their own in the post punk world."
**Manchester's Influence** : The band's rise in 1978 coincided with severe economic and social troubles in their hometown of Manchester, including closed mills, pollution, crime, high youth unemployment, and a crumbling sewer system during a hot summer, which created an audience for their bleak music. "Jordy Vision was also in the right place at the right time. Manchester, their hometown, was in big trouble. The mills had all shut down. What was left was polluted and full of crime. Young people couldn't find work."
**Record Artwork and Cosmology** : The song "Transmission" was chosen to start the show because it came from a record (implied to be *Unknown Pleasures*) whose artwork featured the radio telescope signature of a dying star, fitting a cosmology analogy presented at the beginning. "part of the reason I chose that song to start the show... is because it came from a record whose artwork featured the radio telescope signature of a dying star."

## songs
**Transmission (1979)** : This song was recorded at the BBC on January 31st, 1979, and was used to open the show, noted for its record artwork that featured the radio telescope signature of a dying star, linking it to a cosmology analogy. "Jordy Vision and a version of Transmission recorded at the BBC on January 31st, 1979."

# genre - Punk Rock

## curiosities
**Musical Big Bang Analogy** : Punk rock in the mid-1970s is likened to a "musical big bang" because the ideas and attitudes it generated spread out in all directions, eventually coalescing into new ideas and attitudes, forming entirely new musical "life forms" while sharing basic elements. "If we look at the punk rock of the middle 1970s, we can think of this as a musical big bang. The ideas and the attitudes it generated spread out in all directions and eventually began to coalesce into new ideas and attitudes."
**North American vs. British Evolution** : While in North America, post-punk sounds were marketed as "new wave" (a less angry, violent, and political cousin), the British punk scene burned intensely for less than three years, with a growing backlash against original punks by mid-1979 as safety pins and swastikas in fashion became quaint or offensive. "However, things were different on the other side of the Atlantic. The original British punk seemed burned very bright and very loud for less than three years. And by the middle of 1979, the sex pistols had broken up and there was a growing backlash against the other original punks."
**Shift in Message** : Punk's central message of "screw you" was re-evaluated by a new generation of groups witnessing the British Empire's decline. They concluded the punk message was backward, reflecting a sentiment of "we're screwed" instead, feeling that punks, like hippies before them, had failed to achieve their aims. "If punk's central message was screw you, these new groups had a different outlook. They looked at the British Empire crumbling around them and decided that the punk message was wrong. They got it backwards. It wasn't screw you. It was... We're screwed."
**Musical Maturation and Evolution** : As original punks aged and their values shifted, many amateur musicians who entered music through punk became more proficient at their instruments. The simple, snarling lyrics over two or three chords became insufficient, prompting music to evolve beyond its initial form. "Everyone from the punk days was a few years older. These and values had changed. Some of the amateur musicians who got into music because of punk were now pretty good at their instruments. Snarling lyrics over two or three chords just didn't cut it anymore."
**Inclusivity and DIY Ethos** : A lasting legacy of punk rock was the encouragement that anyone could make music, however they chose, fostering a do-it-yourself (DIY) aesthetic that allowed for diverse sounds beyond sounding like specific punk bands. "This idea that if you wanted to make music, then you should be able to make the music you want, however you choose to make it."
**End of Original Form** : By the summer of 1979, punk, in its initial mid-decade form, was considered "over," necessitating evolution for any remaining groups to survive. "By the summer of 1979, punk was over, in its original mid-decade form anyway. If the remaining groups wanted to survive, they needed to evolve."

Everybody was going on strike and youth employment remained very, very high."

# instrument - Synthesizer

## curiosities
**Post-Punk Development** : The widespread introduction of the cheap and easy-to-program synthesizer was one of the most significant musical developments in the post-punk era, making electronic music production more accessible. "One of the most important musical developments in the post-punk world was the widespread introduction of the cheap and easy to program synthesizer."
**Infinite Sonic Possibilities** : Synthesizers offered the theoretical ability to generate an infinite number of completely new and artificial sounds that had never before been heard by human ears, making experimentation a key attraction. "First of all, here was a device that, theoretically anyway, could generate an infinite number of brand new sounds. And because these sounds were totally artificial, you could make new ones that had never ever been heard by human ears ever."
**Low Barrier to Entry** : A major appeal of the synthesizer was that it required minimal musical skill to play; musicians primarily needed to learn how to manipulate knobs and keys to create pleasing sounds. "Secondly, and this is the big one, you didn't need a lot of musical skill to play a keyboard synthesizer. You just had to learn how to get it to squawk those cool sounds in a pleasing way."
**Practical Advantages over Guitars** : Synthesizers offered practical benefits such as no strings to break or go out of tune, and no heavy amplifiers to transport, making them an appealing and radical alternative to traditional guitar-based music for novice musicians. "No strings to break. No strings to go out of tune. No heavy amplifiers to haul around. And if it was now okay for anyone to get up and thrash about with a guitar, wouldn't even be more radical to thrash away without one?"
**Empowering Novice Musicians** : Synthesizers allowed novice musicians to create professional-sounding music, and with multiple synthesizers and minimal coordination, an individual could produce as much music as an entire band, even in a private bedroom setting. "Suddenly, the novice musician could make music like a pro. And if he had one or two other synthesizers and just a little coordination, you could make as much music as a whole band, even in the privacy of your own bedroom."
**Technological Advancement and Affordability** : As the 1980s began, synthesizers continued to become more powerful and cheaper, with new models regularly entering the market offering enhanced sonic possibilities to musicians. "Synthesizers continued to become more powerful and also cheaper. And every few months, a new keyboard came on the market, which offered the musician more sonic possibilities."
**Musicians' Union Opposition** : On May 24, 1982, the 48th birthday of Dr. Robert Moog (inventor of the portable keyboard synthesizer), a chapter of the British Musicians Union filed a motion to ban all synthesizers from recording sessions and live performances, fearing they would displace human musicians. "And coincidentally, this motion was filed on May 24, 1982, the 48th birthday of Dr. Robert Mogg, the inventor of the portable keyboard synthesizer."
