## songs

## curiosities
**Cult Artist Status** : During his lifetime, Nick Drake was regarded as a cult artist, with limited commercial success. His work gained significant posthumous recognition, largely propelled by its use in advertising.
"Nick was a cult artist at best."
**Post-VW Ad Sales Boom** : Following its debut in a Volkswagen commercial in 2000, sales of Nick Drake's music dramatically increased. Sales went up 500% initially, and then, after the record label added "as featured in the VW ad" stickers to CDs, sales jumped from 6,000 copies a year to 74,000 copies annually in the United States alone.
"As soon as the spot debuted, sales of Nick Drake's music exploded."
**Record Label Marketing after VW Ad** : After witnessing the significant increase in sales following the Volkswagen commercial, Nick Drake's record label responded by adding stickers to his CDs. These stickers read "as featured in the VW ad" to capitalize on the newfound exposure and consumer interest.
"They put a little sticker on the CDs that read, as featured in the VW ad."
**Subsequent Licensing of "Pink Moon"** : After the success in the Volkswagen commercial, the song "Pink Moon" was subsequently licensed for use in other projects, including various TV shows and movies, generating substantial income.
"The song was then licensed to other projects, including TV shows and movies."

# artist - The Apples in Stereo

## members
Robert Schneider
Hillary Sydney

## genres
Indie
Punk Rock

## curiosities
**Early Financial Struggles** : As an indie band from Denver active since 1992, The Apples in Stereo were highly regarded within the indie community but struggled with album sales, typically moving only about 20,000 copies per record. To make ends meet, they toured frequently, playing around 100 shows a year, with gigs earning between $250 and $2,500. When not on the road, members took regular jobs, and t-shirt sales were minimal, bringing in around $100 per show.
"A record was good for maybe 20,000 copies to supplement things."
**Financial Crisis of 1999** : In the summer of 1999, the band's principals, Robert Schneider and Hillary Sydney (both 30), faced a severe financial bind. Both were working telemarketing jobs, and Hillary was eight months pregnant with their first child, which would prevent them from touring—a vital income source. They were concerned about affording their $825 monthly rent.
"In the summer of 1999, they faced a situation that is all too familiar to indie bands."
**Sony TV Ad Offer** : During their financial crisis in 1999, Robert Schneider and Hillary Sydney received a call from a friend at the ad agency Young and Rubikam in New York. The agency was working on a campaign for Sony televisions and wanted to use their song "Strawberry Fire," offering $18,000 for the license.
"They want to use your song Strawberry Fire in a TV commercial."
**Ethical Dilemma over Licensing** : The $18,000 offer for "Strawberry Fire" created an "existential crisis" for The Apples in Stereo, who had always adhered to the purist ethos of punk rock. They worried about compromising their artistic principles and disappointing fans who expected them to maintain that stance.
"Apples and Stereo had always been committed to the ethos of punk rock."
**Band Decision on Licensing** : Despite their punk rock purist ethos, the $18,000 licensing offer for "Strawberry Fire" was more than the band could make in a year. When Robert and Hillary consulted the rest of the band, there was no hesitation, and the unanimous decision was to accept the money.
"Robert and Hillary asked the rest of the band."
**Financial Rescue via Licensing** : Accepting the $18,000 deal to license "Strawberry Fire" for a Sony TV commercial ultimately rescued The Apples in Stereo from a very difficult financial situation, proving to be a pivotal moment for the indie band.
"And that one deal basically rescued a very good indie band from a very tough financial situation."

## songs
**Strawberry Fire** : This song was licensed for $18,000 to Young and Rubikam for a Sony television commercial in the summer of 1999, providing a crucial financial lifeline to the band during a period of severe economic hardship. The decision to license the song led to an "existential crisis" for the band, given their punk rock purist ethos.
"They want to use your song Strawberry Fire in a TV commercial."

# curiosity

**Selling Out Definition 1 - Major Label Signing** : This definition describes an artist as "selling out" when they sign with a major multinational music corporation after previously being cool and independent. The perception is that they become "part of the machine" and will be transformed into something bland for the mainstream.
"A band sells out when they signed to a major label."
**Selling Out Definition 2 - Pursuit of Riches** : This definition states that a band becomes a sell-out when their music is primarily influenced by their pursuit of wealth, implying they will do "whatever it takes" to maximize their earnings, regardless of the artistic consequences.
"A band becomes a sell-out when they allow their music to be influenced by their pursuit of riches."
**Selling Out Definition 3 - Becoming Too Popular** : This definition considers a band a "sell-out" if they become excessively popular for some fans' comfort, selling too many records and concert tickets or appearing on too many magazine covers. Fans may feel their band has been "stolen" by new, trendy followers.
"A band is a sell-out when they become too popular for the comfort level of some fans."
**Selling Out Definition 4 - Music for Product Advertising** : This definition, considered "dodgiest" by some, labels a band as having "sold out" when their music is used to sell a product unrelated to music itself, such as cars, candy, or TVs. Even usage in a scene in a mainstream TV network show can be viewed with "derision" by some, dismissed as "filthy lucre."
"A band has sold out when they allow their music to be used to sell a product unrelated to the music itself."
**Volkswagen "Milky Way" Ad Turning Point** : The year 2000 marked a turning point in music licensing when the Boston-based agency Arnold World Wide created the "Milky Way" TV spot for Volkswagen's new cabriolet model, targeting 20-somethings who perceived VWs as "cooler" and "more underground." The romantic one-minute ad, featuring four hip young people driving in the countryside, sought a unique musical backdrop after testing songs from The Church, The Cure, and Tones on Tail, ultimately stumbling upon Nick Drake's obscure "Pink Moon." The choice was partly driven by budget and the desire for "indiness."
"The turning point came in 2000 when an agency in Boston called Arnold World Wide was tasked by a client to help them reach 20-somethings."
**Volkswagen "Milky Way" Ad Impact** : The Volkswagen ad featuring "Pink Moon" debuted in 2000, causing sales of Volkswagen cabriolets to jump, achieving its primary mission. Simultaneously, the commercial acted as a powerful sales driver for the song itself, demonstrating how a commercial could sell both a product and the music used in it.
"When the Volkswagen ad featuring that song first appeared on TV in 2000, a couple of things happened."
**Shift in Advertising Strategy** : The immense success of the Volkswagen "Milky Way" ad, particularly its use of an obscure artist, caused a significant shift in the advertising world. Agencies began to favor "cool songs by lesser known artists" in TV commercials, which in turn were "legitimately regarded as taste makers" and boosted sales for both products and, if the artists were alive, their concert tickets and merchandise.
"The ad was so successful and so admired in the advertising world that there was a shift to using cool songs by lesser known artists in TV ads."
**Benefits of Music Licensing in Ads** : Getting paid to have music featured in a TV commercial proved to be highly effective for promotion. This led artists, facing financial pressures like music piracy, to proactively seek out managers to secure licensing deals as a new and vital strategy for income and exposure.
"Turns out the getting paid for allowing your music to be heard in a TV commercial was great for promotion."
**Artists Seeking Licensing Deals** : Faced with the financial strain of issues like music piracy, artists began actively approaching their managers to explore opportunities for getting their songs placed in TV commercials. This marked a shift in mindset, from viewing such placements as "selling out" to seeing them as a necessary and effective promotional and revenue-generating strategy.
"music piracy is killing me right now. What can you do to get my song in a TV commercial?"
**Rise of Music Licensing as a Strategy** : The success of early licensing deals, particularly the Volkswagen "Milky Way" ad, marked the beginning of a completely new strategy for musicians to earn income and gain exposure. This strategy has since "exploded," becoming a common and often crucial source of revenue for artists.
"It was the beginning of a whole new strategy, which is since exploded."
**Peer Licensing by Independent Bands** : Around the same time The Apples in Stereo, a fiercely independent band, made their licensing deal with Sony, similar indie bands they admired, such as Spiritualized and Stereolab, were also making deals, specifically with Volkswagen. This trend highlighted a new form of legitimacy and financial opportunity for independent artists through commercial placements.
"At the same time, apples and stereo agreed to have their song in a commercial for Sony TVs, similar bands that they really, really admired happen to be doing the same thing."
**Emergence of Music Licensing Industry** : Following the success of early music licensing deals in advertising, a new industry emerged dedicated solely to navigating the legal complexities of licensing real music from real artists for commercial use. These companies aimed to connect advertisers with meaningful, non-generic music to help sell goods and services.
"New companies began to appear whose sole purpose was to navigate the legality of licensing real music from real artists for use in advertising."
**Music Licensing Deal Value** : The financial compensation an artist can receive from a licensing deal varies significantly, ranging from a quick $10,000 for some placements to potentially half a million dollars or more for a 20 or 30-second snippet if the advertiser is highly motivated.
"The amount in artists can hope to earn from a licensing deal varies widely."
**Unnamed Song - Exponential Licensing Earnings** : One particular song, despite being a "huge single on its own," earned "exponentially more" through its extensive licensing for various TV commercials, including campaigns for Maxwell House coffee and All-State insurance, demonstrating the lucrative potential of commercial placements beyond standalone release sales.
"It was a huge single on its own, but with the TV commercials, Maxwell House coffee, all-state insurance and a ton more, it made some good point."
**Reasons for Music Licensing** : Artists increasingly pursue music licensing opportunities (for commercials, TV shows, movies, video games) as a crucial replacement for lost revenue. Factors like crashing physical music sales, inadequate income from touring or streaming, and global events such as pandemics have driven musicians to find alternative income streams.
"an artist will pursue something like this in order to find a replacement for revenue that has been wiped out, crashing physical music sales, inadequate income from touring or streaming, a pandemic."
**Music Licensing Bypasses Gatekeepers** : Licensing music provides artists with a direct route to their audience, bypassing traditional industry gatekeepers such as record labels, radio stations, and video channels. This approach allows artists to get paid, gain exposure, and potentially open doors to further opportunities without relying on conventional intermediaries.
"Licensing your music also gives you a way around the traditional gatekeepers of the music industry, record labels, radio, video channels, and so on."
**Benefits of Music Licensing** : Licensing music offers a dual advantage: artists are compensated financially, and their music gains significant exposure by reaching a broad audience through various media. This exposure can lead to positive outcomes for the artist's career, including increased sales and public recognition.
"Licensing your music can take it straight to the people, your paid, and your music gets exposure, and maybe good things happen as a result."
**Widespread Adoption of Licensing Strategy** : Following successful campaigns by artists like Moby and Sting, thousands of other acts were inspired to pursue similar music licensing strategies. This led to a significant increase in demand for licensing deals across the industry.
"Campaigns like this opened the door to thousands of other acts who wanted to try the same thing."
**Licensing Fee Disparity** : The influx of artists seeking licensing deals led to a decrease in prices for many, with the exception of "big stars" and "big songs" like AC/DC's "Thunderstruck," which continued to command substantial fees. Indie bands saw the potential earnings from licensing shrink, though it still offered more money than they would otherwise make.
"The big stars with the big songs, think ACDC's Thunderstruck, still brought in big dollars, but indie bands hoping for a leg up saw the amount of money coming their way shrink."
**Licensing as "Free Money" for Artists** : Despite declining fees for some, licensing is often considered "basically free money" for artists because it provides an income stream and exposure that they wouldn't otherwise obtain, especially in an evolving music economy.
"It's basically free money."
**Music Licensing Process - Pitching** : One method for securing a licensing deal involves artists or their representatives pitching a song to an advertising agency for potential future use in a campaign.
"A song can be pitched to an advertising agency for use in something that might come up in the future."
**Music Licensing Process - Agency Outreach** : Alternatively, music personnel or creative directors at an advertising agency might already be familiar with and like a particular band or song. In such cases, they may proactively reach out to the artist if they identify a suitable placement opportunity.
"Or the music people at the agency, the creative directors, might happen to like a band or a song and reach out to them if they feel that there's a possible placement to be had."
**Role of Music Supervisor** : For projects involving TV, movies, or video games, a music supervisor is the key individual responsible for sourcing and selecting appropriate music. This role is crucial in identifying and securing the right tracks for a given project.
"A music supervisor will be in charge of sourcing music for the project."
**Copyright and Master Ownership in Licensing** : An artist stands to earn "big dollars" from a licensing deal if they own both the copyright to the song (publishing rights) and the master recording. However, these rights are commonly shared with a music publisher and a record company, respectively.
"If a musician owns the copyright and the master for a selected song, that can mean big dollars."
**Revenue Splitting in Licensing Deals** : When an artist licenses a song for third-party use, such as a movie trailer, the revenue generated usually needs to be split. This split occurs between the artist and any other entities, such as music publishers and record companies, who hold ownership stakes in pieces of the song.
"third-party revenue, licensing a song for a movie trailer, for example, has to be split between the artist and whoever else owns pieces of the song."
**Ethical Refusals in Licensing Deals** : Some artists maintain strong ethical boundaries, choosing to refuse licensing offers for certain types of products or campaigns. Examples include products promoting meat, anything related to the military, or beer companies, reflecting personal or moral objections.
"Some artists will refuse to let their music be used for certain things."
**Duration of Licensed Music Snippets** : Unless the licensing deal is for an entire soundtrack album, the agreement typically involves using only a short "snippet" of a song. These snippets commonly range from 15, 20, or 30 seconds in length.
"the deal is usually for a snippet of the song, 15 seconds, 20, 30, and so on."
**Negotiation of Music Snippet Usage** : Once a song is selected for licensing, detailed negotiations occur regarding the specific use of the music snippet. This includes agreeing on how the song will be edited to fit the commercial, the duration of the campaign, and the number of different platforms on which it will appear.
"And then the nature of the snippet has to be agreed upon."
**Finalizing Music Licensing Agreement** : A song is granted permission for use in a project only after all specific conditions of the licensing agreement have been thoroughly negotiated and settled. These conditions include details about editing, campaign length, and platform distribution.
"Only when all of these conditions are settled, does the song have permission to be used in whatever the project is?"
**High Earnings Potential from Licensing** : With careful management and a bit of luck, a single song can generate millions of dollars for an artist through strategic licensing deals across various media, highlighting the significant financial potential of this revenue stream.
"With a little luck and some careful stewardship, a song can bring in millions of dollars for an artist."
**TV Show Theme Song Licensing** : Securing a deal for a song to be used as a TV show's theme is another significant source of revenue for artists. Examples include The Who's "Who Are You" for "CSI," Cake's "Short Skirt/Long Jacket" for "Chuck," and Alabama 3's "Woke Up This Morning" for "The Sopranos."
"Another wonderful source of revenue is if you can convince the producers of a TV show to use your song as the show's theme."
**Apple's Impact on Music Licensing** : Apple has played a crucial role in the evolution of music licensing for commercials, dating back to the early 1980s with the Macintosh. However, its impact became particularly pronounced with the launch of the iPod and later the iPhone, which featured stylish commercials that became highly influential.
"Apple's stylish TV commercials go all the way back to the early 1980s when the Macintosh computer was first unveiled."
**Apple's Silhouette Campaign Creator** : Susan Ellen Sangin, an art director at the agency Chia Day, conceived the iconic idea for Apple's iPod commercials featuring silhouettes of people dancing to music on their devices. This approach eliminated the challenge of finding actors with the "right look" and allowed the silhouette shapes and songs to change across different ads.
"The original campaigns were headed up by Susan Ellen Sangin."
**Apple Commercials as Cost-Effective Exposure** : Apple commercials provided massive exposure for many bands that would have otherwise cost millions of dollars in advertising, an amount unaffordable for most. These 30-second spots functioned as highly effective promotional tools, often doubling as music videos.
"A lot of bands got exposure that would have otherwise cost the millions of dollars."
**Apple's "Placement Only" Compensation Model** : Apple reportedly does not pay artists directly for using their music in commercials; instead, the understanding is that artists benefit solely from the placement itself. The exposure is expected to drive audience interest, prompting viewers to identify and seek out the featured songs and bands.
"Apple doesn't pay these artists. The understanding is that they will benefit just from the placement."
**Benefits of Apple Commercial Placement - Music Video Replacement** : For artists, Apple commercial placements offer a dual benefit: they act as a 30-second advertisement for their music, essentially doubling as a music video. This eliminates the need to spend thousands on producing a video that might not get airplay or be discovered on YouTube.
"What these artists get are two things, a 32nd ad for this music that more or less doubles as a music video."
**First iPod Commercial Song** : The very first song featured in an iPod commercial was "Take California" by the British group Propellerheads, which appeared in the fall of 2001, prior to the launch of the famous silhouette-style campaigns.
"What was the very first band to have one of their songs in an iPod commercial?"
**Trend of Artists Selling Song Catalogs** : Towards the end of the 2010s, a notable trend emerged where musicians began selling their entire life's work – their song catalogs and publishing rights – to large corporations for tens, and in some cases, hundreds of millions of dollars. These "huge deals" became a significant business story in the music industry.
"As the second decade of the 21st century came to an end, an interesting series of business stories began to circulate."
**Financial World's Interest in Song Catalogs** : The financial world has recognized song catalogs as valuable assets, leading to the rise of specialized companies. These entities acquire the rights to great songs, anticipating "nice, regular rates of return" through royalty generation over decades.
"The financial world has discovered that there is money to be made in song catalogs."
**Song Catalog Acquisition Companies** : Approximately a dozen companies, including Hypnosis, Primary Wave, Round Hill, and Iconic Artists, are actively involved in the business of buying song catalogs. Their strategy involves acquiring publishing rights with the belief that these songs will generate consistent royalties for decades.
"There are about a dozen companies in the game at the moment."
**Song Catalog Acquisition Strategy** : Companies acquire song catalogs by purchasing the publishing rights, operating under the belief that by carefully managing these assets over time—potentially decades—the songs will consistently generate royalties and provide a steady rate of return.
"They're buying up the rights to some of the greatest songs ever written, believing that if they take care of them over time, decades are necessary."
**Artist Payouts from Catalog Sales** : When artists sell their song catalogs, they receive a large upfront check. This payout is calculated based on a projection of how much their royalties would earn in the future, even extending beyond their lifetime.
"Meanwhile, the artist gets a big check based on a projection of how much their royalties would earn in the future, even after they're dead."
**Song Catalog Valuation Method** : The price for a song catalog is determined by first calculating the annual royalties an artist's catalog generates. This annual figure is then multiplied by a negotiated factor (e.g., 10X or 15X) to arrive at the final lump-sum payout, potentially reaching $100 million to $150 million for a catalog earning $10 million annually.
"A company determines how much in royalties an artist's song catalog realizes in any given year."
**Tax Benefits of Catalog Sales (US)** : In the United States, selling a song catalog offers significant tax advantages. Regular royalty income is taxed at high personal income rates (40-50%), but taking a large lump sum from a catalog sale is treated as capital gains, which may be taxed at a much lower rate (around 20%).
"In a territory like the United States, regular royalty income is taxed as if it were a salary."
**Reasons for Catalog Sales (Older Artists)** : Older artists, particularly those in their 60s or 70s, sell their catalogs for various reasons: to cash out and enjoy retirement, to address financial needs, concerns about future touring revenue (exacerbated by COVID-19), or a struggle to accept declining record sales in the streaming era.
"Some of them just want to cash out and enjoy their remaining years."
**Declining Record Sales Impact** : The music industry has seen a significant shift where "nobody's buying records" anymore, and the revenue model is "all about streaming." This decline in physical sales is a driving factor for artists, especially older generations, to sell their catalogs.
"Nobody's buying records. It's all about streaming."
**Dominance of Streaming Revenue** : The current music economy is primarily driven by streaming. This shift has diminished traditional record sales as a primary income source, pushing artists to explore new revenue streams like catalog sales.
"It's all about streaming."
**Catalog Sale as Accelerated Future Royalties** : The money artists receive from selling their catalog is essentially an accelerated payout of royalties they would have earned over time anyway. This means they get a large lump sum upfront rather than waiting for smaller, periodic royalty checks.
"The money these artists receive is money that would be coming to them anyway if they were willing or able to wait."
**Catalog Sales for Younger Artists** : Catalog sales are also becoming attractive to younger artists, offering financial freedom despite giving up future royalties on older songs. They can still earn from new music and use the substantial cash influx for philanthropy, non-musical investments, activism, or estate planning, eliminating financial worry about new album success.
"But this can also be attractive for younger artists too."
**Declining Licensing Fees for Indie Artists** : While licensing deals initially offered a significant income boost for indie bands, the widespread adoption of this strategy led to increased competition and a subsequent decrease in the amount of money offered for placements, especially for lesser-known acts.
"The problem was that so many wanted licensing deals and placement opportunities that the prices paid started dropping."
**Working as a Musician in the 21st Century** : Being a musician in the 21st century requires a strong focus on business issues due to the changing economy, which differs significantly from previous eras where selling records and playing gigs sufficed. Multiple revenue sources, including sales, streaming, record labels, patrons, crowdfunding, merchandise, and licensing, are now necessary for survival.
"Artists are very, very focused on business issues these days."
**Art and Commerce Intermingling** : The coexistence of art and commerce has always been a reality, but in the present day, this intermingling is "much more obvious" and visible everywhere. This increased awareness is attributed to both widespread commercial placements and a greater public understanding of how the world operates.
"Art and commerce have always been intermingled."
