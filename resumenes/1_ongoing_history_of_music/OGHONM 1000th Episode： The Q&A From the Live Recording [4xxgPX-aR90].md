**Early Radio Show Production (Analog Era)** : The first 250 episodes of the show involved a labor-intensive analog production process. Voice tracks were recorded on quarter-inch tape, then manually edited with razor blades and grease pencils. Music was laid onto multi-track tape in real time, and the entire production was mixed down to two-track tape for real-time radio playback. "So that's how it was for first 250 shows until we moved out of Brampton and went digital. Yeah, and that will go. It was a lot of work."
**Modern Radio Show Production (Digital Era)** : The production process has dramatically evolved with digital technology. The speaker records voice tracks into Pro Tools at his home studio, uploads them to Dropbox, and a producer edits them on a laptop (even on planes). Music is supplied from extensive hard drives. While the radio version airs on Saturday or Sunday, the podcast version is "telescoped down" to 15-second music clips due to complex copyright and rights management issues that prevent playing full songs. "Now I have a studio at home. I have Pro Tools. I record my voice track into Pro Tools. I uploaded to Dropbox, Rob Downs, and look down loads it. Edit it while watching TV on a laptop."

"If we put out a podcast, well, that'll catapalize our radio. Yeah. So we're not going to do that. **Speaker's Musical Evolution** : The speaker's musical journey began in a small town outside Winnipeg, where he was an "album rockhead" and metal drummer until 1983. Working at KX96 in Brandon, Manitoba, exposed him to an eclectic mix including Aztec Camera, The Jam, and The Clash. Moving to CFNY introduced him to alternative music (The Smiths, Depeche Mode, New Order), leading him to realize, "Where has this music been all my life? Why has this been hidden from me?" He converted from a "Mollethead rockhead to an alternative kid" and never looked back, feeling he was on "a mission from God" to teach the world about great music. "But after about a week of being able to go through the entire library, the thought was, where has this music been all my life? Why has this been hidden from me?"

**Acquisition of CFNY Vinyl Library (1996)** : In 1996, when the CFNY radio station moved from a rundown strip mall in Brampton to downtown Toronto, management decided to discard its extensive vinyl library, believing digital music would soon render it obsolete. The speaker, however, hired a U-Haul truck and "stole" the entire library, preserving records with handwritten notes from various station personalities like Chris Shepherd, Pete and Geeds, James Scott, May Potts, and Scott Turner. "So the staff heard about this. And over the next few weeks leading up to the move, records started disappearing... I hired a writer truck. And I backed up the truck to the radio station. And I took the library home."
**Decline of Cultural Gatekeepers and Consensus** : In the past, five major "cultural gatekeepers" (radio stations, record stores, record labels, music magazines, and video channels) determined popular music, creating scarcity and strong consensus around artists like KISS, who became "very socially important." Today, with streaming, this ubiquity and consensus are gone; even huge artists like Taylor Swift do not achieve the same level of universal recognition as bands of previous eras. "There were five major cultural gatekeepers. There were radio stations, record stores, record labels, music magazines, and I guess after a while video channels. Those were the things that determined the music that we listened to."
**Age-Related Music Consumption Habits** : As people age (into their 40s or 45-46+), their music consumption patterns tend to shift. They either revert to listening to the music of their youth exclusively or begin exploring bands they missed during their formative years (14-24). The experience of having heard "an awful lot of music" makes it harder to achieve the same "high" from new discoveries. "You'll go back to the music of your youth and listen to that over and over again for the rest of your life. Or you will go back to the era of when you were 14 to 24 and you'll start investigating bands that you missed the first time around."

"Don't rely on the algorithm if you're listing, if you're streaming because the algorithm only gives you more of what you've already listened to and you end up being in a bubble and you really, you know, explore beyond that bubble."
