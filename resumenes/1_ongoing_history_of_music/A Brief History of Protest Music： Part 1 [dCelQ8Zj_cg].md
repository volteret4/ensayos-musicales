# artist - Joe Hill

## curiosities
**Joe Hill (1915) – Swedish IWW Songwriter, Coined "Pie in the Sky," Executed by Firing Squad; Father of the Modern Protest Song** : Joe Hill was a Swedish-born itinerant laborer and songwriter for the Industrial Workers of the World (IWW), a socialist union founded in Chicago in 1905 that organized logging, mining, farm, and factory workers. Hill is credited with coining the phrase "Pie in the Sky." He was arrested and charged with the murder of two people in Salt Lake City, subject to a major media trial, found guilty, and executed by firing squad on November 19, 1915. He is considered the father of the modern protest song and has many fans in the rock world, including Tom Morello of Rage Against the Machine, who has organized a memorial in Joe's name, performed his songs, and released songs about him. "You can draw a straight line from Rage Against the Machine right back to the 1910s and Joe Hill."


# artist - Billie Holiday

## songs
**Strange Fruit (1939) – Anti-Lynching Song; Banned by Racists; Sent to Southern Senators Who Refused to Outlaw Lynching** : "Strange Fruit" was a landmark 1939 anti-lynching song that was banned in some places by racists who considered it too incendiary. Activists sent copies to Southern senators who refused to outlaw lynching. It created the template for anti-racism and civil rights protest songs that followed. "Strange Fruit, the 1939 anti-lynching song from Billy Holiday that was banned in some places by racists who considered it to be too incendiary."


# artist - Buffalo Springfield

## songs
**For What It's Worth (1966) – Written About Sunset Strip Riot at Pandora's Box Club; Sampled 36+ Times** : "For What It's Worth" was written about a riot that broke out to protest the closing of a Sunset Strip club called Pandora's Box in 1966. It remains one of the most popular 1960s counterculture songs and has been sampled at least three dozen times by artists including House of Pain, Everlast, Chumbawamba, and Butthole Surfers. It has been covered by everyone from Cher to David Cassidy to Ozzy Osbourne to Rush to Public Enemy to Stevie Nicks. "It remains one of the most popular 60s counterculture songs."


# artist - Green Day

## members
Billy Joe Armstrong

## songs
**American Idiot (2004) – Anti-War, Anti-Bush, Anti-Blind-Nationalism; Triggered by Lynyrd Skynyrd Song; 4 Grammys, 25 Million Copies** : Billy Joe Armstrong wrote "American Idiot" after the US interventions in Iraq and Afghanistan and the extensive cable news coverage of those wars. The fuse was lit when he heard a Lynyrd Skynyrd song called "That's How I Like It," which celebrates Southern country nationalism. He folded in all the other things he was angry about — blind nationalism, big media, the Iraq War — and the result won four Grammys in 2005 and became the title track of an album that has sold around 25 million copies. "His thought was, wait, this guy is singing about how proud he is to be a redneck? Sure, enjoy your truck and your Budweiser, but this blind nationalism is insane."


# artist - Sex Pistols

## songs
**God Save the Queen (1977) – Unprecedented Takedown of the Monarchy; Banned by BBC; Released for Queen's Silver Jubilee** : "God Save the Queen" was released to coincide with Queen Elizabeth's Silver Jubilee and was an unprecedented takedown of the monarchy, immediately banned by the BBC. "Anarchy in the UK" was an equally powerful protest song, indicting the whole British order and name-checking groups like the Irish Republican Army, the Ulster Defense Movement, and the People's Movement for the Liberation of Angola. "You have a song that landed like a bomb."


# artist - The Clash

## members
Joe Strummer

## songs
**Career Opportunities, White Riot, Guns of Brixton (Various) – Rock as Journalism; Covered Capitalism, Class, War, Global Order** : Joe Strummer had the idea that rock music should be a form of journalism and education, and no subject was too big for the Clash. They tackled current events, capitalism, materialism, the state of the UK, the state of the US, and the global order. Their protest catalog included Career Opportunities, I'm So Bored With the USA, The Clampdown, Police and Thieves (recontextualized cover), White Riot, Know Your Rights, Lost in the Supermarket, Guns of Brixton, Washington Bullets, and many more. "They were so powerful and so influential that we still call them the only band that mattered."


# artist - Sham 69

## songs
**If the Kids Are United (1978) – Oi Punk Anthem; Working Class, Uncompromising** : "If the Kids Are United" is a 1978 single from Sham 69, one of the most successful English punk bands of their time. Sham 69 were an Oi band — a punk subculture some called "real punk" — entirely devoted to the working class and refusing to compromise on any level. # artist - Artists United Against Apartheid

## members
Steve Van Zandt
Arthur Baker

## songs
**Sun City (1985) – Anti-Apartheid Protest Song; Premiered at the United Nations; Raised Over $1 Million** : Steve Van Zandt (Bruce Springsteen's guitarist) was disgusted that performers including the Beach Boys, Cher, Rod Stewart, Kenny Rogers, Elton John, and Queen were accepting money to perform at Sun City, a whites-only resort in the Bantustan of Bophuthatswana, South Africa, effectively breaking the international boycott against apartheid. Together with producer Arthur Baker (a favorite of New Order), he created "Sun City" under the name Artists United Against Apartheid. Participants from rock included Bono, Bob Dylan, Peter Gabriel, Ringo Starr, Lou Reed, Bob Geldof, Pat Benatar, Clarence Clemons, Jackson Browne, Keith Richards, Ronnie Wood, Joey Ramone, and Peter Garrett of Midnight Oil. Hip-hop contributors included Run DMC, African Bambaata, DJ Cool Herc, Grandmaster Melle Mel, Curtis Blow, and the Fat Boys. Also contributing were Jimmy Cliff, George Clinton, Herbie Hancock, Bonnie Raitt, Hall and Oates, Gil Scott-Heron, and Miles Davis. The single premiered at the United Nations, raised over a million dollars for anti-apartheid efforts, and was a big hit in Canada, Australia, the Netherlands, and the UK. In America it reached only number 38, with many radio stations refusing to play it due to its criticism of President Reagan's "constructive engagement" policy toward South Africa. "The single premiered at the United Nations and raised over a million dollars for anti-apartheid efforts."


# genre - Protest Music

## curiosities
**Definition of a Protest Song – Phil Ochs: Specific, Oppositional, and Cannot Support the State** : Phil Ochs defined a protest song as one "so specific that you cannot mistake it for BS." Such songs must be in direct opposition to something, offer some sort of alternate solution, and arouse emotion in both performer and listener. Protest music has been heard around the world and in all cultures — not just North America, but Europe, Africa, and Asia — and cannot support the state. "If you like things as they are, what's your problem? There's nothing to protest against."

**Origins of Protest Songs – 1860s African-American Spirituals Through IWW Little Red Songbook** : The earliest Western protest songs include African-American spirituals and work songs protesting slavery, such as "No More Auction Block" dating to the 19th century. By the 1900s, the labor movement generated protest music, with the IWW distributing its Little Red Songbook featuring songs by Joe Hill, many set to popular hymn tunes so they could spread quickly. "Messages of protests were often encoded in work songs, religious chants, and various compositions of lament and woe."

**Civil Rights Song Catalog – Thousands of Tracks; Sam Cooke, Nina Simone, Curtis Mayfield** : The civil rights movement generated a massive catalog of protest music, including "A Change Is Gonna Come" by Sam Cooke (1964), "Mississippi Goddam" by Nina Simone (1964), "People Get Ready" by Curtis Mayfield (1965), and Gil Scott-Heron's "The Revolution Will Not Be Televised" (1971 B-side), considered one of the greatest black liberation songs of all time and an important precursor to rap. "If you were to create a playlist of civil rights songs, it would contain literally thousands of tracks."

**Women's Rights Protest Songs – Leslie Gore, Aretha Franklin, Loretta Lynn (Banned)** : Key protest songs for women's rights include "You Don't Own Me" by Lesley Gore (1963, a woman demanding autonomy), "Respect" by Aretha Franklin (1967, recontextualized into a women's anthem), "I Am Woman" by Helen Reddy (1972), and "The Pill" by Loretta Lynn (1975), which was banned by many radio stations for its discussion of birth control. "A woman demanding autonomy and refusing to be dominated by a man? Radical stuff at the time."

**Punk as Protest – British Punks Against Monarchy, Class System, Unemployment** : When punk exploded in the UK, it was very much "us against them": against the monarchy, the government, the class system, and the bleak economic prospects for young people. Beyond the Sex Pistols, protest punk included Crass (anarchists), Stiff Little Fingers (Northern Ireland and the Troubles), and The Jam (Down in the Tube Station at Midnight, Going Underground). "British punks were against the monarchy, the government, the class system, and their crappy chances at meaningful employment."
