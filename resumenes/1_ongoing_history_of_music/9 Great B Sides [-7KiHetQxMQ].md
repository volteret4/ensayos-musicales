# artist - The Smiths

## songs
**How Soon Is Now (1984) – Started as a Throwaway B-Side; Now Considered a Classic of 1980s Music** : "How Soon Is Now" first appeared buried at the very back of a limited-edition 12-inch single from August 1984 (the A-side was "William, It Was Really Nothing," followed by "Please, Please, Please Let Me Get What I Want"). It was then included on the odds-and-ends collection Hat Full of Hollow (November 1984). It only became a single in February 1985 because English fans were paying high prices for Dutch import copies. When the Smiths signed to Sire Records for the American market, it was issued as an edited single. Today it is considered one of the greatest recordings of the 1980s. "To think it started as a B-side."


# artist - Nirvana

## members
Dave Grohl

## songs
**Marigold (1993) – The Only Nirvana Track Written and Sung by Dave Grohl; B-Side of "Heart-Shaped Box"** : Nirvana released exactly one track written and sung by drummer Dave Grohl: "Marigold," which appeared as a B-side on the CD single for "Heart-Shaped Box" (from the In Utero album, 1993). Grohl had developed considerable skills as a singer and guitarist before joining Nirvana, including time with the Washington DC band Scream. Within Nirvana, Dave essentially did what he was told. "Marigold" remains the only place to hear Grohl with Nirvana in a leading role, predating the Foo Fighters. "Long before there was such a thing as a Foo Fighter, here's Dave Grohl singing an original song with Nirvana."


# artist - Nine Inch Nails

## songs
**"Get Down, Make Love" (1990) – Queen Cover with Disturbing Samples; B-Side of Sin EP (Halo 4)** : The Sin EP (Halo 4, 1990) was the last thing Trent Reznor released before an ugly legal battle with his label kept NIN out of circulation for years. Tracks 1, 2, and 4 were different versions of "Sin" from Pretty Hate Machine. Track 3 was a cover of Queen's "Get Down, Make Love" featuring disturbing samples: a spoken-word bit from what appeared to be an old cable-access show called Video Cycle Therapy, and audio from a Japanese pornographic film from a collection belonging to Al Jourgensen of Ministry — who helped Trent produce that portion and insisted on using the samples. "The dead became something of a showstopper whenever Nine Inch Nails played it live."


# artist - Pearl Jam

## songs
**Yellow Ledbetter (1992) – B-Side of "Jeremy"; Greatest Pearl Jam Non-Album Track; Named After a Real Person** : "Yellow Ledbetter" first appeared on a European CD single for "Jeremy" in 1992, meaning North American fans had to hunt for high-priced imports. A live version appeared on a Japanese CD single for "Daughter." Eddie Vedder mumbles through much of it, so internet lyric transcriptions are rough approximations; Eddie frequently improvises the words live and rarely follows the official lyrics. Some fans consider it the greatest Pearl Jam song ever recorded and it might have been a worldwide hit had it been released as a single. The song is actually named after a real person: Tim Ledbetter, an old friend of Eddie Vedder from Chicago — an inside joke. "There really is someone named Yellow Lead Better. Except that his first name is Tim."


# artist - XTC

## songs
**Dear God (1986) – Dropped from Album; Nearly Destroyed XTC; Then Saved Skylarking Sales** : "Dear God" was written by Andy Partridge for the Skylarking album (produced by Todd Rundgren). Everyone involved — Partridge, Rundgren, and Virgin Records — agreed it should not be picked as the single, with Partridge calling it "an arrogant attempt to sum up man's relationship with God in just 3.5 minutes," and Virgin disliking the whiny child at the opening. It was dropped from the album entirely and relegated to B-side on the 7-inch and 12-inch of "Grass" (August 26, 1986). "Grass" bombed completely. Radio stations flipped the single and played "Dear God" instead; listener response was massive. XTC's American label called England in panic. The album was repressed with the track restored; it sold a quarter million copies. Religious groups protested; a Florida radio station received a bomb threat; an 18-year-old student in Birmingham, New York named Gary Pulles held a knife to a school secretary demanding the song be played over the PA repeatedly. "As a result, the record started to sell."


# artist - U2

## songs
**The Sweetest Thing (1987, re-recorded 1998) – Written as Apology for Missing Ali's Birthday; Royalties to Chernobyl Children's Project** : Bono missed his wife Ali's birthday while immersed in sessions for The Joshua Tree (1986) and attempted to make amends by writing a love song. The band was unimpressed; Bono admits he is not good at simple love songs. The song was relegated to the B-side of the 12-inch of "Where the Streets Have No Name" (one of The Joshua Tree's major singles), where it lay mostly forgotten for over a decade. For the 1998 greatest hits collection, the band revisited it, rewrote it, added chords, changed the vocals, re-recorded it, and released it as a single with a video shot in St. Stephen's Green, Dublin, featuring members of Boyzone at Ali's request plus the Chip and Dale Dancers. All royalties from the song go to the Chernobyl Children's Project, a group that funds trips for children out of the contaminated zone. "Bono threw in the Chip and Dale dancers as a bit of a bonus."


# artist - Oasis

## songs
**Acquiesce (1995) – Possibly the Best Oasis Song Never Released as a Single; Liam and Noel Duet** : "Acquiesce" is tucked at the end of the Cigarettes & Alcohol EP (April 24, 1995). One British magazine rated it the second greatest Oasis song of all time, behind only "Live Forever" — ahead of "Supersonic," "Champagne Supernova," and even "Wonderwall." No one has adequately explained why it was not a proper single. It features a rare Liam and Noel Gallagher duet, with Liam sounding more like John Lennon than ever. The timing would have been perfect: Noel had walked out on the band during a US tour in late 1994, generating tabloid coverage for months, and the lyrical content — "we need each other, we believe in each other" — would have carried enormous biographical resonance. It was later collected on the 1998 B-sides compilation The Master Plan. Hardcore fans maintain that you cannot fully understand Oasis without exploring their B-sides, and Oasis has released over three albums' worth of non-album material. "Hardcore fans maintain that you can't understand Oasis completely if you don't get into their B-sides."


# artist - Radiohead

## songs
**Talk Show Host (1996) – One of Radiohead's Greatest Non-Album Tracks; B-Side of "Street Spirit"** : "Talk Show Host" first appeared on the UK, Dutch, and Australian CD single for "Street Spirit" (January 22, 1996). Canadian fans could only access it as an import. Radiohead has consistently matched the quality of their album tracks on B-sides and EPs, generating approximately three CDs' worth of strong non-album material. "Talk Show Host" is frequently cited by fans as one of the greatest Radiohead tracks regardless of format. The Romeo + Juliet soundtrack featured a remix, but fans are advised to seek out the 1996 original. "You're not getting the full effect unless you explore the B-sides."


# artist - Pixies

## songs
**Wave of Mutilation (UK Surf Mix) (1989) – Not a Remix but a Completely Different Recording; 800 Promo Copies** : The Pixies' standard version of "Wave of Mutilation" appears on Doolittle (1989) — loud, fast, screaming. The UK Surf Mix is a slower, more languid, almost tranquil completely different recording, mislabeled as a remix. It first appeared on a one-sided 12-inch promotional record of only 800 copies, then on the B-side of the "Here Comes Your Man" single, then on the soundtrack to the Christian Slater film Pump Up the Volume. For some fans, it is the superior version. A comprehensive collection of Pixies B-sides is available on The Complete B-Sides (released March 2001). "Depending on your view of the Pixies, you may consider this to be the superior version of Wave of Mutilation."
