While illegal, it has historically occurred, particularly in the US, which is described as the biggest payola market worldwide. "no one pays a radio station to play a song, at least not legally. That's called Peola and such pay for play is illegal."
**State of Canadian Music Industry Pre-1971** : Before January 18, 1971, the Canadian music industry was underdeveloped, largely consisting of remote offices of foreign record companies and tiny, uninfluential domestic labels. Canadian radio was dominated by UK and US artists, making it rare to hear Canadian music and leading to a lack of national musical identity. "Before January 18, 1971, there really wasn't much of a Canadian music industry."
**Movement for Canadian Music Support (mid-1960s)** : Starting in the mid-1960s, a movement emerged within Canada to address the neglected state of its domestic music industry. This movement ultimately led to years of discussions, arguments, and pushback regarding the implementation of content regulations. "starting in the mid 1960s, there was a movement within Canada to do something about our more abandoned music industry."
**Introduction of Canadian Content (Cancon) Rules (January 18, 1971)** : Effective January 18, 1971, Canadian radio stations were mandated to ensure that 30% (three out of every ten) of all songs played were by Canadian artists. This quota system faced initial resistance but became law. "beginning on January 18, 1971, all Canadian radio stations would have to ensure that 30% – three out of every 10 – of all salons they played on their radio station were by Canadian artists."
**Dual Objectives of Cancon** : The Canadian content rules served a dual purpose: a cultural strategy to expose Canadians to music from their own country rather than solely American and British fare, and an industrial strategy to foster a domestic music creation infrastructure including studios, labels, producers, engineers, agents, and promoters. "First, make sure that Canadians got to hear music from other Canadians instead of just a steady diet of American and British music. In that sense, it was a cultural strategy. But this was also an industrial strategy."
**Success and Impact of Cancon** : After about a decade, the Canadian content rules proved highly successful. They stimulated a strong domestic music scene and robust music exports, enabling Canada to produce and export more music globally than a country of its size would typically achieve. "But now, the Canadian system cranks out more music to the rest of the world than a country of our size should otherwise. We have a very strong domestic scene and strong music exports. The Cancon rules have been a great success."
**Current Cancon Quota** : The minimum Canadian content requirement for radio stations today is 35%. Some stations voluntarily exceed this minimum, incorporating quotas of 40% or even 45% into their licenses. "Today, the minimum requirement is 35%. Some stations voluntarily wrote into their licenses a percent higher than that, 40%, or even 45%."
