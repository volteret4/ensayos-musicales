**Smells Like Teen Spirit (1991)** : The CD single for "Smells Like Teen Spirit" was released on August 27, 1991, the same day as Pearl Jam's *Ten* album. Iver Hamilton from Polygram Records brought the CD single to a radio station, where it was first played at 11:38 AM, potentially marking its Canadian debut. The song generated an immediate and overwhelming listener response, leading to repeated airplay throughout the day due to high demand on request lines. It was also played at The Kingdom (also known as Energy) in Burlington, Ontario, on a Thursday night for an alternative dance event, where attendees already knew all the lyrics, demonstrating its stunning immediate impact. Police reported that a man in his 20s was found with a shotgun wound to the head, and a suicide note was nearby. An electrician doing work at the home found the body through a window, leading police to break into a cottage above a detached garage where the discovery was made. Cobain had been recuperating from an overdose of painkillers and champagne the previous month in Rome. # curiosity

**Canadian FM Radio Regulations (Decades prior to 1990s)** : For decades, FM radio in Canada was heavily regulated to maintain its distinctiveness from AM radio. Regulations included a ban on playing the same song more than 18 times a week and a limit of 49% of a playlist dedicated to top 40 records. FM stations were also mandated to meet "stringent talk requirements," categorized into surveillance (weather, traffic), mosaic talk (60+ seconds on a specific topic), news in public affairs, and foreground programming (self-contained shows at least 30 minutes long on specific topics). These foreground programming requirements were eventually "ditched sometime in the 1990s." "For decades, FM Radio in Canada was highly, highly regulated."
**Longest-Running Radio Programs** : Several radio programs have achieved remarkable longevity. The BBC's "Your 100 Best Tunes" aired from November 1959 to January 2007, with Alan Keith hosting for 44 years. CBC's "As It Happens" has run for over 54 years, and "Ideas" and "Cross Country Checkup" for over 58 years. According to Guinness World Records, Switzerland's "Voices of the Italian Grisones" is the longest-running factual weekly radio program at 81 years. "Saturday Children's Class" had an 86-year run. The "Grand Ole Opry" began in 1927 on WSM AM out of Nashville and is still active. The "BBC Shipping Forecast," starting audio broadcast in 1924 (telegraph in 1858), predates the Opry. **Critique of Music Streaming** : Music streaming, while convenient, is described as "organized noise" that lacks context, depth, and background information about songs, artists, albums, scenes, sounds, history, or recording processes. This absence makes music less interesting compared to having rich contextual information. "streaming is magic, but it's really organized noise."
**Generational Music Taste** : Every generation inherently believes the music of their youth is "the greatest music of all time." For individuals aged 14 to 24 in the 1990s, the music from that decade will remain "comfort food" for the rest of their lives, highlighting a universal phenomenon of strong attachment to music heard during formative years. "Every generation has a biological right to believe the music of their youth is the greatest music of all time..."
**Radio as a Source of Real-time Information (Pre-Internet)** : Before the widespread adoption of the internet (prior to 1996), radio was the primary and often sole source for real-time information. Record labels and international contacts, such as Lee Carter in the UK, would feed stations the latest music information weeks before music magazines arrived in Canadian stores. "radio at that time was where everyone went to get real-time information."
**"Explore Music" Radio and TV Program** : "Explore Music" was an offshoot radio and TV program that primarily focused on music interviews. "For a while, there was an offshoot radio and TV program called Explore Music."
**Humpty Dumpty Origin Story** : The nursery rhyme "Humpty Dumpty," first published in 1803, originated as a riddle whose answer is an egg, explaining why Humpty Dumpty is always depicted as such. A story linking the rhyme to the Battle of Colchester in the English Civil War in 1648 was later revealed to be a tourism scam perpetrated by the city of Colchester in 1956. "What we have here is the classic Humpty Dumpty, first published, 1803."
