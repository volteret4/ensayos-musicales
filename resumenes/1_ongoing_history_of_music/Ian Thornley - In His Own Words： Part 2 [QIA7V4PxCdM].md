## curiosities
**Post-Big Wreck Version 1.0 Career Transition** : After Big Wreck's initial disbandment around 2001-2002, Ian Thorneley moved back to Toronto and found work as a session player. He contributed to albums by artists such as Nickelback, Sarah Harmer, and Stephen Fearing. "After big rec version 1.0 broke up, Ian moved back to Toronto where he found work as a session player."
**Early Business Exploitation Experience** : During the period with the band Thorneley, Ian encountered individuals in business who attempted to take advantage of him, leading to a significant phone conversation in 2005 or 2006 where his artistic and personal needs were dismissed. This experience taught him about the harsh realities of the music industry. "And the quote was, I don't give a fuck about your child."
**Protecting Artistic Vision** : Ian Thorneley demonstrated a strong conviction in protecting his artistic output, which he likened to protecting his child. This conviction allowed him to resist manipulation and commercial pressures, distinguishing him from many musicians who might compromise for a living. "Now, what's, what I'm finding interesting is that this is your child, your baby. Yeah. And the conviction that you have in protecting your baby is not something that you see in a lot of musicians..."
**Financial Worries and Motivation** : Despite his success, Ian Thorneley consistently experiences financial worries, especially with family responsibilities. This serves as a significant self-motivating mechanism, extending beyond purely artistic concerns to the practical needs of supporting his family. "I have, I have 11 month old at home, you know, and he's, he eats a lot of food. He eats more than I do."
**Connection with Chad Kroeger** : Ian Thorneley has worked extensively with Chad Kroeger of Nickelback, considering him a close friend and family member. However, he acknowledges that Kroeger's successful working methods differ significantly from his own, leading to external pressures from those trying to replicate Kroeger's success. "But having said that, he, he, I, that I've worked a lot with the guy and he's like, he's like a family member and he's a very close guy and very close friend."
**First Velvet Revolver Consideration (Pre-Weiland)** : Before Scott Weiland joined Velvet Revolver, Ian Thorneley was considered as a potential singer. He received a CD with three instrumental tracks to write over, but he didn't pay much attention to it as his first Thorneley record was about to be released. "I remember getting a CD right before the first Thorneley record came out. I got a CD with three cuts on it, just instrumental tracks."
**Second Velvet Revolver Consideration and Rehearsals (Post-Weiland)** : After Scott Weiland's departure, Ian was again approached by Velvet Revolver. Slash himself called Ian to send over material, and Ian subsequently rehearsed with the band in Los Angeles, experiencing Slash's full touring rig and observing the band members as "real dudes." "The phone call came from slash. I was driving back from a gig or something. Oh, the phone rings and it's slash."
**Reason for Rejecting Velvet Revolver Frontman Role** : Despite the musical chemistry and positive vibe during rehearsals, Ian Thorneley ultimately did not join Velvet Revolver. The band sought a "peacock" frontman in the vein of David Lee Roth or Scott Weiland, a role that didn't align with Ian's preference to play guitar on stage. "I'm not that comfortable doing the whole, you know, front guy tambourine thing. It's just not for me."
**Shared "Guitarist First" Affliction with Miles Kennedy** : Ian Thorneley notes a long-standing connection with Miles Kennedy, lead singer for Slash's solo band and Alter Bridge. They share the characteristic of being "guitar players first and singers second," although Kennedy has since embraced the frontman role without a guitar. "Oddly enough, like Miles, who sings with slash all the time, the, the mild and I go way back and, and we suffer from the same affliction where guitar players first and singers second."
**Anecdotes with Ahmet Ertegun** : During Big Wreck's first iteration, Ian Thorneley had experiences with Ahmet Ertegun, co-founder of Atlantic Records, including a dinner at his office where Ertegun heard Big Wreck's music and shared stories about bands like the Rolling Stones and Led Zeppelin. Ertegun was described as a small Turkish man with a perfectly manicured mustache, often accompanied by "Amazonian gorgeous women." "Did you actually go for dinner with Ahmed Erdogan? Yeah, I mean, I was brought up to his office by Craig Calman."
**Live Wardrobe Malfunction** : During a Thorneley tour show in Edmonton or Calgary, possibly on Halloween, the power repeatedly went out due to running too many amps. While Ian was engaging the audience with jumps and antics, his pants split open in the crotch, revealing his anatomy due to not wearing underwear. His guitar tech, Shayne, attempted to tape him up, and a crew member named Timmy retrieved new pants, allowing him to finish the show. "And during one of these jumps, my pants split open in the crotchal area. And these days I didn't wear underwear."

## curiosities
**First Version Disbandment (2001-2002)** : The first iteration of Big Wreck concluded around 2001 or 2002, with Ian Thorneley subsequently pursuing other musical projects. "But that first version of the band was done by 2002."
**Reformation as Big Wreck Version 2.0** : The band reunited around 2010-2011, initiated by Ian Thorneley missing the connection with former members. A Thorneley tour stop near Sarnia, where Brian Dorody lived, led to Dorody filling in for a show when the existing guitarist was unavailable, sparking the idea for a new Big Wreck era. "And then all of a sudden, there's Big Rec version 2."
**Albatross Album Naming** : The album "Albatross" was named after its titular song, which itself was named when the word "Albatross" simply "fell into" Ian Thorneley's lap during writing, rather than a conscious reference to the Rime of the Ancient Mariner or John Cleese's sketch. The general practice for naming albums is to choose a song title, with "In Loving Memory" and "But for the Sun" being exceptions. "Dude, I just named the album after the song."
**Contrast between Industry 1.0 and 2.0** : Ian Thorneley noted a significant difference in the music industry between Big Wreck's first and second versions. The initial era involved experiences like being wined and dined by label executives such as Ahmet Ertegun, while the later period with SRO Anthem offered more creative freedom without the commercial pressures of chasing hits. "The music industry of Big Rec version 1.0. It was very different from what you had to deal with Big Rec version 2.0."

## albums
**Come Again (2004) – Debut Album** : Released on May 11, 2004, this was the debut album for Ian Thorneley's new band "Thorneley" under 604 Records. It produced a huge single on Canadian radio and earned two Juno nominations, with at least four singles making it to radio. "The debut from this new group was called Come Again and it was released on May 11th, 2004."
**Tiny Pictures (2009) – Second Album** : Released in February 2009, five years after "Come Again," much of the material for this album had been written years prior but delayed due to Ian Thorneley's touring commitments with other bands. Ian played most of the instruments, with songwriting collaborations including David Gen. "This was the second album released under the name Thorneley, and came out five years after the first one, February 2009."

## songs
**Make the Leave** : This song is from the "Tiny Pictures" album and was co-written with David Gen, who has also worked with Matt Good and 5440. "This song, for example, was co-written with David Gen, who worked with both Matt Good and 5440. It's called Make the Leave."

## curiosities
**Formation of the Band** : After the first Big Wreck disbanded, Ian Thorneley moved to Toronto, got married, and began writing, leading to the formation of the band "Thorneley." The lineup, except for Brian Dorody, was new. "His relationship with Chad Kruger of Nickelback led him deciding with 604 Records out of Vancouver. And this was the beginning of a brand new band simply called Thorneley..."
**Creative Differences with Management** : Brian Dorody, a holdover from Big Wreck, was initially excluded from the Thorneley lineup because management felt his presence would make the new band too similar to Big Wreck, aiming for Thorneley to be a "separate sort of thing." This decision added strain to relationships. "Brian was initially going to be the other guitar player in Thorneley. And then the sort of what's called the grown-ups, the people on the other side of the desk, where like, it's too much like Big Rec of Brian's around."
**Touring and Lineup Changes** : The band Thorneley engaged in tours, including one where guitarist Paula announced she would be absent for a gig in Edmonton due to her honeymoon. This unexpected vacancy led to Brian Dorody filling in, which subsequently contributed to the reformation of Big Wreck. "Paula was like, oh, by the way, that gig in Edmonton, I'm not going to be there. As I'm going on my honeymoon in Portugal..."

# curiosity

**Music Industry as a "Meritocracy"** : Ian Thorneley challenges the common perception among music fans that the music industry operates as a meritocracy, where good music automatically rises to the top. He states that this is "not necessarily true," indicating that other factors influence success. "first of all, they think the music is a meritocracy and if it's good, it'll rise to the top. Yeah, that's not necessarily true."
**"Star-Making Machinery" Quote** : Ian Thorneley references Joni Mitchell's phrase, "the star making machinery behind the popular song," to describe the unseen, complex processes and pressures that musicians face in the industry, especially during album cycles or while touring. "You have to deal with, you know, to quote Johnny Mitchell, the star making machinery behind the popular song."
**Music Business Exploitation** : Ian Thorneley recounts significant personal experiences of being taken advantage of by business associates within the music industry during the mid-2000s, highlighting the exploitative nature that can exist behind the scenes. "Well, where people try to take advantage of you over the time. Oh, big time."
**Creative Control vs. Commercial Demands** : The narrative frequently contrasts the desire for artistic expression and creative freedom with the commercial demands and statistical analyses imposed by some record label executives to engineer hit songs. The second iteration of Big Wreck was notably freed from these pressures by SRO Anthem. "the statistics say that a hit should essentially you should hit the chorus before, you know, all that. It was just like, we're just making music here."
**Ryerson Film Crew** : During a Thorneley tour, a film crew consisting of students from Ryerson University, led by Michael Fano (who later became president of Apple Canada), accompanied the band, traveling in an RV and filming performances on stage. "We had a film crew with us. I was a group of students from Ryerson. Two sweet kids, all of them. Mike, Michael Fano, he's like, he's like president of Apple Canada now."
**Roadies and Tech Support** : The critical role of crew members is highlighted in an anecdote where Timmy, described as a "second dad," retrieved fresh pants, and Shayne, the guitar tech, attempted to use gaffer tape to address a wardrobe malfunction during a live show. "While Shayne, who's my guitar tech, pulls out the gaffer tape and proceeds to start gaffer taping my, and I was like, dude, don't, no, no, no, no, no, no, no, no, no, no."
