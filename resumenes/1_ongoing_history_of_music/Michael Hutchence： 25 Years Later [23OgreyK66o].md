## albums
**INXS (1980)** : Their self-titled debut album, released in October 1980, featured the Australian hit "Just Keep Walking." "In October 1980, they released their first album, a self-titled record that featured an Australian hit called Just Keep Walking."
**Underneath the Colours (1981)** : Released in November 1981, this was their second album and was initially an Australia-only release. "That release was followed in November 1981 by a second Australia-only album and entitled Underneath the Colors."
**Shabooh Shoobah (1982)** : Released on October 13, 1982, this album served as their international introduction, though it only did "okay" in that regard. "It came out on October 13th, 1982, and this was the big single."
**The Swing (1984)** : Their fourth album and second international release, produced by Nile Rodgers in New York. It was a massive success in Australia (one of the five biggest selling domestic albums at the time), Canada, Argentina, and France, and sold a million copies in the US. The UK remained "immune to InXS's charms." "The swing was a huge hit at home, where it became one of the five biggest selling domestic albums to that point, and was also hit in Canada and Argentina and France in a few other countries."
**Listen Like Thieves (1985)** : Released on October 14, 1985, this fifth album marked their true international breakthrough, fueled by a key song that had the working title "Funk Song No. 13." "It was called Listen Like Thebes, and it appeared on October 14, 1985. This was the international breakthrough they were looking for."
**Kick (1987)** : Released on October 12, 1987, this album was a monumental international success, reaching #1 in Australia, Canada, and New Zealand, #3 in the US and France, and a top 10 record in the UK. It sold an estimated "north of 20 million" copies, including a million in Canada (twice its sales in Australia). Five of its twelve songs became major singles. "Number one album in Australia, in Canada, and New Zealand. Number three in the US and France. And finally, a top 10 record in the UK. The best estimate of total sales is somewhere north of 20 million."
**X (1990)** : Released after *Kick*, this album also sold "extremely well," but marked the beginning of the band's peak decline. "After kick came X in 1990. It also sold extremely well."
**Welcome to Wherever You Are (1992)** : This album underperformed commercially, falling victim to the public's increasing interest in grunge and alternative music. "their 1992 album, Welcome to Wherever You Are, did not do as well as expected. A victim of the public's growing fascination with grunge and all things alternative."
**Full Moon, Dirty Hearts (1993)** : Released in November 1993, this album experienced similar commercial struggles to *Welcome to Wherever You Are*. "Full moon dirty hearts which came out in November 1993 fared no better."
**Greatest Hits (1994)** : Michael Hutchence mentioned this album in a 1994 TV interview, explaining its release was due to the completion of a contract with an American record company and the band's desire to release it "properly" worldwide. "Now, Michael, you've got your greatest hits album out of them."
**Elegantly Wasted (1997) – Title Track Mention**: The title track of this album was released seven months before Michael Hutchence's death. "The title track of the elegantly wasted album released by InXS seven months before the death of their super charismatic singer Michael Hutchins."

## curiosities
**Name Change (1979)** : The group transitioned from "The Farriss Brothers" to "InXS" in 1979. "In 1979, the group changed their name. They became known as in excess."
**Australian Pub Circuit Development**: The band honed their skills by playing at least 300 shows a year on the demanding Australian pub circuit, including mining towns and sheep stations, developing a distinctive "funky swagger." "It excess learned their chops the hard way on the grueling Australian pub circuit."
**First International Deal (1982)** : In 1982, InXS secured their first international record deal, marking a significant step toward global recognition. "The Big Break came in 1982 when in excess signed their first international deal."
**Early International vs. Domestic Success**: By summer 1983, InXS was drawing crowds of 14,000 people in Australia, showcasing their domestic popularity. However, their first American performance in San Diego in March 1983, was attended by only 24 people, highlighting the disparity in their early international reach. "Their first American performance, which was the show in San Diego in March 1983, was in front of 24 people."
**Opening for Major Acts**: InXS toured extensively, frequently serving as an opening act for prominent bands such as The Go-Go's, The Kinks, and Hall & Oates. "The band was on the road almost constantly, usually opening for someone like the Go-Go's or the Kinks or Holland Oats."
**Kick Album Initial Rejection**: After recording *Kick*, InXS's record company in New York rejected it, deeming it "too funky, too dancing," and offered a million dollars for the band to start over. However, their manager bypassed the label by releasing it through college and alternative radio, leading to immediate success. "They took one listen and rejected it. Ah, too funky, too dancing, they said, look, here's a million dollars. Go back to Australia, start again."
**Peak Popularity (1987-1991)** : Between 1987 and 1991, InXS achieved massive global success, with millions in album sales, widespread music and video presence, and hugely profitable concert tours. A show in Mexico City grossed over $1 million, a Wembley Stadium concert in London grossed over $3 million, and over 100,000 fans attended a show in Rio de Janeiro. Some critics even considered U2 their only rivals worldwide. "Between 1987 and 1991, Inexess was everywhere."
**Album Sales**: The band sold close to 80 million albums, establishing them as the second biggest Australian success story, after AC/DC. "His band had sold somewhere near 80 million albums, putting them second only to, who, ACDC I guess, in terms of Australian success stories."
**New Record Deal and Album (1997)** : At the start of 1997, InXS secured a new record deal and had an album in production, signaling their intent for a professional "fresh start." "By the beginning of 1997, in excess had a new record deal and a new album on the way. Professionally, the group was looking for a fresh start."
**20th Anniversary TV Special (1997)** : In November and December 1997, InXS was in Sydney filming a TV special for Australian television to commemorate their 20th anniversary, coinciding with local performance dates. "In excess was back home in Sydney, filming a TV special for Australian TV. Everybody was pumped up for this documentary commemorating the 20th anniversary of In Excess, which coincided with some dates at home in November and December."
**Post-Hutchence Continuation**: After Michael Hutchence's death, the band took a year-long hiatus before continuing with guest singers. They later participated in the reality TV series *Rock Star*, where Canadian singer JD Fortune was chosen as their new frontman. Fortune sang on one album, was fired, but then rehired a few years later. "In excess, tried to continue after taking a year off after Michael died. There was a period when they used guest singers before signing on to do a reality TV series called Rockstar that ultimately resulted in JD Fortune, a Canadian singer, getting the gig."

"an autopsy show that Hutchins blood was filled with alcohol, cocaine, prozac and a few prescription drugs."
**Official Cause of Death and Alternative Theories**: His death on November 22, 1997, was officially ruled a suicide. However, in 1999, Paula Yates suggested on *60 Minutes* Australia that he died from auto-erotic asphyxiation gone wrong, a theory the coroner explicitly rejected due to lack of forensic evidence. This theory gained widespread belief, fueled by Hutchence's known sexual kinks, his mother's mention of him being with sadomasochists, and his own comments about seeking the "ultimate sexual high." "Again, the corner rule that Michael Hutchins died by his own hand in that hotel room on November 22, 1997. But then in 1999, Paula Yates made a suggestion. Totally contrary to everything she'd said up until that point. On the Australian version of 60 minutes, she intimated that Michael may have died as the result of solo sex play gone wrong. Auto-erotic asphyxiation, which he took too far."
**Brain Tumor Theory**: A theory emerged that he was convinced he had a brain tumor and intended to commit suicide before the tumor could kill him, similar to Kurt Cobain's reported stomach issues. Cat scans disproved the tumor, but a friend recalled Hutchence stating he "would do a curt before the cancer got to him." "Another theory was that Michael was convinced he had a brain tumor and he was going to kill himself before the tumor did."
**Cremation and Ash Dispute**: Following his cremation, a significant dispute arose over his ashes involving his family, Paula Yates, and the band. "And later when Michael was cremated, there was a big fight over his ashes between his family, Paula, and the band."
**Financial Status and Estate**: Despite InXS's extensive album sales and his profitable investment in the film *Crocodile Dundee*, Hutchence had been facing severe money problems for years and was "practically bankrupt" at the time of his death. Although a complex series of trusts and holding companies held at least eight million pounds, legal fees left "virtually nothing left" in his estate years later. "But as it turns out, Michael had been having money problems for years and was practically bankrupt."
**Posthumous Works and Legacy**: Numerous books, biographies, and at least two major documentaries, *Michael Hutchence: The Last Rock and Roll Star* (2017) and *Mystify Michael Hutchence* (2019), have been released about him. Despite these, unanswered questions about his death persist. His legacy positions him as one of rock's greatest frontmen, and it is suggested InXS would likely be a successful heritage band touring today if he were alive. "There were half a dozen books in biographies, and there have been at least two major documentaries. Michael Hutchinson's The Last Rock and Roll Star in 2017, and mystified Michael Hutchinson's in 2019."
