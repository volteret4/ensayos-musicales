# genre - Alt-rock

## curiosities
**80s "Outsider-ness"** : In the 1980s, alt-rock was characterized by its "outsider-ness," with participants in the scene, including bands, managers, labels, DJs, record stores, and fans, reveling in their detachment from the mainstream. There was a mutual disinterest between the alt-rock scene and the mainstream.
"People involved in the scene, the bands and the managers, to the labels and the DJs, the record stores and fans, reveled in their outsider-ness."

**Pre-Grunge Diversity in 80s Radio** : In the 1980s, commercial alt-rock radio stations were not connected by industry charts or tracked airplay, leading to a "staggering breadth of music" on playlists. This included techno pop, industrial, various punk styles, early rap/hip pop, Goth, British indie, dance remixes, ethereal dream pop, and a smattering of guitar rock, with no two stations sounding exactly alike.
"Consequently, a staggering breadth of music made the playlist. There was techno pop and industrial, every conceivable flavor of punk, early rap and hip pop, Goth, British indie, remixes that made you want to dance, like Venus from Banana Rama."

**"Golden Age" of Commercial Alt-Rock Radio** : The pre-grunge era of commercial alt-rock radio in the 1980s is retrospectively considered by some to be a "golden age." This period was characterized by hardcore music fans staffing stations, who were on a mission to deliver good music, despite low salaries and the constant threat of format changes.
"And in retrospect, some consider this to be the golden age of commercial alt-rock radio."

**Impact of Grunge on Alt-Rock** : Grunge effectively "ruined" the diverse nature of 80s alt-rock by causing it to coalesce around hard guitar rock, leading to a homogenization of sound. As record labels and sales surged, the focus shifted predominantly to guitar-based music, marginalizing other varied sounds that were once prominent.
"And the thing that did it was grunge. Thanks to riff-ready songs from Pearl Jam, Soundgarden, Allison Chains, and especially Nirvana, Alt Rock began to coalesce around hard guitar rock."

**Post-Grunge Homogeneity on Radio** : The immense popularity of grunge led to alt-rock radio stations across North America adopting a more homogeneous sound. Program directors began to mimic playlists from larger stations like K-Rock in Los Angeles, rather than developing local flavors, resulting in a focus on big guitars and screaming vocalists.
"Grunge was so big and so popular that it effectively became Alt Rock. And those varied sounds that were so big in the 80s, largely squeezed out in favor of big guitars and screaming vocalists."

**Grunge as "Training Wheels"** : Grunge served as "Alt Rock with training wheels," successfully luring millions of people into the alternative music scene with its accessible yet distinct approach to traditional guitar rock. This exposure encouraged listeners to explore deeper into alt-rock and other genres, enriching their musical lives.
"Not only did they make great music, but instead of this, it was also Alt Rock with training wheels that lured people in with its different yet familiar approach to traditional guitar rock."

**Post-Grunge Playlisting Conservatism** : After the grunge era, commercial alt-rock radio became conservative, preferring to play "grunge or grunge-ish" (guitar-based, often with drop-tuning) music rather than taking chances on genuinely new and different sounds. This approach, driven by a desire for stable ratings and revenues, led to tightened playlists and a diminished variety.
"Instead of taking a chance and something truly new and truly different, it was safer from a ratings point of view to play something grunge or grunge-ish."

**Resurgence of Variety Post-2000s** : In recent times, alt-rock radio has "loosened up" and "weaned itself" from its over-reliance on grunge guitars, leading to an explosion of indie rock. The variety of sounds on alt-rock radio playlists is now much greater than it has been in quite some time, although some argue there are too few big guitar bands today.
"Alt Rock radio has weaned itself at least a little bit from Grunge guitars. The variety of sounds is much greater than it's been in quite some time."

**80s Alt-Rock Radio Station Differences** : In the 1980s, commercial alt-rock radio stations operated independently, without industry magazines publishing alternative charts or tracking airplay. **Extended Mixes on 80s Radio** : In the 1980s, radio announcers had significant leeway in choosing music, often preferring extended mixes over album or single mixes. These longer versions were perceived as artistically superior and also provided announcers more time to manage station operations like filing records or answering request lines.
"Back then, the announcer had a tremendous amount of leeway in choosing the music that we played, and many of us found these extended mixes to be better than the album with the single mix, that they were longer to help."

**Radio Ratings vs. Artistic Fulfillment** : While extended mixes and diverse playlists were artistically fulfilling for 80s alt-rock radio staff, they were detrimental to ratings. Commercial radio relies on appealing to the largest possible audience for extended periods, and for many, this means easily singable songs, not necessarily "good music" in the hardcore fan sense.
"You need to appeal to the greatest number of people, and to get them to listen as often as possible, and for as long as possible."

**Financial Struggles of Commercial Alt-Rock Radio (80s)** : Commercial alt-rock stations in the 1980s frequently struggled financially, operating month-to-month with the constant threat of a format change. **Enduring Popularity of 90s Music** : Music from the 1990s, particularly grunge and related acts, was so popular and good that audiences continued to demand it into the 21st century. **Decline of "Star-Making Machinery" in the 21st Century** : In the 2000s, with the rise of streaming and the decline of physical media and record stores, the power of traditional "cultural gatekeepers" (record labels, radio stations, music magazines, video channels) significantly diminished. They could no longer create a consensus around "superstar" acts, as individual listeners became their own music directors with vast access to music.
"All the power swung away from the star-making machinery behind the popular song to the individual. The old cultural gatekeepers, record labels, radio stations, record stores, music magazines and video channels saw their powers of persuasion greatly diminished."

**Competition from Entire Musical History** : Current artists now face unprecedented competition, not just from contemporaries but from the entire musical history of the human race, which is instantly accessible via streaming. **Streaming's Impact on Music Consumption and Value** : Streaming has liberated recorded music from physical limitations, making the entire history of music accessible as pure data. However, this has changed user behavior; without the financial investment of buying physical music, listeners are less inclined to fully engage with an album, often skipping songs quickly and valuing music differently.
"Streaming is not about possession. It's about access and convenience. And this has resulted in some very different interactions with music."

**Listener Skipping Behavior on Streaming Platforms** : Data analysis reveals that a significant portion of streaming listeners skip songs very quickly: 24% skip within 5 seconds, another 29% bail after 10 seconds, and 35% are gone by the 30-second mark. This 30-second threshold is critical because streaming services do not pay artists for a song until 30 seconds have passed.
"Data analysis shows that 24% of streaming listeners will skip to the next song if they don't like it or don't recognize it within the first five seconds, 24% of them."

**Songwriting Adaptation to Streaming Algorithms** : Due to listeners skipping songs within the first 30 seconds and the payment threshold, producers and songwriters now "throw everything they can" into the first 30 seconds of a song. This includes shorter intros, putting choruses upfront, and adding many "sugar high hooks" to grab attention and prevent skips, ensuring payment.
"This means that producers and songwriters now throw everything they can at the first 30 seconds of a song in order to hold our attention."

**Trend Towards Shorter Songs on Streaming Platforms** : Spotify's algorithms increasingly favor shorter songs because listeners tend to stick with them longer. This incentive drives artists to write shorter tracks, as more listens translate to more money. The average song length on the Billboard Hot 100 decreased by half a minute from 2000 (4 minutes) to today (3 minutes 30 seconds), with a rising number of songs under two and a half minutes.
"Spotify's algorithms are increasingly favoring shorter songs due to the fact that more people stick with them longer."

**Streaming's Focus on Singles Over Albums** : In the streaming world, the emphasis is heavily on individual songs, specifically singles, with album tracks receiving much less attention. "With streaming, it's all about individual songs, specifically singles. Album tracks? They don't get much love at all."

**Enduring Role of Albums in the Music Industry** : Despite the streaming-driven focus on singles, albums still serve as the primary "currency" of the recorded music industry. Contracts, awards, and sales in physical stores (CDs and vinyl LPs) are still based on albums, ensuring their short-term survival even as CD sales decline annually.

**Shift from Album Cycles to Continuous Singles Release** : The streaming world encourages artists to keep fans continuously engaged through a "steady, drip, drip, drip" of new songs, which is gradually replacing the traditional album cycle. "That's slowly replacing the process of spending a year recording an album and then touring behind it for three years."

**Historical Precedent for Singles Dominance (Pre-1965)** : The current emphasis on singles over albums in the streaming era is a "throwback" to how the music industry operated before approximately 1965. In that earlier period, rock and roll and pop music primarily existed as 7-inch 45 RPM singles, with albums reserved for genres like classical, jazz, and soundtracks.
"This emphasis on singles over albums is a throwback to the way things used to be before about 1965."

**Album as an Artistic Medium (Post-1965 Shift)** : The perception of the album changed around 1965 when artists like The Beatles and Bob Dylan began to utilize it as a larger creative canvas. They started writing longer, more complex, and carefully sequenced collections of songs meant to be heard as a unified work, a paradigm solidified by albums like The Beatles' *Sgt. "But then things began to shift around 1965. The Beatles, Bob Dylan, and a few others looked at the album and thought, I can work with this."

**Economic Incentive for Albums (Post-1965)** : Record labels found albums significantly more attractive than singles due to "profit margins [that] were so much higher on albums." This economic incentive drove the industry's shift away from singles and into the album-dominated world that has largely persisted until the streaming era.
"It also was very attractive to record labels because the profit margins were so much higher on albums than they were on singles."

**Record Store Day Genesis (2008)** : The idea for Record Store Day originated from independent record store owners in Baltimore who were struggling due to digital music and piracy. Inspired by "comic book day," they conceived an annual event featuring free and exclusive products to attract customers, increase revenues, and boost vinyl sales. The first Record Store Day took place in 2008.
"The whole vinyl resurrection can be traced to a bunch of dudes in Baltimore who ran independent record stores. They were getting killed."

**Vinyl Sales Resurgence (Post-2008)** : Following the first Record Store Day in 2008, vinyl sales experienced consistent "double digit growth" year after year, globally. This boom was so significant that old pressing plants were brought back online, and new state-of-the-art manufacturing facilities were established from scratch to meet demand.
"After the first record store day in 2008, vinyl sales took off as more and more people got back into the format or into it for the first time."

**Vinyl Surpassing CD Sales (2020)** : In 2020, the dollar value of vinyl sold in the United States exceeded that of CDs, a phenomenon that had not occurred since 1986. This milestone underscored the remarkable resurgence of the vinyl format.
"It got to the point that in 2020, the dollar value of vinyl sold exceeded that of CDs in the United States. That hadn't happened since 1986."

**Skepticism About Vinyl Revival's Longevity** : Industry insiders express skepticism about the long-term sustainability of the vinyl resurrection due to several factors: record labels increasing prices to "ridiculous prices," supply chain issues (like a 2020 fire at a lacquer master plant that produced 80% of the world's supply), and the impact of COVID-19 on record store closures.
"I spoke with some people deeply involved with the vinyl industry and I found them to be skeptical about how much longer this will go on for a couple of reasons."

"The whole genesis of everything goth is Mary Shelley's novel Frankenstein, which was published in 1818."

"The movie was complete box office flop everywhere in the world except bizarrely in Winnipeg, where it stayed in theaters for months."
