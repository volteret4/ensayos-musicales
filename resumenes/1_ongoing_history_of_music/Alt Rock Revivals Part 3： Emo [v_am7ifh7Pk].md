# artist - Husker Du

## members
Grant Hart
Bob Mould
Greg Norton

## genres
Hardcore Punk

## albums
**Zen Arcade (1984)** : Released in 1984, this album is considered highly significant for the development of emo. It demonstrated that American hardcore could incorporate melody and meaningful lyrics while still being played very fast and loud. The band also experimented with tape effects, acoustic instruments, and vocal harmonies, and introduced deeply personal and emotional lyrical themes. Many observers have compared its impact on emo to that of The Beatles' "White Album." "In 1984, they released a very important album called Zen Arcade. This record showed it was possible for American hardcore to be played very, very fast and very, very loud, but with melody and with meaningful lyrics."

## songs
**Pink Turns To Blue (1984)** : This track from the "Zen Arcade" album exemplifies early emo elements, despite the term "emo" not yet existing. It features themes of drug addiction, loss, and death, which are delivered against a backdrop of serious, intense guitars. "The song about drug addiction and loss and death. But everything is set against some serious guitars."

## curiosities
**Integration of Melody in Punk** : Husker Du, originally a three-piece hardcore band, pioneered the inclusion of melody into the punk genre. Their approach demonstrated that a band could be both hardcore and sensitive simultaneously without compromising their strength. "Husker Dew was a 3-piece hardcore band who decided that melody had a place in punk after all."
**Emo Equivalent of The White Album** : Their 1984 album "Zen Arcade" has been referred to by more than one person as "the emo equivalent of the Beatles White album," highlighting its profound and foundational impact on the emo genre. "More than one person has called this album, the emo equivalent of the Beatles White album."
**Hardcore and Sensitivity** : Husker Du demonstrated that it was possible for a band to maintain a hardcore identity while also expressing sensitivity, challenging the notion that such emotional depth made a band "a wimp." "Husker Dew showed that you could be hardcore and sensitive at the same time without being a wimp."

"we need to give all kinds of credit to a scene in Washington, D.C. that emerged in the middle 1980s. And this is where we encounter a hardcore band called Rites of Spring."

# genre - Emo

## curiosities
**Four Distinct Waves** : Emo music has manifested in four distinct waves, which sometimes overlap. Each of these waves typically spans a period ranging from 8 to 12 years. "Emo has come through in four distinct waves that sometimes overlap, and each of these waves has periods that run anywhere from 8 to 12 years."
**First Wave (1984-1994)** : The first wave of emo spanned from approximately 1984 to 1994, a decade during which the genre was born and developed. Initially, emo was underground and not widely known. "That's the first wave of emo, a 10 year span during which emo was born and developed."
**Early Characteristics and Hardcore Punk Roots** : Emo, initially a subgenre of hardcore punk, was characterized as brooding, introspective, sensitive, troubled, empathetic, vulnerable, straight-edged, confessional, and emotional. It was a "shrinking violet" within Alt Rock, often hard to find and avoiding attention. "If Alt Rock had a shrinking violet, it was definitely emo, the brooding, introspective, sensitive, troubled, empathetic, vulnerable, straight edged, confessional, emotional, subgenre, and descended of, believe it or not, hardcore punk."
**Musical Variations** : Emo exhibits a range of musical styles, from simple and straightforward compositions to more complex forms featuring unusual song structures and intricate guitar work. "Some emo is pretty simple and straightforward. Other flavors are more complex with unusual song structures and intricate guitar work."
**Definitional Debate** : There is considerable debate regarding what constitutes emo and what does not, leading to divergent opinions on its boundaries and true origins. "And there's a lot of debate, and I mean a lot about what is and is not emo."
**Origins Debate** : The origin of emo is a tricky subject with very divergent opinions, with some researchers even tracing its roots back to The Beach Boys' "Pet Sounds" album in 1966. "And even this is a tricky subject because there are some very divergent opinions on who was responsible for this whole thing."
**Naming Controversy ("Emo Core")** : The term "emo core" began circulating in the mid-1980s but was almost universally despised by the musicians who practiced the genre, with no one wanting to take credit for it. The shortened term "emo" eventually stuck as a way to classify the music. "The term started to be bandied about in the mid 1980s, but no one has really taken credit for it because no one liked it. The original term was emo core, which was almost universally despised by all practitioners of this music."
**Original D.C. Scene Dissolution** : The original D.C. emo scene, prominent around 1988 and largely confined to the Washington D.C. area and bands on the Discord label, had completely dissolved by that year, with all involved bands breaking up and moving on. "However, this original DC emo scene was completely over by about 1988. All the bands involved had broken up and moved on."
**Second Wave (1994 onwards)** : The second wave of emo began in 1994, directly following the first wave. This period saw emo spread and evolve, incorporating more dynamics into songs with explosive screaming juxtaposed with quiet melodic sections. "The traditional hardcore community thought second wave emo groups and their fans were a bunch of whims. But at the same time, though, there were those who appreciated this raw, emotional honesty. Emo had not only spread, but it evolved."
**Musical Evolution in Second Wave** : During the second wave, emo bands introduced greater dynamics into their songs, featuring loud, explosive screaming segments interspersed with quiet, melodic passages. "Bands began to put more dynamics into their songs, large, how'd explosive screaming bits, interspersed with quiet melodic parts."
**Emergence of Emo Fashion** : The second wave saw the emergence of a distinct emo fashion, characterized by horn-rimmed glasses, bead necklaces, sweater vests worn over dress shirts, dress pants, and white socks. All clothing items except the socks were typically in darker colors, contributing to a generally "nerdy" aesthetic. "Emo fashion began to emerge. Horn-rimmed glasses, bead necklaces, sweater vests over dress shirts with dress pants and white socks. Everything but the socks had to be in darker colors too. So in other words, pretty nerdy."
**The Emo Tremble and Tap** : In contrast to slam dancing or mashing, emo developed its own unique dance styles. The "emo tremble" involved ringing one's hands while bouncing on the balls of the feet in time with the music, with a variation called the "emo tap" where one taps their chest to the rhythm. "Because emo wasn't about slam dancing or mashing, a new thing developed. The emo tremble. Basically, you ring your hands while bouncing off the balls of your feet in time with the music. A variation of that is sometimes called the emo tap, where you tap your chest along with the music."
**Anti-Commercial Ethos in Second Wave** : The second wave of emo largely maintained an underground and anti-commercial stance. It was considered "uncool" for bands to sell t-shirts at shows, cover charges for gigs were typically five dollars or less, and charging more than ten dollars for a CD was deemed "gouging." "And everything stayed underground and very anti-commercial. For example, if you were in an emo band, it was almost considered uncool to even sell t-shirts at your show. Covered charge for a gig had to be five bucks or less. And if you charge more than ten dollars for a CD, well, that was considered gouging."
**Late 90s Diversification and Accessibility** : By the end of the 1990s, emo had diversified considerably in terms of sound and style. While traditional emo (maintained by bands like Fugazi) persisted, more bands incorporated catchy guitar hooks instead of droning strumming. Vocals became less screamy, and lyrics, though still emotional, were less dire, moving from depressed to melancholy, with some songs even sounding happy. This increased accessibility to a wider audience. "By the end of the 1990s, emo came in all sorts of different colors and shapes. Sure, there was traditional emo, Fugazi made sure they kept that alive. But more events were using catchy guitar hooks instead of the drony strumming. While a lot of arrangements still had that loud, soft, loud, soft dynamic, vocals started to become a little less screamy. Lyrix were still very emotional but not quite as dire. Some had moved up the scale from depressed to merely somewhat melancholy. And hey, some songs actually sounded downright happy. All this contributed to a situation where emo is more accessible, more palatable to more people."
**Third Wave (2000-2010)** : The third wave of emo, occurring in the early 2000s, represented "peak emo." It began around 2000, peaked around 2008, and started moving into the background by 2010. This period saw emo blow up into the mainstream and fragment into numerous variations, much to the annoyance of purists who often declared "that's not emo." "Third wave emo begins in 2000 and proceeds starting in about 2008 and moved into the background again as of around 2010. This is when things not only began to blow up in the mainstream but also splinter into a million different variations, something that really annoyed the purists. Forgive them if they screamed, that's not emo, about a bunch of acts that were being labeled emo."
**Third Wave Crossover and Fragmentation** : The third wave was characterized by a crossover between "proper emo" and emo-like bands that were also classified as punk, pop punk, post-hardcore, and metalcore. This resulted in significant fragmentation, producing subgenres like emo-pop and screamo. Many third wave bands, some of whom had existed for years, achieved mainstream impact only in the early 2000s. "There was, in short, a crossover between proper emo, if you'll allow me to use that term, an emo-like bands, who were probably best classified as punk, pop punk, post-hardcore, and metalcore. There's a lot of fragmentation. Emo-pop, screamo bands, just a couple of examples. And not all third wave emo bands were brand new, some had been around for years. It was only in the early 2000s that they came of age and started making an impact beyond a small scene."
**Commercial Success and Major Label Interest (Third Wave)** : Third wave emo bands operated in an era where commercial success was attainable. Major labels showed significant interest, hoping to position emo as "the next grunge," and radio stations actively played emo-ish music. Many emo records from bands like New Found Glory, Fall Out Boy, Panic! at the Disco, Taking Back Sunday, The All-American Rejects, Simple Plan, and Alexisonfire achieved top 10 album chart positions. "The one thing just about all these third wave emo bands had in common was that they lived in an era where commercial success wasn't impossible. Major labels were interested, very interested, in turning emo into the next grunge. Radio was interested and was playing a lot of emo-ish music. Emo records from bands like Newfound Glory, Fallout Boy, Patek at the Disco, Taking Back Sunday, All American Rejects, Simple Plan, and Alexis on Fire all rocketed into the top 10 on the album charts."
**Catharsis of Emo Shows** : Emo shows during the third wave provided a hugely cathartic experience for fans, allowing them to collectively express and scream out their pain and fear alongside hundreds or thousands of others. "Fans loved it. There was something hugely cathartic about being able to scream your pain and fear with hundreds or even thousands of people out of show."
**Dark Turn in Third Wave Lyrics** : As third wave emo grew, some bands incorporated darker lyrical themes, including violent imagery, references to self-harm (like cutting), and hateful expressions directed at women. This content later became a source of concern and regret for fans and bands looking back. "Some bands dipped into writing lyrics that contained violent imagery, talk of self-harm, like cutting, and more than a few hateful stabs are doing harm to women. This became a real problem later when fans and bands look back at some of the music and said, what were we thinking?"
**Decline of Third Wave Emo** : Between 2000 and 2010, the emo market became saturated. The music began to feel stale, and as fans matured, their interest waned. Younger generations entering their coming-of-age years sought different sounds, gravitating towards pop, K-pop, and hip-hop, leading to a decline in emo's popularity. "There was a lot of emo for everyone between 2000 and 2010, but you know how music works, right? After a while it was just too much. Things to stale, the fans grew up and grew away. They weren't nearly as depressed. Younger fans now in their coming of age years wanted something different, and for them that was pop, K-pop specifically, and hip-hop, so interest-wained."
**Fourth Wave (2010s onwards)** : The fourth wave of emo began as the third wave was declining, roughly between 2008 and 2010. For most of the 2010s, this wave largely remained underground and regional. It was sustained by bands like Modern Baseball, Basement, Touche Amore, Into It. Over It., The Hotelier, Pianos Become The Teeth, Just Friends, and The World Is A Beautiful Place & I Am No Longer Afraid To Die. "As third wave emo was falling apart between 2008 and 2010, the fourth wave was beginning. However, for most of the 2010s, this wave stayed mostly underground and regional, but it was kept alive by bands like Modern Baseball, Basement, Tuchet Amour, Intu Over It, the Hotelier, pianos become teeth, just friends."
**Influences and Evolution in Fourth Wave** : Fourth wave emo bands draw inspiration from various sources, with some influenced by 90s emo, others developing their sounds independently, and still others incorporating emo elements with their unique twists. The potential for these bands to achieve mainstream consciousness in the 2020s remains uncertain. "Some of the bands are inspired by 90s emo. Others have come up with sounds independently of that. Still others have taken elements of emo and put their own twist on things. Will any of them explode into mainstream consciousness in the 2020s? Hard to say."
**Impact of Third Wave Reunions on Fourth Wave** : The reunion of commercially successful third wave emo bands, such as Alexisonfire and My Chemical Romance, who are releasing new music and performing for large crowds, may potentially provide a boost for fourth wave emo bands. "Meanwhile though, some of those third wave emo bands, the most commercially successful of all the waves, have reformed. They're putting out new music and they're touring before huge crowds. Will these third wave vets result in a leg up for fourth wave emo bands? Let's see."
**Comparison to Punk and Ska Revivals** : Emo revivals are noted to differ from punk and ska revivals. Instead of undergoing a full period of hibernation before returning, emo has arrived in distinct, sometimes overlapping, waves. "emo is slightly different than the punk and sky revivals in that it has come at us in different waves instead of going into full-on hibernation for a while and then coming back."
