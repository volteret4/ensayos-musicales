# genre - Alternative music

## curiosities
**Origin of the Term** : In the 1970s and early 1980s, "alternative music" became the term for music created by "weird kids" that was not played on mainstream radio or signed by major labels. Small independent record labels, offering a "career alternative" for these bands, became known as "alternative record companies," thereby defining the bands and their music as "alternative." "By the very early 1980s, those small indie labels had become known as alternative record companies because they provided a career option, a career alternative for the weird band who couldn't find homes with the majors."
**Overtaking the Mainstream (Mid-90s)** : The "grunge explosion" of the early to mid-1990s saw alternative rock transition from an underground form to the mainstream, largely replacing traditional mainstream rock acts. This shift made the term "alternative" somewhat contradictory, as it was no longer an alternative to anything, prompting a search for new terminology like "New Rock." "In other words, by the middle of the 90s, alternative music wasn't an alternative to anything anymore. It was now the mainstream, which caused much soul searching and hand-winging amongst musicians and fans like."
**Contemporary Meaning** : Today, "alternative music" continues to exist, often referred to as "indie" or "underground music." It describes cutting-edge rock that operates below the mainstream radar, found in small clubs and niche record stores, and is typically sought out by a more selective audience. "So alternative music, rock that is an alternative to everything we see in here in the mainstreams till exists, is just that you have to look for it and maybe sometime call it Indie or underground music."
**UK Interchangeability with Indie** : In the UK, the term "Indie" is often roughly interchangeable with "alternative." This is attributed to the specific structure of the British music industry, which provides independent artists a greater opportunity to penetrate the mainstream compared to North America. "In the UK, Indie can be roughly interchangeable with alternative."

# curiosity

**Mainstream vs. Weird Kids (70s/80s)** : In the 1970s and 1980s, rock fans were rigidly divided into two "tribes": those who listened to mainstream rock (e.g., Led Zeppelin, Pink Floyd) and those who preferred "weird stuff" found in niche record stores (e.g., The Smiths, Depeche Mode). Fans were expected to align with one side, reflecting a strong cultural divide in music appreciation. "In ancient days, say, oh, the 70s and 80s, people were placed into one of two tribes."
**Major Record Labels - Definition and Consolidation** : Major record labels are defined as multinational giants that control most of the world's recorded music. Decades of mergers and consolidation have reduced their number to just four primary entities: Universal, EMI, Sony BMG, and Warner, each managing a multitude of subsidiary labels. "A major label is one of the multinational giants that control most of the world's flow of recorded music."
**Independent Record Labels - Definition** : An independent (indie) label is a company or label completely unaffiliated with a major record label, handling all its business and distribution dealings independently. This can mean operating as a self-contained third-party company or an artist forming their own label, offering total creative control but also demanding full responsibility for risks and hassles. "Indy is short for, of course, independent. It's another way of saying that a particular artist issues their music through a company or label that is in no way affiliated with a major record label."
**Challenges for Indie Artists/Labels** : Indie artists and labels face significant operational burdens, including manufacturing records, securing distribution into stores, collecting sales revenue, managing audits, taxes, contracts, and handling extensive promotional efforts. These tasks typically require large staffs at major labels, often leaving indie artists with limited time for creative work. "However, it also means that you're on the hook for all the risks and hassles. This includes manufacturing your records, actually getting them into as many record stores as you can, and then collecting the money from the sales of those records."
**Boutique Labels** : Boutique labels are record companies that appear to be independent but are, in fact, carefully disguised subsidiaries of a major label. They are established to offer the "street cred" associated with indie labels while still operating under a major's extensive resources and distribution network. "The first was the creation of boutique labels. These are record companies that look and sound indie, but are actually carefully disguised subsidiaries of a major label."
**Vanity Labels** : Vanity labels are a specific type of boutique label, often indie-looking imprints, that are set up by major labels on behalf of important artists. These labels grant artists additional creative control or a platform to develop other talent, all while remaining within the major label system. "Some boutique labels are also vanity labels. Indie-looking imprints set up on behalf of important artists."
**Indie-Major Distribution Deals** : This refers to a mutually beneficial arrangement where a truly independent company continues to develop music independently, and a major label handles the distribution in exchange for a percentage of the profits. This model enables indie artists to achieve wider reach without sacrificing creative independence. "The other option is to forge a mutually beneficial deal between a major and an honest to god Indie."
**Indie as "Underground"** : Beyond its literal meaning of independent, "Indie" often implies "underground" music, referring to music that intentionally flies below the mainstream's radar. This type of music, often synonymous with the new alternative, is typically found in small clubs and niche record stores and appeals to a more selective, cutting-edge audience. "Indie also implies underground, the kind of music that flies below the mainstream's radar."
**UK Music Industry Advantages for Indies** : The UK music industry provides several advantages for independent artists to penetrate the mainstream more easily than in North America. These include the UK's smaller geographical size, national radio networks like BBC Radio 1 dedicated to pop and rock, national TV music programs such as Top of the Pops, and influential weekly music press like the New Musical Express, which actively champions unknown bands. "But because of the makeup of the UK music industry, an Indie artist has a better shot of seeping into the mainstream than they do in North America."
**Changing Fan Attitudes (Modern Era)** : The strict musical tribalism of earlier decades has largely dissipated among young people today. It is now considered "perfectly okay, in fact admirable and inviable" to appreciate a wide range of artists, from contemporary acts like Arcade Fire and Coldplay to classics like The Beatles, Led Zeppelin, Marvin Gaye, James Brown, and Elvis Presley. "Today, that's really not the situation with a lot of young people. That snobbishness is disappearing on both sides."
**School of Rock (Movie)** : The movie "School of Rock" is presented as an example of the "ecumenical musical aesthetic" prevalent in modern times. This reflects a broad appreciation for diverse musical genres and artists, illustrating the contemporary shift away from rigid genre-based fandom. "Just think of the ecumenical musical aesthetic of movies like School of Rock and you get an idea of what we're dealing with here."
