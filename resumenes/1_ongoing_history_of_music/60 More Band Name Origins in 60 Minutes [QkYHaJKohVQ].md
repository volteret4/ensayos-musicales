# curiosity

**More Band Name Origins – 60 Additional Examples (Bandminology)** : A second installment of band name origins. "Bandminology" — coined by the podcast in consultation with linguistics academics and branding experts — is proposed as the term for the study of how musical groups choose their names.

**Rage Against the Machine – A 1979 Chevy Van That Kept Breaking Down, or Political Metaphor** : The phrase "Rage Against the Machine" was used in a punk fanzine called No Answers in 1989 by a writer covering the pre-RATM band Inside Out, which featured Zack de la Rocha. Conventional wisdom holds that "the machine" represents oppressive capitalism. However, de la Rocha once noted literally: the band's constant-breakdown 1979 Chevy van was the thing they raged against, taking sledgehammers to it when they finally had money to buy a new one. "So the machine that they were raging against was 1979 Chevy van."

**Blind Melon – Brad Smith's Father's Description of Hippies; Referenced Cheech & Chong** : Guitarist Brad Smith's father used "blind melon" to describe hippies. He may have borrowed it from a Cheech and Chong character named "Blind Melon Chitlin," itself a parody of real 1920s blues musician Blind Lemon Jefferson. "His father used to describe hippies this way."

**Bush – Suggested by a Music Video Director; Named "Bush X" in Canada Due to Trademark Conflict** : When Gavin Rossdale's band was preparing to release their debut album Sixteen Stone, a music video director suggested "Bush" — short, memorable, graphical, with a mildly suggestive sexual edge. The band was then called Future Primitive. In Canada, however, an existing 1970s band named Bush held the trademark, so Rossdale's group legally traded as "Bush X" in Canada until the conflict was resolved years later. "Until that trademark issue got sorted a few years later, Bush had to legally trade under the name Bush X, but only in Canada."

**Fugazi – Murky Origins: NYC Limo Company, French Land Mine, or Vietnam War Acronym** : Multiple theories: a shady New York City limo company called Fugazi; derived from the French "fugasse," a type of landmine used in both the Korean and Vietnam Wars; or an acronym: "F'd Up, Got Ambushed, Zipped In a Body Bag." "The name origin is a bit murky."

**Mumford & Sons – Marcus Mumford Wanted to Evoke a Family Business** : Lead singer Marcus Mumford liked the idea of a band name that evoked an old family business. "He'd like the idea of a band evoking an old family business."

**Cake – Not About Pastry; About Something That Sticks to Your Shoe** : Formed in Sacramento in 1991, Cake chose their name to convey something that "insidiously becomes part of your life" — the way something cakes onto the bottom of your shoe. "Cake onto your shoe, and it's just sort of there until you get rid of it."

**Wolfmother – Chosen at Last Second From a Tom Robbins Novel ("Skinny Legs")** : When the band showed up for their first gig without a name, bassist Chris Ross had been reading a Tom Robbins novel called Skinny Legs that contained the word "wolfmother." With no time left, they adopted it. Singer Andrew Stockdale and drummer Myles Heskett weren't crazy about it but accepted it on the spot. "With time running out before they were due on stage, they said, oh, okay, we're Wolfmother."

**Garbage – Insult from a Friend Adopted as a Name** : When Butch Vig, Duke Erikson, and Steve Marker were assembling the band in Madison, Wisconsin in 1993, a friend commented that the new material "sounded like garbage." They took it as a cool suggestion for something irreverent. "A friend commented that this new stuff sounded like garbage."

**System of a Down – Changed from "Victims of a Down" to "System" for Shelf Proximity to Slayer** : Originally called Soil, then "Victims of a Down" — referencing the Armenian genocide, drawn from a poem by guitarist Daron Malakian. Bassist Shavo Odadjian suggested replacing "Victims" with "System" to attract more attention, and the change would place them closer to their idols Slayer on record store shelves alphabetically. "Base player Shavo Odagyan suggested that they might find more attention if they changed victims to system."

**Panic at the Disco – From a Line in One of Their Own Early Songs** : The band had an early song called "Panic" containing the line "panic at the disco, sat back and took it slow." The exclamation point (!), which migrated in and out of the name, was never officially part of it from the band's perspective. Guitarist Ryan Ross said fans added it, and it was eventually officially dropped. "It was never part of the name to us."

**Portugal. The Man – A Period Separating the Collective (Portugal) from the Individual (The Man)** : The period separating "Portugal" and "The Man" symbolically separates the many (a country full of people, like a band of multiple members) from the individual (a specific person, like each band member). The name was intended to feel larger than life. "The period that we see between Portugal and the man is to separate the many from the one."

**Blink-182 – Multiple Stories; Most Likely Random; Possibly "F*** Off" in Poway High School Code** : Originally just Blink, forced to add a suffix due to an Irish band with prior rights. The 182 has multiple explanations: times Tony Montana says the F-word in Scarface (widely cited but disputed), Mark Hoppus's ideal weight in pounds, a ship his grandfather served on. Most likely it was pulled randomly out of the air during a phone call with the label. Additional theory: at Tom DeLonge's high school in Poway, California, "182" was code for the F-word (8th letter = H? No: B=18th letter... actually under this theory "go" meaning 182). DeLonge has discussed this, suggesting the name should be pronounced "Blink F**k Off." "Blink 182 or Blink 182 if you want to go with the Tom DeLong explanation."

**The Chemical Brothers – Changed from "The Dust Brothers" After Name Conflict** : Originally called The Dust Brothers, they encountered a band already using that name. They took a word from the title of their third single, "Chemical Beats," and became The Chemical Brothers. "They were originally called the Dust Brothers, but there was another band using that name."

**The Cranberries – Accidental Rename via Typo on a Returned Demo Tape** : Formed as "The Cranberry Saw Us" — a pun the band themselves considered stupid. When they submitted a demo to a record label, it was returned addressed to "The Cranberries" (a typo). They decided it was a better name and kept it. "It was returned, addressed to the cranberries. So it was a typo."

**Mighty Mighty Bosstones – Bartender Suggested Adding "Mighty" to Solve a Name Conflict** : There was already a local a cappella group called the Mighty Bosstones. When the band mentioned the conflict to their bartender, he suggested simply adding another "Mighty." Hence Mighty Mighty Bosstones. "He suggested that they just add another mighty to their name."

**The New Pornographers – Named After a 1966 Japanese Film; Has Caused Misunderstandings** : Guitarist A.C. Newman caught the name from a 1966 Japanese film called The Pornographers. It was also a nod to bandmate Dan Bejar, who had a band called Destroyer with a track called "The Pornographers." The name has caused many real-world misunderstandings over the years with people assuming the band is involved in pornography. (Note: drummer Joe Seiders was later charged with possession of child pornography and is no longer with the band.) "The name has caused many misunderstandings over the years."

**Big Wreck – Named After a Disastrously Bad Band Rehearsal in 1994** : In 1994, the band had a rehearsal where everything went wrong. Guitarist Brian Doherty declared the session had been "a big wreck." The name stuck. "The practice session had been a big wreck."

**Simple Minds – Taken from a David Bowie Lyric; Deliberately Avoided Starting with "The"** : Formed from the ashes of Johnny and the Self-Abusers. Singer Jim Kerr and guitarist Charlie Burchill consulted Bowie lyrics specifically to avoid having a name that started with "The." They found the line "so simple-minded he can drive his module" in a Bowie song and adopted Simple Minds. "They didn't want to be another group whose name began with the."

**Tears for Fears – From Psychologist Arthur Janov's "Primal Scream" Therapy Book** : Named after the writings of American psychologist Arthur Janov, whose book Prisoners of Pain discussed "primal scream" therapy — releasing emotional blockage through crying and expressing pain. The same source inspired the name Primal Scream (#23 on this list). "Inspired by the writings of American psychologist Arthur Janov."

**Echo and the Bunnymen – Just a Dumb Name They Chose from Several Dumb Options** : Legend says Echo was a drum machine or piece of gear. Not true. The band considered many silly names and chose this one. An alternate option that was seriously on the table: "Gilrol and the Fan Extractors." "The echo in echo in the bunnyman was the name of a drum machine or some other piece of gear. Not true."

**Oingo Boingo – The Mystic Knights of the Oingo Boingo, a Secret Society in Amos and Andy** : Danny Elfman's band was originally called The Mystic Knights of the Oingo Boingo, borrowing the name of a fictional secret society from the old Amos and Andy radio show (circa 1930s). Elfman later moved into film and TV scoring, writing themes including The Simpsons. "Which was a secret society used in the old Amos and Andy Radio Show."

**Siouxsie and the Banshees – Siouxsie Sioux's Indigenous Fascination; 1970 Vincent Price Film** : Siouxsie Sioux was fascinated by indigenous North American cultures, which explains "Siouxsie." "Banshees" came from the 1970 Vincent Price film Cry of the Banshee. Banshees in Irish lore are female spirits whose wailing foretells a death. "Banshees, by the way, are a female spirit from Irish lore whose will foretells that someone is going to die."

**The Police – Stuart Copeland's CIA Father Made "Police" More Subversive** : "Police" was provocative in the punk era. Other names considered included Hypochondria, Grand Larceny, and Assault. "Police" won because drummer Stuart Copeland's father worked for the CIA, making the name feel doubly subversive. Interpol (the band) was subsequently named after the real international police organization headquartered in Lyon, France. "Police worn because drummer Stuart Copeland's father was in the CIA."

**Tool – From a 1994 Interview; Possibly Just Short for "Toolshed"** : The best explanation comes from a 1994 drummer Danny Carey interview stating the band conceived of themselves as a tool for understanding the (non-existent) science of lacrimology — the study of tears. It is more likely just a shortened form of their original name, Toolshed. "The band has always loved to twist the truth."

**Metallica – Ron Quintana Chose "Metal Mania" Instead; Left "Metallica" for Lars** : The name was conceived by Lars Ulrich's friend Ron Quintana, who was starting a fanzine and had narrowed his choices to "Metal Mania" and "Metallica." He chose Metal Mania. Lars kept Metallica for his new band. "He chose Metal Mania in the end for his fan scene, leaving Metallica for Lars' new group."

**Econoline Crush – A Ford Econoline Van Jammed Full of Band and Equipment** : Ford manufactured a van called the Econoline. Being crammed with your band and all your equipment into one of these vans — the classic touring band experience — was an Econoline Crush. "If you and your equipment is jammed into that van, it's an Econoline Crush."

**Silverchair – Named After a C.S. Lewis Novel in the Chronicles of Narnia** : "The Silver Chair" is the title of a novel in C.S. Lewis's Chronicles of Narnia series. The band named themselves after it. "The name Silver Chair is in homage to the Chronicles of Narnia writer, CS Lewis."

**Silversun Pickups – From a Silver Lake Liquor Store and Late-Night Booze Runs** : From the Silver Lake area of Los Angeles. There is a liquor store called Silver Sun, and a friend of the band made frequent late-night booze runs he called "Silver Sun pickups." "There's a liquor store called Silver Sun and a friend would often make late night booze runs."

**The Dead Milkmen – From a Tony Morrison Novel ("Song of Solomon")** : There is a character in Toni Morrison's novel Song of Solomon called "Milkman Dead." The band followed the Dead Kennedys model of pairing "Dead" with an unexpected noun, but the specific choice is literary. "There's a character in a Tony Morrison novel called Song of Solomon called Milkman Dead."

**Orgy – Named for an Encounter with a Drag Queen That Embodied Their Mix of Influences** : Inspired by an encounter with a drag queen that the band felt encapsulated their sound — an orgy of multiple influences coming together. "It was inspired by some kind of encounter with a drag queen."

**Minus the Bear – From a Story: A Bear-Filled Date That Was "Like BJ and the Bear, Minus the Bear"** : Stems from a friend's description of a date: "Remember the old TV series BJ and the Bear? It was like that, minus the bear." "Remember the old TV series BJ in the bear? It was like that minus the bear."

**Car Seat Headrest – Singer Will Toledo Rehearsed Vocals in the Back Seat of the Family Car** : At 17, Will Toledo needed a quiet, soundproofed space to rehearse. The best available was the back seat of the family car, staring at the front headrests. "He rehearsed his vocals in the most quiet and most soundproofed place he knew, the back seat of the family car."

**King Gizzard and the Lizard Wizard – Compromise Between "Gizzard Gizzard" and a Jim Morrison Reference** : Frontman Stu Mackenzie wanted to call the band "Gizzard Gizzard." Another member wanted to honor the Lizard King, Jim Morrison. A compromise (assisted by drinks and spliffs) produced King Gizzard and the Lizard Wizard. "And so I guess there were drinks and slifts."

**Rainbow Butt Monkeys (later Finger Eleven) – Joke Name for a High School Band That Got a Record Deal** : Named as a joke because no one expected the high school band to amount to anything. After signing to a label and releasing four EPs and an album under that name, they changed to Finger Eleven — but Rainbow Butt Monkeys artifacts remain in existence. "They picked it as a joke because there was no way their high school band would amount to anything."

**Naked Toddler (later Creed) – Creed's Original Name** : Before becoming Creed, the band was called Naked Toddler. "Naked Toddler. That was what Creed was called in the beginning."

**Shrinky Dinks (later Sugar Ray) – Sugar Ray's Original Name** : Sugar Ray was originally called Shrinky Dinks. "Number 42, Shrinky Dinks. They're now Sugar Ray."

**The Young Aborigines (later Beastie Boys) – Beastie Boys' Original Name** : The Beastie Boys were originally called The Young Aborigines. "The young Aborigines, who became the Beastie Boys."

**The Obelisk / Easy Cure (later The Cure) – The Cure's Pre-History** : The Cure were first called The Obelisk, then the Easy Cure, before finally settling on The Cure. "They turned into the easy cure and finally just the cure."

**On a Friday (later Radiohead) – Named for the Only Day They Could All Rehearse** : The name On a Friday was chosen because Friday was the only day all members could meet to rehearse. The band renamed themselves Radiohead after a Talking Heads song from their True Stories album (1986). "On a Friday. The name chosen by the soon to be Radiohead because that was the only day on which they could all get together and rehearse."

**Hitler's Underpants (later Orchestral Manoeuvres in the Dark / OMD) – The Most Deliberately Pretentious Rename in Rock** : OMD's precursor was originally called Hitler's Underpants. They renamed themselves to "the most pretentious name they could think of" — Orchestral Manoeuvres in the Dark — acknowledging that it too is dumb, but intentionally so. "Their original name was Hitler's underpants."

**The Slits – Self-Explanatory All-Female Punk Band Name** : The all-female punk band The Slits chose a name whose anatomical connotation was clearly intentional. "I'll leave you to figure that one out on your own."

**F**ked Up – Deliberately Offensive Name for a Band Planned to Last 10 Shows** : The group was deliberately formed with members expected not to get along, intending to play 10 shows and break up — hence the name. Instead, they recorded many albums, toured up to 150 shows per year, opened for the Foo Fighters, and won the 2009 Polaris Music Prize. They performed at a festival called the Festival of F Bands, held in a town called Fing, Austria, alongside fellow Canadians Holy F**k, Oakland's F, and Britain's F Buttons. "The situation is definitely F'd up, especially considering that the joke has gone much, much further than anyone expected."

**Butthole Surfers – Accidental Stage Introduction of a Song Title as a Band Name (1984)** : The stage introducer confused the band's name with the title of one of their songs at a 1984 gig. No one can now remember what name they were actually using. Previous names included The Dick Clark Five, Nine Centimeter Worm Makes Its Own Food, The Vodka Family Winstons, Ashtray Babyheads, Eadnzer's Gay, Zip Code Rapists, and several names involving Fred Astaire's anatomy. The Butthole Surfers name stuck because the gig went well, and they eventually had alt-rock radio hits. "Against all odds, they not only survived with that name, but had a couple of alt-rock radio hits."

**Wet Leg – Random Emoji Combination on a Keyboard** : Rhian Teasdale and Hester Chambers found the name by randomly combining emojis together on a keyboard. "Re and T-Stale and Hester Chambers found the name by combining random emojis together on a keyboard."

**The The – Deliberately Hardest Band Name to Search Online; Punk Trend of "The" Bands** : Singer Matt Johnson was advised by friend Keith that in the late 1970s, all new punk bands were "The [something]." So the most trendy and punk name would be "The The" — the ultimate example of the genre's naming convention. It was also nearly impossible to search for online when early search engines ignored definite articles. "What could be more trendy in punk than calling your group the the?"

**!!! (Chk Chk Chk) – From a 1980 South African Film and the Juǀʼhoan Click Language** : Named after the 1980 film The Gods Must Be Crazy, which featured a character speaking Juǀʼhoan, an indigenous South African language that uses mouth clicks. In written form, a click is represented by an exclamation mark. The character's name was pronounced approximately "chk chk chk." "You pronounce the man's name as chick, chick, chick."

**Sublime's Slightly Stoopid Connection – Bradley Nowell Signed Them While They Were in High School** : Slightly Stoopid is a very rock/reggae/ska/punk band from San Diego. Bradley Nowell of Sublime liked them so much he signed them to his label while they were still in high school. In a stunt, they pressed a record not on vinyl but on hash resin — it took $6,000 worth of bubble hash, $1,000 to press, and the record sounded terrible. The failed hash record was simply crushed up and smoked. "Because once it didn't work, you just crush it up and smoke it."
