## albums
**Hail to the Thief (2003)** : This album's touring cycle concluded at the end of 2004, marking a period of transition for the band. The phrase "Burn the Witch" was present on this album, indicating that the band was working on a song with that title as early as 2003. "brought us to the end of the touring cycle for the Hail to the Thief album."
**OK Computer (1997)** : Released a decade before "In Rainbows," this album is hypothetically designated as "zero one" in a conceptual "Theory of Ten" mega album. If combined with "In Rainbows," it is suggested to alternate tracks starting with "Airbag" and cross-fading for ten seconds between songs. "In Rainbows came out ten years after Ok Computer."
**In Rainbows (2007)** : The album was completed by July 2007, amidst the band's dispute with EMI. Radiohead decided to retain full ownership, including publishing, masters, and copyright. It was secretly announced on October 1, 2007, by guitarist Jonny Greenwood on their website Dead Air Space, stating it would be released in 10 days. The album became available on October 10, 2007, at inrainbows.com, featuring a unique "pay-what-you-want" model where fans determined the price, only paying a mandatory credit card processing fee of 45 pence (about 90 cents USD). The songs were 160 kbps, DRM-free, and distributed via a private network to handle anticipated heavy traffic. While many downloaded it for free, only two out of five visitors paid more than the minimum fee, with an average payment of four pounds (about eight dollars). Despite this, the band netted millions in pure profits, and in December 2007, a premium disc box was released for 40 pounds, including the CD, a bonus CD, digital photos, artwork, and 180-gram vinyl. The album's release was tied to a "Theory of Ten" with various numerical coincidences. It generated approximately £3 million in pure profits and sold 3 million copies across all formats, becoming the biggest-selling vinyl record of 2008. "The new album is finished, and it's coming out in 10 days. We called it In Rainbows."
**The King of Limbs (2011)** : This album, the band's eighth, was conceived primarily in a studio setting, contrasting with the road-tested approach of its predecessor, "In Rainbows." Recording spanned almost two years, largely taking place in Los Angeles, with rumors of a temporary studio set up at actress Drew Barrymore's home, which was cryptically acknowledged in the liner notes. The album featured extensive use of samples, loops, ambient sounds, and complex edits, centering its sound around the rhythm section of bassist Colin Greenwood and drummer Phil Selway, and even incorporated turntables with vinyl emulation software. Announced on February 14, 2011, on the band's website, it was made available digitally five days later, with MP3s priced at $9 US and WAV files at $14 US; physical versions were released in May. At 37 minutes, it was their shortest album. Its release was accompanied by the "Newspaper edition," a collaboration with Stanley Donwood, featuring a newspaper-like insert designed to fade in sunlight as a metaphor for natural decay, and distributed as a free paper called "Universal Sigh" at indie record stores globally on March 28, 2011, containing poetry, lyrics, artwork, and short stories. "At 37 minutes, this was the shortest radiohead album to date."
**A Moon-Shaped Pool (2016)** : Recording for this album began in September 2014, two years after the tragic stage collapse in Toronto. The songs are notably presented in alphabetical order. Its release followed a straightforward, coordinated digital strategy through the band's website, iTunes, and, despite Thom Yorke's earlier reservations, streaming music sites, with a special edition physical version also made available. There were no interviews or advance road-testing of the songs; promotional hints came primarily through the band's website and social media, including commissioned short vignettes on Instagram. A notable controversy occurred on June 17, 2016, the day of the physical release, when a listening session at an indie record store in Istanbul, Turkey, was attacked by a gang who objected to music and beer being present during Ramadan, though fortunately no one was hurt. "Not a lot of razzle d'azza with this one, unless you count the fact that the songs are presented in alphabetical order."

## curiosities
**EMI Contract Fulfillment (Early 2005)** : By early 2005, Radiohead had fulfilled their contractual obligations to EMI Records, delivering six studio albums, a live album, half a dozen EPs, and at least four video releases, leading them to seek an exit from the label. "They had fulfilled their end of the bargain to EMI records, six studio albums, a live album, half a dozen EPs, along with at least four video releases."
**Options for Post-EMI Future (Early 2005)** : After fulfilling their contract with EMI, Radiohead considered three main options: negotiating a new, favorable deal with the struggling EMI; signing with another major label despite the industry's decline due to piracy and the internet; or embarking on an unprecedented path to become fully independent artists. "What were their options? Option A was to negotiate a killer deal with EMI... Option B. Signed with another major label... But then there was also option C. Go it alone and redefine what it would mean to be an independent artist."
**Terra Firma Acquisition of EMI (2005)** : EMI Records was purchased for nearly $4 billion by Terra Firma, a private equity firm led by Guy Hans, who had no prior experience in running a record label. This acquisition led to strained relations with artists like Radiohead due to Hans's business-centric approach and cost-cutting measures. "the band's relationship with EMI records had turned poisonous, following its purchase for nearly $4 billion by a company called Terra firma."
**Artist Control vs. Label Business Model** : Radiohead guitarist Ed O'Brien expressed the band's disillusionment with EMI's new ownership under Guy Hans and Terra Firma, citing Hans's lack of understanding of the music industry and his refusal to meet the band's demands for a new deal. This highlighted the fundamental clash between a creative entity and a strictly profit-driven corporate model. "Here's a quote from guitarist, Ed O'Brien. EMI is in a state of flux. It's been taken over by somebody who's never owned a record company before, Guy Hans and Terra firma. And they don't realize what they're dealing with."
**EMI's Cost-Cutting Measures** : Terra Firma, upon acquiring EMI, implemented severe belt-tightening measures, which included cutting advances to both new and established artists. While they did identify and eliminate extravagant expenses like flowers for album release parties, daily flown-in Japanese fish for sushi bars, and hundreds of dollars spent on candles, the impact on artist compensation and creative freedom was significant and poorly received. "But the belt tightening also continued to advance his pay to both new and established artists on the label. Artists were told that they had to work harder, and maybe for less."
**Advance and Copyright Deadlock with EMI** : Radiohead, after having sold 25 million records and delivered tens of millions in revenue to EMI since 1992, requested an advance of £10 million for a new contract, but EMI would not offer more than £3 million. Crucially, EMI also refused to grant Radiohead any control over their back catalog's copyright, insisting on retaining rights for the standard 50 years, leading to an insurmountable deadlock. "Radiohead also wanted some measure of control over their back catalog and how that music would be exploited going forward. But EMI would not budge."
**Radical Independent Experiment (2007)** : After their impasse with EMI, Radiohead decided to go "totally, utterly, 100% independent," embarking on a daring experiment to release music digitally, without a traditional label. This allowed them to retain full ownership of their work (publishing, masters, copyright), a level of control typically unattainable under label deals, and enabled them to explore innovative distribution models like the "pay-what-you-want" approach for "In Rainbows." "Radiohead went totally, utterly, 100% independent."
**Frustration with Music Leaks** : Radiohead was deeply annoyed by the pervasive issue of music leaks, especially in the internet era, where a single file from a hard drive could undermine meticulously planned marketing campaigns and result in significant lost revenue. Their last four albums, plus Thom Yorke's solo record, had all leaked prematurely, sometimes months in advance, prompting the band to consider a radical business model to prevent such occurrences. "In fact, the last four Radiohead albums, plus Tom York's solo record somehow made it into the wild before they were supposed to, sometimes weeks before, and sometimes months before, which really ticked them off."
**Self-Leak Strategy for In Rainbows (2007)** : To counter unauthorized leaks and maintain artistic integrity, Radiohead secretly decided to "leak their own album" (In Rainbows) by releasing it directly to fans online through a pay-what-you-want model. This innovative approach allowed them to control the terms of release and bypass traditional distribution challenges. "As Tom York said to Wired Magazine, we are going to leak our own album."
**DRM-Free Music Release (2007)** : When "In Rainbows" was released digitally, the songs were ripped at 160 kilobytes per second and were "completely free of any digital locks." This meant that once downloaded, fans had unrestricted freedom to trade, move, or share the songs across devices, a significant and groundbreaking stance in 2007. "All songs were ripped at 160 kilobytes per second, and were completely free of any digital locks."
**Internet Unprofitability of In Rainbows (2007)** : Despite the success of the "In Rainbows" pay-what-you-want model, it was later revealed that 60% of the traffic to inrainbows.com resulted in no profit for the band beyond the credit card processing fees, as users either paid only the minimum or downloaded the album from torrent sites after it was in the wild. "That means 60% of the traffic to in rainbows.com was unprofitable for everybody but the credit card companies."
**"Theory of Ten" for In Rainbows** : The release of "In Rainbows" was surrounded by a playful "Theory of Ten," noting its release ten years after "OK Computer," on the 10th day of the 10th month of 2007, announced ten days after its initial mention, and following ten cryptic messages on the band's website using the Roman numeral 'X'. Even the album's working title, "Zero's and One's," mirrored the number ten. "In Rainbows came out ten years after Ok Computer. It was released on the 10th day of the 10th month of 2007. It was released ten days after was announced, and following ten cryptic messages posted on the band's website."
**"OK Computer" and "In Rainbows" Mega Album Theory** : A theory suggests that "OK Computer" (designated as 01) and "In Rainbows" (designated as 10) can be combined into one "mega album" by creating a playlist that alternates tracks from each album, with a ten-second cross-fade between songs. This sequence, starting with "Airbag" from "OK Computer" and "15 Step" from "In Rainbows," is said to flow remarkably well. "If we designate Ok Computer as zero one, and in Rainbows as one zero, the two albums can be combined to create one mega album."
**Post-In Rainbows Public Silence (2008-2010)** : After the "In Rainbows" experiment, Radiohead largely withdrew from the public eye for a couple of years, preferring to communicate through social media and their websites, with no live gigs for over a year, and band members exploring individual projects. During this time, EMI, asserting its rights, released a box set of Radiohead's previous six albums and a greatest hits CD. "There were no Radiohead gigs for over a year. And they also decided to explore various things as individuals."
**The King of Limbs Recording Location Rumors (Early 2010)** : Most of "The King of Limbs" was recorded in Los Angeles over nearly two years, with rumors suggesting the band set up a studio at actress Drew Barrymore's home in the Hollywood Hills in early 2010. While unconfirmed, the album's liner notes included an "ecliptic thank you," and social media showed pictures and celebrity party rumors from the house. "Most of the recording seems to have been done in Los Angeles, with rumors saying that they'd set up at the home of actress Drew Barrymore."
**The King of Limbs - Stanley Donwood's Newspaper Edition (March 28, 2011)** : As part of the release for "The King of Limbs," longtime collaborator Stanley Donwood created the "Newspaper edition." This included a newspaper-like insert designed to fade when exposed to sunlight, serving as a metaphor for the natural decay of living things. This was published under the name "Universal Sigh" and was distributed as a free paper at indie record stores worldwide on March 28, 2011, filled with poetry, lyrics, artwork, and short stories. "There was something called the Newspaper edition created by longtime artwork collaborator Stanley Donwood. It came with a newspaper like Insert, which was designed to fade when exposed to sunlight, thereby creating a metaphor for the natural decay of living things or something of that sort."
**Toronto Stage Collapse Tragedy (June 16, 2012)** : A catastrophic stage collapse occurred at Downsview Park in Toronto on June 16, 2012, shortly before a Radiohead concert. A temporary stage structure, supporting 7,000 kilos of gear, gave way, killing 33-year-old drum tech Scott Johnson, who was struck by a 2,270-kilo video monitor, and injuring three other crew members. The band's light show and most of their musical gear were destroyed. If the crew had been on schedule, Radiohead would have been sound-checking at the time of the collapse. "a part of the stage constructed for that night's gig, a structured laden with 7,000 kilos of gear suddenly gave way, plummeting to the ground, and killing 33-year-old drum tech Scott Johnson"
**Aftermath of Toronto Stage Collapse** : The Toronto show was canceled, and Scott Johnson's death devastated everyone involved. The incident led to a prolonged and unresolved legal process, including investigations, lawsuits, counter-suits, charges against Live Nation, Optics Staging and Services, and engineer Dominic Caligari (all pleaded not guilty), two criminal trials, and a mistrial. As of the end of 2018, no one had been held accountable, and no inquest had been conducted. Scott Johnson's father, Ken Johnson, an expert in scaffolding health and safety, had "no doubt why the accident happened." "And then came the long, farcical train of investigations, lawsuits, counter-suits, charges, switch judges, legal bureaucracies, two criminal trials, and in the end, a mistrial that left everything unresolved."
**Impact of Collapse on Touring and Band Morale** : The stage collapse forced the cancellation of Radiohead's entire subsequent European tour, and the band seriously contemplated quitting touring altogether. This led to a "long, painful hiatus" before they eventually reconsidered, with band members focusing on various side projects during this period. "The entire European tour, which was to follow the Toronto date, was canceled. And the band seriously thought, well, that's it. We're done. We're never touring again."
**James Bond "Spectre" Theme Rejection** : Radiohead composed a theme song for the 2015 James Bond film "Spectre," but it was rejected by the producers for being "too dark." The role was ultimately given to Sam Smith's "Writing's on the Wall," which went on to win an Academy Award and a Golden Globe. Radiohead later released their rejected song, also titled "Spectre," digitally and as a B-side. "There was a brief time when it looked like Radiohead might get to perform the theme song for the James Bond movie Specter, but the producer said it was too dark and ended up going with a Sam Smith song called The Riddings on the Wall"
**A Moon-Shaped Pool's Low-Key Promotion** : The release of "A Moon-Shaped Pool" was marked by a minimalist promotional approach: there were no interviews, and none of the songs had been road-tested in advance. The only hints came through the band's website and social media accounts like Instagram, where they commissioned and released short vignettes. "There were zero interviews for the album. None of the songs were road tested in advance. The only hints came to the band's website and social media accounts like Instagram"
**Istanbul Listening Session Attack (June 17, 2016)** : On June 17, 2016, the day "A Moon-Shaped Pool" was physically released, an organized listening session at an indie record store in Istanbul, Turkey, was attacked by a gang. The attackers reportedly believed the shop should not have been playing music or serving beer during Ramadan. Fortunately, no one was injured during the incident. "A series of listening sessions had been organized at indie record stores around the world, and a shop in Istanbul, Turkey, was attacked by a gang who somehow believed that the shop shouldn't be playing music or serving beer during Ramadan."
**Tragedy Surrounding "Burn the Witch" Recording** : On the very day Radiohead recorded the string section for the song "Burn the Witch," the father of their longtime producer, Nigel Godrich, passed away. "on the day Radiohead recorded the string section, the father of longtime Radiohead producer Nigel Goddard's died."
**Thom Yorke's Personal Tragedy (2016)** : Thom Yorke and his partner, Rachel, separated after 16 years. A few months after the release of "A Moon-Shaped Pool" in December 2016, Rachel tragically died of cancer. "Another sad thing, Tom York and his partner Rachel separated after 16 years, and then she died of cancer that December, a few months after the album came out."
**"Burn the Witch" Video Copyright Accusation** : Radiohead faced accusations of copyright infringement for the distinctive style in which the music video for their song "Burn the Witch" was shot. "And then Radiohead was accused of breach of copyright for the style in which the Burn the Witch video was shot."
**Band Longevity and Creative Freedom** : Radiohead has maintained an unchanged lineup since the late 1980s, working with the same producer and art director, and retaining many crew members for decades. The band's members pursuing individual side projects is seen as a way for them to exercise their own creative urges, which contributes to the band's longevity and gives fans hope for their continued collaboration. "But they haven't had a single lineup change since the late 1980s. Same producer, same art director. Many of their crew have been around for decades."

# label - EMI Records

## curiosities
**Company Status and Internet Impact (2005)** : In 2005, EMI Records was identified as the smallest and weakest among the major labels, struggling significantly under the disruptive influence of the internet and perceived mismanagement, which seemed intent on "driving the label into the ground." "They were the smallest and the weakest of the major labels. The internet was killing the company and management didn't seem to have a clue."
**Acquisition by Terra Firma (2005)** : EMI was acquired for nearly $4 billion by Terra Firma, a private equity firm led by Guy Hans, who had no previous experience running a record label. Hans believed the label could be profitable if managed "like any other company," a philosophy that led to significant cost-cutting measures. "It's been taken over by somebody who's never owned a record company before, Guy Hans and Terra firma."
**Artist Compensation Cuts (2005)** : Under Terra Firma's ownership, EMI implemented strict cost-cutting measures, including reductions in advances paid to both new and established artists. Artists were told they would need to "work harder, and maybe for less," a policy that was poorly received by the label's creative roster. "But the belt tightening also continued to advance his pay to both new and established artists on the label. Artists were told that they had to work harder, and maybe for less."
**Copyright Stance on Back Catalog** : EMI staunchly refused to concede any portion of copyright control over Radiohead's back catalog. Under the copyright law of the day, the label insisted on maintaining control of their six Radiohead albums for the standard 50 years, contributing to an irreconcilable deadlock with the band. "Under the copyright law of the day, they insisted on maintaining control of those six radiohead albums for the standard 50 years. EMI would not hand over even a portion of the copyright to Radiohead."
**Post-Radiohead Contract Expiration Actions** : After Radiohead departed to become independent, EMI asserted its contractual rights by releasing both a box set comprising all six of Radiohead's albums recorded before "In Rainbows," as well as a greatest hits CD. # curiosity

**Traditional Record Deal Duration** : Historically, a first record deal typically spans around seven albums, a structure designed to provide both security for the artist, allowing them time to develop and mature their sound over several releases, and an opportunity for the record label to see a return on their significant upfront investment. "Traditionally, when you sign your first record deal, it's usually for around seven albums."
**Internet Disruption of Recording Industry (Mid-2000s)** : In the mid-2000s, the entire recording industry faced a significant downturn, described as "melting down," primarily due to rampant music piracy and the broader disruptive impact of the internet on traditional sales and distribution models. "when it looked like the entire recording industry was melting down. Again, thanks to rampant piracy and the disruption brought about by the internet."
**Creativity Versus Schedule** : The nature of creative work, particularly in music, defies rigid scheduling; true creativity and inspiration are spontaneous, contrasting sharply with the predictable, assembly-line production model of manufacturing. "You cannot be brilliant on a schedule. Creativity and inspiration happens when it happens."
**Pre-Internet Music Leaks** : In earlier times, before widespread internet access, the creation, manufacturing, and distribution of records were tightly controlled, making it relatively easy to prevent leaks. Marketing, press, and tour schedules could be carefully synchronized for maximum impact, and any rare leaks, usually from inside jobs at studios or pressing plants, were manageable. "Now, in olden times, when the creation, manufacturing, and distribution processes were so tightly controlled, it was easy to keep a record under wraps until the label let it out."
**Internet-Era Music Leaks** : The advent of the internet drastically changed the landscape of music leaks, where a single digital file from a hard drive could instantly put an entire record into the hands of fans, devastating carefully crafted marketing plans and causing substantial lost revenue for artists and labels. "One air at file from a hard drive and suddenly the whole record was in the hands of fans and all those carefully crafted marketing plans were shot to hell."
**Major Bands Burned by Unauthorized Leaks** : Numerous prominent bands, including Korn, U2, Snow Patrol, Metallica, Linkin Park, and Radiohead, have experienced significant frustration and financial loss due to their albums being leaked online weeks or even months before their official release dates. "Corn, U2, Snow Patrol, Metallica, Lincoln Park, and of course, Radiohead."
**Sam Smith's Oscar and Golden Globe Win (2015)** : Sam Smith's song "Writing's on the Wall," chosen as the theme for the James Bond movie "Spectre" over Radiohead's submission, achieved significant critical acclaim, winning both an Academy Award and a Golden Globe for Best Original Song. "Sam Smith song called The Riddings on the Wall, which, by the way, ended up with both an Academy Award and a Golden Globe for Best Original Song."
