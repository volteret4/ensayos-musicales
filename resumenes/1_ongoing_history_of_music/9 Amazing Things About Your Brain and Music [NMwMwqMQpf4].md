# curiosity

**Music and Dopamine – Neurologically, Sex, Drugs, and Rock and Roll Are the Same Thing** : Deep inside the brain, the amygdala, hippocampus, and cerebellum produce a hormone called dopamine — the body's feel-good chemical. The body produces dopamine during orgasm, during cocaine use, and when hearing a great song that gives you chills and makes you want to dance. Neurologically speaking, sex, drugs, and rock and roll produce the same chemical reward. "Our brains are built to respond to sex drugs and rock and roll."

**MP3s and Dopamine Suppression – Compressed Audio May Reduce Emotional Response to Music** : MP3 compression technology was developed in the 1980s by the Fraunhofer Institute in Germany, shrinking audio files to one-tenth their size by stripping out audio the ear cannot hear. However, when the brain receives an MP3, it apparently detects that audio information has been stripped away and spends a few milliseconds searching for the missing material. This delay suppresses dopamine production slightly. In theory, the hierarchy of dopamine response runs: live performance > vinyl > CD > MP3. The more degraded the playback (poor earbuds, laptop speakers), the less emotional impact the music has. "MP3s cheat you out of getting the maximum emotional impact of music."

**Brain Wave Music – Neural Oscillations Can Be Transposed Into Audible Music; Used to Treat Depression and Anxiety** : Brain activity produces electrical neural oscillations (brain waves): delta, theta, alpha, and beta waves at very low frequencies. Scientists discovered that by transposing these waves up into audible frequency ranges, the resulting sounds can be used therapeutically. Doctors have found that playing a patient's own brain waves back to them appears to reboot brain chemistry in ways that treat insomnia, anxiety, and depression. Classically trained cellist Katinka Kleijn demonstrated the technique by playing her own EEG brain waves live alongside her cello. "The music of your own brain can be used to treat certain neurological and psychological conditions."

**Music Memories Are Stored Separately from All Other Memories** : In 2005, a 71-year-old professional classical musician suffered severe meningitis that destroyed parts of his brain, leaving him with total retrograde amnesia — his entire life was erased — and an inability to form new memories. Yet he could still play complicated pieces learned decades earlier, and could sight-read and memorize new music. In another case, an Ottawa woman named Allison Wojciechowska survived surgery to remove a golf ball-sized brain aneurysm but lost the ability to speak, concentrate, read, or write. However, the moment she was wheeled to a piano, she could play. Music therapists used musical phrases to reconstruct her spoken language — she eventually regained the ability to speak in three languages. "Our music memories are separate and isolated from our day-to-day memories."

**Songs Are Deconstructed and Stored in Multiple Brain Locations** : When music enters the brain, the auditory cortex processes the entire signal as one piece. It is then passed to the mid-superior temporal sulcus, which splits the song into music and lyrics. Music is routed to the anterior superior sulcus; lyrics are routed to the brain's speech centers, where word recognition, meaning, and context must all be processed. This creates two separate databases — music and lyrics — in two different brain regions. This explains why most people pay more attention to a song's music than its words, and why singing through a song is often the only way to remember its lyrics. "Processing the lyrics takes more time, and depending on the song, we just might not be up to it."

**Earworms – Involuntary Musical Imagery; Brain Fills in Silence; Best Cure Is a Sudoku** : When a song cuts out, the brain automatically continues it for a few seconds using stored musical memory. Sometimes a snippet catches in a loop — the neurological phenomenon known as an earworm. Almost 100% of the population experiences earworms. Key facts: they last longer and are more irritating for women; more than 75% of earworms involve lyrics; people with OCD are more susceptible; most loops are no longer than 30 seconds (the approximate buffer size of auditory cortex RAM). For some people, earworms are debilitating, persisting for months or years; only strong OCD medication helps. For most people, doing a sudoku puzzle appears to be an effective cure — the kind of brain processing required flushes the audio cache of the auditory cortex. "The best cure for an earworm seems to be to do a sudoku."

**Music Can Assist Brain Surgery – Patients Play Their Instrument While Surgeons Operate** : For certain neurological procedures, patients are awoken from anesthetic and asked to play a musical instrument while surgeons work inside the skull. If the surgeons disturb an area affecting the patient's playing, they know to find a different route. A notable example: Brad Carter, whose Parkinson's disease tremors had left him unable to play guitar, had deep brain stimulation electrodes installed while fully awake and playing guitar, singing, with his skull screwed into a stabilizing frame. The surgery restored his ability to play. "Music can assist with brain surgery."

**Amusia – Up to 4% of Humans Cannot Experience Music as Pleasurable Sound** : Amusia is a neurological condition, either congenital or resulting from brain injury, that prevents the person from processing pitch and music in a pleasurable way. Those with amusia perceive music as meaningless or unpleasant noise — approximately as distressing as fingernails on a chalkboard. They cannot hum a song, tell a major chord from random piano smashing, or detect when a guitar is out of tune. Melody is meaningless to them. It is estimated that up to 4% of humans have some form of amusia. In tonal languages like Mandarin, where pitch changes the meaning of words, amusia creates additional communication difficulties. "Basically amusia is an inability to hear music as pleasurable sound."

**Synesthesia – Music Experienced as Colors; Affects Kanye West, Stevie Wonder, Tori Amos, Eddie Van Halen, and Others** : Synesthesia is a neurological condition where the senses overlap. Musical synesthesia — where notes, chords, and combinations of sounds are perceived as specific colors — has been reported by Kanye West, Stevie Wonder (who is also totally blind), Tori Amos, Billy Joel, Patrick Stump of Fall Out Boy, Eddie Van Halen, and Pharrell Williams. Growing up with synesthesia, these artists generally assumed everyone experienced music this way. Singer Sam Endicott of The Bravery, who also writes songs for Shakira and Christina Aguilera, has credited synesthesia with giving him an unusual range. "They all say that they have music or sound to color synesthesia."
