## albums
**Play (1999) – Pioneering Licensing Strategy** : Moby's fifth studio album, released in 1999, became a landmark by offering all 18 of its songs to advertising agencies, film studios, and TV producers. This unprecedented gamble by his management team aimed to expose his music to a wider audience than traditional touring and radio airplay could provide. The strategy proved immensely successful, with companies like Nordstrom, Volkswagen, and Levi's licensing every single track, leading to widespread recognition for Moby and ultimately selling 12 million copies worldwide. It served as a significant "wake-up call" for the artist community, especially as music piracy surged and physical music sales plummeted in the subsequent years. "Every single track was licensed. Everyone heard about Mobie as a result. And play ended up selling 12 million copies."

## songs
**Porcelain (1999)** : This song from the album "Play" is specifically mentioned as having been licensed to Kahlúa, highlighting the commercial success and broad reach Moby achieved through his innovative licensing approach. The track's use in a liqueur commercial illustrates how artists began to find new revenue streams when physical media sales were declining. "Mobie licensed this track to Kaluah."

## curiosities
**Redefining "Selling Out"** : Moby is credited with being perhaps most responsible for changing the perception of artists licensing their music for commercial purposes, transforming it from a "giant sin" and career-ending insult into an "essential part of any act's income stream." His success with "Play" demonstrated the financial viability and audience reach possible through sync deals, especially as physical sales dropped by 90% and streaming offered little meaningful income for most musicians. This shift made licensing music an aspirational component of an artist's business plan. "After he showed how there was money to be made from licensing music in an industry where physical media was dying, Mobie changed the concept of selling out."

# genre - Indie Rock

## curiosities
**Early 2000s Revival** : The early 2000s witnessed a significant "Indie Rock Revival," a broad cultural movement spanning North America and parts of Europe that brought about a resurgence of "stripped down guitar-based rock." This movement was crucial because, by the end of the 1990s, guitar rock was widely considered "dead," with electronica predicted to dominate music. Bands, many originating from New York (The Strokes, Yeah Yeah Yeahs, Interpol, TV on the Radio), Detroit (The White Stripes), Sweden (The Hives), England (The Libertines), and Scotland (Franz Ferdinand), spearheaded this revival, initially operating underground before their music gained traction on radio, TV, films, and commercials, and at festivals like Coachella and Glastonbury. "This was important because at the end of the 1990s, it did look like guitar rock was dead. It was all about electronica."
**Digital World's Influence** : The Indie Rock Revival of the early 2000s served as one of the earliest and most prominent examples of how the nascent "new digital world" could effectively generate and sustain a novel musical movement. The ease of illegal file-sharing platforms like Napster enabled listeners to effortlessly discover and access new bands, circumventing traditional record store searches and contributing to the genre's organic rise and widespread appeal. "The indie rock revival was one of the very first examples of how the new digital world could produce and sustain a brand new musical movement and trend."

# curiosity

**Shifting Perceptions of "Selling Out" (Post-2000)** : Before 2000, an artist licensing their music for commercial products or services was widely considered a "giant sin," often leading to career-ending scorn and ridicule. However, the early 21st century saw a dramatic shift where licensing became an "essential part of any act's income stream." This change was driven by the decline in physical music sales (down 90% in 25 years) and the minimal income generated from streaming for most artists, making sync deals a crucial way to pay rent and survive. The financial environment for artists transformed dramatically, making licensing an aspiration rather than an insult. "Selling out isn't a bad thing anymore. In fact, it's something to aspire to."
**Music Piracy and Its Impact (Early 2000s)** : The early 2000s witnessed an explosion of music piracy, profoundly impacting the music industry, particularly accelerating the cratering of physical music sales. This phenomenon, alongside the rise of digital access to music, further highlighted the need for artists and the industry to find alternative revenue streams, such as music licensing, as demonstrated by Moby's success with "Play." "music piracy exploded and physical sales cratered."
**Napster and Digital Music Discovery (Early 2000s)** : The advent of Napster and illegal file-sharing platforms fundamentally altered how music was discovered and accessed in the early 2000s. It made it "super easy" for listeners to explore new bands without the need for traditional record store searches, playing a significant role in the proliferation of new musical movements like the indie rock revival. Napster exemplified how the new digital world could both disrupt traditional models and foster new musical trends. "Suddenly, it was super easy to check out some band that you made her that was supposedly really cool. No board dredging around record stores looking for their stuff."
**Mainstream Music Becoming a Niche (Post-2000)** : The concept of mainstream music, once a pervasive cultural force where "everyone generally listened to the same thing" and "big stars were absolutely ubiquitous," has diminished significantly since 2000 with the rise of the internet. Previously, a limited number of gatekeepers—record labels, record stores, radio, TV video channels, and music magazines—filtered music, creating a "mono culture" and a common music vocabulary. With the internet, consumers gained "free and unfettered access" to vast amounts of music, leading to the collapse of traditional gatekeeper business models (e.g., declining physical sales, music magazine revenue). Music culture fragmented into "disconnected or barely connected, self-organizing music communities," making mainstream appeal just "another of many musical avenues and genres" without special status. "A common mainstream music culture we had before 2000 is gone."
**Decline of Traditional Music Gatekeepers (Post-2000)** : The rise of the internet around 2000 led to a rapid decline in the power of traditional cultural gatekeepers in the music industry. Record labels saw their century-old business model of selling physical plastic collapse. Record stores experienced reduced foot traffic, leading to the closure of major international chains. Music magazines suffered a collapse in revenue as labels cut advertising and consumers sought information online. YouTube effectively "killed off music videos on television." Only radio, though still powerful, popular, and profitable, now shares its dominance with the digital world. "The power, the traditional cultural gatekeepers enjoyed before the rise of the internet, waned quickly."
**Live Nation and Ticketmaster Merger (2010)** : Live Nation, which evolved from SFX Entertainment (the largest concert promoter globally by 2000, later Clear Channel Entertainment's spin-off in 2005), acquired Ticketmaster in 2010. Ticketmaster, founded in Phoenix in 1976 and aggressively expanded under CEO Fred Rosen in 1982 by adding service charges and securing exclusive venue agreements, had become a publicly traded company by 2008. Despite regulatory concerns, the merger was approved, creating a dominant force in the live venue world. In 2024, the combined entity ran 11,203 events, sold over 118 million tickets, and owned/managed over 200 venues, effectively shaping ticket purchasing, event promotion, and the overall concert experience worldwide. "The decision was made to buy ticket master. There were regulatory concerns about this, but the merger was allowed by all the authorities. And this made the combo of Live Nation and ticket master the dominant players in all of the live venue world."
**Antitrust Concerns Regarding Live Nation/Ticketmaster** : The merger of Live Nation and Ticketmaster in 2010 created a behemoth in the live music industry, leading to ongoing debates about whether the combined entity is "too big and too powerful" and if its practices constitute an antitrust situation. These discussions have persisted for years, with calls for government intervention to potentially break up the company. "Is the Live Nation Ticketmaster combo too big and too powerful? Are there practices that add up to an antitrust situation? That's been debated for years, and unless government step in to break things up, that debate will continue."
**Ubiquity of Cell Phones at Concerts (Post-2007)** : Before the advent of smartphones around 2007, concert tickets often carried warnings against cameras and recording devices, reflecting a strict industry stance against bootlegging and unauthorized photography. However, with the rise of smartphones, enforcing these rules became a "losing battle," leading promoters and artists to generally accept or adapt to their presence. Reactions vary from making concerts "Instagramable and TikTok Ready" (e.g., Coldplay) to strict crackdowns by artists who demand fans be "in the moment" (e.g., Green Day) or even requiring phones to be locked in bags (e.g., Jack White). This shift has also created a "generational divide" between younger fans who integrate phones into the concert experience and older attendees who find it disruptive, becoming part of a larger conversation about technology in public spaces. "But with the rise of smartphones after about 2007, this became a losing battle."
