# curiosity
**Ancient Origins of Tattoos** : Tattoos have a history stretching back thousands of years, with evidence found on mummified remains. Examples include Otsul, discovered in the Swiss Alps after a glacier melted, who had 61 tattoos and lived over 5,000 years ago. Egyptian mummies, Pacific Islanders, ancient African communities, bodies from Iron Age Britain, early Japanese societies, and indigenous peoples of North and South America all engaged in body art. "Archaeologists have found mummified remains that are thousands and thousands of years old that sport tattoos. There's a guy named Otsul. He was found in the Swiss Elps when a glacier melted. He'd been there for over 5,000 years and the dude had 61 tattoos, Egyptian mummies, Pacific Islanders, members of ancient African communities, bodies dating to Iron Age Britain, early Japanese societies, and the indigenous peoples of North and South America have all engaged in this kind of body art."
**Historical Uses of Tattoos** : Historically, tattoos have served various identification purposes, including marking prisoners and slaves. They also functioned to display religious connections and associations with groups such as armies, navies, bikers, and criminal gangs. "Tattoos have also been used to identify prisoners and slaves to display religious connections and associations with armies, navies, bikers, and criminal gangs."
**Shifting Stigma of Tattoos** : For many years, tattoos carried a significant stigma, often associated with "deviance and weirdos," or with specific groups like sailors, gangs, and the mafia. This perception began to change dramatically over the last 60 years, particularly since the early 21st century, with tattoos moving into the mainstream. "But even with all this, many people still consider tattoos to carry some kind of stigma, only deviance and weirdos get tattoos. But that's changed a lot in the last 60 years, especially since the beginning of the 21st century. Tattoos have long gone mainstream."
**Mainstream Acceptance and Normalization** : Tattoos have become so mainstream that in some social circles, the absence of tattoos can make one an "outsider" or "weirdo." This shift occurred notably in the early 2000s, around 2001-2002, when tattoo shops became widely accessible and common, akin to Starbucks. "In fact, in some circles, if you don't have any ink, you're the outsider in Weirdo."
**Influence of Media on Tattoo Popularity** : The rise in popularity of MTV, VH1, and other video channels played a crucial role in making tattoos mainstream. Viewers saw musicians and bands with tattoos, leading fans to emulate their favorite rock stars by getting inked themselves. "But the culture started to change when I suppose MTV and VH1 and another video channels became popular and you saw these guys and bands were tattoos."
**Blackwork Tattoos - History and Characteristics** : Blackwork tattoos are a style with a history dating back thousands of years. Ancient peoples used such body art to signify identity, loyalties, and authority, a designation that evolved into "tribal tattoo." This style entered modern tattoo culture towards the end of the 1980s, influenced by the book *Modern Primitives*. Due to the extensive ink required, Blackwork pieces demand many hours of tattooing and are associated with significant pain. "This is called a Blackwork tattoo and has a history going back thousands of years. Ancient peoples use such body art to promote their identity, their loyalties, and their authority, and it's the designation as a tribal tattoo."
**Pain and Addictive Nature of Tattoos** : While getting a tattoo is painful, particularly in areas like the elbow and knee where bone is close to the surface, this pain can induce an "adrenaline rush" and "endorphin rush." This physiological response contributes to a "dicking element" (addictive quality), making the process pleasurable for many and leading them to seek more tattoos. "There's this adrenaline rush. There's this endorphin rush that you get. That's why I think it's a dicking element because a lot of people get that rush. And the pain has gone and now it's pleasurable. Not feels good."
**Cost and Time Investment of Tattoos** : Tattoos are not cheap; Goldfinger drummer Darren Fyfer estimated spending between $30,000 to $50,000 on his own tattoos. The time spent in the tattoo chair can also be extensive, with Fyfer estimating a cumulative week of tattooing, ranging from one to eight hours per session depending on size and complexity. "They're not cheap. No, they're not. Well over, maybe 50 grand. You're kidding. All over, yeah. Maybe 30, 35. Okay. And how long do you think you spent in a chair? Another great question. I don't know how to answer it. If I had to venture a guess, I would say probably a week."
**Permanence of Tattoos** : Unlike other trends such as clothing or hairstyles, tattoos are permanent. Once they are acquired, they are "there for life," and cannot simply be shed as one "moves on to other forms of addictions or life forms," emphasizing the lasting commitment involved. "But what tattoos is, once you got them, you got them. And you can move on to other forms of addictions or life forms, you know, being, I'm going to add a lessons to adults and having a real job, again, a quote unquote, a real job. But what you have those tattoos, they're there for life."
**Improved Health and Safety Standards** : In earlier times, getting a tattoo was considered "a bit dodgy" due to significant health risks like infection or gangrene. However, advancements in techniques, equipment, inks, and health and safety standards have made the process much safer and more acceptable over time. "Back in the day, it was a bit dodgy getting one because it was the quickest way to get an infection at the best or gangrene at the worst. But I guess, you know, new techniques, new equipment, new inks, new health and safety standards, all these things conspired to help make tattoos more and more acceptable, I guess."
**Bob Roberts Tattoo Studio** : Bob Roberts Tattoo in Los Angeles is identified as one of the early and very famous tattoo shops in the city. Its owner, Bob Roberts, was a highly sought-after artist, often booked years in advance, and was known to have tattooed Flea from Red Hot Chili Peppers. **Tribal Tattoos as a Fading Trend** : Dave Grohl remarked that tribal tattoos, popular in the 1980s, have become outdated, comparing them to "wearing stone wash jeans" in terms of fashion relevance. "It's funny because in the 80s, it was like, yeah, tribal tattoos. I mean, that now, that's like wearing stone wash jeans."
**Tattoos as a Mainstream Commodity** : The trend of tattoos is becoming increasingly pervasive and commercialized, with tattoo parlors now found inside malls across America. This allows consumers to purchase "rock and roll clothes," shoes, and fresh ink all in one location, leading to a sense of lost uniqueness for early adopters. "It's like you can go to a mall now in the middle of America. And you can buy all the rock and roll clothes at Spencer or whatever the store is in your town. And then you can go and get the shoes over at a rock and roll shoes. And then right next to it, there's tattooed parlors inside malls now. So you can walk out and be fully rock and roll from head to toe, including the fresh ink at your local mall in any city in America."
**Tattoos as Family and Life Markers** : Tattoos are often used to commemorate family members and significant life events. Darren Fyfer has tattoos for his mother and father, and plans to get tattoos of his dogs when they pass away, indicating that even in his early 50s, he continues to plan for new tattoos to mark personal connections. "Family, and tattoos like family, dates. I got my mother. I got my father. I got my family. I got... I'm going to get my dog when my dog passes away several years from now on, I'm going to get my two dogs tattooed on me somewhere."
