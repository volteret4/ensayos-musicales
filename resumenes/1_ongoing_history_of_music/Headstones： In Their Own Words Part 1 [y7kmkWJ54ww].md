## curiosities
**Early Reputation and Sound** : The Headstones were once known as the "scariest band in Canadian music," described as intense, self-destructive, and high-energy. The band's singer, Hugh Dillon, stated that the group's preferred self-description was "furious," highlighting their unique, unpredictable, and ferocious playing style that he likened to a high-energy hockey team. "Once upon a time, the headstones were the scariest band in Canadian music."
**Kingston Origins and Early Formation** : The core members originated from Kingston, where they were part of a vibrant music scene that also produced The Tragically Hip. The band's genesis involved a group called "Sean Penn Atenshry" in the mid-1980s, which included Hugh Dillon, Randy Kwan, Mark Gibson, and Frontini, before Trent Carr joined and transformed their sound, leading to The Headstones' formation. "So how the headstones come out of Kingston? What year are we talking about? 89? No, you came from Kingston earlier than that. It was like 80s."
**Discovery and Signing with MCA** : The Headstones were discovered by Cam Carpenter and Tony Wanamaker, who secured them a showcase at the X-ray and Ultra Sound Club. A pivotal moment was the unexpected appearance and endorsement of Gord Downie and Paul Langlois from The Tragically Hip (who were also signed to MCA), which swayed MCA to sign the Headstones despite initial concerns about their "dangerous" image. "I remember when MCA and Cam Carpenter, you know, signed us at ultrasound, they were like, yeah, is it they're a little dangerous yet."
**Platinum Debut and Studio Challenges** : Their debut album, "Picture of Health," achieved platinum status instantly, which was a significant surprise for the band members. They admitted to feeling "totally green" and overwhelmed in the studio environment, and later questioned some of the choices made by producers and managers at that time. "Again, platinum out of the gate. Was that a surprise to you or did you? Oh, very much so a surprise."
**Intense Touring and "Second Album Syndrome"** : Following their debut, The Headstones maintained a grueling touring schedule, playing between 120 and 160 shows a year during the 1990s. This relentless pace led to the "second album syndrome" for "Teeth and Tissue," as songs were often written and recorded hastily on the road, with less time for full development. "I think we've been on the road so much for that pre that record picture of health. And then after it, how many days, I was just, they were doing like 120 shows a year or something like that during the whole 90s, 160 the first, the first year."
**Live-Centric Songwriting Approach** : The band's songwriting philosophy prioritizes live performance, ensuring that songs can "stand up live" and genuinely resonate with an audience. This focus dictates what makes it into their setlists and onto new albums, as they believe insincerity would be detected by the crowd or fellow band members. "The songs had in my mind had to stand up live. People had to be able to, you could tell if they uncross their own."
**"Cubically Contained" Radio Censorship Incident (1996)** : An incident occurred in 1996 when an unedited version of the song "Cubically Contained" from the "Smile and Wave" album was broadcast on radio at 5:30 PM, containing explicit profanity. This led to significant repercussions for the program director who played it and generated complaints to the Canadian Broadcast Standards Council. "I played an undated adversion of Cubically Contain on the radio."
**Artistic Control in Modern Recordings** : Having learned from past experiences where they questioned management and production decisions, The Headstones now exert full control over their recording process. This autonomy is seen as crucial, with the band believing their newer records, made under their complete direction, are superior and more authentically represent their sound. "That's why now when we make the records, we control everything. And these records to me are the band."
**Subconscious Noodling Songwriting Method** : The band's songwriting often begins with Trent Carr or Tim White "noodling" subconsciously during live performances or jams. Hugh Dillon actively listens for these spontaneous musical ideas, isolating specific notes or riffs that he finds unique and then developing them into full songs, rather than deliberately trying to write "hits." "I'll hear them subconsciously noodle. And that's when I stop everybody and what's that?"
**Unique Band Dynamic and Talent Respect** : The members describe their interpersonal dynamic as jokingly having "no respect" for each other as friends. However, they express profound admiration and shock when they witness each other perform their craft, describing it as a "magic trick" that transforms their perception of their bandmates from mere friends to incredible musicians. "It's funny because I have no respect for you as my friend. You do this magic trick that all of a sudden, whoever that guy, that guy I have all the respect for."

## curiosities
**Kingston Upbringing and Early Influences** : Grew up in Kingston, sharing high school with future members of The Tragically Hip and David Usher. He struggled academically but was intensely drawn to rock and roll, absorbing music from his older siblings and becoming a huge Bob Dylan fan. "I remember being 18 and I grew up in Kingston with David Usher and the guys in the hip. We all went to the same high school."
**Early Connection to The Slinks and Gord Downie** : Served as Gord Downie's "light guy" for his first high school band, The Slinks. Dillon later briefly joined The Slinks as a singer after Downie moved on. "I was Gordowney's light guy at the first high school dance where his band called the slinks performed."
**Past Involvement in Drug Dealing** : In his youth, Dillon engaged in drug dealing, including selling LSD to Gord Downie and cocaine. At 18, he was trafficking large quantities of marijuana (10 pounds at a time) between Ottawa and Kingston, selling to university students, a path he recognized could lead to prison. "My drug career blew up in my face... I was going 10 pounds a dope at a time and bringing it back to Kingston."
**Exile to England** : Following a violent confrontation related to his drug activities and a brush with the law, Dillon's parents gave him an ultimatum to leave Kingston, which he signed an agreement not to return to for several years. He traveled to England, his first time on a plane or subway, where he busked, squatted, and famously drank with Lemmy from Motörhead, resolving to pursue music instead of a life of crime. "So I got on a plane. I went to England, never been on a plane in my life, never been on a subway."
**Musical Development in England** : While in England, Dillon honed his songwriting skills and gained confidence performing covers and his own music on the streets, a period crucial to his development as a frontman and lyricist. "I'd learned to write songs and play on the street in England. And I was playing covers and stuff. So I had no compunction in terms of playing my own music."
**High School Friendship with Gord Downie** : Dillon and Gord Downie shared a strong mutual respect stemming from high school, where they were in the same classes (including dramatic arts) due to their names starting with 'D'. They would jokingly compare Downie's band rehearsals to Dillon's drug exploits. "Gordie and I had always had a mutual respect because in high school it was Dylan Downey, so we were in one of the same classes and the same dramatic arts class because our names started with D."
**Obsessive Record Collector and Musical Knowledge** : Dillon developed an extensive and "crazy" knowledge of rock and roll by obsessively collecting and listening to records for what he estimates as "10,000 hours." This deep immersion in music, including guitar licks and lyrics, allows him to identify and appreciate unique musical elements from his bandmates. "I grew up just obsessively listening to records. I was a record collector and I had crates of them."

## curiosities
**Art School Background** : Attended art school before joining Sean Penn Atenshry as the guitar player, a decision that led him to quit his studies. This background is credited by Hugh Dillon for influencing his unique and less "stereotypical" guitar style. "I was going to art school at the time. I do, I was a slightly different background than you."
**Early Jamming Leading to The Headstones** : As friends with Mark Gibson, Carr began jamming with Randy Kwan at a house on Carlson. Hugh Dillon later joined these sessions, creating an "electric moment" where the musical chemistry felt like a "real band" forming, which eventually became The Headstones. "Mark and Randy and I were jamming and he came in and it was just like, that was cool. Like one of those electric moments."
**Distinctive Guitar Style** : Hugh Dillon highlights Carr's guitar playing as non-"stereotypical dumb guitar rock," attributing its artistic quality to his art school background. Dillon finds inspiration in Carr's riffs, which he describes as subconscious musical pieces. "I think because he was an artist, it wasn't stereotypical dumb guitar rock. There was something in it that I recognized."
**Childhood Friend and Musical Influence on Tim White** : Carr has known Tim White since childhood and played a pivotal role in White's musical journey. He suggested White buy a bass instead of a guitar, and later provided White's "musical rock and roll education" by exposing him to bands like Led Zeppelin and The Who, fostering White's development as a bassist. "Trent said, no, you should get a bass. I'm like, I'm like, I'm like, okay. Yeah."

**Scarborough Upbringing** : Grew up in Scarborough, Ontario, which he characterized as a "cultural wasteland" due to a lack of local musical scene or friends outside of Trent Carr. "I grew up in Scarborough, which was a cultural wasteland."

**Musical Education Through Trent Carr** : With few other friends, White spent weekends at Trent Carr's downtown Toronto home, receiving his "musical rock and roll education." Carr would play him records by bands like Led Zeppelin and The Who, inspiring White to practice bass alone throughout the week. "He'd play me like, let's out on a pink floor and the who and all these bands. And then I'd go home and play bass all week by myself and then come back and show him."

**Perception of Canadian Music (Pre-1990s)** : Before the early 1990s, there was a prevalent belief across Canada that Canadian music was substandard. This perception significantly changed with the emergence of successful Canadian rock bands, leading to a strong nationalistic pride and demand for local talent. "Up until that point, there was generally across the country the feeling the Canadian music was substandard."
**Canadian Broadcast Standards Council (CBSC)** : This council became involved when an unedited version of The Headstones' song "Cubically Contained," containing clear profanity, was broadcast on radio at 5:30 PM. The CBSC received complaints from listeners, particularly those concerned about children, highlighting its role in overseeing broadcast content and standards. "Then there's something called the Canadian Brock East Anders Council. And of course a bunch of people were listing very concerned, you know, what about the children?"
**Malcolm Gladwell's 10,000-Hour Rule** : Hugh Dillon invoked Malcolm Gladwell's concept of 10,000 hours of dedicated practice to achieve mastery, applying it to his own intensive immersion in music through obsessive record collecting and listening. This dedication fostered a deep, almost innate knowledge of rock and roll that aids his songwriting. "I am of the Malcolm Gladwell 10,000 hours because I grew up just obsessively listening to records."
