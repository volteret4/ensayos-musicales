## albums
**Jackson Square** : This debut album was recorded quickly, in roughly two weeks, due to budget constraints, with bed tracks recorded in five days and overdubs done in a cheap studio. It was described as a concise encapsulation of four years of live songs, refined through performance, and characterized by a loud, fast rock sound with edgy guitars and super loud drums, reflecting a less open approach to diverse influences at the time. "We recorded Jackson Square in basically, you know, two weeks or a little bit over two weeks."

**Michigan Left** : This album took three years to release after "Jackson Square," recorded at The Bathhouse studio in Bath, Ontario, which is owned by The Tragically Hip. Initial demos were made at the Tokyo Police Club space and in Dan's basement. It was the band's first time self-producing a record with Dan, leading to a somewhat unfocused but experimental month-long process where they tried new arrangements, instrumentation, and effects like delays and gated reverb, and became more open to synth sounds ("Hollow notes"). The album was completed before they signed with Universal, so it was not conceived as a major label record. "This was also your first major label album."

**High Noon (2014)** : Recorded over a month in Los Angeles in October 2013, this album was the first to feature Anthony as keyboardist. The band secured Tony Hoffer, known for producing Phoenix, M83, and Beck, after he unexpectedly contacted them via MySpace in 2013, despite earlier attempts to reach him. They rehearsed for two months in Hamilton at Spice Factory, treating it like a "day job," before the L.A. sessions where synthesizers played a prominent role; Anthony subsequently purchased Hoffer's Korg GX3P and Roland D50 keyboards used on the record. "It is the first record with, uh, with Tony in the mix."

**Study Music EP** : An acoustic EP featuring acoustic versions of songs from "High Noon," created in response to fan demand for recorded acoustic performances seen on YouTube. Tracks like "1111" and "My Heart's All Yours" from this EP are frequently used for first dances at weddings. The EP was partly a competitive effort to showcase that their songs, when played acoustically, could serve as effective "soothing music" for studying. "It was just a little acoustic thing that we did. That was, uh, acoustic songs from high new."

**Morning Report** : This album includes significant songs such as "My Heart's All Yours," "Private School," and "Drake's Dad." During this period, the band began incorporating horn sections and backup singers into their live shows, a practice that later influenced the arrangements on subsequent recordings like "Knockin' at the Door." "The next album is, is Morning Report."

**Rally Cry (2018)** : Released two months before early 2019, this album is described as having significant energy and a communal feeling, effectively integrating elements and lessons learned from the band's previous records. The spontaneous and open studio approach taken during the creation of the single "Knockin' at the Door" served as an inspiration for the recording sessions of this album. "How do you describe it? Yeah, I think being so close to it is hard to totally describe it, but I can tell you the feeling of it because we're playing a lot live. I think that it has a lot of energy and I think there's a certain kind of like communal energy to the record and I think it brings together a lot of the elements from all the records we've discussed in a really great way."

## songs
**Whistleblower** : One of two singles from "Michigan Left," this song was first performed live at the Olympics, where its initial rendition differed significantly from its final recorded version. It was considered the most aggressive track on the album, sharing the loud, fast rock characteristics of "Jackson Square." "I remember we played it live at the Olympics was the first time we ever played it live."

**Come to Light** : This track served as the first single released from the "High Noon" album. "Come to late was the first single."

**Leather Jacket** : This track was released as the second single from the "High Noon" album. "leather jacket, second single."

**1111 (2014)** : The third single from "High Noon," this song's lyrics draw inspiration from the common practice of making a wish at 11:11, an observation frequently made by a band member in bars. The lyrical motif "you made a wish at 1111" is paired with "I held your hips at 1234," creating a narrative around these specific times. The acoustic version of this song has become a popular choice for first dances at weddings. "I would just see like groups of girls or just bear say, well, it's 1111, make a wish."

**My Heart's All Yours** : A significant track from the "Morning Report" album, this song is a popular choice for weddings, with its acoustic version frequently used for first dances. The lyrics were written about a band member's "special lady," and the band often receives videos from couples featuring the song at their weddings. "Yeah, it is. Is it about anybody in particular? Yeah, it's about my special lady."

**Private School** : Featured on the "Morning Report" album, this song explores the persona of affluent, cool, and intelligent students. While acknowledging their attractive qualities, the lyrics also convey a subtle critique of an "insufferable" lack of self-awareness regarding their inherent privilege and good fortune. Coming from "five white dudes" who recognize their own privilege, the song highlights the comedic aspect of this top echelon not fully grasping their luck. "I want to like you, but like, you don't quite get it, do you?"

**Drake's Dad** : This single from the "Morning Report" album was inspired by an encounter with Drake's father in a Memphis bar during a band road trip in America. The song broadly captures the experience of exploring a new city with friends, with Drake's dad making a brief appearance in the first verse and starring in the accompanying music video. The title was chosen purely for its humorous appeal. "I was on a road trip in America and we ran into Drake's dad in a bar in Memphis."

**Knockin' at the Door** : Released as a standalone single, this song was directly inspired by the night of Trump's inauguration and the subsequent Women's March in Washington D.C., which the band witnessed while touring with Frank Turner. The lyrics reflect themes of demanding change and reclaiming narrative. Drummer Tim's "military marching" beats served as the initial musical foundation, resonating with the concept of street marches. Max, Nick, and Mike created a demo in Hamilton, and producer Eric Ratz later helped speed up the tempo and maintain spontaneity during studio recording. The track incorporates horns and backup singers, influenced by the live performances of Kanye West's "Jesus Walks" and the band's use of horns on their "Morning Report" tour. The decision to release it as a single outside of an album cycle was described as a "hip-hop move." "We were in DC the night before he was inaugurated. So we were kind of taking in the vibes of America. And the next day was the women's march and I was like, oh, you know what? This is a depressing time for a lot of people, but I am inspired by saying these people take the streets and sort of take back the narrative of who we are as people."

## curiosities
**Continuous Audience Engagement** : The band prioritizes consistent engagement with their listeners, understanding the importance of continuously supplying new songs, shows, and special online content. They believe that for most bands, showing earnest care and being visible is crucial, as audiences expect to be engaged constantly. "working consistently and being loyal to your listeners and, and providing them with, you know, the service of new songs and shows and all those things, I think, uh, is really important to remember that."

**MySpace Contact from Producer Tony Hoffer (2013)** : Despite previous unsuccessful attempts to contact producer Tony Hoffer through his manager, Hoffer personally reached out to Arkells via MySpace in 2013. He had discovered them after watching a live session of theirs, thinking they were a "cool band" and asking if they were "thinking of recording anytime soon." "he reached out to us on MySpace. On MySpace saying like, hey, you guys are, wait, in 2013, he reaches out on MySpace."

**Scarlet Fever during Michigan Left Recording** : One of the band members contracted scarlet fever during the recording sessions for "Michigan Left" at The Bathhouse studio, requiring them to be quarantined and out of commission for a couple of days. "I got scarlet fever actually during that, uh, okay. During that recording session. You had to be quarantined."

**Value of a Professional Producer** : After self-producing "Michigan Left," the band learned that hiring a professional producer is "actually a very helpful thing." They found it challenging as "kids doing something for the first time" to work through all the questions and decisions involved in making a record. They view a producer as a "part psychologist," "social worker," and "translator" who can communicate effectively with each musician, ensuring good vibes and progress. "hiring a producer is actually a very helpful thing because I do remember it being tough as a bunch of like kids doing something for the first time."

**Wedding Song Popularity** : Several acoustic versions of Arkells' songs, including "1111," "My Heart's All Yours," and "And Then Some," have become popular choices for first dances at weddings. The band regularly receives emails and videos from couples using their music for these special occasions, considering it a "very cool" and precious recognition. "Like that 1111 acoustic version has been used at like first dances for weddings, like countless times. Same thing with our acoustic versions of my heart, so it's yours. And, and then some, those three songs like we get in the summertime, like a couple of emails every week with videos from someone's wedding."

**Pyeongchang 2018 Winter Olympics "Scam Trip"** : The band "drifted" into performing at the Canada House during the 2018 Pyeongchang Winter Olympics. The opportunity arose after they jokingly tweeted to Art Canada and Canada House (who were using "Knockin' at the Door" as a theme song) about covering their expenses for a trip. Within three days, they were on a plane, flying coach for a 14.5-hour flight without bringing instruments due to last-minute arrangements. They played three house-party style shows over the last five days of the Olympics, largely for athletes who were "ready to party" having finished their events, playing on the ground because the stage was only big enough for drums. "Yeah, that's a 14 and a half hour flight. Yeah. Did they, at least spring for premium economy? No, we were absolutely not. We were in coach."

**Morton's Field "The Rally" Stadium Show** : In 2017, the band curated a massive show at Morton's Field in Hamilton for 24,000 people, aiming to surpass their previous large shows. Collaborating with the Tiger-Cats, they designed the event from the ground up, as the facility hadn't hosted many concerts in decades since a Pink Floyd issue in the 70s. The event featured a curated bill, custom poster, a bike ride from downtown to the stadium, an artist flea market, and local businesses creating Arkells-themed products like beer, sausages, and cupcakes. A portion of proceeds went to Refuge, a healthcare organization for new Canadians, making it a "real communal event." "We got to curate the bill. We got to design the poster. We got to, we organized a bike ride along the Canis Street bike lanes from downtown to the stadium. We had an artist flea market beforehand."

**Anthony's Baby Born Before Morton's Field Show** : Anthony's baby was born approximately 36 to 48 hours before the band's "The Rally" show at Morton's Field. His wife was due June 10th, and the show fell within a two-week window. Anthony did soundcheck the day before after his baby was born at 8 AM, then slept upright in a hospital chair, and drove his wife and newborn home the morning of the show, performing on "fumes." "My wife was due June 10th. And this was like the one show that was slotted in kind of like a two week before, two week after a period."

**Embrace of "Hollow Notes" Sound** : During the production of "Michigan Left," the band underwent a "turning point" where they became open to more influences, particularly synth sounds referred to as "Hollow notes." Previously, they "hated Hollow notes" and focused on a guitar-driven, edgy sound for "Jackson Square," but on "Michigan Left," they found these nuanced synth sounds "actually kind of sound interesting," leading to a slightly more nuanced record. "I hated Hollow notes before we made Michigan left. And then I was sort of, those synths actually kind of sound interesting."

**Continuous Evolution of Songs Post-Release** : The band acknowledges that they have no control over how songs evolve in people's lives after release. They find it interesting to see where their music lands, citing examples like "Knockin' at the Door" being used at the Olympics and "11-11" at weddings, appreciating that songs "can land in interesting places." "You put someone in the world and you go, oh, like knock at the door, I ended up being played at the Olympics. And like 11-11 was played at people's weddings. You know, it's funny how, you know, you have no control over that, but they can land in interesting places."

# genre - Rock and Roll

## curiosities
**Genre Limitations** : A band member expressed personal feelings of being "a little confined" within the traditional boundaries of rock and roll, questioning whether "two guitars based piano drums" was all they were "allowed to do." This sentiment fueled a desire to widen the scope of their music, incorporating elements like female vocals and horn sections, and adopting non-traditional release strategies such as hip-hop inspired single releases. "I've always been interested in what's happening outside of Rock and Roll as a genre. And I think sometimes I personally feel like a little confined when it's like, is this all we're allowed to do?"

# genre - Hip-Hop

## curiosities
**Influence on Single Release Strategy** : The band consciously adopted a "hip-hop move" by releasing "Knockin' at the Door" as a standalone single outside of a traditional album cycle. This approach prioritized releasing a song they liked immediately, disregarding conventional music industry practices. "And also even the nature of putting out the song is a single. That was a very sort of like hip-hop move, right? Like just to go, no, I don't care about any sort of traditional convention. If I like the song, we're going to put it out."

"You were one of these turnkey, turnkey operations that you were able to present, uh, two albums and a fan base to a major label."

# instrument - Synthesizer

## curiosities
**Embrace of Nuance on "Michigan Left"** : During the recording of "Michigan Left," Arkells, particularly Anthony, became significantly more open to incorporating synthesizers, which they had previously disliked ("hated Hollow notes"). This marked a departure from the guitar-focused sound of their earlier work, allowing for a "slightly more like nuanced record." The producer Tony Hoffer, known for being a "big synth guy" with "a lot of vintage stuff," further influenced this direction, leading Anthony to purchase two of his studio keyboards. "I hated Hollow notes before we made Michigan left. And then I was sort of, those synths actually kind of sound interesting."

# instrument - Pro Tools

## curiosities
**Drum Beat Production by Tim** : Drummer Tim utilizes Pro Tools to process and loop drum beats for the band's songwriting. He records practice sessions using various tools like mics, phones, or Zoom recorders, then cuts these recordings into loops and organizes them into folders to share with the other band members for creative inspiration. "I'll take that and I'll just cut up, like, you know, make loops out of these beats and then I'll just dump them all in a folder and send them to these guys so they can, you know, if they're ever sitting around holding a guitar or whatever they're doing, they can pull them up and just start going through them."

# curiosity

**The Bathhouse Studio** : Located in Bath, Ontario, this studio is owned by The Tragically Hip and served as the recording location for Arkells' album "Michigan Left." The band lived in the same building as the studio during recording, allowing for extensive experimentation until late hours (sometimes 5 AM) with various equipment and effects like an old plate reverb. It provided an ideal environment for the then "23 year olds" to explore new sounds and production techniques. "The bathhouse you can, it's the Tragically Hips studio in Bath, Ontario and upstairs there's all these rooms. And so we were living in the same studio, in the same building as the studio."

**The Importance of Providing a Sense of Purpose** : The band members, when returning home from tour, quickly become restless after a few days off, seeking a "sense of purpose." They find that the "best and most interesting work" is creative, collaborative, and can be shared with the world, driving them to continuously work on new material and projects rather than resting for long periods. "After a few days, we're like, all right, what are we doing? Like, you know, like, we, we want to wake up every day with a sense of purpose."

**Olympics Show Announcement with Canadian Figures** : To announce their 2017 show with July Talk at Ampfest in Toronto, Arkells filmed Ron MacLean and Don Cherry announcing the show as if it were a hockey trade. Later, to announce "The Rally" show in Hamilton, they enlisted Canadian Olympic ice dancers Tessa Virtue and Scott Moir, aligning the announcement with the kickoff of the Olympics, after Tessa Virtue had seen them perform at Oshiega. "We got Ron and Don to announce the show. And we filmed them as if they're breaking a hockey trade. So we're thinking."

**"Jesus Walks" Horn Influence** : The horn section arrangement on Arkells' song "Knockin' at the Door" was directly inspired by the live version of Kanye West's "Jesus Walks." Specifically, the idea came from a Dave Sheepell block party where Kanye West performed with a school marching band, creating a "bomb. Boom. Boom. Boom. Boom." sound that the band sought to interject into their song, alongside a solo trumpet reminiscent of going to battle. "Tony, fashion the horns are like, what it like, kind of like Jesus walks by Kanye West."

**"Pink Floyd Issue" at Morton's Field** : Morton's Field in Hamilton had not hosted many concerts for decades due to a historical "Pink Floyd issue" that occurred in the 1970s. This long-standing hiatus meant there was no established formula for putting on events there, allowing Arkells to custom-design their "The Rally" stadium show from scratch. "Yeah, Pink Floyd because of the Pink Floyd issue that happened back in the 70s."
