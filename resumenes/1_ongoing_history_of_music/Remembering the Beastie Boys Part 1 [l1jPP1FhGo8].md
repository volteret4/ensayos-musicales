## albums
**Pollywog Stew (1982)** : This LP was recorded in only two days at the 171A studio because the landlord was threatening to sell all the gear to collect back rents. After recording the eight songs, the band broke up again. An engineer, Scott Jarvis, rescued a tape machine and mixed the tracks in his bedroom. It was released on Rat Cage on November 20, 1982. "The result was an LP called Polywag Stew."
**Licensed to Ill (1986)** : Released on November 19, 1986, this album was one of the first successful fusions of rap, hip-hop, and rock, appealing to rap, alternative, and mainstream rock fans. Kerry King of Slayer played guitar on the album while concurrently recording *Reign in Blood*. It socially and culturally bridged a huge gap between black and white music fans, who were previously "miles apart." The album became one of the fastest-selling debut albums in history, the first rap record to hit number one on the American album charts, and remains a top-selling back catalog item, selling hundreds of thousands of copies annually. Columbia Records, its distributor for Def Jam, recorded it as their fastest-selling record to that point, eventually selling over ten million copies in the U.S. alone. "Musically, it was one of the first successful fusions of rap, hip hop and rock."
**Paul's Boutique (1989)** : Released on July 25, 1989, this album was a significant departure from *Licensed to Ill*, lacking the "frat boy goofiness" and "big guitars" of its predecessor. Initially, sales were dismal, and Capitol Records stopped promotion after two singles, leading to the firing of the label's president and others. Despite its commercial failure at the time, the album was artistically and technically very advanced for its day, blowing away the rap and hip-hop community. The Beastie Boys, with production team The Dust Brothers, spent close to two years meticulously crafting it, sampling no fewer than 105 different songs, expertly woven into the fabric of each track. The band cleared all samples, paying approximately $250,000, which was considered reasonable at the time but would cost millions today due to subsequent sampling lawsuits. Today, it is recognized as an "absolute masterpiece" and a landmark album in hip-hop, sampling, producing, and recording engineering. "But we just didn't see it at the time, but this record was so advanced, both artistically and technically for its day, that we just didn't get it."

## songs
**A Grade on Mojo (1982)** : This song is one of the Beastie Boys' earliest recordings from their hardcore era, featured on the *Pollywog Stew* LP. It is one of the few tracks from that period that survived throughout the band's career. "The song is called A Grade on Mojo, and it's one of their earliest recordings, and it's also one of the few songs from their hardcore era that survived throughout the rest of the band's career."
**Cookie Puss (1983)** : This four-song EP's title track, released in August 1983 on Rat Cage, gained significant college radio airplay and inadvertently made the band "serious money." The concept involved a prank phone call to a local Carvel ice cream store (Carvel sold an ice cream cake called a Cookie Puss), which was then taped and combined with bits from Steve Martin's comedy album *A Wild and Crazy Guy* over some beats. British Airways allegedly used a sample from the song in a commercial without permission, leading to a successful lawsuit where the Beastie Boys won $40,000, which they used to rent an apartment and rehearsal space at 99 Christie Street. "The concept was really simple. First of all, they made a prank phone call to a local Carval ice cream store."
**Rock Hard (1985)** : Released as Def Jam's second single in 1985, this track was soon withdrawn due to containing illegal samples from AC/DC's song "Back in Black." The Beastie Boys had neglected to ask permission, and AC/DC was categorically against sampling at the time. It became a significant collectible for years until it was reissued in Europe in 2007 with AC/DC's blessing, after the Beasties had become hugely successful and could speak directly with AC/DC's peers, who had softened their stance. "The label's second single was a Beastie's track called Rock Hard, but it was soon withdrawn because it contained some illegal AC-DC samples from the song Back in Black."
**She's on It (1985)** : This song was originally featured on the soundtrack of the film *Crush Groove* and was released as a 12-inch single on September 12, 1985. "She's on it, originally from the soundtrack of a film called Crush Groove."
**(You Gotta) Fight For Your Right (To Party!) (1986)** : Often regarded as a "frat boy novelty song," especially after its video release, this track was also a fusion of rock with big guitars and heavy drums, featuring rapping instead of singing. Adam Yauch and a friend named Tom wrote the song as a parody of contemporary rock anthems like Twisted Sister's "I Wanna Rock" and Quiet Riot's "Bang Your Head," though this satirical intent was largely "lost on 95% of the people who heard it." The music video, featuring cameos from LL Cool J, Rick Rubin, and MTV's Tabitha Soren, had an almost nonexistent budget, with all the whipped cream used for pies scrounged from dumpsters behind supermarkets, described as "sour and rancid and smelled awful." "This was supposed to be a parody of those types of songs, but that was lost on 95% of the people who heard it."
**Brass Monkey (1986)** : This song, one of 13 tracks on *Licensed to Ill*, was the fourth single released from the album. It introduced millions of non-rappers and those not familiar with house music to the distinctive sounds of the legendary Roland TR-808 drum machine. "It introduced millions of non-rappers and those not into house music to the sounds of the legendary Roland TR-808 drum machine."
**No Sleep Till Brooklyn (1986)** : Featured on *Licensed to Ill*, this song's riff structure bears a strong similarity to AC/DC's "TNT," which was not accidental. The title itself was intentionally inspired by Motorhead's album *No Sleep till Hammer Smith*. Kerry King of Slayer contributed guitar work to this track. "And if you're a Motorhead fan, any similarity between the title of this song and Motorhead's No Sleep till Hammer Smith is purely intentional."
**Hey Ladies (1989)** : This was the first single released from the Beastie Boys' second album, *Paul's Boutique*. It marked a significant stylistic change from their earlier work, moving away from the "frat boy goofiness" and "big guitars." The track meticulously incorporated samples from 16 different songs, including a line from The Sweet's "Ballroom Blitz," with the song's title derived from Curtis Blow's "Party Time." Additionally, snippets from James Brown, The Commodores, Kool & The Gang, and Edwin Starr can be heard. The complexity of this mix of samples and original music was considered "staggering for its day." "Sixteen different songs are rated for this track."
**Shake Your Rump (1989)** : This was the second single released from *Paul's Boutique*. The song contains eight distinct samples, drawing material from artists such as James Brown, Sly & The Family Stone, and the Sugar Hill Gang. It also incorporated drums from a track by the Southern rock band Black Oak Arkansas. "There were eight samples contained therein, including material from James Brown, Slime the Family Stone, Sugar Hill Gang, and even some drums from a song by a southern rock band called Black Oak Arkansas."

## curiosities
**Early Formation and Evolution (1981-2010s)** : The Beastie Boys began in 1981 as a "goof" by three guys from well-to-do New York families, initially motivated by drinking beer and meeting women. Over three decades, they evolved from a hardcore punk band into esteemed members of the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame, recognized as one of the most important acts of all time in two different genres, ultimately ceasing activity with the death of Adam Yauch. "When they finally had to call it quits when one of them died three decades later, they were esteemed members of the Rock and Wall Hall of Fame and considered to be one of the most important acts of all time in two different genres."
**Beastie Boys Name Origin** : Officially, according to legend, "Beastie" stood for "Boys Entering Anarchistic States Towards Internal Excellence," although this explanation is considered by some to sound like "weird reverse engineering." "Now officially, according to legend, that stood for Boys Entering Anarchistic States Towards Internal Excellence."
**Early Breakups** : The band experienced multiple early breakups; they packed it in after two gigs as The Young Aborigines, then regrouped as The Beastie Boys. Later, after recording the *Pollywog Stew* LP, they broke up again. "After that, they gigged as a hardcore act around New York, supporting the dead Kennedys and working the stage at CBGB. Then they got bored and broke up."
**John Berry's Departure** : Original guitarist John Berry quit the Beastie Boys near the end of 1982 due to what was described as a "myth problem." Adam Horowitz was then drafted in to take his place. "When John Barry quit near the end of 1982, apparently there was a bit of a myth problem, Adam Horowitz was drafted in to take his place."
**Prank Call Leads to Song** : The song "Cookie Puss" originated from a prank phone call made by the Beastie Boys to a local Carvel ice cream store. They recorded this call and incorporated it into the track, which then became the title track of their four-song EP. "First of all, they made a prank phone call to a local Carval ice cream store."
**British Airways Lawsuit (1984)** : The Beastie Boys sued British Airways after the airline allegedly used a sample from their song "Beastie Revolution" (likely referring to "Cookie Puss") in a worldwide commercial without permission. The band won a judgment for $40,000, which marked the first money they ever made from music. They used these funds to rent an apartment and rehearsal space at 99 Christie Street. "The Beastie Boys heard it in New York in the cinema, and sued British airways. It was the first money they ever made out of music."
**Rick Rubin's Influence and Kate Schellenbach's Departure** : DJ Double R, whose real name was Rick Rubin, joined the Beastie Boys and helped introduce more hip-hop into their sound. He also suggested a change in their image, encouraging them to dress like street rappers with matching Adidas track outfits, turned baseball caps, and chunky gold chains. Rubin also "kind of forced" original drummer Kate Schellenbach out of the band, as she became fed up with him, finding him "childish and really, really sexist." "He also kind of forced Kate Shellenbach out of the band."
**Madonna's Tour Opener (1984)** : Madonna selected the Beastie Boys to open for her 1984 *Like a Virgin* tour. She had originally wanted Run DMC, but they were too expensive, making the Beasties a more affordable alternative for the gig, which came about through a mix-up with Russell Simmons. "Madonna, who threw a mix up with Simmons, ended up with the Beastie Boys as her opener for her 1984-like a virgin tour."
**Early "Frat Boy" Reputation** : In the mid-1980s, the Beastie Boys' drunken party fratboy attitude caused "most people to absolutely hate them." This image was prevalent before the release and widespread acceptance of *Licensed to Ill* changed public perception. "In fact, their drunken party fratboy attitude caused most people to absolutely hate them."
**Controversial *Licensed to Ill* Title Suggestion** : Before settling on *Licensed to Ill*, the Beastie Boys wanted to title their debut album "Don't Be a Faggot." This alternative title was ultimately rejected, and the speaker muses about how different things might have been if the band had gotten their way. "They wanted to call this record, don't be a faggot."
**Whipped Cream Budget for "Fight For Your Right" Video** : The music video for "(You Gotta) Fight For Your Right (To Party!)" had an "almost no budget" for the shoot. All the whipped cream used in the pie fight scene was scrounged from dumpsters behind supermarkets and was described as being "sour and rancid and smelled awful." "All the whipped cream used in the pies. It was scrounged from dumpsters behind supermarkets."
**British Controversy and False Stories (1987)** : During their *Licensed to Ill* tour, the Beastie Boys faced severe criticism from British politicians, one of whom advocated for their ban from the UK, citing their stage antics such as inviting female audience members to dance in cages and the presence of a giant motorized inflatable penis. British tabloids targeted them, with one paper running a false story alleging the band laughed at and taunted dying children with terminal leukemia. Although some of the affected children later defended the band, the damage to their reputation had already been done. "The British tabloids really had it out for them. One went so far as to carry a false story about the band laughing and taunting a group of dying children, kids with terminal leukemia."
**Liverpool Riot (May 10, 1987)** : During a show in Liverpool on May 10, 1987, a group of "thugs" began throwing cans and bottles at the Beastie Boys. The band responded by returning the projectiles using baseball bats, leading to a riot. A girl was unfortunately hit in the eye, and Ad-Rock was arrested for assault. The concert lasted only ten minutes before devolving into chaos. "The Beasties responded by returning the missiles using baseball bats."
**Post-*Licensed to Ill* Tour Burnout** : The *Licensed to Ill* tour was extremely demanding on the Beastie Boys, leading to significant interpersonal conflict within the band. Upon returning home, they felt they "wanted to kill each other" and decided to take a break to cool down and recharge. "But the license to yield to her was very rough on the Beastie Boys. By the time they got home, they wanted to kill each other."
**Def Jam Royalty Dispute (Late 1980s)** : After the *Licensed to Ill* tour, the Beastie Boys' relationship with Def Jam deteriorated when the label demanded a new album immediately despite the band feeling burned out and needing to recharge. In response to the band's refusal, Def Jam began holding back royalties that were rightfully owed to them. This dispute "dragged on for months" before the Beastie Boys managed to break free from the label and sign with Capitol Records. "Then the relationship with deaf jam blew up. They wanted a new album right away, but the Beastie said, no, forget it. We're burned out. We need to recharge. So deaf jam started holding back royalties."
**Capitol Records President Fired Over *Paul's Boutique*** : The commercial failure of *Paul's Boutique* upon its release had severe consequences, including the firing of the president of Capitol Records. The album was considered "such a stiff" that his dismissal was directly attributed to allowing the band to be signed to the label, with his being "not the only head that rolled." "It was such a stiff that the president of capital was fired for allowing the band to be signed to the label."

**Business and Creative Ventures** : Beyond music, the Beastie Boys founded a record label, a magazine, a clothing line, and a film company, demonstrating their diverse entrepreneurial and creative interests. "founded a record label, a magazine, a clothing line, and a film company."
**Acting Careers** : The members of the Beastie Boys were accomplished actors, with Adam Yauch notably going to Hollywood after the *Licensed to Ill* tour to act in a couple of films and relax with his then-girlfriend, Molly Ringwald. "Accomplished actors."

**Refusal to Use Songs in Commercials** : At one point, when they were a punk band, the Beastie Boys refused to let their songs be used in commercials. "were once a punk band refused to let their songs be used in commercials."
**Dog Named Rufus** : The Beastie Boys had a dog named Rufus. "had a dog named Rufus."

# genre - Rap / Hip Hop

## curiosities
**Early Acceptance Challenges (Late 1970s - Early 1980s)** : Rap and hip-hop began filtering out of New York in the late 1970s and early 1980s but struggled to gain acceptance outside of urban neighborhoods, often dismissed with phrases like "rap crap." There was a significant divide between urban and suburban audiences, people of color and white listeners, and traditional musicians versus rappers. A "sizeable majority" of the music market, industry, and fans viewed rap as a fleeting fad, expecting a swift return to traditional singing and instrument playing, not "scratching and sampling nonsense." "Rap crap was a phrase that was thrown around a lot. There was a huge divide. Urban areas versus the suburbs. People of color versus white. Workers versus rappers."
**Bridging Racial and Musical Divides** : The Beastie Boys' album *Licensed to Ill* (1986) significantly bridged the gap between black and white music fans, who were previously "miles apart." While it's noted as "kind of suspect that it took a bunch of white rappers to bring them together," the album "got the job done" in fostering wider acceptance and connection between these groups. "Socially and culturally, it bridged this huge gap between black and white music fans. Before the Beastie Boys, these two groups of fans were miles apart."
**Impact of Paul's Boutique (1989)** : While *Paul's Boutique* was a commercial failure initially, the rap and hip-hop community was "totally blown away" by the album. Its advanced artistic and technical complexity, particularly in sampling, set new benchmarks in the genre. "But the rap and hip-hop community was totally blown away by Paul's boutique."

"Paul Sputig came out in the time before a bunch of big lawsuits involving sampling pushed the cost of clearing samples way, way up."
