# curiosity

**Reginald Fessenden – Canadian-Born Engineer, First to Broadcast a Human Voice (1906)** : While Guglielmo Marconi is commonly credited with inventing radio, he actually invented the wireless telegraph, capable of sending Morse code but not voice or music. The first person to actually broadcast a human voice through the air was Reginald Fessenden, a Canadian-born engineer working in a lab near Washington, D.C., who achieved intelligible transmission as early as 1900. On December 24, 1906, from Brant Rock, Massachusetts, he presided over the first proper radio broadcast: he played a Handel piece on a phonograph, read from the Bible, and played "O Holy Night" on his violin. Atlantic sailors accustomed to Morse code were bewildered. Fessenden became simultaneously the first DJ, first radio performer, and first radio announcer. "In the space of those few minutes, Fessenden became the first DJ, the first radio performer, and the first radio announcer."

**XWA Montreal – First Licensed North American Radio Station; First Entertainment Broadcast May 20, 1920** : The first licensed radio station in North America was XWA (the X standing for "experimental"), run by the Marconi Wireless Telegraph Company of Canada. Voice transmission tests began in spring 1919 using a 500-watt transmitter at 173 Williams Street in Montreal; bored engineers started grabbing phonograph records from the nearby Layton Brothers record store to play instead of reciting the alphabet — giving Layton Brothers an inadvertent claim as radio's first advertiser. The first documented entertainment broadcast for the general public occurred on May 20, 1920, when soprano Dorothy Hutton sang two ballads from the XWA studio. KDKA in Pittsburgh — often cited by Americans as the first radio station — did not sign on until November 2, 1926. "KDKA didn't go on the air until November 2, 1926 — later."

**FM Radio Invention and Edwin Armstrong's Tragic End** : Edwin Howard Armstrong, one of the engineers who put KDKA on air, discovered Frequency Modulation (FM) while working for RCA. After more than a decade developing the mathematics and science, the first FM broadcast was made on June 9, 1934, at exactly 10:23am from the top of the Empire State Building — an organ recital that sounded remarkable compared to AM. However, RCA suppressed FM to protect their lucrative AM radio business and patent portfolio, spending years tying Armstrong up in courts. On February 1, 1954, broken and depressed after decades of legal battles, Armstrong dressed in his best suit, put on his hat and gloves, and jumped from his 13th floor apartment window. "On February 1, 1954, broken and depressed, Howard dressed in his best suit and overcoat, put on his hat and gloves and jumped from his 13th floor window."

**AM and FM Frequency Standards Vary by World Region – Western Radios Don't Work in Russia or Japan** : AM radio in North America uses the 520-1710 kHz band with stations spaced 10 kHz apart; much of the world uses 9 kHz spacing starting at 531 kHz, meaning North American digital radios cannot properly receive most global AM stations. FM differs even more dramatically: North America runs 87.9–107.9 MHz; parts of the former Soviet Union and Warsaw Pact countries use 65–74 MHz (fully incompatible with Western radios); Japan uses 76–90 MHz (also incompatible). When the US moved FM to its current band on January 15, 1945, all FM radios sold to that point in history became instantly useless. The world is divided by the International Telecommunication Union (founded 1865) into three broadcasting regions with different regulations. "Did you know that the radio that you're listening to right now won't work in Russia or Japan?"

**Radio Call Letter Conventions – Why Canadian Stations Begin With C, US East Is W, West Is K** : Radio call letters function as a station's license plate. In Canada, all station call signs must begin with the letter C, with the second letter being F, H, I, J, or K (e.g., CFTO, CHUM, CKCO). Most Canadian stations have four letters; the few three-letter stations (e.g., CKX Brandon) date from the 1920s and 1930s. In the US, stations east of the Mississippi must begin with W; stations west of it must begin with K — with historical exceptions grandfathered in from before the rule change. "Why do all Canadian radio stations have call letters that begin with the letter C?"

**First DJ – Canadian-Born Al Jarvis, Los Angeles, 1932** : In the early days of radio, virtually all broadcast content was live. Musician unions actively resisted the playing of records on air, arguing it threatened their livelihoods. It wasn't until 1932 that a Canadian-born announcer named Al Jarvis convinced his boss at an LA radio station to let him create a show consisting entirely of him playing records and talking between songs. In 1935, New Yorker Martin Block launched "Make Believe Ballroom," featuring 15-minute imaginary "performances" by major artists who were, in reality, just records being played. The term "disc jockey" first appeared in print in the showbiz paper Variety on July 23, 1941. By the 1950s, thousands of people were calling themselves disc jockeys. "The first ever proper radio DJ was born in Canada."

**Capital Radio UK (October 16, 1973) – First Private Commercial Radio Station in Britain; Punks Couldn't Get Airplay** : Until October 16, 1973, all legal radio broadcasting in the UK came from the publicly funded BBC. That day, Capital FM signed on at 95.8 FM with 557 AM as the country's first private commercial station. For years Capital played safe mainstream pop. When punk emerged, both the BBC and Capital refused to play it, infuriating The Clash, who wrote a song explicitly about their frustration. "That greatly annoyed the Clash, who couldn't get airplay anywhere."
