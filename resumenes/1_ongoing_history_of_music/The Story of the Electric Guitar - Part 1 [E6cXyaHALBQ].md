**Early Tinkering and Home Studio** : Les Paul was a country and jazz guitarist and an inventor who established one of the very first home studios. **Influence of Charlie Christian on Electric Guitar Design** : In 1939, likely inspired by hearing Charlie Christian play, Les Paul conceived his own design for an electric guitar, which he dubbed "the log." This marked a significant shift in his focus towards electrification. "But in 1939, most likely after hearing Charlie Christian, he came up with an idea for his own electric guitar design."
**The Log Guitar Design** : "The log" was a rudimentary solid-body electric guitar constructed from a 4x4 piece of pine, to which two wings were attached to mimic the appearance of a Spanish guitar. It featured a regular guitar neck, a bridge, and two crude homemade pickups, making it distinct from the hollow-body electric guitars available at the time. "The result was something he called the log. It was a 4x4 piece of pine with two wings attached to it to make it look like a Spanish guitar. He attached a regular guitar neck to that body, along with a bridge and two crude homemade pickups. Unlike all the other electric guitars in the market, this one wasn't hollow. Its body was solid."
**The Log's Recording and Companion Instrument** : A recording made in 1948 features "the log" along with another experimental instrument called "the Clunker." The Clunker was an old hollow-body guitar modified with a hole in the back, allowing Les Paul to easily access and "lobotomize" its internal electronics, effectively making it a playable workbench. "OK, that's not exactly Jimmy Hendrix, but again, that recording was made in 1948. We hear the log on that track, along with another monster called the Clunker, which was an old hollow body with a hole in the back that allowed Les Paul to lobotomize all the electronics inside. It was like a workbench you could play."
**Public Reception and Realization of Solid-Body Potential** : Although "the log" was often mocked for its unusual appearance, its solid-body design led to the realization that guitars could adopt any shape. This opened new possibilities for guitar manufacturing. "People really made fun of the way the log looked. At the same time, they realized that by making a guitar with a solid body, a guitar could basically assume any shape you wanted. That had potential."
**Invention of Sound-on-Sound Recording** : Les Paul is credited with inventing Sound on Sound Recording, now commonly known as multitrack recording, which he developed in his garage home studio. This innovation significantly influenced future recording techniques. "Les Paul went on to invent Sound on Sound Recording, which is what we now call Multitrack Recording. He came up with that in the home studio in his garage."
**Collaboration with Gibson** : Despite approaching multiple manufacturers with his ideas for guitar design, only Gibson expressed interest, leading to the creation of the iconic guitar that bears his name. "Les Paul took his ideas to a couple of different manufacturers, but only Gibson seemed interested."

# instrument - Electric Guitar

## curiosities
**Early Development and Lack of Tradition** : When the first primitive models of the electric guitar emerged in the 1920s, they were largely dismissed. The instrument was new, unproven, and lacked the historical tradition and gravitas associated with established instruments like the piano, violin, or brass instruments. Its reliance on electricity, a relatively new concept for household appliances and radio, also contributed to its initial obscurity. "When the first primitive models appeared in the 1920s, no one really gave them much thought. The electric guitar was brand new, unproven, and completely lacking in any of the kinds of traditions and gravitas enjoyed by the piano, the violin, or any number of brass instruments. Besides, like all the other musical instruments in use, these required electricity, a concept that was still quite new."
**Ascension to Power (1920s-1960s)** : Over a 30-year period, from the 1920s to the 1960s, the electric guitar solidified its place in music. This rise was facilitated by technological advancements, the increasing need for louder volume in performances, evolving social conditions, and shifts in public musical taste. By the 1960s, it was recognized as one of the most potent musical inventions ever. "At over the next 30 years, the electric guitar found its place in music, helped along by technology, the need for volume, changing social conditions, and the ever evolving musical taste of the public. By the 1960s, the electric guitar was regarded as one of the most powerful musical inventions of all time."
**Defining Sound of Late 20th Century** : The electric guitar became the defining sound of popular music for the latter half of the 20th century. It embodied freedom, power, rebellion, joy, heartache, aggression, and remains an essential part of contemporary popular culture, resisting challenges to its supremacy. "In short, the electric guitar defined music for the latter half of the 20th century. It's still an essential part of popular culture, and despite several challenges to its supremacy over the decades, it is not going away any time soon."
**Technological Prerequisites for Amplification** : The development of the electric guitar depended on three key technological advancements becoming widespread and acceptable: widespread availability of electricity by the 1930s, the invention of the vacuum tube by Lee DeForest in 1911 (forming the basis for amplifiers), and the co-development of the first speaker by General Electric and AT&T in 1921. "A couple of things had to happen before George Beachham could make his guitars louder. First of all, electricity had to become both widely available and acceptable everywhere. That happened by the 1930s. Second, someone had to invent some electronics that could take a quiet electrical signal and make it louder. That happened in 1911 when Lee DeForest unveiled a vacuum tube, and that became the foundation for something soon known as an amplifier. And third, someone had to invent something that could turn an electric signal back into sound. And that happened in 1921, when a co-development project between General Electric and AT&T resulted in the first speaker, a paper call that reproduced sound."
**Initial Public Reception as a Novelty** : Despite its growing reputation within certain musical communities, the electric guitar was initially perceived by the broader public as little more than a novelty. "Still, the electric guitar was a little more than a novelty with the public."
**Widespread Manufacturing by Early 1940s** : Following the influential performances of Charlie Christian, demand for electric guitars surged, leading to even more manufacturers entering the production market by the early 1940s. "After Charlie Christian, demand for the electric guitars went way up. And by the early 1940s, even more manufacturers were in the game."

# instrument - Lap Steel Guitar

## curiosities
**Hawaiian Origin and Development** : In the late 1800s, Spanish guitars brought to Hawaii by European and Mexican sailors were repurposed by locals. They re-tuned the guitars to produce a major chord with a simple strum, replaced cat-gut strings with metal ones, and used a metal bar or pipe slid along the strings. This playing style, difficult with a vertical guitar, led players to lay the instruments flat on their laps, giving rise to what became known as Hawaiian guitars and, later, lap steel guitars. Interest in these instruments significantly grew into the 1920s, attracting companies like George Beechum's. "By the late 1800s, European and Mexican sailors had brought Spanish guitars to Hawaii. The locals repurposed that guitar, tuning it so that a simple strum across the strings would produce a major chord without the need for messing around on the fretboard. And instead of using cat-cut, they used metal strings. The changed sounds, a metal bar or a piece of pipe, was slid along the strings. And since this was hard to do while holding the guitar vertically, players laid these Hawaiian guitars flatter on their lap. The interest in these guitars, soon called lap steel guitars, blew up to the 1920s, and several companies got into the business of making them."

**Non-Electric Amplification** : The acoustic resonator guitar was an innovation developed by John Dupira to make guitars louder without using electricity. It featured multiple aluminum cones affixed to the bridge, which amplified the instrument's sound in a manner similar to how large acoustic horns worked on gramophones and phonographs. These guitars gained considerable popularity. "Without getting too technical, the result was something called the acoustic resonator guitar. It wasn't powered by electricity, but it did feature a number of cones made out of aluminum that were affixed to the bridge, amplifying the instrument in the same way big acoustic horns were used on gramophones and photographs. These resonator guitars proved to be quite popular..."

"After much experimentation, a prototype was unveiled. It featured a round body attached to a long neck. It looked a lot like a banjo, but instead it acquired a different name, the frying pan. **Mechanism of Sound Production** : When the steel strings of the frying pan guitar were struck, picked, or strummed, their vibrations generated an electrical signal within the wire coil around a magnet (the pickup). This signal then traveled through a wire to an amplifier, allowing for adjustable volume control. "When its steel strings were struck or picked or strummed, the vibrations created an electrical signal in that coil of wire around the magnet. That signal then traveled down a wire to an amplifier, which could be adjusted to whatever volume you wanted."
**Commercial Availability and Initial Sales** : The frying pan was the first commercially available electric guitar, with the A25 model being introduced by Roe Pa Tyn in 1932. Despite its historical significance, it was not an immediate commercial success, selling only 28 units in 1932 due to the Great Depression. However, sales increased to almost 1300 by 1935. "While the frying pan was the first commercially available electric guitar, it wasn't exactly a hit. **Gage Brewer as First Customer** : Gage Brewer was the first customer to purchase a Rickenbacker A25, acquiring it on September 21, 1932. "It seems to be a guy named Gage Brewer, who bought an A25 on September 21, 1932. In fact, he was the guitar maker's first ever customer."
**A25 Naming Convention** : The "A" in Rickenbacker A25 indicated it was the company's first guitar model, while "25" referred to the 25-inch length of its neck. A later model, the A22, became a better seller. "The A indicated that it was the company's first guitar, and the 25 was the length in inches of its neck. **Elvino Ray's Modification for Aesthetics** : Shortly after its release, guitarist Elvino Ray purchased a frying pan guitar and modified its appearance by mounting it within a wooden frame to make it resemble a more conventional Spanish guitar, seeking to avoid questions about its unusual look. "Shortly afterwards, a guitarist named Elvino Ray bought a frying pan and mounted it inside a wooden frame so it looked more like a regular Spanish guitar. # instrument - Gibson ES150

## curiosities

**Bar Pickup Design** : The Gibson ES150 utilized a bar pickup, characterized by its long, hexagon shape. This pickup was uniquely positioned under the strings, rather than over them, which prevented interference with the player's performance. "It used what was known as a bar pickup. It had a long hexagon shape. Unlike other guitars, this pickup set under the strings not over the top, which tended to interfere with playing the instrument."

# instrument - The Log (Les Paul)

## curiosities
**Prototype for Solid-Body Design** : "The log" was an experimental electric guitar developed by Les Paul in 1939. It was constructed from a 4x4 piece of pine, with attached wings to mimic a Spanish guitar, a standard guitar neck, a bridge, and two crude homemade pickups. Its solid body was revolutionary at the time, departing from hollow designs. "The result was something he called the log. It was a 4x4 piece of pine with two wings attached to it to make it look like a Spanish guitar. He attached a regular guitar neck to that body, along with a bridge and two crude homemade pickups. Unlike all the other electric guitars in the market, this one wasn't hollow. Its body was solid."

**Game-Changing Electric Bass** : The Fender Precision Bass, often called the P-Bass, followed the Telecaster and represented another significant innovation. The Precision Bass solved this problem by offering a portable, Telecaster-sized instrument. "Hot on the heels of the Telecaster came the Fender Precision Base. This again was a game changer. **Frets for Accuracy** : A key feature of the Precision Bass was the inclusion of frets on its neck, a departure from the fretless double bass. This innovation allowed players to accurately place their fingers, minimizing "bum notes" and contributing to the instrument's name, "Precision." "Because it actually had flats on the neck, unlike a double base, the player knew exactly where the fingers had to go. Bum notes became a thing of the past, hence the name Precision."
**Widespread Adoption** : While not the first electric bass, the Precision Bass was the first to gain widespread attention and adoption. # instrument - Fender Stratocaster

## curiosities
**Collaborative Design for Comfort and Versatility** : Crafted in 1953 by Freddie Tavares and Leo Fender, in collaboration with country guitarists, the Stratocaster was designed with an innovative new shape for enhanced comfort. It could be played easily both sitting down (on the knee) and standing (balanced on a strap). Its body featured a slight contour on the back to fit comfortably against the player's ribs. "Working together with some country guitars, Freddie and Leo crafted a new guitar with a new shape that could be comfortably played both sitting down with the thing on your knee, or nicely balanced while hanging on a strap around your neck. And the back of the body is slightly contoured so it fits nicely against the player's ribs."
**Revolutionary Double Cutaway Design** : Unlike the Telecaster's single cutaway, the Stratocaster featured a double cutaway body shape. This design was revolutionary in 1953, as previous electric guitars largely mimicked traditional acoustic shapes. The double cutaway provided easier access for fingers to reach higher positions on the fretboard. "While the Telecaster is known as a single cutaway, the shape of the Stratocaster's body is a double cutaway. This makes it easier for the fingers to get way up high on the fretboard. All this was very revolutionary for 1953. Up until then, electric guitars were more or less crafted to ape the shape of a traditional acoustic guitar. This was something completely different."
**Enhanced Pickup and Tonal Options** : The Stratocaster was equipped with three pickups, one more than the Telecaster. It also included a switch that allowed players to select between these pickups, offering a greater variety of sounds. Additionally, its bridge could be precisely adjusted to accommodate different string types and playing styles. "Oh, and it had three pickups, one more than the Telecaster. And there was a switch that allowed the player to pop between each of the pickups to achieve different sounds. Plus, the bridge could be very precisely adjusted for different types of strings and playing styles."
**Marketing Name (Stratocaster Airplanes)** : The name "Stratocaster" was a marketing choice, echoing the "Stratocaster" airplanes that were capturing public attention in the 1950s. This mirrored Fender's earlier strategy of naming the Telecaster after television. "More marketing. In the 1950s, there were airplanes called Stratocasters that were capturing the public's attention. So just as the Telecaster's name was from television, another new thing, so did the name Stratocaster."
**Rapid Popularity** : The Stratocaster quickly gained immense popularity after its introduction. "Stratz became very popular, very fast."
**Notable Players** : The Stratocaster has been used by an extensive list of influential musicians, including Eric Clapton, The Edge, Kirk Cobain, John Fershianti of the Red Hot Chili Peppers, Mark Knopfler of Dire Straits, Body Rate, P-Towns, and John Lennon, among many others. **George Beechum's Vision for Guitar Amplification** : George Beechum realized that the same technology used to amplify faint radio signals could be applied to a guitar to increase its volume. "George Beachham realized that if this new technology could make a faint radio signal loud, why couldn't it do the same thing for a guitar? ... **Focus on String Amplification** : George Beechum theorized that the guitar's amplifying sound originated from its body, influenced by its strings. He therefore aimed to eliminate the body's role and directly amplify the strings themselves, leading to the development of the pickup. "He realized that the amplifying sound of a guitar came from the guitar's body. Sounds conferred to it by its strings. So why not just eliminate that second part?
