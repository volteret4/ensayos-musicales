# artist - The Velvet Underground
## members
Lou Reed
## albums
**Debut Album (1966) – Scepter Studios Sessions** : An acetate pressing from the Scepter Studios Sessions, which predated and contained material for the band's debut album, was discovered and later sold as the most valuable alt-rock collectible. This brittle aluminum acetate could only be played a limited number of times before being destroyed. "These were known as the Scepter Studios Sessions."
## songs
**Heroin (1966) – Alternate Take** : An alternate, formerly lost, and unknown take of "Heroin," a key song from the Velvet Underground's 1966 debut album, was found on a brittle aluminum acetate from the Scepter Studios Sessions. This test pressing, considered honest-to-god history from the band regarded as the first alt-rock group, was eventually ripped to MP3 to preserve it. "It's Lou Reed and the Velvet Underground with a different take of the song, Heroin."
## curiosities
**First True Alternative Band** : The Velvet Underground is widely considered perhaps the first true alternative band, pioneering a sound and approach that laid groundwork for the alt-rock movement. Their early work and influence are highly regarded in music history. "perhaps the first true alternative band."
**Most Valuable Alt-Rock Collectible (2006)** : A one-of-a-kind, undiscovered acetate pressing of the Scepter Studios Sessions for The Velvet Underground's debut album became the world's most valuable collectible alt-rock record. Discovered in September 2002 by Warren Hill for 75 cents at a street sale in New York City, it was eventually sold in December 2006 for $25,200 after an initial eBay bid of over $155,000 fell through. "making it, for the time being, the world's most valuable, collectible, alt-rock record."
**Brittle Aluminum Acetate** : The most valuable Velvet Underground collectible was a brittle, aluminum acetate pressing that contained an alternate take of "Heroin" from their Scepter Studios Sessions. Such a record could only be played about two dozen times before wearing out and being destroyed, highlighting its extreme fragility and rarity, though it was eventually ripped to MP3 for preservation. "Now a brittle, aluminum acetate, such as this, cannot be played more than two dozen times because it'll just wear out and be destroyed."

# artist - Sex Pistols
## labels
A&M Records
EMI
## songs
**God Save the Queen (1977)** : This 7-inch single, specifically the version released on A&M Records in 1977, was until recently the perennial holder of the high watermark for alt-rock collectibles. The band had a troubled history with record deals, being dropped by EMI and then by A&M after just five days. "God Save the Queen 7-inch single."
## curiosities
**Troubled Record Deals** : The Sex Pistols faced significant challenges in maintaining a record deal, initially being signed and then dropped by EMI. Subsequently, they signed with A&M Records, but this deal lasted only five days due to protests from other artists on the label, forcing management to revoke their contract. "See the pistols had a really hard time keeping a record deal."
**Destroyed A&M Singles** : During their brief five-day tenure with A&M Records in 1977, approximately 25,000 copies of their "God Save the Queen" 7-inch single were pressed. Following their dismissal, orders were given to destroy these singles, and most were, though several dozen copies were "liberated" from the pressing plant, with some given to executives and band members like Steve Jones, who was known to sell copies. "When the pistols were fired, those singles were ordered destroyed."
**Collectible Value of A&M Single** : The rare "God Save the Queen" 7-inch single released on A&M Records in 1977 has commanded high prices at auction, with one instance reaching $24,000. These few surviving copies, "liberated" after the band was dropped by the label and the remaining stock ordered destroyed, have historically held the record for the most valuable alt-rock collectible. "The last price I heard was $24,000."

## songs
**Somewhere There's An Angel (2007)** : This song, released in 2007 by Vancouver-based performer Dan O'Donnell under his professional name The Thirst in Revival, was issued as 100 vinyl copies with a retail price of $200 each. The song was inspired by Arthur Miller's play "The Crucible" and took two years to write. "a song called Somewhere There's An Angel in 2007."
## curiosities
**Highest-Priced Debut Single** : The Thirst in Revival, the professional name for Dan O'Donnell, released 100 vinyl copies of his debut single "Somewhere There's An Angel" in 2007, each priced at $200. This is considered, as far as anyone can tell, the highest price ever set for a debut single. "this is the highest price ever put on a debut single."
**Art Project Single Release** : Dan O'Donnell conceptualized the release of "Somewhere There's An Angel" as an art project, aiming to make a statement on the commodification and perceived devaluation of music. The project involved ten acclaimed British artists, each designing ten unique sleeves for the 100 vinyl copies, and the entire endeavor has been exhibited in galleries. "Dan looks at this as an art project. A statement on the commodification of music and how this kind of art has been devalued in the minds of the public."
**Inspiration from Arthur Miller** : The song "Somewhere There's An Angel" by The Thirst in Revival was directly inspired by the classic Arthur Miller play "The Crucible." The composition and writing process for this track took Dan O'Donnell two years to complete, reflecting a deep engagement with its thematic origins. "Dan says that this song was inspired by the classic Arthur Miller play The Crucible and it took him two years to write."

"Moby's Thousand reached number 38 on the British charts."

# curiosity

**Paul Mooney's World's Largest Private Record Collection (2008)** : Paul Mooney of Pittsburgh amassed the world's largest private record collection, which started about 50 years prior with a Frankie Lane 45. By early 2008, it contained over 3 million records (LPs, EPs, 78s, 45s, cassettes, eight tracks, CD singles, full albums) and 300,000 compact discs, covering every genre. The collection grew so vast that it required a 16,000 square foot climate-controlled warehouse, and was put up for sale on eBay for a minimum bid of $3 million, with an estimated worth closer to $50 million, though the auction fell through. "Paul Mooney, reluctant owner, still, of the world's largest private record collection."
**Beats Per Minute (BPM) as a Tempo Measurement** : BPM, or beats per minute, is a fundamental measurement of tempo, widely recognized in DJ culture for selecting music suitable for the dance floor. It ranges from 80-100 BPM for rock and hip hop, 120-135 BPM for house music, and 200+ BPM for genres like Gabber and Speedcore. "It means beats per minute. It's a measurement of tempo."

"Rebel XD, who wrapped 852 syllables in 42 seconds at a competition in Chicago in July the 27th of 2007."
**Largest Group Rendition of "Smoke on the Water" (2006)** : In 2006, Kansas City, Missouri, witnessed a record-breaking event where 1,721 people simultaneously played the iconic riff from "Smoke on the Water." This mass performance created one of the largest group renditions of a song. "2006, Kansas City, Missouri, 1,721 people riffing at the same time."
**Largest Group Rendition of Bob Dylan's "Knockin' on Heaven's Door" (2007)** : A new record for a large group rendition was set on October 26, 2007, in Shalong, Mejhalaya, India, where 1,730 people played Bob Dylan's "Knockin' on Heaven's Door" in unison. This surpassed the previous record for "Smoke on the Water." "on October 26, 2007 in the town of Shalong in the state of Mejhalaya in India, 1,730 people played Bob Dylan's Knock on On Heaven's Door in Unison."
