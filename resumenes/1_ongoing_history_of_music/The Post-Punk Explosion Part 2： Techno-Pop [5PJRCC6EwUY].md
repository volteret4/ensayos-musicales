# artist - The Normal (Daniel Miller)

## members
Daniel Miller

## labels
Rough Trade

## instruments
Korg 700S
4-track reel to reel machine

## songs
**Warm Leatherette (1978)** : Released in November 1978 under the name The Normal, this song was recorded by Daniel Miller in his bedroom using a Korg 700S synthesizer and a 4-track reel-to-reel machine, despite his lack of traditional musical experience. It became a hit, selling 30,000 copies after Rough Trade record shop in Notting Hill agreed to stock a test pressing, intriguing many young musicians with its minimalist, futuristic, and attitude-filled sound. "After it came out in November 1978, the record became a hit."

## curiosities
**Bedroom Punk Aesthetic with Synthesizers** : Daniel Miller, aspiring to be a punk but unable to play guitar, bought a Korg 700S and a 4-track reel-to-reel machine from his film editor savings. He recorded "Warm Leatherette" in his bedroom, creating a minimalist, futuristic sound with attitude, which sold 30,000 copies. This demonstrated that musical experience wasn't necessary for creating impactful electronic music, embodying a punk sensibility of DIY attitude over traditional ability. "He started experimenting with it in his bedroom. Recorded two songs, found some money to make a test pressing and convinced the rough-trade record shop in Notting Hill to stock it."
**Minimalist Sound as a Contrast** : The minimalist sound of Daniel Miller's "Warm Leatherette" served as a benchmark against which the later, more sonically advanced and textured Technopop songs from 1980-81 were compared, highlighting the rapid technological progress in a short period. "Remember what warm leatherettes sounded like? Now compare that minimalism to something like this, which came out less than two years ago."

## curiosities
**Pioneering All-Electronic New Wave** : OMD was identified as one of the most significant bands of the Technopop era and among the first of the new wave generation to fully embrace all-electronic sounds. Their approach was rooted in a punk rock aesthetic, despite limited traditional musical ability, focusing on creating new sounds with synthesizers. "The perfect example of late 70s technopop from orchestral maneuvers in the dark, they were one of the first of the new wave generation to turn to all electronic sounds."
**Punk Sensibility in Synthesizer Use** : Members Andy McCluskey and Paul Humphreys described their self-taught approach to music-making with synthesizers as having a "punk rock aesthetic." Instead of merely thrashing a guitar, they would play with two fingers on synths, creating sounds the "human brain had never heard before," which they found exciting compared to "rock and roll cliches" of traditional instrumentation. "But you were coming up with... You were coming up with sounds that the human brain had never heard before, which was the cool thing about these new machines. That was the exciting thing about using electronics was it gave us access to a pallet of sounds that were new and exciting, rather than just repeating what we saw in the mid to late 70s, is, you know, just rock and roll cliches."

The lyrics are based on his vision of a future where machines called "friends" provide services, with the friend in the song specifically depicted as a prostitute. ## curiosities

This spontaneous discovery radically altered his musical direction, leading him to abandon plans for a punk album and instead explore the concept of electronic punk songs, marking a pivotal moment in his career and the development of Technopop. "Another milestone in the creation of Tectopop came in the spring of 1979, when Gary Newman, previously a big fan of guitars, started playing with the new synthesizer someone had left in a studio, was a mini-mog."
**Robotic and Dystopian Aesthetic** : Gary Numan's "Are 'Friends' Electric?" showcased a vibe that was robotic, cold, deliberately artificial, emotionally sterile, and intense, mirroring the dystopian and alienated lyrical themes of early Technopop. This particular aesthetic was a slight variation of the nihilism found in guitar-driven punk. "And note the vibe of that song. Much of early technopop was robotic, cold, deliberately artificial, emotionally sterile, and just generally a little intense."

**Catalyst for Musicians Union Backlash Against Synths** : Barry Manilow became the "last straw" for the UK Musicians Union on May 23, 1982, prompting their public grievances against synthesizers. Previously touring with a string section, he replaced his orchestra with just a couple of synth players in 1981, leading to job losses for traditional musicians and escalating calls for a ban on synthesizers. "The last straw wasn't craftwork, or OMD, or Durand Durand, but very Manolo, one of the biggest mainstream artists at that time. In the past, Manolo had gone on tour with a string section, providing musicians with a solid living. But in 1981, he dumped the orchestra and toured with just a couple of synth players. That had to stop."

# genre - Technopop

## curiosities
**Emergence and Definition** : Technopop emerged in the post-punk era, specifically in the late 1970s and early 1980s, beginning in earnest around 1979. It is characterized as post-punk music where electronic keyboards and related gear, such as drum machines, were the dominant instruments, with real guitars and drums often absent from the mix. "And in the post-punk era, which is to say the late 70s and early 80s, we had the era of Technopop. Here's how that happened."
**Early Sound and Lyrical Themes** : Early Technopop, exemplified by bands like Kraftwerk, The Normal, and Gary Numan, featured a robotic, cold, deliberately artificial, emotionally sterile, and intense vibe. Lyrically, it explored themes of futurism, alienation, isolation, and an overall dystopian view of life, reflecting a variation of punk's nihilism. "Much of early technopop was robotic, cold, deliberately artificial, emotionally sterile, and just generally a little intense."
**Evolution of Sound (1980-81)** : Between 1980 and 1981, Technopop began to change significantly due to advancements in technology, allowing for more diverse sounds, especially dance speeds. Songs became less intense, singing more emotive, and instrumentation thicker, thanks to new polyphonic synthesizers and FM synthesis, moving away from thin, trebly sounds. "However, all this began to change in 1980 and 1981, when the technology advanced to allow for even more sounds, specifically dance speeds."
**Commercial Peak and Fragmentation (1981-82)** : Technopop reached its commercial peak in the winter of 1981-82, characterized by its accessibility and danceability, with bands like The Human League, Duran Duran, and Depeche Mode gaining global hits. During this time, the genre began to fragment into various sounds, with some artists remaining fully electronic while others reintroduced standard rhythm sections. "The early 80s saw the biggest explosion in music-making technology since the arrival of the electric guitar 30 years earlier."
**Decline in Pure Form (Mid-80s)** : While popular through 1983-85, pure Technopop began to decline by the mid-1980s for two main reasons: technology improved to the point where synthesized instruments were indistinguishable from real ones, and a backlash emerged from "real rock fans" and musicians who preferred traditional instrumentation. "Technopop continued to be very popular through 1983, 84 and 85, but by the middle of the decade things were changing."
**Lasting Influence and DNA** : Despite the decline of pure Technopop, its musical "DNA" persisted throughout the rest of the 1980s in groups like Depeche Mode (with modified sound), Bronski Beat, The Communards, Camouflage, When in Rome, Information Society, and Kon Kan. Synthesizers, originally central to Technopop, became commonplace in all kinds of non-Technopop applications. "Although the popularity of pure Technopop declined through the rest of the 80s, it didn't go quietly."

# instrument - Synthesizer

## curiosities
**Early Development (1940s-1960s)** : Work on the modern synthesizer began in the 1940s, accelerating in the 1950s with the transistor. Early models were gigantic, some filling entire rooms or requiring rail car transport. By the 1960s, engineers like Bob Moog and Don Buchla built smaller, though still temperamental, hard-to-program, and expensive units that produced unique sounds. "Work on what would become the modern synthesizer goes all the way back to the 1940s. With the advent of the transistor, research into making these kinds of sounds accelerated in the 1950s."
**Adoption in the Late 1970s** : By the late 1970s, synthesizer technology became cheap enough for young musicians, particularly those from the punk rock scene, to adopt them as their primary music-making machines, leading to the rise of genres like Technopop. "And at the very end of that decade, the technology had become cheap enough for young musicians in the last months of the original punk rock scene to adopt these music-making machines as their own."
**Monophonic vs. Polyphonic Evolution** : Early synthesizers were monophonic, meaning they could only play one note at a time, requiring laborious layering on tape machines to build chords. By 1980-81, the advent of polyphonic synthesizers revolutionized this, allowing multiple notes to be played simultaneously, greatly enhancing sonic texture and complexity. "You could only play one note at a time on the old synths, the monophonic ones, so if you wanted to build up a chord, you needed to layer each new note on a new track on the tape machine. Not with the polyphonic machines."
**Technological Advancements (Early 1980s)** : As 1980 turned to 1981, synthesizer technology advanced rapidly, making them cheaper, more portable, and easier to use. This period saw the biggest explosion in music-making technology since the electric guitar's arrival 30 years prior, greatly expanding musical possibilities. "As 1980 turned to 1981, the advances in synth technology just kept coming. They were also cheaper. They were more portable. They were easier to use."
**Backlash and Musicians Union Concerns (1982)** : The increasing popularity of synth music led to a significant backlash, particularly from "real rock fans" and professional musicians. On May 23, 1982, the UK Musicians Union publicly aired grievances, feeling that synthesizers threatened jobs. Prompted by Barry Manilow replacing his string section with synth players, a motion passed calling for a ban on all synthesizers, though this was widely dismissed as "loony." "Synth-based music was so popular that a backlash built. Real rock fans pushed back against Technopop bands because they weren't really playing instruments. They were just programming machines to do the work for them."
**Ubiquitous Integration (Mid-to-Late 1980s)** : By the mid-1980s, synthesizer technology had improved to such an extent that it became difficult to distinguish between real and synthesized instruments. Synthesizers transitioned from being new and futuristic to becoming a common component used across various non-Technopop musical applications. "Meanwhile synth became so common that everybody was using them in all kinds of non-technopop applications. What was once new and futuristic had turned into the common present."

# instrument - MIDI

## curiosities
**Universal Synthesizer Interface Standard (1981-82)** : MIDI, or Musical Instrument Digital Interface, originated from an engineering paper called "Universal Synthesizer Interface" and became a crucial worldwide standard for connecting electronic instruments. Developed around 1981-82, a single MIDI cable could carry 16 channels of information, enabling up to 16 devices to communicate. This meant playing one note on a lead unit could trigger events on up to 15 other devices, effectively turning one musician into a "one-person orchestra" if programmed correctly, vastly expanding creative possibilities in professional studios. "MIDI stands for Musical Instrument Digital Interface. It originated in an engineering paper called Universal Synthesizer Interface and became the new worldwide standard for allowing all these new electronic instruments to talk to each other."
