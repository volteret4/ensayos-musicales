# artist - Foo Fighters

## members
Dave Grohl

## songs
**Everlong (1997) – Whispering Section Is Three Layers of Completely Meaningless Text** : During recording of *The Colour and the Shape*, Dave Grohl wanted to create a creepy, mysterious effect in the whispering passage around the three-minute mark. He had producer Gil Norton record him in three separate passes: the first reading from a love letter, the second from a technical manual, and the third telling a story about one of the studio technicians working that day. The three layers were mixed over each other in such a way that nothing is decipherable — by design. Whatever Dave said is entirely meaningless. "It sounds cool, but it has no meaning whatsoever."


# artist - The Clash

## members
Topper Headon
Joe Strummer

## songs
**Rock the Casbah (1982) – Topper's Piano Riff, Strummer's Lyrics from Iranian Flogging News, Possible Algerian Influence** : Drummer Topper Headon came up with the piano boogie that forms the song's basis. Joe Strummer wrote the lyrics after hearing a news report that people in Iran were being flogged for owning rock albums. However, there may be a second origin. In 1981, after a Clash show in Paris, an Algerian singer-songwriter named Rachid Taha — leader of a French-Arab punk band called Carte de Séjour — handed Joe a cassette demo of his music on the street. Taha says he never heard back from Joe, but a year later he turned on the radio and heard "Rock the Casbah," which stylistically resembled much of what he had given Joe that night. Whether the song was inspired by Taha's demo remains unconfirmed; Taha never seemed bitter and even recorded his own cover version. "Was Rock the Casbah inspired by what was handed to Joe on the sidewalk that night in Paris? Well, maybe."


# artist - Radiohead

## members
Thom Yorke

## albums
**In Rainbows (2007) – Four-and-a-Half-Year Gap Blamed on Thom Yorke's Climate Change Panic Attacks** : After *Hail to the Thief* in July 2003, a new Radiohead album did not appear until October 10, 2007 — a gap of over four years. Thom Yorke later admitted that in the spring of 2005 he was suffering writer's block caused by panic attacks triggered by his fears over global warming. He acknowledged being obsessed by the subject to the point where he could not work on new music. Once he immersed himself in researching the problem and found some framework for solutions, the block lifted and he was able to write again. "This was something that was obsessing me and creating a writer's block."


# artist - Green Day

## albums
**Cigarettes and Valentines (2002–2003) – Nearly Complete Album Stolen; Band Started Over, Created American Idiot** : By 2002, Green Day's career had stalled; their last proper album was years behind them. They recorded a follow-up called *Cigarettes and Valentines* that was almost finished when the master tapes disappeared — apparently stolen by persons unknown. Singer Billie Joe Armstrong insisted the album contained strong material; bassist Mike Dirnt acknowledged backup tapes existed but said they were inferior. The band started over entirely, creating *American Idiot*, which sold in the hundreds of millions and relaunched their career. The stolen album has never surfaced online or in bootleg form. Further suspicion: in September 2003, a mystery group called The Network released a record on Adeline Records — a label owned by Armstrong — featuring a middle section strikingly similar to an early Armstrong demo. Their identities were concealed with disguises and they staged mock feuds with Green Day. "Was there a Cigarettes and Valentines album or was it and the whole Network thing just a big joke?"


# artist - U2

## members
Bono
The Edge

## songs
**Where the Streets Have No Name (1987) – Engineer Physically Blocked Brian Eno from Erasing Tape** : The Edge brought demos to the *Joshua Tree* sessions declaring he had written "the most amazing guitar part he'd ever written in his life" and did a little dance in his home studio. The band then spent enormous amounts of rehearsal time and recording tape unable to nail the song — the timing changes and chord changes proved too complex. Co-producer Brian Eno became so frustrated that he proposed erasing the tape and starting from scratch. Engineer Pat McCarthy disagreed and physically blocked access to the tape machine with his body, resulting in a physical confrontation. The band eventually built the song from three separate basic takes, making it a Frankenstein composite. Even then, Eno and co-producer Daniel Lanois found it too much; Steve Lillywhite was brought in to finish the track, to Eno's annoyance. The result opens the album. "There's the intro, the first two verses, and the third verse. This is a Frankenstein monster of a song."

## curiosities
**Stolen Briefcase (Portland, March 22, 1981) – 70 Pages of U2 Lyrics; Returned 23 Years Later** : After a show at the Foghorn bar in Portland, Oregon, on March 22, 1981, three groupies visited backstage. After they left, Bono realized one had taken his briefcase containing $300 cash, his visa card in his real name Paul Hewson, tour documents, photos, fan letters, personal correspondence from his wife, and notebooks holding 70 pages of lyrics destined for U2's second album — material that had taken three years to accumulate. The resulting crisis forced Bono to improvise most of the lyrics for the *October* album in the studio, which is widely held to explain that album's unfinished quality. In 2003, a woman from Olympia, Washington, named Danielle Ruyom contacted U2: her friend Cindy Harris had found the briefcase's contents in the attic of a house she'd purchased in Tacoma. After a year of negotiations through multiple layers of U2 management, they met Bono on October 20, 2004, and returned everything except the cash and the briefcase itself. "The only thing that was really missing was the case itself."


# artist - New Order

## members
Bernard Sumner
Gillian Gilbert
Stephen Morris

## songs
**Blue Monday (1983) – Written by Accident, Recorded on LSD, Lost Money on Every Copy Sold** : New Order were devising a way to leave the stage early without audiences noticing, by running Stephen Morris's drum machine through the end of their set. They added sequenced keyboard parts and a bass line, and before long had the foundation of a complete song — all written by machines. The full recording session took place while the band was loaded on LSD; the engineers sent them to a café across the street to finish mixing in peace. During production, Gillian Gilbert accidentally missed programming one note in the melody, leaving it slightly out of time with the rhythm track — a mistake they kept because it sounded better. "Blue Monday" was released only as an 8-minute 12-inch single and became the best-selling 12-inch in music history. However, the elaborate die-cut packaging was so expensive to produce that New Order and the label lost money on every copy they sold. "New Order had the foundations of a new song, and it was all being played entirely by machines."


# artist - Elvis Costello

## curiosities
**My Aim Is True (1977) – Debut Album Recorded by Clover, Who Became Huey Lewis and the News** : Before his music career, Elvis Costello worked as a computer operator for Elizabeth Arden cosmetics, running invoices on an IBM 360 mainframe, taking occasional sick days to demo songs. When he signed to Stiff Records, he needed a backing band. An American group called Clover had just arrived in England hoping for their big break, but found themselves stranded in the middle of punk and unwanted. They needed money for plane tickets home. Elvis went to their residency at Headley Grange, told them he needed a studio band and specifically didn't need a singer, so Clover's own singer was sent to wait elsewhere while the rest of the band backed Elvis through six four-hour sessions for his debut. Clover was paid and sent back to the US, where they reinvented themselves as Huey Lewis and the News. The singer who had to hang out during the Costello sessions was Huey Lewis. "That banished singer was Huey Lewis."

## curiosities
**Give It Away Remix (1991) – Ministry's Al Jourgensen Used a Stoned Chicken on the Mixing Console** : Al Jourgensen was invited by the Red Hot Chili Peppers to produce a remix of "Give It Away" for their *Blood Sugar Sex Magik* album. His reported method: he brought a live chicken into the studio, blew marijuana smoke in its face until the bird was intoxicated, then let the stoned chicken stagger across the mixing desk. Wherever the chicken left a dropping, Jourgensen made an adjustment to the mix. "He brought a chicken into the studio and blew pot smoke in the chicken's face... Where the chicken pooped, Al made an adjustment to the mix."


# curiosity

**The Amen Break (1969) – 6-Second Drum Sample from The Winstons That Became the Foundation of an Era** : The Amen Break is a six-second drum solo performed by GC Coleman on "Amen Brother," the B-side to a 1969 Grammy-winning single called "Color Him Father" by a funk soul group called The Winstons. When sampling became widespread in the 1980s and 1990s, this passage was discovered and became one of the most recycled drum breaks in history, appearing in thousands of songs across hip-hop, rave, techno, jungle, and drum and bass. Documented uses include Linkin Park's "Faint," N.W.A.'s "Straight Outta Compton," Public Enemy's "Fight the Power," Oasis's "D'You Know What I Mean," and The Prodigy's "Firestarter." Despite this ubiquity, neither GC Coleman the drummer nor Richard L. Spencer the composer has received a single cent in royalties from any of these uses. "Since then, the Amen Break has been sliced and diced and rearranged into a million different ways in a million different songs."
