**"Coming of Age" Musical Immersion (Ages 14-24)** : Most individuals are deeply immersed in music and music culture between the ages of 14 and 24. The songs listened to during this period significantly shape an individual's musical taste for their entire life. However, once people reach their mid-20s, adult responsibilities like jobs, families, and mortgages often reduce the time, money, and inclination to actively seek out new music and attend shows. "The songs we listen to then sets our musical taste for the rest of our life."
**Spotify Data on Taste Formation (New York Times Analysis)** : An analysis of Spotify data by The New York Times revealed specific age ranges for musical taste formation. For men, the most critical period is between 13 and 16, with their all-time favorite song typically released when they were 14. For women, the most impressionable years are 11 to 14, with their favorite song likely released when they were 13. This data suggests that childhood or preteen musical influences might be stronger for women. "The most important period for forming musical taste is between the ages of 13 and 16. On average, men were 14 when their all-time favorite song was released. It's a little different for women. Their most impressionable years for music are between 11 and 14. Their favorite song probably came out when they were 13."
**Openness to Music (Adolescence vs. Adulthood)** : Young people, particularly adolescents, exhibit greater "open iridness" or openness to music, including unfamiliar styles. A 2013 study of 250,000 people found that adolescents devoted 20% of their waking time to music, compared to 13% in adulthood. This decline in adulthood is partly due to increased busyness but also to "psychosocial maturation," where adults rely less on external factors like music to define their identity. "In fact, our desire for musical discovery and preference for new music is at its all-time peak when we are 23 for women and 24 for men."
**Maturation of Musical Tastes (Mid-30s)** : Social scientists observe that by their mid-30s, people's musical tastes have generally "matured." This is linked to one of the five personality traits, "openness to experience," which tends to be higher in youth. As individuals age, they become more discerning, moving beyond "Top 40" pop to seek music that is "cooler, more substantial, more meaningful, more complex, and more cool." "But by the time we're in our mid-30s, the social scientists say that our tastes have matured."
**Hearing Acuity and Musical Preference with Age** : As individuals age, their "hearing acuity" changes, often resulting in a lower tolerance for loud sounds and high frequencies. This physiological change can contribute to a preference for milder music, as high volume, thumping beats, and high BPMs become less appealing. The stress and cortisol from adult life further reduce the need for adrenaline-inducing music, which may even become irritating. "We can, over time, acquire a lower tolerance for loud sounds and audio with high frequencies, so you can see why that would be an issue."
**Abe Simpson's Prophecy of Musical Aging** : The character Abe Simpson famously articulated a common sentiment about aging and musical taste: "I used to be with it, but then they changed what it was. Now what I'm with isn't it? And what's it seems weird and scary to me. It'll happen to you." This quote from The Simpsons serves as a cultural reference for the inevitable shift in musical relevance and personal preference as one gets older. "I used to be with it, but then they changed what it was. Now what I'm with isn't it? And what's it seems weird and scary to me. It'll happen to you."
**John S. Dwight's 19th-Century Critique of New Music** : John S. Dwight, a 19th-century composer of hymns, critically described the "new music of his day." He observed that such tunes, though widely whistled and sung, were not truly embraced with "musical emotion" and instead "haunt the morbidly sensitive nerves of deeply musical persons," who might hum them involuntarily while "hating them." He likened their pervasive nature to a "morbid irritation of the skin." "Such tunes, although whistled and sung by everybody, are erroneously supposed to have taken a deep hold of the popular mind. But they are hummed and whistled without musical emotion."
**Onset of Musical Paralysis (Ages 27 and 33)** : Research from 2023 involving American Spotify and Amazon Echo users indicates that around age 33, individuals typically begin to "drift away from new music," perceiving it as "a racket, a cacophony, noise," signifying a type of "musical paralysis." This threshold drops to age 27 for those with children, likely due to constant exposure to contemporary pop music played by their kids. "It found that by the time we turned 33, we start to not only drift away from new music, the new music of the day, but we find it to be a rocket, a cacophony, noise."
**Rediscovering "Missed" Music from Youth** : When current music loses its appeal, many adults revert to their "coming of age years" (14-24) to explore songs, albums, and artists they might have missed. This includes music that was "too radical and too different" or simply "fell through the cracks" due to the rapid absorption of new sounds. This material, though decades old, feels "new to you" and offers the familiar power and energy of that era. "So what do many of us do? We go back to those coming of age years between 14 and 24 and start exploring songs, albums, and artists that we missed the first time around."
**Mid-Age Crises and the "Death of Coolness Spiral" (Ages 42-44)** : Around age 42, many individuals experience an "existential mid-age crisis" regarding music, attempting for 12-18 months to reconnect with the music world and recapture the "emotional glory of music discovery" from two decades prior. However, by age 44, most revert to "comfort food music" from their youth, concluding that "this new stuff sucks." This phenomenon is referred to as the "death of coolness spiral," which can be graphically plotted as a line spiraling outwards from the center, showing taste deviating from current music with age. "Around the age of 42, many of us enter existential mid-age crises when it comes to music."
**Musical Knowledge and Deja Vu** : As listeners become more experienced and knowledgeable, they begin to notice cycles and trends in music, leading to a sense of déjà vu. Current sounds may seem familiar, as "history is on repeat," with old elements being recycled and rehashed. For instance, a band like Beauty School Dropout might evoke Blink-182, which descended from Green Day, which in turn incorporated elements of The Ramones' fast 60s pop songs. "Our musical knowledge can make things very annoying because it makes seem that history is on repeat."
**Independent Rediscovery of Musical Styles** : There is a recurring pattern where specific musical styles are "independently rediscovered over and over and over again by subsequent generations." This explains why bands like Greta Van Fleet can sound fresh to young audiences while evoking older bands like Led Zeppelin for experienced listeners.
**Influence of Streaming and Parental Music Collections** : Modern young musicians benefit from unprecedented access to the "entirety of the world's music" through streaming platforms. Additionally, many have "music junkie" parents who eagerly shared their record collections, exposing them to a wide range of older music that can shape their initial artistic directions. "Second, today's young musicians have the entirety of the world's music at their fingertips through streaming platforms."
**Nostalgia: Definition and History** : Nostalgia is broadly defined as "being sentimental about the past," functioning as a "psychic happy place." The term originated in the 16th century with a medical student observing Swiss mercenaries exhibiting anxiety away from home, initially viewing it as a form of melancholy or mental illness. Today, it generally refers to a "pining for the good old days." "Broadly defined, nostalgia is being sentimental about the past. It's a psychic happy place."
**Nostalgic "Sweet Spot" in Youth** : The "good old days for music" typically refer to the period when an individual was around 14 years old and extending into their early 20s. Studies place the specific "nostalgic sweet spot" at ages 17 or 19. This trend remains stable over many years, marking the "all-important coming of age musically years." "Other studies have shown that the nostalgic sweet spot is at age 17."
**Exploitation of Nostalgia by the Music Industry** : Marketers, the music industry, and concert promoters actively leverage the powerful force of nostalgia. This explains why "legacy bands" (acts popular before 2000) continue to "fill arenas," with Gen Xers and increasing numbers of Gen Y fans willing to pay significant amounts to "relive their youth" by seeing bands they connected with as teenagers. "Nostalgia can be a very, very powerful thing. Marketers know this. The music industry knows this. And promoters know this."
**Aging Festival Headliners and Top-Grossing Tours** : The phenomenon of nostalgia is evident in the increasing age of festival headliners, where the average age of a lead singer or solo artist at major festivals is now "at least 50 and is quickly moving higher." Furthermore, excluding contemporary pop acts like Ed Sheeran, Beyonce, and Taylor Swift, the "vast majority of the all-time top-grossing tours" are by acts with an average age of 45 and above. "The average age of a lead singer or solo artist at a major festival is now at least 50 and is quickly moving higher."
**Stagnation in Rock and Hip Hop** : The question of whether current music is as "good" as older music is highly subjective and technical. It's argued that rock, being a relatively young genre, was once constantly evolving, allowing bands like The Beatles, Jimi Hendrix, Eddie Van Halen, Black Sabbath, and Led Zeppelin to be "first" in their innovations. However, rock is perceived to have experienced a "stagnation" as its sound hasn't revolutionized in a long time. Similarly, hip hop, despite being "game changing," has "hit something of a wall," creating its own generation gap. "And let's face it, there hasn't been a revolution in the sound of rock in forever."
**Limits of Western Musical Structure and Repetition** : The Western musical scale comprises only 12 notes, with a finite number of ways to combine them aesthetically and create chord changes. As the current era of popular music, dating back to early 1950s rock and roll, approaches 70 years of age, repetition and potential "stagnation" are inevitable. "There are only 12 notes in the Western musical scale and there are only so many to put those notes together in an aesthetically pleasing manner. There are finite number of chord changes..."
**Ease of Modern Music Creation** : The ease of music creation today is exemplified by software like Ableton Live and GarageBand (with its included "Beats and Stims"), songwriting methods such as "Top Lines and Beats," and the ability to purchase "ready-made beats online for a few bucks." Once created, a song can be uploaded to Spotify "for nothing" and made available globally. This contrasts with older methods that required mastering instruments and navigating a limited industry. "Others will point to the ease with which music can be created today."
**Lifelong Relationship with Music and Evolving Tastes** : Studies consistently show that an engagement with music in childhood lasts a lifetime. While involvement with new music may decrease with age, music remains central to life, evolving to meet changing psychological and social needs. Tastes may mellow (e.g., from Nine Inch Nails to Fleet Foxes), or individuals might seek greater complexity, challenge, and sophistication in jazz or classical music. The purpose of music shifts from identity formation to intimate relationships and personal enjoyment, focusing on positive and relaxing material chosen strictly for oneself. "The music you're into will change to meet your psychological and social needs. Your relationship with music will evolve, but you will never be able to do without music."
**Generational Right to Believe Youth Music is Greatest** : A fundamental truth about music and age is that "every generation has the right to believe that the music of their youth is the greatest music of all time." This belief is universal and immune to individual exceptions. "every generation has a right to believe that the music of their youth is the greatest music of all time."
