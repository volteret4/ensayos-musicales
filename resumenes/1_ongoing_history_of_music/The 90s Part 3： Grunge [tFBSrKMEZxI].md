# genre - Alternative
## curiosities
**Pre-Grunge Definition** : Up until the 1990s, "Alternative" was an umbrella term for a wide variety of sounds that were "left of center," "weird to the mainstream music fan," and "ignored by the media." It included singer-songwriters, indie pop, industrial bands, synth groups, goth groups, noisy guitar bands, and even rap in the 80s. "There wasn't what anyone could call a defining sound."
**Defining Attitude of the 1980s** : In the late 1980s, the term "Alternative" began to describe a certain attitude in rock music, encompassing anything that couldn't fit into regular categories, characterized by being new, weird, and hated by the mainstream. "If you couldn't categorize a song or an artist by tossing it into any of the regular buckets, then there was only one other bucket you could use, and it quickly filled up."
**Post-Grunge Mainstream Shift** : After the rise of grunge, alternative music, largely thanks to Nirvana's "Nevermind," eventually became mainstream music, losing its "alternative" status as it grew too big too fast, with guitar-bass-drums bands dominating. "It wasn't long before alt rock was no longer an alternative to anything."

# genre - Alt Rock
## curiosities
**Pre-Grunge Diversity** : Before the emergence of grunge, Alt Rock encompassed a vast variety of adventurous, different, and sometimes weird sounds, with many different types of artists, including guitar bands, synth-bass groups, singer-songwriters, industrial groups, electronic outfits, and dancey bands. "a vast variety of sounds that were adventurous, different, and sometimes weird."
**Post-Grunge Shift to Guitar-Centric** : Grunge decidedly swung Alt Rock towards big guitar bands, making them the most public and accepted face of the genre, though variety still existed below the surface. "Grunge though swung things decidedly in the direction of big guitars."

# genre - Grunge
## curiosities
**Codification of Alternative** : Grunge emerged as a defining sound that codified the previously scattered alternative rock universe, providing a central point around which everything else could coalesce and organize, making Alt Rock unstoppable for a period. "But then along came something that sort of codified everything, something around which everything else could coalesce and organize."
**Characteristics of the Sound** : Grunge was characterized by low-tuned guitars and an anti-star image for its frontmen and women, becoming the most familiar sound of the 90s Alt Rock explosion and the genre around which all others evolved for years. "Grunge moved from being a sea of different sounds to one that centered on low-tuned guitars and anti-star frontman and women."
**Generational Impact** : Grunge was Generation X's favorite music and served as a "gateway drug" into many other forms of Alt Rock, leading to movements like the mid-90s punk revival (Green Day, The Offspring), aggressive hardcore and industrial sounds, and an uptick in goth music. "Grunge was not only generation X's favorite music, but the gateway drug into so many other forms of Alt Rock."
**Influence and Longevity** : Despite its peak lasting only a couple of years, grunge's immense popularity and influence means it has "never really gone away," changing rock forever, even if surviving bands have distanced themselves from the "G-word." "Even though it's peak lasted for just a couple of years, Grunge was so popular and so influential, it's like it's never really gone away."
**Reactionary Nature** : Like punk, grunge was a reactionary movement that stripped away the "artifice and goop" attached to 1980s rock, such as elaborate costuming, makeup, and MTV video aesthetics, rejecting the image of rich rock stars with lavish lifestyles. "Like punk, Grunge was reactionary. It was a stripping back of all the artifice and goop that had become attached to rock through the 1980s..."
**Pacific Northwest Origins** : The genre developed among a small group of musicians and fans in the Pacific Northwest of the United States, in a "petri dish" environment. This unique mix included influences from classic rock like Black Sabbath, Kiss, and Led Zeppelin, alongside hardcore punk from California bands like Dead Kennedys, Black Flag, and Bad Religion, combined with local factors like rainy weather, coffee, drugs, universities, and isolation from major tours. "But something was happening amongst a small group of musicians and fans in the Pacific Northwest of the United States."
**Cultural Identity** : Grunge transcended mere music; it was a comprehensive culture with its own rules, goals, politics, aesthetic, philosophy, and fashion sense, eventually developing its own class system. "Grunge wasn't just a type of music. It was a culture with its own rules and goals and politics."
**Core Principles** : The grunge movement valued authenticity, humbleness, and "street cred," actively downplaying and avoiding the concept of conventional stardom. It embraced "slacker culture" with its "apathetic egalitarianism," focusing on the people, the fans, and "real, honest music." "Gen X wanted music of their own, music made by their own people that dealt with their own issues and attitudes. And they let it be known that they valued authenticity, humbleness, and street cred."
**"Holy Trinity" of Albums** : The foundation of grunge was formed by three pivotal albums released in the fall of 1991: Pearl Jam's "Ten" (August 27, 1991), Nirvana's "Nevermind" (single August 27, 1991, album September 24, 1991), and Soundgarden's "Badmotorfinger" (October 8, 1991). "Three records formed the foundation of Grunge."
**Reshaping Mainstream Rock** : From 1991 to 1993, grunge profoundly reshaped mainstream rock, with bands like Pearl Jam, Nirvana, and Soundgarden selling millions of records and acting as a "gateway drug" to the broader world of alternative rock. "As we brought through 1991, 1992, and 1993, grunge helped reshape mainstream rock."
**Unveiling the Alternative Universe** : Grunge "punched a hole" into the parallel universe of alternative rock, which had existed for decades alongside mainstream rock, making its diverse artists and music visible and acknowledged by the general public for the first time. "But grunge punched a hole into this parallel universe, allowing everyone to see what was going on over there."
**Industry Exploitation and Sound-Alikes** : Record companies, eager to capitalize on new trends, began signing and marketing alternative groups, leading to an "updraft" that benefited existing bands like Red Hot Chili Peppers and R.E.M., and also propelled new groups, regardless of origin, if they sounded like grunge (low-tuned guitars, slacker heart). "Started signing up and really marketing groups from the alternative side of town."
**Widespread Saturation** : Grunge expanded rapidly from its Seattle origins to become a global phenomenon, leading to a "frenzy and saturation and delusion" where places like Halifax were briefly dubbed "Seattle East" due to the widespread obsession. "that's the kind of frenzy and saturation and delusion that we began to see as grunge expanded from its little corner of America to, well, everywhere."
**Influence on Britpop** : The Britpop scene of the mid-1990s, a nationalistic celebration of British music, was prompted in large part by grunge's popularity, as certain people in the UK were disturbed by the success of American bands with their flannel and detuned guitars. "Consider the Britpop scene in the middle 1990s. That sound, that nationalistic celebration of British music heritage, was prompted in large part by grunge."
**Purist Backlash and Fragmentation** : The mainstreaming of grunge led to an "existential crisis" among purists who questioned what was truly alternative. The inclusion of Metallica on the 1996 Lollapalooza tour exemplified this, causing original alternative fans to lose interest and drift away, marking the beginning of a massive fragmentation of the once cohesive scene. "This caused an existential crisis amongst purists who began to question who and what was truly alternative and who and what wasn't."
**Aging of Generation X and Artists** : As the Gen Xers who championed grunge grew up and moved on, so did the artists. This, coupled with events like Kurt Cobain's death, band issues (Smashing Pumpkins), hiatuses (Alice in Chains), and breakups (Soundgarden), alongside Pearl Jam's deliberate distancing, contributed to the scene's decline. "Besides, the Gen Xers who made grunge when a wad's grew up, got jobs, started families and basically moved on. And so did the artists who made the scene possible."
**Becoming the New Classic Rock** : Today, grunge has, "in some ways," become the "new classic rock," with acts from that era still being promoted and remaining popular. "Well, in some ways, grunge has become the new classic rock."
**Enduring Quality** : Grunge is ultimately "destined to endure" because, like other influential sounds, "good music is good music." "Grunge is destined to endure like so many other sounds that came before it. Because, well, like I said, good music is good music, right?"

**Low-Tuned Sound of Grunge** : Guitars in grunge were famously "tuned low," with techniques like "drop D tuning" becoming common, which contributed to a "darker and dirtier feel" that defined the genre's sound. "Guitars were toot lower. You may have heard terms like drop D tuning, which gave them a darker and dirtier feel."
**Post-Grunge Dominance** : After grunge, guitars, along with bass and drums, came to rule rock, though the way they were played differed, often involving lower tunings. "And this meant that guitars, bass and drums ruled rock, but the way they were played was different."

# instrument - Bass
## curiosities
**Distinctive Grunge Playing** : Bassists in the grunge era adopted a different playing style, characterized by lines that were often "slinky and burbly," sometimes even playing power chords as exemplified by Ben Shepherd of Soundgarden. "And people started playing the bass differently. The lines were often, I don't know, slinky and burbly."
**Foundation for Grunge Bands** : Bass playing was crucial for holding grunge bands together and providing a cool foundation for their sound, as demonstrated by Chris Novoselic in Nirvana and Mike Starr in Alice in Chains. "Listen to how Chris Nova Celich's low end held Nirvana together. Go back and listen to some of those Allison Chains records and hear how Mike Star provided a cool foundation for everything."

# instrument - Drums
## curiosities
**Inventive Grunge Rhythms** : Drummers in grunge were characterized as "hard hitting" and developed "new ways of filling holes with inventive and intricate rhythms," contributing significantly to the genre's distinct sound. "Drummers were hard hitting and worked out new ways of filling holes with inventive and intricate rhythms."

# curiosity
**Alternative's Defining Sound in the 80s** : In the late 1980s, "Alternative" began to be used to describe an attitude in rock, acting as an umbrella term for artists that couldn't be categorized by regular genres. This included singer-songwriters, indie pop, industrial, synth-based, goth, noisy guitar bands, and even rap, signifying anything new, weird, and hated by the mainstream. "If you couldn't categorize a song or an artist by tossing it into any of the regular buckets, then there was only one other bucket you could use, and it quickly filled up."
**1980s Rock Artifice** : Rock music in the 1980s had become heavily laden with "artifice and goop," including excessive costuming, makeup, and focus on image in music videos played on MTV, leading to a reactionary movement like grunge that stripped these elements back. "It was a stripping back of all the artifice and goop that had become attached to rock through the 1980s, things like costuming and makeup and how you looked at that music video that played on MTV."
**Generation X's Discontent** : Generation X, the children of baby boomers, found the late 1980s a "pretty dire time to be a rock fan" due to the "absolutely terrible" pop music (New Kids on the Block, Debbie Gibson, Tiffany) and the prevalence of "rich rock stars with cars and chicks and private jets and mansions in the South of France playing the same old classic rock songs to baby boomers." "If you were part of Generation X, the children of the baby boomers, it was a pretty dire time to be a rock fan."
**Pacific Northwest as a "Petri Dish"** : The Pacific Northwest fostered grunge by blending diverse musical influences like Black Sabbath, Kiss, Led Zeppelin, and hardcore punk (Dead Kennedys, Black Flag, Bad Religion) with local conditions such as rainy weather, coffee, drugs, universities, and isolation from major tours, allowing these influences to develop "unmolested." "Mixing rainy weather, lots of coffee, a variety of drugs, a ton of universities, and the fact that the area wasn't included on the itineraries of a lot of major tours, and you end up with a petri dish, all these influences that were allowed to develop unmolecced it."
**Grunge's Perfect Confluence** : The rise of grunge was propelled by a "perfect confluence of events and conditions" involving art, economics, demographics, and social issues, primarily centered around Generation X. "It arrived at one of those perfect confluence of events and conditions involving art, economics, demographics, and social issues."
**Generation X's Economic and Social Mood** : At the beginning of the 1990s, Gen X faced a recession, unemployment, a crisis of confidence, and the First Gulf War, which created a "dark mood" that demanded heavier, angrier, and more aggressive music than the hair metal ballads and pop of the late 1980s. "The mood was dark and that called for music to reflect that, something heavier and angrier and more aggressive than the endless hair metal ballads and the pop music that we heard at the end of the 1980s."
**Generation X's Musical Coming of Age** : A large segment of Gen X, aged 15-25, was at a crucial point in their lives where music is most defining. They rejected their parents' and older siblings' music—late 80s classic rock "dinosaurs" (Van Halen, Aerosmith, Lynyrd Skynyrd), and pop and hair metal—in favor of authentic music created by and for their own generation. "Gen X was also at the point in their lives when they were coming of age musically. A huge bulge of them were in that sweet spot age range, 15 to 25. The time in our lives when music seems to be one of the most defining things of our being."
**Slacker Culture and Authenticity** : The rise of grunge coincided with "slacker culture," which promoted "apathetic egalitarianism" and a rejection of traditional stardom, emphasizing authenticity, humbleness, and "street cred" in music that was truly "about the people, about the fans, and about real, honest music." "Slacker culture, with its sense of apathetic egalitarianism, took over, it was supposed to be about the people, about the fans, and about real, honest music."
**Music Industry's New Trend Exploitation** : Record companies, always eager to identify and exploit new trends, actively began signing and marketing groups from the "alternative side of town" after grunge broke through, extending opportunities even to new groups outside Seattle who fit the grunge sound. "Meanwhile, record companies always eager to identify and exploit new trends and tastes. Started signing up and really marketing groups from the alternative side of town."
**Fragmentation by New Metal and File Sharing** : The once cohesive alternative scene began to fragment significantly after grunge's peak. The emergence of new metal further polarized tastes, and the widespread adoption of file sharing in the late 1990s via programs like Napster allowed individuals to explore diverse music freely, ending any hope of reuniting the "old alternative nation." "With new metal came along a few years later, things were further polarized. And when file sharing began to take off in the late 1990s thanks to programs like the original Napster, everyone could suddenly explore every facet of music for virtually no cost on their own."
**Music Industry's Struggle to Create Superstars** : The music industry has done a "lousy job" of creating new rock superstars since the turn of the 21st century, being distracted by file sharing, piracy, falling CD prices, and declining revenues, leading to a reliance on mining the 90s through reissues, box sets, and reunions. "The problem I see is that the music industry has done a lousy job of creating new rock super stars. Since they've been distracted by file sharing, piracy, falling CD prices, falling revenues, and the move from CDs to digital, there's been little time devoted to creating a new generation of massive rock bands."
**Generational Shift in Music Consumption** : In 1992, a 15-year-old was uninterested in 20-year-old music like Elvis or The Beatles, viewing it as "old music." Today's 15-year-old, however, might appreciate a 25-year-old Nirvana song, suggesting a new "ecumenical world" where "good music is good music regardless of age" due to technology. "A 15 year old kid in 1992 could not get enough Nirvana. He couldn't care less about music that was 20 years old. Like Elvis the Beatles, the Stones, that's old music. That meant nothing to this kid."
**Evolution of Tribalism in Rock** : During the height of grunge, rock music was highly tribal, with clear divisions between "mainstream rockers" and "alternative kids," and no switching teams. Today, while tribal aspects persist, it's more acceptable to have diverse artists like The Smashing Pumpkins, The Beatles, Black Sabbath, and new indie bands, along with hip hop and pop, on the same playlist. "At the height of the Grunge days, it was all pretty tribal on one side with the mainstream rockers and the other the alternative kids. You were either in one group or the other, no changing teams, that's the way it was."
