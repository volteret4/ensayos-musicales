## curiosities
**"Thatchergate" Hoax (1983-1984)** : Following the Falklands War, this anarchist musical collective created a tape known as "Thatchergate." They cobbled together speeches from Margaret Thatcher and Ronald Reagan to create what sounded like a fake phone call between the two leaders, including a fabricated discussion about firing missiles at allies. When the tape circulated in 1983, the US State Department suspected it was KGB disinformation, and MI6 considered it a possible Argentinian intelligence operation. Its true source was not discovered until January 1984. While not a song, its samples were used in tracks by Crass and the Dead Kennedys. Crass's motivation was to expose governments as "small-minded, petty and stupid," not just corrupt or violent, and the hoax indeed embarrassed the CIA. "The band cobbled together speeches from Margaret Thatcher and Ronald Reagan into what appeared to be a fake phone call between the two of them."

# genre - Punk Rock

## curiosities
**New York vs. British Punk (Mid-1970s)** : The punk rock explosion of the mid-1970s saw distinct ideological differences between its origins. In New York, punk was primarily for "artsy outsiders," with major bands like the Ramones, Blondie, and Talking Heads not being overtly political. Conversely, punk in Britain was intensely political, arising from a context of high unemployment, government financial struggles, industrial decline, widespread strikes, entrenched class systems, and general hopelessness, leading to music that protested "against everything" including the Queen. "In New York, where it all started, punk was for artsy outsiders... But on the other side of the Atlantic, punk was all politics."
**British Punk as Total Rebellion** : In mid-1970s Britain, punk music emerged as a comprehensive protest against the dire socio-economic conditions, including high unemployment, government austerity, and systemic class inequality. The music wasn't just against specific issues but expressed a profound rejection of the entire system, encapsulated as giving society, the government, and the economy "one big FU." No institution, not even the Queen, was spared from its critique. "The music wasn't so much a protest against something as it was a protest against everything. The whole idea of punk was to give society the government and the economy one big FU."

# genre - Hardcore Punk

## curiosities
**Reaction to Right-Wing Politics (Late 1970s / Early 1980s)** : Hardcore punk emerged at the end of the 1970s as a direct reaction to the severe right-wing political turns in both the US (beginning of the Reagan era) and the UK (Margaret Thatcher's policies and austerity measures). Many young people on the left were staunchly opposed to Reagan's conservative policies, for whom "Reaganomics was a four-letter word," fueling the genre's development. "part of the development of hardcore punk was a reaction to the right-wing attitude of America during his presidency."
**Core Ethos and Characteristics** : Hardcore punk was characterized by its anti-hippy stance, strong anti-commercialism, and deep anti-establishment and anti-music industry sentiments. Its practitioners were often deeply committed to social, political, and environmental subjects. The genre was inherently anti-military, anti-authority, and generally anti-violence, placing a high value on authenticity and loudly calling out any perceived "posers." "Hardcore was also anti-hippy. They were against any sort of commercialism. They were very anti-establishment, very anti-music industry, and often all-in when it came to social, political, and environmental subjects."
**Canadian Origin of the Term** : The term "hardcore" itself is believed to be a Canadian invention. Vancouver band D.O.A. released an album titled "Hardcore 81," which, if it didn't formally invent the term, was instrumental in popularizing it and establishing the band as godfathers of the genre. "And the word hardcore seems to have been a Canadian invention. Vancouver's D.O.A released an album entitled Hardcore 81, which if it didn't invent the term, sure made it popular."

**Mid-1970s British Socio-Economic Crisis** : Mid-1970s Britain was plagued by severe socio-economic turmoil, marked by high unemployment (especially among young people), government debt from decades of a welfare state, a plummeting pound, depleted foreign reserves, and a steep decline in industrial production. Conditions were so dire that three-day work weeks were introduced, and widespread strikes by miners, bakers, nurses, garbage collectors, and even gravediggers led to rolling blackouts due to a lack of coal. This environment, coupled with an entrenched class system that predetermined opportunities, fostered widespread hopelessness, setting the stage for the intensely political British punk movement. "Mid-70s Britain was a time of high unemployment, especially among young people. The government had basically run out of money after running a welfare state for decades."
**Late 1970s / Early 1980s Shift to the Right** : The end of the 1970s was a period of intense societal change, characterized by a severe recession and sharp political turns to the right in both the United States and the United Kingdom. In the US, this marked the beginning of the Reagan era, while in the UK, Margaret Thatcher's right-wing policies and austerity measures were deeply affecting the populace. "The end of the 70s was one of those times. There was a terrible recession and both the US and the UK had taken hard political turns to the right."
**Margaret Thatcher's Polarizing Figure (1980s)** : Margaret Thatcher, often referred to as the "Iron Lady," was a deeply polarizing figure in 1980s Britain. Her right-wing policies and austerity measures were lauded by those on the political right but despised by those in the center or on the left. Her tenure inspired a multitude of anti-Thatcher songs, both during and after her time in office, reflecting the intense opposition she faced. "Mrs. Thatcher could be polarizing even on her own side of the political spectrum. But if you were in the center or on the left, you despised her."
**Ronald Reagan's Presidency and Opposition (1980s)** : Ronald Reagan's presidency, which began in the US after a dysfunctional 1970s, saw him promise to "make America great again" with conservative policies. This was met with enthusiasm by many voters but generated strong opposition from young people on the left. "When Republican Ronald Reagan promised to make America great again with his no nonsense conservative policies, it was just what the electorate wanted."

This generation faced a brutal recession upon entering the workforce, leading to fears of being unable to match or exceed their parents' lifestyle. Despite being highly educated, they were often under-engaged and underemployed. Globally, the First Gulf War added to anxieties. These factors fueled the rise of alternative music, which channeled the fears, confusion, anger, and hope of this large demographic. "Generation X was not happy. **Riot Grrrl Movement (1990s)** : The 1990s saw the emergence of the riot grrrl movement, characterized by its "ultrafeminism." Following the 9/11 attacks and the subsequent invasions of Iraq and Afghanistan and the resulting chaos, many musicians picked up their guitars as a means of protest. **Reasons for Decline in Prominent Alt-Rock Protest in 21st Century** : The first half of the 2010s was remarkably quiet for protest songs in alt-rock, despite persistent global conflicts, the 2008 financial meltdown, soaring student debt, the stance of the religious right, the refugee crisis, terrorism, and global warming. Several theories explain this phenomenon: 1) Protests shifted to social media platforms like Facebook and Twitter. 2) Rock music's cultural influence waned, with hip hop and rap becoming the dominant vehicles for protest. 3) Alt-rock fans may feel powerless to effect change. 4) Some suggest this generation grew up without a fighting spirit, accustomed to universal praise ("everybody gets a trophy"). "But the first half of the second decade of the new millennium has been awfully quiet when it comes to protest songs. Despite the never ending wars in the Middle East, the financial meltdown of 2008, crushing student debt, the attitude of the religious right in the US, Republican presidential candidates, Wall Street Crooks who weren't sent to jail, the refugee crisis, terrorism, global warming, more globalization."
**Shift in Artistic Rebellion (21st Century)** : In a notable reversal from previous decades, the 21st century has seen a shift in who creates protest music. In the 1970s, 80s, and 90s, adolescents focused on rebellion and being carefree, while "adult rockers" were the jaded ones writing about life's struggles. Now, the dynamic is inverted, with legacy artists like Radiohead, Springsteen, and U2 continuing to push for change through music, while many newer artists generally avoid this area. "In the 70s, 80s and 90s, adolescents were all about rebellion and wanting to be care-free. It was up to the adult rockers to be jaded to write songs about the trials of life. Now it's the other way around."
