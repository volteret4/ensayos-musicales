## songs
**Blue Monday** : New Order's "Blue Monday" was released as an eight-minute, 12-inch single, gaining its biggest traction in dance clubs. It became the top-selling 12-inch single globally, selling over three million copies. Despite its massive success, Factory Records lost 30 pence for every copy sold due to excessive investment in the sleeve's intricate design and printing, leading to losses of "tens of thousands of pounds." "New orders blew Monday, the top selling 12-inch single in the history of the universe, three million plus copies, yet it lost money."
**Revolutionary Dance Club Impact** : "Blue Monday" by New Order had a profound impact on dance clubs, which at the time featured songs like Olivia Newton-John's "Physical" and Joan Jett's "I Love Rock 'n Roll." New Order's electronic sound was unprecedented and "rocked their world," introducing a new paradigm to dance music. "No one. I mean, no one had ever heard anything like New Order before and it rocked their world."
**LSD-Fueled Recording Session** : The recording session for "Blue Monday" was undertaken by New Order while they were "loaded up on LSD." Despite managing to lay down all the tracks, their intoxicated state led studio engineers to ask them to leave and finish mixing the song themselves. "They managed to get all the tracks down, but they were so wasted that once their parts were done, the studio engineers were fed up with them and they just go across the street to the cafe, let us finish mixing the song and piece."

## curiosities
**Formation and Influential Sound** : New Order formed from the surviving members of Joy Division following Ian Curtis's suicide in 1980. Their subsequent career proved as influential as their first, successfully bridging dance music with post-punk indie culture. Their Joy Division legacy provided unquestionable coolness and indie credentials. "When singer Ian Curtis committed suicide in 1980, the rest of the band regrouped as new order. That second act in their career turned out to be just as influential as their first."
**"Accident of Laziness" Origin of Dance Sound** : New Order's groundbreaking electronic sound and worldwide alt-rock influence partly originated from their reluctance to perform encores. They initially played Stephen Morris's drum machine alone as they left the stage, later adding a sequencer for Gillian Gilbert's synthesizers, and finally, Bernard Sumner conceived sequencing Peter Hook's bass line, leading to songs performed largely by machines. "But their worldwide success and the way they changed alt rock forever was due to an accident of laziness."

**British Isles' Geographic Impact on Music** : The unique geography of the British Isles, as the only English-speaking territory for thousands of miles with 60 million people in a small space, fostered rapid musical innovation. This concentrated environment allowed ideas, trends, and sounds to move very quickly, with national exposure from BBC Radio and TV instantly reaching millions. "Much of this innovation was made possible by simple geography."
**British Music Press's Role** : The British music press, including weekly magazines and tabloids like NME, Sounds, and Melody Maker, was crucial for promoting new music. These publications constantly competed to cover the latest trends, break scoops, and discover new artists, contributing to a rapid churn of sounds. "All these magazines and tabloids, not just coming up monthly, but weekly. Each of them covering the latest in the coolest and each trying to outdo each other with scoops and discoveries."
**BBC Radio and TV Influence** : BBC Radio and TV had a profound impact on British music culture. Any song or band played on these national platforms, which reached "every corner of the empire," instantly exposed millions of people to new sounds, accelerating the spread of musical trends. "If a song or a band or a sound got played on BBC Radio and TV, which reaches every corner of the empire, millions of people would be instantly exposed to it."
**British Radio vs. Press (Pre-1970s)** : Before the 1970s, Britain lacked legal, private, commercial radio because the government deemed radio broadcasting too important for private citizens. Consequently, despite a thriving 1960s music scene with bands like The Beatles and The Rolling Stones, it was nearly impossible to hear these British bands on British radio, a gap filled by the printed music media. "Britain didn't get legal, private, commercial radio until the 1970s."
**British Music Press Philosophy** : The British music press operated on a philosophy of discovering new music and scenes, building up bands with hype and exposure, and then abandoning them for the "next shiny thing." This rapid cycle was driven by intense competition among publications, leading to a "massive churn in sounds and trends." "these publications believe that it was their duty to discover new music, to discover new scenes, build up the bands to hype an exposure and then abandon them for the next shiny thing."
**MTV's Role in Alt-Rock (Mid-1980s)** : MTV and other channels that played music videos were instrumental in the mid-1980s for showcasing "weird new, telegenic bands from the UK." This exposure was vital for introducing these alternative artists to a wider audience in North America, complementing the growth of the alt-rock scene. "But we can't forget the role of MTV and any channel or any show that played videos from all those weird new, telegenic bands from the UK."
