# concert - Glastonbury

## members
Michael Evis (founder)

## curiosities
**Founding and History** : Glastonbury, founded by Michael Evis after seeing Led Zeppelin play outdoors, was first staged in the fall of 1970. It drew on an older Glastonbury tradition of classical music and arts events (1914-1926). Evis initially called it the Piltdown Festival, but that name lasted only one year. "Glastonbury's founder is Michael Evis, who decided to get into the festival business after seeing Led Zeppelin play outdoors."
**Largest Greenfield Festival** : Glastonbury is known as the largest annual Greenfield Festival in the world, meaning people gather in a field, specifically Worthy Farm (a working dairy farm), for five days almost every June. It has been held annually since 1981, with "fallow years" every half-decade to allow the land and local residents to recover from the crowds. "It is known as the largest annual Greenfield Festival in the world, and this simply means that people come to a field, and in this case it's worthy farm, which is a working dairy farm, for five days just about every June."
**Cultural Significance and Attendance** : Glastonbury has become a "right of passage" for music fans, attracting around 175,000 people regardless of weather conditions. Tickets sell out in less than an hour, months before any acts are announced, and performances there can make or break careers. "Glastonbury has become a right of passage for generations of music fans. Each event attracts somewhere in the neighborhood of 175,000 people, regardless of the heat or the cold or the rain or the mud."
**1990s Gold Standard** : By the 1990s, Glastonbury was considered the "gold standard" by which all other music festivals were judged. "By the 90s, that festival was the gold standard by which all others were judged."

# concert - Lollapalooza

## members
Perry Farrell (creator)

## curiosities
**Conception and Traveling Format** : Inspired by the Pixies' 1990 Reading performance, Perry Farrell conceived Lollapalooza as a traveling festival that would move from city to city over several months, a departure from previous U.S. festivals that were rooted in one location. "Here is where we need to talk about Lala Palusa. Now, remember how I said that Perry Farrell was inspired by the pixie set at Reading in 1990. That's where the idea for Lala Palusa came from."
**Initial Purpose as Farewell Tour** : Lollapalooza was originally conceived as a Jane's Addiction farewell tour, which masqueraded as a music festival. This allowed the event to get off the ground, despite being seen by some as a semantic distinction. "The main tradition was winding down and preparing for a farewell tour. And Lala Palusa was conceived as a Jane's addiction farewell tour that masqueraded as a music festival."
**Name Origin** : The name "Lollapalooza" was chosen from old Three Stooges clips, which derived it from a late 19th-century slang word meaning "an extraordinary or unusual thing or event." "The name Lala Palusa was picked because it was a word that came up in old three stuages clips. They got it from a late 19th century slang word that meant an extraordinary or unusual thing or event, which sounded perfect."
**Skepticism and Early Reception (1991)** : The idea for Lollapalooza faced criticism, with many believing a tour of "no name, so-called alternative bands" would be a financial disaster. The very first show, on July 18, 1991, outside Phoenix, did "just okay" at the box office, making some money but not a lot. "The idea had its critics and there were a lot of them. They would say things like, wait, you're going to take a bunch of no name, so-called alternative bands that no one cares about on the road for two months and expect to make money? This is going to be a disaster."
**Missed Timing in 1991** : The first Lollapalooza tour wrapped up on August 28, 1991, the day before Nirvana released "Smells Like Teen Spirit" and Pearl Jam released their debut album. This timing meant the festival missed the explosion of the alt-rock landscape that occurred a year later. "It wrapped up on August 28, 1991. That was the day before some band called Nirvana released a new single called Smell's Like Teen Spirit. And it was also on that day that a new group called Pearl Jam put out their debut record."
**1992 Breakout Success** : The second Lollapalooza tour, beginning July 18, 1992, was a massive success, with "sell out after sell out" and huge excitement, capitalizing on the changed musical landscape after 1991. "A year later, the landscape had changed entirely. And the second Lala Palusa tour began on July 18, 1992. You couldn't get near the place, sell out after sell out, huge excitement."
**Introduction of Second Stage (1992)** : 1992 marked the first year that Lollapalooza included a second stage on the tour, featuring bands like Stone Temple Pilots, Cypress Hill, Rage Against the Machine, and Tool. "And that was also the first year of a second stage on the tour."
**Glory Years (1992-1995)** : The period from 1992 through 1995 is considered the "glory years" for Lollapalooza, as it perfectly represented the state of the alternative nation during that time. "1992 through 1995 were the glory years for Lala Palusa. Nothing, and I mean nothing better represented the state of the alternative nation during that time."
**1996 Headliner Controversy** : While 1996 was an "okay" year, the decision to have Metallica as the headliner "didn't feel right" and was seen as having "hijacked the whole thing," possibly signaling a departure from its original alternative ethos. "1996 was okay too, but the headliner that year was Metallica, which somehow didn't feel right. They kind of hijacked the whole thing."
**Decline and Hiatus (1997-2003)** : Enthusiasm for Lollapalooza waned after the 1997 tour, leading to a hiatus. Another attempt in 2003 was largely forgotten. "There was one more tour in 1997, but by that time enthusiasm had waned. Another attempt was made in 2003, but nobody remembers that."
**Permanent Chicago Home (2005)** : Lollapalooza re-established itself as a major music festival in 2005 when it was given a permanent annual home in Chicago. "It wasn't until Lala Palusa was given a permanent annual home in Chicago in 2005 that it re-established itself as a major music festival."
**Traveling Festival Concept Decline (late 90s)** : By the time the 1990s ended, the idea of a traveling alternative music festival, exemplified by Lollapalooza, was "burning out" as alt-rock itself was on the wane. "By the time the 90s drew to a close, the idea of a traveling alternative music festival was burning out."

# curiosity

**History of Music Festivals** : Music festivals are not a modern creation; people have gathered in fields for centuries to hear music, with some original festivals, like Fiera de la Frequette No La in Italy (started 1450), still occurring today. Other long-standing festivals exist in Germany, England, India, and Latvia. "People have been gathering in fields to hear music for centuries, and in some cases, those original festivals are still happening."
**1990s Festival Boom in North America** : While Europeans were accustomed to festivals in varying conditions, North Americans were late to the party, lacking a strong tradition of summer festivals. This changed significantly in the 1990s, driven by the alt-rock nation, which built the traditions and industry for modern summer festivals. "Europeans were pretty used to standing in fields in the mud, in the rain, in the heat, but we North Americans were late to the party."
**Impact of European Festivals on North America** : The festival culture of the alt-rock nation in North America was heavily influenced by the expertise and experience gained from large European events, with English organizers being particularly skilled in this area. "The festival culture of the alt-rock nation in North America wouldn't have taken root without the expertise and experience gleaned from the big European events."
**Golden Age of Compact Discs (1990s)** : The 1990s were a period of significant financial prosperity in the music industry, largely due to the "Golden Age of Compact Discs," which saw music sales increase year after year. This economic boom, coupled with Generation X's buying habits, facilitated the rise of many music festivals. "Music sales were going up and up and up and up and up every year thanks to the Golden Age of Compact Discs."
**Generation X Consumption** : During the 1990s, Generation X was a major demographic driving the music industry, purchasing a lot of music and attending many shows, contributing to the financial viability of festivals and touring acts. "Gen X was buying a lot of music and going to a lot of shows."
**Creed Syndrome** : The "Creed Syndrome" describes a phase in the rock cycle where new artists appear to be derivative copies of original acts, making the music feel too safe and watered down, contributing to the decline of alt-rock in the late 1990s. "Green also entered the phase of any rock cycle where the new artist coming out seemed like a copy of a copy of a copy of the original, too derivative, too safe, too watered down. We can call this the Creed Syndrome."
**Generation Y Musical Preferences** : Generation Y, entering their years of musical awareness in the late 1990s, preferred pop-oriented music over the brooding grunge or intense post-punk alt-rock of previous generations. Their tastes contributed to the rise of pop acts like the Spice Girls and Britney Spears. "Generation Y just wasn't into brooding grunge or any of the kind of intense post punk all rock that their forebearers were. Their tastes leaned much more to the pop side."
**Late 1990s Economic and Political Climate** : The late 1990s saw an improved economy with a major bull market under President Bill Clinton, the end of the Cold War, and the first Gulf War becoming a memory, leading to a shift away from the "grimness" of the early 1990s and influencing a turn towards more cheerful pop music. "The economy had improved. In fact, America was enjoying a major bull market under Democrat Bill Clinton. Cold war was over. The first Gulf War was a memory. The grimness of the early 1990s was all gone."
**Rise of DJ Culture and Electronic Music** : The popularity of hip-hop and electronic music exploded towards the end of the 1990s, leading to a decrease in guitar sales and an increase in sales of keyboards, samplers, turntables, and mixers, as a new generation became captivated by DJ culture. "Sales of guitars fell. Sales of keyboards and samplers increased. So did turntables and mixers, as this new generation became besotted with DJ culture."
**Napster and MP3 Impact (1999)** : In June 1999, the emergence of Napster and the MP3 format for peer-to-peer file sharing began to significantly worry the music industry, leading to a massive spike in free music downloads and fundamentally altering the record industry. "But you mean I can just go online and download whatever song I want from someone I don't know? And it's free. So that means I don't have to pay $20 for a CD, which has just one good song. This is true. Well, count me in."
**YouTube and iTunes/iPod Impact** : In the years following the 1990s, YouTube would emerge to disrupt traditional music video channels, and iTunes and iPods would further transform music consumption and the industry. "In a few years, something called YouTube would come along and kill off the traditional music video channel. Then we had iTunes and iPods."
