**Formation and Early Rehearsals** : Formed in 1983 in Minneapolis, this three-piece band was named after a Swedish board game. "At about the same time, Husker Dew was stumbling through Ramones covers during their first rehearsals and bassist Greg Norton's basement, another important Minneapolis band was coming together in a basement on Bryant Street South."
**Influence on Hardcore Punk** : Husker Dew had a huge influence on hardcore punk across America. They appeared on stage in street clothes, eschewing typical punk attire like leather jackets or mohawk haircuts. "Husker Dew would ultimately have a huge influence on hardcore punk across America."
**Musical Approach** : Although their music was loud and fast, Husker Dew's songs incorporated elements of a basic pop song, giving melody and song structure equal standing with power and volume. This approach pushed the parameters of punk forward. "And although their music was loud and fast, the songs incorporated elements of a basic pop song. Melody and song structure was given equal standing with power and volume."
**Spreading Influence and Major Label Transition** : Thanks to endless touring and strong support from SST Records, Husker Dew's influence spread throughout North America. They became one of the first bands to rise through the indie ranks and sign with a major label, yet they maintained their uncompromising do-it-yourself ethic until the very end. "Thanks to endless touring and strong support from SST, Husker Dew was able to spread their influence to all corners of North America."
**Pioneering Indie-to-Major Transition** : Husker Dew was among the first indie bands to accept an offer to jump to a major label, a move that initially generated whispers of a "sell-out." However, by sticking to their creative vision, they demonstrated that it was possible to record for a major label while still maintaining an indie attitude. "Husker do had recently done the same thing. At first, there were whispers of sell-out, but both bands stuck to their creative guns."

## curiosities
**Origin in Athens, Georgia** : R.E.M., considered the most successful band of its generation, originated from Athens, Georgia, a town of 70,000 people (20,000 of whom are University of Georgia students), located an hour east of Atlanta on Highway 78. The Athens music scene began in the mid-1970s with groups like The B-52s. "The most successful band of this generation came out of a town an hour east of Atlanta on Highway 78, just 70,000 people live there. And 20,000 of them are students at the University of Georgia."
**Formation and First Gig** : Guitarist Peter Buck, a record store clerk, struck up a conversation with art student Michael Stipe, who was often looking for strange 12-inch singles. Encouraged by the success of their friends, The B-52s, Peter and Michael recruited bassist Mike Mills and drummer Bill Berry, both from Macon, Georgia. Their first gig was at a friend's birthday party in a deconsecrated church in Athens on April 5th, 1980. "Shortly after the B-52s began to attract attention, a record store clerk named Peter Buck struck up a conversation with an art student who was always looking for strange 12-inch singles. His name was Michael Steip, encouraged by the success of their friends, the B-52s."
**Band Name Origin** : On the night of their first party gig in Athens (April 5th, 1980), at least four other groups were using the name R.E.M. However, since no one challenged the Athens group, they kept the name, which was deemed a better choice than other considerations like "Slutbank," "The Male Nurse," and "Twisted Kites." "On April 5th, 1980, the night of that party in Athens, at least four other groups were using the name REM. But because no one ever challenged the Athens group, they got to keep the name."
**Rise via College Radio** : R.E.M.'s rootsy, made-in-America, anti-synthesizer guitar pop initially stood out from the dominant British new wave of the early 1980s and became a staple on college radio across North America. As their popularity grew, commercial radio stations began to follow suit, leading to album sales escalating from the tens of thousands to hundreds of thousands, and eventually into the millions. "REM became a staple of college radio across North America, and as the band's popularity grew, more commercial radio stations began to catch on."
**Major Label Transition and Impact** : In 1988, R.E.M. accepted an offer to move from their indie label, IRS (where they had been since the beginning), to Warner Brothers, then the largest record company in the world. Despite initial "whispers of sell-out," the band stuck to their creative vision, demonstrating that artists could record for a major label and still maintain an indie attitude, a thought deemed very important for the industry. "Then in 1988, the group accepted an offer to jump from IRS. Their indie label, the one they'd been with from the beginning, to Warner Brothers, the largest record company in the world at the time."
**Parallel Ascent with Indie Music** : R.E.M.'s evolution from a "fratboy band" to intelligent, articulate, and politically aware superstars mirrored the rise of indie music in America. For many, R.E.M. served as an entry point into a rock and roll universe that had remained largely undetected by the mainstream for over a decade, and their popularity growth was a testament to the power of college radio. "REM's rise from fratboy band to intelligent, articulate, and politically aware superstars paralleled the ascent of indie music in America."

# genre - Indie Music

## curiosities
**Historical Perception and Growth** : Not long ago, indie music was widely ignored, with the belief that any good music would be picked up by a major label. It was often seen as the domain of "weirdos and outliers," not good enough for broad enjoyment. However, this allowed it to grow in its "Petri dish" without capitalistic interference, to the point where it could no longer be ignored by the early 1990s. "There was a time, not that long ago, when indie music was ignored by most people. The thinking was that if the music was any good, then it would have been picked up and released by a major label, right?"
**Evolution into Mainstream Influence** : By the early 1990s, indie and alternative music began to be taken very seriously. As the number of major labels decreased, the power and importance of independent labels gained more attention, shifting the dynamic of the music industry. "For the early 90s, people had begun to take Indie and alternative music very, very seriously. And as the number of major label shrunk, more attention was paid to the power and importance of independent labels."

# genre - No Wave

## curiosities
**Origins and Philosophy** : Emerging in the late 1970s from New York City's East Village, No Wave was a short-lived movement that combined punk and avant-garde rock. Adherents believed rock and roll had become too conservative and even punk was institutionalized, leading them to create music often lacking musical training, characterized by negativity, harshness, formlessness, noise, and atonality. "A short-lived movement centered in the East Village known as No Wave was born in the late 1970s. Now No Wave was equal parts punk and avant-garde rock."

# label - Subpop

## curiosities
**Formation and Early Strategy** : Bruce Pabbitt, inspired by the "Deep Six" compilation, borrowed $20,000 from his father to form Subpop. The company worked out a deal with Reciprocal Recordings, a local studio, to refer any cool or interesting bands to Subpop. "The borrowing $20,000 from his father, Pabbitt formed a company he called Subpop and worked out a deal with reciprocal recordings, a local recording studio."
**Subpop 100 Compilation (July 1986)** : In July 1986, Subpop released a collection titled "Subpop 100," subtitled "To K-Tell with Love." Although it only featured two local acts, it marked a significant beginning for the label. "Enough bands eventually submitted tapes by July of 1986 for the company to release a collection called the Subpop 100, subtitled To K-Tell with Love. And although the album only featured two local acts, it was at least a start."
**Singles Club Innovation** : By mid-1988, Subpop was a "pretty cool little label." To enhance its profile, the company launched the Subpop Singles Club, offering subscribers a new, limited-edition indie single by mail every month for an annual fee. The very first single, released in November 1988, was from Nirvana. "And as an added touch, the company started the Subpop Singles Club. For an annual fee, subscribers received a brand new limited edition indie single in the mail every single month."
**Financially Strapped Status** : Subpop was financially strapped before Nirvana's eventual success helped to save the label. "Not only would Nirvana eventually be the savior of the financially strapped Subpop label, but they would also become the focus of a worldwide interest in grunge."

# curiosity

**Major Label Historical Role** : Historically, major labels, numbering six or seven at one point, "scooped up all the best stuff" and could afford to take chances on artists. This often left indie labels with "the drags" or music considered unsellable, creating a perceived imbalance in quality. "There was a time when the major labels back when there were six or seven of them scooped up all the best stuff and they could afford to take those kinds of chances back then. The indie labels were for the most part left with the drags or the stuff that could not be sold."
**College Radio's Rise and Influence** : By the mid-1980s, college radio became a highly important and influential factor in music, welcoming indie bands that commercial radio and MTV largely ignored. These stations, not dependent on ratings for revenue, could be more daring and experimental, making them the primary source for the newest and most exciting music for many. "By the mid-80s, college radio was becoming a very important and very influential factor in music. While most commercial radio stations were unwilling to play anything that wasn't on a major label and on MTV, indie bands were welcomed with open arms on campus and nonprofit public radio."
**College Radio's Growing Power** : Though listenership wasn't huge, campus stations became increasingly organized under national and international associations, with conferences, magazines, and their own music charts. This organization significantly amplified college radio's power within the broader music industry. "Now listenership wasn't huge, but as campus stations became more organized under national and international associations with conferences and magazines and even their own music charts, college radio became more and more powerful in the general scheme of the music industry."
**Synergy Between College Bands and Radio** : A perfect synergy existed between college bands and college radio: many indie bands were formed by college students, played their first gigs on or around campus, and toured by playing other colleges, making college radio their natural supporter. This created a well-organized musical subculture. "The synergy was perfect, indie bands were often formed by college students. The first gigs were usually in and around the campus and when they went on the road, they most likely played in and around other colleges. So it was only natural for these college bands to be supported by college radio."
**Boston Area Music Scene** : The Boston area fostered a rich musical subculture, boasting 11 universities and colleges, plus the prestigious Berklee College of Music. This concentration of institutions attracted people from around the world to share ideas, leading to the emergence of many influential indie bands. "For some cities, it was an embarrassment of riches. For example, there are 11 universities and colleges in the Boston area, plus the prestigious Berkeley College of Music. This means people from all over the world meet in the Boston area to share ideas."
**Athens, Georgia Music Scene** : The Athens, Georgia music scene began in the mid-1970s with The B-52s, followed by other groups like The Fans, Pylon, The Tone-Tones, Love Tractor, and Method Actors. This small scene provided the environment for bands like R.E.M. to form and thrive. "The Athens, Georgia music scene, began in the mid 1970s with the B-52s and more obscure groups like the fans and pylon and the tone tones and love tractor and method actors."
**Pacific Northwest Music Wave** : The next significant indie wave emerged in the Pacific Northwest, characterized by slower music, louder and lower guitars, and a "uniform" of flannel, indicative of the grunge movement. "It was a continental way in the Pacific Northwest, where the music was slower, the guitars louder and lower, and where the uniforms were made a flannel."
**Seattle Sound Characteristics** : The unique local hybrid rock sound in Seattle was sludgy, slow, and low, often as negative and pessimistic as nihilistic hardcore acts, yet with more introspective lyrics. The distinctive "look" of the Seattle bands—flannel and functional logging industry clothing from thrift shops—was unintentional, stemming from poverty rather than fashion. "Rock that was sludgy and slow and low. And often everybody's negative and pessimistic is some of the more nihilistic hardcore acts. And there was more. Some of the lyrics seem to be more introspective than usual."
**Deep Six Compilation (1985)** : This compilation, released on the CZ label in the fall of 1985, featured contributions from several local Seattle bands. Although it took two years to sell all 2000 copies, it was a pivotal record as the first time people could hear and recognize the distinct sound emerging from their city. "But then in the fall of 1985, several local bands contributed material to a compilation called Deep Six, a label called CZ. Although it took two years for all 2000 copies to sell out, this was a really important record. The first time people could hear what was happening in their city."
**Origin of the Term "Grunge"** : Mark Arm, a member of Green River, worked at the warehouse at MuZac Corporation and, in the early 1980s, wrote a letter to a local entertainment weekly describing his pre-Green River band as "Pure Grunge." This letter is believed to be the first recorded instance of anyone referring to the Seattle sound using the "G word." "In the early 80s, he wrote a letter to one of the local entertainment weeklies describing his pre-Green River band as Pure Grunge. And this letter seems to be the first time anyone referred to this Seattle sound using the G word."
**Indie Labels as "Minor Leagues"** : Before the internet era, the indie label system often functioned like minor league baseball, allowing artists to mature and develop at that level. Once a group gained enough traction and became "big enough," a major label would typically sign them away, as seen with bands like Husker Dew, R.E.M., Sonic Youth, Soundgarden, and Nirvana. "Back in the days before the internet, the indie label system was kind of like the minor leagues. The small indie labels gave the artist the attention they needed as they matured. Groups developed at that level, and once they got big enough, a major label would swoop in and sign them away."
**Indie Artists Maintaining Ethic with Majors** : The transition of bands like Husker Dew and R.E.M. from indie to major labels, despite initial "whispers of sell-out," proved crucial. By sticking to their creative vision, they demonstrated that artists could record for a major label and still maintain their independent attitude, changing industry perceptions. "I need you to hang on to that thought. REM's rise from fratboy band to intelligent, articulate, and politically aware superstars paralleled the ascent of indie music in America."
