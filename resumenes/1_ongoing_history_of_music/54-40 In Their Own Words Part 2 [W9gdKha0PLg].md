## albums
**Trusted by Millions (1996) – Third Consecutive Platinum Record** : This album was the band's third consecutive platinum record, signifying significant commercial success in Canada. It cemented their reputation with the Canadian audience and featured the song "Love You All." "Trusted by Millions, the group's third consecutive platinum record."
**Smiling Buddha Cabaret (1995) – Sixth Record** : This album was the band's sixth record, released around 1995. Its release preceded a period of transition where the band was dropped by Warner and subsequently picked up by Sony Music Canada. "We ended part one in about 1995 with the band releasing their sixth record, Smiling Buddha Cabaret."
**Show Me – Record the Band Can't Listen To** : This album was considered a "learning experience" for the band, as they followed the "path of least resistance" with producers and record company people they knew. It is the only record that one band member states they "can't even listen to anymore." "That's the only record that we made that I can't even listen to anymore."
**Greatest Hits – Warner Canada Release** : This compilation album was released by Warner Canada during a transition period when the band had lost their American deal but before signing with Sony. It performed "bucket loads" well and "reset the band with the Canadian audience," providing new material for MuchMusic to play and generating "mailbox cash" without significant new effort from the band. "I think that greatest hits record actually sort of reset the band with the Canadian audience because it gave much music, something more to talk about, more to play."
**Since When (1998) – Intimate, String-Laden Record** : Produced by Garth Richardson, this album marked a deliberate "total about face" from their previous work, with the band aiming for a 16-track, two-inch, intimate record incorporating strings, a departure from the "testosterone, big muscle tattoo, thick neck, platinum selling beef records" Richardson was known for. The artistic shift was seen as a potential "career suicide" but allowed for creative freedom. "we wanted to we wanted to do a 16 track, two inch, intimate record. That's really what we wanted to do and we wanted to bring in strings and do all these things."
**Casual Viewing (2000s) – Record at Digital Tipping Point** : This album was released around the turn of the millennium, coinciding with the rise of digital music (Napster) and the decline of traditional CD sales. Sony Music Canada president Rick Kamillary identified "Casual Viewing" as a single and noted that the era of selling physical "masters" was "all over." "we're at the casual viewing record and there's the tipping point."
**Goodbye Flatland (2003) – First Indie Release** : This album marked 5440's return to independent status after approximately 20 years. It was their first release under these new conditions, signaling a significant shift in their career and approach to the music industry. "The first record under those new conditions was called Goodbye Flatland."
**Yes to Everything (2005) – True North Records Release** : Released on True North Records in 2005, this album featured the song "Easy Love," which became their last FM radio top 10 hit in Canada. "Yes to Everything in 2005, which came out on True North Records."
**Northern Soul (2008) – True North Records Release** : Released on True North Records in 2008, this album was followed by a period where the band's output slowed down, moving away from a bi-annual album release schedule. "True North also released Northern Soul in 2008."
**Lost in the City** : This album was released during a period when the band's album output became less frequent, following "Northern Soul" and preceding "Keep On Walking." "There's lost in the city."
**Unnamed Live Unplugged Record** : An unplugged live record was released between "Lost in the City" and "Keep On Walking," contributing to the slowed pace of new studio album releases. "There was the live unplugged record."
**Led Different (Unplugged Record)** : This was another unplugged record released by the band, distinct from their other live unplugged offering, further diversifying their output during a period of reduced studio album frequency. "there was also an unplugged record. Led different."
**Keep On Walking** : This album was released after a noticeable gap following "Lost in the City" and the unplugged records, marking a continuation of their output despite the slower pace. "And there was a big gap to keep on walking."
**El Mocambo Live Album (2023) – Upcoming Release** : One of two new releases planned for 2023, this is a live album recorded at the El Mocambo venue in Toronto. "one live album recorded at the El Macomba in Toronto."
**West Coast Band (2023) – COVID-Inspired Album** : One of two new releases planned for 2023, this album was written during the COVID-19 pandemic. Its creation was inspired by Brad Merritt's idea for Zoom calls where the band members shared stories about their history, which then evolved into new songs. "another written during COVID called West Coast Band."
**New Record (2023) – Story-Based Album** : This upcoming record, specifically "West Coast Band," features songs that are essentially stories drawn from the band's long history and experiences. Each track is based on anecdotes and events that have happened to the band members, making it a highly personal collection. "all the songs on the new record our stories of the band and things that have happened In the band."

## songs
**Love You All (1996)** : This song is featured on the 1996 album "Trusted by Millions," which achieved platinum status. "54 40 and love you all from their 1996 album, Trusted by Millions."
**I Go Blind** : This older 5440 song gained significant popularity after being covered by Hootie & the Blowfish. The American band, who were fans from the 930 Club in Washington, DC, used it as a B-side on a cassette single and later it was included on the "Friends" soundtrack. Hootie had asked that it not be released as a single for the soundtrack, but radio stations in Boulder/Denver started playing it organically due to Hootie's popularity, defying their record label's attempt to push Hootie's new single. "An American band had blown up with their first album. And they decided to cover an old 5440 song. So this would be a good time to revisit I Go Blind."
**Since When (1998)** : This song was the title track of their 1998 album and was described as potentially their "only AM single that tracked." It was licensed for use in TV commercials, specifically for Telus and High on Day, which helped the band recoup their Sony publishing deal. "we had the song since when, which I think was our, oh, I don't know. I was told our only AM single that that tracked it might be."
**Moving On** : This song is noted as the band's highest charting single. It was mentioned in conjunction with the title track of "Since When." "the band's highest charting single, Moving On."
**Casual Viewing (2000s)** : This song is taken from a line inspired by Genesis' "Lamb Lies Down on Broadway" and Marshall McLuhan's concept of casual viewing. Its theme explores how communication is changing the world, referencing the emerging internet era. The music video for the song was filmed globally using high-end Sony digital cameras in locations like Casablanca, Bangkok, and Essaouira, Morocco. "the song casual viewing is taken from a line, er, er, from Brad and I were huge Genesis fans from Lamb Lice down on Broadway. You know, Marshall McClue and casual viewing. So the theme of the song is how communication is changing the world."
**Take Me Out (2003)** : This song is featured on the 2003 album "Goodbye Flatland," which marked the band's return to independent status after two decades. "Take Me Out from the 2003, 5440 album Goodbye Flatland."
**Easy Love (2005)** : Featured on the "Yes to Everything" album, this song was co-written by Dave Genn and was the band's last top 10 hit on Canadian FM radio. "Easy love is on Yes to Everything... that was the last sort of FM radio, top 10 we had in Canada. That was a Dave Riff, by the way."
**Table For One** : This song is inspired by an anecdote involving drummer Matt Johnson. During a gig in North Bay, after soundcheck, Matt opted to go to a Canadian Chinese buffet alone before showering, cutting a long line and rudely demanding a "Table for one" when asked by staff how many people, much to the horror of his bandmates. "Matt, the drummer is a table for one because that's the way he likes to operate... he goes Table for one."
**Vodka Surprise** : This song is named after a traditional drink made by band member Dave for the group, which they jokingly describe as having a different "surprise" feeling each morning. "We have this drink that Dave makes for us called vodka surprise... So we have a song named after that."
**Hey Brad** : This song is inspired by Dave Genn's initial impression of Brad Merritt when he first joined the band. Dave, not understanding Brad's personality at the time, asked, "where did you find that guy?", which became the basis for the song. "Dave first joined the band We did we're on the road and he didn't quite understand Brad's vibe and personality And he turned to me and go where did you find that guy? And so there's a song about Brad. It's called hey Brad."
**Food Loser** : This song directly relates to the common experience of dining out with a group where one person's order is incorrect, delayed, or cold. "We have a song called food loser which everybody can relate to this is when you're out dining with a group of people and somebody's order It gets wrong or it doesn't come where it's cold."
**Same Guy Different Body** : This song depicts the common touring experience of a drunk individual who infiltrates the dressing room, initially confident and friendly, but then becomes aggressive and confrontational when perceived as being unwanted. "We have another song called same guy different body Which is that drunk person that somehow worms their way into the dressing room and is all brave And you can't get rid of them and then they get all dark and want to fight you because they there's not that drunk that they know you don't like them."
**Options** : This song recounts a story from a show in Regina, where Blue Rodeo was also playing. Greg Keelor of Blue Rodeo visited 5440's dressing room, and upon hearing Neil Osborne express that he couldn't imagine doing anything else if he stopped making music, Keelor wisely responded, "nice to know your options are behind you," acknowledging a common dilemma for career musicians. "We have another song called options which is a story of When you're playing in Regina at the same time as Blue Rodeo and Keylor came over to check out our set... he goes nice to know your options are behind you."

## curiosities
**Band Longevity and Lineup Stability (1980-Present)** : 5440 has been active since 1980. Despite a few lineup changes (three by one account), the core members have remained with the group, demonstrating remarkable stability and dedication over four decades. "This band has been a going concern since 1980. And well, there have been a couple of lineup changes over the years, three by my account. The core of the group is still there."
**Revolutionary Mission and Influences (Early Career)** : Singer Neil Osborne describes the band's early days as being "on a mission" to "change the world," viewing themselves as "revolutionaries" in the vein of bands like The Clash and U2. This reflects an early ethos driven by artistic and social ambition. "We were on a mission. We were going to change the world. We were like revolutionaries, you know, like the clash and sad and eats the and all that."
**Evolving Perspective on Longevity** : Neil Osborne recounts his changing feelings about the band's duration as he aged, from dreading still being in the band at 30 and 40, to embracing it as "amazing" at 50, and expressing gratitude at 60 for the opportunity to continue. "I'm 30 now shoot myself... I'm 40 shoot me... I'm 50. Amen. This is amazing. And now I'm 60. And it's like, oh my God, I can't believe I get to do this."
**Rolling Stones Longevity Metric** : Brad Merritt uses The Rolling Stones as a benchmark for 5440's own career length, stating that when The Rolling Stones quit, that would serve as 5440's "20 year warning," as 5440 started 20 years after the Stones. "when the rolling stones quit, that's our 20 year warning because we started 20 years after they started."
**Philosophy of Continuous Creation** : The band's guiding principle for longevity and success is to "just keep creating, keep writing" and to pursue what you love, trusting that "everything should take care of itself." "Just keep creating, keep writing. If you love it, do it. And then maybe something will happen. Maybe it won't, but at least you'll be doing what you love."
**Existential Moment: Dropped by Warner (c. 1995)** : After the release of their sixth album, "Smiling Buddha Cabaret," the band was dropped by their American label, Warner. This presented an "existential moment" where they considered quitting but ultimately decided to continue on their own terms rather than following external pressures. "Another existential moment. Another moment where you could pack it in or decide you're going to go on."
**Decision for Artistic Autonomy** : Following a negative experience with their "Show Me" record, which they felt was too influenced by the record company's path of least resistance, the band resolved to "stick together as a band" and pursue their "own thing," rather than solely listening to external advice. "So I think, well, we're just going to stick together as a band and not listen to these people anymore."
**Transition Period and Songwriting Volume (c. 1995-1997)** : During the period after being dropped by Warner and before signing with Sony Music Canada, the band entered a "couple of years" of transition, actively writing a large volume of songs. For a potential record (referred to as "dear, dear record"), they had accumulated approximately 30 to 40 songs. "Drew, we must have had 30 or 40 songs. We have about 30 songs for the dear, dear record."
**Securing a Deal with Sony Music Canada (c. 1997)** : Sony Music Canada, under the leadership of Paul Berger and A&R representative Richard Zuckerman, became interested in 5440 as it sought to grow. This led to a new record deal for the band, though without an accompanying American contract. "Paul Berger was brought in to run the company. And he was eager to find stuff. And somebody mentioned us to him and Richard Zuckerman, who was his A&R guy."
**Hootie & the Blowfish's Fandom and Cover of "I Go Blind"** : Hootie & the Blowfish were early fans of 5440, having attended their shows at the 930 Club in Washington, DC, and covered nearly every song from 5440's "Green Record" in their own cover band, alongside R.E.M. songs. "they came up from University of South Carolina and they were fans. And they played almost every song off the Green Record in their cover band and every REM song that we were there."
**"I Go Blind" on the Friends Soundtrack** : The 5440 song "I Go Blind" was included on the soundtrack for the TV show "Friends." This came about because the A&R executive assembling the soundtrack was the same person who had signed Hootie & the Blowfish to their label. Hootie had recorded "I Go Blind" but cut it from their first album, allowing its use on the soundtrack with the stipulation that it not be released as a single. "It ends up with a friend soundtrack, right?"
**Organic Radio Success of "I Go Blind" Cover** : Despite Hootie & the Blowfish's request not to release "I Go Blind" as a single from the "Friends" soundtrack, radio stations in places like Boulder or Denver started playing it due to Hootie's immense popularity and its "anti-Nirvana" vibe. This led to organic fan requests and widespread airplay, effectively overriding Hootie's new official single. "Just picked just because Hoody was like all the rage, right? It was sort of the anti-Nurvana and people started requesting it."
**Hootie & the Blowfish's Support for 5440** : The members of Hootie & the Blowfish were very supportive of 5440 regarding the success of "I Go Blind." They invited 5440 to tour with them, and their drummer, Sony, personally showed them a Billboard chart indicating "I Go Blind" at number three on Hot AC, assuring them of incoming royalties. "The band were very, very happy for us. We did some tour with them... Sony was, came in with the billboard... he goes, you guys are going to get some money. You know, this is great. I make sure you get every penny."
**Unexpected Royalties from "I Go Blind"** : The significant financial success of Hootie & the Blowfish's cover of "I Go Blind" generated a substantial amount of "mailbox money" for 5440. The first royalty check was so large that Neil Osborne's wife initially believed it was a mistake due to the numerous zeros. "My wife calling me because she used to get the checks in the mail. They made a mistake. They made a mistake. We got to give it back... because there's so many zeros on that first check."
**Garth Richardson's Production Style for "Since When"** : Producer Garth Richardson, known for producing numerous platinum-selling "testosterone, big muscle tattoo, thick neck" records, adapted his approach for 5440's "Since When." The band specifically requested a 16-track, two-inch, intimate record with strings, a departure from his usual "beef records," which Richardson executed well. "Garth is an excellent producer and he went with it and you know, we had the song since when."
**Commercial Licensing of "Since When" Song** : The song "Since When" was licensed for TV commercials, including campaigns for Telus and High on Day. This licensing helped the band to recoup their Sony publishing deal, highlighting a new revenue stream in a changing music landscape. "that song, because it was a tell us and a high on day commercial and some other thing and we recouped all of our Sony publishing publishing."
**Ambivalence Towards Publishing Deals** : The band describes publishing deals as a "double-edged sword." While they provide upfront money (e.g., down payments for houses), musicians later realize they are giving away rights to half their future income after the initial recoupment. "you sell the rights to half of your songs... Wait a minute. I'm giving you half of all my money now. So it's double edged sword there."
**Early Anti-Commercial Stance (Pre-1998)** : Initially, 5440 was strongly against commercial endorsements, embodying a "nearly young don't sing for Miller don't sing for Bud kind of vibe." They turned down offers from cigarette companies and had contractual control over stage visibility, once refusing to play a festival opening for Pearl Jam until a Labatt's balloon was deflated, leading to a standoff. "we were totally against that... cigarette companies... nearly young don't sing for Miller don't sing for Bud kind of vibe."
**Raine Maida's Intervention in Labatt's Standoff (c. 1997)** : During a standoff at a festival in Manitoba (part of the "Trusted by Millions" tour, around 1997-1998) over a Labatt's balloon on stage, Raine Maida, the singer from Our Lady Peace, intervened. He explained 5440's contractual clauses to the promoters, resulting in the balloon being deflated, allowing 5440 to perform. "thanks to him, they took it down."
**Loss of Control Over Venue Branding** : The band eventually lost the battle against corporate branding in venues. Molson, Bud, Labatt's, and cigarette companies began directly contracting with venues and promoters, making it impossible for bands to play without encountering corporate logos. This meant bands no longer had a choice in performing on a "Coca-Cola stage" or similar, losing the fight without realizing it. "the Moulson and the cigarette companies went directly to the venues, went directly to the promoters where if you wanted to play at all, you had to see Moulson or Bud or LaBaz or, you know, players light or whatever it was, or Coke, you didn't have a choice anymore."
**Shift to Accepting Commercial Licensing (Post-1998)** : The band's stance on licensing evolved, particularly after signing with Sony, which offered publishing deals that provided upfront money for things like house down payments. They also recognized that pop songs in commercials were becoming a primary driver of song discovery, similar to what TikTok does today, making it a "new reality." "at that time, songs, pop songs on commercials, we're driving, it's kind of like what TikTok is doing now. People were re-discovering hit songs and stuff like that and you just sort of had to accept once again, that's what that's the new reality."
**Music Video Production Frustrations (Late 1990s)** : The band expressed frustration with the traditional music video production process, which involved large budgets (e.g., 200 grand) for what they perceived as uninspired concepts, often by trendy young directors, such as standing in a "cold, cold puddle in a warehouse." "We would do these videos for 200 grand and they'd hire some student out of Mexico City... I'm like, well, what the hell is this about? This is stupid."
**Digital Revolution and Industry Warnings (Early 2000s)** : During the production of the "Casual Viewing" album, Sony Music Canada president Rick Kamillary warned Neil Osborne that the traditional music industry was "over" due to digital changes like Napster, with CDs "giving away masters" and record companies seeking an "exit plan." This foreshadowed the dramatic shift in music consumption. "He goes, it's over, Neil, it's all over... We're giving away masters, CDs is giving away masters and then Napster and all that started to happen. He goes, I'm looking for my ex a plan."
**Existential Conversation: Adapting to the Digital Age (2000s)** : Facing the decline of the traditional music business and seeing friends lose record deals, Neil Osborne and Brad Merritt had an "existential conversation" at Nathan Phillips Square in Toronto. They decided to leverage their existing body of work from the "old system" while learning to navigate and adapt to the emerging "digital world." "because that was another point where we were saying, yeah, we could just pack it in now and find something else to do. Or how are we going to sort of move forward?"
**Music Ownership Structure** : 5440 owned all their publishing rights for songs created before their Sony deal. For songs recorded with Sony, Sony owned both the masters and the publishing rights, although the band was "paid handsomely" for these arrangements. "we had all of our publishing from before Sony... all the Sony publishing was owned by all the songs we recorded with Sony. They published... They own the masters. Yeah."
**Changing Radio/TV Support (Mid-2000s)** : The band experienced a shift in support from Canadian music channels like MuchMusic, being told they were being moved to outlets like "MuchLOUD" or other less prominent channels. This indicated that their generation of bands was being phased out of mainstream "cool" rotation. "you get told like, yeah, much music isn't going to play. They're throwing you over too much light or whatever the hell it was called."
**COVID-19 as an Impetus for New Music** : During the COVID-19 pandemic, Brad Merritt initiated Zoom calls with the band members, who live in different locations (three on islands, Dave in Vancouver). These calls, initially for reminiscing and telling stories, became the creative spark for their upcoming album, "West Coast Band," where stories from their history are transformed into songs. "Brad came up with the idea of, you know, doing a Zoom call. So we got them and this is this became the impetus, which is for the record that's going to come out next, because we just started telling stories."
**Touring Accident: Lane Guy Falls from Bus** : During a tour stop at a Tower Records Convention in Sacramento, the band's "lane guy" (likely a sound or stage tech) accidentally missed a jump seat in the front of the bus. He hit the door handle on the way out and rolled out of the moving bus. Although not funny at the moment, he emerged unharmed, making it a memorable and eventually humorous road story. "He hits the door handling the way out and then rolls out the bus and we're literally you know 100 meters before we're able to stop So That was it wasn't funny at the moment, but when he rolled around got up and said I'm okay."
**Trip to Moscow During Gorbachev Era** : The band embarked on a week-long exchange trip to Moscow while Mikhail Gorbachev was still in power. They witnessed the last Communist May Day parade and performed a show with a local band called The Scramblers, who later got into trouble with authorities but were released after mentioning "Gretzky." "We were there for a week in an exchange thing while Gorbachev was still the head of It was we saw the last made a parade the last communist made a parade We did a show there with a band called the scramblers, you know who ended up getting in jail and stuff like that Once they said the word gretzky they got let out a jail."
**Encounter with an Aggressive Fan in St. Catharines** : During a show in St. Catharines, an intoxicated woman managed to get into the dressing room. She became increasingly annoying and aggressive, eventually getting into an altercation with a bouncer, breaking his nose. It took three bouncers to remove her from the premises. "We did a show in St. Catherine's whereas it was a woman who worked her way into the dressing room and You know started to be really annoying and you know sit on my lap and I like you and all that and then I sort of Indicated to the bouncer like can you get rid of her please? And so he tried and she went off and hauled and broke his nose And she was tough So it took three bouncer's to get her out."
**Dual Genre Identity: Classic vs. Modern Rock (Pre-COVID)** : Just before the COVID-19 pandemic, 5440 experienced a weekend where they played two distinct festivals. On one day, they were the "youngest band on the bill" playing alongside classic rock acts like Boston and REO Speedwagon, effectively "graduating to classic rock." The very next day, they occupied a middle slot with younger, "super cool bands" like Wintersleep, realizing they were simultaneously embraced by "both camps." "We played two different festivals... So when we were we were a band new band in a classic rock we played with Boston and our EO speed wagon... and then the next day we were sort of in the middle slot of With winter sleep and all these I don't even know the names of these bands the super cool bands."

## curiosities
**Early Fandom of 5440 and R.E.M.** : Before their rise to fame, Hootie & the Blowfish were avid fans of 5440 and R.E.M. They regularly attended 5440's shows at the 930 Club in Washington, DC, and, as a cover band, performed almost every song from 5440's "Green Record" and every R.E.M. song. "They're two favorite bands were REM and 5440 oddly."
**Cover of "I Go Blind" by 5440** : Hootie & the Blowfish covered 5440's song "I Go Blind." Although they had recorded it, it was cut from their first album. It later appeared as a B-side on a cassette single and was licensed for the "Friends" soundtrack. They specifically requested that it not be released as a single from the soundtrack. "They had recorded I Go Blind. It didn't make their first record. It was cut. But they said, you can have this song, but just promise us you won't release it as a single for the friend soundtrack."
**Anti-Nirvana Vibe and Organic Radio Success** : Hootie & the Blowfish were perceived as having an "anti-Nirvana" sound or vibe during their rise in the mid-1990s. This appeal contributed to the organic success of their "I Go Blind" cover on radio, as DJs in cities like Boulder or Denver picked it up and fans requested it widely. "It was sort of the anti-Nurvana and people started requesting it."
**Record Label's Failed Attempt to Stop "I Go Blind" Airplay** : Hootie & the Blowfish's record label attempted to halt the widespread radio play of "I Go Blind" in favor of their newly released official single. However, the new single "pretty well died" on radio, with stations opting to continue playing the popular 5440 cover. "They tried to stop it. I mean, they're new record just been released and they basically told Radio, look, shut that song down. Here's our new single. They released that single and it pretty well died."
**No US Airplay Royalties for Cover Songs** : As an American artist playing a cover on American radio, Hootie & the Blowfish did not receive radio airplay royalties for "I Go Blind." These royalties are typically paid only to the composers and publishers of the original song. Their only benefit came from mechanical royalties (sales) of the "Friends" soundtrack or the cassette single. "if you are an American artist on American radio and you're playing a cover, you don't reap any radio airplay royalties from that because it only goes to the composers."
**Positive Relationship with 5440** : The band maintained a very positive relationship with 5440 following the success of "I Go Blind." They were genuinely happy for 5440's financial gain, toured together, and their drummer, Sony, made efforts to ensure 5440 received their due royalties. "The band were very, very happy for us. We did some tour with them... Sony was, came in with the billboard... he goes, you guys are going to get some money."

These royalties are exclusively directed to the original composers and publishers of the song, meaning bands like Hootie & the Blowfish only benefited from mechanical royalties (sales) for their covers. "if you are an American artist on American radio and you're playing a cover, you don't reap any radio airplay royalties from that because it only goes to the composers."
