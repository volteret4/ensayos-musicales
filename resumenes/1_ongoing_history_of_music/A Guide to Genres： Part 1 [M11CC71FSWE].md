# genre - Rock and Roll

## curiosities
**Rock and Roll to Rock – The Transition Happened June 1, 1967 with Sgt. Pepper** : "Rock and roll" described a specific style of music that rose in the 1950s and reigned for about a dozen years, originating from rhythm and blues. On June 1, 1967, the Beatles released Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band, and this marks the moment when rock and roll graduated from a trendy, disposable music for teenagers into a recognized modern art form taken as seriously as Beethoven, jazz, and Broadway. From that point on, "rock" (as distinct from "rock and roll") referred to anything descended from the sounds of the early-to-mid 1950s. "At first, rock and roll was this trendy, fattish, disposable, immature music for kids and teenagers that would no doubt soon fade away."

# genre - Punk Rock

## curiosities
**Punk Rock (1976) – Reactionary Not Revolutionary; Return to Short Sharp Songs; The Rock Great Schism** : Punk rock emerged almost simultaneously in the US, UK, Canada, and Australia in 1976. It was a reactionary backlash against how complicated and artsy rock had become — a return to short, sharp, melodic songs, but faster, louder, more anti-establishment. The Ramones were the most important early spreaders of the gospel from 1974 onward. British punk was less artsy and more political, concerned with the class system. 1976 was to rock what the Great Schism of 1054 was to the Catholic Church — it split rock into two mostly independent and autonomous streams that ran parallel, crossing over only rarely, each disavowing the other. "It wasn't revolutionary at all. It was reactionary."


# genre - New Wave

## curiosities
**New Wave – Sire Records Marketing Term for Melodic Post-Punk; Short Choppy Songs** : The term New Wave appears to have come from the marketing department of Sire Records in New York City, which was packaging the more melodic, pop-oriented, less angry music emerging after punk. New Wave songs tended to be short, sharp, choppy, somewhat agitated, with odd vocals and clever spins on the pop formula, drawing on the Clash, Bowie, and Roxy Music. Examples include Blondie, the Police, the Cars, the Pretenders, XTC, and the Talking Heads. In the UK, "New Wave" was never used — they said "post-punk" instead. "This was a catch-all phrase that seemed to have come out of the marketing department of Sire Records in New York City."


# genre - Synthpop

## curiosities
**Synthpop / Technopop – Guitars Out, Electronics In; Filled the Post-Disco Dance Void** : Synthpop (also called Technopop) emerged as one of the biggest offshoots of New Wave, based on the idea that guitars were out and electronics were the future. It was also extremely danceable, filling the post-disco dance void that had been created when disco was deemed uncool. Devo (from Akron, Ohio, supported by David Bowie) were among the original synthpop pioneers, and keyboard-focused UK acts like Depeche Mode, Orchestral Manoeuvres in the Dark, and Soft Cell pushed the sound further. "For practitioners and fans, guitars were out. The future was all about electronics."


# genre - Goth

## curiosities
**Goth / Gothic Rock – Joy Division and The Cure into Bauhaus, Alien Sex Fiend, Siouxsie** : Gothic rock began with dark, somber, bleak groups such as Joy Division and The Cure, then got darker with groups like Bauhaus, Alien Sex Fiend, and Siouxsie and the Banshees. The genre focused on the dark side of music, often with genuine Gothic overtones drawn from the original Gothic culture of the 1800s — elements of doom, sometimes romantic, occasionally supernatural. "Goth or Gothic Rock, which focused on the dark side of the music, often with genuine Gothic overtones."


# genre - Alternative Rock

## curiosities
**Alt Rock (North America) vs Indie (UK) – Same Thing, Different Words** : The term "alternative" evolved in North America from FM radio stations in the 1970s that played slightly different music from corporate rock stations (an "alternative" to Journey, Styx, and Boston), and from bands who recorded for independent labels because major labels wouldn't have them. Campus radio stations spread the term. In the UK, the exact same thing was called "Indie," because these bands recorded for independent labels. Both terms describe left-of-center rock that descends not from the Beatles/Stones/Led Zeppelin lineage but from Iggy Pop, Bowie, the Velvet Underground, and the Ramones. "Same say, in North America these artists recorded for independent labels that were an alternative to the mainstream, hence it was alternative music. In the UK these artists recorded for Indy labels."


# genre - Post-Punk

## curiosities
**Post-Punk – Not a Genre but a Collection of Genres; Punk Had to Happen First** : Post-punk is not itself a genre but describes a collection of genres that followed punk. These genres did not necessarily sound like punk, but something like punk had to have happened for them to emerge. The variety of music within the post-punk sphere was enormous. It was not blues-based like mainstream rock — it was punk-based. "Post-punk describes a collection of genres that followed punk. These genres didn't necessarily sound like punk, but something like punk had to have happened for these sounds to have emerged."


# genre - Blues Rock

## curiosities
**Blues Rock → Hard Rock → Heavy Metal; Heavy Metal Term Popularized by Steppenwolf** : Blues Rock became the progenitor of Hard Rock, a term that began appearing around 1968, which led into Heavy Metal by the end of the decade. Heavy metal was largely a British invention — Led Zeppelin, Black Sabbath, Deep Purple — though the term "Heavy Metal" itself was popularized by the Canadian singer John Kay of Steppenwolf. "Blues Rock became known as the progenitor of Hard Rock, a term that began showing up around 1968, which led us into heavy metal by the end of the decade."
