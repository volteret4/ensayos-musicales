## curiosities
**Early Sound System Inadequacy (1972)** : In 1972, The Grateful Dead held a meeting in their Novato, California, warehouse practice space to discuss the inadequacy of their concert sound. Their system, designed by Bob Hyal in 1968, utilized Alltec's "Voice of the Theater" series cabinets, radial horns (a novel inclusion at the time), ring tweeters, and a custom crossover, and while "better than most," it was deemed "lacking by 1972" for the band's needs.
**Development of the Wall of Sound (1972-1973)** : Following the realization that their existing system was inadequate, The Grateful Dead's crew embarked on a year-long experimentation with PA gear, led by Owsley "Bear" Stanley. Their efforts culminated in the unveiling of the revolutionary "Wall of Sound" in 1973, a system designed to drastically improve live sound. "Over the next year, their crew experimented with PA gear, led by a former ballet dancer from Kentucky turned LSD drug dealer name Ausley Bear Stanley."
**The Wall of Sound (1973)** : The "Wall of Sound" was an innovative and massive concert sound system unveiled by The Grateful Dead in 1973. It weighed 75 tons, stood three stories tall, was 100 feet wide, and required four semis and 21 people for transport. The system contained hundreds of speakers, specifically 568 JBLs and approximately 50 Electro Voice tweeters, powered by mostly Macintosh tube amps pumping out around 26,000 watts. Vocals went through one set of speakers while instrumentation went through others, arranged in "line arrays" for even sound dispersion. "The wall contained hundreds of speakers, 568 JBLs, and about 50 electro voice tweeters, to be specific, along with amplifiers, mostly Macintosh tube amps, pumping out something around 26,000 watts, which was absolutely insane for its day."
**Wall of Sound Debut and Dismantling (1974)** : The Grateful Dead's "Wall of Sound" made its debut at the Cow Palace in San Francisco on March 23, 1974. Despite its impressive scale and sound quality—described as "so loud and so clear that it sounded great at a quarter of a mile"—it proved to be too expensive to operate and maintain. Consequently, the band had the system dismantled within a year of its introduction. "Within a year, though, it proved to be too expensive, and the band headed dismantled."

# instrument - Speaker Cabinets
## curiosities
**Horizontal Piles (1970s)** : Throughout most of the 1970s, typical concert sound systems consisted of "horizontal piles of black speaker boxes" stacked on either side of the stage. These boxes were often "aimed haphazardly" without much consideration for venue acoustics, reflecting the prevailing attitude of "make it loud enough, and nobody's going to care." "During this period, which, honestly, consisted most of the 1970s, concert sound systems consisted of horizontal piles of black speaker boxes on either side of the stage."
**Hoisting from Rafters (Bill Hanley)** : Bill Hanley pioneered the practice of hoisting speaker cabinets from venue rafters using chain-driven electric motors. This innovation not only "freed up more performance space on the stage" but also allowed the music to "flow out over the crowd rather than down into it," significantly improving sound dispersion. "Hanley was the first guy to hoist speaker cabinets from the rafters using chain driven electric motors."
**Semi-Cylindrical Vertical Clusters (1970s-1980s)** : PA technicians discovered that organizing speakers into "semi-cylindrical vertical clusters" and hanging them from scaffolding or roofs produced improved results for sound dispersion. Although this was a significant step forward, it was later found that these clusters could only be made "so big before an unacceptable amount of distortion set in." "PA text learned that organizing speakers into semi-cylurgical vertical clusters produced the best results."
**Line Source Systems (1990s)** : The 1990s brought about the major development of "line source systems," which are single rows of speakers hanging from rigging. While this is one of the oldest PA system concepts, it only became practical in the 90s due to more powerful and affordable amplifiers and efficient, safe hanging methods, making setup and teardown quick and easy. "The big development was line source systems. These are single rows of speakers hanging from the rigging of the rafters."
**Curved Line Arrays (Post-1990s)** : As the 1990s progressed, line arrays evolved to incorporate a slight curve. This design refinement enabled "more clear, more focused sound" by minimizing interference and efficiently directing sound. Due to their "highly directional nature," little amplifier power is wasted on walls or the space above the audience, a technology now dominant in the concert industry. "As the 1990s passed, the up and down line arrays developed a slight curve."

# instrument - Monitor Speakers
## curiosities
**Improvised Stage Monitors (1965)** : During The Beatles' notoriously poor-sounding Shea Stadium concert in 1965, the band couldn't hear themselves. In a desperate, last-minute solution, one of the road crew members "flipped over some of their amplifiers and pointed them directly at the band," effectively creating the earliest form of on-stage monitoring. "As a last second fix, one of the road crew flipped over some of their amplifiers and pointed them directly at the band."
**Wedge-Shaped Stage Monitors (Bill Hanley)** : Bill Hanley was the first to employ "wedge-shaped monitor speakers that fired the music back at the performers." This innovation, inspired by The Beatles' improvised solution at Shea Stadium, allowed musicians to "actually hear what was happening on stage" and avoid confusion from main PA echoes, significantly improving performance quality. "Hanley was the first to employ those wedge-shaped monitor speakers that fired the music back at the performers so they could actually hear what was happening on stage and not get confused by any echoes from the main PA coming back at them."
**Side-Fill Monitors** : In addition to wedge-shaped floor monitors, some stage setups included "side-fill monitors," which were large speakers positioned on either side of the stage and pointed towards the middle. These ensured that performers "would be no place you could go on stage without hearing yourself," providing comprehensive stage coverage. "Some setups also had side-fill monitors, big speakers and either side of the stage pointing towards the middle so there would be no place you could go on stage without hearing yourself."
**Loud Stage and Hearing Loss** : The necessity for traditional stage monitors to "overpower the sound of the main PA" meant that the stage itself became an "extremely loud place." This environment significantly contributed to the hearing loss experienced by many musicians throughout their careers. "No wonder so many musicians went deaf."
**Disappearance of Traditional Monitors (Post-2000)** : Traditional wedge-shaped monitors and side-fill speakers have "largely disappeared" from concert stages, particularly since around 2000. They have been replaced by wireless in-ear monitors, which offer a more controlled, quieter, and personalized listening experience for performers. "Remember those wedge-shaped monitors and side-fill speakers for performers? They've largely disappeared and have been replaced by what are essentially earbuds."

# instrument - In-Ear Monitors
## curiosities
**Invention by Jerry Harvey for Alex Van Halen (1990s)** : In-ear monitors were invented by sound engineer Jerry Harvey, who was working with Van Halen. He developed them specifically after drummer Alex Van Halen complained that the "constant on-stage volume was killing his hearing," leading to a breakthrough in musician monitoring. "The inventor was a guy named Jerry Harvey, a sound engineer who worked with Van Halen."
**Components and Customization** : Jerry Harvey's initial in-ear monitors, developed for Alex Van Halen, were constructed using tiny components sourced from Japan, including a speaker originally designed for a heart pacemaker. These devices were custom-fitted to Alex Van Halen's ears, with wires leading to a transceiver on his belt. "He saw some tiny components from Japan, including a speaker that was originally designed for a heart pacemaker."
**Functionality and Benefits** : Wireless in-ear monitors provide performers with their "personal mix of vocals and instrumentation" while on stage, effectively "shut[ting] out all the extraneous noise" and reducing overall volume. This protection prevents hearing damage and ensures clarity, enabling musicians to hear exactly what they need without blowing out their eardrums. "These wireless in-ear monitors shut out all the extraneous noise, tone down the volume, and allow them to hear exactly what they need to hear without blowing out their eardrums."
**Initial Adoption Challenges and Cost (Early 2000s)** : In-ear monitors, which began appearing around 2000, typically cost between $700 and $900 a pair. Jerry Harvey faced initial difficulty convincing performers to adopt them, as musicians were concerned about the resemblance to hearing aids and the new technology. "Jerry had a hard time convincing performers that, no, no, it's not going to look like you're wearing hearing aids."
**Integration of Crowd Noise (Post-2000)** : To address performers' complaints that in-ear monitors isolated them from the audience, modern systems incorporate "a couple of microphones on or near the stage pointing out towards the audience." These mics capture cheering and cycle it into the earpieces, allowing performers to feel the audience's energy. **Early Acoustics - Stonehenge** : Some archaeologists believe that part of Stonehenge's design involved principles of natural amplification, suggesting an ancient understanding of how to enhance music and chanting through acoustic properties. "Some archaeologists think that part of the design of Stonehenge involved creating something that would naturally amplify music and chanting."
**Ancient Greek and Roman Amphitheaters** : The Greeks and Romans constructed large amphitheaters for public performances and discovered that draping a tent over the top improved acoustics by containing the sound. This technique was notably employed in structures like the Roman Colosseum to enhance audio. "They built large amphitheaters, which they discovered worked better for acoustics if you draped a tent over the top to keep the sound in."
**Gothic Cathedral Acoustics** : Gothic cathedrals, primarily built from stone, reflect approximately 98% of all sound, creating highly echoey environments with reverberation times potentially exceeding three seconds. These acoustics made intelligible human speech difficult but perfectly suited for music with long notes, which took advantage of the natural amplification and reverberation, explaining why mass was chanted instead of spoken. "Hearing intelligible human speech is pretty much impossible in a Gothic cathedral."
**Opera Singer Projection Techniques** : Opera singers developed their distinctive vocal projection techniques out of necessity, needing to project their voices to "vast crowds" without any microphones or amplification. This need directly influenced the architectural design of opera houses, which were built to facilitate the natural flow of sound from the stage back over multi-level audiences. "No mics or amps. They needed to project to vast crowds, which is also where the design of opera houses came in."
**First Sound Guy - Gaspar P. Schotto (1657)** : Gaspar P. Schotto is recognized as the first true sound guy, publishing a book on acoustics titled "Megave Universalis" in 1657. He was a pioneer in systematically studying "the behavior of sound," with a particular interest in its distribution, amplification, collection, reflection, and how it traversed spaces of varying sizes and shapes. "The first true sound guy was Gaspar P. Schotto. He wrote a book on acoustics called Megave Universalis in 1657."
**Musician Volume Solutions (Pre-Amplification)** : Before the advent of amplification, musicians had two primary strategies to increase their audibility for larger crowds in bigger venues: "either A played harder and thus louder, or B added more people to your band." This fundamental challenge explains why symphony orchestras are so large and why vocalists, like those in vaudeville, had to sing louder. "Why do you think symphony orchestra is so large?"
**Post-WWII Shift to Smaller Combos** : After World War II, the high cost of maintaining large big bands led to a trend towards "smaller combos of four and five people." This shift forced musicians to "play harder in order to be heard," consequently affecting the music's composition and performance style, making the beat "bigger and more important." "But after World War II, it became awfully expensive to maintain a big, big band, so the trend was towards smaller combos of four and five people, and everybody was back to playing harder in order to be heard."
**Early Rock Concert Sound Systems (1950s-1960s)** : In the 1950s and 60s, a "proper concert sound system" did not exist. Most setups consisted of a combination mixer-amplifier powering two PA columns, positioned on either side of the stage, each containing two 12-inch speakers. These columns "weren't much bigger than standard guitar amps, and frankly, they weren't very loud or clear."
**Fundamental Challenge of Live Sound Amplification** : The core problem in live sound reinforcement is to amplify small sound sources on a stage to reach large numbers of people in a vast space while achieving "maximum realism and minimum distortion." This technical challenge remains complex, requiring sophisticated equipment and acoustic understanding. "To make this work, you have to amplify the small sound sources on a stage to reach large numbers of people in a large space with maximum realism and minimum distortion."
**Inefficient Early PA Systems (1970s)** : While significant progress was made in PA systems after early festivals, those developed and used throughout most of the 1970s, though loud, "weren't very efficient." They demanded "a lot of power to drive them" and "still didn't sound very good," offering little realism in their audio delivery. "Big strides, yes. But while this system and the new PA systems that followed were quite loud, they weren't very efficient."
**Concert Sound as "Piled-Up Experiments" (1970s)** : During the 1970s, concert sound systems were often described as "piled up experiments" — hand-built systems that were not thoroughly tested before being deployed on the road. Companies supplying this gear frequently used a "mix-and-match approach" with random components from various manufacturers, indicative of an industry in its early, experimental phase. "I've heard them described as piled up experiments, hand-built systems that weren't really properly tested for performance before being sent out on the road."
**Quadrophonic Sound Introduction** : Quadrophonic sound was an emerging technology that added depth to concert audio by utilizing additional speakers, specifically "front left and right, and rear left and right." This innovation aimed to create a more immersive and spatially rich listening experience for the audience. "Another new trick was quadrophonic sound."
**Delay Towers for Outdoor Festivals** : To address sound weakening and synchronization issues at large outdoor festivals, "delay towers" were introduced. These secondary speaker sets were placed strategically where primary sound began to fade, ensuring that people in the back could hear almost as well as those near the stage and that sound matched visual cues. "These were secondary sets of speakers placed where the sound from the primary speakers began to weaken."
**Mono Mixing in Live Concerts (1980s)** : Contrary to common assumptions, many live shows are mixed in mono rather than stereo. This practice is chosen because "sound that seems to come from a single source has less distortion," which translates to "better fidelity" for the audience, prioritizing clarity over a stereo image. "Plenty of shows are not mixed in stereo. They're mixed in mono."
**Shift to Ceiling-Hung Sound Systems (Mid-1980s)** : By the mid-1980s, a major transformation occurred in concert sound systems with "almost everyone" moving their speakers "from the floor to the ceiling." These hung setups, whether on either side of the stage or evenly distributed above, provided "more room for stage setups," enhancing the visual spectacle of rock shows. "By the middle 1980s, almost everyone was moving their sound systems from the floor to the ceiling."
**Loudest Band Measures (Historical Overview)** : Throughout concert history, various bands have pushed the boundaries of live volume. The Who set a record at 126 decibels in 1976, followed by claims of Motörhead hitting 130 decibels (causing structural damage in 1986), Leftfield reaching 137 decibels (shedding plaster in 1998), and Hanson eventually measuring 140 decibels in 1998, the highest the meter could register. "what's the loudest band has ever played?"
**Concert Industry's "Good Enough" Standard (Pre-1990s)** : For decades, the concert industry operated under the premise that "a certain level was good enough for the audience." After experiencing years of poor sound, most attendees "expected the sound to be reasonably lousy and distorted," leading to a stagnation in significant sound innovation until later. "The concert industry had long come to the conclusion that a certain level was good enough for the audience."
