# curiosity

**US Historical Censorship of Music – Jazz Banned on Sundays (1920s), R&B Called "Dirty Music" (1930s–50s)** : Despite the First Amendment, the United States has a long history of attempting to censor music. In the 1920s, there were efforts to ban jazz on Sundays on the grounds that it was "evil." In the 1930s through 1950s, rhythm and blues — music primarily by Black artists — was routinely described as "dirty music." "In the 1920s there were attempts to ban jazz on Sundays as 'evil,' and in the 30s–50s R&B was called 'dirty music.'"

**"Divestiture Laws" – Financial Censorship Forcing Pension Funds to Sell Shares in Music Companies** : A subtle form of financial censorship used in the United States, "divestiture laws" compel state agencies and funds — such as teachers' pension funds — to divest from companies that distribute or control music considered objectionable. For example, a teachers' pension fund could be forced to sell its shares in Seagram, which owned Universal Music, home of Marilyn Manson. "Forced state agencies and funds to sell shares in companies distributing 'objectionable' music."

**Florida Legislator Withheld $100,000+ from Public Radio Station for "Frequently Praising Kurt Cobain" (1997)** : In 1997, a Florida legislator successfully blocked more than $100,000 in funding to public radio station WMNF as punishment for its programming, which included what was characterized as "frequent praise of musician Kurt Cobain." "A legislator withheld more than $100,000 from a public radio station as punishment for its 'frequent praise of Kurt Cobain.'"

**Ohio School Extended Band Clothing Ban to Christian T-Shirts After Student Protest** : After one Ohio school banned clothing bearing "Satanic symbols," students protested by wearing Christian band t-shirts with Biblical quotations. The school responded by extending the ban to those garments as well. "After banning 'Satanic symbol' clothing, the school extended the ban to Christian band t-shirts with biblical quotes after student protests."
