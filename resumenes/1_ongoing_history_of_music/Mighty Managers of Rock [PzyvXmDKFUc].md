## albums
**Santa Monica 72 (2008) – Live Album** : This live album began as a live radio broadcast, later morphed into a bootleg, and was eventually released semi-officially before receiving a full legal release in 2008. The journey of this record caused numerous headaches for David Bowie, his manager Tony DeFries, and others involved.
"Not recording, taken from a live album entitled Santa Monica 72, started as a live radio broadcast, and then morphed into a bootleg, and then was ultimately released semi-efficiently before getting full legal release in 2008."

## curiosities
**Manager Tony DeFries' Early Guidance (1970-1972)** : Tony DeFries, a solicitor by profession, signed on as Bowie's manager in 1970 when Bowie was a struggling young singer. Within approximately 18 months, DeFries secured Bowie a new deal with RCA records and proceeded to transform him into an international star. The entirety of Bowie's early 1970s career, including the iconic Ziggy Stardust era, was carefully guided by DeFries.
"The entire early 70s period of Bowie's career, including the Ziggy Stardust era, was guided by Tony DeFries."
**Contractual Dispute with Main Man (1972-1975)** : David Bowie wanted out of his contract with Tony DeFries' management company, Main Man, by the end of 1974, just two years into their relationship. Despite selling millions of albums, Bowie realized that most of the profits were not reaching him, suspecting that the new contract signed with Main Man at the end of September 1972 was "weird." On January 29, 1975, Bowie informed RCA, his record label, of his intention to split from DeFries, sparking a battle over who owned Bowie's next album.
"Although he was selling millions of albums, most of the profits were not coming to him."
**Bitter Split from Tony DeFries (April 1975)** : Following Bowie's decision to split from Tony DeFries, a 48-hour marathon negotiation took place in April 1975 to finalize his departure. The terms of the split were so harsh that it's said Bowie broke down and cried at the conference table, remaining in shock for a week. The agreement stipulated that Bowie could leave only if Main Man took half of his earnings from albums dating back to *Hunky Dory* in 1971 through an upcoming live album, and DeFries was guaranteed 16% of Bowie's gross earnings for the next seven years, through to 1982.
"The terms of the split were so horrible that it said that Bowie broke down and cried at the conference table and was in shock for a week."

## curiosities
**Formation and Vision (1975)** : Malcolm McLaren conceived the Sex Pistols as a living, breathing advertisement for his Kings Road shop, which by then was renamed Sex. The band was also intended to be an outlet for McLaren's semi-seditious political beliefs, designed to shock and shake up the stuffy British establishment. McLaren assembled the group using "snotty kids" who frequented his store, naming them "Sex" after his shop and "Pistols" because it sounded dangerous.
"He came up with the idea of creating a band that would be a living, breathing advertisement for his shop and an outlet for his semi-siditious political beliefs."
**Record Label Controversies (1976-1977)** : The Sex Pistols, managed by Malcolm McLaren's Glitter Best, first secured a deal with EMI Records. However, they were soon dropped due to their controversial nature and were essentially paid to leave. Next, they signed with A&M Records, a contract that notoriously lasted only five days before the band was again paid to go away. Their third and final deal was with Virgin Records, and this one "stuck," allowing the Sex Pistols to become a sensation and indeed challenge the British establishment.
"He managed to get the pistols a deal with EMI, but they were dropped because the band was too controversial."
**Disputes with Manager Malcolm McLaren (1978)** : As the Sex Pistols gained fame, members, particularly singer Johnny Rotten, grew suspicious of Malcolm McLaren, accusing him of mismanagement of "everything, really." The band frequently found that there was no money available when they wanted to get paid. This deteriorating relationship culminated in the band's implosion at the end of a disastrous American tour in 1978.
"But the band, especially singer Johnny Rotten, soon felt that Malcolm was up to no good."
**Litigation and Control (1978-1987)** : Following the Sex Pistols' implosion in 1978, years of litigation ensued involving the band members and Malcolm McLaren's management company, Glitter Best. It took until 1987, nearly a decade after their breakup, for the Sex Pistols to finally wrestle away control of their music from McLaren.
"Years of litigation followed involving members of the band and Glitter Best."

# artist - Malcolm McLaren

## albums
**Duck Rock (1983)** : This album, released in 1983, featured Malcolm McLaren's exploration of rap and hip hop, sounds he discovered from New York in the early 80s. It included the World's Famous Supreme Team, a DJ duo credited with introducing the idea of scratching. *Duck Rock* was massively influential, particularly in the UK, and helped McLaren perpetrate hip hop similar to how he did with punk, but with fewer legal issues.
"In 1983, he featured a record called Duck Rock that featured the world's famous Supreme team, a DJ duo, who helped introduce the idea of scratching."

## songs
**Buffalo Gals (1983)** : This song from Malcolm McLaren's 1983 album *Duck Rock* is a notable example of his integration of rap and hip hop elements into his music. It became well-known for its distinctive vocal hook.
"Two Buffalo Gals go around the outside, round the outside, round the outside."

## curiosities
**Early Life and Fashion Career** : Born in Yemen and raised by his grandmother in London, Malcolm McLaren attended various art schools before deciding on a career in fashion. He was always a schemer and, along with his girlfriend Vivienne Westwood, opened a shop at 430 Kings Road in London, initially called Paradise Garage in 1971. The shop evolved through names like Too Fast to Live, Too Young to Die and Sex, becoming a hub for unusual clothing, including fetish and bondage gear.
"He was born in Yemen and raised by his grandmother in London."
**New York Dolls and Band Management** : After a trip to a New York fashion fair in 1973, Malcolm McLaren became deeply interested in the New York Dolls and decided to try his hand at band management. This initial venture was a disaster, and the Dolls soon broke up, but McLaren remained undeterred.
"After a trip to a New York fashion fair, the National Fashion boutique in 1973, he became deeply interested in the New York dolls and eventually decided that he would like to give band management a go."
**Embracing Hip Hop and Electronic Music (1980s)** : In the early 1980s, Malcolm McLaren discovered rap and hip hop from New York and became a major evangelist for the new sound, incorporating these elements into his own records. He later ventured into electronic and club music, releasing a series of singles and albums that achieved success on the dance charts.
"By the early 80s, Malcolm had discovered something else from New York, rap and hip hop."
**British Airways Commercial Collaboration (1980s)** : Malcolm McLaren worked on TV advertisements for British Airways, famously arranging a piece called "The Flower Duet" with Greek composer Yanni. This piece was cribbed from the French opera *Lakmé* and became widely recognized as part of the "world's favorite airline" campaign.
"Together with Greek composer Yanny, he arranged a piece called The Flower Duet, which was cribbed from a French opera named Lock Pee for the airline."
**Film, TV, and Art Ventures (1980s-1990s)** : McLaren diversified his career into film and TV projects, serving as both a director and producer, and occasionally as a performer. He was also a painter and, in 1985, he took a job with CBS Films where he oversaw various productions, including a movie titled "Heavy Metal Serving Nazis." He was also involved in a biopic about Led Zeppelin manager Peter Grant.
"Malcolm got into film and TV projects as both a director and a producer."
**Political Ambition and Reality TV (1990s-2000s)** : In 1999, Malcolm McLaren launched a campaign to be mayor of London, famously promising to allow public libraries to serve alcohol, though he never made it to Election Day. In the 2000s, he became a regular on reality TV shows like *Big Brother*.
"In 1999, he launched a campaign to be mayor of London, and one of his big promises was to allow public libraries to serve alcohol."
**Final Illness and Legacy (2009-2010)** : In October 2009, Malcolm McLaren was diagnosed with mesothelioma, a form of lung cancer often linked to asbestos exposure. He died on April 8, 2010, at a hospital in Switzerland. His burial featured a coffin spray-painted with "Too Fast to Live, Too Young to Die," and Sid Vicious's version of "My Way" was played. His headstone encapsulates his philosophy with the epitaph: "Better a Spectacular Failure than a benign success."
"In October 2009, Malcolm McLaren was diagnosed with mesothelioma, a form of lung cancer, most often associated with exposure to his bestest."

**Battle of the Bands Victory (March 1978)** : In March 1978, The Hype (later U2) won a battle of the bands competition, earning enough money to record their very first demos. Jackie Hayden, a marketing manager at CBS Ireland whom the band frequently pestered for industry advice, was one of the judges at this concert.
"He had also been one of the judges of a battle of the band's concert in March 1978, when the hype won enough money to record some of their very first demos."

Clayton convinced McGuinness to attend a gig at the Project Art Center where the band, now renamed U2, was opening for a group called The Gamblers. McGuinness was impressed by what he saw and heard.
"Paul was stalked by Adam Clayton, the bass player who was acting as the band's manager at the time."
**Paul McGuinness's Management Agreement** : After meeting the band and liking their performance, Paul McGuinness agreed to manage U2. He made a modest promise that "one day, they might be able to afford their own band." His commission deal was set at 20% for all five people involved, with all profits to be shared equally, an arrangement noted as "unusually high" but justified by McGuinness's dedication.
"He wouldn't promise the band much. Other than one day, they might be able to afford their own band."
**Decades of Management by Principal Management** : U2's career was directed for four decades by Principal Management, the company headed by Paul McGuinness. McGuinness stepped down from managing the band in 2014, selling Principal Management to Live Nation.

This led to a bitter dispute and Bowie's desire to terminate the contract.
"There was something weird about that new contract he signed with Main Man at the end of September 1972."
**Profits from Bowie Split (1975)** : As part of Bowie's 1975 split from Main Man, the company took half of Bowie's earnings from albums, dating back to *Hunky Dory* (1971) through an upcoming live album. Additionally, DeFries was guaranteed 16% of Bowie's gross earnings for seven years, through to 1982.
"The terms were this, Bowie could leave only if Main Man took half of Bowie's earnings from albums back to Hunky Dory in 1971 through an upcoming live album."

# curiosity

**Role of Music Managers** : Managers are crucial figures who handle all the business aspects of an artist's career, allowing musicians to focus on making music. Their responsibilities include booking gigs, marketing, promotions, publicity, managing support staff and road crews, collecting money, paying bills, and overseeing legal and accounting infrastructure. Beyond business, they act as advisors, sounding boards, fixers, and even informal psychologists or physicians, needing to be informed on trends, connected within the industry, and adept at navigating record companies and contracts. This is a 24/7 job where they can "make or break a career," typically earning around 15% commission.
"Bottom line is that a manager can make or break a career."
**Sphin Gali Archetype in Management** : The term "Sphin Gali" originates from a character in the 1894 book *Trilby*, an evil hypnotist who manipulates a young female performer, turning her into a pawn. This name has evolved into a noun used to describe anyone who exerts such dominating and controlling influence, notably applied to Malcolm McLaren, the manager of the Sex Pistols.
"That character's name, Sphin Gali, has since become a noun describing anyone who does just that."
**Managerial Prominence and Reputation** : Managers are considered the most important people in any musical organization, second only to the artists themselves. Their impact can be so significant, whether positive or negative, that some managers achieve a level of fame almost comparable to their clients, leading to much written about their careers.
"Next to the artists themselves, the manager is the most important person in any musical organization for all the reasons I've already outlined."
**Post-Breakup Litigation in Music** : The music industry, particularly after band breakups or disputes, can be plagued by lengthy legal battles. The Sex Pistols, for instance, endured years of litigation with Malcolm McLaren's Glitter Best management company following their 1978 implosion, finally regaining control of their music only in 1987.
"Years of litigation followed involving members of the band and Glitter Best."
**Fashion's Influence on Music Management** : The intersection of fashion and music played a significant role in the careers of several managers, notably Malcolm McLaren and Bernie Rhodes. McLaren, who came from a fashion background and owned several shops on Kings Road, explicitly conceived the Sex Pistols as a "living, breathing advertisement" for his store. He also managed Bow Wow Wow specifically to showcase Vivienne Westwood's new fashions, highlighting how style and image were integral to his management philosophy.
"He came up with the idea of creating a band that would be a living, breathing advertisement for his shop and an outlet for his semi-siditious political beliefs."
**Challenges of Record Contracts for Artists** : Young and emerging artists often face significant difficulties in understanding and navigating complex record contracts, which can lead to exploitative situations. David Bowie's "weird" contract with Main Man resulted in him receiving minimal profits despite millions in album sales, while The Clash's manager Bernie Rhodes negotiated a "very poor" deal. Even U2's young members sought help from a label marketing manager to comprehend their contracts.
"Help us read through this record contract so that we all understand what it means."
**Varying Manager Commission Structures** : While a typical manager's commission is around 15% of an artist's earnings, arrangements can vary. Paul McGuinness, for example, negotiated a 20% commission with U2, which was considered "unusually high." However, in this case, the deal also stipulated that all profits would be shared equally among the five individuals involved, demonstrating a commitment to the band's collective success.
"His commission deal was for 20%. For all five people involved. And all profits were to be shared equally."
**Managerial Succession and Impact** : The transition of managers can have profound effects on a band's trajectory. After Brian Epstein, who guided The Beatles from a local bar band to global stardom, died in 1967, he was succeeded by Alan Klein. Klein, who also managed The Rolling Stones, is noted for playing a role in the breakup of The Beatles, underscoring the critical influence a manager can wield over a group's fate.
"He was succeeded by Alan Klein, also the manager of the Rolling Stones, who played a role in the breakup of the Beatles."
**Managerial Reputation and Fear Tactics** : Some managers gain notoriety for their intimidating and aggressive methods. Peter Grant, Led Zeppelin's manager, was known for protecting the band with "fear and constant threats of violence." Similarly, Don Arden, who managed acts like Black Sabbath and Air Supply, earned monikers such as the "English Godfather" and the "Al Capone of Pop" for his tough approach.
"He was the fearsome scary Peter Grants, who protected Led Zeppelin with fear and constant threats of violence."
**Linda Stein's Tragic Death (October 30, 2007)** : Linda Stein, former co-manager of The Ramones and a successful "real estate agent to the stars," died on October 30, 2007, at the age of 62. She was found dead in her Manhattan apartment from blunt force trauma, and her death was immediately ruled a homicide. Her former personal assistant, Natavia Lowry, confessed to the murder, citing Stein's yelling as the motive, and also stole $30,000 in cash, receiving a sentence of 27 years to life, though she later recanted her confession.
"But then she died on October 30th, 2007 at the age of 62."
**Managerial Career Path from Film to Music** : Paul McGuinness, U2's long-term manager, began his career as a film technician in Ireland and the UK, even appearing in the credits of the 1974 sci-fi film *Zardoz*. His heroes were managers like Brian Epstein, Andrew Luke Oldham, and Albert Grossman, and he was drawn to music management for its creative potential to build something with "cultural impact" despite not being a musician himself.
"He began as a technician working on films through Ireland and the UK."
**Managerial Longevity and Sale of Company** : Paul McGuinness demonstrated significant longevity in music management, directing U2's career for four decades through his company, Principal Management. He eventually stepped down from managing U2 in 2014 and sold Principal Management to Live Nation, later transitioning to work primarily in television.
"He stepped down from managing you to in 2014 and sold Principal Management to live nation."
**Managerial Work-Life Balance and Death** : Rob Gretton, a central figure in the Manchester punk and dance scene who managed Joy Division and New Order for 20 years, balanced his management duties with other roles such as a baggage handler at Manchester Airport and a DJ. He was widely regarded as kind and generous, but died prematurely on May 15, 1999, at the age of 46, due to a heart attack.
"But then he died on May 15th, 1999 at the age of 46. It was a heart attack."
**Female Managers in Rock History** : Susan Silver is highlighted as "maybe the most important woman of the Grunge era that you've never heard about," and as "one of the most successful female managers in rock history." She started managing bands in Seattle in 1983, including The U-Men, Screaming Trees, Soundgarden, and Alice in Chains, demonstrating significant influence in a male-dominated industry.
"Susan Silver, maybe the most important woman of the Grunge era that you've never heard about."
**Managerial Discretion and Professionalism** : John Silva, who runs Silver Artist Management (managing Foo Fighters, Queens of the Stone Age, Beck, and many others), is characterized by his and his staff's extremely low-key approach. There is very little public information about Silva, who prioritizes his clients above personal publicity, rarely doing interviews, posing for photos, or seeking headlines.
"As for John himself, there's very little public information about him."
**Founding of Q Prime and Specialization** : Cliff Bernstein, who started in A&R at Mercury Records in the 1970s, and Peter Mensch, a former college radio DJ, co-founded the management company Q Prime in 1982. Their first client was Def Leppard, and the company quickly specialized in heavy rock, eventually managing an extensive roster including AC/DC, Scorpions, Metallica, and The Red Hot Chili Peppers.
"In 1982, he and another manager named Peter Minch, a former college radio DJ, who had been fired from his job at another management agency where he looked after Erasmith, founded Q Prime."
**Q Prime's Impact and Privacy** : Cliff Bernstein and Peter Mensch, founders of Q Prime, have collectively helped their artists sell hundreds of millions of records over the years. Despite their enormous success and influence, they maintain a very private profile, rarely giving interviews, posing for photos, or being seen partying, actively staying out of headlines.
"They don't do a lot of interviews. They don't pose for photos and aren't seen partying anywhere and generally stay away from making any headlines."
