# curiosity

**Vinyl's Decline and Resurgence (Early 2000s - Present)** : At the start of the 21st century, vinyl records were largely considered an obsolete format, viewed as dusty, scratchy, noisy, space-consuming, and prone to damage, especially in contrast to the then-emerging portable MP3s. Manufacturing significantly declined, with pressing plants closing. Only hardcore collectors and DJs maintained interest. However, from 2008 onwards, vinyl experienced a remarkable "zombie-like" resurgence, and by the 2020s, it began generating more revenue than compact discs due to premium pricing, despite ongoing challenges like supply chain issues and rising costs. "Starting in 2008, a weird thing happened. Like some zombie in one of those old Italian horror movies of the late 70s and early 80s, the format rose from the dead."
**Vinyl Record Origin Date (1948)** : The modern vinyl record format was introduced in 1948. "It dates back to 1948."
**Analog vs. Digital Sound Reproduction** : Vinyl records are an analog format, designed to smoothly capture real-world sounds. Compact discs (CDs), established with standards in the 1970s, are digital, sampling sound 44,100 times per second with a 16-bit depth, which can store up to 65,536 levels of information to define dynamic range, operating at 44.1 KHz 16-bit. CDs generally offer superior sound quality to MP3s, as MP3s are compressed files with removed information, which the brain attempts to compensate for. "Vinyl is an analog format, which means it smoothly captures real-world sounds. The compact disc is digital."
**Loudness Wars (Late 1990s - Present)** : The "loudness wars" describe a mastering trend, particularly prevalent with CDs, where music is excessively compressed to achieve a louder overall volume. This process squashes dynamic range, leading to clipping and distortion in the loudest parts of the audio. Notable offenders include Metallica's *St. Anger* (2003) and *Death Magnetic* (2008), Red Hot Chili Peppers' releases since 1999, Green Day's *American Idiot*, Oasis's *(What's the Story) Morning Glory?*, and Foo Fighters' *Echoes, Silence, Patience & Grace*. "This means the dynamic range is squished, and this in turn can result in clipping. That means the loudest bits end up being distorted, and this has become known as the loudness wars."
**Vinyl Sound Characteristics** : Vinyl recordings are particularly effective at preserving mid-range frequencies, which benefits sounds like "fuzzy rock guitars." However, the format can struggle with extremely high and low frequencies, where CDs excel. The perceived "warmth" of vinyl might be attributable to slight distortion in the bass frequencies. Additionally, sound quality on a vinyl record can degrade as the stylus moves closer to the center, where the smaller circumference and increased stylus speed make accurate groove tracing more difficult. "Vinyl recordings are very good at preserving the mid-range frequencies. That's where fuzzy rock guitars live, so they might sound better on vinyl to some people."
**Analog vs. Digital Source Material for Vinyl** : If the original source material for a vinyl record was recorded digitally, the resulting vinyl record is merely an analog copy of a digital recording, which may diminish the perceived benefits of vinyl. A truly noticeable difference in sound quality is most likely when the entire production chain, from initial studio recording to the final pressing of the record, remains purely analog. "it may not matter if the original material for a vinyl record was recorded digitally. You just end up with an analog copy of a digital recording."
**Vinyl Manufacturing Quality Eras (Late 1970s - Early 1980s)** : The quality of vinyl record manufacturing significantly declined between the late 1970s and early 1980s. During this period, raw polyvinyl chloride (PVC) was often of poor quality, frequently recycled with impurities. Labels also reduced the amount of vinyl used per record, resulting in lighter discs (e.g., 90 grams) compared to the 180-gram vinyl commonly produced today. Consequently, a record manufactured in 1972 is likely to sound superior to one made in 1979. "When CDs first came out, there was no question that they sounded better than the vinyl records that were coming out at the time. That's because the manufacturing quality of vinyl in the late 70s and early 80s was unmitigated crap."
**Upgraded Audio CDs (SACD/DVD-Audio)** : Certain CDs offer enhanced audio quality, such as specialized versions included in box sets (e.g., DVD versions) or the now less common Super Audio CDs (SACDs). These discs are produced with higher sampling rates and bit rates, often 96 kilohertz 24-bit, and are noted for their superior sound quality. "you can buy CDs that have upgraded audio, a DVD version that comes in a box set for example, or maybe a now hard to come by super audio CD."
**Polyvinyl Chloride (PVC) Properties and Environmental Impact** : Polyvinyl chloride (PVC) is the third most popular plastic globally, with applications ranging from bottles and plastic cards to plumbing products, having been used in sewer pipes since the 1920s. Most PVC is produced in China and Southeast Asia. PVC production is a byproduct of fossil fuels. In its raw, brittle state, it requires stabilizing chemicals known as plasticizers, which historically included toxic substances like lead and cadmium. Carbon is added to make the plastic black. As plastic, vinyl is non-biodegradable and records in landfills can leach hazardous chemicals for centuries. Melting old records is costly and releases highly toxic hydrogen chloride gas, a significant environmental problem given the billions of records produced since 1948 (e.g., 160 million kilos of PVC for records in a typical 1970s year). "Polyvinyl chloride is plastic. It's the third most popular type of plastic ever."
**Eco-Friendly Vinyl Initiatives** : Efforts are underway to develop more environmentally sustainable vinyl records. Some European pressing plants are achieving nearly 99% carbon neutrality in their manufacturing processes. Other companies are exploring the use of recycled vinyl, although historically (e.g., during the 1970s oil crisis), recycled vinyl was often impure. Experiments also include biodegradable PVC substitutes and even edible vinyl alternatives. "people are working on creating more eco-friendly vinyl records. There are a couple of pressing plants in Europe that approach being 99% carbon neutral in their manufacturing process."
**Environmental Impact of Music Consumption (Physical vs. Streaming)** : Contrary to popular belief, physical music formats like vinyl records and CDs can be more environmentally friendly than streaming, depending on consumption patterns. Streaming and downloading music require energy for servers, routers, and playback devices. However, playing a downloaded song consumes significantly less energy than repeatedly streaming it. An estimated 80% reduction in carbon emissions from digital music consumption could be achieved if all streamed songs were downloaded instead. If an album is streamed 27 times, it becomes more ecologically sound to purchase a physical copy. "If you stream an album 27 times, it's actually better for the planet just to go ahead and buy a physical copy of it, be that a vinyl record or a CD."
**History of Picture Discs** : The earliest known picture discs emerged in the UK in the early 1920s, followed by Adolf Hitler's releases of his speeches for the Hitler Youth in the 1930s. The first modern picture disc is believed to be a 1969 compilation by the German label Metronome, featuring bands like The Doors and MC5. These became popular collector's items in the late 1970s and early 1980s, sometimes featuring interviews. "The first modern day picture disc was probably a compilation of bands like the Doors and the MC5 released by a German label called Metronome in 1969."
**Audio Quality of Novelty Vinyl** : The audio quality of novelty vinyl records varies significantly. Picture discs are generally considered to have the worst sound quality, followed by glow-in-the-dark records. Records incorporating glitter or spattered colors can also produce "dodgy" sound. For optimal audio fidelity on vinyl, it is typically recommended to purchase basic black records. "In my experience, picture discs sound the worst, followed by any record made with something that makes them glow in the dark."
**Novel Vinyl Pressing Materials** : Beyond standard and colored vinyl, records have been experimentally pressed using a variety of unusual and sometimes shocking materials. These include blood, hair, urine, marijuana leaves, razor blades, gunpowder, and even human cremated remains. One notable example is an album for Emperor Yes, released in 100 copies, each sprinkled with dust from a 16th-century asteroid. "There have been similar pressings that incorporate blood, hair, urine, marijuana leaves, razor blades, gunpowder, and even someone's cremated remains."
**Vinyl Record Sizes** : Vinyl records are produced in a range of diameters, including common sizes such as 5, 7, 10, and 12 inches, with some even reaching 16 inches. Nine Inch Nails, for instance, famously released a record with a 9-inch diameter. "Vile can come in all sizes, 5 inches, 7 inches, 10 inches, 12 inches, even 16 inches"
**Smallest Vinyl Record (Nano Made)** : Researchers from the Nano Made section at the Technical University of Denmark created the world's smallest "vinyl" record, measuring an astonishing 15 micrometers across. Although not made of traditional polyvinyl chloride, it functions like a full-size record, playing 25 seconds of Brenda Lee's "Rockin' Around the Christmas Tree" in stereo. "The hook here is that this record is just 15 micrometers across. You can't even see it."
**Polyvinyl Chloride (PVC) Shortage (2020 onwards)** : A global shortage of polyvinyl chloride (PVC) began in 2020, primarily due to supply chain disruptions caused by the pandemic. While increased online record sales and backed-up pressing plants contributed, a major factor in the shortage was the surging demand for plastic containers used by take-out restaurants. "Starting in 2020, the world suffered a shortage of polyvinyl chloride because of supply chain issues during the pandemic."
**Soviet X-Ray Records ("Ribs")** : During the period when Western records were prohibited in the Soviet Union, resourceful music enthusiasts created black market recordings known as "ribs" (reo bra in Russian). These were made by repurposing old medical X-rays retrieved from hospital dumpsters, onto which music was cut at 78 RPM using adapted gramophones. A cigarette was used to burn the center hole. Despite very poor sound quality, these cost around one ruble and provided illicit access to artists like The Beatles, The Rolling Stones, Elvis, and The Beach Boys. Possession of these records could lead to imprisonment in a gulag, and they are now sought-after collectibles. "These black market recordings were known as ribs, reo bra in Russian, because you could still see the bones of someone in the film."
**Flexi-Discs (Eva-Tone Soundsheet)** : Flexi-discs are thin, pliable sheets of vinyl designed to be included as inserts in books or magazines. They first appeared in 1962, initially marketed as the Eva-Tone Soundsheet. The Beatles notably embraced this format, sending out special Christmas flexi-discs to their fan club from 1963 to 1969. Throughout the 1970s, flexi-discs were commonly found in various publications, including Mad Magazine, National Geographic, and music magazines. The format continues to be produced, appearing on Record Store Day and favored by punk bands and artists like the Foo Fighters. "Flexi disks first appeared in 1962, and were known as the Eva-Tone Soundsheet."
**McDonald's Flexi-Disc Contest (1988)** : In 1988, McDonald's hosted a contest featuring a flexi-disc with school children singing "The Menu song." The grand prize of one million dollars was awarded if the flexi-disc contained the entire song. With odds of approximately one in 80 million, the winning flexi-disc was discovered by a 13-year-old boy in Virginia within a pile of newspapers destined for burning. "In 1988, McDonald's had a contest involving a flexi disk, where the grand prize was a million dollars."
**Correct Plural of "Vinyl"** : The correct plural form of the word "vinyl" is "vinyl," akin to "deer." Referring to multiple records as "vinyls" is considered incorrect and a tell-tale sign of an inexperienced collector or "newbie." "The plural of vinyl is like the plural of deer. You have a single deer named Bambi or whatever, and you also had a herd of deer, not deers. You also have a piece of vinyl, a single record, and also a collection of vinyl."
**Guidance on Buying Used Vinyl** : Buyers are advised to exercise caution when purchasing vinyl records pressed between the mid-1970s and early 1980s, as manufacturing quality during this period was notoriously poor, often involving recycled, impure PVC and lighter records (e.g., 90 grams). Exceptions include audiophile editions made from virgin vinyl or records produced before 1972. Similarly, records from the 1980s and 1990s should be scrutinized, as many were likely direct-from-CD pressings that were not properly mastered for vinyl and generally exhibit inferior sound. "Be very careful buying vinyl, but was pressed in the mid to late 70s and early 1980s. Manufacturing quality, and I can't overemphasize this enough, was terrible during that period."
**Advantages of Modern New Vinyl (Post-2008)** : Since approximately 2008, purchasing new vinyl records has become less risky due to improved manufacturing standards. Modern vinyl is typically made from "virgin" PVC (not recycled) and is produced to be notably heavy, with discs weighing 180, 200, or even 220 grams. This increased weight contributes to better stability on the turntable, reducing warping and allowing the stylus to track grooves more accurately, which can lead to improved sound quality depending on the source material and playback system. "Since about 2008, buying new vinyl has come with less of a risk. The vinyl is virgin, and the records tend to be nice and heavy, with each disc weighing 180, 200 or even 220 grams each."
**Rise of Turntable and Audio Gear Purchases** : The vinyl resurgence, which began around 2008, has stimulated a significant increase in the sales of turntables and various audio equipment. This trend is driven by music fans across generations, including millennials and Gen Z, who are seeking a more immersive and higher-quality music listening experience, mirroring the considerable investments in stereo equipment seen in the 1970s and 1980s. "This is led to an upswing in the purchase of turntables in all manner of audio gear, as people crave a deeper and better sounding music experience."
