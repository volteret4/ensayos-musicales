**Seven Nation Army** : The riff for this song, featuring many different colors of distortion, was created by running an early 1960s K Hollow Body guitar, equipped with a single pickup, into a Digitech Whammy pedal, which shifted the notes Jack White played down an entire octave. ## songs
**Rocket 88 (1951)** : Recorded around March 1, 1951, this song features accidental guitar distortion after Ike Turner's band's guitar amp, belonging to Willie Kitsert, was damaged during a drive to Memphis, Tennessee. One account suggests it fell off the car, another that it was dropped from the trunk. The speaker cone burst, and newspaper was wadded and jammed into the enclosure as a makeshift repair. "And the sound that came out of this amp was pretty cool."

**Maestro Fuzz Tone (1962)** : Gibson introduced the Maestro Fuzz Tone in 1962, marking the release of the first-ever commercial fuzz pedal. This foot pedal, developed with engineer Glen Snottie and TV engineer Revis Hobbs following an inquiry about replicating Grady Martin's fuzzy sound, allowed guitarists to deliberately distort their electric guitar sound. Initially, 5,000 units were shipped but sold poorly in 1964; however, sales skyrocketed after Keith Richards used one on The Rolling Stones' "(I Can't Get No) Satisfaction" in 1965. "And in 1962, they started selling the first ever Fuzz pedal, a gizmo that could carefully and deliberately distort the sound of an electric guitar."

The resulting unit, the Big Muff, was the first to use the word "distortion" in addition to "fuzz" in its description, and it was a massive hit, influencing the sound of the 1970s and 1990s alt-rock. "Electroharmonics actually used the word distortion in addition to fuzz to describe what they were doing. They were the first."
**Big Muff Variations and Impact** : Electroharmonics produced numerous versions of the Big Muff pedal, which experienced fluctuating popularity over musical trends. It became an almost indispensable tool for 90s alt-rock bands, with artists like Korn, Bush, Dinosaur Jr., and Sonic Youth being fans. Mudhoney even named an EP "Superfuzz Big Muff" in its honor, and a specific model was crucial for The Smashing Pumpkins' sound. A special orange version was sold, famously described as "all the guitars on Simee's Dream in a box." "Electroharmonics sold many different versions of the Big Muff, which has gone in and out of style with trends and music."

# instrument - Amplifier

## curiosities
**Early Fragility and Distortion** : Early amplifiers were generally fragile and not very good, easily malfunctioning to produce sounds that were not clean or pure. This inherent flaw inadvertently led to the discovery of desirable distorted sounds. "Early amplifiers were fragile things, and frankly not very good."
**Overdrive Mechanism** : Distortion, also known as overdrive, occurs when an electrical signal is pushed beyond the capacity of an amplifier's circuitry. This excessive power causes the signal to "clip," cutting off its peaks and troughs and adding new characteristics like overtones, sustain, and compression. Extreme overdrive results in distortion. "Distortion is also known as overdrive. Basically it means you've pushed the electrical signal past what the circuitry and the amplifier can handle."
**Pre-amplifier Sections** : To allow for powerful distortion at lower, more manageable volumes, pre-amplifier sections were added to standard amplifiers. Turning up the pre-amp creates distortion, while a master volume knob controls the overall output volume. This innovation enabled guitarists to achieve "big amp" sounds even in small spaces or at low volumes. "This was solved by adding a pre-amplifier section to a standard amplifier. Turn that up and you get distortion."

# instrument - Maestro Fuzz Tone

## curiosities
**First Commercial Fuzz Pedal (1962)** : The Maestro Fuzz Tone, also known as the FZ1, was the first fuzz pedal ever sold, introduced by Gibson in 1962. It was developed by engineer Glen Snottie and TV engineer Revis Hobbs to replicate the accidental fuzzy sound achieved by Grady Martin. Marketed as a versatile device capable of creating a "completely different sound effect," it initially sold poorly but became a sensation after Keith Richards used it on The Rolling Stones' "(I Can't Get No) Satisfaction" in 1965. "And in 1962, they started selling the first ever Fuzz pedal, a gizmo that could carefully and deliberately distort the sound of an electric guitar."
**Versatile Sound Effects** : The Maestro Fuzz Tone was marketed with a demo record highlighting its ability to create a wide range of unique guitar sounds beyond simple fuzz. These included "organ-like tones, mellow woodwinds and whispering reeds, booming brass and bell-clear horns," and many more. It was also promoted for its expanded range of effects when combined with reverberation, tremolo, and vibrato, even demonstrating its use with an electric bass for a "Sousa-Phone effect." "Organ-like tones, mellow woodwinds and whispering reeds, booming brass and bell-clear horns, plus many, many more."

# instrument - Big Muff

## curiosities
**Sustain and Distortion Innovation (1969)** : The Big Muff was introduced in 1969 by Electroharmonics, founded by Mike Matthews, who sought to advance beyond existing fuzz units like his own Muff Fuzz. Developed by Bell Labs engineer Bob Meyer, it was designed to not only provide on-demand fuzz but also to make a guitar sound "ring for a long time," introducing the concept of significant sustain. It was also notably the first unit to explicitly use the term "distortion" alongside "fuzz" in its description. "He hired an engineer at Bell Labs, named Bob Meyer, to come up with a foot pedal that could provide on-demand fuzz, but also one that could make a guitar sound ring for a long time. And the word here is sustain."
**Broad Influence Across Genres** : The Big Muff pedal became immensely popular and influential among guitarists across various genres and eras, contributing to the "Electrohar sound" of the 1970s. Notable users included Jimi Hendrix, David Gilmour of Pink Floyd, Carlos Santana, Ace Frehley and Paul Stanley of Kiss, Thin Lizzy, and Frank Zappa. Its versatility allowed it to work effectively with both chords and individual notes, making them stand out. Its impact continued into the 90s alt-rock scene, with bands like Korn, Bush, and The Smashing Pumpkins heavily relying on it. "The Big Muff was a huge hit with guitars everywhere."
**Beyond Guitar Use** : Fuzz boxes and distortion pedals, particularly the Big Muff, proved versatile enough to be used on instruments other than guitars. Any electrical signal could be routed through the pedal to achieve a growling sound. Examples include John Lorde of Deep Purple running a Hammond organ through a pedal on "Highway Star" (1972) and Martin Gore and Vince Clark of Depeche Mode experimenting with their Korg and Roland synthesizers through a Big Muff on their first album in 1980. "Fuzz boxes and distortion pedals, especially those like the Big Muff, could be used for things other than a guitar."

# instrument - Drop D Tuning

## curiosities
**Heavy Sound Creation** : Drop D tuning is a guitar tuning where the lowest string is tuned down a whole step from E to D. This modification, without delving deeply into music theory, alters how chords are formed and sound, resulting in a grittier, heavier, and more powerful guitar tone. When combined with a foot pedal and an overdriven amplifier, it produces a distinctive and impactful sound. "This is where you tune the lowest string on a guitar to a D instead of an E. That's a whole step."
**Genre-Defining Impact** : Drop D tuning became a signature element for the grunge genre, heavily utilized by bands like Nirvana, Soundgarden, Pearl Jam, and Alice in Chains. Its influence extended beyond grunge, appearing in songs by a wide range of artists including the Foo Fighters, Muse, Tool, Green Day, and Rage Against the Machine, establishing it as a popular technique for achieving a heavier sound in modern rock. "Drop D not only became synonymous with grunge, Nirvana, Sound Garden, Pearl Jam, Allison Chains, but it can be heard in a lot of other songs by the Foo Fighters, Muse, Tool, Green Day, Rage Against the Machine, and dozens of others."

**Feedback Definition** : Feedback occurs when a signal is routed back into its own original input within a circuit. In its uncontrolled form, this manifests as sharp, high-pitched squeals and shrieks from PA systems and amplifiers. However, feedback can also be intentionally harnessed and controlled to produce a distinct type of distortion, creating a musical landscape. "Feedback.
