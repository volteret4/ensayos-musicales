## curiosities
**Downsview Park Stage Collapse (June 16, 2012)** : On Saturday, June 16, 2012, a stage under construction for a Radiohead concert at Downsview Park in Toronto collapsed around 4:00 PM, an hour before the gates were scheduled to open and just before the band's soundcheck. The stage roof fell, crushing Phil Selba's drum tech, Scott Johnson, with a 5,000-pound video monitor, killing him. Three other people were injured, one seriously, and much of the band's lighting and stage gear was destroyed. "On Saturday, June 16, 2012, a stage was almost finished being built at Downsview Park in Toronto ahead of that night's gig, headlined by Radiohead."
**Inquest into Stage Collapse Findings** : A lengthy and complex inquest was held following the Downsview Park tragedy, involving the Ontario Ministry of Labor, promoter Live Nation, and stage builder Optex. The initial case ended in a mistrial, requiring a restart. The inquest ultimately determined that the stage roof's design was outdated and unable to support 76,000 pounds of equipment, leading to 28 recommendations from the jury, and declaring Scott Johnson's death accidental. "In the aftermath, a long and confusing inquest was held involving the Ontario Ministry of Labor, Live Nation, the promoter, and Optex, the company commissioned to build the stage."
**Engineer's Errors, No Jail Time** : The engineer central to the Downsview Park stage collapse acknowledged that errors were made in the stage design, but despite this admission and the subsequent recommendations, no one involved in the incident was sent to jail. "The engineer at the center of everything acknowledged that errors were made, but nobody ever went to jail."
**Return to Toronto and Memorial (July 19, 2018)** : Radiohead did not perform in Toronto again until July 19, 2018, six years after the fatal stage collapse. During this concert, with the legal case still stalled, Thom Yorke publicly addressed the situation from the stage, and the band observed a moment of silence for Scott Johnson before continuing their performance. "Radiohead didn't play Toronto again until July 19, 2018."

## curiosities
**Cincinnati Concert Stampede (December 3, 1979)** : On December 3, 1979, a stampede occurred outside a sold-out Who concert at Riverfront Coliseum in Cincinnati, which had sold 18,348 tickets. A large crowd, many with general admission tickets, pushed forward after hearing music, believing The Who had started early. Only two doors were initially open, and the intense crush over 40 minutes led to 11 deaths by asphyxiation and 26 injuries, mostly among teenagers and young adults. "whenever you go through the stories of rock disasters, you always end up what happened at a who concert in Cincinnati on December 3, 1979."
**Multiple Factors in Cincinnati Tragedy** : Observers attributed the Cincinnati stampede to several factors: the prevalence of 80% general admission tickets, which incentivized a rush for prime viewing spots; the prolonged waiting times for fans, some of whom had consumed alcohol or drugs; and the breaking open of only a single pair of doors, creating a bottleneck that led to the crush. "Observers blamed the tragedy on several factors."
**Band's Reaction to Cincinnati Disaster** : The members of The Who were deeply shaken by the Cincinnati tragedy, with one band member stating they would never play again if the incident had occurred inside the venue. While acknowledging the "football game boxing match feeling" that fans enjoy at rock concerts, they later appealed to authorities for assistance in making future rock concerts safer. "The concert went on as planned, but the members of the who were badly shaken."
**Buffalo Concert Safety Changes** : Following the Cincinnati tragedy, The Who's subsequent concert in Buffalo, New York, implemented reserved seating for all attendees, a stark contrast to the general admission policy that had contributed to the stampede in Cincinnati, in an effort to enhance crowd safety. "And later this week, their concert in Buffalo, New York was, unlike Cincinnati, all buffalo seats were reserved."

**Bataclan Terrorist Attack (November 13, 2015)** : On November 13, 2015, a sold-out Eagles of Death Metal concert at the Bataclan in Paris, attended by about 1500 people, became the target of a terrorist attack. Three gunmen, dressed in black and wearing suicide vests, entered the venue at 9:49 PM, after shooting people outside, and opened fire with AK-47s into the general admission area during the guitar solo of "I Love the Devil," plunging the hall into chaos with muzzle flashes as the only light. "One of the most shocking terrorist attacks to ever hit Europe happened at a concert on November 13, 2015 at a Paris venue called the Bataclan."
**Attack Progression and Hostage Taking** : The shooting inside the Bataclan lasted at least 10 minutes, followed by the gunmen reloading and resuming random firing, even shooting four survivors they found playing dead. Two gunmen ascended to the balconies, taking approximately 20 hostages, forcing some to act as lookouts, while the third remained downstairs. Police arrived at 10:15 PM and locked down the building. Negotiations over a cell phone for almost an hour focused on France's involvement in Syria. "The shooting lasted for at least 10 minutes before the gunman reloaded and then they started to shoot again at random."
**Escape and Hiding Tactics During Attack** : During brief lulls in gunfire at the Bataclan, people desperately sought escape or refuge. Those in the balconies found safety in toilets or on the roof by forcing open a trap door, while others broke false ceilings to hide in metal rafters. Some barricaded themselves in dressing rooms with the band using a fridge and furniture, and others managed to escape through emergency exits behind the stage curtain, scrambling over deceased bodies. "Meanwhile, during that brief break in the gunfire when everybody was reloading, people tried to escape."
**End of Siege and Casualties** : At 12:18 AM, the order was given to storm the gunmen, an operation that lasted three minutes. One gunman detonated a suicide belt, and the second was shot before he could detonate his. In total, 89 people died, including Eagles of Death Metal's tour manager. The trial for 20 men charged with plotting the various attacks, including at the Bataclan, concluded in 2022. "In the end, 89 people died, including the Eagles of Death Metal tour manager."
**Jesse Hughes' Testimony and Resilience** : Eagles of Death Metal singer Jesse Hughes, alongside other band members and 300 survivors, returned to Paris to testify in the trial following the Bataclan attack. He defiantly stated, "Evil did not win. You can't kill rock and roll," and three months after the shootings, the band returned to Paris to complete the interrupted show. "'Evil did not win,' he said. "'You can't kill rock and roll.'"
