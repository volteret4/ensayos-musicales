## albums
**The Man Who Sold the World (1970)** : This album was released in November 1970. Its original artwork was highly scandalous for 1970s Britain, featuring Bowie in a dress with hormone hair and what appeared to be a purse for his makeup. This image sparked outrage, as homosexuality had been a crime in the UK until 1967, leading hundreds of record stores to refuse to stock the album if that cover was used. As a result, the original artwork was suppressed for many years. "Still the album came up. November 1970, this is David Bowie, with the title track of The Man Who Sold the World."

## songs
**Space Oddity (1969)** : This dark, pseudo-folk song was written by Bowie in the context of a painful breakup with Hermione Farthingdale, feelings of depression, and a fascination with the American space program, particularly the Apollo moon landing and the film *2001, A Space Odyssey*. The very first version was recorded on February 2nd, 1969, the day after his breakup. Several more versions followed, with the definitive one recorded on June 20th and released on July 11th, just in time for the Apollo 11 mission. Broadcasters, including the BBC, adopted it as an unofficial theme for the July 20th moon landing. The song became Bowie's breakthrough hit and turned him into a star, though he feared becoming a one-hit wonder. Its lyrics about Major Tom sparked speculation about the character's identity. Bowie recorded the definitive version after a chance meeting with Elton John led him to a producer. "The definitive version was recorded June 20th, and then released to the public on July 11th, just in time for the Apollo 11 mission."

## curiosities
**Early Career and Personal Struggles** : Before his breakthrough, Bowie was a lost, wandering artist, taking mime lessons, and had recorded a series of albums that largely went nowhere. He was reduced to working for a company that made copies of legal documents for law firms. During this period, he endured a painful breakup with Hermione Farthingdale, which led to depression, feelings of loss, and a short dalliance with heroin. "Bowie had just endured a painful breakup with a woman named Hermione Farthingdale."
**Fascination with Space Program** : In the late 1960s, Bowie became fascinated by the American space program, particularly influenced by the 1968 film *2001, A Space Odyssey*. He was transfixed by the Apollo program's push towards a moon landing, noting how the Apollo astronauts were worldwide superstars in 1969. This fascination, combined with personal struggles, inspired his song "Space Oddity." "When the Apollo program pushed towards a moon landing, Bowie was absolutely transfixed."
**Manager Tony DeFries and Image Transformation** : Bowie's new manager, Tony DeFries, believed that for Bowie to succeed, he needed a provocative image and a weird brand marketed alongside his music. DeFries encouraged Bowie to experiment with makeup and costumes, a plan Bowie embraced. He acquired long, flowing, and expensive gowns from a designer named Mr. Fish, continuing a tendency to dress to shock that dated back to his mod days in high school. "DeFries encouraged Bowie to experiment with makeup and costumes."
**Public Declaration of Sexuality** : In January 1972, Bowie publicly professed to be gay in an interview in *Melody Maker* magazine. This statement was far more scandalous in 1970s Britain than his earlier cross-dressing, as homosexuality had only been decriminalized in the UK in 1967. This declaration made his previous dress-wearing seem "minuscule." "The big media storm came when Bowie publicly professed to be gay in an interview in Melody Maker magazine in January of 1972."
**Ziggy Stardust Persona** : Introduced in 1972, Ziggy Stardust became Bowie's most famous character, transforming the course of rock music. Ziggy was a doomed, drug-addled, glam spaceman, drawing inspiration from figures like Andy Warhol, Lou Reed, Bob Dylan, Frank Sinatra, and an obscure American singer named Vince Taylor. The character was described as part *Clockwork Orange* and part autobiography. Ziggy, performing with his band The Spiders From Mars, existed for only two years but became an enduring rock and roll icon, subject to books, PhD dissertations, and debates by scholars. Bowie's unique use of image and theater made Ziggy one of the most profound inventions in rock history, inspiring British kids bored with mainstream rock to create punk rock. "Bowie called his creation Ziggy Star Dust."
**Patronage of Iggy Pop and The Stooges** : Bowie was a great admirer and musical hero of Iggy Pop, fascinated by his craziness, almost constant mental instability, and ability to see beyond the drugs and self-destructive stage antics to Iggy's intelligence. Bowie convinced his manager to take on Iggy and The Stooges as a project, providing a cash injection that funded their *Raw Power* album. He later visited Iggy when he was in a mental hospital. Bowie's acceptance of Iggy also helped propel the first three Stooges albums to become important templates for punk rock. "Iggy's craziness had made him into one of Bowie's musical heroes."
**Patronage of Lou Reed** : Bowie was also a great admirer of Lou Reed and The Velvet Underground, recognizing Reed's talent despite him being on the fringes and largely unknown. Bowie offered his patronage, leading him to produce Reed's 1972 album *Transformer*, collaborating with his Spiders From Mars guitarist, Mick Ronson, to help Reed find a new sound. "Bowie did, though, and he offered to lend his patronage to Lou."

## curiosities
**Formation and Naming** : The band formed in the fall of 1971, comprising four former teenage gang members from Brooklyn who found playing in a band more appealing than petty burglaries. They took their name from the New York Doll Hospital, a real store on Jamaica Avenue in Queens specializing in doll repairs. "So taking their name from the New York Doll Hospital, which was a real store on Jamaica Avenue in Queens that specialized in fixing rare dolls, the New York Dolls began scary people in Manhattan in the spring of 1972."
**Provocative Image and Early Gigs** : The New York Dolls started performing in Manhattan in the spring of 1972, with their first gig at a sleazy hotel in Times Square. They created a shocking image by wearing makeup, spandex, and fishnets, and professing to be gay or bisexual. This look pushed the emerging glam rock aesthetic from London "miles further," distinguishing them significantly. "They showed up wearing makeup, and dressed in spandex, and fishnets, and professing to be gay."
**Musical Philosophy** : The band's music was characterized by short and raunchy songs, deliberately avoiding solos and complex arrangements. They prioritized attitude and energy over musical virtuosity, believing these qualities were more important than technical accomplishment. This approach was a reaction against the increasing sophistication of mainstream rock. "And as for the music, all the songs were short and raunchy. No solos, no complex arrangements."
**Cult Following and Influences** : Despite being dismissed by many for their unconventional style, the New York Dolls developed a significant following among those who appreciated "weird things." They were seen by some as embodying the true spirit of rock and roll, where attitude and energy surpassed virtuosity. The band regularly played alongside other influential artists like Iggy Pop and KISS. "Soon, the dolls were playing regular gigs alongside Iggy Pop, and a band who took the makeup thing even further."
**London Show and Drummer's Death** : In November 1972, Rod Stewart invited the New York Dolls to London to open a show for him. During this trip, drummer Billy Mercier became intoxicated, and after being taken back to an apartment by two groupies, he was dunked in cold water in a bathtub and had coffee poured down his throat, leading to his suffocation and drowning. "But then drummer Billy Mercier got drunk. Two groupies hauled them back to their apartment, dunked them in cold water in the bathtub, and poured coffee down his throat. He suffocated and he drowned."
**Malcolm McLaren's Management** : After being dropped from their record deal, the band hired Malcolm McLaren, the inexperienced but ambitious owner of a small, unusual clothing shop in London, as their new manager. McLaren, who discovered the Dolls during a New York clothing show, envisioned a complete image makeover, replacing their spandex, makeup, and big hair with red leather. He also proposed using a big Soviet flag as a stage backdrop for live performances, a move deemed not particularly smart at the time and less engaging than their previous sexual weirdness. "He thought the dolls needed a complete image makeover. That would fix everything."
**Demise of the Band** : After a couple of tours under Malcolm McLaren's management, the band began to fall apart. By March 1975, while on the road in Florida, the Dolls were nearing their end, as evidenced by surviving live recordings from that period. Shortly after this, the band was "done." "Short time later, they were done."

## songs
**Walk on the Wild Side (1972)** : This song, released in November 1972, became a genuine Top 40 hit and an AM radio success on both sides of the Atlantic, despite its "raunchy references." The lyrics recounted true stories of individuals in Andy Warhol's circle, including Holly Woodlawn (a transsexual from Miami), Candy Darling (another transsexual), and Joe Campbell, referred to as the "Sugar Plum Fairy." The first version of the song was recorded with drumsticks, but it was later re-recorded using brushes to achieve a "smoky, sleazy nightclub feel." The saxophone part at the end was played by Ronnie Ross, who had taught David Bowie saxophone in the 1950s. The iconic sliding bass line was created by session player Herbie Flowers, who layered two bass lines—one from a stand-up double bass and another from a fretless electric. Flowers was reportedly paid $30 for his contribution, and the bass line has since been sampled countless times in hip-hop and dance songs worldwide. "Released in November 1972 and an AM radio hits on both sides of the Atlantic, despite all the raunchy references that's Lou Reed and walk on the wild side."

## curiosities

"In 1972, Lou ended his exile at his parents' house on Long Island, where he had been quietly running poetry for more than two years after stomping out on the velvet."

# genre - Rock and Roll

## curiosities
**Evolution in the Early 1970s** : As rock and roll neared its sixteenth year in the early 1970s, it had significantly matured, becoming more sophisticated in both sound and execution. The original rockers from the 1950s and 1960s had developed into more accomplished musicians, capable of more complex compositions beyond simple three-chord songs. This sophistication was facilitated by advancements in recording technology and musical equipment. "As Rock and Roll approached its sweet 16 birthday in the early 1970s, it was obvious that it had grown up quite a bit."
**Technological Advancements and Complexity** : In the early 1970s, rock music became more complex due to available tools, as four-track recording studios became antiquated. The industry shifted to studios with 16 and 24-track consoles, larger tape recorders, and racks of machines for advanced effects. Guitar amplifiers became bigger and more powerful, allowing for fatter chords, longer sustains, and more expressive feedback, while a vast array of foot pedals enabled guitarists to create unique signature sounds. "Rock was also becoming more complicated because, well, because it could, it had the tools."
**Backlash Against Sophistication** : For some, the increasing sophistication of rock in the early 1970s, with its more accomplished musicianship and slick recording techniques, was seen as detrimental. These individuals felt that the pursuit of "technical perfection" had led to a loss of something fundamental, and it was "time to get primal again." "There were those who felt that the road to technical perfection was not a good one to be on. Something had been lost."
