## albums
**The Big Come Up (2002)** : The Black Keys' debut album, recorded in Patrick Carney's parents' unfinished basement in Akron, Ohio, using an 8-track digi recorder between January and March 2002. Released on May 14, 2002, on CD and in ten different colors of vinyl (limited to under 1,000 copies each, now collector's items). It was their only release on the LA-based label Alive. "It was called The Big Come Up and was recorded in Patrick's parents' unfinished basement in Akron using an 8-track digi recorder between January and March of 2002."
**Thickfreakness (2003)** : The band's second album, funded by Dan Auerbach and Patrick Carney by mowing lawns. It was recorded in a single 14-hour sprint in Patrick Carney's basement after signing with indie label Fat Possum. Critically acclaimed, its Japanese release was retitled *Inezuma Rock and Blues* (Inezuma meaning lightning) because the original title didn't translate well. "This one was called Thick Freakness, and like the first record was loved by critics."
**Rubber Factory (2004)** : The third album, recorded over five months in the second floor of a former General Tire factory in East Akron, Ohio, rented for $500 a month. The band used a recording console bought on eBay (once owned by a Loverboy sound engineer, but deemed "crap") and recycled, baked recording tape provided by their label. Despite glitches with the space and gear, it received good reviews, secured a slot at Lollapalooza, a performance on David Letterman, and was their first album to break into the Top 200 album chart, peaking at number 143. "In the end, they called it, what else, rubber factory."
**Magic Potion (2006)** : The fourth studio album, released in 2006 on the Nonesuch label. For this record, the band returned to Patrick Carney's basement, using about $5,000 worth of inexpensive gear, preferring the "vibe and weird sounds" of that space. The band was reportedly unhappy with the album's mastering, which resulted in a lack of low end. Despite their reservations, it sold better than their previous three albums, reaching the top 100 on the American album charts and number 12 in Australia, marking encouraging progress. "On the plus side, this record sold better than the other three, reaching into the top 100 on the American album charts, and it's highest number 12 in Australia."
**Attack & Release (2008)** : The fifth studio album, marking the band's transition to a professional studio (Summa in Painsville, Ohio) and their first collaboration with an outside producer, Danger Mouse. Danger Mouse was initially unavailable due to a commitment to Ike Turner but became available when that project fell through; he played keyboards and bass on the album, with other musicians contributing guitars, vocals, and clarinets. Patrick Carney adopted a John Bonham-inspired drum setup for this album in the summer of 2007, resulting in a bigger, deeper, tighter drum sound. "From Attack and Release, that's the album, this is called I Got Mine."
**Brothers (2010)** : The band's sixth album, released on May 18, 2010, after seven years and five albums of steady growth. The cover features a longer, nonsensical title as a tribute to Howlin' Wolf's 1969 album. The production involved Danger Mouse for one song and much of it was recorded at the historic Muscle Shoals Sound Studio in Alabama, known for its amazing acoustics and natural echo chambers, despite challenges with outdated in-house gear and electrical issues frying modern equipment. The recording rooms emphasized the bass on the record. The album was nominated for five Grammy Awards, winning Best Alternative Album and Best Artwork; the physical CD was designed to change color (black to white in US, white to black in Australia, silver to black in Europe) when heated by a CD player's laser. "The Brothers record was big too, with five Grammy nominations overall."
**El Camino (2011)** : The seventh studio album, released just 19 months after *Brothers*, following a period of intense touring. Recorded in Nashville in spring 2011 at Dan Auerbach's Easy Eye Sound studio, with Danger Mouse producing the entire record and co-writing all 11 tracks. The album's songs were composed around short hooks inspired by favorite bands like The Beatles, The Clash, The Cramps, Elvis, and The Ramones, simplifying the creative process as they had no finished music upon entering the studio. The album cover features an old Chrysler van, similar to their "Grey Ghost" tour vehicle. It achieved huge success, with platinum sales globally and triple platinum in Canada, where it was a best-performing territory. It won Best Rock Album at the Grammys, and two other awards went to its single "Lonely Boy," while Dan Auerbach won Producer of the Year. "Huge success. Platinum sales around the planet. Triple platinum in Canada by the way, where, with about half the population of the UK, we bought almost as many copies."
**Turn Blue (2014)** : The band's eighth studio album, released on May 12, 2014. Recorded in fits and starts over more than a year and a half, it is described as Dan Auerbach's divorce album, with the lyrics reflecting his personal struggles. The album's existence was announced via a tweet from Mike Tyson. Its title and cover art are an homage to Gullardy, a Cleveland horror movie show host from the 1960s, drawing on his interest in mind control, brainwashing, and hypnotism. Five singles were released from the album. "Turn blue, the eighth album, was recorded in fits and starts over more than a year and a half before being released on May 12, 2014."
**Blackroc (2009)** : A collaboration record with Damon Dash, featuring numerous R&B and hip-hop guest artists. "In 2009, they recorded a record called BlackRock, which was a collaboration with a guy named Damon Dash."
**The Moan (2004)** : An EP release by The Black Keys. "one of which is called The Mone from 2004".
**Chulahoma (2006)** : A tribute record dedicated to blues singer Junior Kimbrough, an artist who significantly influenced Dan Auerbach in high school, inspiring him to pursue guitar full-time. "and a tribute record called Chula Homa, which is dedicated to a blues singer named Junior Kimbrough."
**Live in Austin (2003)** : An official bootleg album released with the band's approval, recorded on October 24, 2003, at Emo's in Austin, Texas, during the tour for the *Thickfreakness* album. Its initial release format was as a podcast. "An official boot leg from the block keys that's hard-ro, and early single, captured in Austin, Texas on October 24, 2003, back in a moment."

## curiosities
**Akron, Ohio Roots** : Both Dan Auerbach and Patrick Carney hail from Akron, Ohio, having met around ages 8 or 9. They became friends in high school, despite contrasting personalities (Dan as a "jock" and Patrick as a "nerd"), and both attended the University of Akron. Their hometown's historical significance as a tire manufacturing hub and their attendance at Firestone High School are notable local connections. "Dan and Patrick are both from Akron, Ohio."
**Accidental Band Formation** : The Black Keys formed when Dan Auerbach, seeking to create a demo as a singer-songwriter, asked Patrick Carney, who owned a 4-track recorder, for help. They invited musician friends to round out the sound, but only Dan and Patrick showed up on the scheduled recording day, leading them to form the band as a duo out of necessity. "On the appointed day, no one except Dan and Patrick showed up. So that left the two of them to form a band on their own."
**Early Demo as "Holy Grail"** : An early demo recorded by Dan Auerbach and Patrick Carney, which they submitted to various indie labels, is considered a "holy grail" by their hardcore fans. While it can be found online, the band members themselves view it as "very, very substandard" and prefer it not to be widely heard, making decent copies difficult to locate. "That demo, the one they sent out to all these indie labels, is like the holy grail to hardcore block keys fans."
**Rejection of Mayonnaise Commercial Deal** : Early in their career, The Black Keys famously turned down a 200,000-pound offer from a UK mayonnaise brand to license one of their songs for a TV commercial. Despite their financial struggles, touring in a 1994 Chrysler van called the "Grey Ghost," they believed accepting the offer would compromise their artistic integrity and "anti-sell-out" image, a decision that angered their parents. "they turned it down because they figured that would damage the credit."
**Evolution of Licensing Stance** : The band later revised their stance on song licensing, allowing their music to be used in various commercial contexts, including movies, TV shows (Sony), video games, and advertisements for products like jeans, Nissan, American Express, and Victoria's Secret. This shift reflected a growing priority for financial stability and sustaining their career on the road over strictly adhering to an "anti-sell-out" position. "These opportunities came along when Pagan the Ranton's Day on the Road was more important than preserving some kind of anti-sale-out stance."
**Comparison to The White Stripes** : The Black Keys' career benefited from the concurrent rise of The White Stripes, another prominent "garaji, punky, two-piece band." As The White Stripes gained mainstream attention, public interest in The Black Keys also grew, indicating a shared musical space and audience appreciation for their raw sound. "The bigger the white stripes got, the more interesting to grow in the black keys."
**Recording Studio in Tire Factory** : For their third album, *Rubber Factory*, The Black Keys repurposed the second floor of a former General Tire factory in East Akron, Ohio, renting the space for $500 a month. This unique studio choice highlighted their connection to their hometown's industrial heritage, as Akron was a major tire manufacturing hub, and they even attended Firestone High School. "The third album was recorded in a different spot, and the band-in-tire factory in East Akron that was once run by General Tyre".
**Band Internal Crisis (Brothers Era)** : During the period leading up to and during the *Brothers* album, Dan Auerbach and Patrick Carney faced severe interpersonal conflicts, with Patrick explicitly stating they were not "friends" outside of band work. Tensions escalated when Dan recorded a solo album without Patrick's knowledge, leading to months of silence, and further complicated by drama involving Patrick's wife, Denise, whom Dan disliked. Denise's alleged infidelity, "mind games," and financial misconduct contributed to Patrick's depression and increased drinking, bringing the band close to collapse until Patrick ended his marriage while on tour in Europe, which helped restore the band's stability. "The thing that probably kept the block keys together at this point was some stuff that initially kept them apart."
**Patrick Carney's Divorce Album (Brothers)** : The *Brothers* album is retrospectively linked to Patrick Carney's tumultuous marriage and subsequent divorce, reflecting his personal struggles during its creation. "I talked to both of them around this time, and I came away with a distinct impression that these guys were a bit sick of each other."
**Dan Auerbach's Divorce Album (Turn Blue)** : The *Turn Blue* album is characterized as Dan Auerbach's divorce album, with its lyrics providing insights into his "weird divorce" experiences. A notable anecdote involved a public misunderstanding that he was battling over a lock of Bob Dylan's hair, when in fact, the dispute centered on a poster featuring Bob Dylan with "really big hair," which they informally referred to as "Bob Dylan's hair." "Turn blue, the eighth album, was recorded in fits and starts over more than a year and a half before being released on May 12, 2014."
**Album Announcement by Mike Tyson** : The Black Keys' *Turn Blue* album was uniquely announced by boxer Mike Tyson via a tweet, creating an unusual media buzz around its release. "Its existence was announced by Mike Tyson, yes, him in a tweet."
**Gullardy Homage (Turn Blue)** : The title and cover art of *Turn Blue* serve as an homage to Gullardy, a Cleveland host of a 1960s late-night horror movie show. The album's imagery reflects Gullardy's thematic interests in mind control, brainwashing, and hypnotism found in movies. "The title was co-opted from a catchphrase by a dude named Gullardy, who hosted a late night horror movie show in Cleveland back in the 1960s."
**Extensive Discography** : The Black Keys' extensive catalog, often underestimated by casual listeners, includes 8 full studio albums, 2 EPs, 1 live album, and nearly two dozen singles, alongside various collaborations and tribute recordings. "No, they have 8 full albums, 2 EPs, 1 live album and close to 2 dozen singles."
**Grammy Awards - The Black Keys Accolades** : The Black Keys received five Grammy nominations for *Brothers*, winning Best Alternative Album and Best Artwork. For *El Camino*, they won Best Rock Album and two awards for "Lonely Boy," with Dan Auerbach also earning Producer of the Year. "The Brothers record was big too, with five Grammy nominations overall. It won for Best Alternative Album, and it picked up an award for Best Artwork."
**Record Album CD Color Change** : The physical CD for The Black Keys' *Brothers* album was designed with a unique feature: it would change color (black to white in the US, white to black in Australia, silver to black in Europe) when heated by a CD player's laser during playback. "See, out of the package, the CD is black. But as it heats up with the laser in your CD player as it plays, it turns white."
**Japanese Album Title Change** : The Black Keys' album *Thickfreakness* was retitled *Inezuma Rock and Blues* for its Japanese release because the original title did not translate well, with "Inezuma" meaning lightning in Japanese. "So that's why they decided to call it Inezuma Rock and Blues. Inezuma means lightning in Japanese."
**Indie Labels - Early Submissions** : The Black Keys sent their early demo to numerous independent record labels, most of which disregarded their submission, with Alive being the notable exception. "They recorded a demo that they sent to a bunch of different indie labels, all of which ignored them."

**Patrick Carney's Record Label** : Audio Eagle Records is an independent record label owned by Patrick Carney, which notably signed the band Drummer, in which Carney also played bass. "Patrick Carney was watching an indie band called Drummer, who was signed to his audio-eagle records."

# concert - Lollapalooza

## curiosities
**The Black Keys Performance Slot** : The Black Keys earned a performance slot at the renowned Lollapalooza festival following the positive reception of their third album, *Rubber Factory*. "a slotted Lollapalooza".

# concert - Emo's (Austin)

## curiosities
**Black Keys Live Recording Venue** : Emo's in Austin, Texas, was the venue where The Black Keys performed on October 24, 2003, a show that was later released as the official bootleg *Live in Austin*, recorded during their *Thickfreakness* tour. "It was recorded like a boot leg, but was released with the approval of the block keys. It's simply called Live in Austin, and dates all the way back to a show they played in October 24, 2003, at a place called Emo's, when the band was touring the Thick Freakness album."

# instrument - Guitar

## curiosities
**Dan Auerbach's Full-time Pursuit** : Dan Auerbach's decision to pursue playing the guitar full-time was significantly influenced by listening to blues singer Junior Kimbrough in high school. "In fact, listening to Kimbrough, while smoking a lot of dope, helped him decide to pursue the guitar full time."

# instrument - Drums

## curiosities
**Patrick Carney's John Bonham Phase** : During the recording of *Attack and Release* in summer 2007, Patrick Carney was heavily influenced by John Bonham, setting up his drum kit in the studio in a similar fashion. This approach contributed to a bigger, deeper, and tighter drum sound on the album. "he was going through a whole John Bonham phase, and started setting up his kit in the studio like Bonzo used to, and the result is a bigger, deeper, tighter sound."

# instrument - 4-track recorder

## curiosities
**Instrumental in Black Keys' Demo** : Patrick Carney's ownership of a 4-track recorder was crucial for recording The Black Keys' initial demo, which was essential for Dan Auerbach to secure out-of-town gigs and ultimately led to the band's formation. "Since Patrick had a 4 track recorder, he asked Patrick if he would help him make one of these demos."

# instrument - 8-track digi recorder

## curiosities
**Used for Debut Album** : The Black Keys' debut album, *The Big Come Up*, was recorded using an 8-track digi recorder in Patrick Carney's parents' unfinished basement. "It was called The Big Come Up and was recorded in Patrick's parents' unfinished basement in Akron using an 8-track digi recorder between January and March of 2002."

# instrument - Recording console

## curiosities
**eBay Purchase for "Rubber Factory"** : The Black Keys acquired a recording console on eBay, which was used for their *Rubber Factory* album. This console had a history, having been previously owned by the sound engineer for Loverboy, though the band ultimately considered it "crap" and left it behind. "They bought a recording console on eBay, which happened to have been owned by the guy who Winston Soundford lover boy. But it was crap, and they ended up just leaving it in the factory when they were done."

# instrument - Recording tape

## curiosities
**Recycled and Baked for "Rubber Factory"** : For the *Rubber Factory* album, The Black Keys used recycled recording tape that they "baked" from their label, likely to restore it or prepare it for use. "Oh, and all the recording tape they used was recycled stuff that they baked from their label."

# instrument - Digital equipment

## curiosities
**Challenges at Muscle Shoals** : New digital recording equipment brought into Muscle Shoals Sound Studio for *Brothers* was largely fried due to stray voltage introduced into the building from electrical line work on the streets. "But then work on the electrical lines on the streets and all kinds of stray voltage into the building, frying most of the modern stuff."

# curiosity

**CBC show The Bridge - "I Got Mine" Theme** : The Black Keys' song "I Got Mine" was selected and used as the opening theme for the CBC television series *The Bridge*. "Remember the CBC show The Bridge? That's the opening theme."
**School of Rock - "Set You Free" Feature** : The Black Keys' song "Set You Free" was featured in the popular movie *School of Rock*, starring Jack Black. "If you ever saw the movie School of Rock with Jack Black, you might recognize this."
**David Cross - "10 A.M. Automatic" Video Director** : Comedian David Cross took on the role of director for the music video accompanying The Black Keys' single "10 A.M. Automatic." "the video was directed by comedian David Cross."
**General Tire Factory - Studio Conversion** : The Black Keys repurposed the second floor of a former General Tire factory in East Akron, Ohio, as their recording studio for the *Rubber Factory* album. General Tire had ceased operations at the plant in 1982. "the band-in-tire factory in East Akron that was once run by General Tyre, but they bailed out in 1982."
**The Grey Ghost - Chrysler Tour Van** : In their early touring days, The Black Keys used a 1994 Chrysler van affectionately named "the Grey Ghost" to travel from gig to gig. "chugging from gig to gig in a 1994 Chrysler van that they called the Grey Ghost".
**Mayonnaise Brand Commercial Offer - Black Keys Rejection** : A UK-based mayonnaise manufacturer offered The Black Keys 200,000 pounds for a song for a TV commercial. The band turned it down, fearing it would harm their anti-sell-out image, despite financial hardship. "The manufacturer of a brand of mayonnaise, based on the UK, offered the keys 200,000 pounds for the use of one of their songs."
**Muscle Shoals Sound Studio - Legendary Studio** : Muscle Shoals Sound Studio in Alabama is a renowned historical recording location where legendary artists like The Rolling Stones, Lynyrd Skynyrd, Bob Dylan, and Bob Seger recorded. The Black Keys later used it for their *Brothers* album, valuing its acoustics despite technical challenges. "A bunch of it was made at the famous Muscle Shoal Sound Studio in Alabama, a place used by the Rolling Stones, and Leonard Skinner, Bob Dylan, and Bob Seeger."
**Summa Studio - Black Keys' First Professional Studio** : Summa, located in the small college town of Painsville, Ohio, was The Black Keys' first professional studio choice for their *Attack and Release* album, though it was not considered extravagant. "The studio they chose wasn't exactly what you'd call extravagant. It was a place called Summa, in the tiny college town of Painsville, Ohio."
**Easy Eye Sound - Dan Auerbach's Nashville Studio** : Easy Eye Sound is Dan Auerbach's personal "butter zone studio" in Nashville, where The Black Keys recorded *El Camino* in a relaxed environment with functional gear. "Dan had butter zone studio by this time, a place called Easy Eye Sound".
