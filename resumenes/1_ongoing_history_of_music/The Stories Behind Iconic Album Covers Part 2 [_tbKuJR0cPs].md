# artist - Imperial Orchestra
## curiosities
**Association with Richard Rodgers** : The Imperial Orchestra was highlighted on a marquee sign at the Imperial Theatre in New York City, alongside Richard Rodgers and Lorenz Hart, as part of a promotion for a new record release in 1938. This specific marquee setup was photographed to create what became the music industry's first-ever album cover artwork. "He changed the sign to read Columbia Records, Imperial Orchestra under Richard Rogers, smash song hit by Rogers in heart."

## albums
**Definitely Maybe (1994) – Debut Album** : The artwork for Oasis's debut album, released in May 1994, was designed by Brian Cannon with photography by Michael Spencer Jones. It features the five band members in the front room of rhythm guitarist Paul "Bonehead" Arthurs' home in Didsbury, Manchester, an idea inspired by a Beatles compilation. The meticulously staged shot includes personal items, carefully chosen props, and features the album title in Cannon's own handwriting. "Artwork was important to Oasis from the very beginning."
## songs
**Rock 'n' Roll Star** : This song is one of the tracks featured on Oasis's debut album, "Definitely Maybe," released in 1994. "Rock and roll star from definitely maybe."
## curiosities
**Album Artwork Collaboration (1993-1998)** : Noel Gallagher first met designer Brian Cannon about a year before Oasis's debut album and was impressed by his work for The Verve, bonding over Adidas running shoe designs. Noel specifically requested Cannon to design Oasis's artwork once they were signed, a collaboration that continued for the next four years, covering their early albums and singles. "Telling Brian, when we get signed, I want you to do our artwork."
**"Definitely Maybe" Photoshoot Location** : The front cover photograph for Oasis's 1994 debut album, "Definitely Maybe," was taken in the living room of rhythm guitarist Paul "Bonehead" Arthurs' home in Didsbury, Manchester. The room was much smaller and narrower than it appears in the final image, necessitating photographer Michael Spencer Jones to use a wide-angle lens to include all band members. "The setting was the front room of the home of rhythm guitars Paul Bohnhead Arthur's."
**"Definitely Maybe" Album Art Inspiration** : The photoshoot for Oasis's "Definitely Maybe" album drew inspiration from the back cover of The Beatles' 1966 compilation, "A Collection of Beatles Oldies." This choice was a compromise with Noel Gallagher's initial idea, which was a shot of The Beatles in Japan all sitting around a table, a concept deemed uninteresting by photographer Michael Spencer Jones. "The inspiration was another album, specifically the back cover of a 1966 Beatles compilation entitled A Collection of Beatles Oldies."
**Liam Gallagher's Mummy Pose** : On the cover of Oasis's "Definitely Maybe" album, Liam Gallagher is seen lying on the floor in a pose reminiscent of an Egyptian mummy. This specific pose was inspired by photographer Michael Spencer Jones's visit to the Egyptology collection at the Manchester Science Museum. Additionally, the pose helped to conceal the rough, recently stripped floors of Bonehead's living room. "If you want to know why Liam is lying on the floor in the position of an Egyptian mummy, that's why."
**Props and Symbolism on "Definitely Maybe" Cover** : The "Definitely Maybe" album cover is rich with personal objects and symbolic elements chosen by the band members, at the request of photographer Michael Spencer Jones. These include a pink flamingo and an Epiphone Riviera guitar belonging to Bonehead, a pack of Benson and Hedges cigarettes, and portraits of Manchester City football star Rodney Marsh and Manchester United's George Best (Bonehead's favorite). Burt Bacharach's picture was Noel's contribution, and a TV plays "The Good, the Bad and the Ugly," Noel's favorite movie, while a blow-up globe brought by sound guy Mark Coyle and roadie Phil Smith signaled aspirations for "world domination." "Spencer asked everyone to bring personal objects with them, things that meant something to them."
**"Definitely Maybe" Drink Prop** : During the "Definitely Maybe" photoshoot, empty cans of Red Stripe beer were initially present but were replaced by glasses of a diluted British soft drink called Rabena. This substitution was made because real red wine tends to photograph as black, and the diluted Rabena was specifically prepared to achieve the visual effect of red wine. "It's a British soft drink called Rabina that was diluted just enough to look like red wine because if you photograph real wine, it tends to look black in the photographs."
**"Definitely Maybe" Title Lettering** : The lettering for the album title "Definitely Maybe" on the cover is a unique detail, as it is not a commercially available font. Instead, it features the actual handwriting of the album's art director, Brian Cannon. "That's not a special font or anything. That's Brian Cannon's actual handwriting."

# artist - The Cranberries
## albums
**Everybody Else Is Doing It, So Why Can't We? (1993) – Debut Album** : The cover art for the Cranberries' 1993 debut album was conceived by art director Callie and photographed by Andy Earl. The concept involved renting a specific couch and having three band members sit on it, with one member positioned on the floor in front, establishing a visual motif that would recur in their future work. "An art director called Callie was hired. The decision was made to rent a couch and have the members sit on it."
**No Need to Argue (1994) – Second Album** : For their 1994 second album, "No Need to Argue," art director Callie and photographer Andy Earl made a concerted effort to locate and reuse the exact same couch from their debut album. The couch was transported around Dublin for various photos, with the final cover shot taken in a specially constructed stark white studio room, featuring the band in suits, an idea inspired by a recent Blur photoshoot. "Callie called up Earl and had him find that very same couch."
**Something Else (2017) – Unplugged Album** : The distinctive couch that featured prominently on the Cranberries' early album covers made a return appearance on the cover of their 2017 unplugged album, "Something Else." Its whereabouts in the intervening years remain unknown, though it was seen in a Supergrass video. "The couch disappeared from the Cranberries world until 2017 when it appeared on the cover of the unplugged album."
## curiosities
**The Recurring Couch Motif** : A specific rented couch became a distinctive and recurring visual element for The Cranberries' album artwork. It appeared on the covers of their debut album, "Everybody Else Is Doing It, So Why Can't We?" (1993), their second album, "No Need to Argue" (1994), and surprisingly, their 2017 unplugged album, "Something Else." The same couch also made a cameo in the 1996 music video for the Supergrass song "Alright." "The couch disappeared from the Cranberries world until 2017 when it appeared on the cover of the unplugged album."

## curiosities
**Model on Self-Titled Album Cover** : Tony Tiller, the woman with red hair and piercings featured on Godsmack's 1998 self-titled album cover, had her photograph taken in Brooklyn in 1994 while she was deeply involved in the club scene and its fashion drama. She only discovered her image was used when the album was released and now lives off the grid, bald, often barefoot, and dedicated to meditation, esoteric studies, cooking, and unique artsy hobbies, jokingly running a "reform school for rude kittens." **"Godsmack" Album Controversy & Sales Impact** : The 1998 self-titled Godsmack album sparked controversy due to its liner notes, which included a Wiccan pentagram and references to "Salem Witches," along with lyrics deemed "outrageously offensive" by a concerned parent. This led to complaints about the absence of a parental advisory warning, resulting in major retailers Walmart and Kmart refusing to sell the record. The label then issued an altered version with the advisory sticker, which ironically caused the album to sell even better. "Some father in the US took notice of the record and was outraged that there wasn't a parental advisory warning on the cover."

## albums
**Is This It (2001) – Debut Album** : The Strokes' 2001 debut album featured dual cover art designs. The North American version prominently displayed a scientific photograph of subatomic particles in a bubble chamber, while international releases depicted a provocative shot of a woman's naked rear and hip adorned with a black glove, photographed by Colin Lane. The band ultimately preferred the scientific image for global release, but it was too late for the initial pressings. "If you bought the record in North America, we see something very scientific."
## curiosities
**Dual Cover Art for "Is This It" (2001)** : The Strokes' 2001 debut album, "Is This It," was released with two distinct covers. The North American version showcased a scientific image of subatomic particles in a bubble chamber, an image previously seen on Prince's "Graffiti Bridge" and a book by Freeman Dyson. The international version, which initially appeared on early North American editions (July and August 2001) before a change, featured a controversial shot of photographer Colin Lane's then-girlfriend's naked rear and hip, wearing a black Chanel glove. "Record collectors will often look for an album that has different covers in different countries."
**Reason for North American Cover Change (2001)** : The decision to use the scientific bubble chamber image for the North American cover of "Is This It" was made by The Strokes themselves, as they found it "a lot cooler" than the international nude shot. However, by the time singer Julian Casablancas contacted the label, the first edition of the album had already been pressed with the international cover. Consequently, buyers in North America during July and August 2001 received the original cover before the bubble chamber shot became standard. "When the band stumbled on the bubble chamber picture, they just thought it was a lot cooler and they wanted it to be the cover of the album for the world."
**Mystery Model on "Is This It" Cover** : The woman featured on the international cover of The Strokes' "Is This It," depicted from behind with a black glove, was the then-girlfriend of photographer Colin Lane. Lane took the spontaneous shot as she stepped out of the shower, utilizing a Chanel glove from a previous shoot. Despite the image's widespread recognition, Lane has never publicly revealed her name, and the couple are no longer together. "Oh, and who is the woman in that photo? No idea."
**Stylistic Title Formatting for "Is This It"** : The deliberate omission of a question mark in The Strokes' album title "Is This It" was a conscious stylistic decision made by the art director. This choice was based on the belief that including a question mark simply "didn't look right" with the chosen typography and overall design aesthetic. "That's a stylistic decision. According to the art director, it just didn't look right."

## albums
**Audioslave (2002) – Debut Album** : Audioslave's 2002 debut album cover, designed by legendary artist Storm Thorgerson, depicts a golden, flame-like sculpture situated in a desolate landscape, accompanied by a solitary figure in a red shirt and shorts. Thorgerson described the music as "brooding and sultry" and explained the flame symbolizes "the eternal flame, or the flame of remembrance," embodying the band's previous incarnations, Soundgarden and Rage Against the Machine. The image is a real, posed photograph taken on the volcanic island of Lanzarote. "The cover was designed by Storm Thorgerson, probably the most famous album art creator of all time."
## curiosities
**Symbolism of "Audioslave" Album Cover** : Storm Thorgerson, designer of Audioslave's 2002 debut album cover, explained its profound symbolism. Set on the volcanic island of Lanzarote, the large metal sculpture resembling flames represents "the eternal flame, or the flame of remembrance," embodying the collective memory of the band's two previous incarnations, Soundgarden and Rage Against the Machine. The solitary figure in the landscape represents "pilgrims or slaves of sound." "The flame is the eternal flame, or the flame of remembrance, embodying the memory of two previous incarnations, namely Soundgarden and Rage Against the Machine."
**Authenticity of "Audioslave" Album Cover** : The cover artwork for Audioslave's 2002 debut album is notable for its authentic production, being a genuine, posed photograph captured in a real-world location on the volcanic island of Lanzarote, one of the Canary Islands. The design deliberately eschewed modern digital manipulation, meaning "there's no photoshop. There's no AI" involved in its creation. "Now what I love about covers like this is that there's no photoshop. There's no AI. This is a posed photograph in a real location."
**Censored Alternative Cover for "Audioslave"** : During the creative process for Audioslave's debut album, an alternative cover featuring a naked man shot from behind was considered. However, due to concerns about American sensibilities and potential censorship, the decision was made not to use this version for the album's release in the United States. "The cloth figure was nearly replaced by a naked man shot from behind, but America being America, the decision was made not to show any new."

# label - Odeon
## curiosities
**Pioneering Record Bundles (1909)** : In 1909, Odeon, a German company, was the first to introduce the practice of bundling multiple records together. These bundles, consisting of 10-inch, 78 RPM discs packaged in bound book-like formats, served as the direct precursor to what would become known as record albums. "The first company to create such bundles of records was Odeon, a German company in 1909."

# instrument - 10-inch, 78 RPM discs
## curiosities
**Standard Record Format (Pre-1938)** : Prior to the advent of album cover artwork, all commercial music releases were produced on 10-inch, 78 RPM discs, with each side typically containing a single song. These individual discs were commonly packaged together in rudimentary book-like bindings to form what were then termed "phonograph albums." "In those days, all music came on 10-inch, 78 RPM discs. One song per side."

# curiosity

**Alex Steinweiss – Pioneer of Album Art (1938)** : In 1938, 23-year-old graphic designer Alex Steinweiss was hired as Columbia Records' first-ever art director in New York, where he pioneered the concept of album cover artwork. His revolutionary idea to photograph a specially arranged marquee sign for a Richard Rodgers and Lorenz Hart release, and use it as the cover, dramatically increased sales and launched a new creative industry. "In 1938, a 23-year-old graphic designer named Alex Steinweiss was hired as the first-ever art director for Columbia Records in New York."
**Origin of "Record Album" Term** : The terms "phonograph album" or "record album" originated from the early 20th-century practice of packaging multiple 10-inch, 78 RPM discs. These individual records were bound together in book-like formats with paperboard covers and sleeves, resembling a photo album. The German company Odeon first introduced such bundles in 1909. "It was an album for records, and this is where the term phonograph album or record album came from."
**Pre-Artwork Record Packaging (1909-1938)** : For three decades following Odeon's 1909 innovation of bundling records, all records were sold in plain paper sleeves, typically with minimal text. These early "phonograph albums" were largely anonymous, consisting of simple paperboard bindings without any decorative or photographic artwork. "For the next 30 years, all records were sold in plain paper sleeves."
**Evolution of Album Artwork Industry** : The success of the first album cover artwork by Alex Steinweiss led to the creation of an entirely new industry, with design studios worldwide dedicated to creating album art. This profession faced challenges as album formats shrunk from 12x12 inch vinyl to smaller CD covers (6x6 or 5x5 inches) and then to digital files and streaming, though the resurgence of vinyl has revitalized interest in the art form. "A whole new industry was created."

**Ronald Reagan – "Evil Empire" Speech** : The title of Rage Against the Machine's 1996 album, "Evil Empire," was directly sourced from a speech delivered by U.S. President Ronald Reagan. "It comes from a Ronald Reagan speech where he called the Soviet Union just that the evil empire."
