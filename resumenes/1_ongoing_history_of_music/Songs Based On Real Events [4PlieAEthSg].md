## albums
**The Clash (1977) – Self-Titled Debut** : This album was recorded in CBS Studio 3 and featured a collection of songs based on real people and events, reflecting the band's observations of London during the punk years of late 1976 and 1977. The back cover of the album features a picture related to the Notting Hill riot. "When they entered CBS Studio 3 to record their self-titled debut album, they were armed with a bunch of songs based on real people and real events."

## songs
**White Riot (1977)** : This song was directly inspired by the Notting Hill riot on August 30, 1976, where local black West Indian immigrants clashed with police. Joe Strummer was fascinated by the event, wondering why white people didn't show the same fortitude in fighting for their rights against the police. The song was misinterpreted by some as racist or an invitation to cause trouble, leading The Clash to stop playing it by the end of 1979, despite it becoming a classic. "Joe Strummer was fascinated by all this. He wondered why white people didn't have the same fortitude when standing up to police."

## curiosities
**Inspiration from London's Troubles** : In the late 1970s, particularly 1976 and 1977, The Clash drew inspiration from the dire conditions in London, which included an economy in shambles, widespread strikes, high unemployment, rolling blackouts, and the crushing British class system. The band sought to document the experience of being young, poor, and desperate in Britain during this period. "Inspiration was taken from what they saw around them in London during the punk years."

U2, being against the fighting extending beyond Ireland, decided not to march, leading to a confrontation between McGinnis and some IRA members. The song's sentiment questions the longevity of such hatred. "It's not actually about what happened in Dairy on January 30, 1972. It's really more about the dilemma and the conflict U-2 had during the 1982 St. Patrick's Day Parade in New York City."

# artist - R.E.M.

## songs
**What's the Frequency, Kenneth? (1994)** : This song is based on the bizarre October 1986 assault on CBS News Anchor Dan Rather in New York City. Rather was attacked by two well-dressed men who repeatedly asked, "Kenneth, what's the frequency?" while punching and kicking him. The attackers were never caught, and their motive remained a mystery, though theories later emerged linking it to a mentally ill man, William Tanger, who believed media personalities like Rather were beaming signals into his head. Another theory connected the phrase to fictional stories by Texas writer Donald Bartholome, who featured a character named Kenneth and the exact phrase, as well as a newsman named Mr. Lather, potentially stemming from a lingering grudge from the late 50s/early 60s when Rather and Bartholome both worked in Texas journalism. "All of which shall culminate in one of REM's best known songs."

# artist - Pearl Jam

## songs
**Jeremy (1991)** : The song tells the story of Jeremy Wade Dell, a 16-year-old from Richardson, Texas, who committed suicide in front of his English class on January 8, 1991. Jeremy, a loner with divorced parents, pulled out a loaded .357 magnum and shot himself in the mouth after his teacher, Fay Berrett, admonished him for missing classes. The incident received international attention, occurring seven years before the Columbine tragedy, highlighting the then-unfathomable idea of a student bringing a gun to school to kill himself. Eddie Vedder was particularly affected by this story because of a similar incident from his own school days in San Diego, where a classmate named Brian brought a sawed-off shotgun to school and shot up a fish tank. Vedder, who had previously had a confrontation with Brian, wondered if he had been in the wrong place at the wrong time and feared more serious consequences could have occurred. "One guy who was particularly fascinated was this surfer dude from San Diego named Eddie Vetter."

## curiosities
**Father's Anguish Over "Jeremy"** : After Pearl Jam's song "Jeremy" became a hit, reporters tracked down Jeremy Dell's father, Joseph Dell. He expressed profound anguish over the song's impact, describing how fans often leave notes and artifacts at Jeremy's grave, and how numerous websites, frequented by young teenagers, romanticize his son's suicide, often displaying adoration for Eddie Vedder. Joseph Dell's letter highlights the pain caused by the song's glorification of the act. "My anguish is just as deep with each call, note or email."

# concert - Notting Hill Carnival

## curiosities
**Origin and Notting Hill Riot** : The Notting Hill Carnival is an annual summer event in London that began in 1964. On August 30, 1976, during the carnival, bad relations between the mostly black, West Indian immigrant community of Notting Hill and the police escalated into a riot. The police had a policy of arbitrary stop-and-search harassment, and about 1,600 officers were present, an extreme number given no trouble was expected. The riot was triggered around 5 p.m. by an attempted arrest of a pickpocket, leading residents to fight back. The confrontation resulted in 300 police officers hospitalized, 35 police vehicles overturned and burned, and significant property damage. 66 people were arrested, with 17 black youths facing 79 charges in trials that cost a then-record quarter-million pounds. "On August 30, 1976, there was a riot in the Notting Hill area of London... the neighborhood was enjoying its annual carnival, a summer event that began in 1964."

Despite being a good photographer, she disliked school and had been sent to a special facility for problematic kids, reporting suicidal and depressed tendencies. **Bloody Sunday Massacre (1972)** : On January 30, 1972, in Derry, Northern Ireland, a group of unarmed protesters marched despite a ban on parades imposed by the mayor to de-escalate "The Troubles" between Irish Protestants and Catholics. Up to 15,000 people took to the streets, and when stones were thrown at British soldiers, the situation escalated. Ultimately, 26 people were shot, with 13 dying at the scene and one later. The event became known as the Bogside Massacre to some, and Bloody Sunday to others, influencing U2's song "Sunday Bloody Sunday." "On January 30, 1972, a group of unarmed protesters were marching in the northern Irish town of Derry."

They were never caught, and their true motive was never discovered. Although some accused Rather of fabricating the incident, the doorman confirmed his account. "CBS News Anchor Dan Rather is walking down Park Avenue in New York City after having dinner at a friend's house. Suddenly, two well-dressed white men get in his way."
