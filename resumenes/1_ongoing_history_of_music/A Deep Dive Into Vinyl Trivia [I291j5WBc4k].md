# curiosity

**Why 78 RPM? – The Handbook Writer Just Had a Machine That Ran at That Speed** : The first turntable (the gramophone) was introduced in the early 1890s playing at "about 70 RPM." By the end of the 19th century a handbook for record manufacturers recommended the use of governors to standardize speed at 78 RPM. The reason that exact speed was chosen: whoever wrote the handbook had a machine that turned at that speed. The standard propulsion motors selected to power electric turntables ran at 3,600 RPM with a 46:1 gear ratio, yielding 78.26 RPM at 60 Hz — close enough. "The most likely reason is that whoever wrote the handbook had a machine that turned at that speed."

**Why 33⅓ RPM? – Derived from Vitaphone Film Sync System; Math: 3,600 ÷ 108 = 33⅓** : The 33⅓ RPM speed was not arbitrary but derived from the Vitaphone film-sync system introduced in 1931 when movies first had synchronized sound. Film reels were 1,000 feet of 35mm film, good for 11 minutes of viewing. To make audio last exactly 11 minutes to match one film reel, the 3,600 RPM motor required a 108:1 gear ratio: 3,600 ÷ 108 = 33⅓. RCA tried to introduce this format to the public but the Great Depression killed consumer interest, and RCA foolishly let all the patents expire. Columbia Records later picked up the expired technology. "RCA let all the patents expire. Fast forward to the late 1940s."

**Columbia's LP (June 1948) – Polyvinyl Chloride from Sewer Pipe Industry; Microgrooves; 24 Minutes Per Side** : Columbia Records introduced the long-playing album in June 1948 using polyvinyl chloride (PVC), which had first gone into use in the 1920s for sewer pipes. PVC was far more durable than the shellac used for 78s and allowed grooves to be placed much closer together using a tiny stylus — Columbia called these microgrooves. A standard 78 held a spiral about 270 feet long (roughly 4 minutes per side); the new LP had a spiral over 1,600 feet long, yielding up to 24 minutes of music per side. LP stands for Long Playing. "Columbia picked up on the older technology as they were experimenting with the new substance called polyvinyl chloride, which first went into use in the 1920s for sewer pipes."

**Why 45 RPM and the Large 1.5-Inch Hole? – RCA's Revenge; Jukebox Torque Distribution** : When RCA discovered Columbia had beaten them using their own expired patents, they fought back with a 7-inch, 45 RPM single also made from PVC. The 45 RPM speed delivers a 35% increase in groove velocity compared to 33⅓, greatly reducing distortion and loss of high frequencies. The unusually large 1.5-inch center hole served two purposes: (1) RCA initially sold turntables that could only play 45s to lock consumers into their format; (2) the larger hole distributed the sudden rotational torque over a longer path (π × 1.5 ≈ 4.71 inches), preventing the hole from going out of round — crucial for the jukebox industry, which was enormous business in the 1940s–50s. "The larger hole allowed the sudden rotational force to be distributed over a longer path, pi times 1.5 or about 4.71 inches."

**Jack White's 3 RPM Record – Third Man Label's 3rd Anniversary; Every Blue Series Single** : The slowest-turning record ever made was created by Jack White's Third Man label for the label's 3rd anniversary. The special series of records spun at just 3 RPM and contained every Blue Series single from Third Man. It was a total gimmick, never really designed to be played. White also sold tiny 3-inch records and turntables called the Triple Inch Ophone (2005–2006), and in 2019 collaborated with Crosley to create a working turntable for 3-inch records. "But the slowest turning record ever made was created by Jack White's 3rd Man label."

**Record Size Extremes – Largest: 50 cm (Europe 1900–1930); Smallest: 2-Inch Compilation; 16-Inch for Film/Radio** : The biggest records ever made were 50 centimeters (about 20 inches), seen in Europe between 1900 and 1930. Sixteen-inch records were used for movie soundtracks (the Vitaphone system) and radio transcription discs for pre-recorded programming before magnetic tape arrived in the 1940s. The smallest known records are 2 inches in diameter: one was a P-brain compilation of six bands each playing a 10-second song; the other was a 20-second hardcore song called "Soda Tax" by Two Minute Minor, protesting Cook County, Illinois accidentally imposing a tax on soft drinks. "The smallest record I know of has a diameter of just two inches."

**Parallel Groove Records – Two Independent Spirals on One Side; Monty Python's Matching Tie and Handkerchief (1973)** : A parallel groove record places two completely independent audio spirals on the same side, creating three sides on a two-sided record — whichever groove the stylus catches first determines what you hear. Monty Python's 1973 album Matching Tie and Handkerchief confused people who kept getting different audio each time they dropped the needle on side one. This technique dates to 1901 when Victor issued a fortune-telling record with three different fortunes on the same side. Artists who have used it include Laurie Anderson, the Sugar Cubes, Stereolab, Fine Young Cannibals, Marillion, Mr. Bungle, Kate Bush, Garbage, and Jack White. Tool's 1992 Opiate EP has a double groove that plays either the listed track or a hidden bonus track ("The Gaping Lotus Experience"). "People couldn't figure out why they kept getting different audio each time they dropped the needle on side one."

**Locked Grooves – Beatles Sgt. Pepper Final Chord; Flipper "Brainwash" 5.5-Hour YouTube Version; Arcade Fire The Suburbs** : A locked groove turns the final groove on a record side into a continuous loop instead of leading to the silent runoff area. Examples include the Beatles' Sgt. Pepper (the final chord of "A Day in the Life" loops forever in some editions), Sonic Youth's 1986 album Evol (track labeled "Infinity"), San Francisco band Flipper's B-side "Brainwash" (30 seconds that loops 12 times over almost 7 minutes, with a YouTube version running 5.5 hours), and Arcade Fire's The Suburbs (side three ends with a continuous loop of a piano chord from "We Used to Wait"). "There is a version on YouTube that runs five and a half hours. Somebody had way too much time."

**Inside-Out Records – MegaDeath 1993 Sweating Bullets Blue Vinyl; Plays from Center Outward** : In 1993, MegaDeath released their Sweating Bullets single on 12-inch blue vinyl that plays from the inside out — the listener places the tone arm on the innermost grooves and the spiral moves toward the outer edge. This was, to the host's knowledge, the first rock act to try this. A special edition of Camper Van Beethoven's Keyline Pi (2014 reissue) similarly hid the closing theme of side one on innermost grooves playing at 45 RPM while the rest of the record played at 33⅓. "In 1993, MegaDeath released their sweating bullet sing along a slab of 12-inch blue vinyl."

**No-Hole Record – Gay Bikers on Acid "Drill Your Own Hole" (1987); About 1,000 Copies** : In 1987, British psych-garage band Gay Bikers on Acid released their debut album on Virgin Records with approximately 1,000 copies containing no center hole at all — the album was entitled Drill Your Own Hole, and fans were instructed to drill their own if they wished to play the record. "Virgin Records released about a thousand copies without a hole, instructing fans to, yeah, drill their own if they wish to listen to the record."

**Squeeze 5-Inch Record (1980) – Songs Mixed in Mono Due to Space Constraints** : In 1980, the band Squeeze issued a five-inch record spinning at 33⅓ RPM. Space was so tight that the songs had to be mixed in mono. This was one of many non-standard record sizes attempted over the decades. "Space was so tight, the songs had to be mixed in mono."

**16 and 2/3 RPM – Spoken Word, Children's Records, and Chrysler In-Dash Turntables** : Turntables were briefly manufactured to operate at four speeds: 33⅓, 45, 78, and 16⅔ RPM. Slow-speed records were used for spoken word, children's releases, and as an in-dash turntable option sold by Chrysler in the 1950s. The Chrysler turntables were not practical (too much bouncing), and fewer than 50 titles were released, available only at Chrysler dealers. Audiobook records for the blind were made at speeds as slow as 4⅙ RPM. "Several audiobook records were once manufactured for the blind with speeds of 24, 8 and 1 3rd, and 4 and 1 6th RPM."
