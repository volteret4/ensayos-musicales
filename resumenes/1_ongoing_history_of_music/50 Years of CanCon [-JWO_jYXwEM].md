# curiosity

**Canadian Content Regulations (CanCon) – January 18, 1971; 30% Canadian Music Required on AM Radio** : On January 18, 1971, the Canadian Radio Television and Communications Commission (CRTC) mandated that 30% of all music heard on Canadian AM radio stations had to be of Canadian origin. The regulation was developed largely through lobbying by Stan Klees and Walt Grealis, co-founders of RPM Magazine (established Toronto, 1964), Canada's first domestic music industry trade publication tracking radio and charts. Grealis also conceived the Gold Leaf Awards in 1970 (renamed the Juno Awards in 1971, named after CRTC head Pierre Juneau), which began as an annual column with a small wine and cheese party for 250 people. The first Juno Awards took place February 22, 1971. "It turned Canada into a global musical powerhouse eventually."

**Pre-CanCon Canadian Radio Bias – The Guess Who Had to Pretend to Be British (1965)** : In January 1965, Canadian band Chad Allan and the Expressions recorded "Shakin' All Over" on Quality Records. Quality Records knew Canadian radio would not add it because stations only played Canadian songs after they had become US hits. The label shipped 17 singles to stations across Canada with no band name listed — just a question mark reading "Guess Who?" — implying it was a mystery British band. The ruse worked. When stations realized in mid-March 1965 they had been tricked into playing a Canadian group, the name had already stuck. The band later officially became The Guess Who. "Quality purposely left the name of the group off the record."

**The MAPL System – Defining What Counts as Canadian Content** : Stan Klees developed the MAPL system (after a pie chart with four quadrants printed on Canadian releases): M = Music composed by a Canadian; A = Artist performing is principally Canadian; P = Production in a Canadian studio (or live Canadian broadcast); L = Lyrics written by a Canadian. A song qualifies as CanCon if it meets at least two of these four criteria. This creates surprising outcomes: Lenny Kravitz's "American Woman" qualifies because music and lyrics were written by Canadians (Burton Cummings and Randy Bachman), even though Kravitz was born in New York. Bryan Adams's "Everything I Do (I Do It for You)" did NOT qualify because collaborators Mutt Lange (British) and Michael Kamen (American) brought it below the two-point threshold — which led to the so-called "Brian Adams Rule" that now allows more leniency for international collaborations. "Still my least favorite record."

**CanCon's Industrial Impact – Labels, Recording Studios, and Infrastructure Created by Mandate** : By creating artificial demand for Canadian music, the CanCon rules required the creation of a domestic music industry infrastructure: recording studios, producers, engineers, promoters, booking agents, and record labels. Within a year of the rules taking effect, new Canadian labels appeared including GRT, Attic, Stony Plain, Aquarius, Nimbus 9, Axe, and Daffodil. Initially, Canadian records were often technically and artistically inferior; stations created so-called "Beaver Hours," playing heavily edited Canadian songs between 11 p.m. and midnight to meet quotas in one concentrated block. The CRTC closed that loophole. "For a while, Canadian meant bad."

**FACTOR and Video FACT – Funding Bodies Born from CanCon (1982 and 1984)** : The Foundation to Assist Canadian Talent on Records (FACTOR) was introduced in 1982, requiring radio stations to contribute funds distributed to artists passing industry juries. The money can be used for recording, touring, and general artist development. When foreign artists learn that Canadian artists have access to such a fund, the common reaction is shock and envy. In 1984, MuchMusic launched as Canada's music video channel, and Video FACT was established to fund Canadian music video production. "When foreign artists find out that Canadian artists have access to such a fund, they just can't believe it."

**CanCon Raised to 35% on January 3, 1999; Most New Stations Promise 40%** : After 20+ years of development, the Canadian music scene was strong enough that when quotas were raised from 30 to 35% on January 3, 1999, "almost nobody noticed." Since 2000, most new radio station applications have voluntarily promised a 40% CanCon quota — evidence that programmers believe such a ratio works with audiences. Canada is the fifth or sixth largest music market in the world, regularly competing with Australia for that ranking, always behind the US, Japan, UK, Germany, and France. "One of us is the US, Japan, the UK, Germany and France, all of which have larger populations than us."

**CanCon Controversies and Edge Cases – Dallas Green + Pink, Pop Evil, Arcade Fire** : Dallas Green of Alexis on Fire and City and Colour collaborated with Pink on a 2014 album called "You and Me." Because files were exchanged between home studios across countries, a CRTC ruling was needed. The album received CanCon certification because the CRTC ruled Green's contribution as a lead artist was sufficient for a full "A" — but this came retroactively, after the album was already released. Pop Evil, a Michigan band with a Canadian founder (Lee Kelly), also required a CRTC ruling on whether their songs qualified. Arcade Fire has American-born members but qualifies as a Canadian act because they are landed immigrants. "You do not want to screw up your cancon numbers."

**Streaming Services and CanCon – Unregulated Platforms Drain Canadian Revenue** : Streaming platforms — all foreign-owned — operate in Canada without CanCon obligations. Unlike Canadian radio stations, they are not required to invest in Canadian culture or contribute to funds like FACTOR. Critics argue this drains revenue from the country while providing nothing back to the domestic music ecosystem. Canada, France, Australia, New Zealand, Mexico, South Africa, Nigeria, Jamaica, Venezuela, Israel, and the Philippines all have domestic music quotas for radio for this reason. "Streaming services put nothing back into the system."

**The Can Rock Renaissance of the 1990s – Tragically Hip, Alanis Morissette, Nickelback, and Dozens More** : By the 1990s, after two decades of CanCon nurturing, Canadian music had come of age. Major domestic acts selling tens or hundreds of thousands of records included Matthew Good, Tea Party, I Mother Earth, Barenaked Ladies, Pursuit of Happiness, Sloan, The Watchmen, Moist, The Headstones, Econoline Crush, Limblifter, Bif Naked, Real McKenzies, Tragically Hip, and Alanis Morissette. Alanis Morissette, Sarah McLachlan, Shania Twain, and Céline Dion became international megastars. In the 2000s, Nickelback's "How You Remind Me" became the most-played song on American radio between 2001 and 2009. More recently, Michael Bublé, Justin Bieber, Drake, Shawn Mendes, and The Weeknd have all emerged from the Canadian system. "Depending on the year, we are either the fifth or sixth largest music market in the world."
