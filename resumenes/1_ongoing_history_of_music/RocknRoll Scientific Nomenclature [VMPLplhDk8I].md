# curiosity

**Carl Linnaeus and Taxonomy** : The firm rules for naming living things on Earth are traceable to Carl Linnaeus, a Swedish scientist who originated the idea of using Latin for naming and classifying all things, living and dead. This system, known as taxonomy, utilizes Latin because it is a dead language, ensuring neutrality and international agreement without offense. "These rules can be traced to a guy named Carl Leneas, a Swedish scientist who came up with the idea of using Latin for naming and classifying things both living in dead."
**International Nomenclature Rules** : Over decades, international codes have standardized scientific naming rules for all species, from bacteria to whales, and from extinct species hundreds of millions of years old to new discoveries. A key rule allows the discoverer of a new species to name it, provided they follow standard nomenclature, and they can choose to name it after a famous person or group. "One of the rules is that whoever finds a new thing is allowed to give it a name as long as they follow the standard nomenclature rules and should a scientist or whoever so desire, he or she can choose to name their discovery after a famous person or group."
**Trilobites** : Trilobites were among the planet's most successful early animals, appearing over 500 million years ago and surviving for more than 270 million years. There are believed to be at least 17,000 different types of these beetle-like creatures that once populated the oceans. "We think that there were at least 17,000 different types of these beetle-like things, applying the oceans beginning over 500 million years ago and we think they survive more than 270 million years."
**Naming of Celestial Bodies** : The International Astronomical Union (IAU) governs the naming of objects in space. While stars are rarely named after people (with only about two dozen exceptions), asteroids (officially minor planets) are fair game for discoverers to name, pending IAU approval. "The International Astronomical Union is in charge of setting the rules for naming things in space."
**Asteroid Naming Rules** : Asteroids, officially known as minor planets, are open for naming by their discoverers, subject to approval by the International Astronomical Union. Historically, classical musicians dominated asteroid namings, but this trend has shifted in recent decades to include rock and popular music figures. "If you discover a brand new asteroid, then you get to name it, pending approval from the International Astronomical Union, of course."
**Lunar Crater Naming Rules** : On Earth's moon, craters are generally named after deceased scientists, scholars, explorers, and artists. Thousands of craters require thousands of names, providing ample opportunity for recognition. "On our moon, craters are generally named after deceased scientists, scholars, explorers, and artists."
**Martian Crater Naming Rules** : Craters on Mars are typically named after deceased scientists and writers who have made contributions to the study and lore of Mars. This rule generally guides the recognition of individuals on the red planet. "The rule for Mars is similar. Dead scientists and writers who have contributed to the study and lore of Mars."
**Venusian Feature Naming Rules** : Due to its murky atmosphere, features on Venus are difficult to observe. However, scientists have agreed that any discovered features must be named after females, such as goddesses. "Venus, well, it's very tough to see anything through the murky atmosphere, but scientists have agreed that if they do spot anything, it has to be named after females, goddesses and the like."
**Mercurian Crater Naming Rules** : Craters on Mercury must be named after "famous deceased artists, musicians, painters, and authors." As scientists are still extensively mapping Mercury, only about a hundred craters have been named, predominantly after classical personalities, though this is beginning to change. "The rule for craters on Mercury is that they must be named after, quote, famous deceased artists, musicians, painters, and authors."
**Rarity of Moon Crater Recognition** : Having a moon crater named after an individual is an extremely rare honor. John Lennon and Michael Jackson share this distinction with historical giants such as Leonardo da Vinci, Christopher Columbus, Isaac Newton, Jules Verne, and Julius Caesar. "Very, very few people get moon craters named after them."
**Discovery of New Elements** : Scientists have discovered four new elements (113, 115, 117, and 118) using particle accelerators, which involve bombarding existing elements to jam more protons and neutrons into a nucleus. These synthetic elements are highly unstable, existing for a second or less, but their creation advances the understanding of nature's fundamental building blocks. "Using these particle accelerators, existing elements were bombarded with subatomic particles in hopes of jamming more protons and neutrons into a nucleus thereby creating a brand new element."
**Naming Conventions for New Elements** : Scientific nomenclature tradition dictates that the discoverers of new elements are granted the privilege of naming them. For a period, these elements use placeholder designations until permanent names are officially assigned. "Now once again, scientific nomenclature tradition says that the discoverers of the new elements get to give them names."
**Names of New Elements** : Element 113 is named nihonium (after "Nihon" for Japan), element 115 is muscovium (for a similar reason, implying Moscow/Russia), element 117 is tenacine, and element 118 is oganeson, named after nuclear physicist Yuri Oganesian, who was integral to its discovery and is one of only two living humans with an element named after them. "Element 113 is called nihonium based on the word nihon, which is a common name for Japan, which is a fair cop since the co-discoverers were all Japanese. Element 115 is called muscovium for a similar reason. Element 117 is called tenacine, but element 118 is different. It's called oganeson after Yuri Oganesian, a nuclear physicist who was in tegro in its discovery."
**Rules for Naming Elements after People** : The rules for naming a new element state that its name must originate from a mythological concept, a mineral, a place, a country, a property, or a scientist. Musicians are explicitly not permitted under these rules. "the rules regarding the naming of a new element say that its name must come from a mythological concept, a mineral, a place, a country, property, or scientist. Musician does not count."
**Heavy Metal Element Classification** : Elements 113, 115, 117, and 118, despite being the heaviest yet discovered, are classified as non-metals on the periodic table because they occupy the right-hand side, an area reserved for non-metals. Element 117 is a halogen, and 118 is a noble gas, while 113 and 115 have only distant or predicted transitional metal properties, not fitting the "heavy metal" designation. "if we look at the periodic table, you'll see that these new elements occupy space on the right hand side. This is the area of the table reserved for non-metals. Actual metal elements are grouped on the left side of the table."
