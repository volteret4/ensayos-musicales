# artist - George W. Johnson

## genres
Pop
Vaudeville

## labels
Metropolitan Phonograph Company

## songs
**The Laughing Song (1890)** : This was George W. Johnson's most popular track, recorded in 1890 using Thomas Edison's wax cylinders. By 1894, it became the best-selling song ever, reaching number one on the nascent music charts and making Johnson the first Black performer to top those charts. "Johnson's most popular track was The Laughing Song, which was recorded in 1890."
**The Laughing Song (1897 Rerecording)** : A later recording of "The Laughing Song" was made in 1897. Due to the inability to reproduce wax cylinders, Johnson had to record each cylinder individually, sometimes singing the song 50 times a day, leading to estimates of 12,000-15,000 performances to meet orders for 50,000 copies. "All I can tell you is that it was a later recording from 1897."

## curiosities
**World's First Recording Star** : George W. Johnson, a former slave freed around 1853, became the world's first recording star and biggest pop star in the late 1890s. He sang on the streets of New York City before being recruited in 1890 by Charles Marshall to make recordings for coin-operated phonographs. "George W. Johnson was the world's first and biggest pop star in the late 1890s."
**First Black Performer to Reach Number One** : Johnson achieved a historic milestone when his song "The Laughing Song" reached number one on the brand new music charts of the day by 1894, making him the first Black performer to top those charts. His recordings also led to him becoming a major Vaudeville star. "In fact, he was the first black performer to bake those charts."
**Unique Recording Process** : Due to the technical limitations of Thomas Edison's wax cylinders, which were impossible to reproduce, every cylinder was a master recording. Johnson, with his strong voice, could be recorded by up to five machines simultaneously, and he would often repeat a song 50 times a day to produce enough copies. "So in other words, every single cylinder had to be recorded individually."
**Early Marketing of a Black Artist** : Johnson's record label explicitly marketed him as a Black man, which was an unusual and bold move during a time when slavery was a recent memory for many and segregation was still prevalent. "What was interesting is that his record label marketed him as a black man, something that just wasn't done when slavery was still fresh in the minds of many and segregation was strong."
**Career Decline and Death** : After achieving immense popularity as the world's first pop star, George W. Johnson's career faded. He eventually worked as an office doorman and died in 1914 at the age of 67. He was buried in an unmarked grave in Queens, New York. "But like so many careers, Johnson's popularity faded and he ended up working as an office door man."

**Early Electric Guitar Soloing** : Charlie Christian was an excellent guitarist who saw the potential for the electric guitar beyond chords and rhythm. He started improvising solos in jam sessions as early as 1931 and, after joining Benny Goodman's big band in 1939, was given the freedom to solo extensively, transforming the amplified guitar's role. "In 1931, he started soloing during jam sessions with fellow musicians."
**Untimely Death and Legacy** : Christian died young from tuberculosis on March 3rd, 1941, at the age of 25. Despite his short career, he is credited with taking the amplified guitar out of the shadows and establishing the foundational role of the lead guitarist in modern music. "Until he died of tuberculosis on March 3rd, 1941 at the age of 25, Charlie took the new amplified guitar out of the shadows and is now recognized as the person to create the foundations of the lead guitarist as we know it today."

## songs
**Rocket 88 (1951)** : Recorded on March 2nd, 1951, and released in late March 1951, "Rocket 88" is often considered by some, particularly producer Sam Phillips, as the first true rock and roll record. The song features distorted bass due to a damaged amplifier speaker cone, a sound that Phillips intentionally kept, contributing to its innovative sound. "But then producer Sam Phillips, a white guy, said, hold on, sounds kind of cool. Let's use it as is."

## curiosities
**Accidental Bass Distortion on "Rocket 88"** : During the recording session for "Rocket 88" on March 2nd, 1951, bass player Willie Kizart's amplifier was accidentally dropped, jarring loose the speaker cone. This resulted in a distinctive buzz and vibration. Producer Sam Phillips decided to embrace this "cool" distorted sound, which became a new and defining characteristic of the track, potentially making it the first rock and roll record. "But then producer Sam Phillips, a white guy, said, hold on, sounds kind of cool. Let's use it as is."
**Claim to First Rock and Roll Record** : While the exact "first rock and roll record" is debated, "Rocket 88" by Jackie Brenston and his Delta Cats is strongly nominated, largely because producer Sam Phillips consistently touted it as such. Phillips' endorsement, coming from the discoverer of Elvis Presley, helped solidify this legend into a widely accepted fact. "It was because Sam Phillips said it was."
**Recording Journey to Sun Studios** : On March 2nd, 1951, the Delta Cats packed into a station wagon with their instruments and drove 75 miles from Clarksdale, Mississippi, where they had been rehearsing at the Riverside Hotel, to Sun Studios in Memphis along Highway 61 to record. "On March 2nd 1951, all the Delta Cats packed up an old station wagon in front of the Riverside Hotel in Clarkstale, Mississippi where they'd been rehearsing and were ready to make a record."

# genre - Blues

"The structure of these songs with their three line stances can trace back to the field songs in the Mississippi Delta."
**Foundational Influence on Modern Music** : The blues served as a fundamental building block for many subsequent genres, influencing everything from rock and R&B to funk and hip hop, due to its distinctive structure and expressive storytelling. # genre - Rock and Roll

## curiosities
**African-American Roots** : Rock and roll, as we know it today, emerged in the early 1950s from a rich history and tradition of Black music. It incorporated elements of spirituals, gospel, blues, boogie-woogie, ragtime, jazz, rhythm and blues, bebop, and was blended with country, western, hillbilly, and R&B. "And in the early 1950s, this music with its rich history and traditions was incorporated with country, western, hillbilly, R&B and a few other ingredients to become what we now call rock and roll."
**Evolution of the Term "Rock and Roll"** : The phrase "rock and roll" originally referred to the movement of a ship on rough seas. Its earliest public use in song was by Johnny Morton's Minstrels in Victoria, BC on April 24, 1881. It also described animated gospel singing in Black churches before evolving into a secular euphemism for sex, a shift from the sacred to the profane. "Rocking and rolling went from a joyous spiritual experience at church on Sunday morning to a euphemism for sex."
**Allen Freed's Role in Popularization** : In 1951, white radio DJ Allen Freed, playing "race records" (songs by Black artists) in Cleveland, began using the term "rock and roll" to describe the music. While he defined it as "swing music with a modern name" rooted in levees, plantations, folk songs, blues, and rhythm, he likely understood its coded sexual connotations. "Fried was pretty plugged into the culture and knew the phrase rock and roll and started referring to the records he played with that term."
**Gradual Birth and Landmark Recordings** : The birth of rock and roll was a gradual process, evolving from a mixing and mutation of disparate popular sounds. While it's difficult to pinpoint a single "first" rock and roll record, landmark recordings like Roy Brown's "Good Rockin' Tonight" (1947) and Jackie Brenston's "Rocket 88" (1951) are frequently cited as early examples. "The birth happened gradually. As all of this disparate, popular sounds of the day mixed and matched and evolved and mutated."
**Overwhelming Black Artist Contributions** : The vast majority of proto-rock and roll songs, those foundational tracks leading to the genre's emergence, came from Black artists. This highlights the indispensable role of African-American musicians in shaping the origins of rock and roll. "What isn't up for discussion is that the vast, vast majority of these proto-rock and roll songs were from black artists."

# instrument - Wah-wah pedal

## curiosities
**Mechanical Precursors and Rock Innovation** : The wah-wah effect had been mechanically reproduced by brass players as far back as the 1920s. However, it was truly brought into rock music in 1967, with Jimi Hendrix and Eric Clapton releasing records featuring the pedal within days of each other, marking its innovation in guitar effects. "This particular effect had been reproduced mechanically by brass players as far back as the 1920s."

# curiosity

**African-American Contribution to Music History** : The history of rock music and its ongoing development are deeply indebted to people of African descent. Tracing important innovations and accomplishments backward often leads to Black culture, from the rhythms and songs brought by enslaved Africans in 1619 to the modern genres like rock and roll, funk, and hip hop. "We would not be sitting here talking about rock music if it weren't for people of African descent."
**Music as Sustenance During Slavery** : Over centuries of forced displacement to the West, music, including work songs, protest songs, and satirical songs, served as a crucial means of sustenance for enslaved African people. These songs, with regular or syncopated rhythms, helped them endure brutal times and preserved their identities, history, and traditions. "And these songs and rhythms helped sustain them through those awful brutal times."
**Recordings by Black Musicians on White-Owned Labels** : Throughout the 1910s and 1920s, many Black musicians were recorded by white-owned record labels. These recordings, released on 10-inch, 78 RPM records, gained widespread popularity across all audiences, highlighting the broad appeal of early Black music. "Through the 1910s and into the 1920s, many black musicians found themselves being recorded by white-owned record labels."
**Radio Broadcasting Shift in Late 1940s/Early 1950s** : The emergence of television caused major networks (NBC, CBS, Mutual) to pivot away from radio programming, leaving hundreds of local radio stations without content. This led to a major shift where stations, seeking cheap and fast solutions, began hiring DJs to play popular records, moving radio's focus from dramas and variety shows to music and aiding the mixing of R&B, Blues, Country, and Western into rock and roll. "The networks began to pivot away from cross-country radio program to cross-country TV program."
**Voyager Golden Disk** : Launched in 1977, the Voyager I and Voyager II spacecraft each carry a gold phonograph record called the "Voyager Golden Disk." This durable disk contains images, coordinates, drawings, sounds of Earth, spoken word recordings, and a significant amount of music, intended as an introduction to humanity for any advanced alien species that might discover the probes in interstellar space. "This is the Voyager Golden Disk. It's a phonograph record made of gold, one of the toughest elements in the universe."
**Black Rock Coalition** : The Black Rock Coalition is a non-profit organization dedicated to providing assistance and support for Black artists within the music industry. "Black Rock Coalition, and nonprofit that provides help for Black artists."
