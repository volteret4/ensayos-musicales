## The Ongoing History of New Music: A Retrospective Analysis

This document explores the genesis, evolution, and significant impact of Alan Cross's *Ongoing History of New Music*, drawing from insights shared during its 1000th episode celebration. It delves into the unique broadcasting environment that fostered its creation, the artistic philosophy underpinning its enduring success, and the cultural moments it has chronicled.

### Show Origin and Early Context

*   **The Catalyst for Creation (January 1993):** The show was born out of a specific industry shift. The radio station where Alan Cross worked for seven years was sold, and new management, instead of opting for a financially safer country format, recognized the burgeoning "Grunge" movement and the rise of Lollapalooza. They decided to "double down on an alternative format." This strategic pivot created a need for a program to educate new, non-specialist listeners.
*   **Mandate for Inclusivity:** Alan Cross was tasked with creating a show that would be "inclusive" and "welcoming," moving away from the "music snobs" attitude prevalent in some alternative circles. The goal was to "explain the music we're playing" to a broader audience.
*   **An Accidental Historian:** Alan was chosen to host the show, christened "The Ongoing History of New Music," primarily because he held a history degree, a unique qualification among the station's staff. This academic background implicitly shaped the show's approach to contextualizing music.
*   **Personal and Professional Upheaval:** The show's inception was tied to a significant personal and professional restructuring for Cross. His full-time employment was severed, and he was re-engaged as a private contractor. This cost-saving measure meant working three days a week on the show, supplemented by hourly weekend shifts. Initially, Cross was "stunned," disliked the proposed show title ("stupid name"), and faced considerable personal stakes, having just married and bought a house with a high-interest mortgage. He also initially doubted the "portable skills" of discussing records for a living.
*   **Inauspicious Beginnings (February 28, 1993):** The first episode aired before the widespread adoption of the internet or cell phones. Early production relied on an outdated 386 computer running a DOS word processor (Q&A, which proved incompatible with later software) and extensive use of "miles and miles of analog magnetic tape" for recording. Cross initially expected the show to last only "a couple of years."
*   **The "Preview Show" Paradox:** The first episode was designed as a "preview," with Cross candidly admitting he "had absolutely no idea what I was doing." Despite this, management approved it and subsequently "never stuck their nose into it at all," granting him significant creative autonomy. Early series charted alternative music year-by-year from 1963-1992, with Cross now deeming those initial efforts "a bit embarrassing" compared to the show's current quality.

### Broadcasting Context and Regulations

*   **Canadian FM Radio's Unique History:** The existence and format of *The Ongoing History of New Music* were deeply influenced by Canada's historically "highly, highly regulated" FM radio landscape. The goal of these regulations was to keep FM distinct from AM radio.
*   **Strict Content Quotas:**
    *   **Song Repetition Limits:** FM stations were legally forbidden from playing the same song more than 18 times a week.
    *   **Top 40 Limits:** No more than 49% of an FM station's playlist could consist of records that made the Top 40.
    *   **Talk Requirements:** FM stations were mandated to include a significant amount of talk, categorized into:
        *   **Surveillance:** Weather, traffic updates.
        *   **Mosaic Talk:** Announcers had to stop music for a minimum of 60 seconds to speak on a specific topic.
        *   **News & Public Affairs.**
        *   **Foreground Programming:** This category was crucial for *Ongoing History*. It required creating self-contained shows of at least 30 minutes focusing on a specific topic.
*   **Survival Beyond Mandate:** Although foreground programming requirements were "finally ditched sometime in the 1990s," *The Ongoing History of New Music* had become so well-established and popular that it continued to run, even though the station was no longer legally obliged to broadcast it.
*   **Breaking the Rules of Radio:** The program's sustained success is particularly noteworthy because it defies three core principles of conventional radio:
    1.  The host (Alan Cross) "talks too much."
    2.  The radio station's name is "never mentions... within the body of the program."
    3.  The show "is often filled with unfamiliar music," which is typically considered "ratings suicide."

### Style and Sound

*   **Thematic Depth:** The show's mission, as stated in its inaugural episode, was "to track what happened to music between punk and the present," bridging acts like the Ramones and Sex Pistols to Red Hot Chili Peppers and Pearl Jam, and exploring connections (e.g., The Jam to Jane's Addiction, Dead Boys to Depeche Mode).
*   **Detail-Oriented Approach:** Alan Cross emphasizes his commitment to "detail and telling you stuff that you don't know," aiming for a "wow, that's cool" reaction from listeners. He views his role as enriching the listener's experience by providing context, background, and emotional meaning to music, which he contrasts with the decontextualized nature of streaming ("organized noise").
*   **Emotional Investment:** Cross states that he puts "heart and soul into every script," and that the emotion heard in his voice is "all very, very real." He has autonomy over topics and angles, prioritizing "maximum emotional impact and the minimum number of words."
*   **Evolutionary Focus:** Despite the title, Cross asserts he "almost never focus[es] on the past. It's always been about what's next," implying a constant search for new historical angles and emerging trends within the framework of music history.

### Production Details

*   **Early Analog Workflow:** The first 110 shows were produced by Craig Van using a multi-track analog tape machine, with everything bounced down to quarter-inch reel-to-reel tape for broadcast.
*   **Enduring Partnership:** Alan immediately requested a technical producer. After Craig Van, Rob Johnson was assigned and has remained the show's producer and head of affiliate relations, with Cross crediting him for making him "sound competent."
*   **Intensive Research and Writing:** A single hour-long radio show requires 10-14 hours of writing and research, a testament to the depth and detail provided.

### Cultural Impact and Significance

*   **Milestone Longevity:** Reaching 1000 episodes over 30 years is a rare feat in modern radio, which Cross notes "doesn't happen in this business anymore." He compares its longevity to legendary broadcasts like the BBC's *Your 100 Best Tunes* (44 years with one host), CBC's *As It Happens* (54 years), *Grand Ole Opry* (since 1927), and even the BBC *Shipping Forecast* (audio since 1924, telegraph since 1858). A key distinction for *Ongoing History* is its continuous single host.
*   **Pre-Internet Information Hub:** Before the internet and social media, radio was the primary source for "real-time information." The show served as a crucial conduit for news and context, as exemplified by its live reporting of Kurt Cobain's death.
*   **"JFK Moment" for a Generation:** Alan Cross describes his live reporting of Kurt Cobain's death (April 8, 1994) as a defining, "chilling" moment for an entire generation, akin to the impact of the deaths of JFK, Elvis, or John Lennon.
*   **Impact of "Smells Like Teen Spirit":** Cross recounts being among the first, if not *the* first, in Canada to play Nirvana's "Smells Like Teen Spirit" on August 27, 1991. The immediate, overwhelming phone response from listeners—who were "losing their minds" and wanted it played "again and again"—underscored its instant cultural resonance. Its rapid ascent to selling 300,000 copies a week and knocking Michael Jackson off the #1 Billboard spot symbolized the arrival of alternative music.
*   **The "Favorite Era":** Alan Cross identifies the period between 1991 and 1995 as his "favorite era of music," characterized by a daily influx of "this new sound, this new song, this new scene" encompassing grunge, American indie, the Lollapalooza generation, gangster rap, and early Britpop.
*   **Generational Identity and Music:** Cross articulates the powerful phenomenon where "every generation has a biological right to believe the music of their youth is the greatest music of all time." He asserts that for those 14-24 in the 1990s, that era's music will remain their "comfort food forever," highlighting the show's role in defining and celebrating this period.
*   **Global Reach and Bootlegging:** The podcast has been downloaded over 21 million times since 2018, reaching every country except "a couple in West Africa and North Korea." The show has also been extensively bootlegged, with cassettes sold in independent record stores and broadcasts appearing on shortwave radio, indicating its broad, unauthorized distribution and fervent following.
*   **British Music Gateway:** The show and its originating station were instrumental in breaking British acts in North America, particularly in Toronto (described as the "third leg of a triangle" with New York and London). Many British bands from 1984 onwards credit the station for their North American success.

### Collaborations and Interviews

*   **Extensive Interview Portfolio:** Alan Cross has interviewed a vast array of influential artists globally, including U2, Depeche Mode, R.E.M., Gordon Downey, Eddie Vedder, Robert Smith (The Cure), Bruce Dickinson (Iron Maiden), Noel and Liam Gallagher, Daniel Lanois, Sum 41, Billy Corgan, Courtney Love, Beastie Boys, Foo Fighters, Morrissey, Peter Hook (Joy Division/New Order), Paul McCartney, and Ringo Starr.
*   **"In Their Own Words" Series:** A favorite recurring series aims to bring multiple band members into the studio to recount their collective history. Featured bands include Sloan, Kings of Leon, Franz Ferdinand, Our Lady Peace, Billy Talent, Matthew Good, Finger Eleven, USS, Arkells, Alexisonfire, Headstones, The Trews, and 54.40.
*   **The Four Steps to a Great Interview:** Alan Cross adheres to a structured approach to secure candid interviews:
    1.  **Propriety:** Exhibiting proper, respectful behavior without being overly familiar or rude.
    2.  **Competence:** Demonstrating thorough knowledge of the artist and their work.
    3.  **Comfort:** Allowing the artist to relax and lower their guard.
    4.  **Trust:** Building a rapport where the artist feels safe to share anything. He stresses the need to achieve these steps quickly.
*   **Bono's Charisma:** Cross describes Bono as a uniquely charismatic interviewee who makes him feel like "the only person in the world that matters," talking endlessly with "wonderful, flawed phrases" and the "soul of an Irish poet." Bono's interviews often conclude with him still talking as he's being escorted out.
*   **David Bowie's Unforgettable Encounter:** In 1990 (pre-*Ongoing History*), Cross had a 30-minute interview with David Bowie during his Tin Machine era. Overwhelmed by star-struck awe ("This is Bowie... Thin White Duke..."), he couldn't remember his questions or Bowie's answers and famously forgot to unpause his tape recorder. Years later, he recounted the story to Bowie, who listened intently, placed his hands on Cross's shoulders, and remarked, "Well, that was stupid, wasn't it?"
*   **Artist Demeanor:** Cross observes that "the bigger the interview subject, the better that they seem to treat you," citing examples like Gord Downie offering to get him coffee, Angus Young making him tea in the early 80s, and John Paul Jones offering tea.
*   **Trent Reznor's Vulnerability:** Reznor provided one of Alan's most honest interviews in 2005, discussing his struggle with addiction, reaching a "fork in the road" of "death by my own hand or... fix myself," and his moment of clarity to turn his life around.
*   **Happy Mondays and "Madchester":** Drummer Gaz Whelan shared the anecdotal origin of the "Madchester" term, which was coined by video director Sue Ladson for an EP, and then popularized by the press after a band member jokingly named a track "Rave On." Whelan also humorously noted he lived in Burlington, Canada, because his wife saw a real estate show.
*   **Liam Gallagher's Persona:** Cross prefers Liam Gallagher to be "a jerk, just like you want him to be," noting that politeness would be unexpected and undesirable for that specific rock personality.
*   **Gord Downie's Poetic Ambiguity:** When asked about song meanings, Downie would often redirect, asking, "What does it mean to you?" believing interpretation was the listener's role.

This show was "oddly... canceled."

### Trivia and Anecdotes

*   **April Wine Fandom:** Cross admits to being a "giant April Wine fan," a preference he had to keep "very, very quietly" hidden at an alternative radio station in the mid-90s, where liking non-alternative music could lead to ostracization or even firing.
*   **North Korea "Visit":** Alan jokes that he has technically "been to North Korea" by stepping across the conference table line in one of the negotiation huts in the South Korean DMZ.
*   **Regretful Conspiracy Theory:** Cross expresses regret over dedicating episodes to the "Kurt Cobain was murder" conspiracy theory, acknowledging in retrospect it was "conspiratorial garbage" after becoming friends with Courtney Love and understanding the reality.

### Personal Philosophy and Future Outlook

*   **Love for the Job:** Alan Cross expresses a deep love for his work, stating he has "no idea... what I do otherwise" and will continue the show "as long as somebody will ask me to do it and pay for it."
*   **Continuous Evolution:** The show and podcast are committed to "continue to evolve and bob and we belong with whatever is happening in music."
*   **Aspirations:** Cross harbors aspirations for the show to be carried by more stations (especially in the US) and to potentially spin off into a TV series, despite acknowledging the complexity and past dead ends.
*   **Listener Gratification:** His core motivation remains to enrich the listener's life by providing fascinating details that empower them to share knowledge with friends, thereby giving them "validation."
