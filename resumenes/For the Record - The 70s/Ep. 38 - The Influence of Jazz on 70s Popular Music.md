## songs
**Spinning Wheel (1969)** : This song was written by David Clayton Thomas two years before he joined Blood, Sweat & Tears, and was initially rejected in his native Canada for being "too jazzy" and hard to sell. It became a perfect fit for the band, noted for its "horns forward jazz" style, and was one of the biggest hits of 1969. The lyrics discuss letting go of troubles, not conforming, and allude to the 1960s psychedelic movement, without being overly intellectual or vacuous. "The song Spinning Wheel does not require too much intellectually but it's not completely vacuous either."

## curiosities
**First Rock Band on Vegas Strip** : Blood, Sweat & Tears became the first rock band to play on the Las Vegas strip, performing six sold-out shows in three nights at Caesar's Palace in 1969, breaking attendance records previously held by Frank Sinatra. "it is within this environment that blood sweat and tears took to the stage at Caesar's Palace in Las Vegas for six shows in three nights in 1969. All of the shows sold out and that is what blood sweat and tears were accused of being by the underground rock press sell out."
**Accused of Being "Sellouts"** : Despite breaking attendance records and achieving commercial success, the band was accused of being "sellouts" by the underground rock press, a perception that intensified due to their later involvement with the US government. "All of the shows sold out and that is what blood sweat and tears were accused of being by the underground rock press sell out."
**Band's Musical Identity** : Al Kooper originally created the band with a vision of it being a rock band, but other members perceived it as a jazz band. David Clayton Thomas stated that without his R&B and soul influences, the band would have been "just a jazz band," asserting his role in making it more of a rock band. "Clayton Thomas said that without him it was just a jazz band but he brought in this R&B and this soul to make it more of a rock band."
**David Clayton Thomas' Departure (1972)** : David Clayton Thomas left the band for three years in 1972, citing the overwhelming touring schedule as the reason. While he received songwriting royalties, most other band members relied solely on touring for their income, which perpetuated the continuous touring. "Clayton Thomas said that he left the band for three years in 1972 because the touring was too much while he was getting songwriting royalties."
**Controversial Cold War Tour (1970)** : In the summer of 1970, during the height of the Cold War, the band embarked on a tour of communist countries including Poland, Romania, and Yugoslavia, after the US State Department offered Canadian David Clayton Thomas permanent residency in the United States in exchange for their participation. The band was surprised by censorship and directives to play more jazz and less "rhythm" because it was deemed to be "inciting rioting." "It was the summer of 1970 and the Cold War is still hot and the band went on a tour of communist countries, Poland and Romania and Yugoslavia."
**Accused of Being Nixon Administration Collaborators** : Despite having protested the Vietnam War and played a concert at Kent State after the May 4th, 1970 shootings, the band was seen as "sellouts" by anti-war activists like Abby Hoffman for working on behalf of the federal government and the Nixon administration due to their Cold War tour. "They reviewed as sellouts by the likes of anti-war activists like Abby Hoffman for working on behalf of the federal government and the Nixon administration."
**"What the Hell Happened to Blood, Sweat & Tears" Documentary** : A documentary, with the working title "What the Hell Happened to Blood, Sweat & Tears," was announced in November 2020. It promises to offer "humor and fresh insight" into the "strange, never-before-told story" of the band's entanglement with the Nixon administration and their controversial Iron Curtain tour, which placed them "in the crossfire of a polarized America." "It was announced in November 2020 that the documentary, at least with the working title, I don't know what it will end up with, what the hell happened to blood, sweat and tears is going to offer, I quote, humor and fresh insight into this strange, never-before-told story of a tangle with the Nixon administration, a controversial tour behind the Iron Curtain that put them in the crossfire of a polarized America."

**Sir Duke (1977)** : An R&B tribute to Duke Ellington with deep jazz roots, released as a single in 1977 from the album *Songs in the Key of Life*, this song reached number one on the Billboard Hot 100 on May 21st and stayed there for three weeks. Its lyrics honor jazz greats like Basie, Miller, Sachmo, and Ella, and its drum rhythm subtly references "Take the A-Train," with Stevie's chromatic scale playing echoing jazz styles. Stevie Wonder composed the song to show appreciation for musicians who leave a lasting legacy. "I knew the title from the beginning. But wanted it to be about the musicians who did something for us. So soon they are forgotten. I wanted to show my appreciation."

Originally conceived with a big band arrangement, it was crafted with layered rhythm tracks, vocals, and horns, including Tom Scott on sax, aiming for a "tight, romantic, and Duke Ellington Cloudfield" backdrop. ## curiosities
**Bard College Origins** : Walter Becker and Donald Fagen met as students at Bard College, located about 90 miles north of New York City, where they discovered their shared appreciation for jazz and Beat poets like Jack Kerouac and Allen Ginsberg. "This is a decade after Walter Becker and Donald Fagan met while students at Bard College about 90 miles north of New York City."
**Band Name Origin** : The band's name, Steely Dan, was derived from "Steely Dan," a steel dildo mentioned in *Naked Lunch*, a book by the Beat poet William Burrows. "They would name their band after a steel dildo mentioned in a book by another beat, Naked Lunch by William Burrows."
**Resistance to Jazz Label** : While their love of jazz influenced many of their songs, Steely Dan resisted being labeled as "jazz rock" or "jazz fusion." Walter Becker stated that they played "rock and roll" but strived for the "ongoing flow, that lightness, that forward rush of jazz" that makes their music "swing." "Becker said, we played rock and roll, but we swing when we play. We want that ongoing flow, that lightness, that forward rush of jazz."
**Complex and Cryptic Lyrics** : Steely Dan's lyrics are often described as more complex and cryptic than typical 1970s rock songs, a characteristic noted by critics like Michael Duffy, who found them "pleasantly obtuse and cynical." "Their lyrics are a lot more complex and some might even say cryptic than the typical rock songs of the 1970s."
**Blending Rock Planning with Jazz Improvisation** : Becker and Fagen wrote both the words and music for their songs, carefully planning the arrangements as is typical in rock, but they also deliberately left room for soloists to improvise, thus integrating an essential element of jazz into their polished sound. "They wrote both the words and the music of their songs, but they left room for soloists to do their thing and to improv."
**"Crimson Tide" Reference in "Deacon Blues"** : Walter Becker clarified that the reference to "Crimson Tide" in "Deacon Blues" held no specific meaning to the band beyond representing "the exaggerated grandiosity that's bestowed on winners," poking fun at the idea. "Becker said Crimson Tide didn't mean anything to us except the exaggerated grandiosity that's bestowed on winners."

# genre - Jazz

## curiosities
**Commercial Struggles in the 1970s** : Despite being deeply embedded in American history, jazz faced significant commercial challenges and a decline in popularity at the start of the 1970s, failing to achieve the same commercial success as other genres, particularly rock and roll. "As a commercial art form, jazz already was in a bit of trouble when the 70s began."
**Impact of Jazz Legends' Deaths (1970s)** : The 1970s saw the deaths of numerous jazz legends, including Louis Armstrong, Duke Ellington, Jean Crouppa, Charlie Mingus, and Stan Kenton, which contributed to the perception of the genre's decline in popularity, though this was not the sole cause. "It would be too simple to say that it's decline in popularity was because so many of the jazz legends died in the 70s. Louis Armstrong, Duke Ellington, Jean Crouppa, Charlie Mingus, Stan Kenton, the list goes on."
**Musicians Rejecting Jazz Association (Mid-60s to Mid-70s)** : According to jazz critic Gary Giddin in a 1979 Village Voice article, musicians actively rejected being associated with jazz between the mid-1960s and mid-1970s. Herbie Hancock described 1960s jazz as "complicated, atonal and difficult, if not impossible to sing," making it non-participatory for audiences unlike rock. "between the mid 60s and mid 70s musicians were rejecting even being associated with jazz."
**Loss of Connection with the Common Fan** : In 1977, jazz musician Paul Mitchell observed that jazz had "lost touch with the common music fan" because musicians began to move "a little far from things that the laymen could understand," and jazz musicians "stopped entertaining." "musicians started getting a little far from things that the laymen could understand for a while jazz musicians stopped entertaining."
**A Challenge and a Risk** : Noted jazz critic Gary Giddin characterized jazz in a 1979 Village Voice article as "a challenge to the listener and a risk to the performer," suggesting it demanded a high level of engagement and effort from its audience. "Gidden said in that village voice article that jazz was, quote, a challenge to the listener and a risk to the performer."
**Reflection vs. Participation** : Herbie Hancock posited that jazz invited reflection from its listeners, a contrast to rock music which invited participation, making rock more appealing and capable of fulfilling both roles in the 1970s. "Herbie Hancock suggests that rock music invited participation while jazz invited reflection."
**Essence of Freedom and Improvisation** : The fundamental characteristic of jazz is its embrace of freedom and improvisation, a quality that many musicians sought to evoke even when creating rock or pop music for commercial viability. "The very essence of jazz is freedom in improvisation, but they are also making rock or pop music because they want to make some money."

# genre - Rock

## curiosities
**Accessibility and Popularity** : Rock music achieved widespread popularity in the 1970s because it was perceived as "easy" and participatory, allowing audiences to dance to it, unlike the more complex and less accessible jazz of the era. "Rock was popular because it was easy. You could dance to rock, but not to jazz of the period."
**Critical Recognition as "Serious Music"** : During the 1970s, music critics began to take rock music seriously, treating it with the same intellectual rigor previously reserved for classical music and jazz. This elevated rock to the status of the "serious music fans' genre," though it sometimes led to debates among critics about genre definitions. "In the way that music critics took classical music and jazz music seriously at one time, they began to take rock music seriously. Rock became the serious music fans of genre."
