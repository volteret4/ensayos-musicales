## curiosities
**Festival Express Performance - Toronto (June 27, 1970)** : Janis Joplin delivered an electrifying performance in Toronto, described by Rolling Stone writers David Dalton and Jonathan Cott as a "Bacchanalian little red riding hood with her bag full of tequila and lemons lurching from car to car like some tropical bird with streaming feathers." Canadian writer Bill Damper noted that her one-hour set changed the value of the $16 admission, generating enough personality and electricity to light up the stadium. "It was worth that much and more to see in here Janice Joplin, Long Haired, Swivel Hipped, Janice Joplin singing with the insistence and power of a pile driver and generating enough personality and electricity to light up a stadium all by herself."
**Emotional Center of the Train** : On the Festival Express, Janis Joplin was described by those present as the emotional center of the train, constantly singing, drinking heavily, and laughing loudly, appearing "free, not concerned." "Janice is described by those who were there as kind of the emotional center of the train."
**Jamming with Rick Danko** : Film footage from the Festival Express captures a very intoxicated Janis Joplin jamming with an equally intoxicated Rick Danko from The Band on guitar. This footage is poignant given Joplin's impending death, but also shows the simple pleasure of music creation. "The film footage of a very intoxicated Janice jamming with a very intoxicated rick denko from the band on the guitar is hard to watch in some ways because well, we know what is coming for Janice very soon."
**Influence on Other Musicians' Drinking** : Bob Weir of the Grateful Dead noted that Janis Joplin was able to turn the members of the Grateful Dead and other "heads" into "good night Irene singing drunkards" on the Festival Express. "only Janice could have turned the dead and other assorted heads into a bunch of quote good night Irene singing drunkards."
**Heroin Overdose Death (October 1970)** : Janis Joplin tragically died of a heroin overdose three months after her participation in the Festival Express tour. "Now we do know that three months after this, of course, Janice died of a heroin overdose."

## albums
**Workingman's Dead (1970)** : Released approximately two weeks before the Festival Express tour departed, this album marked a shift towards a folk rock approach for the band, featuring more concise songwriting and stripped-down arrangements. "They released Working Men's Dead about two weeks before the train left the depot."
**American Beauty (1970)** : Released about four months after the Festival Express, this album further solidified the Grateful Dead's transition to a folk rock sound, leaning into Americana traditions and producing radio hits like "Uncle John's Band" and "Sugar Magnolia." Richard Williams of Melodymaker described the band's sound as "disarming" and "beautiful," praising their ability to "steal your head through a combination of musicality and sheer enjoyment." "They released Working Men's Dead about two weeks before the train left the depot and then American Beauty about four months after that."

## songs
**Uncle John's Band (1970)** : A radio hit from `Workingman's Dead` or `American Beauty`, released in 1970, which showcased the band's new folk rock direction. "We get radio hits from these, we get Uncle John's Band."
**Sugar Magnolia (1970)** : A radio hit from `Workingman's Dead` or `American Beauty`, released in 1970, exemplifying the band's Americana-influenced folk rock sound. "we get Sugar Magnolia."
**Friend of the Devil (1970)** : Written by Jerry Garcia, Robert Hunter, and John Dawson, this song was originally intended for New Riders of the Purple Sage before being released on the Grateful Dead's `American Beauty` album in November 1970. Robert Hunter, who wrote a significant portion of the band's lyrics, considered it their closest thing to a classic song, notable for its tension between dramatic lyrics about a fugitive and an upbeat tempo, featuring Jerry Garcia's instantly recognizable acoustic guitar lines. Later in the 70s, live versions often took on a slower, heavier, and more retrospective character, demonstrating how the song could evolve. "Robert Hunter says that Friend of the Devil is the closest thing to a classic song that the Grateful Dead ever had, we take that ungood authority."

## curiosities
**Formation and Early Influence (1965)** : The Grateful Dead formed in 1965 in the Bay Area and were a focal point of the 1960s psychedelic scene. "The Grateful Dead formed in 1965 in the Bay Area and they were obviously a focal point of the 60s psychedelic scene."
**Festival Express Experience** : Bob Weir noted that there was little sleeping but much drinking on the Festival Express train, a new experience for the band members who typically used LSD and pot. The booze was also reportedly dosed with psychedelics, leading them to "achieve liftoff." They ran out of alcohol before reaching Winnipeg, prompting an unscheduled stop to clear out a liquor store. "Bob Weir of the Grateful Dead said that there was really not a whole lot of sleeping on this train but there was a lot of drinking which was something that he and his bandmates were as he put it new to."
**Shift to Folk Rock in the 1970s** : As the 1970s approached, the Grateful Dead shifted from sprawling studio experiments and psychedelic rock to a folk rock approach, characterized by concise songwriting, storytelling, stripped-down arrangements, and a focus on Americana traditions like Appalachian, Bakersfield Country Sound, and Front Porch Folk storytelling. "But as the 70s approached, they were taking more of a folk rock approach. We don't have any more of these sprawling studio experiments. Instead we have very concise songwriting, we have storytelling, we have stripped down arrangements."
**Robert Hunter's Songwriting Recognition (1994)** : Robert Hunter, who wrote almost all a significant portion of the Grateful Dead's songs but never played an instrument for them, was inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame alongside the band in 1994, acknowledging his crucial role. "Robert Hunter wrote almost all a significant portion of the Grateful Dead songs. He did not play a note for the band, but his role as songwriter is so important that when the dead were inducted into the rock and roll hall of fame in 1994, he was inducted along with them."

# artist - Buddy Guy

## genres
Blues
Modern Rock

## albums
**A Man and His Blues (1968)** : This album features Buddy Guy's cover of "Money (That's What I Want)," which further signaled the direction rock music would take. "He recorded it in 1968 for his album A Man in His Blues, which is another signal about what is to come for rock music."

## songs
**Money (That's What I Want) (1968)** : A cover of the song originally recorded by Barrett Strong for Tamla in 1959, written by Janie Bradford and Berry Gordy, which became Motown's first hit in 1960 and was notably covered by The Beatles in 1963. Buddy Guy's electrified version was recorded in 1968 for his album `A Man and His Blues` and was performed to electrify crowds on the Festival Express tour. "Buddy Guy electrified the crowds on the festival Express tour with his own cover of Money."

## curiosities
**Influence on Modern Rock** : Buddy Guy is considered a foundational figure of modern rock music, directly influencing artists like Jimi Hendrix. He pioneered techniques like distortion, string bending, and theatrical stage presence, which were radical in the early 1960s. "Buddy guy isn't just a great blues guitarist, which he is. He's part and parcel of the foundation of modern rock music."
**Theatrical Stage Act** : Known for his spectacular stage act, Buddy Guy would walk into the audience mid-solo, play behind his back, and perform in seemingly impossible positions, generating intense blues agony. Mike John, writing for The New York Times in December 1968, described his act: "He strikes a particular note and rises to wrap himself around it. His eyes elevated to the sky in blues agony. He plays one handed behind his back over his head in positions that seem impossible to even hold a guitar, let alone play it and play it well."
**Volume and Loudness** : Buddy Guy was known for playing very loud, a radical approach in the early 1960s. "Buddy guy also played loud, like really loud. In the early 1960s, this was kind of radical."
**Influence on British Musicians** : Young British musicians, including members of The Rolling Stones and Cream, paid close attention to Buddy Guy and other blues artists in the early 1960s, rediscovering American blues records. Eric Clapton continued to carry this influence into his solo career. "Young British musicians were paying very close attention to what buddy guy and other blues artists were doing."
**Festival Express Sobriety and Observation** : Buddy Guy claimed to be mostly sober during the Festival Express, while many others were heavily intoxicated with various substances. He recalled others attempting to share their substances with him and taking his whiskey when the train ran out of booze. He also uniquely recalled fans jumping on and off the train at stops. "On that train, everybody was messed up but me. I still was drunk a little cogniac because I'm still nervous when it comes to playing."
**Commercial Struggles and Resurgence** : While many rock stars he influenced achieved great commercial success, Buddy Guy's career commercially stalled in the 1970s. However, his career experienced a turnaround with a renewed interest in blues in the late 1980s and early 1990s. "Unfortunately, while many other rock stars that he influenced would go on to have great commercial success, that was not true for him and his career kind of stalled out at least commercially speaking in the 1970s."
**Ongoing Career** : As of the recording, Buddy Guy is 90 years old and still touring. "As I record this, Buddy Guy is 90 and I most recently saw him at the end of sinners that I won't say anything more in case you have not seen that movie."

# concert - Festival Express

## curiosities
**Concert Concept** : The Festival Express was a traveling rock festival in the summer of 1970, conceived by Canadian public relations legend Frank Duckworth to combine the "romance of traveling by train" with "rock and roll, which is the most vital form of communication today." Canadian promoter Ken Walker and Thor Eaton further developed the vision, aiming to cut costs by moving bands, crews, and equipment together on a 14-car Canadian National train. "Woodstock was a treat for the audience, but the train was a treat for the performers."
**Documentary (2003)** : The `Festival Express` documentary, released in 2003, captured long-lost audio and film footage from the event. It is noted for its raw, unpolished nature, with cameramen reportedly as intoxicated as the performers. "In the 2003 documentary, Festival Express, Mickey Hart of the Grateful Dead said, Woodstock was a treat for the audience, but the train was a treat for the performers."
**Not a Financial Success** : Despite its stacked lineup, the Festival Express was not a money-maker. What began as a business decision quickly evolved into a cultural experiment. "Was there great music? Yes. A rolling rock jam? Yes. A money maker? No."
**Initial Planned Route** : The tour was planned to go from Montreal, across Canada, to Vancouver. "It was supposed to go all the way to Vancouver."
**Montreal Cancellation** : The first show, scheduled for Montreal, was canceled due to conflicts with an annual festival in the city. "The first show was supposed to have been in Montreal, but the city cancelled the show due to some conflicts with an annual festival."
**Toronto Protest (June 27, 1970)** : The first actual show took place in Toronto on June 27, 1970. Protesters, fueled by anger following the Kent State shootings and targeting the "establishment," objected to the $14 ticket price (equivalent to over $100 today), arguing music should not be for profit. Jerry Garcia intervened by offering a free mini-concert on a flatbed truck for approximately 6,000 people before the paid show, which 37,000 attended. "There was a potentially very dangerous situation when protesters tried to force their way into the venue. Jerry Garcia came to the kind of temporary rescue."
**Winnipeg Stop** : The next stop after Toronto was Winnipeg, with fewer than 5,000 people in attendance, partly due to the city's smaller population and some people avoiding potential trouble after the Toronto protests. "After the train left Toronto, the next stop was Winnipeg with fewer than 5,000 in attendance."
**Vancouver Cancellation** : The planned final stop in Vancouver was canceled at the last minute because no permits would be issued, primarily due to concerns about bathrooms and overall security. "Vancouver was a last minute cancellation. No permits would be issued due to concerns about bathrooms and overall security."
**Calgary Shows (July 4-5, 1970)** : Instead of Vancouver, the tour concluded in Calgary with two shows on July 4th and 5th. The mayor of Calgary demanded free admission for young people, calling promoter Ken Walker "Capitalist scum" for refusing, to which Walker responded by punching the mayor. "The mayor of Calgary demanded that the young people of Calgary that they should be allowed to see the show for free and then he called Ken Walker Capitalist scum for not agreeing to which Walker responded by punching the mayor in the face."
**"Temporary Society" on the Train** : Once musicians boarded the train, the boundaries between on-stage and off-stage dissolved. Without dressing rooms or green rooms, musicians like Janis Joplin, The Grateful Dead, The Band, Traffic, Buddy Guy, Delaney & Bonnie, and The Flying Burrito Brothers lived in close quarters, fostering spontaneous creativity. Sylvia Tyson of The Great Speckled Bird noted, "we left our egos at the station," leading to a unique "temporary society" where music happened whenever someone felt like playing. "What happened on the train is what made festival express legendary. There were no schedule for being creative, music just happened whenever someone felt like playing."
**Lack of Sleep and Abundant Alcohol** : Bob Weir of The Grateful Dead recalled that there was little sleeping but much drinking on the train. The musicians consumed so much alcohol that they ran out before reaching Winnipeg, requiring an unscheduled stop to clear out a liquor store. The booze was also reportedly laced with psychedelics. "There was so much drinking on this train that they ran out of booze before they made it to Winnipeg. So they stopped the train and cleared out a liquor store before rolling onward."

**Continuum of Eras** : Cultural shifts, including in music, do not happen instantaneously with calendar turns but rather evolve along a continuum, with events and ideas building upon each other. "Things happen on a continuum, events and ideas build on each other."
**Rock and Roll as Vital Communication** : In the early 1970s, rock and roll was considered the most vital form of communication. "rock and roll, which is the most vital form of communication today."
**Kent State Shootings (May 4, 1970)** : Weeks before the Festival Express's Toronto show, the Ohio National Guard's shooting of unarmed students at Kent State University fueled significant public anger towards the "establishment," influencing protests such as those against the festival's ticket prices. "It's June 1970 just weeks after the shooting by the Ohio National Guard of On-Arm students at Kent State University."
**The Future of Rock Music** : The 1970s saw a rapid transformation in music, with rock splintering into multiple definitions, the rise of mega-stars who retreated into isolation rather than pursuing collaboration, and a shift towards computer-based music creation, lessening the need for bands. "We're going to create mega stars who retreated to isolation rather than reaching out for collaboration. Does that sound like I am waxing nostalgic? I don't know, maybe I am."
**Collaborative Nature of Music** : The period was marked by a collaborative spirit in music, evidenced by artists borrowing songs and band members, such as Dave Mason's "Only You Know and I Know" being covered by Delaney & Bonnie. "it speaks to the collaborative nature of music borrowing songs borrowing band members."
**American Roots Traditions in 70s Rock** : The fusion of country, gospel harmonies, and blues—inspired by artists like Delaney & Bonnie—became central to 70s music, influencing bands like The Allman Brothers, The Band, and Lynyrd Skynyrd. "This song and the entire on tour album is going to help push rock back toward American roots traditions. A direction that became central to 70's music through artists like the Almond brothers, the band, Leonard Skinnerd, that fusion of country, gospel harmonies and blues that defined much of early 70's rock."
**Nostalgia for the American South** : The nostalgic country twang resonating in pop charts reflected a cultural yearning to escape the "corrupt present" by returning to a "virtuous past." However, this was often a nostalgia for an imagined past, as the American South in 1970 was increasingly resembling the rest of the United States, with malls becoming as common as mules. "tapping into nostalgic country really was nostalgia because the American south was starting to look a lot like the rest of the US in 1970."
