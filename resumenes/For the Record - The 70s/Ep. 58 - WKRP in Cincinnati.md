# genre - Freeform Radio

## curiosities
**Emergence and Purpose** : Freeform radio came into existence in the late 1960s and early 1970s following a U.S. law that prevented stations with both AM and FM frequencies from merely duplicating their AM programming on the FM band, as AM was then dominant. To fill the FM airwaves, disc jockeys often turned to rock music, particularly progressive rock, allowing them to play entire album sides. "Freeform radio came into existence in the late 60s and early 70s when the law was passed in the United States that stations with both AM and FM could not simply use FM to duplicate whatever they were playing on AM because AM was king at this time."
**Early Focus on Creativity** : In its initial phase, underground FM stations operating under the freeform model prioritized creativity over ratings, granting disc jockeys the autonomy to program their own music, leading to unique and experimental broadcasts. "Ratings were not really a major concern for those underground FM stations. The focus was much more on creativity."
**Shift Towards Commercialism** : Over time, freeform radio evolved as FM stations increasingly focused on ratings, profit, and demographic targeting (age, race, gender) to attract advertisers, leading to greater control over the music selection by DJs. "It would not take long for that to change as FM stations became much more interested in ratings, profit, and then controlling what DJs played. FM radio became very focused on demographics, age, race, gender, so that advertising could be more targeted to them."

# curiosity

**WKRP in Cincinnati - Unique Celebration of Music** : The television sitcom *WKRP in Cincinnati*, which premiered on September 18, 1978, was noted for its unparalleled celebration of music, especially in its portrayal of a struggling AM radio station's format change from "beautiful music" to rock and roll. The show's creator, Hugh Wilson, insisted on using actual songs by real artists to maintain authenticity. "The television sitcom which celebrated music unlike any other show had done before, maybe since maybe glee did something in terms of celebration of music that be on par but definitely not in the same way."
**WKRP - Music Rights and Syndication Challenges** : *WKRP in Cincinnati*'s commitment to using real music by real artists created significant financial hurdles for its syndication, as licensing costs for songs like Pink Floyd's dramatically increased from $3,000 for a short clip during original broadcast to $103,000 when rights expired, making authentic re-broadcast financially unfeasible. "It is just too expensive to license the music from the shows. The show could not afford bands in the first place like the Rolling Stones, the Beatles, Michael Jackson, but there were plenty of other top tier artists that they featured that were much more budget friendly."
**WKRP - Record Company Interaction** : Record companies engaged with *WKRP in Cincinnati* as if it were a legitimate radio station, supplying the production with band posters and merchandise, much of which was used directly on the set, demonstrating their recognition of the show's authentic approach to music. "Record companies began to treat the show like a radio station and they gave them banned posters and in the merch that they would give stations, a lot of which went directly onto the set."
**WKRP - Fictional Station's Realism** : When *WKRP in Cincinnati* first aired on September 18, 1978, the portrayal of a freeform AM radio station where DJs programmed their own music was largely fictional, as such stations were very rare, early freeform being more characteristic of FM progressive rock stations. "In reality, there were very few, if any radio stations like WKRP in existence when it first aired on September 18, 1978."
**WKRP - High Realism in Radio Portrayal** : Many 1970s radio professionals regarded *WKRP in Cincinnati* as one of the most realistic depictions of radio they had ever seen in television or film, a fidelity stemming from creator Hugh Wilson's personal experiences as an advertising salesman at Atlanta's WQXI. "There were people who worked in radio in the 70s who said that WKRP was as realistic as they had ever seen in a TV show or movie."
**WKRP - Dr. Johnny Fever's Character Arc and Popularity** : Dr. Johnny Fever (Howard Hesmond), initially Johnny Caravella, was a nomadic DJ who gained popularity despite being depicted as a "pothead" in subtle ways to bypass network censors due to the show's moderate ratings. Music journalist Ben Fontores recognized him as the show's most popular character in 1979, akin to Fonzie, appealing due to his "dark, mysterious, antihero weirdness" and embodying the "goof up in all of us." "The fever character has emerged as the show's most popular. He is WKRP's Fons, referring to Arthur Fons' rally in Happy Days."
**WKRP - Dr. Johnny Fever's Music Programming Freedom** : Howard Hesseman, as Dr. Johnny Fever, was granted creative freedom in selecting the music his character played, mirroring a real freeform radio DJ. This allowed music to serve as more than just background, actively scoring scenes, suggesting moods, or creating counterpoints to the narrative. "Like a real freeform radio DJ, Hesseman was allowed some freedom in programming the music that Johnny played. And with that, we can see that the music in WKRP was not just the backdrop."
**WKRP - Music as Essential Scene Scoring** : Music was frequently and intentionally used in *WKRP in Cincinnati* to "score the scene," whether for serious or humorous effect, enhancing the narrative. Examples include the use of Foreigner's "Hot Blooded" for Les Nessman's preparations, Earth, Wind & Fire's "After the Love Is Gone" to convey Bailey's disappointment, and Pink Floyd's "Dogs" during Johnny's on-air dozing. "I didn't have to be talking with someone else to make a joke. It could be about a piece of music, or I could be listening to a piece of music and let it score the scene, or suggest the mood, or counterpoint what was going on."
**WKRP - Network Conflict Over "The Who" Episode** : Hugh Wilson, *WKRP*'s creator, faced significant pushback from CBS network executives when he insisted on producing an episode about The Who's 1979 Cincinnati concert tragedy. The network, arguing against a comedy tackling such serious issues, initially refused, but after Wilson threatened to leave the next week's slot empty, they allowed taping for review, and the Cincinnati City Council unanimously approved its airing. "About two days into it, we're a guy from the network that we're going to do this show. Because they hated you and they hated the stuff that you're doing. You're a comedy stop taking on issues."
**WKRP - Venus Flytrap's Character and Social Commentary** : Venus Flytrap (Tim Reed) was a pioneering African American character on TV, whose backstory revealed him as Gordon Sims, a Vietnam War deserter hiding from the US Army until Mr. Carlson's encouragement led to his honorable discharge. As the smooth nighttime DJ, he balanced Johnny's rock edge. Reed also wrote the Season Two episode "Family Affair," addressing racial tensions through Venus dating Andy Travis's sister, culminating in both men striking a racist character to demonstrate Andy's evolving understanding, with Reed specifically rejecting a "white savior" narrative. "Venus was an interesting character. For one thing, he was one of the few African American characters on TV at that time."
**WKRP - Tim Reed's Experience with Hate Mail** : Actor Tim Reed, who played Venus Flytrap, received hate mail and required a bodyguard after a scene depicting his character kissing Bailey Quarters (Jan Smithers), highlighting persistent racial prejudice in America. "In real life, Tim Reed had to get a bodyguard because of the hate mail he received after a scene in which he kissed the white Bailey quarters who was played by Jan Smithers."
