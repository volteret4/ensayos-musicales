## songs
**Give Peace a Chance (1969)** : This song was recorded live on June 1st, 1969, in room 1742 of the Queen Elizabeth Hotel in Montreal, during John and Yoko Ono's second "bed-in" peace protest. They invited guests like Tommy Smothers (on guitar), beat poet Allen Ginsberg, and Timothy Leary to participate. It quickly became a worldwide anthem for peace. "All we all say is give peace to chance. If it sounds like that they are banging on random things in a hotel room, they are."
**Imagine (1971)** : Released on October 11th, 1971, this song became a single in the United States three months after the 26th Amendment was certified, reaching number three on the Billboard charts. It emerged as another global anthem for peace and was inspired by Yoko Ono's poetry from her 1964 book *Grapefruit*. While Lennon initially took sole songwriting credit, Yoko Ono finally received credit 48 years later in 2017. "In the middle of the night, imagine all the people living for today. Imagine there's no country, it isn't hard to do."
**Happy Xmas (War Is Over) (1971)** : Released in December 1971, this song was another peace anthem from John Lennon, coinciding with journalists taking note of the potential of reaching new, young voters at concerts. "That was also when he released Happy Christmas War is over."
## curiosities
**Marriage to Yoko Ono (1969)** : John Lennon married Yoko Ono in March 1969, and their honeymoon became a platform for political activism and peace protests. "He married Yoko Ono in March 1969, and John and Yoko used their honeymoon to stage a peace protest that they called a bed in."
**Bed-Ins for Peace (1969)** : As a form of peace protest, John and Yoko Ono staged "bed-ins" where they remained in bed for a week, giving interviews about peace. They first held one in Amsterdam and then another in Montreal. They were denied entry into New York to stage a third due to Lennon's prior marijuana arrest, but Lennon stated the "silly" nature was intentional for the protest. "It was essentially John and Yoko in bed for a week, giving interviews about peace."
**Ann Arbor John Sinclair Freedom Rally (1971)** : On December 10th, 1971, John and Yoko performed at a concert in Ann Arbor, Michigan, to support musician and activist John Sinclair, who was sentenced to 9.5-10 years for selling marijuana to undercover officers. The event, which also featured Stevie Wonder, Bob Seger, Allen Ginsberg, Bobby Seale, and Jerry Rubin, cost $3 for admission and was broadcast on Michigan radio. An article in the *New York Daily News* highlighted the event's "immediate inescapable impact" regarding the "potential power of the new young voter," and remarkably, John Sinclair was set free the following Monday morning. "John and Yoko performed at a concert in Ann Arbor, Michigan that was on behalf of Johnson Claire, a local musician and a political activist who happened to make the very unfortunate decision to sell marijuana to two undercover police officers."
**Planned Anti-Nixon Tour (1972)** : John Lennon planned an ambitious anti-Richard Nixon tour for 1972, intending to follow the President around the country and conclude with a three-day music festival outside the Republican National Convention in Miami. This plan, however, never materialized as Lennon's legal troubles and the attention of the FBI, instigated by Senator Strom Thurmond, led to a deportation attempt. "He thought, you know, maybe I could do a tour. An anti-Richard Nixon tour in 1972 and follow Richard Nixon around the country."
**Immigration Issues and Marijuana Conviction (1968/1972)** : John Lennon faced significant immigration challenges in the United States due to a marijuana conviction in England in 1968. This conviction was used by the US government as a reason to attempt his deportation, initiated in part by South Carolina Senator Strom Thurmond. This legal battle tied Lennon up in immigration court for a year and a half, preventing his planned anti-Nixon tour. "Lennon was not permitted into the country. He had a little bit of a problem with a previous arrest due to possession of marijuana."

# concert - The Midnight Special

## curiosities
**Origin and Purpose (1972)** : The Midnight Special was a late-night television music show created by Bert Sugarman, conceived in response to the 26th Amendment and the need to engage young voters. Its pilot, a 90-minute special hosted by John Denver in August 1972, explicitly encouraged young people to vote in the upcoming presidential election. "The Midnight Special was a show that featured live music by a wide variety of bands and musicians. And I mean live, not lip synced like American bandstand or Soul Train."
**Live Music Format (1973-1981)** : Unlike other music shows like *American Bandstand* or *Soul Train*, *The Midnight Special* featured live performances, not lip-syncing. It premiered as a weekly series on February 2nd, 1973, and ran for 450 episodes until 1981, covering diverse genres including rock, pop, country, and comedy. "The Midnight Special was a show that featured live music by a wide variety of bands and musicians. And I mean live, not lip synced like American bandstand or Soul Train."
**Impact on Late Night Television (1970s)** : Before *The Midnight Special*, networks typically signed off at 1 AM. Creator Bert Sugarman correctly predicted an audience for a late-night music show among young people returning from bars or concerts. The show moved up 30 minutes when Johnny Carson's show shortened, marking a significant shift in late-night programming. "This was an era, I mean before the Midnight special, when networks signed off at one o'clock in the morning."
**Diverse Lineup and Artist Exposure** : The show was known for its incredibly diverse musical lineup, featuring a "who's who" of music, including B.B. King, the Bee Gees, Marvin Gaye, Alice Cooper, Jose Feliciano, Glen Campbell, and comedians like Richard Pryor. It provided a crucial platform for many artists, with some bands, like The Doobie Brothers, making their television debut on the show. "I mean the guest list is a variable who's who of music? Baby King, the Bee Gees, Marvin Gaye, Alice Cooper, Jose Feliciano, Glenn Campbell, all sorts of great comedians, Richard Pryor was on Midnight special."
**Role of Wolfman Jack** : Wolfman Jack, a legendary DJ with a distinctive raspy voice, served as the MC for *The Midnight Special*. He played a key role in convincing rock musicians to perform live, calming their nerves about how they might sound without lip-syncing. He was a radio icon of the 1960s, appeared in the 1973 film *American Graffiti*, and was inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 1996. "Wolfman Jack had to convince some of these rock musicians to come out and play and to calm their nerves a little bit about how they might sound."

# curiosity

**26th Amendment (1971)** : Proposed by Congressman Jennings Randolph and ratified in just four months, the 26th Amendment was approved by Congress on March 23rd, 1971, and certified by President Richard Nixon on July 1st, 1971. This amendment lowered the national voting age to 18, making 11 million more people eligible to vote and leading to significant efforts to engage youth in politics. "on March 23rd, 1971, Congress approved the 26th Amendment to the Constitution and it was certified by President Richard Nixon on July 1st, 1971."
**"Old Enough to Die, Old Enough to Vote" Slogan** : This was the prominent slogan used by proponents of the 26th Amendment during the Vietnam War, highlighting the disparity that 19-year-old American soldiers could die in combat but not vote in most states where the voting age was 21. "Old enough to die, old enough to vote. That was the slogan for the pro 26th Amendment crowd during the Vietnam War."
**Music's Role in Youth Voter Engagement (1970s)** : After the ratification of the 26th Amendment in 1971, pop and rock music became a primary vehicle for reaching and encouraging 18-year-old voters. Public service announcements, benefit concerts, and politically-charged songs were widely used to mobilize this new demographic. "In the early 70s, you met them in music, pop and rock music."
**Musicians Campaigning for Specific Candidates (1972)** : The 1972 presidential election marked a significant shift where A-list musicians moved beyond general calls to "go vote" and explicitly campaigned and raised money for a specific candidate, George McGovern, demonstrating a new level of political engagement in popular music. "It was the first time that our A-list musicians did not just say go vote, but they said go vote for this candidate."
**FBI Surveillance of Musicians (1970s)** : The FBI's attention to John Lennon, initiated by South Carolina Senator Strom Thurmond in 1972 due to Lennon's political activism and songs, highlights the extent to which national politics were moving to the right in the 1970s and the government's concern over celebrities' potential to mobilize political causes. "The fact that the FBI followed John Lennon around because of some of his songs and because he was a rather outspoken, acted this, but celebrity, I guess that'd be probably the most accurate way to describe him. Shows you a couple of things."
**George Lucas's *American Graffiti* (1973)** : This 1973 movie, directed by George Lucas, featured Wolfman Jack playing a fictionalized version of himself, showcasing the cultural prominence of radio DJs and the practice of dedicating songs on the radio during the 1970s. "Wolfman Jack appeared on the big screen in the George Lucas movie American graffiti in which he played a fictionalized version of himself."
