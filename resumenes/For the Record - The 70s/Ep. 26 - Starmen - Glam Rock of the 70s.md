## curiosities
**American Glam Rock Acceptability** : Alice Cooper's brand of glam rock made the genre acceptable for American audiences, distinguished from British glam by its "butch manly" presentation. David Bowie noted that Cooper's use of guillotines, fireworks, muscle, and metal offered a less ambiguous sexual bent that was more readily accepted in places like Ohio, contrasting with the "snippily" British approach. "Before an apt, we were bookended by Alice and Kiss butch manly glam with lots of guillotines and fireworks, muscle and metal."
**"Alice" Persona Description (1974)** : In a 1974 interview, Alice Cooper clarified that "Alice" is a dangerous persona he cannot embody constantly. He described the band as "all American kids" who reflect America's current attitude, acting as a social mirror that can be frightening to parents. He emphasized not taking a serious standpoint or preaching, opting instead for entertainment that incorporates "hard rock" with theater, asserting that the old "jam" style of rock groups from the 60s was "dead." "Alice is a, you know, I can't be Alice all the time because he's a little bit too dangerous."

## albums
**The Rise and Fall of Ziggy Stardust and the Spiders from Mars (1972) - Rock Opera** : This rock opera was released in the late spring of 1972 and details the story of Ziggy Stardust, an omnisexual rock star sent to Earth five years before its demise due to resource depletion. Ziggy is a messenger from the Starman, tasked with delivering a message of peace and love, but he is ultimately destroyed after believing too much in his own importance as a prophet. The album was added to the National Registry of the Library of Congress in 2016 for its historical and cultural significance. "Ziggy Stardust is an omnisexual rock star who is sent to Earth five years before it dies due to the people of Earth depleting its natural resources."

## songs
**Starman (1972)** : This song served as the lead single from *The Rise and Fall of Ziggy Stardust and the Spiders from Mars* and was David Bowie's first hit since "Space Oddity" in 1969. Its success earned Bowie a performance spot on Top of the Pops, where he appeared with short-cropped hair and a multicolored jumpsuit, famously flirting with guitarist Mick Ronson and shocking parents while thrilling young audiences. The lyrics are written from the perspective of someone listening to Ziggy on the radio, hearing "hazy cosmic jive." "Here's a Starman. Waking up the sky."
**All the Young Dudes (1972)** : Originally intended for Bowie's *Ziggy Stardust* album, this song was instead offered to Mott the Hoople when Bowie learned they were breaking up, after they rejected his initial offer of "Suffragette City." It became Mott the Hoople's signature song. The track explores themes of electricity loss and kids no longer wanting rock music, with Ziggy delivering "no news" to them. Despite lines like "Lucy looks sweet because he dresses like a queen," often interpreted as a gay anthem, Bowie denied this intention, but acknowledged that art is open to interpretation. Written and produced by Bowie, it became a Top 40 hit in the United States in November 1972, reached number three in the UK, and was recognized by the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 2004 as one of the "500 songs that shaped Rock and Roll." "All the young dudes was written and produced by David Bowie."

## curiosities
**Patron Saint of Glam** : Music critic Jim Farber, who experienced a sexual identity crisis in the 1970s, described David Bowie as "the patron saint of glam," noting that Bowie's presence in pop culture at that time created a safe space for personal expression. "David Bowie was the patron saint of glam and I cannot argue with that."
**Filmmaker Ambition, Musical Output** : David Bowie, interviewed by Cameron Crowe when Crowe was 19, revealed he had never considered himself a musician, aspiring instead to be a filmmaker. He described his musical creations, particularly rock operas like *Ziggy Stardust*, as "film on records," indicating his cinematic approach to music. "He wanted to be a filmmaker and ended up making film on records."
**Lack of Formal Musical Training** : Bowie did not possess formal musical training, unlike many of his contemporaries. This was viewed as a positive attribute, as it allowed him to avoid the constraints of conventional musical "rules." "Sometimes it helps to not be constrained by what you think the rules are."
**Artistic Philosophy - No Message** : In an interview with Cameron Crowe, Bowie stated he had "no message whatsoever" and "nothing to say," offering no suggestions or advice. He aimed to "suggest some ideas that will keep people listening a bit longer," hoping listeners would derive their own meaning and "come up with the message and save me the work." He accepted his role as an entertainer who did not take himself seriously, comfortable with personal interpretations from his audience, such as those by Jim Farber and other gay kids seeking representation. "I have no message whatsoever. I really have nothing to say, no suggestions or advice."

## curiosities
**Glam to Punk Bridge** : Roxy Music is recognized as a bridge between glam rock and punk, a genre that gained significant popularity by the end of the 1970s. Their music was often described by critics as more sophisticated than that of bands like T. Rex or Sweet, and it also resonated with 80s new wave fans. "Roxy Music was kind of a bridge between glam and punk, which was very popular, of course, at the end of the 70s."
**Early Live Performance Critique (1973)** : Music critic Tony Palmer, reviewing a Roxy Music live show in the spring of 1973 after the release of two albums, called their albums "overproduced." However, he described the live performance as "demonic sinister apocalyptic, monstrous, dazzling flashy. What opera might have been in the 1970s had it not lost its nerve." Palmer's review also included a warning against the band resorting to gimmicks or selling out. "The group was, and I quote, demonic sinister apocalyptic, monstrous, dazzling flashy."
**Pioneers in Reinventing Rock** : Roxy Music is credited with taking glam rock a step further and, in many ways, reinventing rock music, challenging the notion that glam was merely about theatrics. An article in *The Economist* posited that the only reason the band isn't praised as much as David Bowie for their historical contributions is that their debut album was released on the same day as Bowie's *Ziggy Stardust*. "Roxy Music took it a step further, and in many ways reinvented rock."
**Rock and Roll Hall of Fame Induction (2019)** : Roxy Music, including Brian Eno and Brian Ferry, was inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 2019, a considerable time after David Bowie's induction in 1996. "They got into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 2019."
**Contemporary Critical Reception** : In the 1970s, critical reception for Roxy Music was sharply divided, described as a "love it or hate it" situation. John Wadsworth, writing for *The Daily Tar Heel* in 1974, offered glowing praise, calling Roxy Music "the mating of the electronic with the lucid rock in the dimensionless bed of the avant-garde" and comparing Brian Ferry's voice to "divine impulses that have blessed our senses with William Blake and Sun Ra." "no earthly voice compares to Brian Ferry's."
**Theatrics as Entertainment** : Brian Ferry echoed the sentiments of Alice Cooper and Mark Bolan regarding the band's hair, makeup, and theatrics, stating that these elements were merely an "exciting part of the show" and a form of entertainment, not the entirety of their artistic output. "He essentially said that it was just an exciting part of the show, that it wasn't everything but that it was entertainment."

# genre - Glam Rock

## curiosities
**Birth and Popularity** : Glam rock emerged and became popular in the early 1970s, though its peak period was relatively short. British music critic Simon Reynolds, who grew up in early 70s England, perceived glam as synonymous with pop music. "Why was Glam rock born? Why was it so popular? If only for a short period of time."
**Definition and Aesthetic** : Glam rock, or glitter rock as Americans called it, was defined less by a specific rock style and more by the theatricality of its live performances. It was characterized by men in sparkles, sequins, spandex, feather boas, and high heel boots, pushing the gender-bending flamboyance initiated by Little Richard to its limits. The visual aspect was observed for its own sake, aiming to shock with costumes and stage antics. "The thing that makes glam glam or glitter rock as many Americans called it is not so much the style of rock as it is that theatrix that deliver the rock in a live performance."
**Social and Sexual Commentary** : Emerging barely post-Stonewall, glam rock challenged traditional notions of masculinity within rock music, creating a gender-bending brand of rock and roll. While not all glam stars were gay, the movement made space for non-heterosexual identities and reflected a societal sexual identity crisis. "Rock music was and is the domain of masculinity but glam rock challenged what that even means."
**Critical Disdain (1970s)** : Many rock critics of the era disliked glam rock. Mike West, writing for the *Daily News Journal* and *Murphy's Pro Tennessee* in 1974, described glam's greatest attraction as its "decadence," with "veiled reference to bisexuality, violence and falloness." He criticized the lack of musical substance, vividly picturing a 17-year-old boy in six-inch plastic heels, black stockings, a gold lame jumpsuit, pink hair, and heavy makeup, suggesting only The Who and The Allman Brothers Band still made "music." "glam rocks greatest attraction lies in its decadence."
**American vs. British Glam** : David Bowie distinguished British glam from American glam, noting that British glam rock struggled to impact Middle America to the same extent as artists like Alice Cooper and Kiss. He described American glam as "butch manly glam with lots of guillotines and fireworks, muscle and metal," lacking the sexual ambiguity of British acts, which was the "only way Ohio could accept lipstick on males." "British glam rock never made an impact on Middle America to any extent."
**Transition to Hair Metal** : By the late 1970s, glam rock began evolving into 80s hair metal, demonstrating that like many 70s genres, it did not die but was "reborn as something else." "By the late 70s Glam is on its way to becoming 80s hair metal."
**Societal Mirror and Safe Space** : Glam rock is seen as a mirror of society, reflecting and creating a safe space for changing viewpoints on gender and sexual identity. The theatrical packaging, though sometimes criticized as inauthentic, was compared to the rhinestone Nudie suits of male country artists, suggesting that authenticity is a personal relationship between the listener and the music. "If popular music really is a mirror of society As I believe it is Then Glam was not only a method of expressing Some of our changing viewpoints It created this safe space for it."

# curiosity

**The Rocky Horror Picture Show (1975) - Glam Rock Musical Film** : This film, a cult classic that started as a 1973 stage musical, is highlighted as arguably the best use of glam rock in a stage production or movie. It features Tim Curry as Dr. Frank-N-Furter, a transvestite scientist from Transsexual, Transylvania, who creates a Frankenstein-like being named Rocky while dressed in spangled women's lingerie, hosting a castle full of bizarre guests and a drag show. The film's energy largely derives from its music, and it became a phenomenon of audience participation, complete with costumes, prop throwing, and the "Time Warp" dance. Nell Ward, an English teacher, compared it to a "modern Greek chorus" and noted its significance in providing a common cultural touchstone for young people seeking identity. "I don't think that there is a stage production or movie that made better use of glam rock than Rocky Horror Picture Show."
**Rocky Horror Picture Show - Cultural Impact** : The film fostered a unique form of audience participation, with attendees dressing in costumes, throwing rice during Brad and Janet's wedding scene, and performing the "Time Warp" in front of the screen. For many young people, especially those feeling marginalized, it provided a sense of community and a safe space for self-expression. Nell Ward, an English teacher in Phoenix in 1980, observed that it gave kids in smaller cities something in common with those in Los Angeles and New York, offering a more engaging alternative to aimless cruising. "A lot of people went for fun, but a lot of other young people went to find their people."
**Rocky Horror Picture Show - Soundtrack Importance** : The soundtrack for *The Rocky Horror Picture Show* is considered as essential to the film's overall impact as its costumes. Tim Curry, who played Dr. Frank-N-Furter, stated in a 1974 interview that the film's energy largely "comes from the music." While the entire film is influenced by glam, not every song is purely glam rock; for example, Meat Loaf's performance of "Hot Patootie - Bless My Soul" is an homage to 50s rock, whereas "Sweet Transvestite" is described as "glam all the way." "While the entire film is influenced by glam, not every song was, like a meatloaf's performance of Hot Petudi, Bless My Soul, that's more of an homage to 50s rock."
**Rocky Horror Picture Show - "Sweet Transvestite" (1975)** : Sung by Tim Curry to Barry Bostwick's Brad and Susan Sarandon's Janet, this song is presented as a quintessential glam rock anthem from *The Rocky Horror Picture Show*. It is described as "every bit as balzy and sexy and gender-bending as anything performed by Bowie or Mark Bolan," perfectly embodying the gender-fluid spirit of the genre. "I'm just a sweet trans bestite from trans sexual transovader."
