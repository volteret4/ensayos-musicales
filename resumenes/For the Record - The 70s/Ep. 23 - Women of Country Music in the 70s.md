# artist - Genie C. Riley

## genres
Country
Gospel

## labels
Plantation
MGM

## concerts
Grand Ole Opry

## songs
**Harper Valley PTA (1968)** : Genie C. Riley, at 22, recorded this song written by Tom T. Hall, which became a smash hit due to its sassy narrative about small-town hypocrisy. It debuted at number 81 on the Billboard Hot 100 in September 1968, rapidly climbing to number 1 by September 21st, sharing the top 10 with artists like The Beatles, Steppenwolf, and Aretha Franklin. It also spent three weeks at number 1 on the Billboard Country Chart. The song is notable for its modern take on a traditional folk song, with a rebellious "I'm not going to take your bullshit" vibe, and serves as a time capsule with references to mini skirts, Peyton Place, and "Sock It To Me." "Harper Valley PTA debuted at number 81 on the Billboard Hot 100 in September 1968 and one week later it jumped up 74 spots to number 7."

## curiosities
**Image vs. Authenticity Struggle** : Despite singing a song about a strong woman, Genie C. Riley faced a cruel irony where her producer, Shelby Singleton, heavily controlled her image. Singleton transformed her into an "over-sexed hillbilly girl" trope, making her wear mini skirts on stage and even shortening a floor-length gown she intended to wear to the 1968 Country Music Association Awards. This exploitation extended to promotional appearances where her husband was forbidden from accompanying her when he tried to intervene. "It is this cruel irony that she was singing a song about a strong woman but she was not permitted her own authenticity."
**Transformation to Gospel Singer** : After years of struggling for control over her image and career, Genie C. Riley broke her contract with Shelby Singleton's Plantation label and moved to MGM. By the end of the 1970s, she had become a gospel singer, performing in full-length gowns, an image she presumably controlled herself. "Eventually, Riley broke her contract with Singleton and went to MGM to try to assert some control over her own career and her life and by the end of the decade she was a gospel singer who performed in these full-length gowns and presumably was now the person who had taken some control of her image."
**Harper Valley PTA - Movie and TV Show** : The hit song "Harper Valley PTA" inspired a movie starring Barbara Eden, known from "I Dream of Jeannie." In 1980, it was further adapted into a TV show. "Harper Valley PTA also inspired a movie starring Barbara Eden... and then in 1980 it became a TV show."

Its popularity was immense, but Gentry proved to be more than a one-hit-wonder. "It is hard to overstate the popularity of Ode de Billy Joe, but Gentry was no one hit wonder."
**Fancy (1969)** : Written and recorded by Bobby Gentry in 1969, "Fancy" was a modest country and pop crossover hit in 1970. Gentry considered it her strongest statement for women's liberation, addressing themes of survival and using one's circumstances to rise above them. The song tells the story of a poor teenage girl pushed into prostitution who ultimately uses the situation to her advantage. "Fancy was a modest country and pop crossover hit in 1970. It was released in 1969 and made into the charts in 1970 for Bobby Gentry."

## curiosities
**Early Life and Musical Education** : Bobby Gentry hailed from Mississippi but was not the "hillbilly" stereotype the industry tried to impose on her. She studied music in California from the age of 13. "And even though she was from Mississippi, she was not the hillbilly that the industry tried to make her into. She had studied music in California starting at the age of 13."
**Diverse Talents** : Beyond her songwriting and singing, Bobby Gentry was a DJ for Armed Forces Radio and is believed to have painted the portraits for her album covers, including "Fancy" and "Patchwork." "According to Tara Murtha who wrote an excellent book on Gentry in Ode de Billy Joe for the 33 in a third book series, Gentry was a DJ for Armed Forces Radio and she probably painted the portraits for the album covers for fancy and patchwork."
**Las Vegas Residency (1960s-1970s)** : For ten years, Bobby Gentry starred in critically acclaimed shows in Las Vegas. These performances were known for their over-the-top set designs, outrageous costumes often designed by Gentry herself, and stellar choreography, including a "gender-bending tribute to Elvis Presley" where she performed in a skin-tight, glittering pantsuit. "In for 10 years, she starred in shows in Las Vegas that were... critically acclaimed for over-the-top set design, outrageous costumes she often designed herself in stellar choreography, including a gender-bending tribute to Elvis Presley performed in a skin-tight, glittering pantsuit."
**Advocacy for Women's Liberation** : Bobby Gentry explicitly stated that her song "Fancy" was her "strongest statement for women's lib," expressing her agreement with the movement and its core issues such as equality, equal pay, daycare centers, and abortion rights. This was a powerful stance for a woman in 1970. "Gentry said, Fancy is my strongest statement for women's lib if you really listen to it."
**Retirement from Music (Late 1970s)** : Bobby Gentry abruptly walked away from her music career by the end of the 1970s. Her last known public appearance was on the Christmas episode of Johnny Carson's show in 1978, after which she ceased performing, giving interviews, or participating in retrospectives. "She was on the Christmas episode of Johnny Carson's show in 1978 and that was it. No more performing, no more interviews, no retrospectives, nothing. She just walked away."

## curiosities
**Southern Rural Femininity** : Loretta Lynn embodied a unique notion of southern rural femininity that transcended traditional expectations of submission and dress. "Loretta proved to be a very good businesswoman too and what she was able to do as the 70s progressed that other women in the industry at the beginning of the decade could not was take some control of her career."

Lynn personally selected Sissy Spacek to play her, and Spacek won an Academy Award for her performance. The movie itself also won the Academy Award for Best Picture. "Loretta hand selected the Sissy Spasak to play her in the movie and Spasak who is fabulous in the movie won an Academy Award and then the movie won the Academy Award for Best Picture."
**Shared Background with Dolly Parton and Tammy Wynette** : Like Dolly Parton and Tammy Wynette, Loretta Lynn was born poor in the rural South. All three achieved superstar status through their ability to sing and write relatable songs, and they proudly celebrated their roots. "All three were born in the south, all three were born poor and all three reached their superstar status because they could sing and write songs that people could relate to and they did it really, really well."

# genre - Country Music

## curiosities
**Dominance of Female Artists in 1970s** : In 1970s country music, there were 50 number one songs by women by 1978, a significant increase compared to 14 in the entire 1960s (eight of which belonged to Tammy Wynette alone). "Robert Hilburn, he wrote in 1978 that by that point there were 50 number one songs in country music by women and the decade wasn't even over yet."
**Race Problem** : Country music, similar to rock, has historically had and continues to have a "race problem" where opportunities and representation for non-white artists are limited. "There is no question that country music like rock has a race problem. I'm not saying had it still has a race problem."
**Storytelling Heart** : At its core, country music is a storyteller's genre, celebrating the ability to tell compelling narratives. This tradition stems from multicultural and multiracial origins, rooted in the traditional ballad or folk song introduced to the South by British immigrants, and incorporating rhythmic influences and instruments like the banjo from Africa, contributing to "hillbilly music." "At the heart of country music is the story. It is a story tellers genre."
**Blurring Lines with Pop (1970s)** : The sound of country music in the 1970s increasingly blurred the lines with pop, a trend seen clearly on the Billboard charts. This "country-politan" or "country-pop" sound, characterized by big orchestral arrangements, was intentional, designed to increase radio airplay and record sales for country artists. "Another piece of this puzzle is the changing sound of country music in the 70s which blurred the lines between country and pop."
**Image vs. Authenticity for Women** : Throughout the 1970s, female country singers faced challenges regarding their image, often being marketed as sex symbols (a "cruel trade-off" contrary to the feminist movement) or confined to archetypes like the "faithful wife" or "literal mother." The focus on their physical attractiveness, rather than solely their music, was a persistent issue not resolved in the decade. "The question of image and appreciation for the music without also referring to a woman's looks will not be solved in the 70s."
**"Good Old Boys Network" (Contemporary)** : The contemporary country music industry, particularly in Nashville, is still characterized as a "good old boys network," which contributes to the current gender inequality where female voices are underrepresented on radio. "Reba McIntyre said that none of that surprises her because Nashville is still a good old boys network"

# curiosity

**Angelie Perfume Commercial (Late 1970s-Early 1980s)** : The "Angelie" perfume commercial aired in the late 1970s into the early 1980s, aiming to market perfume to "the working woman." Its campaign celebrated the idea of women working outside the home while still fulfilling their domestic roles. The commercial was inspired by the 1962 song "I'm a Woman" by Jerry Lieber and Michael Stolar. "That's for a commercial for the perfume, Angelie, which aired in the late 70s into the early 80s."
**Women's Rights Movement - Second Wave Feminism (1960s-1970s)** : The second wave of the women's rights movement, or feminist movement, is widely credited by historians and sociologists as beginning with Betty Friedan's 1963 publication of "The Feminine Mystique." This movement, born in the 1960s and taking off in the 1970s, centered on the idea of choice and challenged traditional roles, inspiring progress and changing societal views that were felt even in country music. "Betty Friedan published The Feminine Mystique which many historians and sociologists credit with being the beginning of the second wave of the women's rights movement or even we could go ahead and say the feminist movement."
**Historical Legal Restrictions on Women's Rights (Pre-1970s)** : Before the mid-1970s, women faced significant legal and societal restrictions: they could be denied birth control until 1972, illegal abortion until 1973, and married women could be denied credit cards or bank loans without their husband's permission until 1975. Women also lacked equal job opportunities, could be fired for pregnancy, and were not allowed to serve on juries in all states until 1971. "Women could not freely choose to use birth control. They could be denied access to birth control up until 1972."
**USC Annenberg Inclusion Initiative Report (April 2019)** : A study by USC's Annenberg Inclusion Initiative in April 2019 highlighted persistent gender inequality in country music. The report revealed that only 16% of country artists and 12% of country songwriters are female, and none of the highest-selling female performers are over 40, contrasting with the majority of top-selling male artists who are over 40. "In that report it was revealed that only 16% of country artists are female and only 12% of country songwriters are women."
**Billboard Charts** : Both the Billboard country charts and the Billboard Hot 100 pop chart serve as key indicators of musical success and trends, particularly in the 1970s, where they showed a significant rise in female country artists and crossover hits. "Now just a glance at both the Billboard country charts and the Billboard Hot 100 pop chart in the 70s shows us that this was not always the case."
**Marketing of Women as Sex Symbols in Country Music** : The country music industry often marketed women as sex symbols, sometimes against their will, a practice seen as a "cruel trade off" that contradicted the feminist movement's ideals. This approach was common even for young artists like 13-year-old Tanya Tucker. "In fact the marketing of women as sex symbols whether they wanted it or not and most did not is completely contrary to what the feminist movement stood for. But that is exactly what country music did."
**Southern United States Rebellious Spirit** : The traditional southern United States possesses a rebellious spirit that, while historically leading to negative outcomes like the Confederacy and Jim Crow, can also be channeled into creating space for new storytellers and challenging traditional femininity, offering women an opportunity to tell their stories. "There is also this rebellious spirit of the traditional southern United States which taken to the extreme gave us the Confederacy and Jim Crow which followed that."
**Peyton Place** : The soap opera "Peyton Place" is referenced in Genie C. Riley's song "Harper Valley PTA" as a cultural touchstone of the late 1960s or 1970s. "It is also kind of a time capsule of the late 60s or 70s with its reference to mini skirts, the soap opera, Peyton Place and Socket To Me which was made famous on the variety TV show Laffin."
**Sock It To Me / Laugh-In** : The phrase "Sock It To Me," popularized by the variety TV show "Laugh-In," is referenced in Genie C. Riley's song "Harper Valley PTA," anchoring the song within its contemporary cultural context. "Socket To Me which was made famous on the variety TV show Laffin."
**Tara Murtha's "Ode to Billy Joe" Book (33 1/3 series)** : Tara Murtha authored an "excellent book on Gentry in Ode de Billy Joe" for the 33 1/3 book series, which is cited as a source for details about Bobby Gentry's diverse talents. "According to Tara Murtha who wrote an excellent book on Gentry in Ode de Billy Joe for the 33 in a third book series, Gentry was a DJ for Armed Forces Radio and she probably painted the portraits for the album covers for fancy and patchwork."
**Women's Limited Material Choice in Country Music (Pre-Late 1970s)** : Until the late 1970s, women in country music were often severely limited in the material they were allowed to perform and often lacked the autonomy to make these decisions for themselves, as exemplified by Cher being prevented from recording "The Night the Lights Went Out in Georgia" by Sonny Bono. "until we get into the late 70s, I would say women in country were often very limited in the material that they could perform. They may not even have been allowed to make that decision for themselves."
**Tyler, Texas Newspaper Article (1973)** : An article published in a Tyler, Texas newspaper in 1973, titled "Women Country Singers far removed from their counterparts," focused primarily on the physical attractiveness of singers like Connie Smith and Lynn Anderson rather than their musical craft, illustrating a prevalent issue of image over substance for female artists. "I want to read to you the beginning of an article from 1973 that was published in a newspaper in Tyler, Texas. The title of the article is, Women Country Singers far removed from their counterparts."
**National Organization for Women (NOW) (1966)** : The National Organization for Women (NOW) was co-founded in 1966 by Betty Friedan, Shirley Chisholm, Polly Murray, and Muriel Fox, playing a crucial role in the women's rights movement. "Friedan, Shirley Chisholm, Polly Murray, Muriel Fox, they all co-founded now the National Organization for Women in 1966."
