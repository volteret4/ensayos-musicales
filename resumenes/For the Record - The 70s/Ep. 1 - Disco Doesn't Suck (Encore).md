# artist - David Mancuso

## curiosities
**Loft as Pre-Disco Birthplace (1970)** : David Mancuso's loft at 647 Broadway in New York is regarded as the birthplace of the pre-disco era. Mancuso, an introverted man, opened his home to all, regardless of sexual orientation or race, inviting them to dance. "David Mankuso's loft in New York is probably the place where predisco is born."
**Valentine's Day 1970 Party** : On Valentine's Day, 1970, David Mancuso hosted a party at his loft. Invitations carried the message "love saves the day," aiming to create a safe space for people to enjoy each other's company and dance. "On Valentine's Day, 1970, David Mankuso opened up his loft at 647 Broadway, and he had a party. The invitations said, love saves the day."
**Atmosphere Creation** : Mancuso created atmosphere in his loft using music, lights, and even changes in temperature. He was unable to sell alcohol or food, and drugs were less common than at later clubs like Studio 54. The core vibe came from the music, an amalgamation of different styles united by a common beat. "Mankuso used music and lights and even changes in temperature to create atmosphere."
**Sanctuary for Marginalized Communities** : The Loft was the beginning of "members-only sanctuaries" that particularly attracted gay men, but also welcomed people of all races and genders, legitimizing the presence of the queer community. These spaces offered refuge where individuals could dance freely without fear. "The loft was the beginning of the members only sanctuaries that attracted gay men in particular, because it was places like the loft, the sanctuary, paradise garage that welcomed even legitimized the presence of the queer community."

In 2011, the Library of Congress added it to the National Recording Registry for its historical and cultural significance. "I Feel Love by Donna Summer was not just a disco breakthrough. # genre - Disco

## curiosities
**Underground Origins** : Disco music originated in New York's underground dance clubs, primarily catering to non-white and non-heterosexual people. "disco was in the dance clubs of New York, which catered to non white and non heterosexual people. It was underground."
**Confrontation with Rock Music** : The conflict surrounding disco began when it moved from underground clubs to mainstream radio, television, movies, and record stores. Rock music, which primarily served straight white men and took itself seriously in the 1970s, viewed disco's expansion as an affront to its established cultural space. "It is when disco moves from underground to radio, television, movies, your local tower records that the conflict begins because rock music took itself very seriously in the 1970s."
**Outlet for the Disenfranchised** : Bruce Schulman, a history professor at Boston University, described disco as "an outlet for the disenfranchised," serving as a refuge for gay, Latin, and African American people who felt uncomfortable or unwelcome in other public spaces in the early 1970s. "Bruce Schulman, a history professor at Boston University and the author of a book on 70s culture said that disco was and I quote an outlet for the disenfranchised."
**Legal Restrictions on Same-Sex Dancing** : In the 1960s, same-sex dancing was illegal for the queer community, meaning even the right to dance with a chosen partner was questioned, creating a need for safe spaces like early disco clubs. "Remember that for the queer community in the 60s, it was not just same sex sex that was illegal. That was also true for same sex dancing."
**Pre-Disco Era Defined** : Music journalist Vince Letty referred to a "pre-disco era" for music existing before the "disco" label was commonly used and before major phenomena like Donna Summer's rise or "Saturday Night Fever." "Vincileti, the music journalist who was without a doubt, the very first to take disco seriously refers to a predisco era."
**Club DJs as Hitmakers** : In the early disco era (before 1974-1975), club DJs, not radio DJs, were primarily responsible for making songs hits. They controlled the atmosphere on the dance floor and dictated the music flow without taking requests. "The DJs in the clubs were the ones who made a song a hit, not the radio DJs. In these clubs, DJ was king."
**Regional Scenes** : In the early period, disco music popularity was very regional, with different songs being "hot" in clubs in cities like Chicago, Philadelphia, or Los Angeles. "music that was hot in a club in Chicago might be different from music that was like hot in Philadelphia or Los Angeles. It was very regional at that time."
**First Billboard Disco Dance Chart (1974)** : In late October 1974, Billboard began publishing a Disco Dance Chart, which was compiled based on reports from club DJs and mirrored Vince Letty's weekly disco column in Record World. "Billboard began publishing a disco dance chart, which was compiled based on whatever the DJs were reporting."
**Record Company Investment (1975)** : By February 1975, record companies recognized the commercial potential of disco, beginning to issue remixes and extended LPs of club hits to capitalize on their popularity. "In February 1975, we can see that record companies are starting to capitalize on the club hits."
**7-Inch Records for DJs (1975)** : By 1975, record companies started producing 7-inch records specifically labeled "for disco DJs only," as this format was easier for DJs to work with. "Seven inch records that were labeled for disco DJs only were produced because they were easier for DJs to work with."
**"Four on the Floor" Dominance (1975)** : In the second half of 1975, "four on the floor" dance music became increasingly prevalent on the radio, with multiple hits in this style charting between late summer and New Year's Eve.
