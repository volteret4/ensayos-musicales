## curiosities
**Early Booking at CBGB (March 1974)** : Tom Verlaine approached Hilly Crystal to book a rock band, after which their manager, Terry Ork, "badgered" Crystal into booking Television repeatedly. Crystal initially thought they were "god awful" and "horrible," noting that they only played on Sunday nights, the worst night for live music. Despite this, they began attracting their own audience. "it was Terry Ork who badgered me into having television back time and time again because they were so god awful when they started."
**Band's Early Development** : Tom Verlaine and Richard Hell ran away from a private school in Delaware with the intention of becoming poets in New York. While Verlaine and Richard Lloyd played guitar in Television's earliest gigs, their sound was described as "a lot of raw noise," which was original but not necessarily good. "It was original yes, good, no, not that much."
**Tom Verlaine's Guitar Style Evolution** : Verlaine, who had jazz roots, initially avoided the guitar, considering it a "wimpy instrument." His perspective changed after hearing The Yardbirds, particularly guitarists like Eric Clapton, Jimmy Page, and Jeff Beck. Over time, he developed a unique style, even drawing inspiration from surf guitarists like Dick Dale to cultivate his sound. "Then hearing the yard birds changed that and I guess that would that would happen. Eric Clapton, Jimmy Page, Jeff Beck, hearing those guys will change your mind about the guitar."
**Influence and Scene Approval** : Over time, Television gained significant influence in the CBGB scene, with any band booked to play there needing their approval. Their manager, Terry Ork, was deeply committed to helping Television express their art, bringing his "art see friends" to CBGB and fostering an avant-garde artistic atmosphere. "Over time, any band that was going to play at CBGB had to get the approval of television."
**Opening for Peter Gabriel (1977)** : Following the critical success of *Marquee Moon*, Television opened for Peter Gabriel in 1977. This pairing, however, was not successful, as Television fans found Gabriel's theatrics excessive, while Gabriel's fans considered Television "cold and distant," highlighting a clash between two different rock genres. "Television fans thought that Gabriel's theatrics were too much and Gabriel's fans thought that television was cold and distant."
**Seven-Week CBGB Residency with Patti Smith (Spring 1975)** : For seven weeks in the spring of 1975, Television and the Patti Smith Group alternated as opening and closing acts, performing two sets a night, four nights a week. This period was instrumental in solidifying Patti Smith's band within the New York scene and helping them truly cohere as a musical unit. "For seven weeks in the spring of 1975 television and the Paddy Smith group alternated between being the opening act and the closing act two sets of night, four nights a week."

## curiosities
**Poetic Foundations** : Patti Smith identified herself as a poet first, sharing insights from her 1976 European tour about her inspirations. She confessed to struggling with grammar and extensively studied authors like Henry Miller, Mickey Spillane, Burroughs, and Rimbaud to learn sentence construction, while also studying T.S. Eliot for rhythm and music, alongside listening to Jajuca tapes, Berber tapes, and "the great primitives, American Rock and Roll." "Art was a great revelation to me and then all my life it's like still that freedom I had as a child."
**Rock and Roll Hall of Fame Induction (2007)** : Patti Smith was inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 2007 by Zach De La Rocha of Rage Against the Machine. De La Rocha lauded her for revitalizing rock music, noting that it "was getting too slick for its own good when Paddy Smith burst on the scene and tore it apart." "She shrieked and howled literate yet street savvy lyrics, unflinchingly confronting topics from religion to the beat movement."
**Gratitude to Clive Davis** : At her Rock and Roll Hall of Fame induction, Patti Smith sincerely thanked Clive Davis of Arista Records for giving her a chance, later posting a photo of them together on Instagram to express her continued appreciation. "On that night she thanked Clive Davis of Aresda for giving her a chance. I think she really meant it too."
**Hilly Crystal's Impression** : Hilly Crystal, the owner of CBGB, was deeply impressed by Patti Smith's charisma and performance, describing her as possessing "magnetism." He found her capable of staying on pitch, bending notes expertly, and communicating effectively, making her one of the few artists he could listen to repeatedly. "She may not have been a singer but she sure sounded like one. She stayed on pitch, she bent the notes just right, she sang real well, she communicated."
**Influence on Michael Stipe** : Michael Stipe, at 15, was profoundly influenced by Patti Smith after reading about her in *Cream* magazine and purchasing *Horses* on its release day. Stipe noted that punk rockers like Smith conveyed the idea that "you did not have to be special to start a band," a concept that particularly resonated with him. "He said that the punk rockers like Patty gave him the idea that they were not special And that you did not have to be special to start a band."
**Marriage and Retirement (1980)** : Patti Smith married Fred Sonic Smith, a member of MC5 and her collaborator in the 1980s, in 1980, shortly after the release of her album *Wave*. Following their marriage, she largely retired from public life to live with Fred and raise their children in Detroit. "They got married in 1980 Not long after Wave was released and then she kind of just retired From public life to go live with Fred and to raise their children in Detroit."
**Last Performance at CBGB (October 15, 2006)** : Patti Smith fittingly performed on the last night of CBGB, October 15, 2006. ## albums
**Horses (1975) - Commercial Breakthrough** : Patti Smith's commercial breakthrough album, released in 1975, was described as "poetry that was set on fire" and an "invocation." Produced by John Cale of The Velvet Underground, the record was characterized by a chaotic yet spiritual essence, with chaos being "kind of the point." It opened with a rewritten version of Van Morrison's "Gloria," featuring the line "Jesus died for somebody's sins but not mine," setting a tone of independence and transforming punk into art. "Horses wasn't really just music, it was kind of like this invocation. It opened with her rewriting Van Morrison's Gloria and she kind of turned it into this declaration of her own independence."
**Radio Ethiopia (1976)** : This album is mentioned in the context of Patti Smith's 1976 European tour, suggesting it had been released by then. Critic Robert Christgau later found a track from it, "seven ways of going and broken flag," to be "unlistenable and less interesting" than *Radio Ethiopia* as a whole, in comparison to *Wave*. "I find seven ways of going and broken flag As unlistnable and less interesting than radio etiopia."
**Easter (1978)** : Robert Christgau considered *Easter* "more generally satisfying" than *Wave*, implying it was a successful or well-regarded album in her discography. "But this is an often inspired album Quirk here, then the more generally satisfying Easter."
**Wave (1979) - Sonic Shift** : By her fourth album, *Wave*, released in 1979, Patti Smith exhibited a notable shift towards a smoother, more melodic sound. Produced by pop artist Todd Rundgren, the album, while still retaining anger and grit, presented these qualities beneath a "shiny, glossy" exterior, with Smith sounding calmer and less confrontational. It reached number 18 on the Billboard album chart. "By the time we get to her fourth album, Wave, there is this notable shift in her sound. In 1975, Patty Smith kind of kicked down the door of Rock and Roll with this album that sounded like poetry that was set on fire."

## curiosities
**Origin of Sound** : Tommy Ramone stated that the band's distinctive sound arose from a desire to play music that wasn't heard on the radio, leading them to create it themselves. "We used to sit around and listen to the radio and not hear anything like the stuff we like So we decided to play it ourselves."
**Aesthetic and Anti-Theatrics** : The Ramones' "stripped down aesthetic"—featuring shaggy hair, motorcycle jackets, worn jeans, and T-shirts (not torn for effect)—was a direct reaction to the "corporatization of rock" and the overt theatrics of artists like Elton John. "They were a reaction to the corporatization of rock And they had this stripped down aesthetic, the shaggy hair and the motorcycle jackets Worn jeans, t-shirts that were really worn and not just torn up for effect This was all a response to the theatrics of somebody like say Elton John."
**Dislike of Avant-Garde and Europe** : The Ramones actively disliked the avant-garde scene and demonstrated a strong aversion to European culture during a tour with the Talking Heads, disliking the food, only wanting to hear English, and refusing to leave their tour bus even to see Stonehenge. Danny Fields, their manager, famously compared taking them to Europe to "taking Archie Bunker to the Vatican," emphasizing their populist, not artistic, nature. "They were not part of the avant-garde scene at all, they did not like it."
**Hilly Crystal's Initial Impression** : Hilly Crystal of CBGB initially found The Ramones "pretty terrible" but noted that the crowd responded positively to their stripped-down style. He continued to book them because they generated a reaction and consistently brought their own equipment, which was beneficial as CBGB could not afford a house PA system. "my first impression of the Ramones was that they were just a very, very loud band They'd come in from Queens where nobody liked them."
**Intense Live Performances** : The Ramones were known for their exceptionally fast-paced live shows, often blazing through eight songs in approximately 17 minutes. Writer Norman Mailer likened watching them to "being taken out for a ride at 100 miles an hour" in an old car. "They were known to blaze through eight songs in say 17 minutes."
**CBGB as Unique Venue** : CBGB was the only bar in New York City willing to allow The Ramones to perform, highlighting the venue's commitment to original music and its openness to bands others rejected. "And CBGV was the only bar in New York that was going to let the Ramones take the stage."
**Influence in England (1976)** : Despite their dislike for Europe, The Ramones were well-received there, particularly in England. Their performance at the CBGV Rock Festival Showcase Auditions in 1975 introduced them to the British press, and their 1976 tour in England greatly influenced punk bands like Generation X, whose member Billy Idol considered them "brilliant." "When the Ramones went to England in 1976, a punk band called Generation X Which was started by someone called Billy Idol saw them play Billy Idol said he thought they were brilliant and it influenced how his band chose to play."
**Rock and Roll Hall of Fame Induction (2002)** : The Ramones were inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 2002 by Eddie Vedder, recognizing their immense impact on rock and punk music. "The Ramones were inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 2002 And they were inducted by Eddie Better."
**Joey Ramone's Passing** : Joey Ramone, a member of The Ramones, was among the deceased musicians honored by Patti Smith during her final performance at CBGB in October 2006, highlighting his legacy and connection to the venue. "She got up on the stage and she read off a list of musicians who had played there, who were no longer living Joey Ramone being one of them."

**Early Formation and Living Situation** : David Byrne (guitar, vocals), Chris Frantz (drums), and Tina Weymouth (bass) met at the Rhode Island School of Design before moving to New York. Weymouth had to audition three times to join the band. As young adults in their twenties, the original three members lived together in a loft apartment near CBGB, which provided a convenient space for practicing and developing material, where they "flubbed up it was only in front of 10 or 12 people." **Rejection of Punk Label** : Despite debuting at CBGB, a venue known for launching punk and new wave bands, the Talking Heads consistently denied being a punk band and expressed a desire to avoid labels altogether. Chris Frantz stated in 1978 that their appreciation for artists like KC and the Sunshine Band and Funkadelic was a key differentiator from punk groups like the Sex Pistols. "Having heads always insisted they were no punk band and really did not want a label at all France said in 1978 the big difference between us and punk groups is that we like KC in the Sunshine band in Funkadelic You asked Johnny Rotten if he likes KC and he'll spit in your face."
**Beyond Punk's Aggression** : Jerry Harrison acknowledged punk rock's rejection of 1970s rock overproduction but emphasized that the Talking Heads aimed to explore emotions beyond the anger and aggression characteristic of punk. Leslie Goldberg of the *San Francisco Examiner* noted their "Warhol habit of placing ordinary things from everyday life in unusual contexts," to which David Byrne agreed, stating that mixing things from different places creates art. "Harrison said that the band appreciates punk rock's rejection of and reaction to the overproduction of some 70s rock But unlike punk bands the talking heads tap into emotions that go beyond anger and aggression."
**David Byrne's Musical Journey** : David Byrne is described as a self-taught musician who, despite not being a "great singer," decided music would be his life, whether performing in his living room or for small crowds at CBGB. He was previously in a cover band called The Artistics that played at high school dances. "Bern is a self-taught musician and he's not a great singer but he loved it So he decided that music would be his life even if he was playing in his living room or for a crowd of less than a dozen people at CBGB."

**Debbie Harry's NYC Migration** : Debbie Harry, raised in New Jersey, always intended to move to New York, knowing the suburbs weren't for her. "Debbie Harry, who was raised in New Jersey, said she always knew that she was not going to stick around the suburbs and that she would migrate to New York."
**Band Name Origin** : The band's name, Blondie, was an homage to the "cat calls" Debbie Harry frequently received while walking through the streets of New York City. "The band name was an homage to the cat calls that Debbie Harry got as she walked through the streets of New York."
**CBGB Relationship** : Blondie had a "love-hate relationship" with CBGB; Debbie Harry described it as a "dark and smelly" place infested with rats and flies, but its proximity to her home (five or six blocks away) made it a convenient venue for regular performances. She wasn't fully aware of being part of a "movement" at the time, and found it hard to concentrate in the disgusting environment. "Blondie had a love-hate relationship with CBGB. It was dark and smelly with rats and flies on one hand, but it was five or six blocks from home and the band could play there on the other hand."
**Early Scene Development** : Clem Burke, Blondie's drummer, noted that much of the band's early development happened "by accident." He viewed the insular New York scene as an opportunity for bands, comprising about 100 people (85 in bands), to make mistakes publicly and learn. He considered CBGB their version of "the Cavern where the Beatles started." "Crucially, we could make mistakes in public, finding our way at those early shows. Even at the time I saw CBGB's club as our version of the cavern where the Beatles started."
**Unique Appeal of Debbie Harry** : Debbie Harry possessed a unique quality that set her apart from contemporaries; she wasn't a punk in the traditional sense, nor a Janis Joplin clone, and only shared "50s and 60s sensibilities" with The Ramones. Mike Chapman, her producer, recognized "there was just something about her that was different." "Anyone who watched her could see that there was just something about her that was different. She was not exactly punk, although the band Blondie had a bit of the 50s and 60s sensibilities that the remones had."
**Studio Challenges with Mike Chapman** : Producer Mike Chapman described Blondie as "tough in the studio," citing significant animosity among members (except for Chris Stein and Debbie Harry). He found them "juvenile in their approach to life," prioritizing fun over hard work. Debbie Harry admitted Chapman "drove me crazy" but also credited him with teaching her to "listen more carefully." "He said the Blondies, as he called them, were tough in the studio, real tough. None of them liked each other except Chris and Debbie and there was so much animosity. They were really, really juvenile in their approach to life. A classic New York underground rock band and they didn't give a fuck about anything. They just wanted to have fun and didn't want to work too hard getting it."
**Feminine Approach to Rock** : Debbie Harry, reflecting in 1976, expressed a desire to carve out a feminine space in the masculine rock and roll business, distinguishing her approach from Patti Smith's "masculine and intellectual" style. She aimed to "do something in it" as a girl. "Debbie said it was the other assholes that hung around. They always had something to say."
**Rock and Roll Hall of Fame Induction (2006)** : Blondie was inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 2006, recognizing their significant impact on music as they transitioned from the gritty CBGB scene to chart-topping success within four years. "Oh, by the way, Blondie was inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 2006."
**Joey Ramone's "Sell Out" Comment** : Chris Stein and Debbie Harry were unsure if Joey Ramone genuinely believed Blondie had "sold out" when their sound became more pop-oriented. Debbie Harry explained that the early punk scene was diverse, and genre labels were often applied retrospectively or based on the prevailing time period rather than the music itself. "Debbie and Chris are not sure that Joey Ramone really meant it when he said that they sold out."

# artist - Hilly Crystal

## member of
The Village Vanguard (former manager)

## curiosities
**CBGB Founding Vision (December 1973)** : Hilly Crystal, formerly the manager of The Village Vanguard, leased a former biker bar space in the Bowery, New York City, in December 1973. His original intent was for a venue featuring country, bluegrass, and blues music, which he named CBGB, adding OMFUG (Other Music For Uplifting Gourmandizers) to sound "uncouth or crude." "In December 1973, Hilly Crystal, the former manager of the village vanguard, he leased a space in the Bowery in New York City that was formerly a biker bar."
**Original Music Rule** : Crystal established a strict rule that all bands booked at his club must play only original music, stating, "originality to me was prime technique took second place." This principle guided CBGB's booking policies, even for bands he initially found unappealing. "Crystal had a very firm rule that any bands that were booked for his club could only play original music. He said originality to me was prime technique took second place."
**Early CBGB Environment** : CBGB's location in a "pretty awful" Bowery neighborhood, below a flop house inhabited by alcoholics and Vietnam veterans, contributed to cheap rent and a lack of complaints about loud music. Despite the unsanitary conditions, including dodging liquor bottles and dog poop, Crystal fostered a unique musical environment. "It was nothing to have to step over the bodies of guys who were passed out on the sidewalks. Of course, this all meant that rent was cheap and nobody was really going to complain about loud music."
**Skepticism Towards Television** : Crystal initially found Tom Verlaine's band, Television, to be "god awful" and "horrible" when they first started playing at CBGB, noting that their manager, Terry Ork, had to badger him repeatedly to book them. Despite his initial skepticism, their ability to draw an audience ensured their continued presence. "He kept saying, well, we'll have another band with them next time and we'll try some new things. It was always one thing or another thing to get them back."
**Patti Smith's Magnetism** : Crystal was deeply impressed by Patti Smith's stage presence, observing that "she just had magnetism." He praised her vocal abilities, noting that she "stayed on pitch, she bent the notes just right, she sang real well, she communicated," making her one of the few performers he could listen to repeatedly. "He said she just had magnetism. She may not have been a singer but she sure sounded like one."
**Ramones' Loudness and Self-Reliance** : Crystal's first impression of The Ramones was that they were "a very, very loud band" from Queens whom "nobody liked." He valued them because they consistently brought their own amplifiers and PA system, which CBGB could not afford. He recognized their hard work, self-belief, and their determination to "make themselves happen." "my first impression of the Ramones was that they were just a very, very loud band They'd come in from Queens where nobody liked them They worked very, very hard and played more and more."
**Enduring Vision and Death (2007)** : Crystal maintained his vision for CBGB as a venue for original music even as the punk scene evolved to feature "faster and angrier" bands like Murphy's Law and Bad Brains in the early 1980s. Despite legal battles and gentrification leading to the club's closure on October 15, 2006, Crystal died a year later in 2007. "Billy Crystal was not thrilled with all of it, but he kept true to his vision of CBGB being a venue for original music."

# artist - Mike Chapman

## curiosities
**Producer for Blondie** : Mike Chapman, a producer with credentials from working with Sweet and Suzi Quatro, was enlisted by Chrysalis Records to help Blondie achieve a hit after their first two albums failed to gain traction. He was responsible for adding "some polish" to the band's sound. "They were with Chrysalis records and they wanted Blondie to get a hit so they hooked them up with producer Mike Chapman."
**Challenging Studio Sessions** : Chapman described working with Blondie in the studio as "real tough," citing significant animosity among band members (except for Chris Stein and Debbie Harry). He found them "really, really juvenile in their approach to life" and focused on fun rather than hard work. "He said the Blondies, as he called them, were tough in the studio, real tough. None of them liked each other except Chris and Debbie and there was so much animosity."
**Influence on Debbie Harry** : Debbie Harry acknowledged that while Chapman sometimes drove her "crazy" due to his demand for multiple takes, she learned valuable lessons from him, particularly to "listen more carefully," a skill she found useful in acting as well. "I had a hard time doing a lot of takes, but I learned a lot from Mike too. I learned to listen more carefully, which isn't valuable in acting as well, where listening is more important than your own portrayal."

# genre - Punk Rock

## curiosities
**NYC Birthplace** : America considers CBGB in New York City to be the birthplace of its punk scene. "On today's episode we will examine the former biker bar in New York that America considers to be the birthplace of its punk scene, CB GB."
**Early Definition vs. Later Representation** : Debbie Harry noted that in its nascent stages, the punk scene was diverse, encompassing "a lot of different styles and a lot of different types of music." She observed that it was only later that the term "punk" narrowed to represent a particular kind of music, with genre labels often being applied based on the prevailing time period. "when it was happening, the punk scene embodied a lot of different styles and a lot of different types of music. It was only later that punk came to represent a particular kind of music."
**Reaction to Overproduction** : Punk rock emerged as a reaction against and rejection of the "overproduction of some 70s rock," favoring a stripped-down, aggressive sound. "Harrison said that the band appreciates punk rock's rejection of and reaction to the overproduction of some 70s rock."
**Evolution in the 1980s** : By the early 1980s, even as legendary bands from CBGB had moved on, the club adapted to a "new generation of punks" who were described as "faster and angrier and messier," with bands like Murphy's Law and Bad Brains using the tiny stage as their proving ground. "There was this new generation of punks that arrived. They were faster and angrier and messier."

# concert - CBGB

## curiosities
**Founding and Naming (1973)** : In December 1973, Hilly Crystal leased a former biker bar in the Bowery, New York City, intending to feature country, bluegrass, and blues music. He named it CBGB, adding OMFUG for "other music for uplifting gourmandizers," despite its crude initial appearance, and enforced a strict rule that only original music could be played. "In December 1973, Hilly Crystal, the former manager of the village vanguard, he leased a space in the Bowery in New York City that was formerly a biker bar."
**Harsh Environment, Unique Music** : The neighborhood surrounding CBGB was notoriously awful, located below a flop house and requiring patrons to step over passed-out individuals. Despite the grim conditions, including dodging liquor bottles and animal feces inside the club, it offered cheap rent and a unique opportunity to listen to music unlike anything else in New York. "You did have to dodge liquor bottles that fell from the windows from the stories above the club. You might have to wipe your shoe after stepping in the poop left behind by Crystal's dog, which had free access to the whole club."
**The Ramones' Exclusive Venue** : CBGB was the only bar in New York City that would allow The Ramones to perform on its stage, underscoring Hilly Crystal's commitment to booking bands that played original music, even if they were initially perceived as "terrible." "And CBGV was the only bar in New York that was going to let the Ramones take the stage."
**Blondie's Love-Hate Relationship** : Blondie's Debbie Harry described a "love-hate relationship" with CBGB, acknowledging its "dark and smelly" interior, complete with rats and flies, contrasted with its convenient location just five or six blocks from her home, which allowed the band to play there every weekend for seven consecutive months. "Blondie had a love-hate relationship with CBGB. It was dark and smelly with rats and flies on one hand, but it was five or six blocks from home and the band could play there on the other hand."
**Proving Ground for Bands** : Clem Burke of Blondie likened CBGB to "the Cavern where the Beatles started," viewing it as an essential proving ground where bands could make mistakes in public and refine their craft. The insular New York scene consisted of roughly 100 people, with about 85 being in bands, fostering a unique community. "Crucially, we could make mistakes in public, finding our way at those early shows. Even at the time I saw CBGB's club as our version of the cavern where the Beatles started."
**Evolution and Legacy (1970s-2000s)** : By the late 1970s, CBGB became a dangerous yet thrilling venue. While most legendary bands moved on by the early 1980s, it "mutated" to host a new generation of "faster and angrier" punks like Murphy's Law and Bad Brains. In the 1990s, it became a famous tourist destination, and despite its closure in 2006, its legacy as a site for risk-taking, community, and creativity endures, especially for early 80s new wave fans. "By the late 1970s, it was kind of dangerous and definitely thrilling for many people who got the opportunity to go there in New York."
**Legal Battles and Closure (October 15, 2006)** : Gentrification in the Bowery, with the rise of boutique hotels and luxury condos, led to legal battles with the landlord, the Bowery Residence Committee, which claimed Hilly Crystal owed tens of thousands in back rent. Despite benefit shows and public protests, CBGB closed on October 15, 2006, with Patti Smith performing a farewell. "This brought on some legal battles. The landlord for CBGB's, the Bowery Residence Committee claimed that Billy Crystal owed tens of thousands of dollars in back rent."
**Post-Closure Transformation** : After its closure and Hilly Crystal's death in 2007, the original CBGB space was transformed into a designer clothing store. "Now the original space, the last that I saw is a designer clothing store."
