# artist - Muhammad Ali

## albums

Round one was aptly titled "I am the greatest," featuring spoken-word poetry predicting his boxing prowess and impact. "I am the greatest. My Cashus Clay. This is the legend of Cashus Clay, the most beautiful fighter in the world today."

## songs
**I am the greatest (1963)** : This was the first track on his comedy album "I Am the Greatest," serving as a self-proclamation of his greatness and showcasing his distinctive "gift of gab" and confidence. "I am the greatest. My Cashus Clay."
**Stand By Me (1963)** : A cover of Ben E. King's popular 1961 hit, this song was included on Ali's "I Am the Greatest" album. "Cashus Clay's cover of Stand By Me, made famous by Ben E. King in 1961."

(1963)** : Featured as round eight on Ali's "I Am the Greatest" album, this track directly challenged heavyweight legend Sonny Liston through spoken word. **Cassius Clay (1973)** : A reggae song released in 1973, simply titled after Ali's birth name, reflecting his growing influence in popular culture. "There was a reggae song in 1973, simply called Caches Clay."

## curiosities
**Early Life & Ambition (1963)** : Born Cassius Clay in Louisville, Kentucky, he was interviewed for a Time magazine cover story at 21 years old in 1963. He expressed grand ambitions for his future, including driving down Walnut Street on Derby Day and being recognized as an Olympic champion and aspiring heavyweight champion. "I'm going to drive down Walnut Street in a caddy on Derby Day. And all the people will point and say, there goes Cashus Clay."
**Victory over Sonny Liston (1964)** : Ali, then Cassius Clay, defeated Sonny Liston on February 25, 1964, in Miami. Liston was widely considered one of the toughest and meanest fighters in boxing history and was managed by the mafia. "Clay did beat Sonny Liston, who by all accounts is one of the toughest meanest fighters ever to step in the ring."
**Conversion to Islam & Name Change (1964)** : Six months after his victory over Sonny Liston, Cassius Clay announced he had converted to Islam and changed his name to Muhammad Ali, a decision met with significant speculation and suspicion. "Cashus Clay announced that he was a Muslim and his name was now Muhammad Ali."
**Liston Rematch & Controversy (1965)** : Ali beat Sonny Liston again in a rematch in May 1965, held in Lewiston, Maine. This unusual location for such a high-profile fight was chosen because very few cities wanted to host Ali or his fights following his announcement of converting to Islam. "In May 1965, Ali beat Liston in a rematch in Lewis and Maine of all places."
**Draft Resistance & Conviction (1965-1970)** : Ali initially failed his United States Army induction test due to low writing and spelling scores, but his draft status was revised in November 1965. Citing his opposition to the Vietnam War, Ali refused induction at the Houston induction center, stating, "I will not go if draft it." He was subsequently charged and convicted of draft evasion, stripped of his heavyweight title and boxing license for three years, and faced five years in prison. "He was eventually charged and convicted of draft evasion. He was stripped of his heavyweight title. He was stripped of his licensed box for the next three years and he was facing five years in prison."
**Dick Cavett Show Appearance (1970)** : On May 25, 1970, Ali appeared on The Dick Cavett Show, where he famously articulated his reasons for draft resistance. When asked about going to jail, he connected his personal sacrifice to the broader struggle of black people, stating, "So why should I worry about going to a little old jail to free my poor people who've been catching hell here for 400 years?" This statement received significant applause from the studio audience. "So why should I worry about going to a little old jail to free my poor people who've been catching hell here for 400 years?"
**Divisive Figure (1970s)** : In the 1970s, Muhammad Ali was an incredibly divisive figure in America. Many people "simply hated him" and would watch his fights hoping he would lose, a sharp contrast to his later image, such as lighting the Olympic torch in Atlanta in 1996 while suffering from Parkinson's disease. "A lot of people simply hated him and watched his fights just hoping he would lose."
**Return to Boxing (1970)** : In September 1970, a federal judge restored Ali's right to fight, ending his three-year boxing ban. Despite California Governor Ronald Reagan's refusal to allow a "draft Dodger" to box in his state and similar opposition from Georgia's governor, a fight was approved in Atlanta by its mayor. "In September 1970, a federal judge restored Ali's right to fight."
**Jerry Quarry Fights (1970, 1972)** : Ali's return to the ring on October 26, 1970, saw him defeat Jerry Quarry by technical knockout. He later won a rematch against Quarry in 1972 in Las Vegas. "The fight between Ali and Jerry Quarry was on for October 26th. Ali won it on a technical knockout and the same for the rematch in 1972 in Las Vegas."
**Loss to Ken Norton (1973)** : On March 31, 1973, Ali suffered only his second career loss to Ken Norton, during which Norton also broke Ali's jaw. "including a match in which he lost to Ken Norton, only the second loss of his career, and Norton also broke his job. That was March 31st, 1973."
**Victory over Joe Frazier (1974)** : In January 1974, Ali defeated Joe Frazier, a pivotal win that positioned him for a shot at the heavyweight title. "Ali beat Joe Frazier in January 1974, he had his eye on the heavyweight title."
**Underdog Status for Rumble in the Jungle (1974)** : At 32 years old, Ali was widely regarded as a significant underdog against the heavyweight champion George Foreman for the Rumble in the Jungle. This perception helped transform him from a controversial figure into a national treasure, with even his corner man, boxing legend Archie Moore, expressing fears that Foreman might kill Ali. "And you would have been hard pressed to find anyone who believed that Muhammad Ali at the age of 32 could beat the heavyweight champion, George Foreman."
**Mobutu's Favor (1974)** : For the Rumble in the Jungle, Muhammad Ali received $5 million to fight in Zaire and was flown to the country on President Mobutu's personal plane, highlighting the preferential treatment he received from the Zairian dictator. "They didn't get to fly into Zaire on Mobutu's personal plane the way Ali did."
**Overloaded Flight to Zaire (1974)** : Ali's plane journey to Zaire was so heavily laden with James Brown's musical equipment that it "barely made it off the ground," illustrating the significant logistical challenges and the volume of gear transported for the music festival. "Ali crammed onto a plane that was so overloaded with James Brown's gear that the plane barely made it off the ground."
**Rumble in the Jungle Victory (1974)** : In the iconic Rumble in the Jungle fight in Zaire, Muhammad Ali knocked out George Foreman in the eighth round, reclaiming the heavyweight championship of the world in 1974. This victory, timed for US television, cemented his comeback as an underdog and further solidified his status as potentially "the most famous black man in the world." "Muhammad Ali knocked out George Foreman in the eighth round and reclaimed the heavyweight championship of the world."
**Later Career & Retirement** : Ali later lost and subsequently reclaimed the heavyweight title a third time in fights against Leon Spinks. These later fights occurred "long after he probably should have retired to preserve his health," despite his continued ability to compete at an elite level. "He would lose it again and reclaim it at third time, both in fights to Leon Spanx long after he probably should have retired to preserve his health."

## songs
**The Thrill Is Gone (1970)** : This song, the last track on his 1969 album "Completely Well," became B.B. King's breakthrough pop hit at the age of 45, reaching number 15 on the Billboard Hot 100 in 1970. Instantly recognizable by its opening notes from his guitar, Lucille, it earned him a Grammy for Best R&B Male Vocal in 1970 and was inducted into the Grammy Hall of Fame in 1998. It was a highlight of his eight-song set at Zaire 74, captivating the crowd including Muhammad Ali. "The thrill is gone made it to number 15 on the Billboard Hot 100 in 1970."

## curiosities
**Career Decline & Crossover (Late 1960s)** : In the late 1960s, B.B. King's career stalled as younger black music fans gravitated towards soul music, viewing the blues as outdated. His career revived dramatically when he played to a predominantly white, "hippie" audience at the Fillmore West in San Francisco in 1967, receiving a standing ovation before even playing. This resurgence was further aided by rock stars like Eric Clapton publicly acknowledging his profound influence on rock music. "By the late 1960s, BB King's career was grinding to a halt."
**Influence on Electric Guitar** : Music journalist Ralph J. Gleason, in his liner notes for B.B. King's 1969 album "Completely Well," attributed King with effectively inventing the electric guitar for practical purposes, underscoring his widespread and foundational impact on rhythm and blues and rock and roll music. "He is truly the man who for all practical purposes invented the electric guitar."

## curiosities
**Zaire 74 Involvement** : Bill Withers accepted an invitation to perform at Zaire 74 from Gary Stromberg, the festival's PR representative. He viewed it as a "once in a lifetime opportunity" to interact with prominent figures like Muhammad Ali, George Foreman, and writers Norman Mailer and George Plimpton, having previously met Ali in 1967. "Whither's first met Muhammad Ali in 1967 after Ali had refused induction into the army. And so he said he would do it."
**Critical Acclaim (1973)** : By 1974, Bill Withers was a highly respected artist. A 1973 review of his "Live at Carnegie Hall" concert in the Pittsburgh Courier by Greg Mims lauded his songwriting for portraying "real things, real people and honest emotions." Years later, Questlove described Withers as "the closest thing black people have to brew spring steam." "The world's boldly sketched in Whither's song lyrics are populated by real things, real people and honest emotions."
**Unconventional Path to Stardom** : Bill Withers' journey to becoming an R&B star was atypical; he hailed from a coal mining town in West Virginia, a place that inspired "Lean On Me." He developed an interest in music during his time in the Navy and was working as an airplane mechanic at McDonnell Douglas in California in 1971 while submitting demos. The cover of his debut album, "Just as I Am," famously features him at his mechanic job, holding his lunch box, a candid moment teased by his co-workers. "The cover of Just as I Am, the album that has Aino Sunshine and Grand Maws Hands, is a photo of Bill Whither's holding his lunch box at McDonald Douglas where he worked."
**Breakthrough via Johnny Carson (1971)** : After being laid off from McDonnell Douglas, Bill Withers received two significant letters: one inviting him back to his job, the other inviting him to The Johnny Carson Show. His appearance on The Tonight Show in November 1971 was instrumental in launching his career, as "Ain't No Sunshine" broke into the Billboard Top 10, enabling him to quit his mechanic job at the age of 33. "The other was inviting me on the Johnny Carson show. It was that appearance on the Tonight Show in November 1971 that helped him quit working as an airplane mechanic because Aino Sunshine made it into the Billboard Top 10."
**Discomfort with Performing** : Bill Withers expressed a notable discomfort with performing for audiences, speculating that this might stem from not having grown up with the ambition to be a musician. "It's interesting to think about how uncomfortable Whither's was with performing for other people."
**Anticipation of Retirement (1970s)** : Throughout the 1970s, Bill Withers consistently indicated that he did not foresee himself as a lifelong musician. In 1978, he stated his aversion to being a "50 year old man out somewhere at 3 in the morning trying to entertain a bunch of half drunk people," emphasizing his desire for intellectual growth beyond being a "middle age rock and roller." "it wouldn't surprise me if at some point in my life I all of a sudden looked into something else."

## songs
**Soul Makossa (1972)** : This 45-single became a pivotal early disco hit in 1972, initially popularized by DJ David Mancuso at his Loft house parties and later by legendary WBLS Radio DJ Frankie Crocker, who gave it heavy radio rotation. Cited by music journalist Vince Aletti as the perfect example of the emerging disco sound, it demonstrated the power of DJs in breaking hits and highlighted the African roots of disco. "In 1972, Manucuso came across the 45 for Soul Macosa and he started playing it at his parties."
**Soul Makossa (1973)** : The single version of this song successfully entered the Billboard Top 40 in 1973, despite its chart performance being complicated by the proliferation of multiple bootlegged versions. "Soul Macosa's the single snuck into the Billboard top 40 in 1973."
**Soul Makossa (1972)** : The distinctive opening of Manu Dibango's "Soul Makossa" was famously incorporated by Michael Jackson into his 1982 song "Wanna Be Startin' Somethin'" from the "Thriller" album. "Michael Jackson borrowed that for want to be starting something in 1982."

# concert - Zaire 74

## curiosities
**General Information (1974)** : Zaire 74 was a three-day music festival held in Africa in conjunction with the 1974 boxing match known as the Rumble in the Jungle. The event, which included dozens of musicians from the United States and Africa, drew at least 80,000 people on its third night in Kinshasa and held significant symbolism about black power and African-American ancestral heritage. "dozens of musicians from both the United States and Africa performing for on the third night at least 80,000 people in Kinshasa."
**Political Context & Financing (1974)** : Zaire, now the Democratic Republic of the Congo, was led by the dictator President Mobutu, who was supported by the US for his anti-communist stance. Mobutu approved and helped fund the boxing match, providing $5 million each to Ali and Foreman, but did not fund the music festival, although musicians did receive half their pay upfront. "Mobutu gave his stamp of approval for the fight, which he agreed to help pay for and the accompanying music festival, which he would not help pay for."
**Festival Conception (1974)** : The idea for Zaire 74 originated with New Yorker Stewart Levine and exiled South African jazz musician Hugh Masekela, who envisioned it as an opportunity to unite African American and African performers under the theme "a return to the beat." "They saw this as an opportunity to bring together African American and African performers. They phrased it as a return to the beat."
**Intended Audience & Ticket Pricing (1974)** : The Zaire 74 festival was primarily intended for foreign tourists, not the local Zairian population. Tickets ranged from $3 to $24, a price well beyond the reach of the average person in Zaire, whose per capita income was approximately $100 according to The New York Times. "Tickets ranged from $3 to $24 but that was well out of the price range for the average person living in Zaire."
**Fight Postponement & Free Entry (1974)** : The boxing match's postponement for over a month due to George Foreman's eye injury led to many foreign tourists not attending the music festival. Consequently, on the third night, after performers like James Brown played to largely empty stadiums, everyone was granted free entry, reportedly at the urging of Muhammad Ali and George Foreman. "So on the third night rather than having James Brown and others play to a mostly empty stadium like they had been doing just about everybody was led in for free."
**Underrepresentation of African Artists** : Despite the presence of many "great African musicians" at Zaire 74, the festival's financial failure meant that the anticipated exposure for these artists did not materialize. An album dedicated solely to the African musicians' performances was not released until 40 years after the event. "The fact that the festival was not a financial success meant that the exposure for the African musicians that was hoped for just really did not materialize."
**Financial Failure & Etta James Refusal** : Zaire 74 was ultimately not a financial success, partly due to persistent problems with ticket sales and a reported refusal by Etta James to perform at the event. "Edda James reportedly refused to perform and then there were problems with ticket sales like I mentioned earlier."
**Documentary Legacy (1996, 2008)** : Despite its financial challenges, Zaire 74's legacy was eventually documented through film footage that resurfaced in the 1996 documentary "When We Were Kings," and a dedicated music festival documentary "Soul Power" released in 2008. One of its creators, Stewart Levine, later reflected that they might have been "foolish to think that they could pull it off." "Legal troubles kept the film under wraps for years until some of it resurfaced in when we were kings in 1996."
**Cultural Significance (1974)** : When evaluated beyond its financial outcomes, Zaire 74 is considered a cultural success, serving as a timely and powerful opportunity for African Americans to explore and celebrate their black identity and ancestral roots during a period of such significant self-examination. "If we view success in terms of legacy rather than dollars, then Zaire 74 was every bit a success."
