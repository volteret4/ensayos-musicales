## curiosities
**Formation at The Troubadour** : The Byrds were formed when Roger McGuinn, initially a traditional folky, began playing Beatles songs at The Troubadour. # artist - Tom Waits

## genres
Folk
Jazz
Blues

## labels
Asylum Records

## albums
**Closing Time (1973) - Debut Album** : This first record for Asylum, released in March 1973, was very much in the classic 70s singer-songwriter vein, evoking the atmosphere of a dark, smoky club. Initially, it did not attract much attention, and Waits himself seemed unsure if he was a jazz musician or a folk singer. "That first record, closing time, is very much in the classic 70s singer songwriter vein the type where you can kind of just close your eyes and you feel like you are in a dark smoky club somewhere."

## songs
**Old 55 (1973)** : The opening track from his debut album, "Closing Time," this hauntingly beautiful song refers to a car but is metaphorically about the morning after a hookup. It attracted the attention of The Eagles, another Asylum band and Troubadour regulars, who recorded a cover version in 1974. "The title refers to a car but the song is about the morning after a hookup."

## curiosities
**Early Career and Move to LA** : Tom Waits got deeply involved with the folk scene in the 1960s, leading him to drop out of high school in his senior year. He then migrated north from suburban San Diego to Los Angeles to pursue his musical ambitions. "Tom Wates was one of those musicians that got involved with the folk scene in the 60s so much so that he dropped out of high school in his senior year and he migrated north from suburban San Diego to LA."
**The Troubadour Hootnanny Experience** : On Mondays, The Troubadour hosted an open mic night called "The Hoot." Tom Waits would arrive when the club opened, around 9 AM, to get his name on the list for a chance to perform a three or four-song set, often waiting all day for his 15 minutes on stage. His first performance there left him "scared shitless." "I used to take the bus to the troubadour and stand out front at 9 o'clock in the morning on a Monday and wait all day to get up and do 15 minutes on stage because you know you never had confidence."
**Musical Style and Categorization** : Described as difficult to categorize, Tom Waits was seen as a misfit, a "refugee from the beat era of the 50s" but writing about the 70s. His phrasing, a hybrid spoken/sung scat, owed a strong debt to jazz and blues, with musical accompaniment blending influences from Thelonious Monk, Oscar Brown Jr., and Oscar Peterson. He developed a unique brand of gravelly storytelling. "Wates is a misfit, a refugee from the beat era of the 50s but living and writing very much about the 70s."
**Signing with Asylum Records** : After being seen performing at The Troubadour in 1971 by Herb Cohen, Waits was signed by Cohen as his manager. Following continued performances, he secured a recording contract with Asylum Records in 1973. "This unique brand of gravelly storytelling led to him getting a manager, Herb Cohen, who saw him at the Trubidor in 1971 and then after he continued to play there, he was signed to a recording contract with Asylum Records in 1973."
**Dislike for The Eagles' Cover of "Old 55"** : Tom Waits, the original writer of "Old 55," was not a fan of The Eagles' 1974 cover version of his song, finding their recording "too clean" compared to his own interpretation, which he introduced as being about his first real luxury automobile, a 1955 Buick Roadmaster. "Tom Wates thought it was too clean."
**Relationship with Ricky Lee Jones** : Tom Waits and Ricky Lee Jones began a "turbulent relationship" after meeting at The Troubadour. They, along with Chuck Weiss, frequently hung out together, often drinking and doing drugs. "While the three of them did hang out together, usually drinking and doing drugs, she began a bit of a turbulent relationship, not with Chuck E, but with Tom Waits."

## curiosities
**Early Life and Musical Upbringing** : Born in St. Louis, Donnie Hathaway was a professional gospel singer by the age of three. He pursued music lessons and classes throughout his life, including into college, demonstrating an early and profound connection to music. "He grew up in St. Louis and was a professional gospel singer at the age of three."
**Gospel vs. Pop Dilemma** : Throughout his career, Hathaway struggled to reconcile his deeply gospel-oriented background, as noted by his high school band director, with the demands of making pop music. This internal conflict led him to consistently include at least one gospel song on his records. "He had a really hard time reconciling making pop music instead of gospel. So he always included a gospel song on his records."
**Howard University Scholarship** : In the fall of 1963, Hathaway enrolled in classes at Howard University, having received a fine art scholarship primarily due to his exceptional piano skills. Initially, he considered paths like teaching or preaching, but music was ingrained in his DNA. "In the fall of 1963, he enrolled in classes at Howard University, thanks in part to a fine art scholarship because of his piano skills."
**Live Performance Style** : Like a true jazz musician, Donnie Hathaway possessed a seemingly innate ability to make each live performance a unique experience. David Ritz, author of a short book about "Donnie Hathaway Live," emphasized that the live Donnie was the most moving, unadorned, and direct representation of his artistry. "Like a true jazz musician, he had this seemingly innate ability to make each live performance, a new performance."
**Producer and Arranger Work** : In the late 1960s, before launching his solo recording career, Hathaway worked extensively as a producer and arranger for prominent artists such as The Staple Singers and Aretha Franklin. "In the late 1960s, Hathaway worked as a producer and an arranger for the likes of the staple singers and Rita Franklin."

# artist - Neil Diamond

## genres
Singer-songwriter

## labels
Bang Record label
UNI

## albums
**Neil Diamond Gold (1970) - Live Album at The Troubadour** : Recorded live at The Troubadour in July 1970, this album features ten songs. Neil Diamond wrote every single track on the album, with the sole exception of "Both Sides Now," which was penned by Joni Mitchell. The crowd's reaction to "Sweet Caroline" on this recording was notably polite clapping, decades before it became a popular anthem at sporting events and weddings. "Ten songs, he wrote every single one of them except for both sides now, which of course was written by Joni Mitchell."

## songs
**Solitary Man (1966)** : Originally recorded in 1966, this song was one of three early hit singles for Neil Diamond on the Bang Record label. Initially, it struggled to get airplay in the States but eventually became a big hit in California. Diamond recounted pulling over in Toronto to listen when it came on the radio, feeling it was "the fulfillment of everything I ever wanted." Thirty-five years later, after four years of Freudian analysis, he realized he had written it about himself, as reflected in the autobiographical lyrics about seeking a partner who won't play games. "I was in Toronto and Solitary man had just been released. Nobody would play it in the States. But finally, it was played and became a big hit in California."
**Cherry Cherry (1966)** : This was one of three hit singles Neil Diamond recorded for the Bang Record label in 1966, following his financial breakthrough after "Sunday and Me." "Neil Diamond recorded three songs, Solitary Man, Cherry Cherry, and I got the feeling. And that, my friends, was three hit singles for the Bang Record label."
**I Got The Feeling (1966)** : This was one of three hit singles Neil Diamond recorded for the Bang Record label in 1966. "Neil Diamond recorded three songs, Solitary Man, Cherry Cherry, and I got the feeling. And that, my friends, was three hit singles for the Bang Record label."
**Sweet Caroline** : This song is featured on Neil Diamond's live album "Neil Diamond Gold," recorded at The Troubadour in July 1970. At the time, the crowd reacted with polite applause, long before the song gained its widespread popularity as an anthem at baseball games, bars, and weddings. "Yeah, sweet Carolina's on there, but look, this is decades before we all start singing it at baseball games and in bars and weddings and things like that."

## curiosities
**Early Career Path and Influences** : Neil Diamond's parents had hoped he would become a doctor, but he ultimately pursued music, initially assuming he would be a songwriter. His inspiration for songwriting came from the folk legend Pete Seeger. "When it became clear that he was going to go into music rather than be a doctor, which his parents would have preferred, he assumed it would be as a songwriter. His inspiration came from the folk legend Peepseger."
**Struggling Songwriter to Hitmaker** : For about eight years, Diamond made only around $20 a year in royalties as a songwriter, forcing him to sell vitamin pills door-to-door to support himself. His financial situation changed significantly after Jay and the Americans recorded "Sunday and Me" in 1966, providing him with enough money to enter a recording studio and record his own three hit singles. "He made about 20 bucks a year in royalties. And he had to do things like sell vitamin pills, door to door to make money to do things like eat."
**Creative Control and Urge to Write** : Upon signing with UNI, the same label as Elton John, Neil Diamond gained significantly more control over his songwriting. He described his urge to write as an "uncontrollable force," wishing he could write only when he felt like it, at his own pace. "At UNI, the label that also signed Elton John, he had a lot more control over what he wrote, which was almost kind of like this uncontrollable force in him."
**Introducing Elton John at The Troubadour** : Two months after recording his own live album "Neil Diamond Gold" at The Troubadour in July 1970, Neil Diamond returned to the venue. He took the stage to introduce his new British rock singer and UNI record label mate, Elton John, who was making his American debut that night. "Two months after recording Neil Diamond Gold, the record that was recorded at the Trubidor, he returned to the Trubidor to introduce a new British rock singer and a UNI record label mate who was making his American debut at the Trubidor."

# artist - Elton John

## labels
UNI

## albums
**Elton John (1970) - Second Album** : This album, Elton John's second, was noted by critic Robert Hilburn as being "earlier and closer to the heartbeat of rock than Elton's first record." Elton John himself suggested that some of its songs might remind listeners of the band The Band. "Hillburn said the second record is earlier and closer to the heartbeat of rock than Elton's first record."

## songs
**Take Me to the Pilot (1970)** : This song is from Elton John's second album, titled "Elton John." When played live at The Troubadour, Linda Ronstadt famously said the club "levitated." The meaning of the song remains a mystery, even to Elton John and lyricist Bernie Taupin, with John stating it's "much more abstract" than religious interpretations and is more about the performance than the lyrics. It was the B-side to the "Your Song" 45 single. "But if you bought the 45 to your song, your song was the A side, meaning the primary side. Flip it over, the B side is take me to the pilot, you scored."

## curiosities
**American Debut at The Troubadour (September 12, 1970)** : Elton John made his pivotal American debut at The Troubadour on September 12, 1970, introduced by Neil Diamond. The club was packed with record executives eager to find the next big star. Reflecting on Doug Weston's death in 1999, Elton John stated that his "whole life came alive that night, musically, emotionally, everything," transforming him from a fan to an accepted musician and turning him into a star within a week in Los Angeles. "My whole life came alive that night, musically, emotionally, everything. It was like everything I had been waiting for suddenly happened."
**Critical Acclaim from Robert Hilburn** : Robert Hilburn, music critic for the Los Angeles Times, attended Elton John's American debut at The Troubadour and immediately announced the arrival of a new rock star. Hilburn praised John's originality, noting that his "crazy outfit was his only showbiz flair," with the rest being "music industry business," and welcomed him as a "new bit of magic" during a period Hilburn considered unproductive for new talent in rock. "Hillburn announced that there was a new rock star in town."
**Immediate Post-Debut Success** : Following his electrifying first show at The Troubadour, Bill Graham, the owner of the Filmore East in New York, offered Elton John $5,000 to perform there. This amount was a significant endorsement, as it was more than Graham had ever offered a new artist before. "After his first show at the Trubidor, Bill Graham, the owner of the Philmore East in New York, offered Elton John $5,000 to perform there, which is more than he had ever offered a new artist before."

# concert - The Troubadour

## curiosities
**Opening and Location** : The Troubadour was opened by Doug Weston in September 1957 as a jazz club on La Cienega Boulevard in Los Angeles. Four years later, it relocated to 9081 Santa Monica Boulevard and expanded into a space previously occupied by a Porsche mechanic shop, which featured a steel-reinforced balcony. "Doug Weston opened the troubadour as a jazz club on La Siena Gable of Art in Los Angeles in September 1957."
**Intimate Venue to Expanded Space** : Initially, The Troubadour had seating for about 65 people, described as "intimate." After moving and expanding into a former Porsche mechanic shop, it could accommodate some 300 music fans, allowing it to host a larger audience for emerging folk and rock legends. "At that time, it had seating for about 65. You know, we like to call that intimate. Four years later, he moved it to 9081 Santa Monica Gable of Art and it eventually expanded into a space that was formerly occupied by a Porsche mechanic shop, hence the steel reinforced balcony that it used to have."
**Community Hub for Musicians** : In the late 1960s and first half of the 1970s, The Troubadour became a vital community hub where both newcomers and established stars would gather. Linda Ronstadt called it a "cafe society," and Bonnie Raitt noted it was "the heart of an entire music scene" where numerous musicians became friends, playing music and continuing their camaraderie in Laurel Canyon. "But so is the community that was created. In the late 1960s and the first half of the 70s, this was the place where the newcomers and the established stars to hang out."
**"Hootnanny" Open Mic Nights** : On Mondays, The Troubadour hosted an open mic night affectionately called "The Hoot," offering an opportunity for anyone who could sing (or thought they could) to perform a three or four-song set to an audience of 10 to 15 acts. Getting on the list required arriving early when the club opened. "On Mondays at the troubadour, they had this kind of an open mic night that they called the Hootnanny. Well, it was a Hootnanny. They called it the Hoot, which was this opportunity for anyone who could sing or thought they could sing to put their name on the list."
**Comedy Showcase and Controversy** : The Troubadour also featured comedy acts, serving as a venue before dedicated comedy clubs became widespread. It was the site of controversy when legendary comedian Lenny Bruce was arrested on October 24, 1962, for uttering an "obscene word," "schmuck," making it his third arrest in three weeks. "Even the troubadour itself was the site of controversy when legendary comedian Lenny Bruce was arrested by the LA County Sheriff's Vice Squad on October 24th, 1964. Oh pardon me, 1962 after Lenny uttered a so-called obscene word in both English and Yiddish, it was his third arrest in three weeks and it was for saying the word schmuck."
**Decline in the Late 70s** : By 1978, The Troubadour was largely empty, described by Ricky Lee Jones as a "club for used to be singers" and a "sad remnant" of its former glory. Although it later became a heavy metal venue in the 1980s, its golden age for folk, rock, and singer-songwriters had passed. "By 1978, the troubadour was as Ricky Lee Jones put it, it used to be club for used to be singers. Occasionally it drew in heavy metal bands. **Competition from The Roxy** : A major factor in The Troubadour's decline was the opening of The Roxy by Lou Adler and David Geffen in 1973, located not far from The Troubadour, which provided a new, popular alternative venue. "Part of the problem for the troubadour is that Lou Adler and David Geffen opened up the Roxy not far from there in 1973."
**Doug Weston's Role in Decline** : The club's owner, Doug Weston, contributed to its downfall. He reportedly became envious of the success of artists, record executives, and managers, leading him to impose "unreasonable conditions" in artist contracts. These conditions, such as clauses requiring famous artists to return to the small club after gaining stardom, alienated performers. Weston was also known to be an abuser of drugs and alcohol, further impacting the club's environment. "Another problem for the troubadour was Doug Weston himself. It seemed like Weston started envying the success of not only the artists that were making big names for themselves and making money, but he was envious of the record executives and the managers."

**70s Desire for Authenticity** : The 1970s saw a strong desire for authenticity in music, particularly in the singer-songwriter movement. Artists like James Taylor and Carly Simon wrote autobiographical songs about personal struggles and relationships, leading audiences to assume similar deeply personal narratives in the songs of artists like Jackson Browne. "Much of this was playing into this desire for authenticity that we had in the 70s, or at least music that sounded like it was authentic."
**Loss of Traditional Authority in the 70s** : According to Judy Coutulis in her book "After Aquarius Dawn," the 1970s witnessed a decline in the influence of traditional authority, especially among the younger generation. This shift led them to seek credibility from new experts in the real world, a role often filled by musicians. "Judy Coutulis in her book about living with the changes that the 60s brought. It's called After Aquarius Dawn said that in the 1970s traditional authority had lost its influence, especially over the younger generation, driving them toward a host of new experts with real world credibility. And they gave that credibility to musicians."
**Impact of Electronic Devices on Community** : The widespread use of electronic devices, particularly cell phones with headphones, is posited as a potential distractor from genuine human interaction and community building. This is compared to the camaraderie found among musicians at The Troubadour or even among sports players in locker rooms, suggesting that the conditions for such organic community formation might not be replicable today. "But it makes me think that this electronic device might actually distract us from interacting with others around us and makes us more independent."

**The 45 Record Format (A-Side/B-Side)** : The 45 RPM record was a common format for single releases, with the A-side being the primary song intended for radio play and the B-side often containing another significant track.
