## albums
**Metal Health (1983)** : This album, released in 1983, was the first by a heavy metal band to reach number one on the charts, marking a significant milestone for the genre. The band's popularity was immensely boosted by its accompanying video, which featured lead singer Kevin Dubrow in a face mask and red straightjacket, escaping a padded cell to join the band on stage. The crowd depicted in the video consisted of students from the California Institute of the Arts. The video for "Metal Health" cost $10,000 to make. "Metal health the album went to number one which was the first time a heavy metal band ever had a number one album."

## songs
**Metal Health (Bang Your Head) (1983)** : This song was famously featured in the movie *Footloose*, blaring from Ren McCormick's VW bug radio in Beaumont. A young female fan described the song as being about "feeling crazy" in a mental institution setting, with a guy in a metal mask, ultimately symbolizing teenagers releasing their frustration. She praised its catchy lyrics for articulating feelings of wanting "more power" and "our own space," and it was noted for its loud, crunchy guitars. "The songs about feeling crazy the setting is a mental institution there's a guy running around in a metal mask and then the video cuts back to the band playing."
**Cum On Feel the Noize (1983)** : A cover of a 1973 hit by the glam rock band Slade, this track was the first single from the *Metal Health* album and quickly climbed to number five on the Billboard Top 40 in November 1983. Its music video, which cost about $40,000 to produce, played a crucial role in breaking the band into the mainstream, receiving airplay every half hour on MTV. "Cum On Feel the Noize a cover of the 1973 hit from the glam rock band slayed. Was the first single it made its number five on the Billboard top 40 in November 1983."

## curiosities
**Early Formation and Industry Frustration** : Quiet Riot formed eight years before 1984, playing in Los Angeles clubs like the Whisky a Go Go since the mid-1970s. The band members expressed significant frustration that fellow LA hard rockers Van Halen secured a major record deal before them, despite believing Quiet Riot was more popular. This perceived injustice contributed to Randy Rhoads' decision to leave the band and join Ozzy Osbourne. "The guys in quiet riot were pissed that their fellow LA hard rockers man hailing had been signed to a major record deal before they were."
**Randy Rhoads' Departure and Tragic Death** : Randy Rhoads, an original member, left Quiet Riot to play with Ozzy Osbourne, partly due to the band's struggle to secure a major record deal before Van Halen. This period was also marked by various "shenanigans involving guns and intoxicating substances." Sadly, Rhoads died in a plane crash while on tour in 1982, a significant loss to the heavy metal community. "Rhodes left to go play with Aussie also there were. Various shenanigans involving guns and intoxicating substances sadly Rhodes died in a plane crash while on tour in 1982."
**Post-Signing Industry Boom** : After Quiet Riot finally secured their major record deal, Kevin Dubrow observed a sudden surge in similar signings across Los Angeles. He commented in 1984 that "now that we have signed they are signing every dimwit heavy metal band in LA," highlighting a shift in record company interest following their breakthrough. "Dubrow said in 1984 the funny thing is now that we have signed they are signing every dimwit heavy metal band in LA."
**MTV's Role in Early Band Exposure** : Bassist Randy Sarzo explained that in MTV's early days, the channel lacked sufficient video content from bands, as music videos were not typically part of band budgets focused on radio airplay for publishing residuals. Record companies initially hesitated to fund expensive videos due to a perceived lack of return, but eventually saw increased record sales, which included publishing and mechanicals, as justification. Quiet Riot's first video for "Metal Health" cost $10,000, and the subsequent video for "Cum On Feel the Noize" cost $40,000, significantly boosting their exposure through constant rotation on MTV, even before MTV had widespread subscriptions, as stores and bars began playing the channel. "All of a sudden an MTV did not have enough content that is enough video from anybody. Those bands were not you was it wasn't part of the budget."

**Pour Some Sugar On Me (1987)** : The initial idea for this song emerged in 1986 when Joe Elliott, alone in the studio, began playing an acoustic guitar and singing a hook. Producer Mutt Lange heard it and insisted it be developed into a song for the *Hysteria* album, despite the band having already worked for over two years. Lange quickly programmed a drum machine, added bass and chords, and Elliott sang the chorus for a "crappy demo." Phil Collen later contributed an iconic guitar riff for the verse. The song was the quickest to produce, taking only ten days, and evolved from an initially slower vocal melody (compared to The Beatles' "Help!") to a doubled-up, mid-tempo dance track popular in clubs, including among strippers. Released in Summer 1987, the single hit number two the following summer. Joe Elliott's lyrics, which he described as "nonsensical but it's got a fanatic power to it," were partly inspired by the Run-DMC and Aerosmith collaboration on "Walk This Way." Elliott also considered it "singer songwriter stuff" akin to Neil Sedaka, Carol King, or Burt Bacharach, aiming for a more mature sound. "Paulson Sugarmay's initial seed was 1986 and we the band thought we had the album pretty much in the can."

## curiosities
**"Heavy Metal for People Who Think" Image** : In the summer of 1983, a Grand Junction, Colorado, newspaper headline humorously dubbed Def Leppard "heavy metal for people who think," implying their sound was "safe, not scary like Black Sabbath." This image was reinforced by their "squeaky clean" appearance, featuring nicely tailored clothes and neatly styled 80s hair, which lead singer Joe Elliott attributed to their working-class upbringing and proud parents' emphasis on self-respect. "Deaf Leopard, heavy metal for people who think."
**Rejection of Heavy Metal Label** : Def Leppard actively disliked being categorized as "heavy metal," fearing it would negatively impact their record sales. Joe Elliott specifically expressed displeasure at the music media lumping them in with bands like Motley Crue or Warrant, underscoring their desire to appeal to a broader audience beyond the traditional heavy metal fan base. "But in 1983 the band's image was such that they did not even want the heavy metal label for fear it would negatively affect record sales."
**Joe Elliott on Band's Image** : In a 1983 interview, lead singer Joe Elliott, with a charming northern English accent, stated, "that's just the way we are." He expressed that the band "never wanted to look like tramps," criticizing groups like Motorhead for appearing in "car mechanic overalls with unwashed hair," which he considered "abusing the audience." Elliott believed their "squeaky clean image" stemmed from their working-class roots and parents who instilled self-respect, ensuring they avoided looking disheveled. "He never wanted to look like tramps. Some of these bands like Motorhead just sort of turn up in their car mechanic overalls with unwashed hair. To me, that's really abusing the audience."
**Steve Clark's Tragic Death** : Steve Clark, one of Def Leppard's guitarists, tragically died in 1991 from a lethal combination of prescription drugs and alcohol. This event stands in stark contrast to Joe Elliott's earlier comments about the band's "squeaky clean image" and self-respect. "There is some sad irony in reading that now knowing that Steve Clark, one of Deaf Leopard's guitarists, died from a lethal combination of prescription drugs and alcohol in 1991."
**MTV Influence and Comparison** : Joe Elliott later commented on MTV's influence on Def Leppard's career, suggesting that if MTV were credited for their success, the same could be said for artists like Michael Jackson or Peter Gabriel. He noted that Jackson and Gabriel, like Def Leppard, had established careers long before MTV's inception, implying MTV amplified existing talent rather than solely creating it. "Elliott responded years later that if you were going to credit MTV for Deaf Leopard's career, maybe you could say the same thing about Michael Jackson or Peter Gabriel."

## songs
**Jump (1983)** : Released in December 1983 as the first single from the album *1984*, this song controversially featured Eddie Van Halen playing a keyboard synthesizer. "Jump" was nominated for a Grammy for Best Rock Performance by a Duo or Group with Vocal but lost to Prince and The Revolution's "Purple Rain." It was included in the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame's list of 500 songs that shaped Rock and Roll, was ubiquitous in 1984-1985, and opened the door for synthesizers in rock bands, exposing Van Halen and metal to a wider audience. "Jump was released in December 1983. It was the first single from the album 1984."

## curiosities
**Embracing Keyboards and Disregarding Genre Norms** : Eddie Van Halen openly explored keyboards, stating that "with synthesizer, you can fool around with sounds to the point where you will be happy." He dismissed concerns about fan perception regarding his use of keyboards over guitar, asserting, "I think as long as I do whatever I do well, whatever they say, I don't really care. I mean, they can't say it sucks. If they don't like seeing me play the keyboards, that's too bad." He also expressed uncertainty about the meaning of "Heavy Metal," indicating a disregard for strict genre definitions and expectations. "I think as long as I do whatever I do well, whatever they say, I don't really care."
**Impact on Synthesizer Use in Rock** : With the success of "Jump," Eddie Van Halen's integration of the keyboard synthesizer into a hard rock context made it "okay to use synthesizers and rock bands." This groundbreaking move opened the door for other artists, like Jon Bon Jovi, to incorporate synthesizers into their music without fear of alienating their audience. "Eddie Van Halen opened the door for synthesizers and John Bonjouvi walked right in."

# genre - Heavy Metal

## curiosities
**Critical Reception vs. Fan Loyalty (70s/80s)** : In the 1970s and early 1980s, what would become known as heavy metal (initially called hard rock) was met with significant disdain from rock critics. However, it was adored by its loyal fan base, predominantly young white males, who often came from lower to middle-class socio-economic backgrounds, highlighting a clear class division in rock appreciation. "As the 80s begin critics still do not think much of heavy metal radio has been taken over by corporations that continue to ignore heavy metal this time in favor of new wave acts like Durant Iran, the go goes arena rock like sticks, journey. And fans of heavy metal still could not care less if you liked their music or not it was still true that heavy metal bands owed their existence to the fans who went to their live shows, bought their t-shirts and really did not give a shit what critics thought in fact so much the better if heavy metal was not on Casey casem's American top 40."
**Market Growth in the Mid-80s** : Despite initial critical and radio neglect, heavy metal experienced a remarkable surge in popularity in the mid-1980s. In 1983, it accounted for only 8% of music sold, but by 1984, that figure dramatically increased to 20%. This growth signaled its transition from a subculture to a more mainstream genre, eventually leading MTV to devote an entire show, "Headbangers Ball," to it just two years later. "In 1983 only 8% of music sold was classified as heavy metal but a year later by 1984 that number jumped to 20% in two years after that MTV devoted an entire show to heavy metal called headbangers ball."
**Debate Over Coining the Term** : The origin of the term "heavy metal" remains a subject of debate among music historians and critics. Possible coiners include legendary rock critic Lester Bangs (portrayed by Philip Seymour Hoffman in *Almost Famous*), author William Burrows in his novel *Naked Lunch*, or Mars Bonfire, who wrote the Steppenwolf classic "Born to Be Wild" which contains the lyric "heavy metal thunder." "Was it the legendary rock critic Lester bangs who you might recall being portrayed by the great Philip Seymour Hoffman in the movie almost famous? Was it the author William Burrows and naked lunch? Was it Mars Bonfire who wrote the Steppenwolf classic born to be wild you know heavy metal thunder?"
**Rick Johnson's Critiques** : Rick Johnson, a writer for *Cream* magazine, offered a vivid and often critical perspective on heavy metal throughout its evolution. In a 1979 article titled "Is Heavy Metal Dead," he described it as "rock and roll that gives your ears the urge to make voodoo dolls out of your stereo speakers" and predicted its decline, calling established bands "fumbling flyfarts." Years later, in "Heavy Metal Must Be Destroyed," he argued it was "no longer music it was a marketing strategy," reflecting its mainstream commercialization. "Heavy metal is rock and roll that gives your ears the urge to make voodoo dolls out of your stereo speakers."
**Audience Transformation and Mainstreaming** : Heavy metal's audience underwent a significant transformation from the early to mid-1980s. Initially the music of "misunderstood teenagers," its reach expanded far beyond its subcultural roots to embrace suburban audiences, shifting from "dark" to "party music, fun music, loud and fun." Author Chuck Klosterman noted that metal began telling alienated kids they were "not different at all," but rather "hyper normal," "extremely popular," and "totally cool." "instead of telling an alienated kid that it was okay to be different metals seem to say that you're not different at all in fact your hyper normal in fact your extremely popular and totally cool."
**Reflection of 80s Culture** : Heavy metal music in the 1980s served as a reflection of the era's cultural landscape, embodying themes of "excess," "newer, bigger, better, greed as good materialism." This intrinsic connection meant that the genre's evolution mirrored the prevailing societal values and economic trends of the time. "Music changes to reflect the era in 80s. That's the time of excess. Newer, bigger, better, greet as good materialism, all of it. And heavy metal reflected that."

# genre - Hair Metal

## curiosities
**Defining Aesthetic and Origins** : Hair metal, the most common term for 1980s metal, was defined by the "sky high hair styles" of its members, heavy makeup, tight leather pants, and metal studs. Musically, it originated in the clubs of Los Angeles in the late 1970s and achieved mainstream status through the power of MTV. Its roots are traced back to 1970s glam rock, drawing influences from artists like David Bowie, T. Rex, the New York Dolls, and Kiss, which emphasized visual performance as much as the music. "the metal of the eighties has come to me most commonly known as hair metal because of the sky high. And it had hair styles of the members of the bands musically this is largely the heavy metal that sprung from the clubs of Los Angeles in the late seventies and then went mainstream through the power of MTV this is the type of metal that has its roots in seventies glam rock you know like David Bowie T Rex definitely the New York dolls maybe a little kiss which put a focus on the visual aspects of the performance as much as the music."
**MTV's Mainstreaming Impact** : The visual focus of hair metal meant that MTV was as, if not more, important than radio in propelling the genre into the mainstream. In an era without widespread internet or smartphones, MTV became "appointment television," making bands like Quiet Riot and Def Leppard household names through constant video rotation. "And with the focus on how bands look as much as how they sound that meant that MTV was as important if not more important than the radio when it came to mainstreaming heavy metal in an era when the internet and pocket computers masquerading a phones were not dominating our attention spans MTV could be appointment television."
**From Dark Rebellion to Party Music** : Hair metal marked a significant shift in the genre's thematic content, moving away from the "dark" and alienated sentiments of traditional metal towards being "party music, fun music, loud and fun." This transformation also opened the door for androgynous looks, as seen in bands like Motley Crue, further broadening the genre's appeal beyond its original niche. "And with this change metal goes from being dark to being party music fun music loud and fun and hey you can dress up like you can actually go kind of androgynous if you want because hey have you seen motley crew mic mars Tommy Lee niki six been snail they had been in bands before and they brought their very own diverse experience and look to the band we're talking sky high t's terror tight leather pants lots of metal studs carefully done makeup like kiss and the new york dolls had offspring in david bowie was the babysitter."

**Rick Johnson's 1979 Heavy Metal Description** : In a 1979 article for *Cream* magazine titled "Is Heavy Metal Dead," Rick Johnson offered a colorful and visceral description of heavy metal, characterizing it as "rock and roll that gives your ears the urge to make voodoo dolls out of your stereo speakers." He vividly depicted its extreme loudness ("as loud as having an eviction notice nailed to your forehead"), piercing sound ("screechy as a rusty craniotomy saw"), and physical impact ("so heavy that it can sometimes only be listened to while curled into a modified gagging dolphin position"), along with its memorable lyrics and varying production values. "Heavy metal is rock and roll that gives your ears the urge to make voodoo dolls out of your stereo speakers."
**Rock Critics' Disdain for Heavy Metal** : In the 1970s and early 1980s, what was then commonly referred to as hard rock (and later heavy metal) was met with considerable scorn from rock critics. This critical dismissal contrasted sharply with the adoration of the genre's loyal fans, primarily young white males, reflecting a discernible class division within the rock music audience. "As the 80s begin critics still do not think much of heavy metal."
**Radio's Corporate Control and Heavy Metal** : The early 1980s saw corporate-controlled radio largely overlook heavy metal, instead prioritizing new wave acts like Duran Duran and The Go-Go's, and arena rock bands such as Styx and Journey. This commercial gatekeeping persisted despite a significant leap in heavy metal's market share, which surged from 8% of music sales in 1983 to 20% by 1984. "radio has been taken over by corporations that continue to ignore heavy metal this time in favor of new wave acts like Durant Iran, the go goes arena rock like sticks, journey."
**Evolution of Heavy Metal Audience in the 80s** : Heavy metal's audience underwent a dramatic shift in the 1980s, expanding from primarily young white male, lower to middle-class backgrounds to encompass a broader suburban demographic. This transformation saw metal's message evolve from telling alienated kids it was "okay to be different" to affirming they were "hyper normal," "extremely popular," and "totally cool," thereby converting the genre from "dark" rebellion into "party music, fun music, loud and fun." "instead of telling an alienated kid that it was okay to be different metals seem to say that you're not different at all in fact your hyper normal in fact your extremely popular and totally cool."
**The 80s Era of Excess and Music** : The music of the 1980s, particularly heavy metal, was a direct reflection of the era's pervasive themes of excess and materialism. It embodied the cultural aspirations of "newer, bigger, better, greed as good materialism," demonstrating how musical trends often mirror the prevailing societal values and economic conditions of their time. "Music changes to reflect the era in 80s. That's the time of excess. Newer, bigger, better, greet as good materialism, all of it. And heavy metal reflected that."
**Economic Recession and 90s Music Shift** : The economic recession of the 1990s, triggered by 1980s debt, deregulation, high interest rates, and soaring oil costs after the Gulf War, profoundly impacted music trends. This financial downturn correlated with MTV's shift away from 80s hair metal, favoring grunge and alternative rock bands like Pearl Jam, Soundgarden, and Nirvana, whose music and image resonated more with the changing economic and social landscape.
