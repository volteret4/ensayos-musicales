**Richard Nixon's Musical Tastes** : President Richard Nixon, a capable piano player, primarily favored classical music, with his preferences leaning towards artists like the Boston Pops or soundtracks such as "Oklahoma." Despite being a music fan who showed off his stereo and record collection to White House visitors, his collection notably excluded popular rock bands like the Rolling Stones, the Beatles, or Creedence Clearwater Revival. "Nixon, who was a pretty good piano player, was a classical music fan. His tastes ran more along the lines of the Boston Pops or perhaps the soundtrack to Oklahoma."
**Richard Nixon's Anti-Pop/Rock Stance** : Richard Nixon was not a fan of popular music and publicly blamed the pop and rock music of the mid to late 1960s for contributing to drug use among American youth. His Vice President, Spiro Agnew, acted as an unofficial surrogate, delivering speeches denouncing pop music for "brainwashing baby boomers into a drug culture." "he blamed the pop and rock music of the mid to late 60s for drug use and America's youth."
**Vice President Spiro Agnew's Anti-Drug/Anti-Pop Speech (September 14, 1970)** : On September 14, 1970, Vice President Spiro Agnew delivered a speech to Republicans in Las Vegas, Nevada, in which he declared that pop music and movies were "brainwashing baby boomers into a drug culture" and promoting "a depressing lifestyle of conformity that has either life nor style." Agnew served as an unofficial anti-drug and anti-pop music surrogate for President Richard Nixon. "Vice President Spiro Agnew became the unofficial anti-drug anti-pop music surrogate for Nixon and he gave a speech to his fellow Republicans in that Bastion of Purity Las Vegas, Nevada on September 14, 1970 to announce that pop music and movies were quote brainwashing baby boomers into a drug culture and quote a depressing lifestyle of conformity that has either life nor style."
**White House Conference for Drugs in the Radio Industry (October 1970)** : Approximately a month after Spiro Agnew's speech, President Richard Nixon hosted a conference at the White House for radio industry executives. While Nixon himself did not issue direct commands on song choices, FCC Chairman Dean Burch stated that the FCC would "look favorably upon stations that avoided playing songs with drug references," leading to songs like "White Rabbit," "Eight Miles High," and "Mother's Little Helper" being placed on a "no-go list" by various stations across the US. "Nixon hosted a conference at the White House for Radio Industry Executives called the White House Conference for Drugs in the Radio Industry."
**FCC Drug References Policy** : Following the White House Conference for Drugs in the Radio Industry in October 1970, Dean Burch, the chairman of the FCC, announced that the commission would regard stations favorably if they chose to avoid playing songs containing drug references. This indirect guidance led to songs such as "White Rabbit," "Eight Miles High," and "Mother's Little Helper" being unofficially banned or placed on a "no-go list" by radio stations in major cities from New York to Dallas. "Dean Perch, who was the chairman of the FCC at the time, said that the FCC would look favorably upon stations that avoided playing songs with drug references."
**Banned Songs Due to Drug References** : Following the FCC's policy encouraging stations to avoid songs with drug references, several popular tracks were placed on a "no-go list" by radio stations across the US. These included "White Rabbit," "Eight Miles High," and "Mother's Little Helper." This informal ban reflected the government's stance against the perceived promotion of drug culture in music. "That meant songs like White Rabbit, Eight Miles High, Mother's Little Helper, were on the no-go list and some stations from New York to Chicago to Dallas would not play music that seemed to celebrate the drug culture."
**Richard Nixon's Initial Popularity** : Before the Watergate scandal led to his downfall, Richard Nixon was a popular president. He was easily re-elected in 1972, winning 520 electoral votes to George McGovern's 17, and maintained an average popularity rating of around 56% during his first term. This popularity was higher than many subsequent presidents, making him a less obvious target for songwriters at the beginning of the 1970s than he appears in retrospect. "Nixon cruised into a second term in 1972, the man won 520 electoral votes to George McGovern's 17. His average popularity rating in his first term was around 56%."
**Operation Intercept (September 1969)** : In September 1969, President Richard Nixon launched Operation Intercept, an initiative that significantly increased inspections at the Mexican border. The stated goal was to halt the transport of marijuana into the U.S., but it also aimed to criminalize Black people and hippies, and assert U.S. dominance over Mexico. While few drugs were found, the operation caused severe traffic jams, widespread traveler frustration, and job losses for many Mexicans, despite Nixon declaring it a victory. "Nixon announced the launch of Operation Intercept, which amounted to inspections at the Mexican border. The intent was to stop marijuana from being transported into the US with a side dish of criminalizing black people and hippies and showing Mexico who was boss, which was some red meat for Nixon's base."
**Richard Nixon's Cambodia Bombing Campaign (May 1969 - 1973)** : On April 30, 1970, President Richard Nixon announced a preemptive strike in Cambodia, allowing South Vietnamese troops to cross the border, ostensibly to cut off North Vietnamese supply lines. However, the U.S. had already been secretly bombing Cambodia since May 1969, a fact reported by The New York Times but largely unknown to the public. This four-year carpet bombing campaign ultimately dropped 540,000 tons of bombs on neutral Cambodia, with Nixon lying to Congress about its initiation. "the US had already been bombing the hell out of Cambodia. For a year, the US was carpet bombing Cambodia and when all was said and done after four years of this, 540,000 tons of bombs had been dropped on Cambodia."
**Kent State Shootings (May 4, 1970)** : On May 4, 1970, the Ohio National Guard fired into a crowd of unarmed students at Kent State University, killing four and wounding nine. The protests began on April 30, 1970, in response to President Nixon's announcement of the Cambodian invasion, escalating into clashes between students and the National Guard, who had been called in by Governor James Rhodes. The National Guard, dressed in riot gear and armed with M1 rifles and bayonets, were initially ordered to kneel and point their weapons as a show of force before, for unclear reasons, firing for 13 seconds into the crowd. "Ohio was a response to the Ohio National Guard shooting 12 unarmed students on the campus of Kensington University on May 4, 1970."
**Kent State Victims** : During the Kent State shootings on May 4, 1970, four unarmed students were killed: William Schroeder (390 feet away), Santa Scheuer (390 feet away), Jeffrey Miller (263 feet away, shot through the mouth), and Allison Kraus (343 feet away, shot while trying to take cover). Nine others were wounded, including Dean Kailer (300 feet away), who was permanently paralyzed from his gunshot wound. The farthest student wounded was 750 feet away. "William Schroeder, 390 feet away, was killed. Santa Scheuer, 390 feet away, was killed. Jeffrey Miller, 263 feet away, shot through the mouth and killed. Allison Kraus, who tried to take cover 343 feet away, was shot and killed. Nine others were wounded, including Dean Kailer, who was 300 feet away and he was permanently paralyzed from his gunshot wound."
**Public Blame for Kent State Victims** : For years after the Kent State shootings, many people, including the parents of the victims and other students who witnessed the event, blamed the students themselves or "outside agitators." **Watergate Scandal (May 28, 1972 - August 9, 1974)** : The Watergate scandal began on May 28, 1972, when a team of burglars led by G. Gordon Liddy broke into the Democratic National Headquarters at the Watergate Hotel to bug phones and gather intelligence for Nixon's re-election campaign. Five individuals were arrested within two weeks, initially denied by the White House. Despite Nixon's re-election in November 1972, investigative reporting by Bob Woodward and Carl Bernstein of The Washington Post, followed by congressional hearings in January 1973, exposed the White House's involvement. Nixon repeatedly denied culpability, even offering a million dollars in cash to keep the scandal under wraps. "On May 28, 1972, a team of bungling burglarers headed by G Gordon-Liddy broke into the Democratic National Headquarters at the Watergate Hotel in Washington, D.C. They bugged the phones, of course their goal is to get dirt on Democrats and get Nixon back into the White House Within two weeks, five people were arrested and the White House denied knowing anything about it"
**Richard Nixon's Resignation (August 8, 1974)** : On August 8, 1974, Richard Nixon announced his resignation from the presidency, effective noon the following day, becoming the first and only U.S. president to do so. In his address, he stated that he no longer had "a strong enough political base in the Congress" to justify continuing his term, acknowledging that he would have been impeached due to the Watergate matter. He framed his decision as putting "the interests of America first," emphasizing the need for a "full time president and a full time Congress" without the distraction of a fight for "personal vindication." "Therefore I shall resign the presidency effective that noon tomorrow Vice President for will be sworn in as president at that hour in this office"
**Gerald Ford's Pardon of Richard Nixon (September 8, 1974)** : On September 8, 1974, in his first official address to the nation, President Gerald Ford controversially pardoned Richard Nixon for any crimes he may have committed while in office. Ford declared that the country had "been through enough" and that "our long national nightmare was over," aiming to heal the nation. However, this act was met with public outrage and accusations that Ford, who was unelected to either vice presidency or presidency, was part of a corrupt system, leading to a significant tanking of his approval ratings. "On September 8th 1974 in his first official address to the nation Gerald Ford pardoned Richard Nixon for any crimes he may have committed because he decided that the country had been through enough and declared that our long national nightmare was over"
**Vice President Spiro Agnew's Resignation (1973)** : Spiro Agnew, who served as Richard Nixon's Vice President, resigned from his position in 1973 due to his own income tax bribery scandal while he was governor of Maryland. This event further contributed to the perception of a corrupt political system, especially when Gerald Ford, who replaced Agnew, later pardoned Nixon. "Ford was being accused of being selected to replace Spiro Agnew who had to resign due to his own income tax bribery scandal And he was the governor of Maryland"
**Decline of Protest Songs as Hits** : The period following the 1970s saw a decline in protest songs achieving hit status, attributed to several theories. The "Dixie Chick Theory" suggests that backlash against politically outspoken artists, like the Dixie Chicks after criticizing George Bush in 2003, deterred others. The "social media theory" posits that modern songwriters can express political critiques more directly and instantaneously through platforms like Twitter, Instagram, or TikTok, diminishing the traditional role of a song for such commentary.
