# instrument - CB Radio
## curiosities
**1976 Craze** : America in 1976 experienced a widespread craze for Citizens Band (CB) radio, which originated from truckers using them during the 1973 oil crisis to communicate about gas, speed traps, and for general conversation to combat monotony. "America in 1976 was in the midst of a CB or citizens band radio craze."
**FCC Regulations and Overload** : CB radios operated on airwaves with strict rules, including not using real names and especially no swearing, which the FCC enforced rigidly. At one point, about half a million people applied for a CB license with the FCC every month, overwhelming the agency to the point where they ceased trying to keep up with applications. "At one point about half a million people applied for a license with the FCC every month. Then the FCC said the hell with it the agency could not keep up with all the applications."
**Transition to Mainstream Use** : Initially a vital tool for truck drivers, CB radios rapidly moved into family station wagons, allowing parents to communicate about "smokies" (police), gas, or simply to join in the fun. Their popularity stemmed from the appeal of 24/7 communication, entertainment, and information, similar to modern pocket computers. "So how did the CB go from being a vital tool for truck drivers to our family station wagons in the 1970s?"

# curiosity
**America's Bicentennial (1976)** : The celebration of America's 200th birthday in 1976 was complicated by recent national events, including the Watergate scandal, Nixon's resignation and pardon, the fall of Saigon, a shaky economy, the women's liberation movement, and ongoing issues in the post-civil rights era. This led to diverse perspectives on the celebration, with some African Americans supporting Jesse Jackson's call for a boycott while others, like Betty Shabazz, actively participated in planning. Native American activists celebrated the 100th anniversary of the Battle of Little Big Horn, while others joined local July 4th events. Initial federal plans for an expo in Philadelphia and bicentennial parks in every state were scrapped between 1970-1973 due to partisan politics and excessive cost under President Nixon, leaving states and local communities to organize events through grants, funding, and donations, resulting in a nostalgic, small-town feel. Queen Elizabeth and Prince Philip visited President Ford, attending a state dinner and bringing a replica of the Liberty Bell. The pop charts of 1976, however, showed no singular theme, reflecting a conflicted national mood that found common ground in "fun and light" music, with even protest songs like "Convoy" being primarily entertaining, and nostalgic hits like Stevie Wonder's "I Wish" gaining popularity over more serious songs like "Black Man." "For all these reasons, celebrating our nation's 200th birthday was complicated."
**1973 Oil Crisis** : In October 1973, the Organization of Arab Petroleum Exporting Countries (OPEC) imposed an oil embargo on countries supporting Israel in the Yam Kippur War, including the United States, UK, Canada, Japan, and the Netherlands. This caused oil prices to surge from about $3 to $12 a barrel, leading to high gas prices and shortages, with long lines at gas stations and restrictions on gas purchases based on license plate numbers. "In October 1973, the organization of Arab petroleum exporting countries issued an oil embargo on countries that supported Israel in the Yam Kapoor War."
**National Speed Limit 55 (1974)** : Implemented in 1974, the National Speed Limit of 55 mph was an attempt to conserve gas during the 1973 oil crisis. This measure proved impractical for truck drivers, who needed to transport goods efficiently, leading to widespread unhappiness and inspiring protest songs like "Convoy." "the National Speed Limit was set at 55 in an attempt to conserve gas."
**70s Nostalgia for the 50s** : In the 1970s, there was a widespread nostalgia for the 1950s, which was perceived as a simpler time, even if this was a myth. This sentiment fueled a surge in cover songs, such as "How Sweet It Is" by James Taylor and "Please Mr. Postman" by The Carpenters, and contributed to the popularity of TV shows like "Happy Days" and "Laverne & Shirley," the movie "American Graffiti," and the musical "Grease." "America was nostalgic for the 50s in the 70s it just felt like a more simple time even if much of that was a myth."
**John Lennon's Criticism of Paul McCartney's Post-Beatles Work** : John Lennon frequently criticized Paul McCartney's solo career, dismissing his work as "too pop" and once accusing him of writing "pizza and fairy tales." Lennon even wrote the song "How Do You Sleep" as a direct attack. However, Andrew Grant Jackson noted Lennon himself released an album of oldies covers in 1975, implying he also engaged in similar "pizza and fairy tales" in his own work. "Lenin took shots at McCartney's post Beatles work which he thought was all together to pop."
**Cultural Accord in Mid-1970s America** : By the mid-1970s, America had reached a certain cultural accord, where elements like long hair, denim jackets, and pot became somewhat accepted by the mainstream, as depicted in "The 70s Show." Even the sexual revolution was assimilated with 1950s nostalgia, exemplified by "Happy Days" character Marion Cunningham's "frisky" comments to her husband Howard. "culturally America had reached and accord long hair denim jackets and pot were sort of accepted by the mainstream a la the 70s show."
**Chinese Exclusion Act (1882)** : This was the United States' first piece of federal legislation specifically designed to stop immigration, following the significant role Chinese immigrants played in building the majority of the western section of the transcontinental railroad. Stevie Wonder's song "Black Man" highlighted this historical injustice as part of an inclusive look at American history. "that was quickly followed by the Chinese exclusion act our first piece of federal legislation that was designed to stop immigration."
