**Formation of Dawn** : In 1970, former doo-wop singer and music executive Tony Orlando was asked by producer Hank Medress to record "Candida." After the song became a hit and fake "Dawn" groups started appearing, Orlando recruited background singers Thelma Hopkins and Joyce Vincent to become the "real Dawn" for touring. "So Tony Orlando agreed to record the vocals for the song but he was a little iffy about it because he was working for a different record label and to record this song would have meant well he was working for a competitor. **Interracial Makeup** : Tony Orlando and Dawn was unique for its interracial makeup, with Orlando (of Greek and Puerto Rican descent, identifying as white) performing with two African American singers, Thelma Hopkins and Joyce Vincent. Orlando was reportedly advised in 1974 that such a "black and white act" could not succeed. "Orlando said in 1974 that he was told he was nuts to leave the music publishing business to go on the road with two African American singers. He said there was no way a black and white act could make it."

"Tim Burke writing for Rolling Stone in 1975 said the music was very innocuous and very pleasant after years of pop music striving for significance."
**TV Show Popularity (1975)** : The group's music, despite being described as innocuous, was boosted by the incredible popularity of their TV show, "Tony Orlando and Dawn," in 1975, which garnered attention even from publications like Rolling Stone. "Tony actually seemed surprised that Rolling Stone was the least bit interested in the group's music, but I think that this shows just how incredibly popular the TV show Tony Orlando and Don was in 1975."

# curiosity

**Chicano Movement Origins and Goals** : The Chicano movement of the 1960s and 70s originated from Mexican American veterans returning from World War II with unfulfilled expectations of social and political equality. It involved community organization in states like Texas, Arizona, and California, youth leadership, and activism for migrant workers led by Dolores Huerta and Cesar Chavez, pushing for political representation and an end to discrimination. "The Chicano movement of the 60s and 70s was in many ways born out of World War II. Returning Mexican American veterans had expectations of some social and political equality that were not met."
**Civil Rights vs. Chicano Movement Musical Reflection** : Unlike the Civil Rights Movement, which had a pop music soundtrack, the Chicano movement lacked a similar mainstream pop or rock music soundtrack, primarily due to language barriers, as hit records for the most part needed to be at least partially in English. "A difference between these two movements is how they were reflected musically. There is not a pop music soundtrack for the Chicano movement in the same way that there is for the civil rights movement."
**Mexican American Population Growth (1970s)** : During the 1970s, the Mexican American population in the United States experienced a significant growth of 93%, reaching 8.7 million, and spread out beyond the American Southwest. "the United States was about to enter a decade when the Mexican American population grew 93% to 8.7 million in the 1970s."
**Origin of the Term "Hispanic"** : The US government created the term "Hispanic" in the early 1970s as an umbrella term for Spanish Americans, though the term "Latino" is now more commonly favored. "In fact, the government created the term Hispanic in the early 70s to create this umbrella term for Spanish Americans, although now the term Latino is much more in favor."
**Delano Grape Strike (Late 1960s)** : The Delano Grape Strike involved migrant Mexican American and Filipino grape pickers who went on strike, facing harassment and threats. It was supported by artists like Santana who played a benefit concert for the farm workers, and famously involved Cesar Chavez's hunger strike, ultimately leading to pickers agreeing to $1.80 per hour plus 20 cents per box in 1970. "Santana played again at the Filmor West, but this time they were playing to help raise money for the farm workers who were taking part in the Delano Grape Strike."
**Diversity of 1971 Billboard Pop Chart** : The Billboard Pop Chart in January 1971 showcased a remarkable diversity in popular music, with the top five including "I Think I Love You" by The Partridge Family (#5), "Black Magic Woman" by Santana (#4), "One Less Bell to Answer" by The Fifth Dimension (#3), "Knock Three Times" by Dawn (#2), and "My Sweet Lord" by George Harrison (#1). "That was a really crazy top five, by the way, which really showed the diversity of what was considered pop in 1971."
**Wider Spread of Hispanic Culture (1970s)** : The 1970s saw a wider spread of Hispanic culture in the US, reflected in popular culture through initiatives like children learning Spanish on "Sesame Street" and the proliferation of salsa nightclubs, which provided a more receptive environment for Spanish-language music. "It certainly did not hurt that there was this wider spread of Hispanic culture in the US at that time, whether it was little kids learning Spanish on Sesame Street or salsa night clubs."
**Billboard Magazine's "Latin Music Scene" Column** : During the 1970s, Billboard magazine recognized the growing prominence of Latin music by featuring a dedicated column for the "Latin music scene," along with special surveys of Latin albums in the US. "If you go back and look at the issues of Billboard magazine from this time frame, you can see that the Latin music scene, as they called it, had its own column."
**Changes in Country Radio (Late 1960s/Early 1970s)** : In an effort to attract more listeners, country radio stations in the late 1960s and early 1970s hired disc jockeys who, often lacking country music knowledge, were instructed to avoid music with "too much twang or fiddle" and stick to hits. These rock-bred DJs favored country artists who incorporated a "rock edge" like Waylon Jennings and Willie Nelson, benefiting artists such as Freddie Fender. "These DJs who were born and bred on rock tended to favor the country artists who had a little bit more of a rock edge to them, kind of like Whalen Jennings, Willie Nelson. Freddie Fender was a beneficiary of this too."
**The TV Show "Chico and the Man" (1974-1978)** : This sitcom, starring Freddie Prinz as Chico and Jack Albertson as Ed, aired from 1974 to 1978, with its existence partly credited to the rise of the Chicano movement and increased awareness of Mexican American and Hispanic portrayals on camera. Prinz, a working comedian, incorporated his Puerto Rican ethnicity and "ethnic one liners" into his character, though the show's bantering between Chico and the cantankerous Ed was often criticized for being mean-spirited from Ed's perspective. "The very existence of the series can be credited at least in part to the rise of the Chicano movement and the beginning of this increased awareness of how Mexican Americans and Hispanic people were portrayed on camera."
**Freddie Prinz's Portrayal of Chico** : Freddie Prinz, who was Puerto Rican, was cast as Chico, a young Mexican American man, because of his strong chemistry with co-star Jack Albertson. While advocacy groups were upset a Puerto Rican played a Mexican American, Prinz was a comedian who integrated his ethnicity into his material, borrowing "ethnic one liners" from his stand-up acts for the show. "Prinz was hired to play Chico because he had the best chemistry with Jack Albertson. He was already a working comedian who made his ethnicity a part of his material. The show actually borrowed from Freddie's comedy shows."
**Freddie Prinz's Tragic Death (January 1977)** : Freddie Prinz died by suicide in January 1977 at the young age of 22, leading to the final year of "Chico and the Man" airing without him. His untimely death is likened to Richie Valens's, as it robbed the public of seeing where he might have further advanced representation for Latin Americans on television. "He died from suicide in January. 1977 at the age of 22, the very existence of the series can be credited at least in part to the rise of the Chicano movement and the beginning of this increased awareness of how Mexican Americans and Hispanic people were portrayed on camera."
**Frito Bandido Commercial Character** : The Frito Bandido was a "horrible" Frito Lays commercial character, representative of the negative portrayals of Mexican Americans on camera that the Chicano movement sought to challenge. "You might remember or have heard of this Frito Lays commercial that had this horrible Frito Bandido character, things like that."
**Educational Reforms from Chicano Movement** : The youth activism within the Chicano movement led to significant educational reforms, including a Supreme Court ruling affirming non-English speaking students' legal right to public school education. This also spurred the implementation of more bilingual education programs, mandated by Congress's Equal Opportunity Act of 1974, and the expansion of the Voting Rights Act of 1965 in 1975 to include language assistance at polling places. "The youth activism within the Chicano movement led to various educational reforms, including the Supreme Court ruling that non-English speaking students are lawfully entitled to a public school education."
