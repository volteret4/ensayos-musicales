# artist - Casey Kasem

## curiosities
**Early Career and Voice Persona** : Casey Kasem was known as a DJ and a B-grade actor, notably providing the voice for Shaggy in Scooby Doo. Initially, he adopted an FM underground radio station sound to avoid appearing "bubblegum," as pop music was not considered "cool" in 1970 when rock and roll was dominant. Over time, his persona evolved to a slightly hip-dad vibe, especially during the disco era, where he was upbeat and sincere. "He admits that he was trying to not sound quote to bubblegum because a show that is highly dependent on pop music was just not considered cool in 1970."
**Inspiration for Trivia Segments** : The idea for Casey Kasem's popular trivia bits, which offered teasers before commercial breaks and kept listeners engaged week after week, came from a book he found in the trash at a San Francisco radio station. The book, titled "Who's Who in Pop Music 1962," became a fortuitous discovery after his station manager asked him to incorporate more information about musicians and less comedy into his broadcasts. "The inspiration for that part of the show was a who's who in pop music 1962, this book that Casey found in the trash at the radio station in San Francisco where he worked."
**Elvis Presley Tribute (1977)** : Following Elvis Presley's death on August 16, 1977, Casey Kasem delivered a heartfelt tribute on American Top 40, acknowledging Elvis's monumental impact as "the most important individual of the rock era." He highlighted Elvis's journey from a polite country boy driving a delivery truck to one of the century's most salable entertainment talents, crediting Colonel Tom Parker with his discovery (though the transcript corrects this to Sam Phillips's secretary). "This is Kasey Kasey on American Top 40, AT 40 originates in Hollywood and our countdown continues. And now I have the most important individual of the rock era, the man who broke open the dam that flooded the world with rock and roll."

## curiosities
**Twelve Top 10 Records** : Elvis Presley was noted as one of only two solo acts to achieve 12 top 10 records in a 10-year period, alongside Marvin Gaye. "Elvis Presley and this next singer are the only two solo acts to have 12 top 10 records in the past 10 years."
**Jungle Room Recording Sessions (1976)** : The "Jungle Room," originally the den off the kitchen at Graceland, was converted into a recording studio by RCA in October 1976 to encourage Elvis Presley to record. These sessions, which produced his final hit "Way Down," were reportedly chaotic. Elvis was overweight, anxious, and financially unstable, largely due to spending habits and dependence on prescription drugs supplied by Dr. Nick. He struggled to focus, disappearing upstairs, demanding everyone test new motorcycles, and even playfully threatening to blow up speakers with a machine gun. "I think it is safe to say that the recording session that produced way down was a bit of a disaster."
**Posthumous Chart Revival** : After Elvis Presley's death on August 16, 1977, his single "Way Down" experienced a significant chart resurgence. The song was already "on the way out" of the Billboard Hot 100 when he died but reversed course, climbing back up to number 18 on the Hot 100. "Way down was on the way out of the Billboard Hot 100 when Elvis died. And then after his death, the song reversed course and made it back up all the way to number 18 on the Hot 100."
**Discovery Controversy** : Casey Kasem's tribute to Elvis Presley on American Top 40 credited Colonel Tom Parker with discovering Elvis. However, the true credit for discovering Elvis is attributed more accurately to Sam Phillips, or specifically, to the secretary who worked for Sam Phillips at Sun Records. "Of course Tom Parker didn't discover Elvis. He did discover how much money could be made from Elvis, but Sam Phillips, more accurately, the secretary who worked for Sam Phillips at Sun Records actually, discovered Elvis."
**Jungle Room Outtakes (2016)** : In 2016, outtakes from the notorious "Jungle Room" recording sessions were released, offering a fascinating listen. These recordings provide insight into Elvis's interactions with his band amidst his personal struggles, showcasing that he still cared deeply about his music despite his pain. "In 2016, the Jungle Room recording session outtakes were released. And it's an interesting listen because you can hear Elvis banter a little bit with his band through all of the pain that this man was in."

# artist - Rupert Holmes

## genres
Pop
Musical Theatre

## songs
**Escape (The Piña Colada Song) (1979)** : This song became the last number one song of the 1970s, hitting the top spot on December 22, 1979, and remaining there for three weeks into the 1980s. Originally titled "Escape," it was renamed due to public demand in record shops. Rupert Holmes wrote the song when he needed an upbeat track to complement an album full of ballads, focusing on lyrics due to the repetitive music. The inspiration came from personal ads in The Village Voice, with Holmes pondering what would happen if he answered one, surmising it would probably be his own wife. His slightly less-than-perfect singing voice was considered crucial to the song's success, making him sound like a relatable character. "The last number one song of the 70s, making it there on December 22nd, 1979, and staying there for three weeks to take us into the 1980s, that song was everywhere."

## curiosities
**Multi-Talented Artist** : Rupert Holmes is most remembered for "Escape (The Piña Colada Song)," though he also has a distinguished career in musical theatre, having written the musical "The Mystery of Edwin Drood" for which he won Emmys and Tonys. He has also written a novel that was adapted into a movie. Despite these accomplishments, he remains predominantly identified with his most famous song. "He for example also wrote the musical, The Mystery of Edwin Druid, He's One Emmys, He's One Tonys, He's written a novel that was made into a movie, but he will always be the Pina Colada Song Guy."
**Song Title Evolution** : The song "Escape (The Piña Colada Song)" was originally just titled "Escape." However, due to popular demand and the way people requested it in record shops, it was officially retitled to "The Piña Colada Song" to match how the public identified it. "The song was originally just called Escape, but when people went into the record shops to request the Pina Colada Song, and there was no record called the Pina Colada Song, the logic dictates that you make it the Pina Colada Song."
**Lyric Writing Process** : Rupert Holmes wrote "Escape (The Piña Colada Song)" when he needed an upbeat track to balance an album of ballads. With the music already composed and being repetitive, he focused intensely on the lyrics, trying various ideas. He discarded some, like "That's the law of the jungle in the school of the street," finding them bad and sounding like a Billy Joel song. He also considered "Cruel to Be Kind" but realized Nick Lowe already had a song with that title. "He had the music already, which is quite repetitive, so he needed the lyrics to be the focal point."
**Personal Ad Inspiration** : The inspiration for the lyrics of "Escape (The Piña Colada Song)" came from a copy of The Village Voice and its personal ads, which happened to be lying nearby. Rupert Holmes wondered what would happen if he answered one of these ads, immediately thinking it would probably turn out to be his own wife. This spontaneous thought became the core narrative of the song. "As sometimes happens with writers, he was inspired by something that just happened to be laying nearby. In this case, it was a copy of the village voice and the personal ads."
**Vocal Delivery as Key to Success** : Rupert Holmes's singing voice, described as "not that great," was considered a crucial element in the success of "Escape (The Piña Colada Song)." It made him sound like an ordinary person who would respond to a personal ad and contemplate cheating on his partner, lending authenticity to the song's narrative that a more polished singer, like Neil Diamond, might not have achieved. "I mean, if somebody with real pipes sang this, like if Neil Diamond sang it, it just wouldn't work. # curiosity

**American Top 40 Debut (1970)** : American Top 40 launched on July 4, 1970, debuting on just seven stations. The inaugural episode was recorded in a small Southern California studio, famously featuring furniture built by a local carpenter aspiring to be an actor, Harrison Ford. The show's first song played was Marvin Gaye's "End of Our Road" at number 40, and Three Dog Night's "Mama Told Me Not To Come" was the first number one hit. "When American top 40 debuted, it was on seven stations."
**Billboard Magazine as Source** : American Top 40, hosted by Casey Kasem and co-created with Don Bustany, Tom Rounds, and Ron Jacobs, utilized Billboard magazine as its primary source for counting down the top 40 hits in the United States. The show's creators vowed to play every song on the list, regardless of genre, in order. "Kaseyam along with Don Vostani, Tom Browns, and Ron Jacobs used Billboard magazine as their source for this national radio show that counted down the top 40 hits in the United States."
**Early Song Length Traditions** : For many years, a strong tradition in radio dictated that singles should be no longer than three minutes, or they wouldn't get airplay. This began to change in the mid to late 1970s, as songs became progressively longer, eventually necessitating the expansion of American Top 40 to a four-hour show. "For many years, it was tradition that it was no longer than three minutes. If it was longer than that, it wasn't going to be on the radio."
**AT40 Censorship and Affiliate Autonomy** : American Top 40 had a policy of editing offensive words, such as "shit" from Bob Dylan's "George Jackson" and issuing warnings about words like "crap" in Paul Simon's "Code of Chrome." However, they refused to outright eliminate songs based on genre or content, allowing individual affiliated stations the option to edit out songs like Chuck Berry's "My Ding-a-Ling" if they chose. "American top 40 was not going to eliminate any songs, although they did shorten some as singles became more and more lengthy."
**Regional vs. National Radio Playlists** : In the 1970s, radio playlists were highly regional, meaning different cities often heard different songs on the air. American Top 40 aimed to transcend this by providing a national countdown based on Billboard's sales data from about 100 U.S. cities, exposing listeners to hits popular elsewhere. This approach, however, sometimes caused friction with local program directors who preferred to tailor their content to their specific market, especially regarding R&B songs in the first hour of the show. "Remember, as I've said many times, this is an era when radio was still regional. You heard different playlists in different cities."
**Program Directors' Complaints (1973)** : In 1973, some radio station program directors, such as J.J. Jordan of WGRQ in Buffalo, New York, expressed complaints about American Top 40's music balance, particularly the "high clusters of R&B material" in the first hour. These directors argued that such songs might not be pop hits in their rock-oriented markets. Tom Rounds responded in a memo dated October 5, 1973, acknowledging the "problem" but asserting AT40's reluctance to prejudge records, citing examples like "The Funky Worm" that became unexpected national hits. "Because that is where you tended to hear more R&B songs. And you might say like three or four in a row or maybe five out of ten. Some stations did not like that."
**Tom Rounds's Defense of AT40's Inclusivity (1973)** : In an October 5, 1973, memo to WGRQ program director J.J. Jordan, American Top 40 co-creator Tom Rounds addressed concerns about the show's music balance, specifically the concentration of R&B in the first hour. Rounds stated that Billboard's data sometimes included records "so new they haven't broken in some markets," and AT40 was "super reluctant to make a judgment ourselves as to whether a particular record does or doesn't belong in a top 40." He emphasized the show's role in giving records "another chance on a national basis," citing how perceived "stiffs" could become giants while "sure shots" often fizzled. "For this reason, we are super reluctant to make a judgment ourselves as to whether a particular record does or doesn't belong in a top 40."
**The Funky Worm as an Unexpected Hit** : Tom Rounds cited "The Funky Worm" as an example of a song that, despite early skepticism, went on to become a pop hit. This was used to justify American Top 40's policy of playing all charted songs without making snap judgments about their potential success in different markets. "He goes on to say, who knew, for example, that the funky worm would become a pop hit?"

**Long Distance Dedications (1978)** : Introduced in 1978, the "Long Distance Dedication" segment became a highlight of American Top 40.
