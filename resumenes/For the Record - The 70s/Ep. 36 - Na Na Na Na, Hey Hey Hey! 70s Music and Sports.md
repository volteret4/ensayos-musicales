**Protest Song Climate in 1975** : The climate for protest songs in 1975 was significantly different and more challenging than in earlier years like 1965 or even 1971-72. **Organ Music in Baseball Stadiums** : Starting in the 1940s, organ music became a tradition during baseball games. **Nancy Faust's Innovative Organ Playing** : Nancy Faust, the organist for the Chicago White Sox for 40 years, revolutionized ballpark music by interjecting contemporary songs into games, a practice not common at the time. She didn't read music; instead, she would buy 45s of pop/rock songs, listen repeatedly to learn them, and then deploy them at opportune moments. Her famous playing of "Na Na Hey Hey Kiss Him Goodbye" in 1977 during a White Sox vs. Kansas City Royals game, as manager Whiti Herzog removed his pitcher, led to the song becoming a deeply ingrained sports chant. "Nancy Faust doesn't read music. **We Are Family (1979)** : Kim Sledge stated that the group had committed to playing in Brussels, even at a point when the Pittsburgh Pirates were down three to one to Baltimore. The song's lyrics are uncomplicated, but its significance stems from its powerful message of unity combined with the success of the Pittsburgh Pirates and the team's embrace of this message. # curiosity

**Pittsburgh Pirates 1979 Roster and Caps** : The 1979 championship roster for the Pittsburgh Pirates notably included 15 black and Hispanic players out of a 25-man team. This racial composition contributed to the team's iconic throwback baseball caps becoming highly popular among young African Americans in Minnesota, as noted by sports writer Jay Weiner in an 1981 article for the Minneapolis Star Tribune. However, Weiner also observed that the team was not particularly popular with black people within Pittsburgh itself, and white residents in Pittsburgh sometimes referred to them as the Homestead Grays, a famous Negro league team from the city. "There were 15 black and Hispanic players on the 25 man roster for the 1979 championship team."

**Symbolism of Black Achievement in Sports** : Tracy Thompson, a 22-year-old African American working at a Minneapolis community center, highlighted the profound importance of "We Are Family" and the Pittsburgh Pirates' success to young black people nationally. He explained that when a black individual like Willie Stargell achieves world renown, the wider black community feels a personal connection and pride in that accomplishment, seeing it as a shared success rather than merely a triumph over a white counterpart. "It's that I'm proud a black man did it."

**Music and Sports Connection** : The intrinsic link between music and sports is evident, as both serve as potent forms of entertainment that generate experiences and lasting memories. When music and sports are combined, they create a unique experience and memory that effectively harnesses the power of both mediums. "When you combine the two, you have created all at once an experience and ultimately a memory that harnesses the power of both."
