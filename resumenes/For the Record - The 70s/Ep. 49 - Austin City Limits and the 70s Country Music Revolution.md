**Bloody Merry (1970)** : This was the original title of "Bloody Mary Morning" and was included on Willie Nelson's 1970 album, "Both Sides Now." "So the original title was Bloody Mary, M-E-R-R-Y, not M-A-R-Y as it is now known."
**Bloody Mary Morning (1974)** : This song was the third in Willie Nelson's set list for the Austin City Limits pilot in 1974 and was crucial for his desired creative control. Originally written by Nelson for his 1970 album "Both Sides Now" (then titled "Bloody Merry"), it was heard by Atlantic Records VP Jerry Wexler in Nashville in 1972, leading to Nelson's signing with Atlantic for their new country division. He rewrote it for "Phases and Stages," changing the spelling to "M-A-R-Y" and the narrative to a man migrating to Texas from Los Angeles to forget a former lover. It reached number 17 on the Billboard Country Chart in 1974 and became a staple of Nelson's live shows. It was produced by R&B legend Jerry Wexler and inspired by Nelson's desire to be a better parent. "Bloody Mary Morning, that is actually from the pilot episode of Austin City Limits that was written by Willie Nelson produced by R&B Legend, Jerry Wexler and forever after that a staple of Willie's live shows."

## curiosities
**Nashville House Fire (1969)** : Willie Nelson's house in Nashville burned down right before Christmas in 1969. This event, combined with his feeling constrained by the type of music country purists expected, led him to return to his home state of Texas. "Willie Nelson's Nashville house burned down right before Christmas in 1969."
**Austin Refuge for Progressive Artists** : Willie Nelson, favoring long hair and denim over "sparkling nudie suits," found Austin, Texas, to be a refuge in the early to mid-1970s. This was due to Austin's liberal-minded college town atmosphere, which accepted his radical appearance and provided artistic freedom away from Nashville's polished country sound. "Nelson preferred to do that. Did that preferred the denim over the sparkling nudie suits that were so popular too?"
**Creative Control at Atlantic Records (1972)** : Jerry Wexler, VP of Atlantic Records, offered Willie Nelson a "deal he could not refuse" after hearing "Bloody Mary Morning" at a Nashville party in 1972. This deal was significant not just for the money, but for the creative control it granted Nelson, allowing him to make critically acclaimed records like "Shotgun Willie" and "Phases and Stages." "He offers Nelson a deal he could not refuse, which was as much about creative control as it was about the money."
**Iconic Austin City Limits Debut (1974)** : Willie Nelson's performance on October 17, 1974, served as the official pilot for Austin City Limits. A concerted effort was made to ensure a large audience, which resulted in people being "crammed everywhere," with fans even sitting on the stage. He played a 20-song set, opening with "Whiskey River." "Scott Newton who was a photographer for Austin City Limits said, there would be no Austin City Limits if there was no Willie Nelson."
**Statue on Willie Nelson Boulevard** : Austin City Limits now has a new, bigger studio located on Willie Nelson Boulevard, featuring a statue of Willie Nelson out front. "Austin City Limits now is it's a whole brand. They have a new bigger studio on Willie Nelson Boulevard. There's a statue of Willie out front."

# artist - B.W. Stevenson

## albums
**Eight Albums (1972-1980)** : B.W. Stevenson was a prolific writer, releasing eight albums between 1972 and 1980. Despite his output, none of these albums translated into significant solo success for him. "He wrote eight albums between 1972 and 1980, but none of it really translated to success for him, at least as a solo artist."

## songs
**Shambhala (1973)** : B.W. Stevenson collaborated with Daniel Moore on this song and released his version before "My Maria." However, Three Dog Night's version, released a week later, became a much bigger hit, reaching number three on the Billboard Hot 100 in 1973. "They also collaborated on another song that you might be familiar with, Shambhala, and Stevenson released his version of that before My Maria, but the version of Shambhala that was released a week after that by three dog night was a much bigger hit."
**My Maria (1973)** : B.W. Stevenson released this song in August 1973, and it went to number nine on the Billboard Hot 100 in the same month. Daniel Moore had been working on the lyrics for two years when Stevenson quickly finished them, with Moore stating he likely wouldn't have completed the song without Stevenson's help. Ronnie Dunn of Brooks & Dunn later covered it in 1996 but was initially hesitant, viewing it as a rock song and wary of its falsetto. "My Maria by BW Stevenson released in August 1973."

## curiosities
**Early Career and Progressive Country (1970s)** : B.W. Stevenson was a pioneer of the progressive country movement, alongside artists like Willie Nelson and Jerry Jeff Walker. For a period, he sold shoes during the day and played music at night, suggesting his dedication to his craft despite not yet achieving widespread commercial success as a solo artist. "Like Willie Nelson and Jerry Jeff Walker and others, he was, though, a pioneer of the progressive country movement."
**Respected Session Musician** : Throughout his career, B.W. Stevenson was highly regarded as a session musician, even if his solo work didn't always achieve commercial success. "He was always well respected as a session musician."
**Aborted Austin City Limits Pilot Performance (1974)** : B.W. Stevenson was scheduled to perform for the pilot episode of Austin City Limits alongside Willie Nelson in 1974. His performance was ultimately scrapped due to technical issues and poor publicity leading to a low audience, not because of any fault of his own. Keith Mateland, director of a documentary about Austin City Limits, commented that his success on the pilot would have benefited Stevenson more than the show. "So Stevenson did perform for the pilot episode of Austin City Limits as did Willie Nelson, but according to Bruce Skate, there were some technical issues and there were not a lot of people in the audience for Stevenson's performance."
**Later Austin City Limits Appearance (1976)** : Although his pilot performance was scrapped, B.W. Stevenson did get another opportunity to perform on Austin City Limits in 1976. However, this appearance did not carry the same weight as being on the pilot during the peak of his hit song "My Maria." "Stevenson did get another opportunity to be on Austin City Limits. He was on in 1976, but you know, it's not the same as being on the pilot when he is still riding the wave of a big hit."

## songs
**Hello in There (1971)** : Written by John Prine when he was 25 years old, this acoustic song was one of two with which he opened his first Austin City Limits show in 1978. Broadly, the song is about not forgetting the elderly, reflecting Prine's unique perspective and storytelling ability at a young age. "He opened the show with two acoustic songs, Fish and Whistle, a song from his new album, and hello in there, a 1971 song that he wrote when he was 25 years old."
**Angel from Montgomery (1971)** : Written by John Prine around 1970 or 1971 when he was 25, this song tells the story from the perspective of a middle-aged woman who feels old and trapped in a "thankless marriage." Prine conceived the idea while working as a mailman, visualizing a woman by a dishwasher who wished for an angel to take her away. He recorded it himself, but his friend Bonnie Raitt's 1974 recording made it a hit. Prine and Raitt performed it together multiple times, including on Austin City Limits in 2002. "Make me an angel that flies from Montgomery. Make me a poster of an old rodeo. Just give me one thing that I can hold on to to believe in this living is just a hard way to go."

## curiosities
**"Mark Twain of American Songwriting"** : In a 2017 Rolling Stone article, Patrick Doyle referred to John Prine as "the Mark Twain of American Songwriting" due to his exceptional storytelling ability. His music offered reassurance and resonated with those seeking comfort in life's bigger problems, making him a perfect fit for Austin City Limits despite his hard-to-categorize blend of country, folk, and blues. "In a Rolling Stone article about John Prine's life that was published in 2017, Patrick Doyle referred to Prine as the Mark Twain of American Songwriting."
**Mailman as Lyricist** : Before his musical career took off, John Prine worked as a mailman. He would use the time walking his mail route to think through his lyrics, indicating a deep engagement with his songwriting process even during his day job. "Now back then, Prine was a male man. And so he would just use that time when he was out walking his route to think through his lyrics."
**Perspective in Songwriting** : John Prine demonstrated a unique empathy in his songwriting, notably with "Angel from Montgomery," written at age 25 from the perspective of a middle-aged woman feeling trapped and old. Bonnie Raitt, a young feminist at the time, deeply resonated with this song's portrayal of a woman in a thankless marriage, highlighting Prine's ability to transcend his own experiences. "It's a song written by a 25 year old man in 70 or 71 whenever he actually wrote it. From the perspective of a middle-aged woman who feels old and it works, Bonnie said that as a young feminist that there was something about a song written by a man from the perspective of a woman who was in kind of a, what seemed like a thankless marriage that really resonated with her."
**Eight Austin City Limits Appearances (1978-2002)** : John Prine performed on Austin City Limits a total of eight times, with his first appearance in 1978 and another notable one in 2002 where he sang "Angel from Montgomery" with Bonnie Raitt. "One Prine was on Austin City Limits eight times, the first time being in 1978."
**Songwriter's Hall of Fame Induction (2019)** : John Prine was inducted into the Songwriter's Hall of Fame in 2019 by his friend, Bonnie Raitt, celebrating his profound contributions to American songwriting. "John Prine was inducted into the song writer's Hall of Fame in 2019, inducted by his friend, Bonnie Raid."
**Austin City Limits Hall of Fame Induction (2024)** : In January 2024, John Prine was inducted into the Austin City Limits Hall of Fame during its ninth annual ceremony. Actor Ethan Hawke delivered the induction speech, praising Prine as a singer-songwriter who pursued excellence and truth over fame. "In January 2024, Austin City Limits held its ninth annual Hall of Fame ceremony and John Prine was inducted into that Hall of Fame."
**Post-COVID Loss** : The transcript notes the significant loss felt by John Prine's passing during the COVID pandemic, highlighting his immense value as a songwriter. "And John Prine's loss, that's still hurt."

## curiosities
**Formation and Byrds/Burrito Brothers Connection (1974)** : Firefall formed in Boulder, Colorado, in 1974, with founding members including Rick Roberts (who had replaced Gram Parsons in The Flying Burrito Brothers) and Michael Clarke (drummer for The Byrds and The Flying Burrito Brothers). This lineage links Firefall directly to country rock pioneers. "Their DNA is in the music of the Eagles and their DNA is in the band that formed in Boulder, Colorado in 1974, Firefall."

He stated that every part played by the band, including David Muse on saxes, flute, and keyboards, was to further the song, which contributed to their first three records sounding great even today. "It wasn't really what you played, it's what you didn't play, the spaces you leave."
**Sound Description (Rick Roberts)** : Rick Roberts, Firefall's lead singer, described the band's sound as "swish," characterizing it as a smooth beat that just flows. "Rick Roberts was asked to describe Firefall's sound and he said, swish, a smooth beat that just flows."

**The Devil Went Down to Georgia (1979)** : This song, from the 1979 album "Million Mile Reflections," was originally intended as instrumental filler with no lyrics, to provide fiddle music to fill gaps on the album. Lyrics were later added, developing a "sell your soul narrative" about a character named Johnny. It was inspired by Stephen Vincent Benét's poem "The Mountain Whippoorwill." Charlie Daniels considered the fiddle a bridge from rock to the band's bluegrass roots. The song became a huge crossover hit, reaching number three on the Billboard Hot 100 in 1979, and was prominently featured in the 1980 movie "Urban Cowboy," introducing its "bad-ass fiddle playing" to new audiences and regions. The Charlie Daniels Band performed it on Austin City Limits in 1981. "The devil went down to Georgia made it to number three on the billboard hot 100 in 1979. Yes, it was a huge crossover hit."

## curiosities
**Versatility of Charlie Daniels** : Charlie Daniels was a well-respected and versatile musician. He played guitar and bass on Bob Dylan's classic "Nashville Skyline" and also "hung out with the Beatles," highlighting his broad musical connections and talents beyond his own band. "I mean this guy, I mean he played guitar and bass on Bob Dylan's classic Nashville Skyline. He hung out with the Beatles."
**First Nashville Band on Austin City Limits (1976)** : The Charlie Daniels Band was the first Nashville-based band to perform on Austin City Limits in 1976. Their inclusion marked a necessary expansion for the show beyond Austin-centric artists, showcasing their blend of jazzy, bluesy rock with country roots. "By that, I mean they had to include some Nashville bands and the first Nashville band to be on Austin City Limits was the Charlie Daniels band in 76."
**Uncategorizable Music (1980 Interview)** : In a 1980 interview, Charlie Daniels expressed difficulty in categorizing his band's music, stating, "It ain't country. It ain't rock." He criticized radio for categorizing things too closely, emphasizing his preference for variety. "I can't describe my music. It ain't country. It ain't rock. Radio categorizes things too closely. They seem to do, they see too much in black and white and I like variety in life."
**Embodiment of Austin City Limits' Mission** : The Charlie Daniels Band, with its diverse sound rooted in country but incorporating jazz, blues, and rock, was considered the epitome of what Austin City Limits aimed to accomplish: showcasing a wide variety of music with country roots. "In many ways the Charlie Daniels band was really the epitome of what Austin City Limits was trying to accomplish, which was to showcase a wide variety of music that had its roots in country."
**Fiddle Technique for Grittier Sound** : Charlie Daniels intentionally pressed down harder on the fiddle strings to achieve a grittier sound, demonstrating a specific instrumental technique contributing to their distinctive musical style. "Charlie Daniels said that by pressing down harder on the fiddle strings, it made for a grittier sound."
**Prominent Feature in "Urban Cowboy" (1980)** : The Charlie Daniels Band, and specifically their song "The Devil Went Down to Georgia," were prominently featured in the final part of the 1980 movie "Urban Cowboy," particularly during the indoor rodeo scenes at Gilley's. This exposed their music and fiddle playing to a massive new audience across the United States. "Kids, if you like that fiddle music, you might just want to go check out Urban Cowboy, especially the last part of the movie that prominently features the Charlie Daniels band at that big indoor rodeo at Gillies."
