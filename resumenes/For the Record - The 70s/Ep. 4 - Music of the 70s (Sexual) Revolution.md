# artist - Loretta Lynn

## genres
Country Music

## albums
**The Pill (1975) - Controversial Release** : Loretta Lynn's record label initially refused to release this record, recorded in 1972, until 1975 due to its controversial subject matter. The album, which features the song "The Pill," celebrates the empowerment of women through birth control, allowing them to control their reproductive lives and enjoy sex without the fear of pregnancy. "However, Loretta's record label would not release this record until 1975."

## songs
**The Pill (1972) - Anthem for Birth Control** : This song, written by Lorraine Allen, Don McCann, and T.D. Bayless, and produced by country music legend Owen Bradley, was recorded in 1972 but not released by Loretta's record label until 1975. The lyrics celebrate the freedom offered by the birth control pill, with the singer expressing joy at no longer being a "baby making machine" and being able to have sex for fun. Its progressive message about female sexual autonomy was considered "distasteful" by some radio stations in 1975, yet it became a reflection of the importance of the pill in both the sexual revolution and the women's rights movement. "In 1972 she recorded the song The Pill, which was written by Lorraine Allen, Don McCann, and T.D. Bayless."

## curiosities
**Early Marriage and Motherhood** : Loretta Lynn famously married at the age of 15 and gave birth to the first of her five children when she was 16. This personal history positioned her uniquely to celebrate and advocate for the arrival of the birth control pill. "Loretta famously married at the age of 15 and gave birth to the first of her five children at the age of 16."
**Abusive Husband Doolittle Lynn** : Loretta's husband, Doolittle Lynn, was described as an "alcoholic abuse of jerk" who frequently cheated on her, even leaving her while she was pregnant and sleeping with his own sister-in-law. This challenging personal life context further justified Loretta's celebration of the birth control pill. "Do little Lynn, her husband, was such an alcoholic abuse of jerk that he cheated on her with such regularity he didn't even bother to try to hide it."
**Fearless Expression** : Loretta Lynn earned a reputation for not being afraid to express her opinions, a characteristic powerfully embodied by her willingness to release "The Pill," a song that boldly reflected her views on women's rights and the sexual revolution. "Loretta Lynn has never been accused of being afraid of speaking her mind."

## albums
**Let's Get It On (1973) - Erotic and Socially Aware** : Released in 1973, this album featured liner notes written by Marvin Gaye himself, which included a quote from T.S. Eliot: "birth and copulation and death." While undoubtedly about sex, the album is also described as being as "socially and politically aware" as his earlier "What's Going On." Sociology professor Michael Eric Dyson praised it as a brave album for a black man to create such erotic content, directly countering the black exploitation films of the era by celebrating black sexuality, eroticism, and love. It reflects a part of the sexual revolution that viewed sexual freedom as beautiful, natural, and potentially a "gift from God." "Marvin Gaye released the Let's Get An On album in 1973."
**What's Going On (1971) - Socially Aware Precedent** : Released two years prior to "Let's Get It On," this album is noted for its profound social and political awareness. It served as a benchmark for conscious artistry that "Let's Get It On" matched, despite its different thematic focus. "This is as socially and politically aware an album as what's going on, which was released two years prior to this."

## songs
**Let's Get It On (1973) - Spirituality in Eroticism** : The title track features Marvin Gaye singing "I've been sanctified," which carries a genuine spiritual tone despite the song's overtly sexual themes. The presence of handclaps in the background gives the song a "gospel kind of feel," subtly weaving his background as a minister's son and spiritual undertones into the erotic context. "Even in Let's Get It On, he sings, I've been sanctified."
**You Sure Love a Ball (1973) - Explicit Sexual Content** : This track from the "Let's Get It On" album is described as "blatantly sexual" and notably includes "a woman moaning in the background," serving to explicitly reinforce the album's central theme of sexual celebration and freedom. "Even as this album, which includes the blatantly sexual usher-loved aball, which has a woman moaning in the background, is clearly about sex, but it's not faultless sex."

## curiosities
**Minister's Son and Spirituality** : Marvin Gaye was the son of a minister, a background that influenced the spiritual undertones often present in his music, even in his most sensual work. This spiritual dimension, for instance in lyrics like "I've been sanctified" from "Let's Get It On," provides depth to his exploration of sexuality. "We should also not forget that he was the son of a minister, the man who by the way would shoot him and kill him in 1984."
**Tragic Death** : In 1984, Marvin Gaye was tragically shot and killed by his own father. "We should also not forget that he was the son of a minister, the man who by the way would shoot him and kill him in 1984."

# artist - Donna Summer

## genres
Disco

## labels
Casablanca Records

## albums
**Love to Love You Baby (1975) - Extended Sensuality** : The album features a 17-minute version of its title track, which famously occupied the entire Side One. This extended version was conceived after Neil Bogart, founder of Casablanca Records, observed the mood-setting power of the original shorter track at a party and sought to create a record specifically for relaxation and lovemaking without interruption. "Well, Neil Bogart got a 17-minute version, which became the entire side, side one of the album love to love you, baby."

## songs
**Love to Love You Baby (1975) - Queen of Disco's Erotic Breakthrough** : This song established Donna Summer as the "queen of Disco" and became renowned for its explicit pushing of boundaries in female sexual expression. Initially 3 minutes and 50 seconds, Neil Bogart requested a longer version after witnessing its impact at a party, leading to the iconic 17-minute track. Donna Summer reportedly recorded the song's sensual vocal moans and breathing in a dark booth, lying on the floor, due to her self-consciousness. Bogart himself noted its appeal as music "for people who just want to sit and relax and make love." "The song that made her the queen is love to love you, baby."
**Bad Girls (Late 1970s) - Female Sexuality in Focus** : Released at the end of the 1970s, this song about "being a hooker" is mentioned as part of a trend of women in music claiming ownership over their own sexuality, alongside LaBelle's "Lady Marmalade." "Do songs about being a hooker like this one that was released in 1974 or Bad Girls, which was released at the end of the 70s, do they count as women claiming their own sexuality?"

## curiosities
**Queen of Disco Title and Renunciation** : Donna Summer earned the title "queen of Disco" largely due to the success of "Love to Love You Baby." However, she later renounced this title, causing disappointment among her fanbase. "And Donna Summer was the queen of Disco, although she did renounce that title later in her life, much to the disappointment of her fans."
**"Love to Love You Baby" Recording Anecdote** : During the recording of "Love to Love You Baby," Donna Summer reportedly went into a dark booth by herself and lay on the floor to record the song's famously sensual vocalizations. This was due to her extreme self-consciousness about the sounds she was making, which sounded "like she was doing" something explicitly sexual. "The story goes that when Donna recorded this part of the song, she had to go into a booth by herself and the dark."
**Neil Bogart's Vision for "Love to Love You Baby"** : Neil Bogart, the founder of Casablanca Records, saw a gap in the music market for a lengthy song that would allow people to "sit and relax and make love" without interruption. He believed that conventional four-minute songs broke the mood, and he specifically envisioned "Love to Love You Baby" to fill this niche, leading to its extended 17-minute version. "But there was no music for people who just want to sit and relax and make love, you know?"

## songs
**Because the Night (1978) - Collaborative Poetic Lust** : Released in 1978, this song features co-writing credits between Patti Smith and Bruce Springsteen. Springsteen provided the music and chorus, but his work was stalled due to legal issues with his former manager, Michael Pell. Jimmy Iovine then gave a tape of the song to Smith, who, despite her usual meticulousness, wrote the rest of the lyrics in a single night while waiting by the phone for her future husband, Fred 'Sonic' Smith. Shirley Manson of Garbage hailed it as unusual and thrilling for a woman to so openly discuss lust, a theme typically reserved for male artists in the 1970s. "She labored over her lyrics because her songs are poetry and the words within them matter, all of which makes because the night even more incredible."

## curiosities
**Poetic Approach to Songwriting** : Patti Smith is characterized as a "poet who sings her poetry," known for her belief in music as a means for reflection and societal change. She meticulously "labored over her lyrics," emphasizing that "the words within them matter" to achieve her artistic vision. "She wrote songs from the perspective of someone who believes in music as a means of reflection and the force of change."
**Hesitation to Collaborate** : Initially, Patti Smith was hesitant to write "Because the Night" because she preferred to write songs that were "completely hers," showcasing her strong artistic integrity and dedication to her own creative control. "She was just hesitant to write a song that was not completely hers."
**Inspiration for "Because the Night"** : Patti Smith penned the lyrics for "Because the Night" in a single night while waiting for a phone call from her future husband, Fred 'Sonic' Smith. This personal experience of anticipation and desire infused the song with its powerful emotional and sexual themes. "Patty, again, known to agonize at length over her lyrics, wrote because the night in one night as she waited by the phone for her future husband, Fred Sonic Smith to call."
