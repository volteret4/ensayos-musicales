## songs
**Spirit in the Sky (1970)** : Greenbaum, who is Jewish, was inspired to write this song after hearing country star Porter Wagoner sing "Pastor Absent on Vacation" on television. He felt that the "love generation fizzled out into revolution, but I think some people really wanted to believe in something." The song was notable for its distinctive fuzz tone, produced by a fuzz box built directly into his Fender guitar. Background vocals were provided by the Stovall Sisters, a gospel trio from Oakland, California. It reached number three on the Billboard Hot 100 in April 1970. "He took Spirit in the Sky to number three on the Billboard Hot 100 in April 1970."

## curiosities
**Inspiration from Country Gospel** : Norman Greenbaum, despite being Jewish, was inspired to write "Spirit in the Sky" by a country gospel song, "Pastor Absent on Vacation," performed by Porter Wagoner. This shows the cross-genre influence of religious themes. "Norman Greenbaum, yes, he is Jewish, was inspired to write Spirit in the Sky by hearing Country Star Porter Wagner sing Pastor Absent on vacation on television."
**Reception and Intent of "Spirit in the Sky"** : Some listeners were unhappy with the lyric "never been a sinner, never sinned," perceiving it as making light of religious beliefs. Greenbaum clarified 50 years later that he "met no harm" and didn't intend it as a joke or against anyone, simply that "It wasn't my religion, I just did it." He noted that many churches later incorporated the song into their services. "It did upset some people. When I said I can do this, that didn't mean I could do it perfectly."

# artist - George Harrison

## member of
The Beatles

## members
John Lennon (fellow Beatle)
Paul McCartney (fellow Beatle)
Ringo Starr (fellow Beatle, drummer on *All Things Must Pass*)

## instruments
Slide guitar

## albums
**All Things Must Pass (1970) - Triple Album** : This was George Harrison's debut triple album as a solo artist, released "out of the gate" after The Beatles' breakup. It was co-produced by Phil Spector and featured a strong backing lineup including Eric Clapton, Billy Preston, Gary Wright, Ringo Starr, and all four members of Badfinger. The album is regarded by some as possibly the best of the solo Beatles' initial releases and is particularly noted for Harrison's signature slide guitar. "A triple album with Phil Spector as co-producer, an embarrassment of musical talent backing him up."

## songs
**My Sweet Lord (1970)** : George Harrison was inspired to write this song by the gospel hit "Oh Happy Day" by the Edwin Hawkins Singers. He took great thought in writing it, anticipating that "a lot of people might get weird about it" due to fear of the words "Lord" and "God." Lyrically, the song is a risk as it pays homage to both Christianity (with "Hallelujah" in the background) and Hinduism (with "Hare Krishna" in the background), reflecting Harrison's affection for both. Musically, it was determined by a judge to have unintentionally plagiarized "He's So Fine" by The Chiffons. The song featured a prominent tambourine and went to number one in December 1970, holding the top spot for about a month. "Harrison, who's considered the third beetle, always in the shadow of John Lennon and Paul McCartney, was writing the song something? Was that just a fluke?"

## curiosities
**Solo Career Launch and Beatle Shadow** : After The Beatles, Harrison was considered the "third Beatle," often in the shadow of John Lennon and Paul McCartney, leading to questions about his identity as an artist outside the band. His debut solo album, *All Things Must Pass*, was a significant statement to establish his independent musical identity. "Who was George Harrison if he was no longer a beetle?"
**Fear of Religious References in Music** : Harrison anticipated public backlash when writing "My Sweet Lord" due to his intention to use explicit religious terms like "Lord" and "God," noting that "Many people fear the words Lord and God. It makes them angry for some strange reason." This highlights the perceived risk of overtly religious themes in popular music. "I would be committing myself publicly and I anticipated that a lot of people might get weird about it."
**Plagiarism of "He's So Fine"** : George Harrison's "My Sweet Lord" was found by a judge to have unintentionally plagiarized "He's So Fine," a 1963 song by The Chiffons, written by Ronnie Mack. John Lennon, however, expressed skepticism about the unintentional nature of the plagiarism. It was also considered "a bit odd that Phil Spector did not pick up on this." "Musically though, I would be remiss to gloss over the fact that Harrison plagiarized he's so fine, written by Ronnie Mack for the chiffons in 1963."
