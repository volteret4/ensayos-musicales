## curiosities
**Rock Music Definition (1970)** : As the 1970s began, the renowned rock critic Lester Bangs, later immortalized in film by Philip Seymour Hoffman in "Almost Famous" (2000), wrote that rock music is fundamentally an adolescent form, reflecting the specific rhythms, concerns, and aspirations of a specialized age group. **Quadrophenia (1973)** : Released in 1973, this album was written entirely by Pete Townshend and tells the story of Jimmy Cooper, a young British mod. Jimmy, who quit his postal worker job to join the mod subculture, grapples with four distinct personalities, each symbolizing a member of The Who. Sean Murphy of *Pop Matters* (2011) lauded the album for covering universal rock themes like alienation, rebellion, redemption, sex, drugs, and rock and roll, alongside specific cultural references to mods, rockers, punks, and various personal struggles. An uncredited critic in the *Chicago Sun Times* (Fall 1973) compared its singular achievement to works like James Joyce's *Ulysses*, Beethoven's Symphony No. 9, and Goethe's *Faust*, describing it as "ever uncorrupted rock and roll, standing by itself in isolated splendor."

## songs
**A Quick One While He's Away (1966)** : This 10-minute song, comprising six movements, was recorded for The Who's second album, *A Quick One*. **Pinball Wizard (1969)** : A key song from the rock opera *Tommy*, Pete Townshend originally wrote this track to impress influential British rock critic Nick Cohn, who was a fervent pinball enthusiast. The song's composition was inspired by Henry Purcell's 1680 piece "Fantasia Upon One Note," with Townshend analyzing every chord to adapt it for guitar. Despite Townshend initially viewing it as "a clumsy attempt at songwriting," Cohn was ultimately impressed, which helped *Tommy* gain essential attention. The lyrics vividly describe a skilled player: "He stands like a statue, becomes part of the machine, feeling all the bumpers, always playing clean. He plays by intuition, the digit counterspaul, that deaf, dumb, blind kid, shore plays a mean pinball."

The album version includes distinct sounds of rain and a gong crash, which are absent from the single version. Pete Townshend initially envisioned Roger Daltrey's vocal as a "whimper" to convey the character's profound distress, reflexively dismissing Daltrey's more powerful interpretation until engineer Ron Nevisin persuaded him to accept it. While not a *Billboard* Hot 100 hit, the song is firmly established in the classic rock canon. "Townsend said that he envisioned the song's song as more like a whimper."

## curiosities
**"Fantasia Upon One Note" (1680) - Inspiration for Pinball Wizard** : Henry Purcell composed "Fantasia Upon One Note" in 1680, a piece characterized by one instrument maintaining a middle C throughout while other instruments weave around it. Speculation suggests Purcell might have created this piece to allow a friend with limited musical ability to participate by simply holding the middle C. # artist - Meat Loaf

## albums
**Bat Out of Hell (1977)** : Meat Loaf's debut album, released in 1977, is a rock opera based on "Neverland," a musical adaptation of Peter Pan, featuring seven songs "oozing with teenage angst." The songs were written by Jim Steinman, who drew influences from both Wagnerian opera and rock and roll artists like Little Richard and Jerry Lee Lewis. Producer Todd Rundgren, a Rock and Roll Hall of Famer, ultimately funded the recording himself after discovering Meat Loaf lacked a record deal with RCA. Clive Davis, then head of CBS Records, famously rejected the project, telling Steinman he had "no idea how to write a rock song." Critic Dave Marsh of *Rolling Stone* noted Meat Loaf's "outstanding voice" but criticized his "Way Too Stage Struck" phrasing, suggesting "a little less West Side Story and a little more Bruce Springsteen." The album became one of the best-selling rock albums of all time, having sold approximately 34 million copies. "That out of hell is Meatloaf's debut album. It is a rock opera based on Neverland, which is a musical based on Peter Pan."
**Bat Out of Hell II (1993)** : This album was created in 1993, following the immense success of Meat Loaf's debut, despite Jim Steinman noting Meat Loaf's apprehension about creating a follow-up. **Paradise by the Dashboard Light (1978)** : Released in 1978, several months after the *Bat Out of Hell* album, this song is a theatrical performance blending drama and rock. "Paradise by the Dashboard Light is every bit a theatrical performance, and it's as much drama as it is rock."

# genre - Rock Opera

## curiosities
**Definition and Distinction** : A rock opera, like traditional opera, is fundamentally a story told entirely through singing. This distinguishes it from rock musical theater, which typically incorporates spoken dialogue alongside songs. "In essence, a rock opera, like traditional opera, is a story that isn't told entirely through singing while musical theater usually has spoken dialogue as well as songs."
**Origins as Serious Art Form** : The rock opera genre emerged in the 1960s, driven by a desire for rock music to be recognized and taken seriously as an art form. This ambition was notably demonstrated by The Who, who daringly sought to produce work on a scale comparable to Wagnerian opera, a venture that proved successful with their fans. "Like so many things in the 70s, the rock opera was born in the 60s and it's part of this desire for the genre of rock music to be taken seriously as an art form. Really, it was incredibly balsy of the who that think that they could produce something on the scale of Wagner and that their fans would buy it, but it worked."
**Early Candidate: A Man Dies** : Ryan Fertel, a historian and author of a book in the 33 1/3 series on Drive-By Truckers' *Southern Rock Opera*, argues for "A Man Dies" as the first rock opera. He describes this early 1960s work as an attempt to stage the New Testament for the "boomer generation of rock and roll mad teenagers," addressing significant societal issues such as racism and poverty. "He makes a very compelling case for a man dies, which he described as an early 1960s attempt to stage the new testament for the boomer generation of rock and roll mad teenagers."
**Thematic Focus on Youth** : Classic rock operas frequently explore the "trials and tribulations of youth." This thematic connection makes the rock opera an ideal vehicle for a dramatic interpretation of life, particularly in the 70s, an era characterized by self-reflection. "They can. And an examination of some of the classic rock operas shows that they are, in fact, examining the trials and tribulations of youth."

**Tom Wolf on 70s Narcissism** : Author Tom Wolf characterized the 1970s, an era marked by widespread self-reflection, simply as "narcissism." This label captured a prevailing cultural sentiment of inward focus during that decade. "If you couple this with the 70s and era of self-reflection, which the author Tom Wolf would later simply just label as narcissism."
**Pinball Legality in the 70s** : In the 1970s, pinball machines were deemed illegal in several US cities, where they were classified as gambling devices. The Chicago Mafia was reportedly involved in the production of many of these machines. It wasn't until 1974 that the California Supreme Court ruled pinball to be a game of skill rather than chance, leading to New York City loosening its ban a few years later. "Pinball was very much a part of 70s pop culture in the United States too. And those machines in the 80s were right alongside Pac-Man and many arcades, but they were actually illegal in some cities in the 70s."
