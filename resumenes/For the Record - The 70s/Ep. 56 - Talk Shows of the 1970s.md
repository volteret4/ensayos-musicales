## curiosities
**Composition of the Group** : ABBA was a Swedish pop group composed of two married couples who later divorced. "And the Swedish pop group that was comprised of what would be too married and then later two divorced couples."
**Eurovision Victory (1974)** : ABBA won the 1974 Eurovision Contest with their song "Waterloo." "and they were the winner of the 1974 Eurovision contest."
**Unique Mid-70s Pop Vibe** : ABBA possessed a distinct European look and sound, setting them apart from other mid-70s pop groups. They were not categorized as rock or part of the singer-songwriter movement, and their rise occurred before the full advent of the disco era in 1974. "Abba had a different vibe from other pop groups of the mid 70s they were just European in their look in their sound they were not rock they were not part of the singer songwriter movement and we were not quite yet into the disco era in 1974."
**Struggling After Eurovision Victory** : After winning Eurovision with "Waterloo," ABBA faced difficulties with their follow-up singles in Europe, placing them on the verge of becoming a one-hit wonder before they had even gained recognition in the United States. "The problem for Abba in Europe was that their follow up singles after they won Eurovision with Waterloo didn't really do very much. They were on the verge of becoming the dreaded one hit wonders before anyone in the United States had really ever even heard of them."
**Identity Crisis and Emulation of Sweet** : Early in their career, ABBA experienced an identity crisis and considered emulating the glam band Sweet, who had hits like "Fox on the Run," "Little Willy," and "Love Is Like Oxygen." Bjorn Olvea later stated this was "stupid because we were always a pop group." "They were trying to emulate sweet the glam band that had hits with like a fox on the run little Willie love is like oxygen."
**Revolutionizing Pop Music** : According to Frida, ABBA's emergence marked a significant shift in the music scene, suggesting that their unique style of pop music did not exist before them. "Frida said at one point the music scene changed with us something like Abba did not exist before pop like that was not invented yet."
**"Mamma Mia" as Career Catalyst** : The release of "Mamma Mia," a song they were initially hesitant to release due to its "too European" title and even offered to Brotherhood of Man, ultimately saved ABBA from becoming one-hit wonders and launched their career in Europe and Australia. "What saved them from the one hit wonder fate was a single that they were not even sure that they wanted to release Mama Mia."
**US Market Strategy and Live Presence (1976)** : In October 1976, while in Los Angeles for TV tapings, Benny Anderson stated that ABBA did not need the U.S. market, being already huge in Europe and Australia. He described their efforts to make it in the U.S. as "more for the sport and the challenge," emphasizing that they had no interest in being an opening act and had virtually no live music presence in the United States, aside from a few cities on their 1976 world tour, with Seattle being the first. "Benny Anderson made it clear that Abba did not need the United States. They were already huge in Europe and Australia by that time. They had no interest in being an opening act because it was not worth the time and the money and they didn't have a big enough audience in the United States to justify it."
**US Popularity through Television and Radio** : ABBA's popularity in the United States was primarily driven by television and radio exposure, as they had minimal live presence in the country. Most Americans first saw them on TV shows like "Saturday Night Live," "American Bandstand" in 1975, or their initial appearance on Dina Shore's show that same year. "Abba's popularity in the United States stems from television and radio. They had virtually no live music presence in the United States ever."
**Second Appearance on Dina (1976)** : ABBA made their second appearance on Dina Shore's talk show on November 26, 1976, where they performed two songs: "Fernando" and "Mamma Mia." "On their second appearance on Dina, on that November 26, 1976 episode, Abba performed two songs, Fernando, and the song that you that very well may have saved their pop music careers, Mamma Mia."

# curiosity

**1970s Television Talk Shows as Cultural Intersection** : Talk shows during the 1970s served as a crucial intersection of music, politics, and culture, offering a platform for artists to promote their work and providing viewers with insights into contemporary societal issues. "Talk shows collectively were the true intersection of music, politics and culture."
**Evolution of Television Ownership (1950-1970)** : Television ownership in American households saw a dramatic increase between 1950 and 1970; in 1950, 9% of households had a TV, which rose to 90% by 1960, and further to 95.5% by 1970, making TV a pervasive medium for content like talk shows. "9% of American households had a television set in 1950. In 1960, 90% in 1970, 95.5% of American households had at least one television."
**Quasi-Variety Show Format** : Most 1970s talk shows maintained a quasi-variety show format, incorporating elements of talking, singing, comedy, and even demonstrations, serving as a pre-internet and pre-social media opportunity for audiences to connect with stars or discover new personalities. "Most of those talk shows still followed this kind of quasi-variety show format. Some talking, some singing, some comedy, maybe some demonstrating how to cook chicken catchatory or something like that."
**Talk Shows as a "Publicity Man's Paradise" (1969)** : An article from the El Miro New York Star Gazette in January 1969 noted that talk shows had become the primary vehicle for publicists to promote their clients, superseding the traditional method of feeding items to columnists. This shift meant stars, singers, and authors would appear on shows to plug movies, records, or books. "Talk shows have become a publicity man's paradise. In the old days, publicists used to spend their time feeding choice items to columnists. Now it's more important for them to get their clients on any and all of the talk shows."
**List of Popular Talk Show Hosts (1979)** : In 1979, two writers for a Lancaster, Pennsylvania newspaper identified "the Magnificent and not Magnificent 7" of popular talk show hosts, a list that included Johnny Carson (King of Late Night TV), Dick Cavett, Phil Donahue, Mike Douglas, Merv Griffin, Dina Shore, and Tom Snyder. "Here's the list. Johnny Carson of course. Other hosts on the list were Dick Cavett, Phil Donahue, Mike Douglas, Merv Griffin, Dynastore and Tom Snyder."
**Phil Donahue's Pioneering Format** : Phil Donahue was important for pioneering a talk show format characterized by focusing on one issue, with the host engaging directly with the studio audience using a microphone to elicit questions for the interviewee. This template was later credited by Oprah Winfrey as an inspiration for her own successful show. "He was his format, which was usually one issue and him standing in the crowd with a microphone and running back and forth with a microphone to get a crowd questions. And then whoever was being interviewed would respond to those questions. Oprah is going to do that. And Oprah has given Phil Donahue credit for kind of setting the format, if you will, or setting the template I should say for her own feature success."
**70s Political and Social Context for Talk Shows** : The 1970s were a period rich with political and social issues, including women's rights (e.g., Roe v. Wade), the Vietnam War, and the ongoing integration of 1960s civil rights legislation, providing ample controversial topics for talk shows to engage with. "This was the era of women's rights and row versus Wade... This was the era of the Vietnam War. This was the era of the United States living with the civil rights legislation of the 1960s."
**Celebrity Culture and Host Rapport in the 70s** : Celebrity culture thrived in the 1970s, making talk shows an appealing venue for audiences to see famous actors or singers in a less formal setting. The host's ability to establish rapport with guests was crucial for encouraging them to talk and entertain the audience, benefiting both the audience and the interviewee's promotional efforts. "Certainly we were all in on the celebrity culture in the 70s and it was kind of fun to see a famous actor or singer in a less formal setting, which made the host of the talk show very important. The host needed to establish some kind of rapport with the guests to get them talking and to entertain the audience."
**Tom Snyder's Late Night Niche** : Tom Snyder was recognized primarily by "Night Owls" because his talk show aired at midnight or 1 a.m., typically following "Johnny Carson," making his audience specific to late-night viewers. "I'll say about Tom Snyder that he was really only known to Night Owls. His show was on at midnight or 1 a.m. depending on your time zone and he usually came on after Johnny Carson."
