# artist - Jose Feliciano

## genres

Mexican Rhapsodic Guitar Improvisation

## labels

## members
Jose Feliciano

## albums
**José Feliciano Felicinavi Dott (1970)** : This Christmas album was released after Feliciano wrote his signature Christmas song, "Felicinovi Dott," in 1970. Despite earlier controversies surrounding his rendition of the Star-Spangled Banner, the album was very well-received. Bob Stewart of the Baltimore Sun praised its soulful blend of Mexican rhapsodic guitar improvisations, subtle walking bass, and cocktail-type strings, especially in "White Christmas," and its colorful blending of old and new in "We Three Kings." The album was seen as appealing to both sides of the "Generation Gap." "This is from Bob Stewart of the Baltimore Sun. How simply, how intensely he sings and plays these songs. His rendition of White Christmas transforms it into a soulful combination of Mexican rapsodic guitar improvisations, joined by a subtle walking bass and cocktail-type strings."

## songs
**Light My Fire (1968)** : This cover song became Jose Feliciano's first hit single, released in 1968, the same year he won a Grammy for Best New Artist, marking his initial success in the music industry. "His first hit single was his cover of Light My Fire, which was released in 1968."
**Felicinovi Dott (1970)** : Jose Feliciano wrote this song in Los Angeles in 1970, inspired by missing his family and holiday traditions, after his producer suggested he write a Christmas song. While other bilingual Christmas songs existed, Feliciano considered this an entity unto itself, expressing both joy and loneliness. It has become a classic, currently sitting at number 8 on Billboard's Holiday 100 chart. "It was expressing the joy I felt on Christmas and the fact that I felt very lonely. I missed my family, I missed Christmas carols with them, I missed the whole Christmas scene."

## curiosities
**Early Life and Discovery** : Jose Feliciano was born in Puerto Rico in 1945, one of 12 children, and began playing the ukulele at age three. After his family moved to New York, he was performing in coffee houses in Greenwich Village by the age of 15, where he was discovered by a music executive from RCA. "Jose Feliciano was born in Puerto Rico in 1945. He was one of 12 children. He started playing the ukulele when he was three. And by the age of 15, he was playing coffee houses and Greenwich Village after his family moved to New York."
**Grammy Award for Best New Artist (1968)** : Jose Feliciano won this Grammy award in 1968, the same year his cover of "Light My Fire" became a hit single. "That was also the same year that he won a Grammy for Best New Artist."
**Star-Spangled Banner Controversy (1968)** : At Game 5 of the World Series in Tiger Stadium, Detroit, Jose Feliciano performed a unique rendition of the Star-Spangled Banner in 1968, which was deemed "not traditional enough" by some, with Carlos Santana even calling it "too black." This performance caused significant backlash, leading American Legion clubs to call for a law dictating how the song should be sung, and Feliciano was controversially labeled a communist during the Cold War era. This event reportedly led to a period where he struggled to get radio play in America and had to perform in other countries, though sources debate if he was truly blacklisted, as he still secured a TV special in 1969 and played to packed houses. "I had to leave America and play in other countries because I wasn't getting any radio play after I did the Star Spangled Banner."
**Comparison to Marvin Gaye's Anthem** : Ironically, the night before Jose Feliciano's controversial rendition of the Star-Spangled Banner at the World Series, Marvin Gaye had sung the anthem at the request of Tigers Broadcasting legend Ernie Harwell. Gaye, known for his Motown sound, sang his version "straight" rather than in his characteristic style, in stark contrast to Feliciano's interpretation that upset many. "The irony of people being upset about Feliciano's rendition of the Anthem is that Marvin Gaye sang at the night before and at the request of the Tiger's Broadcasting legend Ernie Harwell."

## songs
**Close to You (1970)** : This song was a major hit for The Carpenters, recognized by Billboard magazine as the "Song of the Summer" in 1970, establishing their status as pop music sensations. "The Carpenter's had two hits under their belts. They had a close to you, which Billboard magazine called the Song of the Summer in 1970."
**We've Only Just Begun (1970)** : This was one of the Carpenters' early hits, alongside "Close to You," helping to cement their status as pop music sensations by 1970. "The Carpenter's had two hits under their belts. They had a close to you... and we've only just begun."
**Merry Christmas Darling (1970)** : The lyrics for this Christmas song were originally written in 1944 by an 18-year-old Richard Puler for a girl he loved, intended as a Christmas present, but they broke up before he could give it to her. He later made a recording on a 78-rpm record but couldn't recall the singer. In the late 1960s, Puler, who was a director of the Cal State Long Beach choir and had been hired by Karen Carpenter's parents to give her voice lessons, collaborated with the Carpenters on the song. Richard Carpenter kept the lyrics and re-arranged the music, turning it into a Christmas classic. Critics, like John Wasserman and Kevin Henry, dismissed it as "overwhelmingly feeble-minded" and "overly sentimental," suggesting the band was merely "chasing money." Initially, it was released as a single in 1970 with "Santa Claus Is Comin' to Town" as the flip side, available only by mail order through the Carpenters' fan club for $1.25. It later appeared on a Goodyear Tire Company Christmas album in 1972 before finally being included on a Carpenters album, "Christmas Portrait," in 1978. Karen Carpenter's vocal performance on the track, longing for a distant loved one, was described by Melinda Newman of Billboard as "pure sublime perfection," conveying a mix of yearning, sadness, and hope, and it remains a "soundtrack for any college couples separated during the holiday break." A remastered version features on the "Christmas Portrait" album. "Her vocal on Merry Christmas darling, as she longs for her loved one, who is a way for Christmas a New Year's Day, is pure sublime perfection. She conveys the perfect mix of yearning, sadness and hope, since she knows even though it's unspoken, they will be joyfully reunited after the holidays."

# artist - The Kinks

## genres

## songs
**You Really Got Me (1964)** : This was the Kinks' first hit single, released in 1964, establishing them as a prominent blues-influenced rock band from the UK. "Their first hit was You Really Got Me, released in 1964."
**Father Christmas (1977)** : Released in 1977, this song was written by Ray Davies and is notable for its thoughtful, dark humor, particularly as punk rock was gaining popularity. It tells the story of a mall Santa who is mugged by a group of hoodlums who demand money and jobs for their fathers instead of toys, commenting on societal disparity during the holidays. The BBC was hesitant to play it due to its controversial lyrics, which included lines like "give my daddy a job because he needs one" and "if you've got one, I'll have a machine gun." Dave Davies contributed "interesting guitar parts" and "Christmasy chimes," appreciating the song's message about the "have and have nots." Ray Davies defended the song as an opportunity for rock music to convey ideas and opinions, aligning it with the spirit of "true punks" like the Ramones and Lou Reed. "Father Christmas, give us some money. We got no time for your silly toys. We'll beat you up if you don't hand it over. Give all the toys to the little rich boys."
**The Prince of Punks (1977)** : This song was released as the B-side to "Father Christmas" in 1977. Ray Davies used it to make a statement on the emerging punk scene, with lyrics depicting a "well-known Groover, rock and roll user" who failed at various musical styles and "became a punk because he thought he'd make a little more bread," implying some adopted the genre for commercial gain rather than artistic conviction. "Leave it to Ray Davies to make a statement on the flip side of a Christmas song."

## curiosities
**Band Evolution and Creativity** : Formed in 1963 as a blues-influenced rock band, The Kinks went through various phases, including a "rock opera phase" in the mid-70s and later repurposed themselves as an "arena rock band" in the late 70s, gaining a new generation of fans for their live performances. Throughout these transformations, the band consistently maintained creativity in its sound and lyrics, primarily written by Ray Davies. "When the band first formed in 1963, they were a blues influenced rock band like many of the bands coming out of the UK at that time. Their first hit was You Really Got Me, released in 1964."
**Ray Davies' Songwriting Philosophy** : Ray Davies, inducted into the Songwriters Hall of Fame in 1990, viewed rock music as an important platform for conveying ideas and opinions, especially about "things I can fight for." He noted that while many songwriters create lighthearted Christmas songs, his "Father Christmas" aimed for deeper social commentary with a "dark humor" approach, addressing the disparity between the rich and poor. He expressed approval for "true punks" like the Ramones and Lou Reed for their genuine expression, contrasting them with bands he felt were merely "jumping onto" trends. "He said he sees rock as an opportunity to get ideas and opinions across, but it's just become opinionated."
**Dave Davies' Guitar Contributions to Father Christmas** : Dave Davies, brother of Ray Davies, found "Father Christmas" to be a "very special" song due to the "opportunity to put interesting guitar parts in there," including "guitar riffs that I think sound great." He also appreciated the "little Christmasy chimes and stuff" which he considered part of the "theater of the tune," alongside the song's message about socio-economic disparity during the holiday season. "Dave Davies, brother to Ray, said this of the song. Father Christmas is very special to me because it was an opportunity to put interesting guitar parts in there. Guitar riffs that I think sound great."
**BBC Censorship of Father Christmas** : The BBC was not enthusiastic about playing "Father Christmas" due to its controversial lyrics, which included themes of mugging a Santa Claus and demands for jobs and even a machine gun instead of toys. This reaction led Ray Davies to comment on a perceived double standard, where new wave bands could express such sentiments, but established artists faced scrutiny. "The BBC was not so enthusiastic about playing this song though, no doubt, because of the lyrics."

## albums
**The Long Run (1979)** : This album was the much-anticipated follow-up to "Hotel California." The band's cover of "Please Come Home for Christmas" and the original B-side "Funky New Year" were recorded to appease the record label and break up monotony while "The Long Run" was still in production, helping to keep the label patient until its release in 1979. "It did the trick of keeping a silent record at bay until the long run was released in 1979."

## songs
**Please Come Home for Christmas (1978)** : The Eagles released their cover of this song in 1978. Don Henley, who grew up hearing the original by blues legend Charles Brown in Texas, suggested it to the band, and Glenn Frey believed a bluesy track would be perfect for Henley's vocals. Henley's rendition stays true to the song's blues roots, offering a melancholy but bluesy tone. This single marked the first Eagles release featuring new bassist Timothy B. Schmidt, who replaced Randy Meisner. It reached number 18 on the Billboard charts and, at last check, was number 25 on the Billboard Holiday 100, serving to satisfy their record label while they worked on their next studio album. "Henley stays true to the blues roots of this song with his cover of Please Come Home for Christmas."
**Funky New Year (1978)** : This Eagles original song served as the flip side to their 1978 single, "Please Come Home for Christmas." Don Henley stated that the band felt more comfortable writing an original song about New Year's hangovers, as it was "in their wheelhouse," compared to composing an original Christmas song. "Don Henley said that the band knew a lot more about being hung over on New Year's. So writing an original about that felt a little more like it was in their wheelhouse than writing an original Christmas song."

## curiosities
**Post-Hotel California Pressures** : By 1978, the Eagles were immensely popular following the success of "Hotel California" a year prior. However, their record label was growing impatient for a follow-up album. To manage expectations and alleviate the band's own monotony and stress from being months behind on their main project, they decided to record a Christmas song, which ultimately became their cover of "Please Come Home for Christmas." "Hotel California had cemented their status as superstars a year earlier, but their record label was getting a bit impatient waiting for the follow-up."
**New Bassist Debut** : The Eagles' cover of "Please Come Home for Christmas" was the first single released by the band to feature their new bassist, Timothy B. Schmidt. He joined the band as a replacement for Randy Meisner. "This was the first eagle single, believe it or not, to be released featuring the new bassist Timothy B. Schmidt, who was replacing Randy Meisner."

# curiosity

**Christmas in the 1970s Cultural Landscape** : The 1970s Christmas experience for many included items like the Easy Bake oven, Rock 'Em Sock 'Em Robots, and Atari under the tree, along with the eagerly anticipated Sears or Penny's Christmas catalogs and trips to festive local malls. However, the most defining elements were the endless variety of TV specials and the abundance of Christmas music. "For me, all of that was true. But what really says Christmas in the 70s was the endless variety of TV specials and the music."
**70s Christmas Television Specials** : The 1970s were characterized by a non-stop stream of variety shows, which translated into numerous Christmas specials airing between Thanksgiving and Christmas. These included specials from Sonny and Cher, Donnie and Marie, The Osmonds, Tony Orlando and Dawn, and Captain and Tennille. Additionally, animated classics like "How the Grinch Stole Christmas," "It's a Charlie Brown Christmas," and "Frosty the Snowman," alongside stop-motion "animatic" specials such as "Santa Claus Is Comin' to Town" and "Rudolph the Red-Nosed Reindeer," were staples of holiday programming. "Because the 70s was a seemingly non-stop extravagant of variety shows. And for more on the variety shows, go check out episode 27 of this podcast."
**Difficulty of Creating a Hit Christmas Song** : It is considered challenging to create an original Christmas song that achieves lasting success and stands the test of time, with few even coming close to the enduring popularity of Bing Crosby's "White Christmas," released in 1942. "Now it is not easy to make a hit Christmas song. That original song that lasts and stands the test of time."
**Billboard Holiday 100 Chart** : The Billboard Holiday 100 is an active chart that ranks the top 100 holiday songs of all time, with songs rising and falling based on public tastes. For example, Brenda Lee's "Rockin' Around the Christmas Tree," released in 1958, currently holds the number one spot. "About 10 years ago, Billboard came up with the holiday 100, the top 100 holiday songs of all time. It's an active chart, meaning songs rise and fall with the public tastes."
**The Year Without a Santa Claus (1974)** : This stop-motion "Animagic" Christmas special, produced by Rankin/Bass, aired in 1974, ten years after their first special, "Rudolph the Red-Nosed Reindeer." Based on Phyllis McGinley's 1956 children's book, the plot revolves around Santa (voiced by Mickey Rooney) deciding to skip Christmas due to a cold and perceived lack of belief, reflecting the cynicism of 1974 (war, Watergate, oil embargoes). Mrs. Claus (voiced by Shirley Booth in her final acting role) dispatches elves Jingle and Jangle to find proof of belief. The show is particularly famous for its Vaudeville-style musical performances by the Heat Miser and Snow Miser (characters created by Rankin and Bass), which are considered the highlight of the show. "Ten years after Rudolph, the year without a Santa Claus was released. The plot centers on Santa, of course, who has a cold and decides to skip Christmas this year because nobody believed in him anymore anyway."
**Rankin/Bass Animagic Stop-Motion Technique** : Rankin/Bass pioneered a "single-cell stop action technique" they called "Animagic" for their Christmas specials. The production for these shows, including the crafting of characters from wood, wire, and wool by Japanese puppet makers, was mostly done in Japan. "The first of the Rankin Bass Stop Action Christmas specials aired in 1964, that was Rudolph, they actually called them Anna Magic. It was this single-cell stop action technique."
**Heat Miser and Snow Miser Characters** : The Heat Miser and Snow Miser characters from "The Year Without a Santa Claus" (1974) were not part of Phyllis McGinley's original children's book but were created by Arthur Rankin and Jules Bass. Heat Miser, living in a volcano, controls heat and is jealous of his brother's publicity, while Snow Miser, residing in the North Pole, controls cold weather and snow, appearing less crabby. Their Vaudeville-style musical performances, influenced by Arthur Rankin's family background in Vaudeville, are considered the show's highlight, and their songs are the most requested for remakes. "Now the heat miser and snow miser were not part of the children's book. They were characters that were made up by Rankin and Bass."
**The Wall of Sound Recording Technique** : Popularized by Phil Spector in the 1960s, the "Wall of Sound" technique involved a room filled with 10 to 12 or more musicians, strategically placed to maximize echo. A variety of instruments, including multiple guitars, drums, keyboards, and even unusual additions, were used. Spector would rigorously rehearse the band to prevent any unplanned solos, creating a dense, layered sound, as exemplified by The Ronettes' "Sleigh Ride." Elton John aimed to emulate this technique with his 1973 song "Step into Christmas." "The wall of sound was created by having a room full of musicians were talking 10, 12 more. They were in a room that was sure to provide some sort of echo that would bounce off the walls."
