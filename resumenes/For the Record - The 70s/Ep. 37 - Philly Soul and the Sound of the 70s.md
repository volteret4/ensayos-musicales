# artist - Kenny Gamble

## member of
The Romeos

## curiosities
**Paul McCartney of 1970s** : Kenny Gamble, alongside Leon Huff, was described as the Paul McCartney of the 1970s, highlighting his pivotal role as a prolific and influential songwriter and producer in the Philadelphia soul movement. "Kenny Gamble and Leon Huff were the Paul McCartney and John Lennon of the 1970s."
**Music for Generations** : In a 1997 BET television interview, Kenny Gamble articulated his belief that the music he, Leon Huff, and Tom Bell created was specifically tailored for the generation of the 1970s, emphasizing the concept that each era inherently produces its own unique sound. "Each generation has its own music, he said."
**Songwriting Partnership with Leon Huff** : After a fortuitous encounter in Philadelphia's Schubert building, Gamble and Huff quickly established a strong songwriting collaboration, with Gamble focusing on lyrics and Huff composing the music at the piano, despite neither having the ability to read musical notation. "Gamble wrote the lyrics, Huff wrote the music, sitting at the piano, even though neither could read music."
**PIR Founding** : Gamble, partnering with Leon Huff, secured a deal from Clive Davis, then president of CBS Records, to establish Philadelphia International Records, an initiative driven by Davis's ambition to position CBS as a significant force in the "Black Music Market." "Clive Davis, the president of CBS Records, offered Gamble and Huff a deal that allows them to create Philadelphia international records."
**First Plane Ride to Motown** : Kenny Gamble's inaugural plane trip was a journey to Detroit with Leon Huff, undertaken to tour the Motown facilities, which served as a foundational blueprint for their aspirations to create their own record label. "Gamble's first plane ride ever was with Huff on a trip to Detroit to tour Motown."
**Payola Scandal (1975)** : Kenny Gamble was implicated in a payola scandal in 1975, for which he received a "slap on the wrist" fine, while his partner Leon Huff was not penalized in the investigation. "In the end Gamble had a slap on the wrist fine assessed and huff didn't."
**Conversion to Islam & "Black Lens" Focus** : Following his conversion to Islam, concerns emerged within Philadelphia International Records' inner circle that Kenny Gamble was excessively focused on interpreting all company endeavors through a "black lens," a perspective some writers believed restricted their artistic and commercial reach. "There were concerns though in their inner circle that Kenny Gamble who had also recently converted to Islam was too focused on viewing everything that PIR did through through the black lens."
**Enduring Philosophy on Message Music** : Decades later, Gamble and Huff consistently maintain their conviction that the message conveyed in music is of utmost importance, aspiring to create not merely enjoyable sounds but music designed to uplift and enlighten audiences, as exemplified by the lasting relevance of anthems like "Love Train." "He remains convinced that the message in the music is what matters the most, and they created music that made people feel better and see better."
**360 Degrees of Knowledge** : In a 2021 interview with The New York Times, Kenny Gamble characterized their music as delivering "360 degrees of knowledge," encompassing essential love songs alongside tracks focused on community building and personal development, highlighting their commitment to leaving a meaningful legacy for future generations. "when you think of our music it was 360 degrees of knowledge that we gave, a lot of great love songs which is important in life, but also a lot of songs about building our community, building people."

# artist - Leon Huff

## curiosities
**John Lennon of 1970s** : Leon Huff, in partnership with Kenny Gamble, was characterized as the John Lennon of the 1970s, signifying his crucial role as a prolific and impactful songwriter and producer within the Philadelphia soul genre. "Kenny Gamble and Leon Huff were the Paul McCartney and John Lennon of the 1970s."
**Songwriting Partnership with Kenny Gamble** : Following a chance encounter in Philadelphia's Schubert building, Huff and Gamble rapidly formed a successful songwriting team, with Huff composing the music at the piano while Gamble penned the lyrics, despite neither possessing the ability to read musical notation. "Gamble wrote the lyrics, Huff wrote the music, sitting at the piano, even though neither could read music."
**PIR Founding** : Huff, together with Kenny Gamble, was offered a deal by CBS Records president Clive Davis, leading to the creation of Philadelphia International Records, an initiative motivated by Davis's aim for CBS to become a dominant force in the "Black Music Market." "Clive Davis, the president of CBS Records, offered Gamble and Huff a deal that allows them to create Philadelphia international records."
**Motown Study Trip** : Leon Huff accompanied Kenny Gamble on Gamble's first ever airplane journey, a trip to Detroit specifically intended to tour the Motown facilities, which served as a crucial model for their aspirations to establish their own record label. "Gamble's first plane ride ever was with Huff on a trip to Detroit to tour Motown."
**Payola Scandal (1975)** : While Kenny Gamble faced a payola scandal in 1975 and incurred a fine, Leon Huff was not penalized during the investigation. "In the end Gamble had a slap on the wrist fine assessed and huff didn't."
**Architects of Disco** : Despite their later disavowal of disco, Gamble and Huff are recognized as two of its primary architects, notably through their innovative production work on songs like "The Love I Lost," which introduced the seminal "four-on-the-floor" beat. "Gamble and Huff rejected Disco later in the decade, but that does not change the fact that they were two of its architects."
**Enduring Philosophy on Message Music** : Even 50 years after their initial success, Huff and Gamble remain committed to their core philosophy that the message embedded in music is paramount, striving to create not only musically appealing tracks but also songs that inspire and enlighten their listeners. "They wanted to make something more than music that sounded good."

# artist - Tom Bell

## member of
The Romeos

## curiosities
**Not Part of PIR** : Tom Bell, while a close associate of Kenny Gamble and Leon Huff, consciously chose not to be a permanent fixture at Philadelphia International Records, instead opting for the flexibility to select his own projects and collaborations. "Tom Bell was never part of PIR. He picked and chose who he wanted to work with and when."
**Avoidance of Political/Religious Songs** : Tom Bell intentionally refrained from writing political or religious songs, holding the belief that such themes were excessively personal and carried the risk of alienating a segment of his audience. "He was not going to write political songs, and he was not going to write songs ahead. He sort of religious bent to them."
**Musical Experimentation** : Bell was notable for his readiness to integrate unconventional instruments into soul music, exemplified by his use of a sitar on The Delfonics' "Didn't I Blow Your Mind This Time" and an oboe on The Stylistics' "Betcha by Golly Wow." "Bell was very willing to try new things musically like he used the Cittar on Didn't I Blow Your Mind This Time by the Delphonics."
**Producer's Style - No Improv** : Tom Bell maintained a stringent production methodology, requiring vocalists and musicians to perform songs precisely as he envisioned them, disallowing any improvisation during the recording process. "You just had to sing and play the songs his way, no improv."
**Definition of Soulful Music** : In 1972, Tom Bell articulated his perspective that the quality of "soulful" music was not exclusive to Black artists, citing diverse examples such as country singer Charlie Pride, The Beatles, Frank Sinatra, and at times, Andy Williams, to illustrate his point. "There is no such thing as a person being soulful because he's black."
**Tom Bell's Wager with The Spinners** : To persuade The Spinners to allow him to produce their next record, Tom Bell made a bold wager: he promised each member $1,000 if he failed to secure a number one hit, but in return, they would owe him a custom Cadillac if he succeeded. "If I do not get you a number one record, I will give each one of you $1,000. If I do get you a number one record, you owe me a custom Cadillac."
**Wrote Songs for His Children** : Tom Bell composed songs for all of his children, though "Rubberband Man" remains the sole recorded track among them. The song was originally conceived with the lyric "fat man" but was changed to "rubber band man" to uplift his son, Mark, who was overweight. "Tom Bell also wrote songs for all of his kids, but rubber band band. That's the only one that was ever recorded."

# artist - The O'Jays

## members
Eddie Levert

## labels
Philadelphia International Records

## albums
**Backstabbers (1972)** : This album marked Philadelphia International Records' first smash hit, selling 700 million copies in 1972 and achieving the coveted crossover success that CBS Records had sought. "The album Backstabbers sold 700 million copies in 1972 and it was the crossover success that CBS wanted."
**Ship Ahoy (N/A)** : This concept album, focused on the theme of slavery, featured cover art that explicitly depicted a slave ship and included sounds of whips and chains in its title track, serving as a powerful social and political statement from both PIR and The O'Jays. "So when the OJs released the album Ship of Hoy and the cover signaled to all who bought the record that the ship in question was a slave ship."
**Message in the Music (1976)** : Kenny Gamble coined the phrase "understand while you dance" for this album, articulating a vision to fuse social commentary with danceable rhythms. However, as the 1970s progressed into the disco era, the demand for "message music" diminished. "On the back of message in the music the 1976 album by the OJs. Gamble wrote understand while you dance."

## songs
**Backstabbers (1972)** : The inaugural smash hit for PIR, this song was not interpreted as a failed romantic relationship, but rather as a commentary on the systemic treatment of Black men and women in America, a theme Greil Marcus characterized as "music of worry and confinement." "Backstabbers is not really about a relationship gone bad. It's about how black men and women were treated in America."
**For the Love of Money (1974)** : Featured on the *Ship Ahoy* album, this song is frequently misconstrued as an endorsement of wealth, but it is in fact a "funkified warning" about the corrupting influence of greed and an appeal to prevent money from altering one's character, with its lyrics vividly depicting human depravity driven by avarice. "For the love of money is a funcified warning about what people will do for money and a plea to not let it change you."

## curiosities
**Pre-PIR Career History** : Before signing with Philadelphia International Records in 1971, The O'Jays had a protracted and challenging career, performing extensively on the Chitlin' circuit for years, recording for 20 different labels, and accumulating over 30 years of combined experience (shared with Harold Melvin & The Blue Notes) without achieving a single Top 40 hit; they were also dissatisfied with the artistic direction imposed by their previous record label. "The OJs were hardly new kids on the block. They had played the Chitlin circuit for years and were not happy with the direction their record label was taking them."
**Initial Industry Underwhelm** : Despite Kenny Gamble and Leon Huff's high regard for The O'Jays, particularly Eddie Levert's vocal talents, the music industry at large initially showed little enthusiasm for PIR's signing of the group, with the event largely going unnoticed. "the music industry was entirely underwhelmed by PIR signing this group, so approximately nobody paid any attention to this signing at all."
**Eddie Levert's Stance on Message Songs** : Initially, Eddie Levert and The O'Jays favored enjoyable, high-energy songs over tracks with explicit messages. However, Gamble and Huff were resolute in their commitment to incorporating social statements into their music, believing that songwriting should inherently convey a part of the artist's soul. "Eddie Levert of the OJs said at first they did not want to do songs. They had a message. They wanted something fun. They wanted to do something that had some kick to it."

# artist - Harold Melvin & The Blue Notes

## members
Harold Melvin
Teddy Pendergrass

## labels
Philadelphia International Records

## songs
**If You Don't Know Me by Now (1972)** : This song became their first hit for Philadelphia International Records and was originally conceived by Gamble and Huff for the vocal group LaBelle. It reached number three on the Billboard Hot 100 and topped the Billboard R&B chart in 1972, following Teddy Pendergrass's transition to lead vocalist. "Their first hit for PIR was if you don't know me by now, which was originally intended for the ladies of LaBelle."
**The Love I Lost (1973)** : Initially envisioned as a slow ballad by Gamble and Huff, this song was dynamically transformed by session drummer Earl Young. He significantly increased the tempo and introduced a "four-on-the-floor" beat with a prominent high-hat, an innovation that effectively created the foundational disco beat. "The session drummer Earl Young felt like it was dragging too much. So he sped things up with this four on the floor playing the bass drum on every beat and he hit that high hat symbol to drive the beaten holy shit."

## curiosities
**Pre-PIR Career Struggles** : Before their 1971 signing with Philadelphia International Records, Harold Melvin & The Blue Notes, similar to The O'Jays, endured a lengthy and difficult career. Collectively, the two groups had over 30 years of experience, recorded for 20 different labels, and had never achieved a Top 40 hit, primarily performing on the hotel lounge circuit. "Harold Melvin in the Blue Notes had pretty much given up on recording careers and were playing the hotel lounge circuit."
**Teddy Pendergrass's Transition** : A pivotal shift in the group's trajectory occurred when Teddy Pendergrass moved from his role as drummer to become the lead singer, a crucial strategic decision that coincided with their signing to PIR and significantly contributed to their subsequent success. "Teddy Pendergrass stepped out from behind the drum kit into the role of lead singer."

# artist - Billy Paul

## labels
Philadelphia International Records

## songs
**Me and Mrs. Jones (1972)** : Presented as a slow jam by Gamble and Huff, this song initially puzzled jazz singer Billy Paul, who questioned why he was being offered a pop song about an affair. However, he agreed to record it due to his personal marital issues, which allowed him to relate to the theme, and it subsequently became a number one hit for three consecutive weeks in 1972. "Me and Mrs. Jones was number one for three weeks in 1972."
**Am I Black Enough for You (1973)** : Released as the follow-up to his international hit "Me and Mrs. Jones," this funky song, characterized by a driving baseline and direct lyrics advocating for change and against poverty, proved to be a commercial flop. Billy Paul attributed its failure to "limited appeal" among white audiences, though it did find popularity among his African American fanbase. "As a follow up to me and Mrs. Jones, an international hit, this was a complete flop."

## curiosities
**Jazz Background** : Billy Paul primarily identified as a jazz singer, having performed with the legendary Dinah Washington as a child and jammed with Charlie Parker, a background that initially made him reluctant to record the pop-oriented "Me and Mrs. Jones." "Paul was a jazz singer and wondered why he was being given a pop song This was a guy who sang with the great Dino Washington when he was a child, he had jammed with the bird Charlie Parker and he was supposed to sing a pop song about a couple of people having an affair"
**Personal Connection to "Me and Mrs. Jones"** : Billy Paul's decision to record the pop song "Me and Mrs. Jones" was influenced by his own marital difficulties, as he felt a personal connection to the song's narrative of an extramarital affair, which he believed he could authentically convey. "he was having some marital problems and well, he had his own Mrs. Jones, he thought maybe he could relate and well, he sure sang as if he could"
**Criticism of "Am I Black Enough for You" Release** : Billy Paul openly blamed Kenny Gamble and Clive Davis for releasing "Am I Black Enough for You" as the successor to his international hit "Me and Mrs. Jones," contending that its explicitly targeted appeal to Black audiences limited its commercial reach and consequently hindered his subsequent career. "Billy Paul blamed Gamble and Clive Davis for releasing a song that he said had, quote, limited appeal."
**"Gray Eyed" Philly Soul** : In 1978, Billy Paul characterized Philadelphia soul as "gray eyed," implying that it was, and always had been, a racially integrated musical form, appealing across diverse audiences despite some songs specifically targeting Black communities. "Paul said in 1978 that at that all Philadelphia soul is what he called gray eyed meaning racially integrated and it always has been."

# genre - Philly Soul

## curiosities
**Distinctive Sound Description** : Journalist Jim Farber (2021) vividly described Philly Soul as "a mixture of creamy strings, punching horns, snaking bass lines and fullsome melodies all combined to create something at once complex and light, a sonic suple fired by soul." Producer and guitarist Bobby Eli famously encapsulated the sound as "funk, dressed in a tuxedo." "He wrote, it is a mixture of creamy strings, punching horns, snaking bass lines and fullsome melodies all combined to create something at once complex and light, a sonic suple fired by soul."
**Signature Element: Strings** : The prominent and "creamy" use of string arrangements is identified as the hallmark of Philly Soul, providing its distinctive and enveloping sound that sets it apart from other soul genres, such as Motown. "Of course, the element that's missing that makes Philly soul, Philly soul are the strings."
**Producer's Genre** : Philly Soul is characterized as a "producer's genre," underscoring the significant influence of producers like Kenny Gamble and Leon Huff in shaping its signature sound and intricate arrangements, a characteristic that also served as a precursor to the rise of Disco. "Philly's soul is a bit of a producer's genre, also foreshadowing Disco by the way"
**"Lush" Sound** : The music associated with Philadelphia International Records and Philly Soul is frequently described as "lush" in various writings, with its characteristic string instruments creating an enveloping, wave-like sonic experience that naturally encourages physical movement. "The word that comes up so often in articles and books and essays about Gamble and Huff is lush."
**Integration of Social Commentary** : Beyond its appealing and sweet sonic qualities, Gamble and Huff's Philly Soul consciously integrated social commentary, reflecting their attentiveness to contemporary world events and their intention to use music as a medium for conveying deeper, meaningful messages. "There's something more underneath that sweetness, but Gamble and Huff made no pretenses that they didn't pay attention to what was happening in the world."
**Racial Integration ("Gray Eyed")** : In 1978, Billy Paul described Philadelphia soul as "gray eyed," signifying its inherent and enduring racial integration in both its appeal and composition, thereby challenging racial stereotypes often associated with "black music." "Paul said in 1978 that at that all Philadelphia soul is what he called gray eyed meaning racially integrated and it always has been."
**Blending Motown and Stax** : Mark Neal, a professor of African American Studies at Duke, characterized Gamble and Huff's unique achievement in Philly Soul as successfully melding "the Motown machine with the Stacks grit," resulting in a sound that is simultaneously glossy and smooth yet possesses the raw intensity associated with Stax Records. "Mark Neal, professor of African American Studies at Duke said of Gamble and Huff, quote, they found a way to marry the Motown machine with the Stacks grit."
