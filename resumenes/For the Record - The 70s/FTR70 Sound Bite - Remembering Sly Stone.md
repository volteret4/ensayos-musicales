# artist - Sly Stone

## curiosities
**Death and Legacy** : Sly Stone passed away on Monday, June 9th, 2025, at the age of 82, a lifespan many considered longer than expected given his personal struggles. His notorious drug use, subsequent erratic behavior, and frequent missed shows are cited as reasons why he has not been collectively remembered as the musical genius he clearly was. "No doubt 82 was longer than many people ever expected him to live."
**Memoir Publication** : In 2023, Sly Stone published his memoir titled "Thank You for Letting Me Be Myself Again." This publication marked a return to the public eye after a period of musical inactivity. "You can go read his 2023 memoir. Thank you for letting me be myself again."
**Questlove's Documentary** : A documentary about Sly Stone, titled "Sly Lives," directed by Questlove, began streaming on Hulu approximately six months prior to Stone's death. This film delves into the "demons" and personal burdens faced by Sly Stone, including the significant pressure of being not just a superstar, but an African American one. "You could go watch Quest Love's documentary on Sly Sly Lives, which began streaming on Hulu about six months ago."
**Musical Inactivity Period** : Sly Stone effectively dropped off the musical radar in 1982, remaining largely inactive until the publication of his memoir and Questlove's documentary brought renewed attention to his work. "When musically he dropped off the radar in 1982 that was really kind of it until he was he published his memoir and then Quest Love did the documentary."
**Clyde Davis's Influence** : Following the commercial failure of Sly and the Family Stone's first album, "A Whole New Thing," Sly Stone was encouraged by CBS Records executive Clyde Davis to write music more suited for radio. This directive led to the creation of songs like "Dance to the Music." "after that Sly wrote more for the radio because well that's what CBS records executive Clyde Davis told him to do giving us dance to the music."
**Template for 70s Music** : Sly Stone is recognized for providing a foundational template for the music of the 1970s, particularly through his innovative use of instrumentation and sound. His work showed what was musically possible and influenced subsequent acts. "another example of how Sly gave us a template for 70s music."
**Burden of African American Stardom** : The immense expectations placed on Sly Stone, not only as a superstar but specifically as an African American superstar, were perceived to be a significant burden on him. Questlove's documentary explores this aspect in greater depth. "ultimately the expectations on Sly to be not just a superstar but an African American one I think proved to be too much of a burden."

## albums
**A Whole New Thing (1967)** : This was the band's debut album, released in 1967. Despite being their first offering, the album failed to achieve commercial success or critical traction upon its release. "The band's first album a whole new thing was released in 1967 and it went nowhere"
**Stand! (1969)** : Released in 1969, this album further developed the distinctive psychedelic soul sound that Sly Stone was crafting. It includes significant tracks like "I Want to Take You Higher." "The album stand was released in 1969 and Sly builds on the psychedelic soul sound that he was creating with I want to take you higher."
**Greatest Hits (1970)** : This compilation album, released in 1970, is considered one of the era's most significant rock albums. It features brilliantly arranged and musically sharp songs that formed a crucial part of the soundtrack for the first half of the 1970s, containing no "clunkers." "The greatest hits album is one of the greatest rock albums of this era with songs that are just they're just brilliantly arranged and they're musically sharp and to borrow cliche very much part of the soundtrack of the first half of the 70s."
**There's a Riot Goin' On (1972)** : Released in 1972, this album exhibited a markedly different tone and overall vibe compared to Sly and the Family Stone's previous work. It is characterized by a "muddy" sound and is notably distinct from disco music, featuring the groundbreaking song "Family Affair." "a riot a riot's going on is not a disco record. In fact the whole album is in tone and just overall vibe very different from Sly's previous work. It's just muddy that's the word that comes to mind"
**Fresh (1973)** : This album was released in 1973, following the successful "There's a Riot Goin' On." "and fresh in 1973."

## songs
**Dance to the Music (1967)** : This song was created after CBS Records executive Clyde Davis advised Sly Stone to write more radio-friendly material. Despite the band initially finding it not "hip enough," it became a top 10 hit and established a foundational blueprint for the soul and R&B sound that emerged in the early 1970s. "The band wasn't all that into dance to the music at first it didn't seem hip enough but it was a top 10 hit and it set the blueprint for the soul and R&B sound that was to follow in the early 70s."
**I Want to Take You Higher (1969)** : This track is associated with the 1969 album "Stand!" and exemplifies the psychedelic soul sound that Sly Stone was developing at the time. "Sly builds on the psychedelic soul sound that he was creating with I want to take you higher."
**Family Affair (1971)** : Featured on the 1972 album "There's a Riot Goin' On," this song is explicitly not a disco record. It is characterized by a "muddy" sound, a muted guitar (in contrast to the typical front-and-center role in rock bands), and is notable for being the first pop song to use a drum machine as a central focal point, creating a "heartbeat" like rhythm. "family affair is muddy while the guitar was typically front and center and rock bands here the guitar is muted. But listen to what happens when the drum machine takes the center stage. Okay it it sounds like the heartbeat right that's the first time that a pop song used a drum machine in that way"
