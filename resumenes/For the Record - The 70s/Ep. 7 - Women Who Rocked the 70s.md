## curiosities
**"One of the Guys" Perception** : Producer Paul Rothschild described Janis as "one of the guys," noting that her male balance was as strong as his female balance. However, Joe McDonald, former lead singer of Country Joe and the Fish and Janis's former partner, strongly disagreed, stating, "Sexism killed her. People kept saying that she was just one of the guys. That's a real sexist bullshit trip because that was fucking her head around."
**Live-in-the-Moment Philosophy** : Janis exuded a philosophy of living fully in the present, which Joe McDonald believed stemmed from a perceived need to compete and outdo men in typically masculine behaviors like drinking and performing. She encouraged seizing the day, as tomorrow is never guaranteed. "If you got a cap for one day, man, I mean, say maybe you want a cap for 365 days, right? You ain't got it for 365 days. You got it for one day, man. Well, I'll tell you that one day, man, better be your life, man."
**Louisville Concert Reception (June 12, 1970)** : During a show at Freedom Hall in Louisville, Kentucky, a conservative southern town, Janis initially saw "country club chicks" in the front rows and expressed her frustration. Despite a slow start, her performance led the crowd to go "bonkers," with dancing and pushing toward the stage, causing friction with security guards. "Janice looked out on the crowd and she didn't see what she might call her people in the front row and she said, shit, man, why do those country club chicks and their panty girdles always have to be sitting in the front rows?"
**Engagement with Audience** : At the Louisville concert, Janis responded to a fan's request to "try a little bit harder" by yelling back, "Hey, I'm doing my part." Later, she jumped off stage to dance with a young man, causing the crowd to surge forward. When security guards pushed back, she interjected, "I permit them to dance. ... in fact, I demand it." "Janice jumps off the stage and starts to dance with a young guy near the front and now the crowd is going bonkers."
**Authenticity and Presence on Stage** : Unlike some artists who performed under the influence, Janis was always present and engaged during her shows, pouring everything into her performances. She was genuinely happy with her musical transition from psychedelic rock towards a bluesier sound. "She was present and she was happy and she was happy with the transition that her music was taking her even though it was taking her away from the psychedelic rock and more toward the bluesy rock."
**Blues of Disappointment** : Janis's blues style differed from the traditional blues of lack often sung by Black blues artists, focusing instead on personal disappointment. This led to an ironic dynamic where she entertained many male fans by singing about her disappointments in them. "The blues that Janice sang about was a blues of disappointment and not the traditional blues of lack that black blues artists sang about."
**Potential Matriarch of Rock** : Her untimely death prevented her from becoming a "70s something matriarch of rock," which the speaker suggests might have led to women in rock being less of a novelty. Her voice as a person, beyond her musical talent, was a significant loss. "What we lost with Janice was not just the musical voice but her voice as a person."

## curiosities
**Double Induction into Rock and Roll Hall of Fame** : Stevie Nicks was inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame twice: first with Fleetwood Mac in 1998, and then as a solo artist 21 years later, making her the first woman to achieve this honor. Twenty-two men had received this honor previously. "Stevie Nicks was inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame for the second time. She was inducted with Fleetwood Mac in 1998, and then 21 years later as a solo artist."
**Aspiration for "Straight Up Rock and Roll"** : Upon becoming a solo artist, Stevie Nicks expressed a desire to make a "Tom Petty album" and pursue "straight up rock and roll," indicating a preference for a harder sound than Fleetwood Mac's 70s "softer rock." "She wanted to make a Tom Petty album. At least that's what she said when she became a solo artist. She wanted to do what she called straight up rock and roll."
**San Francisco Scene Influence** : Stevie Nicks was part of the San Francisco music scene, learning from artists like Janis Joplin, Grace Slick, and Buffalo Springfield, which she considered a "literal rock and roll school." "Now, Stevie and Lindsey Buckingham were part of that whole San Francisco scene that Janice Joplin was part of. Stevie learned from watching Janice and Grace Slick and Buffalo Springfield, a literal rock and roll school."
**Early Career Sexism Experience** : Stevie Nicks recalled a single instance early in her career where a man in the crowd made "lute remarks," and legendary concert promoter Bill Graham immediately intervened, telling him to leave. She considered her overall experience with sexism in the industry to be relatively easy. "She can remember one time, very early in her career, when a guy in the crowd made some lute remarks to her, and the legendary concert promoter Bill Graham told him to essentially get the fuck out, and that was that."
**Pledge with Christine McVie** : On the day Stevie Nicks joined Fleetwood Mac, she and Christine McVie made a mutual vow never to be treated as "less than" or "second class citizens" within the male-dominated rock environment. They promised to fight for their place, ensure their songs and music were equally as good as the men's, and be included in creative discussions. "She said that they said to each other, we will never be treated like second class citizens. We will never be not allowed to hang out in a room full of intelligent, crazy rock and roll stars, because we are just as crazy and just as intelligent as they are."

**Musical Influences of Stevie Nicks** : Stevie Nicks cited The Supremes and "amazing black musical groups who were top 40 when I was in the fourth grade" as key influences on her singing style, also mentioning songwriters Carol King, Gerry Goffin, Barry Mann, and Cynthia Weil. ## curiosities
**1970s Evolution and Branches** : Rock and roll underwent significant evolution in the 1970s, branching into various subgenres such as heavy metal, southern rock, punk rock, and arena rock. These branches sometimes led to a "culture war" among their respective fan bases by the end of the decade. "Rock and roll underwent an evolution in the 1970s. In fact, we end the 70s with branches of the rock and roll tree and a culture war between the fans of the various branches."
**"Man's World" and Patriarchy** : The rock and roll industry in the 1970s was predominantly a "man's world," characterized by a patriarchal society where women struggled to make their way and were often considered a "novelty." Discussing their musical achievements often necessitated acknowledging their gender and the pervasive misogyny of the era. "Rock and roll was a man's world. The music industry was a patriarchal society and women who tried to make their way in it or did make their way in it were unique."
**Expected "Masculine" Behavior for Women** : For women to succeed in the male-dominated rock world of the 1970s, it almost required them to adopt or even outdo perceived masculine behaviors, such as being tough or participating in heavy drinking. "For a woman to play in a man's world, it almost demanded that she be like one of the men, maybe even outdo the men at behavior that was perceived as masculine."

# genre - Arena Rock

## curiosities
**Late 1970s / 1980s Explosion** : Arena rock began to gain momentum in the late 1970s and "totally blew up" in the 1980s. This genre, characterized by power ballads, attracted a new breed of rock fans, predominantly younger women and their mothers, filling large venues. "Arena Rock and the power ballad that filled up those arenas with a brand new breed of rock fans, the younger women and probably their moms too, took hold of rock."

# curiosity

**19th Amendment 50th Anniversary (1970)** : The year 1970 marked the 50th anniversary of the 19th Amendment, which granted women the right to vote in all states, primarily benefiting white women at the time. "1970 marked the 50th anniversary of the 19th Amendment which gave women in all states the right to vote, although we realize that this primarily referred to white women, but still it was there on paper."
**Civil Rights Act of 1964 and Gender Discrimination** : Passed six years prior to 1970, the Civil Rights Act of 1964 not only outlawed racial segregation but also banned employment discrimination based on gender. The inclusion of women was a tactical move by opponents hoping to prevent its passage, believing it would be too controversial, but the act passed nonetheless. "It was six years after the passage of the Civil Rights Act in 1964, which in addition to making racial segregation illegal, it banned employment discrimination based on gender."
**Women's Strike for Equality (August 26, 1970)** : On August 26, 1970, 50,000 women marched down Fifth Avenue in New York City during rush hour as part of a nationwide strike for equality. While numbers for the strike itself are unknown, numerous demonstrations across the nation (sit-ins, teach-ins, marches) aimed to highlight women's collective power and the existing lack of gender equality. "On August 26th of 1970, 50,000 women marched down Fifth Avenue in New York in the midst of rush hour."
**Male Domination in Major Industries** : In the 1970s, nearly every major industry in the United States was dominated by men, with women frequently denied jobs and explicitly told it was due to their gender. The music industry was no exception to this widespread discrimination. "Just about every major industry in the country was dominated by men. Women were denied jobs, told outright. It was because they were women."
**Record Label Marketing Challenges for Female Artists** : Record companies in the 1970s often struggled to market female artists, particularly those in less mainstream genres like blues. They didn't know how to effectively promote what they had, leading to overlooked talent. "The record companies didn't know exactly how to market what they had. And this was especially true for Bonnie Raight because Bonnie Raight was blues."
