# genre - Pop

## curiosities
**Origin as Youth Music (1960s)** : In the 1960s, "pop music" primarily referred to music aimed at younger audiences, encompassing bands like The Rolling Stones. "Keep in mind that in the 1960s pop music referred to music for younger people. So for example, the Rolling Stones might have been referred to and were referred to as a pop music group."
**Shifting Definition and Negative Connotation (1970s)** : In the 1970s, the "pop consensus began to fray," and the term "pop" increasingly acquired a negative connotation. It often came to signify the absence of other defined genres, being used to describe music that wasn't "rock enough or enough R&B or enough disco," thereby evolving into its own distinct genre. "in the 70s, the pop consensus began to fray. Increasingly, the word pop became negative end quote."
**Resistance to Classification (1970s)** : The 1970s saw the emergence of a resistance among many artists to being classified specifically as "pop," a trend that solidified pop into its own genre. This focus on "proper representation of the genre" also characterized the decade. "is a product of the 1970s as is the resistance by many artists to being classified as pop, which has become its own genre."
**Reflecting Public Weariness (1976)** : In episode 31 ("The Spirit of 76"), it was noted that Americans, particularly adults, were exhausted by the chaos of the previous 10-15 years. This weariness might explain why audiences in the mid-70s appreciated "simple, unprobing artists" like Barry Manilow, moving away from the "commentary invite" found in artists like Bob Dylan. "Moods of people change, and the Dylan era represents years past."
