## songs
**Say It Loud – I'm Black and I'm Proud (1968)** : Written by James Brown and his band leader, Pee Wee Ellis, this song was released in the summer of 1968 and became a number one hit on the Billboard R&B charts for over a month. It served as a powerful anthem for black power and black pride. A legend, possibly started by Brown himself, suggests he wrote it after the Black Panther Party left a hand grenade on his doorstep, though its importance and sincerity remain undiminished. Randall Kennedy, a Harvard law professor, noted in 2018 that singing this song felt exhilarating and marked a period where the term "Negro" was rejected in favor of "black," until Jesse Jackson led a movement in 1988 to replace "black" with "African American." It was James Brown's last pop hit, and he lost many white fans due to its message, as white listeners often interpreted "black power" as "anti-white." "I was trying to do two things. One, give the power structure, which in America means the white power structure, a way to understand how we felt and know that we had people who could do things and just wanted a fair shake. Two, I wanted young black kids to wake up and realize that they should be proud of who they were, get an education and try to make something of themselves."

## curiosities
**Early Political Stance** : Prior to 1968, James Brown was not widely known for being political, though he did perform at a civil rights rally at a historically black college in Mississippi and gave cash to the Congress for Racial Equality. After Dr. Martin Luther King Jr.'s assassination, Brown played a significant role in calming the city of Boston, demonstrating his influence beyond music. "Up until 1968, James Brown was not known to be political. He did help calm the city of Boston after King's assassination."
**Afro Hairstyle Statement** : In the late 1960s, James Brown briefly wore an Afro, which was a significant statement at the time. This change in appearance, combined with the explicit messaging in his music like "Say It Loud – I'm Black and I'm Proud," contributed to him receiving criticism from some black power supporters and alienating a substantial portion of his white fanbase, who perceived his shift as anti-white. "So when he briefly had an Afro in the late 60s, that too was a statement."

## songs
**Back Stabbers (1972)** : This song marked a significant milestone for The O'Jays, becoming their first number one R&B single in 1972. It is characteristic of the "Philadelphia sound" produced by Kenny Gamble and Leon Huff, evident in its prominent use of strings. Before this breakout hit, The O'Jays had a long history performing on the "Chitlin circuit" in the South. Scholars like Alice Eccles suggest that while the song can be interpreted as relationship betrayal, it also carries a deeper political commentary, possibly echoing Dr. King's sentiments about white moderates and reflecting a broader theme of betrayal within the Black Power movement. "Backstabbers was the first number one R&B single for the OJs who had been playing what was called the Chitlin circuit of the South for a long time before that song took off in 1972."
**Love Train (1972)** : Mentioned as a stark contrast to the theme and style of "Back Stabbers," indicating the O'Jays' stylistic versatility. "It's a world away from love train."

## curiosities
**Philadelphia Sound Pioneers** : The O'Jays' "Back Stabbers" is a prime example of the "Philadelphia sound," characterized by its lush string arrangements, a signature of producers Kenny Gamble and Leon Huff. This sound distinguished their work and became highly influential in soul music. "That is also the Philadelphia sound of Kenny Gamble and Leon Huff you can just hear it with those strings."
**From Chitlin Circuit to Chart-Toppers** : Prior to the success of "Back Stabbers" in 1972, The O'Jays had a long and extensive career performing on the "Chitlin circuit" across the American South. This network of venues was historically vital for black entertainers during segregation, and their eventual mainstream success marked a significant transition from a regional touring act to national prominence. "Backstabbers was the first number one R&B single for the OJs who had been playing what was called the Chitlin circuit of the South for a long time before that song took off in 1972."

# artist - Isaac Hayes

## genres
Soul
Funk

## labels
Stax Records

## albums
**Hot Buttered Soul (1969)** : This album, recorded by Isaac Hayes, featured only four epic songs. It was a pivotal release for Stax Records, which had recently split from Atlantic Records and needed to quickly build a new catalog. Stax released 27 albums simultaneously, offering many artists a chance, but Hayes insisted on doing it his way. Despite his personal indifference to its commercial performance, stating he didn't care if it sold, the album went on to redefine soul music. "Isaac Hayes recorded four epic songs and only four songs for the album Hot Buttered Soul in 1969."

## songs
**Walk On By (1969)** : Isaac Hayes's 12-minute rendition of Dionne Warwick's 1963 song "Walk On By" exemplifies his groundbreaking approach to soul music. His extended, soulful reinterpretation transformed the "very pleasant" original into an immersive and "cool" experience, showcasing his unique artistic vision and disregard for conventional song structures. "That song is 12 minutes of cool."

## curiosities
**Redefining Soul Music** : Long before his role as the voice of Chef on *South Park*, Isaac Hayes was a seminal figure who fundamentally redefined soul music. His distinctive style, particularly evident in his *Hot Buttered Soul* album, pushed the boundaries of the genre by embracing extended song lengths and a deep, immersive sound, establishing a new paradigm for what soul music could be. "He was the man who redefined what soul music could be."
**Bald Head and Gold Chains** : The album cover for *Hot Buttered Soul* famously featured only the top of Isaac Hayes's bald head, a bold visual statement. His live performances were equally striking, as he often wore a suit made out of gold chain, which he humorously referred to as his "natural air conditioning." This powerful stage presence contributed to his aura, with drummer Willie Hall describing him as "cool as shit." "It's just the top of Isaac's bald head. When he was on stage, he wore a suit made out of a gold chain. He said it was his natural air conditioning."
**Black Moses LP Jacket - Seizure of Musical Space** : The LP jacket for his album *Black Moses* unfolded to reveal a full-length portrait of Hayes in a robe, arms outstretched. Emily Lordi, a historian of black popular music, highlighted that this "seizure of musical space," both visually and sonically, made a profound political statement during a time when black people were feeling acutely unwelcome in public spheres, facing policing and violence. Hayes's deliberate claiming of time and space in his art resonated deeply with listeners. "His seizure of musical space, literalized in the LP jacket for black Moses, which unfolded to reveal a full-length portrait of Hayes in a robe, his arms outstretched, made a political statement at a time when black people were being made to feel acutely unwelcome in the public sphere."
**Assassination of Dr. King's Influence** : While *Hot Buttered Soul* was not an overtly political album, it was profoundly inspired by the pain and anger Isaac Hayes felt following the assassination of Dr. Martin Luther King Jr., whom he considered a friend. Initially, Hayes was so overcome with anger that he couldn't create music, but he channeled this emotion into his art and a determination to succeed. He resolved to become successful and powerful enough to use his voice to effect change. "Even though this album was not an overt political statement, it was inspired by the pain of Dr. King's assassination. Hayes considered Dr. King a friend and said that after King's death he was so angry he couldn't make music."

# genre - Soul

## curiosities
**Redefinition in the 1970s** : Soul music underwent a significant redefinition in the early 1970s, notably by artists like Isaac Hayes, who expanded its sonic and thematic boundaries. This era saw a shift from previous popular formulas, with artists exploring funkier sounds and more explicit social commentary. "Isaac Hayes... redefined what soul music could be."
**Black Music for Black People** : During the Black Power movement, soul music was increasingly made by and for African Americans, reflecting the movement's goals of self-determination. Artists like Curtis Mayfield emphasized that the primary intention was to create music for the black community, with crossover success to white audiences being a secondary consideration, if it happened at all. "Curtis Mayfield said that black musicians were making black music for black people and if it crossed over it did. But that was not the point."
**Decline of a "Brand of Soul"** : By the mid-1970s, a particular "brand of soul" music began to fade. Radio programmers perceived it as repetitive, and listeners felt its themes about persistent life problems were no longer resonating as powerfully. This decline coincided with the rise of disco, which offered an escape through dance. "From the perspective of the music, radio programmers thought that if it was getting kind of repetitive and that the people who had been buying it felt like it had more than thin that it was time to move on."

# genre - Disco

## curiosities
**Emergence and Dominance** : Disco began to emerge and gain prominence as a distinct genre around the mid-1970s, coinciding with the waning popularity of a specific "brand of soul" music. From 1975 to 1979, disco "ruled the charts and the clubs and the radio," becoming the prevailing sound of the era. "It is not a coincidence that as this brand of soul is fading out, disco was moving in."
**Purpose and Cultural Significance** : At its core, disco was dance music focused on "forgetting your problems and dancing." Beyond being popular dance music, it held significant cultural importance for communities like the gay community, offering spaces for expression and celebration. "Yes, it was more than that to the gay community, of course. But at its heart, it is dance music."
**Contribution to Hip Hop** : Disco was one of the key genres from which hip hop borrowed during its formative years. Though disco dominated the mainstream charts and clubs in the late 1970s, hip hop was developing by integrating elements from funk, soul, and disco. "It's going to borrow from funk and soul and disco and it is going to have a say."

# genre - Hip Hop

## curiosities
**Origins and Influences** : While disco dominated mainstream music from 1975 to 1979, hip hop was simultaneously "brewing in the background." This nascent genre would go on to borrow extensively from the stylistic and sonic elements of funk, soul, and disco, eventually developing into a powerful new musical and cultural force. "Hip hop was brewing in the background. Disco may have ruled the charts and the clubs and the radio between 1975 and 1979, but hip hop was there."
**Social Commentary Resurgence** : Hip hop emerged as a genre that would continue the tradition of social commentary in music made by and for African Americans. Despite the FBI's efforts to suppress the Black Power movement, the issues it raised—such as poverty, police brutality, inadequate housing, and healthcare—persisted, and hip hop would later provide a platform to address these ongoing problems. "Well commentary in music made by and for African Americans was not and is not dead. Hip hop was brewing in the background."

# label - Motown

## curiosities
**Criticism for Lack of Political Content** : Motown faced criticism from African Americans for its perceived reluctance to be more politically outspoken in its music. This was largely due to Berry Gordy's cautious approach, as he remained hesitant about overtly political messaging in his releases until the early 1970s. "Like James Brown Motown was also criticized by African Americans for not being a little more political but Barry Gordy was always afraid of that at least up until the early 1970s."
**Shift from Formulaic Sound** : By 1971, Motown began to move away from its established and highly popular formula, particularly one that had found widespread acceptance among white audiences. This evolution allowed for a "very different funky or sound," as exemplified by songs like The Undisputed Truth's "Smiling Faces Sometimes." "It's 1971 and Motown is finally starting to get out of that formula that they had that was so popular in particular with white people that's a very different funky or sound for Motown."
**Crossover Goal vs. Black Power Ethos** : Historically, Motown's primary goal was to achieve crossover success with a white audience, as this guaranteed radio play and increased record sales. However, this objective became antithetical to the core principles of the Black Power movement, which prioritized black self-determination and cultural pride over seeking approval or acceptance from white audiences. "Yes, that was the goal in the past especially for Motown because crossing over to a white audience meant radio play and record sales but it would be counter to the whole goal of the black power movement to care if white people liked the soul music that was being produced."

# curiosity

**Civil Rights Movement (1954-1968) - Goals and Nature** : This movement, spanning from 1954 to 1968, was characterized as a middle-class movement that primarily sought equality with white Americans. Its strategies often centered on achieving integration and equal rights within existing societal structures, distinct from the later Black Power movement which asserted inherent equality. "The difference, I would say, between the civil rights movement of 1954 to 1968 and the black power movement was that the civil rights movement sought equality with whites. And it was a middle-class movement."
**Martin Luther King Jr. - Non-Violent Civil Disobedience** : Dr. King's central strategy for fighting for the civil rights of African Americans was non-violent civil disobedience. This involved intentionally being present in places where black people were unwanted or legally forbidden, such as lunch counters, and refusing to fight back even if violence was provoked. His philosophy was based on the belief that suffering non-violently would appeal to the conscience of the opponent. "Dr. King's policy was that non-violence would achieve the gains for black people in the United States. His major assumption was that if you are non-violent, if you suffer, your opponent will see your suffering and will be moved to change his heart."
**Martin Luther King Jr. - Letter from Birmingham Jail (1963)** : While imprisoned in Birmingham, Alabama in 1963, Dr. King authored a lengthy response to white clergymen who criticized his protests and urged negotiation instead. In this letter, he argued that "non-violent direct action seeks to create such a crisis and foster such attention that a community which has constantly refused to negotiate is forced to confront the issue." He also expressed profound disappointment with "the white moderate" who, in his view, was "more devoted to order than to justice." "I have almost reached the regrettable conclusion that the Negro's great stumbling block in his stride toward freedom is not the white citizens counselor or the Ku Klux Klaner but the white moderate who was more devoted to order than to justice."
**Montgomery Bus Boycott (December 1955)** : Initiated after Rosa Parks' refusal to give up her seat, the Montgomery Bus Boycott was organized by Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. in response to the inhumanity of segregated bus seating. Black people were required to pay the same fares as white people but had to sit at the back, only if all white seats were taken, and had to stand if a white person was standing. King rallied the community with the statement, "This is inhuman. We will boycott your bus system," which he viewed as the most passive political act. "Dr. King decided that in Montgomery, Alabama, black people had to pay the same prices on the buses as white people, but we had to sit in the back. And we could only sit in the back if every available seat was taken by a white person."
**Stokely Carmichael - Evolution from Non-Violence to Black Power** : Stokely Carmichael, later known as Kwame Ture, was a key figure in the Civil Rights Movement from 1960-1965, initially advocating for non-violent civil disobedience alongside Dr. King. However, after the shooting of James Meredith in June 1963, Carmichael and other activists became disillusioned with non-violence, feeling "angry and tired... tired of folks being brutalized or killed with impunity." This led to his pivotal "Black Power" speech in Greenwood, Mississippi, which marked a decisive shift away from King's philosophy towards a focus on black self-determination and community building. "In order for non-violence to work, your opponent must have a conscience. The United States has none."
**James Meredith - Voter Registration March and Shooting (June 1963)** : On June 6, 1963, James Meredith, an Air Force veteran and the first African American to enroll in the University of Mississippi, began a one-man voter registration march from Memphis to Jackson, Mississippi. On the first day, he was shot by a white man named Aubrey Norville. Meredith survived and recovered, eventually rejoining the march that other civil rights leaders had continued in his name. This event was a catalyst for Stokely Carmichael and other activists to question the effectiveness of non-violent strategies. "On June 6, 1963, James Meredith, Air Force veteran and the first African American to enroll in the University of Mississippi was in day one of his one man voter registration march from Memphis to Jackson, Mississippi. A white man named Aubrey Norville shot Meredith."
**Black Power Movement - Principles and Identity** : The Black Power movement, emerging after 1968, assumed the inherent equality of black individuals and sought "merely the opportunity to express that equality." This movement emphasized black pride, celebrating features like "kinky hair" and "black skin," and rejected the notion that black people needed to assimilate into white society, which it viewed as based on a belief in white superiority. Its core tenets involved black people uniting, forming their own communities with distinct goals, and supporting each other. "The black power movement assumed equality of person and merely sought the opportunity to express that equality by saying we are a proud people. We don't need you to tell us that. Kinky Hair is glorious. Who our black skin is something we're proud of. And we are who we are."
**Black Panther Party - Community Organization and Self-Defense** : Primarily based in Oakland, California, the Black Panther Party was a visible and influential part of the broader Black Power movement, skilled at using media to amplify its message. Beyond their well-known image, they actively organized communities, registered voters, established health centers, and distributed food. They also advocated for African Americans to be exempt from the Vietnam War draft and openly exercised their Second Amendment rights by carrying firearms, even overseeing the arrests of other African Americans. "The black panthers did organize their communities and they did register voters. They opened health centers and they distributed food. They also did say that African Americans should be exempt from the draft, which was sending young black men to Vietnam in large numbers."
**Black Panther Party - California State Capital Protest (May 2, 1967)** : On May 2, 1967, several members of the Black Panther Party entered the California State Capital Building openly carrying shotguns, dressed in their signature berets, dark sunglasses, and leather jackets. This act was a protest against the Mulford Act, proposed legislation that aimed to repeal the right to openly carry firearms in California. Their demonstration directly led to the Mulford Act's passage, which was notably supported by both then-Governor Ronald Reagan and the NRA. "On May 2, 1967, several members of the black panthers walked into the California State Capital Building wearing their berets and dark sunglasses and leather jackets, openly carrying shotguns as a show of protest about the Mulford Act."
**FBI COINTELPRO - Disruption of Black Panther Party** : FBI Director J. Edgar Hoover considered the Black Panther Party a threat to national security and intensified the FBI's counterintelligence program, COINTELPRO, against them. COINTELPRO actively sought to create chaos within the party, including documented actions like sending fake letters to leaders Huey Newton and Eldridge Cleaver to incite conflict. A 1976 Senate committee later reported that the FBI's conduct "contributed to the climate that led to foreign members of the black Panther Party being shot and killed not to mention creating, quote, gang warfare with the Panthers in Chicago and an organization called the United Slaves in Southern California."
**Assassination of Fred Hampton (December 4, 1969)** : Fred Hampton, the leader of the Chicago Black Panthers, was assassinated in his bed alongside his bodyguard Mark Clark in the early morning hours of December 4, 1969. The National Archives website uses the term "assassination by the FBI" to describe his death. An FBI informant provided information about Hampton and his apartment layout and slipped a sedative into his drink. When the Chicago Police Department and FBI stormed his apartment, Hampton was not mortally wounded initially, but an officer subsequently shot him twice in the head at point-blank range. Law enforcement fired 99 shots, while Clark managed only one shot as he was dying. "Fred Hampton was the leader of the Chicago Black Panthers until he was shot and killed, along with his bodyguard Mark Clark and his own bed in the early morning hours of December 4, 1969."
**Jesse Jackson - Shift to "African American" (1988)** : In 1988, Jesse Jackson led a movement to replace the term "black" with "African American" as the preferred identifier for people of African descent in the United States. This followed a period from 1968, sparked by "Say It Loud – I'm Black and I'm Proud," where "Negro" was largely rejected in favor of "black." "This was the time when the term Negro was rejected in favor of black until 1988 when Jesse Jackson led the movement to replace black with African American."
**Black Is Beautiful - Cultural Healing** : The "Black Is Beautiful" movement, strongly reflected in songs like Earth Wind and Fire's "Mighty Mighty," served as a powerful antidote to generations of "spiritual assaults" on African Americans. For decades, the dominant culture had cast black people as "abnormal and inferior," devaluing their features like "thick lips" and "kinky hair." By the 1970s, this movement fostered a reclamation and celebration of black physical traits and identity, helping to heal deep-seated wounds. "It helped to heal my wounds by declaring that black was beautiful. Before the civil rights and black power movements, African Americans suffered relentless spiritual assaults by constantly being cast by the dominant culture as abnormal and inferior."
**Contemporary Relevance of Black Power Issues** : The fundamental issues raised by the Black Power movement—such as poverty, police brutality, inadequate housing, and healthcare—remain tragically relevant decades later. These problems are not merely historical but persist in the present and will continue into the future unless significant, real change occurs, suggesting that a new wave of soul music could still address these same struggles. "The issues that were raised by that movement were not wiped out though. It's going on 50 years later and a new round of soul music could be created that addresses the same problems, poverty, police brutality, inadequate housing and healthcare and we would not think of those problems as history because they're not."
