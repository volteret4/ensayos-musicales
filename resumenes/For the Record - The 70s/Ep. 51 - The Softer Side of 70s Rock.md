## albums
**Déjà Vu (1970)** : Released on March 11, 1970, this was the band's second album and their first with Neil Young, who only played on half of its tracks. Critics considered it "too perfect" and expressed doubts about replicating its "beautiful harmonies" live, sometimes commenting that it "just wasn't messy enough." *Rolling Stone* magazine has listed *Déjà Vu* multiple times in its "top 500 albums of all time," most recently at number 220. "Some critics felt that the second album and the first with Neil Young deja vu was just too perfect young only played on half of those tracks by the way there were doubts that these beautiful harmonies could be replicated live there were comments that it just wasn't messy enough."

## curiosities
**"Godfathers of Soft Rock" Influence** : Crosby, Stills, Nash & Young are considered strong candidates for the "godfathers of soft rock" due to their significant influence on many mid-to-late 1970s bands that achieved hits with soft rock sounds, particularly those emphasizing vocal harmonies. "if there is such a thing as godfathers of soft rock I think you could make a very strong case for crossbeast is nation young so many of the bands of the mid to late 70s who had hits with the soft rock sound especially those who leaned into harmony credited CS and why with being a major influence."
**David Crosby's Harmonic Genius** : David Crosby, despite his "famously difficult" reputation, was a "musical genius" responsible for arranging most, if not all, of the harmonies on *Déjà Vu*. "he arranged most if not all of the harmonies on this record and in doing so he plants the seeds for the soft rock boom to come."

When America's song "A Horse with No Name" was released in 1971, its sound was so similar that many listeners mistakenly believed it was a Neil Young track. ## albums
**Fleetwood Mac (1975) - The "White Album"** : This album, often called the "white album," marked a new era for the band after the 1975 addition of Lindsey Buckingham and Stevie Nicks. It successfully blended "songwriter confessional trend" with "laid back California rock" and "beautiful harmonies," yielding hit songs like "Rhiannon," "Over My Head," and "Say You Love Me." "This rather magical mix took the whole songwriter confessional trend mixed it in with the laid back California rock and the beautiful harmonies and it gave us what we typically call the Fleetwood Mac white album."
**Rumours (1977)** : In a 2007 retrospective, Patrick McKay described *Rumours* as a "safe middle ground" for pop music bands, positioned between punk rock (like Richard Hell) and conventional music. Critic Robert Christgau praised it, noting its role in reminding listeners that "rock and roll is supposed to be about pleasure as well as all the heavy stuff" during the "year of the punk." Conversely, critic Robert Hilburn found its weakest moments to be "a nutritionist nightmare 16 zillion calories and no substance," criticizing its sweet but "unfulfilling" vocals and "muted" guitar work compared to their live performances. Despite some initial critical reservations, it has become one of the best-selling albums of all time, cherished for its "near musical flawlessness" and lyrics that deeply resonated with listeners, particularly "boomers" navigating divorce in the late 1970s. "Patrick McKay wrote in a retrospective review of the album rumors in 2007. This he said this about rumors to pop music bands Fleetwood Mac must have seemed like a safe middle ground between Richard Hell punk rocker by the way and very good."

## curiosities
**Early British Blues Formation (Late 1960s)** : The band originally began its life in the late 1960s as Peter Green's Fleetwood Mac, a "British blues band." "the band began life as Peter greens Fleetwood Mac which is a British blues band in late 60s."
**Making Rock More Palatable (1970s)** : After its lineup changes, Fleetwood Mac's version of rock music broadened the genre's appeal, making it "more palatable to those who were no longer interested in Jimmy Hendrix or Led Zeppelin." "I do think that their version of rock made rock more palatable to those who were no longer interested in Jimmy Hendrix or Led Zeppelin."
**1975 Lineup Changes** : In 1975, when Bob Welch left the band, Lindsey Buckingham was invited to join. Buckingham stipulated that his girlfriend, Stevie Nicks, also be accepted into the band. This led to American folk rock singer-songwriters Buckingham and Nicks joining Mick Fleetwood (drums), John McVie (bass), and Christine McVie (keyboards, singer-songwriter). "in 1975 Lindsey Buckingham insisted that his girlfriend Stevie Nicks be accepted into what remained of Fleetwood Mac at that time when Lindsey Buckingham was invited to fill the void. That was left when Bob Welch left the band so you have a pair of American folk rock singer songwriters Buckingham and Nicks joining the drummer Mick Fleetwood the bassist John McVee and keyboard player and singer songwriter Christine McVee yes John's wife."
**Internal Relationship Drama and Drug Use Fueling Songwriting** : During the mid-to-late 1970s, the band was immersed in intense personal drama, including a "messy love triangle" involving Stevie Nicks, Mick Fleetwood, and Lindsey Buckingham, simultaneous with the impending divorce of John and Christine McVie. This, combined with "a whole lot of drugs," provided the band "a lot of material to write about" for their songs, despite the personal challenge of having to perform together nightly. "It would not take long though for relationship drama Stevie and Mick and Stevie and Lindsey form a messy love triangle while the same time the McVees were on their way to divorce. You take that and a whole lot of drugs and you combine it and it gives the band a lot of material to write about if they could survive it and if they could stand to be near each other on the stage."
**Radio-Friendly and Non-Aggressive Lyrics on *Rumours*** : The lyrics on the *Rumours* album were described as "meaningful," "radio friendly," and "not aggressive," attributes that contributed to its widespread success and appeal. "The Lyrics, the meaningful, the songs, radio friendly, not aggressive."
**Post-Release Appreciation for *Rumours*' Flawlessness** : Over time, *Rumours* has gained collective appreciation for its "near musical flawlessness," described as having "not a clunker in the mix." Its lyrics were deeply meaningful not only to the band members who lived the experiences but also to listeners, contributing to its enduring legacy. "Over time we collectively have come to appreciate the near musical flawlessness of this record not a clunker in the mix and how the lyrics were and are not just meaningful to them but to the listeners as well."

Goble attributed its greater popularity in the US compared to Australia to its depiction of "the world that was my internal world," a fantasy of 1940s and 1950s American pop culture that resonated strongly with Americans raised in that era. The record label initially overlooked its potential as a single. "All that is not exactly what's it's my it though it was written by gobel it was inspired by a chord that David Briggs showed him it was simply from that chord and it's a C9 for those who care about that sort of thing that these lyrics and melody float."
## curiosities
**Criticism as "Too Bland" or "Too Soft"** : The band frequently faced criticism that their music was "too bland, too safe, too soft," highlighting a common perception leveled against softer rock acts. "Little river band was told that their music was too bland to save to soft."
**Significant Lineup Changes and Nationality Shift** : Little River Band has undergone numerous lineup changes, so extensive that tracking them would require a flowchart. The band, originally Australian, eventually became an American band with "no original members," though Wayne Nelson has been a member since approximately 1980.
