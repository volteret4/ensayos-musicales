## songs
**Sweet City Woman (1971)** : The song is not about a woman, but rather about the band's move from Calgary to Toronto, capturing the energy of a new place with city lights. It features a banjo, which is seen as a clever nod to their Calgary origins, a city with a western identity. The song also includes a French phrase, "bonsei bong," as a shout-out to their fans in Montreal. It was a number one hit in Canada for a month in 1971, reaching number one on three different Canadian charts, and peaked at number eight on the Billboard Top 40 in the United States. When the song hit number one, the band was performing in high schools, as there were not many other venues available to them at the time. "Sweet Sweet City Woman. See your face, I can hear your voice, I can almost touch you."

## curiosities
**Canadian Success vs. American Perception** : From an American perspective, The Stampeders are considered one-hit wonders as they never had another hit in the United States. However, from a Canadian perspective, they are not, having achieved seven more top ten hits in Canada before disbanding in 1980. They were a very popular touring band and toured internationally more than any other Canadian band of their era. While they opened for acts like The Beach Boys and Steely Dan in the US, they were headliners in Canada. "The Stamp Eaters won four Juneau Awards in 1972, which is the equivalent of the Grammys in the United States."

# artist - Danny O'Keefe

## genres
Singer-songwriter

## labels
Atlantic Records

## songs
**Good Time Charlie's Got the Blues (1972)** : This song was Danny O'Keefe's third attempt at recording it; the first was unreleased, and the second appeared on his self-titled debut album in 1971. The third version is slower and more introspective, fitting perfectly into the early 1970s singer-songwriter wave, both lyrically and emotionally, alongside artists like James Taylor, Jackson Browne, and Carole King who explored intimacy and confessional music. The production and arrangement are simple, focusing on O'Keefe's voice and lyrics. The song's lyrics include lines like "My heart keeps telling me you're not a kid at 33. You play around, do you lose your wife, you play too long, you lose your life. I got my pills to ease the pain, can't find a thing to ease the rain. I'd love to try and settle down, but everybody's leaving town. Some got a win, some got a lose. Good time Charlie's Got the Blues." The song was released in the late summer of 1972, made it to number nine on the Billboard Hot 100, and was recorded in Muscle Shoals, Alabama. O'Keefe himself stated in 1973 that the song was "almost a trademark" for him, noting that out of 50 or 60 songs he had written, this was the one that stuck, and he could "play that song forever." Although he continued writing and performing, he never replicated this level of success. The song has been covered by numerous artists, including Elvis Presley (on his 1974 album *Memphis*), Waylon Jennings, Charlie Rich, and Dwight Yoakam, and O'Keefe wrote other songs that were recorded by many prominent musicians. "Good Time Charlie's Got the Blues fits that mold almost perfectly."

## curiosities
**Los Angeles Times Critical Recognition** : In January 1973, Robert Hillburn, an early-career music critic for the Los Angeles Times, who was generally not a fan of singer-songwriters, wrote a lengthy article titled "Good Luck to Three Friends Who Will Need It" for the Sunday edition of the paper. This article featured large pictures of John Prine, Bruce Springsteen (then an unknown), and Danny O'Keefe. Hillburn was drawn to O'Keefe by the lyric "You're Not a Kid at 33?" from "Good Time Charlie's Got the Blues," which resonated with him and many others. Hillburn noted Springsteen's potential and his own intention to listen to his next album, but he found O'Keefe, in many ways, "the best of the three," yet wondered if such "short-term friends are worth the effort." "Hillburn had sampled like popular favorites, like John Denver, those who had just had breakthrough hits like O'Keefe, a couple of critical successes like Prine, and then three quote unknowns."

# artist - Sammy Johns

## instruments
Chevy Van (customized)
Ferrari (purchased with song earnings)

## songs
**Chevy Van (1975)** : The idea for "Chevy Van" originated when Sammy Johns saw a fellow musician in a Ford Econoline van, but "Chevy" sounded better for the song. The song's narrative of hooking up with a stranger in a van reflects a very specific mid-70s idea of freedom—romantic, hazy, and transient, with no real-world consequences, focusing more on the space (the van) than the people. The lyrics include lines such as: "I gave a girl a ride in my wagon, she crawled in and took control, she was tired as her mind was dragon. I said get some sleep and dream of rock and roll, because like a picture she was laying there, moonlight dancing off her hair, she woke up and took me by the hand, she's going to love me in my Chevy Van and that's all right with me." and "Her young face was like that of an angel. Her long legs were tanning brown. Better keep your eyes on the gold sun. Better slow, just be it going down." The song was first released in 1973 on Johns' debut album but was re-released in 1975, becoming a huge radio hit and reaching number five on the Billboard Top 40. The session musicians known as The Wrecking Crew from Los Angeles backed Johns on the track. Despite the adult themes, Johns noted that the song appealed to "teeny-boppers," and while he never liked hard rock, he believed "Chevy Van did a lot for a whole lot of people's souls in 1975." "The idea of writing a song about hooking up with a stranger in a van is very 70s though."

## curiosities
**Pre-Fame Financial Struggles** : Before writing "Chevy Van," Sammy Johns was earning $150 a week playing clubs in the Charlotte area. "That is not bad for a guy who was making $150 a week playing clubs in the Charlotte area before he wrote this hit song."
**Post-Hit Aspirations and Reality** : After the success of "Chevy Van," Johns was not concerned about a follow-up, confidently stating, "I would bet I have at least four top ten songs off my next album. The only thing that would stop me is if I don't want to do it anymore." However, he never achieved the same level of success with subsequent music, despite continuing to make music and providing the soundtrack for the 1977 movie *The Van*. "He made the soundtrack for the 1977 movie The Van."
**Financial Success and Personal Acquisitions** : In 1975, the Charlotte Observer reported that the song "Chevy Van" generated enough money for Sammy Johns to purchase a Ferrari and acquire a customized Chevy Van of his own. "the song Chevy Van made Sammy John's so much money, he was able to buy a Ferrari and he was about to get a customized Chevy Van of his very own."

# artist - Ace

## concerts

## songs
**How Long (1975)** : Lyrically, "How Long" appears to be about infidelity, but it was actually written by Paul Carrack about internal band turmoil. The inspiration came from an incident where bassist Tex Comer was considering joining two other bands at a time when Ace was already facing problems due to their drummer having quit. Carrack described the band's state in 1975 to Rolling Stone magazine, saying, "we were really at the bottom, quite down with various problems." He sketched out the lyrics on the back of a bus ticket while traveling to see his future mother-in-law. The song's laid-back groove made it a popular soft rock track on radio stations in the mid-70s, drawing comparisons to bands like Little River Band and Steely Dan. Ellen King, who sang vocals and played guitar for Ace, noted that the song had an "American feel to it." It made its chart debut in March 1975 and reached number three on the Billboard Top 40 on May 31, 1975. "How long has this been going on? How long has this been going on?"

## albums
**Time for another (1975)** : This album was the follow-up to Ace's hit single "How Long" and was generally considered a disappointment. According to Paul King, the album lacked energy, likely because the band went straight into the studio after an exhausting tour with Yes. In 1977, King expressed a desire to forget they ever recorded the album and wished they could re-record the songs. "Paul King said that their follow-up album Time for another was definitely a disappointment, but not because of the song. So he said it lacked the energy."

## curiosities
**Differing Views on Success** : Paul King, a member of Ace, did not believe the success of "How Long" came "too much too soon," stating that the band had been "slogging through clubs for two years" and were ready for success. However, Paul Carrack, the songwriter, later reflected in the early 1980s that success with Ace "was too much too soon" and that the band "took a lot of hard knocks on the way down." "When Ace broke up, Carrick went to go play with Roxy Music, which he said helped him get over the frustration of trying to get another hit record, he said. It was the first step to recovery."

# artist - Wild Cherry

## members
Rob Parissi (songwriter, singer, producer)

## genres
Rock (early)
Funk
Disco

## songs
**Play That Funky Music (1976)** : Originally a rock band, Wild Cherry found it increasingly difficult to get work as disco gained popularity. Rob Parissi, the band's songwriter, singer, and producer, told the band they needed to start playing more dance music. The idea for the song came from audience members at a disco where they were playing, who would ask, "When are you going to play some funky music, white boy?" Parissi wrote the first two verses and the chorus before getting to the stage, and the last verse in his car on the way home, viewing himself as a "reporter" documenting their experiences. His father had advised him to remove the "white boy" part, but it ultimately became a selling point. The song became a massive hit, reaching number one on the US Billboard Hot 100 on September 18, 1976, and also topping the Billboard Hot Soul singles chart. Many people initially believed the band was African American and were surprised to see they were white when they appeared on TV shows like American Bandstand and Midnight Special, or when opening for artists like George Benson and Santana. Despite the success, Parissi stated in 1976 that the band did not want to continue playing funk or disco. The band dissolved by the end of the 1970s after failing to produce more hits. "I wrote, sang and produced, Play That Monkey Music White Bull."

# artist - Anita Ward

## genres
Disco

## songs
**Ring My Bell (1979)** : This song was originally written by Frederick Knight for Stacey Lattisaw, who was 11 years old at the time (and 14 when she later covered "Love on a Two Way Street" in 1981). Anita Ward, then 23 and a teacher, was initially hesitant about the song. It was rewritten with more adult lyrics, having originally been conceived about teenagers talking on the phone, and then given the "disco treatment." The use of a synthetic drum machine and Ward's unique, high soprano voice are credited with significantly contributing to its success. The lyrics include: "I'm glad you're home. Now did you really miss me? I guess you did by the look in your eye." The song was tested in dance clubs and "caught fire right away," with a Memphis newspaper comparing its impact to how Elvis caught on. When the song was climbing the charts, Ward was asked to perform on Midnight Special but initially hesitated due to her teaching commitments. It won a Grammy in 1980 for Best R&B Female Vocal Performance, but Ward had no further hits. "I'm glad you're home. Now did you really miss me? I guess you did by the look in your eye."

## curiosities

## songs
**Cruel to Be Kind (1979)** : Co-written years earlier with Ian Gomm, this song had been recorded in rougher forms before its 1979 release. It is described as a "perfect pop song" that is tight, catchy, and under three minutes. "Cruel to Be Kind" exemplified the emerging New Wave sound, which pushed back against the late 1970s rock trend of long songs, big solos, and a perceived need for everything to be "super important." New Wave, and this song in particular, prioritized efficiency: a quick intro, verse, chorus, verse, and then out, with no wasted space. This approach made it accessible for radio listeners and casual fans, offering something new that wasn't overly complex. It made it to number 12 on the Billboard Top 40 in 1979. "Cruel to be kind doesn't seem like it's anything revolutionary, it's very tight, it's catchy, it's under three minutes, that's kind of the point because by the time we get to the late 1970s, rock had gotten very big, we had long songs, big solos, we got this idea that everything had to be super important."

## curiosities
**Critical Acclaim vs. Commercial Success** : In a 1986 interview with Karen Schlossberg for Cream magazine, Nick Lowe reflected on the emotional toll of his career, stating that he had released three albums he considered "really good" with "great critical reviews by and large," but they "disappeared without a trace." This pattern led him to "wonder about your own sanity." He expressed a dislike for the "sound which all records have got to have nowadays in order to get on the radio," preferring spontaneity and "a little element of the human being at work" over perfect, error-free recordings. "I listen to the radio nowadays and I keep on dying to hear a record with a mistake on it. They're also bloody perfect and they really get on my nerves."

**Reaction to 70s Rock** : By the late 1970s, a new sound called New Wave emerged, pushing back against the prevailing trend of 70s rock, which had become characterized by long songs, big solos, and a perception that everything had to be "super important." Early New Wave, exemplified by songs like Nick Lowe's "Cruel to Be Kind," focused on being tight, catchy, and concise (under three minutes). "New wave especially the early version of it pushes back on this idea and Cruel to be kind is a perfect example of how to do that."

# curiosity

**One-Hit Wonders Defined** : A "one-hit wonder" is a term that can be dismissive, akin to a footnote, but in reality, many of these artists were talented, hardworking musicians who happened to catch the "exact right or wrong combination of timing, taste, and industry momentum." Some had long careers behind the scenes, some were regional stars, and others simply couldn't replicate the magic of their one perfect song. "But one hit wonder is also kind of a tricky label. This sounds a little dismissive like a footnote."
**1970s Music Industry Climate** : The 1970s was a period when the music industry and the Billboard charts were "wide open," allowing a single track to become a significant cultural moment, sometimes overnight. This environment made it almost inevitable that some artists would achieve massive breakthrough success just once. "this was still the time when the music industry and the Billboard charts were wide open. It also still had a lot of power and could turn a single track into that cultural moment."
**Singer-Songwriter Wave of Early 70s** : The early 1970s saw a significant wave of singer-songwriters who focused heavily on intimacy and a confessional style of music, with simple production and arrangements that highlighted the voice and lyrics. Their songs felt less like performances and more like intimate conversations, resonating with Baby Boomers who were transitioning into adulthood during this decade. "it sits right in the middle of that early 70s singer-songwriter wave, both lyrically and emotionally. In that period, you think about James Taylor, Jackson Brown, Carol King, and they were leaning very heavily into this intimacy, almost confessional type of music."
**1970s Van Culture** : In the 1970s, vans were more than just vehicles; they represented an identity. People customized them with murals, shag carpeting, and beds in the back, using them for road trips and sometimes as living spaces. Owning a van was a statement about personal freedom and a desire not to be tied down. "Vans were not just vehicles. They were like this whole identity. People customized them with murals and shag carpeting. They had beds in the back. They were parts of road trips. They were parts of living spaces sometimes."
**Impact of Disco on Music Industry** : The rise of disco made it increasingly difficult for rock bands to find work, leading many rock clubs to close. This shift forced bands to adapt by playing more dance music or risk being unable to book gigs. "And the rock clubs were closing down. And the only place that could get work, we were hardworking four, five, six nights a week. And everybody had cars, home, you know, stuff like that."
