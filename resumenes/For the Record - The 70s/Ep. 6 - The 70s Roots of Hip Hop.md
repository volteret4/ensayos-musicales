## songs
**Rapper's Delight (1979)** : This song became the first mainstream hip hop hit, marking the beginning of hip hop's dominance in popular music. Kenny Arnold, an Iowa Hawkeyes basketball player, was known to play the song on repeat for hours in 1980. The track was essentially rapping over the bassline of Chic's "Good Times." While the quick wordplay was known in live rap performances, this was unprecedented in mainstream music. The rapping is described as tame compared to later hip hop. The three members, initially naive to the music industry, were recruited by Sylvia Robinson, who found Big Hank at Krispy Krust Pizza and the others by driving around asking rappers to record over "Good Times." The song was recorded in 14 minutes. Sylvia Robinson mailed records to black radio stations and pushed for airplay, which led to listener demand and the birth of the hip hop industry. Music writer Eric Reese stated that "Rapper's Delight" was a "tear in the veil" and a "standard" for hip hop's success when companies were hesitant to finance rap artists or labels. It broke financial barriers and inspired rappers to develop their own styles. "Now what you hear is not a chest on the rapid to the beat. And me, the groove and my friends are gonna try to move your feet."
## curiosities
**Legal Troubles Over Names** : The members of The Sugar Hill Gang encountered significant legal troubles over their own names and who had the right to be "The Sugar Hill Gang." This was attributed to their naiveté regarding the music industry's practices, such as the conflict of interest when the record label and band management are the same entity. "These guys encountered a whole lot of legal troubles over their own names and who really had the right to be the Sugar Hill gang. But that's because they were naive, I think, to the ways of the music industry."

# artist - Gil Scott Heron
## members
Gil Scott Heron
## genres

## albums
**Small Talk at 125th and Lennox (1970)** : This album is considered a precursor to rap, featuring Gil Scott Heron reciting poems, including "The Revolution Will Not Be Televised," accompanied by a bongo. The recording took place with a small group of people on folding chairs. Its release coincided with the emergence of FM Radio in bigger cities, allowing some cuts to be played on underground FM stations and boosting record sales. In 2005, the original version of "The Revolution Will Not Be Televised" from this album was added to the National Recording Registry by the Library of Congress due to its historical and cultural significance. Malik Al Nassir noted that without this album, hip hop might have been limited to just "Rapper's Delight." "I went to the studio with a small group of folks on folding chairs and did poems from Small Talk in a few songs I had done on pianos and coffee houses."
**Pieces of Man (1971)** : This album, created with Brian Jackson, placed much more emphasis on music compared to "Small Talk at 125th and Lennox." It features the version of "The Revolution Will Not Be Televised" that is most widely known, serving as a "literal time capsule of early 70s America." The song includes references to celebrities of the era like Glen Campbell, Natalie Wood, and Tom Jones, and offers commentary on white America and mass consumerism. "It is a literal time capsule of early 70s America, not just with the references to the celebrities of the era like Glen Campbell, Natalie Wood, Tom Jones, but with commentary on white America as well as mass consumerism."
## songs
**The Revolution Will Not Be Televised (1970)** : This piece, initially released on the album "Small Talk at 125th and Lennox" in 1970, features Gil Scott Heron reciting (or "rapping") words over a bongo accompaniment. A more musically emphasized version was released in 1971 on "Pieces of Man." The lyrics provide sharp social commentary, critiquing consumerism and media, with lines like "The Revolution will not go better with Coke" and "The Revolution will not fight the germs that may cause bad breath." The 1970 original was inducted into the National Recording Registry by the Library of Congress in 2005 for its historical and cultural importance, and is seen as a foundational piece influencing hip hop's later political direction. "The Revolution will not be televised."
## curiosities
**Early Life in the Bronx** : Gil Scott Heron was born in Chicago but was sent to live with his grandmother in the Bronx around 1961, when he was about 12 years old. This experience likely informed his later work, as the Bronx's socio-economic conditions were deteriorating throughout the 70s, which became a fertile ground for the birth of hip hop. "Gil was born in Chicago, but he was sent to live in the Bronx with his grandmother when he was about 12, which was around 1961."
**Poetic Foundations** : Before his musical career, Gil Scott Heron established himself as a poet. His first book of poetry, titled "Small Talk at 125th and Lennox," was published in 1970, and he subsequently recorded an album of the same name. This background in poetry directly influenced his spoken-word musical style, which is seen as a precursor to rap. "He was a poet, and his first book of poetry was published in 1970 and was called Small Talk at 125th and Lennox."

# artist - DJ Kool Herk
## members
Clive Campbell
## genres

## curiosities
**Father of Hip Hop** : Clive Campbell, known as DJ Kool Herk, is widely regarded as the father of hip hop. Born in Jamaica, he moved to New York and began his DJ career by playing at his sister's birthday party. He quickly gained recognition for his skills at house parties and block parties before transitioning to club DJing. "Clive Campbell, a native of Jamaica moved to New York. He is considered by many people to be the father of hip hop."
**The Merry Go Round Technique** : DJ Kool Herk pioneered the "merry go round" technique, which involved using two turntables to extend the "break" sections of funk and soul records. He observed that dancers (B-boys) would wait for specific parts of records to perform their special moves. By extending these instrumental breaks, he allowed dancers more time to "do their thing" and showcase their best moves, which became known as breakdancing in the 1980s. He discussed this experiment in a 2005 interview with Terry Gross on NPR's Fresh Air. "He took that to turn table technique to extend the break like the ones we just heard and give it up or turn it a loose, which he called the merry go round."
**Apache and the Perfect Beat** : In his 2005 interview with Terry Gross, DJ Kool Herk recounted how his "merry go round" experiment took off with the song "Apache," often referred to as "The Incredible Bongo Rock." He noticed that people would specifically wait for this part of his set. The success of using "Apache" in this manner led other DJs to search for the "perfect beat," though, according to Herk, no one has ever truly beaten "that record" (Apache). "Everybody would come and really wait for that particular part of my format for me to get into it. And that's when everybody started searching for the perfect beat, trying to beat that record."

**Student of Breaks** : As a pioneering figure in hip hop, Grandmaster Flash dedicated three years of his teenage life to immersing himself in music, specifically focusing on understanding and manipulating "breaks" in funk and soul records. He developed a meticulous approach, noticing that the breaks could sometimes be physically seen in the vinyl grooves. To mark these sections, he would use a grease pencil or crayon directly on the records. "Grandmaster Flash said that he devoted three years of his life as a teenager just immersing himself in the music and trying to figure out what to do with these breaks."
**Breakthrough with The Message** : After being signed by Sugar Hill Records, Grandmaster Flash and the Furious Five achieved a significant breakthrough in 1982 with their impactful song, "The Message." This track further cemented the political and social commentary aspects of hip hop, which had roots in earlier works like Gil Scott Heron's. "Sugar Hill then went on to sign Grandmaster Flash in the Furious Five who broke through in 1982 with the message."

# artist - Mike Viner's Incredible Bongo Band
## albums
**Bongo Rock (1973)** : This album, featuring the cover of "Apache," became a seminal piece in early hip hop. "Apache" was a 1973 cover of a 1960 instrumental by British composer Jerry Lorton, and it quickly became known for its extended, minute-long percussion break. Music writer Michael Angelo Matos described the band's rendition as "the most crazed piece of orchestral funk ever recorded," maintaining the original's "noble and dramatic" shape while taking the liberty of extending the break with dueling trap drummer and congaist. This particular track is heavily sampled and considered the "national anthem of hip-hop." "Viner and his crew had concocted the most crazed piece of orchestral funk ever recorded."
## songs
**Apache (1973)** : This 1973 cover of Jerry Lorton's 1960 instrumental became famously known as "The Incredible Bongo Rock" and is often dubbed the "national anthem of hip-hop." The track is characterized by its "crazed piece of orchestral funk" and, most significantly, a minute-long percussion break featuring a trap drummer and congaist dueling. DJ Kool Herk successfully used this break to popularize his "merry go round" technique, leading to its widespread use in early hip-hop. Music writer Michael Angelo Matos highlighted its enduring influence, noting it would be remade and sampled countless times, especially when sampling was less legally regulated. "Everybody is still using Bongo Rock's Apache."

## albums
**Vescaix (1979)** : This album, although referred to as "Vescaix" in the transcript, featured the highly influential track "Good Times." By this point, the band's sound had evolved to be much more complex and intricate. Barry Crocker, writing for The New York Rocker in 1980, described the song's "lush string orchestrations and colorful poly melodies" as concealing a "raunchy pulse" derived from the streets, embodying a "gut level instinct" that was raw and authentic, free of artificial trappings. "The sound of the band had become much more complex and intricate by then. This was their third album titled Vescaix."
## songs
**Le Freak (1978)** : This song was Chic's first number one hit, preceding "Good Times" in December 1978. Its success established the band's prominence in the disco era. "following Le Freak in December 1978."
**Good Times (1979)** : Released in June 1979, this disco track became Chic's second number one song, topping the Billboard Hot 100 by August 18th and reaching number three on the disco chart. Nile Rogers noted that he and Bernard Edwards recognized it as "the perfect hip hop record" due to its extended breakdown, which could accommodate long rhymes. Barry Crocker, reviewing for The New York Rocker in 1980, described its appeal as coming from a "raunchy pulse" and a "gut level instinct," despite its lush orchestrations. The song's bassline was compared to a "jungle drum" and its handclap to a "heartbeat lifeline." It is ranked number 220 on Rolling Stone's list of the top 500 songs of all time, largely due to its extensive sampling. "Good Times" is recognized for its profound influence on hip hop, with its DNA woven into the genre, and its baseline forming the core of "Rapper's Delight." "The breakdown took so long to develop that they could have rhymes that could go on forever."
## curiosities
**Lawsuit Against Sugar Hill Records** : When Nile Rogers first heard "Rapper's Delight" and recognized his song "Good Times" being sampled without proper credit, he and Bernard Edwards were upset, particularly since their names were absent from the Sugar Hill Records album. Their label, Atlantic, declined to sue, so Rogers and Edwards personally filed a lawsuit against Sugar Hill. Despite financial investor Morris Levy's known mafia connections to Sugar Hill, they proceeded with the suit, resulting in a favorable out-of-court settlement. This action set a precedent for stricter copyright enforcement in sampling and ensured they received writing credit on "Rapper's Delight." "Nile and Bernard were understandably not happy that their music was basically stolen with no financial compensation to them, but their record label Atlantic would not sue. So Nile and Bernard sued Sugar Hill on their own."

# genre - Hip Hop
## curiosities
**Birth in the Bronx** : Hip hop was born in the Bronx in the 1970s, a period when the borough was experiencing severe poverty and neglect following decades of white flight and discriminatory housing practices. The lack of basic services and widespread building abandonment and arson created a "hellscape" where young black and Latino residents sought to create their own culture and music. The context of urban decay and social strife contributed to hip hop's origins as an art form that reflected its creators' realities. "Many people know that hip hop was born in the Bronx in the 70s."
**Evolution from Funk and Disco** : Hip hop's genesis is deeply intertwined with funk and disco, borrowing the two-turntable technique and perfected break beats from house DJs. Pioneers like DJ Kool Herk and Grandmaster Flash sifted through used funk and soul records, crafting a new genre around these instrumental breaks. While hip hop embraced elements of disco, it also emerged as a rejection of disco's polished, orchestral sound and its perceived lack of celebration for black artists, aiming for something more "real" and reflective of the Bronx's conditions. "That my friends has a lot to do with funk and disco."
**Political Turn in the 80s** : While early mainstream hip hop like "Rapper's Delight" was tame in its lyrical content, the genre took a distinct political turn in the mid to late 1980s, aligning more closely with the social commentary found in Gil Scott Heron's earlier works like "The Revolution Will Not Be Televised." This evolution saw hip hop become a powerful voice for reflecting the political and social realities of its creators. "The political turn that hip hop takes in the mid to late 80s is indeed a fair distance from rappers delight, but not so far from the Revolution will not be televised."
**Sampling as a Core Element** : Sampling records, particularly disco and funk, was a foundational technique in early hip hop and rap songs. Artists integrated parts of existing records into their own, with James Brown being the most sampled artist and The Winstons' "Amen Brother" also being highly significant. This practice, though legally dicey in later years, was central to crafting new sounds from existing musical components and led to a complex relationship between artists and source material. "In the late 70s and into the 80s, many, many, many hip-hop artists sampled disco and funk records and integrated them into their own records."

# genre - Disco
## curiosities
**Complicated Relationship with Hip Hop** : The relationship between disco and hip hop was complex, marked by both influence and rejection. Hip hop borrowed key techniques like the two-turntable method and the use of break beats, perfected by house DJs in discos. However, hip hop also emerged as a reaction against disco's polished, orchestral sound and its focus on production rather than the black artists who often created the music. Disco's "glitz and glamour" was seen as unrelatable to the realities of the Bronx, leading young artists to create their own "more real" music. "However, it is also true that the relationship between disco and hip hop is complicated."
**End of Dominance** : Chic's "Good Times," one of the last disco hits, ironically sparked the creation of "Rapper's Delight," the first mainstream hip hop record. This event signaled the eventual end of disco's dominance on the pop charts, as hip hop began its rise to prominence. "Good Times was the last hit record that Sheik ever had, and so I guess it's appropriate that it sparks one of the things that will signal an end to Disco's dominance on the pop charts, which is of course hip hop."

# label - Sugar Hill Records
## curiosities
**Founding and Early Challenges** : Sugar Hill Records was founded by Sylvia Robinson with the explicit goal of recording rap music. Initially, Robinson faced significant challenges in convincing rap artists to record, as rap was widely considered a live performance art, and there was skepticism about its translation to vinyl and commercial audience appeal. Her perseverance led to the discovery and recording of The Sugar Hill Gang, whose "Rapper's Delight" became the label's first major success. "Sylvia Robinson knew she wanted to record a rap record, but she was having a really hard time finding a rap artist who would agree to be recorded."
**Impact on Hip Hop Industry** : The label played a pivotal role in establishing the hip hop industry. After "Rapper's Delight" was recorded, Sylvia Robinson aggressively mailed records to black radio stations and continuously called them until they provided airplay. Once the song aired, listener demand surged, leading to commercial success and effectively birthing the hip hop industry. Sugar Hill Records later signed other pioneering acts like Grandmaster Flash and the Furious Five. "Once the record got airplay, listeners wanted to know where they could get a copy and well, hip hop, the industry was born."
**Lawsuit by Chic Members** : Sugar Hill Records faced a lawsuit from Nile Rogers and Bernard Edwards of Chic due to the unauthorized sampling of their song "Good Times" in "Rapper's Delight." Despite financial investor Morris Levy's known mafia connections, Rogers and Edwards pursued legal action after their own label, Atlantic, refused to sue. The resulting out-of-court settlement established a crucial precedent for copyright enforcement in sampling and ensured proper writing credit for the sampled artists. "It is not insignificant that Morris Levy, a record executive with known mafia connections was a financial investor in Sugar Hill."

# instrument - Turntables
## curiosities
**Two-Turntable Technique** : The use of two turntables was a fundamental innovation in early hip hop, borrowed from house DJs who perfected the technique in discos. Pioneers like DJ Kool Herk utilized this setup to extend instrumental "breaks" from funk and soul records, creating continuous loops for B-boys to dance to. This technique, which Herk called the "merry go round," allowed for the creation of entirely new musical experiences from existing records. "hip hop did borrow the two turntable technique and it used the break beats that house DJs perfected in discos."

# curiosity
**White Flight and Urban Decay (Post-WWII)** : After World War II, white families left American cities in large numbers, moving to suburbs fueled by the GI Bill and affordable, assembly-line housing. This migration was driven by a desire to avoid increasingly racially diverse neighborhoods, with discriminatory practices and federal policies like the FHA's restrictive covenants (e.g., in Levittown on Long Island) actively keeping minorities out of suburbs. This outflow of tax dollars and resources severely impacted cities like the Bronx, leaving black and Latino communities in conditions of extreme poverty, lack of services, and urban decay, which ironically became the crucible for hip hop's birth. "white people left American cities and droves, not wanting to live in neighborhoods that were becoming more and more racially diverse, white families packed their station wagons and headed for the suburbs."
**The Bronx in the 1970s** : In the 1970s, the Bronx was described as the "most hellacious of the boroughs," with over 40% of its children living in poverty. This was a stark contrast to its early 1900s prosperity driven by European immigration and developments like Yankee Stadium. Following the Great Depression and World War II, and exacerbated by "white flight," buildings deteriorated due to rent control making improvements difficult for landlords, and a severe lack of basic services like sanitation, police, and fire. Building owners resorted to arson for insurance money, leading to the phrase "The Bronx was literally on fire," creating a desperate environment that fostered the creation of new art forms like hip hop. "In the 70s, the Bronx was the most hellacious of the burrows. It was just not a safe place to be."
**National Recording Registry - Library of Congress** : In 2005, the original 1970 version of Gil Scott Heron's "The Revolution Will Not Be Televised" from the album "Small Talk at 125th and Lennox" was added to the National Recording Registry by the Library of Congress. This recognition highlights its profound historical and cultural significance as a precursor to rap and a powerful piece of social commentary. "The original from Small Talk at 125th and Lennox was added to the National Recording Registry by the Library of Congress in 2005 because of its historical and cultural significance."
**Most Sampled Songs Resource - Whosampled.com** : Whosampled.com is cited as the most comprehensive resource for identifying sampled songs. According to the website, James Brown's songs have been sampled 8,261 times, making him the most sampled artist. The Winstons' "Amen Brother" is the second most sampled, and Lyn Collins' "Think (About It)" is sampled over 3,000 times, making her the third most sampled artist mentioned. "According to whosampled.com, James Brown songs have been sampled 8,261 times by far the most."
**Kenny Arnold's "Rapper's Delight" Obsession (1980)** : On March 21st, 1980, the Des Moines Register featured a headline "Kenny's delightful music irritates a few ears" about Kenny Arnold, the star guard for the Iowa Hawkeyes basketball team. Teammates revealed that Arnold, despite being loved, would play "Rapper's Delight" by The Sugar Hill Gang repeatedly on his stereo radio, sometimes for two hours straight, irritating those around him with its 15-minute length. This anecdote illustrates the immediate, though sometimes polarizing, impact of the first mainstream hip hop hit. "Arnold said that he had two of the four versions, and he confirmed that he was known to play the song on repeat for two hours in a row."
