## curiosities
**"Gamble" Songwriting (1972)** : In a 1972 interview with Rolling Stone, Paul Simon characterized "Mother and Child Reunion" as a "gamble," explaining that it represented a style of music he felt unable to create during his partnership with Art Garfunkel. "Simon told John Landau in an interview for Rolling Stone in 1972 that Mother and Child Reunion was the type of song that he could not do when he was teamed up with Art Garfunkel and he considered the song a quote gamble."
**Jamaican Music Admiration** : Paul Simon expressed a strong affinity for various musical genres beyond typical American styles, specifically highlighting his deep appreciation for Jamaican music while noting that very few other people were interested in it at the time. "In this interview, he made a point of saying how much he liked Jamaican music but have few people were actually into it at the time."
**Jamaican Recording Approach** : To achieve an authentic sound, Paul Simon traveled to Jamaica for recordings, where he innovatively compensated session musicians by suggesting they were playing three songs instead of one, effectively providing them more money than the standard "notorious racket" of about seven dollars per song with no royalties, and crucially, he allowed them to freely play "whatever they want." "So I sat down and said you play it, play what you want. That's the key thing. Let them play whatever they want and then you change, you go their way."
**Graceland Album Inspiration** : More than a decade after his early reggae experiments, Paul Simon found a pivotal source of inspiration for his Grammy-winning album "Graceland" in the form of a cassette tape featuring South African street music. "More than 10 years before he would find some inspiration for that Grammy winning album Graceland in the form of this cassette tape of South African street music."

## curiosities
**Contract Sale to Island Records** : Bob Marley's initial contract with Johnny Nash's label, Joe, die Records, was eventually sold to Chris Blackwell's Island Records, a pivotal moment that significantly influenced Marley's international career trajectory. "Nash's label eventually sold Bob Marley's contract to Chris Blackwell's Island records."
**US Radio Absence** : Despite his eventual global stardom, Bob Marley and The Wailers never achieved a number one hit or even a Top 40 hit in the United States, and were not considered a radio-friendly act until "classic rock" emerged as a specific radio genre. "There was no place for Bob Marley in the Whalers on American radio. There never would be really."
**White Audience Concern** : Bob Marley was reportedly troubled by the fact that his American audience was predominantly white, a phenomenon music writers attributed partly to American black audiences gravitating toward funk and disco in the 70s and wanting to avoid the perceived "stigma of primitivism" associated with African roots. "It did bother Marley that his American audience was mostly white."
**"Rock Star" Marketing Strategy** : Chris Blackwell intentionally positioned Bob Marley as a rock star, a strategic move made after acknowledging that Marley would not get airplay on black radio stations, aiming instead for mainstream rock exposure and further emphasized by his American booking agent, Don Wine-trop, who insisted on headline-only shows for Marley. "Chris Blackwell was going for. He told Marley he was not going to get played on black radio stations, so he was going to market him as a rock star."
**Refusal to Open for Rolling Stones** : Bob Marley's American booking agent, Don Wine-trop, saw Marley as such a prominent "star" that he rejected an opportunity for The Wailers to open for The Rolling Stones, maintaining a strict policy that Marley only headline performances. "Wine-trop even said no to the rolling stones. He said Marley headliner only."
**First Live US Appearance (1975)** : During his first live performance in 1975 at the Paramount Theatre in Oakland, California, promoted by Bill Graham, Bob Marley's "bizarre appearance" in his street clothes and dreadlocks garnered a strong reaction from the audience. "Stephen said that the crowd, quote, gasped at the bizarre appearance of Marley in his street clothes and dreads and roared in approval when he whipped the dreads around."
**Ed McCormick's Interview Impression (1976)** : In a 1976 interview for Rolling Stone, Ed McCormick described encountering Bob Marley "whacked out of his skull" and in a state of "deep and herbal meditation" while smoking a spliff at his Hope Road house in Kingston. "In fact, Marley was so whacked out of his skull that it was possible to study him in perfect Nubian carving profile for several seconds before even dawned on him that he had company."
**Death from Skin Cancer (1981)** : Bob Marley's life was tragically cut short by skin cancer in 1981, though his passing did not diminish reggae's growing influence on global music. "Marley's death from Skin Cancer in 1981."
**Rock and Roll Hall of Fame Induction (1994)** : Bob Marley was inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 1994, an event that was regarded as effectively inducting reggae itself and formally recognizing its profound historical importance in music. "There is a reason that he was inducted into the rock and roll hall of fame way back in 1994. It was like inducting reggae and acknowledging the historical importance of it."
**Bono's Induction Speech** : During Bob Marley's Rock and Roll Hall of Fame induction, Bono delivered a speech highlighting Marley's ability to "race to the edges embracing all extremes creating a oneness, his oneness, one love," contrasting him with typical rock and roll archetypes. "He raced to the edges embracing all extremes creating a oneness, his oneness, one love."

"The title track to legalize it is just what you would think it is. It is a song about legalizing marijuana."

## curiosities
**Departure from The Wailers (1973)** : Peter Tosh left The Wailers in 1973, citing unhappiness with Chris Blackwell's refusal to support his solo work and a perception that Bob Marley, being lighter-skinned, received preferential attention within the band and from the label. "Tosh left the wailers in 1973. He was unhappy that Chris Blackwell would not let him put out a solo album and there were rumblings about the attention going to Marley."
**Bitterness Over Marley's Skin Tone** : Part of Peter Tosh's resentment stemmed from his belief that Bob Marley garnered more attention due to his lighter skin, leading Tosh to fundamentally align himself with the "black camp" amidst Jamaica's societal dynamics, as described by author Colin Grant. "Some of that bitterness was from Tosh's point of view due to Marley being lighter skinned than he was."
**Marijuana Dealer Funding** : After Chris Blackwell declined to fund his solo album, Peter Tosh secured the necessary money for "Legalize It" from a marijuana dealer, who initially expressed displeasure with the album's controversial title. "Since Chris Blackwell would not fund his solo album, Tosh got a marijuana dealer to give him the money for legalize it, although the dealer initially was not too happy about the name of the album."
**Political Activism** : Peter Tosh was a fervent political activist who personally endured police brutality, and his protests were profoundly influenced by the works of American civil rights leaders such as Dr. Martin Luther King, Stokely Carmichael, and Malcolm X, even though their writings were banned in Jamaica. "He did not just protest police brutality because of articles he read. He experienced it firsthand."
**Newspaper Ad for "Legalize It"** : In response to the Jamaican government's attempt to ban his record, Peter Tosh took a defiant stance by purchasing an advertisement in a Jamaican newspaper to print the complete lyrics of "Legalize It," ensuring his message reached the public. "Tosh also took out an ad and a Jamaican newspaper and printed the lyrics within the ad."

# genre - Reggae

## curiosities
**Pronunciation and Perception Issues** : When reggae first emerged and was unfamiliar to American audiences in the 1970s, many people struggled with its pronunciation, and some even mistakenly equated its sound with disco. "Reggae was so new, so unfamiliar to the American ear that some people weren't even sure how to pronounce the word Reggae. Some people thought it was disco."
**Birth Environment (c. 1968)** : Reggae emerged around 1968 in Jamaica, born out of a tumultuous period that saw the disillusionment of post-independence celebrations, rampant poverty in Kingston exacerbated by government bulldozing of squatter communities, and systemic political corruption that failed to address citizens' urgent needs for jobs. "It was in that environment that reggae was born around 1968."
**Musical Ancestry** : Reggae is characterized as a synthesis of various musical styles, drawing heavily from Jamaican Mento (local folk/country music), dancehall swing (like Benny Goodman or Glenn Miller), and a significant infusion of American rhythm and blues or early rock (such as Fats Domino), with powerful radio signals from Miami reaching Kingston playing a key role in its development. "It is the product of a lot of different musical styles, much of it from the United States, not along by some powerful radio stations out of Miami, whose signals reached Kingston."
**Early Distribution and Censorship** : In its formative years, reggae music was primarily consumed at dances because many Jamaicans could not afford to buy records, and the Jamaican government frequently banned much of the genre from airwaves due to its overtly political and critical lyrical content. "Many Jamaicans could not afford records, and the Jamaican government would not let a lot of reggae on the air because it was too political."
**Limited Performance Venues** : Due to political sensitivities and economic realities in early Jamaica, reggae bands often found that the recording studio was one of their very few options for performance, as opposed to public concerts. "If you were in a reggae band, one of the very few places that you could play was the recording studio, not at a concert."
**Momentum Building (1972)** : Reggae began to gain notable momentum in 1972, a year that coincided with the release of the influential Jamaican film "The Harder They Come," which featured Jimmy Cliff's popular title track and helped bring the genre to a wider audience. "We can see a bit of reggae momentum building in 1972, the same year that the film The Harder They Come was released and Jimmy Cliff made the title track to that movie's soundtrack, a hit."
**Radio Absence for True Artists (70s)** : Throughout the 1970s, it was exceedingly difficult to find reggae songs performed by genuine reggae artists on American radio, despite the genre's growing cultural impact. "You would have been hard pressed to find a reggae song by a true reggae artist on the radio in the United States at any point in the 70s."
**Black Radio Avoidance (70s)** : During the 1970s, black radio stations in America generally avoided playing reggae, perceiving its sound as too "primitive" when compared to the more sophisticated and elaborately produced American rhythm and blues and disco music of the era. "Black radio stayed away from reggae because it sounded too primitive when compared to American rhythm and blues."
**"Computer Reggae" Shift (1985)** : The landscape of reggae on black radio began to transform around 1985 with the advent of "computer reggae," a subgenre that incorporated sounds more akin to hip hop, thereby making it more accessible and appealing to a broader black audience. "That began to change in 1985 with what he called computer reggae, which sounded more like hip hop."
**White Fan Base Factors** : Reggae's primary American fan base in the 1970s was predominantly white, a phenomenon attributed partly to American black audiences' preference for "elaborately costumed funk and disco" (and their desire to avoid the "stigma of primitivism" linked to African roots), alongside reggae's non-threatening, laid-back rhythms and its association with marijuana use and "mellowing out." "Perhaps at least part of the answer to this can be found in an article from Music writer Roger Stephens... American blacks, Marley's most coveted audience, remained impervious to him and his message throughout the 70s."
**Disco's Impact (1977)** : The overwhelming dominance of disco in 1977, both in dance clubs and on radio, swept over the music industry "like a tidal wave" and significantly hampered reggae's ability to achieve mainstream success in the American market. "My theory on this is very simple disco disco was king not just on the dance floor, but on the radio in 1977 it came over the music industry like a title wave. And reggae did not stand much of a chance."
**Political Disengagement** : In the mid-to-late 1970s, amidst a "weary nation," many Americans may have consciously disengaged from the potent political messages embedded in Bob Marley's music, preferring to "tune it out completely" rather than confront its deeper meanings. "I also wonder if most Americans were into the politics of Marley's music. We were a weary nation in 1976, 1977, which was part of the disco fuel by the way. Some may have just tuned it out completely and didn't engage with that part of the music."
**Authenticity Dilemma (70s)** : The issue of authenticity significantly challenged reggae in the 1970s, much like it affected rock in the 1980s and folk music in the 1960s after Bob Dylan's shift to electric, as purists often dismissed popular manifestations of the genre as "watered down." "The question of authenticity plagued reggae in the 70s in a way it plagued rock in the 80s and maybe even the way it plagued folk music in the 60s, especially after Dylan went electric."
**Arrangement Limitations (70s)** : Ken Williams observed in 1975 that reggae struggled to compete with mainstream 70s rock and pop due to its lack of "mugs and the strings," referring to the synthesizers and orchestral arrangements that were integral to the popular sound of those genres. "He said in 1975 that he did not think that reggae could compete with the mugs and the strings, meaning that reggae did not have the synthesizers and orchestral arrangements that were such an integral part of rock and pop in the 70s."
**"Rock Enough" for Radio Issue** : Despite strategic marketing efforts to portray Bob Marley as a rock star, reggae, and by extension Marley's music, was arguably not considered "rock enough" for mainstream radio in the 1970s, leading to more airplay for reggae-inspired rock rather than authentic reggae tracks. "I think that even though Bob Marley was marketed as a rock star, he and by default reggae was not rock enough for the radio."
**First "True" Reggae Hit (1980)** : According to music historian Tim Breham, the first "true reggae hit single" to break through in the United States did not occur until 1980, exemplified by Blondie's successful cover of "The Tide Is High." "He argues that the first true reggae hit single in the United States did not come until 1980."
**Continuing Influence** : Even after Bob Marley's death from skin cancer in 1981, reggae's profound influence on rock and pop music persisted and continued to expand throughout the 1980s and 1990s. "Reggae's influence on rock and pop music continued to grow into the 1980s and 90s, even after Marley's death from Skin Cancer in 1981."
**Diverse Nature** : Reggae is characterized as a multifaceted genre, comprising "many styles" and "many messages," emphasizing that attempts to narrowly classify it miss its true essence and its significant, complex place in the history of rock and pop music. "Reggae is not just one thing, it is many styles, it is many messages. **Jamaica's Post-Independence Struggles** : Within five years of Jamaica gaining independence from British colonial rule in 1962, the initial celebrations gave way to hardship as the government used bulldozers to clear squatter communities in Kingston, displacing the poorest citizens under the pretext of unpaid utilities and taxes, all while rampant political corruption failed to address job creation. "Within five years, the Jamaican government would be literally taking a bulldozer to squatter communities and chasing some of the poorest of the poor out of their homes in Kingston."
**Paul Simon's Preference for Non-American Music** : In a 1972 interview, Paul Simon openly expressed his preference for musical styles that were distinct from those commonly heard in America, explicitly highlighting his appreciation for Jamaican music. "Simon told Landau that he liked many types of music more than the type you would hear in America."
**Jamaican Session Musician Exploitation** : Jamaican session musicians were notoriously exploited, typically receiving only about seven dollars per song with no royalties, an unjust system that Paul Simon attempted to mitigate slightly by misrepresenting the number of songs being recorded to provide them with more pay. "At this time the Jamaican session musicians got about seven bucks a song. It was a notorious racket that totally screwed over the musicians and kept them from getting any royalties."

"The short version is that this is a movie made in Jamaica, by Jamaicans about a musician who turns to a life of crime because he can't break through in the music industry."
**Roger Stephens' Analysis of Black Audience (70s)** : Music writer Roger Stephens observed that American blacks, Bob Marley's intended audience, remained unmoved by his music and message throughout the 1970s, preferring "elaborately costumed funk and disco" and actively seeking to avoid any reminders of their "African roots" to sidestep "the stigma of primitivism." "American blacks, Marley's most coveted audience, remained impervious to him and his message throughout the 70s. They were under the sway of a elaborately costumed funk and disco and seemed determined to avoid any reminder of their own African roots, fearful of the stigma of primitivism."

"Murray Elias, who was known as Jawfish to those who listened to his New York radio show in the 70s, which featured Jamaican music. I think it was the only show that did.
