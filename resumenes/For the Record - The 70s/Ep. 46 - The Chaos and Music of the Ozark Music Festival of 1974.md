# artist - Earl Scruggs

## instruments
**Scruggs Style Banjo** : Earl Scruggs revolutionized banjo playing in bluegrass with his unique "Scruggs style," involving picking the five-string banjo with two fingers and a thumb. Although he did not claim to invent it, this was the prevalent style from his home in North Carolina. "The Scrugg's style is picking at the five string banjo with three fingers, two fingers and a thumb actually."
## curiosities
**Boredom with Traditional Bluegrass (1960)** : By 1960, Scruggs grew bored with traditional bluegrass and desired to play more contemporary music, stating he would get "depressed" if he didn't. "By 1960 though, Scrugg's was getting a bit bored with traditional bluegrass."
**Playing for Rock Fans** : Scruggs enjoyed performing for rock fans, as evidenced by his appearance at the 1968 Miami Pop Festival, which featured artists like Janis Joplin, Joni Mitchell, Marvin Gaye, and The Grateful Dead. "He liked to play for rock fans like he did at the 1968 Miami pop festival."
**Breakup with Lester Flatt (1969)** : His desire to play more contemporary music contrasted with Lester Flatt's commitment to traditional bluegrass, leading to their breakup in 1969, which shocked fans and the music industry. "Lester Platt though wanted to stay true to bluegrass and their breakup in 1969 was a shock to their fans and to many people in the entire music industry."
**Anti-Vietnam War Protest (1969)** : In 1969, Scruggs upset many in country music and their fans by appearing at a peace rally (an anti-Vietnam war rally) in Washington, D.C. He stated that while he would not protest if he believed there was a valid reason for American troops to be there, he could not see a reason to support the war at that time. "Earl upset a lot of people in country music and their fans when he appeared at a peace rally in Washington, DC, which is also, of course, a way of saying an anti-Vietnam war rally."
**Collaboration with Nitty Gritty Dirt Band** : Scruggs admired the Nitty Gritty Dirt Band's album "Uncle Charlie and his dog Teddy," which led to their collaboration on the bluegrass triple LP "Will the Circle Be Unbroken." "Earl Scrugg's really liked the record. And this led to Scrugg's and the nitty-gritty dirt band collaborating on the Bluegrass triple LP will the circle be unbroken."

## instruments
**Hyle Talk Box** : For the song "Rocky Mountain Way," Walsh initially used a small, quiet talk box by Pete Drake in the studio, which distorted the sound to create a unique, almost robotic effect. Bob Hyle, whom Walsh met through ham radios, then created a high-powered talk box specifically for Walsh to replicate this sound live. This custom talk box, with a tube attached to the mic stand, became an "indelible sound of 70s rock" and was later notably used by Peter Frampton. "This is Joe Walsh on the Hyle talk box in Rocky Mountain Way."

The lyrics were inspired as he looked up at the Rocky Mountains while mowing his lawn in Colorado, reflecting on accepting his departure from the James Gang and appreciating his life there. A bootleg copy of his live performance at the Ozark Festival indicates it "sounded great live." "I'm writing Rocky Mountain Way."

# genre - Bluegrass
## curiosities
**Foundations and Characteristics** : Bluegrass has its roots in mountain music and is a mixture of gospel, blues, and country. It is distinctly known for its use of the banjo and fiddle, as well as the beautiful close harmonies typically sung in trios or quartets. The fiddle music of Appalachia and the Ozarks forms part of its very foundation. "Bluegrass has its roots in mountain music. Like rock it's really a mixture of many other genres too."
**1950s Decline and Late 1960s Revival** : The genre experienced a decline in popularity in the 1950s with the rise of rock music but underwent a significant revival in the late 1960s. "Bluegrass kind of faded away a bit in the 1950s when rock became popular but then it had a revival in the late 60s."

# concert - Ozark Music Festival (1974)
## curiosities
**"Three Days of Sodom and Gamora"** : A local Missouri Senate committee report, whose phrasing was verified, described the festival as "three days of Sodom and Gamora." A local newspaper echoed this sentiment. "A local Missouri Senate committee report said this was three days of Sodom and Gamora."
**Described as a Disaster** : A local newspaper unequivocally labeled the Ozark Music Festival as "a disaster." A state senate report further concluded that the scene at the festival made "the degradation of Sodom and Gomorrah appear mild," with "Natural and unnatural sex acts" becoming "a spectator sport." "The Ozark Music Festival can only be described as a disaster."
**Unpreparedness for Scale** : The festival, organized by Kansas City concert promoter Chris Fritz, was held on the weekend of July 19, 1974, in Sedalia, Missouri, at the State Fairgrounds. Fritz anticipated approximately 50,000 attendees, but estimates suggest the actual attendance soared to around 300,000, with no accurate count possible once security abandoned posts and gates became irrelevant. The town of Sedalia was profoundly unprepared for the influx. "Who could have predicted that bags of ice would go for 30 bucks and hookers who could be found in nearby trailers would advertise their services for five."
**Security Failure and Chaos** : Teenagers hired as "security" abandoned their posts to join the crowd. The festival suffered from a severe lack of basic amenities, with only five porta-potties and insufficient food, leading to reports of hungry fans butchering local livestock. Golf carts used to shuttle bands were destroyed, and some performers expressed fear about the unfolding chaos. "The golf carts that were used to shuttle the bands around were destroyed and a security guard said that many of the performers were a bit afraid of everything that was happening."
**MC's Contribution to Disorder** : Wolfman Jack, the legendary master of ceremonies, actively encouraged misbehavior at the festival, including the display of naked breasts, stating his desire to see "a lot of love." "He encouraged a lot of it including the display of naked boobs because he said he wanted to see a lot of love."
**Kent State Comparison** : The production manager for the festival recalled the 1970 Kent State shootings when the National Guard contemplated intervention, and he declined a gun offered by a local factory worker who was protecting equipment. "Now the production manager for the festival said that when the National Guard wanted to come out all he could think about was Kent State the shootings at Kent State in 1970."
**Aftermath and Bans** : Following the festival, Chris Fritz was banned from Sedalia for life, and rock bands were prohibited from performing in Sedalia for the next 15 years. Promoters faced grand jury probes and lawsuits, being compelled to pay for damages including lost livestock and destroyed fields. The state fairgrounds required extensive cleanup, with lime sprinkled on the topsoil before a bulldozer removed the entire top layer to clear trash and drug paraphernalia. "Chris Fritz, he was banned from Sedelia for life and rock bands were banned from Sedelia for the next 15 years."
**Financial Cost and Legacy** : The artists' fees for the festival amounted to approximately $190,000 in 1974. Chris Fritz estimated in 2015 that reassembling the same lineup would cost around $25 million. Despite the behavioral problems, the festival is recognized for showcasing the diverse range of rock music, particularly Southern rock. "This was a showcase of the diversity of this big tent that we call rock music and hopefully we are approaching the day maybe we've arrived at the day when it's not the drugs and misbehavior but it's the music that was showcased that is the legacy of this festival."
**Lineup** : The festival featured a lineup of 27 artists, including Aerosmith, Lynyrd Skynyrd, Nitty Gritty Dirt Band, Black Flag, Elvin Bishop, Joe Walsh, The Eagles, Marshall Tucker Band, REO Speedwagon, and Bob Seger. Bruce Springsteen and the E Street Band were scheduled but canceled due to transportation issues. "You would be hard pressed. To find a 70s music festival that had a better line up than this."

# instrument - Talk Box
## curiosities
**Early Studio Use** : Pete Drake created a small, quiet talk box that was initially used for steel guitar in the studio, designed to distort the sound into something unique and almost robotic. "Pete Drake. This small kind of quiet talk box for a new song called Rocky Mountain Way."
**Live Performance Innovation** : Joe Walsh recognized the cool sound of the talk box but struggled to replicate it live. Bob Hyle, an electronics expert who bonded with Walsh over ham radios, created a high-powered talk box specifically for live performances of "Rocky Mountain Way," overcoming the limitations of existing equipment and helping to establish an "indelible sound of 70s rock" that Peter Frampton also utilized. "Hyle created a high powered talk box just for Rocky Mountain Way."
