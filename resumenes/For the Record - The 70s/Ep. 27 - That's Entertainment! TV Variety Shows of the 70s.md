## albums
**Odessa (1969)** : This was the band's sixth album, released in March 1969, but it was not well received at the time. ## curiosities
**Early Television Appearances (1969-1970s)** : Despite their later iconic status in the 70s, the Bee Gees had relatively few TV appearances during the decade. They were featured on an episode of Season One of "Laugh-In" in 1969 and also made appearances on "The Tonight Show with Johnny Carson" and twice on "The Midnight Special." "their TV appearances were few and far between."
**Saturday Night Fever Soundtrack and Disco Embrace (1977)** : The band's fortunes dramatically shifted towards the end of the 1970s following their "reluctant embrace of disco" and the immense success of the "Saturday Night Fever" soundtrack in 1977. This period led to 14 top 10 hits, 9 number one singles, and 20 entries in the Hot 100, solidifying their status as defining artists of the decade. "their reluctant embrace of disco and the Saturday Night Fever soundtrack in 1977."

"Does not even count the songs that they wrote and didn't perform on like if I can't have you by Ivan Elliman or Shadow Dancing by their brother, Andy Gibb."

This special, featuring footage from their "Spirits Having Flown" tour concert in Oakland that summer, represented a bridge between traditional variety shows and the emerging music video era. "they closed out the 70s, not with a variety show, but their own 90 minute concert special on Primetime, on NBC In the final weeks of the 70s, November 21, 1979."
**HBO Documentary: How Can You Mend a Broken Heart (2020)** : HBO released a new documentary titled "The Bee Gees, How Can You Mend a Broken Heart," directed by Frank Marshall. The film chronicles the highs and lows of brothers Barry, Maurice, and Robin Gibb, exploring the evolution of their prolific career, their navigation of the ever-changing music industry, and the complex dynamics of family and fame through interviews and never-before-seen footage. "HBO's new documentary The Bee Gees, how can you mend a broken heart? # label - CBS

## curiosities
**The Rural Purge (1970-1971)** : Under the leadership of Fred Silverman, who took over CBS in 1970-1971, the network underwent a significant programming shift known as the "rural purge." This initiative systematically canceled any show perceived as "remotely country," regardless of its ratings, including popular programs like The Johnny Cash Show, Petticoat Junction, The Beverly Hillbillies, The Red Skelton Show, Green Acres, and even The Ed Sullivan Show. The move was replaced by "urban friendly programming" such as All in the Family, MASH, and The Mary Tyler Moore Show, driven by a desire to target more heavily populated areas for higher ratings. "he gutted the network of any show that seemed remotely country. It did not matter what the ratings were."
**Capable of Inspired Variety Show Production** : CBS was noted by critic John Leonard for its unique capability to produce variety show "production numbers" with an "inspired comic touch," particularly when programming for audiences "over the mental age of eight." This quality was observed in shows like The Smothers Brothers Show, The Carol Burnett Show, and The Sonny & Cher Show. "of which only CBS seems capable whenever CBS decides reluctantly to program for people over the mental age of eight."

**Ed Sullivan Show's Enduring Legacy (1948-1971)** : "The Ed Sullivan Show," broadcast on CBS from 1948 to 1971, was an incredibly long-running variety show that essentially brought vaudeville to television. It famously featured an unparalleled array of entertainers, from opera singers to country artists, tap dancers, magicians, and pivotal performances by rock and roll legends like Elvis, The Beatles, and The Rolling Stones, as well as The Supremes, BB King, and Gladys Knight & The Pips. Despite lacking a "cool factor," its star power was unmatched. "Sullivan show was on CBS from 1948 to 1971, which is an incredible run for a variety show. It was a Vodville act on TV essentially."
**Justice Department's Prime Time Rule Change (1971)** : In 1970, the Justice Department reduced the number of prime-time viewing hours from three to two and a half, a measure that went into effect in 1971. This change aimed to decentralize network power and provide local and independent television stations with more opportunities to air their own programs. "First, the Justice Department reduced the number of prime time viewing hours from two three from three to half to try and loosen up some of the power that the networks had and give local and independent TV a shot at getting programs on the air. That went into effect in 1971."
**Motivation for CBS's Rural Purge (Early 1970s)** : Fred Silverman's decision to implement the "rural purge" at CBS in the early 1970s was partly motivated by a strategic desire to target demographics in more heavily populated urban areas. He believed that by replacing rural-themed programming with "urban friendly programming," the network could boost ratings and better fulfill its potential. "One argument is that Silverman was going after the more heavily populated areas because he wanted the ratings. He didn't think that the network was living up to its potential."
**Negative Mood in Early 1970s America** : Robert Folsom, in a 2013 analysis for The Socionomist, suggested that the programming shifts at CBS, including the rural purge, were symptomatic of a pervasive "negative mood" in the United States during the early 1970s. This societal weariness was largely attributed to the ongoing Vietnam War and the recent assassinations of prominent figures like Martin Luther King, Robert Kennedy, and Malcolm X. "Much of that negativity was brought on by the Vietnam War and the recent assassinations of Martin Luther King and Robert Kennedy and Malcolm X."
**Shifting Television Content (1970s)** : The 1970s presented a paradox in television content: variety shows increasingly embraced "sweet and family friendly" themes, while popular sitcoms like "All in the Family," "Good Times," and "Chico and the Man" adopted a "harder edged" approach that was sometimes not considered entirely family-friendly. "What is odd about the evolution of variety shows that will fill up our imaginary shelf is that they became more sweet and family friendly. While the sitcoms got a little harder edged."
**The Midnight Special (Off Air 1981)** : The music program "The Midnight Special" went off the air in 1981, indicating a shift in television's approach to music programming away from traditional performance shows and towards the emerging format of music videos. "The midnight special went off the air in 1981."
**"The Partridge Family" (1970-1974)** : This sitcom, which aired from 1970 to 1974, centered on a fictional singing family and their mother. It starred real-life heartthrob David Cassidy, contributing to the abundance of music available on television in the 1970s. "Then there were sitcoms like The Partridge family about the singing Partridge siblings and their mom. The star of that show was the real life heartthrob David Cassidy."
**The Wrecking Crew's Signature Sound** : The famed Wrecking Crew Studio Musicians were known for their ability to bring a "classic LA sound" to a variety of recordings. Their distinctive contribution was evident when they backed Vicki Lawrence on her 1972 hit, "The Night the Lights Went Out in Georgia," which, despite having country elements, achieved success on the pop charts. "Again, backed by the Reckon Crew who brought that classic LA sound to this kind of sort of country song."
**FAME Studios, Muscle Shoals, Alabama - A Legendary Hub** : FAME Studios in Muscle Shoals, Alabama, is renowned as a legendary recording facility where numerous iconic artists, including Aretha Franklin, Wilson Pickett, The Rolling Stones, Etta James, and even The Osmonds, recorded significant works. Its diverse roster cemented its place in music history. "this is what the studio where Aretha Franklin and Wilson pick it And the Rolling Stones and edit James and acts like that recorded."
**Donnie Osmond's View on Modern Variety Shows (2015)** : In 2015, Donnie Osmond asserted that a musical comedy variety show like "The Donnie & Marie Show" could no longer succeed today. He attributed this to a change in audience preferences, which now prioritize "reality" and "shock" over the "simplicity and innocence" that his show embodied. "nobody, it's not possible because the audience got to the point where the desire is on reality And the desire is on shock, and the Donnie and Marie show was built on simplicity and innocence And we just don't have that anymore."
**Evolution of Music on TV: From Variety to MTV (1970s-1980s)** : The landscape of music on television underwent a significant transformation from the 1970s to the 1980s. While the 70s saw artists earning TV shows through record sales, the 80s were defined by MTV, which propelled artists to stardom and encouraged them to create music specifically with accompanying videos in mind, as demonstrated by figures like Michael Jackson and Madonna. "Before the 1980s, you sold some records and you were given a TV show In the 1980s, MTV made you that star and performers like Michael Jackson and Madonna created music with the video in mind."
**Historical Importance of TV Variety Shows (Telli Davidson)** : According to author Telli Davidson, television variety shows deserve a "revered place" in 20th-century entertainment history. They were instrumental in introducing artists like Elvis and The Beatles to mass audiences and kept legends such as Ella Fitzgerald, Duke Ellington, and Gene Kelly in the public eye. Davidson views shows from the late 1960s and early 1970s as valuable "time capsules" that reflect the politics, fashion, music, TV, sports, and film of their era, offering insights into societal tastes. "they should have a revered place in 20th century entertainment history It is how most people found out about Elvis and the Beatles."
