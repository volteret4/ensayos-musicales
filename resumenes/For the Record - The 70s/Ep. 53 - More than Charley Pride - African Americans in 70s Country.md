## curiosities
**Early Life and Name Change (1961)** : Born Thelma in South Carolina in 1961, her stage name "Linda" was suggested by a DJ. She began singing at age six and developed roots in singing, hearing country music on the radio alongside her R&B foundations. "although her given name was Thelma, a DJ said she should be Linda, so Linda it was."
**Discovery by William Duke Rayner (1969)** : In 1969, William Duke Rayner, a furniture store owner from Nashville with aspirations in music, discovered Martell. He heard her covering country songs at an Air Force Base in South Carolina and was impressed by the audience's reaction, convincing her to let him fund a demo and become her manager. "He liked the reaction that he heard from the audience when Linda was singing at a Air Force Base in South Carolina."
**Rapid Career Launch** : Within three days of meeting William Duke Rayner, Linda Martell secured a manager, signed a contract with Plantation Records, and recorded her debut album, "Color Me Country." This rapid progression marked a swift entry into the country music scene. "In a matter of three days, three days, she had a manager, she signed a contract with Shelby Singleton's Plantation Records... and cut her first album, Color Me Country."
**Grand Ole Opry Debut (1969)** : Linda Martell was the first solo female black artist to perform at the Grand Ole Opry, playing there a total of 12 times. During her performances at the Ryman Auditorium, she would have seen the words "Confederate gallery" hanging in the balcony, a sign that remained until its removal in 2017. "She was the first solo female black artist to do so and in fact she would play there 12 times in total."
**Views on Black Artists and Country Music (1972)** : In a 1972 interview with the Meican News, Linda Martell discussed the scarcity of African American country artists. She suggested that black artists often dismiss country music without listening to it, thereby missing potential career opportunities. "It's not that the Negro artists aren't interested in country music. They don't know if they're interested or not because they won't listen to it."
**Experience with Racism and Advice from Charlie Pride** : Initially, Martell reported minimal racist heckling, primarily from "a couple of drunkards" at a Pennsylvania show. However, "we learned later that there were other incidents." She sought advice from Charlie Pride, who told her to develop a "thick skin" and get used to the racism. Her manager, Shelby Singleton, advised her against addressing the audience directly during heckling. "He told her to develop a thick skin and get used to it."
**Lawsuit and Career Stalling (1970)** : In May 1970, manager William Duke Rayner filed a lawsuit against Martell, claiming he was owed royalties and asserting she "does not possess business ability" to handle money, suggesting a court should sequester 25% of her earnings. Although Shelby Singleton reportedly made the lawsuit "go away," he then informed Martell he would prioritize promoting white artist Jeannie C. Riley. Singleton subsequently released Martell from her contract but threatened to sue any future label she signed with, effectively blacklisting her. "Singleton let Martell out of her contract, but then threatened to sue the next label she signed with."
**Alice Randall's Theory on Visibility** : Alice Randall, author of "My Black Country," theorized in 2009 that Martell's career may have been hindered by her visibility. Randall questioned whether it would have been more beneficial for Martell's career if her voice had been heard without her race being immediately apparent, suggesting that some performances might have been canceled once it was revealed she was not white. "Would it have been better for her career just to let her voice be heard without people thinking that is a black woman singing the song?"
**Hee Haw Appearance (1970)** : Linda Martell appeared on "Hee Haw," a popular country music TV variety show, in 1970. She demonstrated her country vocal style, including yodeling, on the program. "Here is Linda sounding very country on he-ha in 1970."

## songs
**Just Between You and Me (1967)** : This song served as Charlie Pride's breakthrough. It led to his Grammy nomination in 1968 and was the song he performed for his television debut on "The Lawrence Welk Show" in 1967. "Charlie Pride's breakthrough song, just between you and me, got him a Grammy nomination in 1968."
**Kiss An Angel Good Morning (1971)** : Released in 1971, this was Charlie Pride's significant pop crossover hit, bringing him to the attention of non-country fans. Written by Ben Peters, the song features Pride's baritone voice, which had "no hint at all of blues" unlike Ray Charles, embodying the country-pop sound popular on AM radio in the 1970s. "That includes Kiss and Angel Good Morning, the pop crossover hit that put him on the radar of people who were not country fans."

## curiosities
**Comparison to Linda Martell** : Charlie Pride was frequently used as the frame of reference for any African American country singer, including Linda Martell, who was often referred to as "the female Charlie Pride." Unlike Martell, displaying his face on album covers eventually became a non-issue, and he made numerous television appearances. "Linda fits the pride pattern. Typical, as she was frequently referred to as the female Charlie Pride."
**Country Music Association Awards (1971, 1972)** : Charlie Pride won the Country Music Association Male Entertainer of the Year award in both 1971 and 1972, marking significant recognition in the country music industry. "He won the Country Music Association Male Entertainer of the Year award in 1971 and 1972."
**Major Label Exclusivity (1967-1991)** : Charlie Pride was the only African-American country singer signed to a major record label, RCA, between 1967 and 1991. This highlights his unique position and the industry's limited diversity during that period. "he was the only African-American country singer that was signed to a major record label between 1967 and 1991. That was RCA."
**Early Life and Baseball Career** : Born into poverty in Mississippi, Charlie Pride initially pursued a career in baseball, playing in the Negro leagues. He was paid extra to sing before games to boost attendance, but his baseball career was cut short by an elbow injury. "he did play in the Negro leagues and he was paid extra to sing before games to encourage attendance, but that career, the baseball career, was derailed by an elbow injury."
**Discovery in Montana** : Pride was discovered singing in country clubs in Montana, a state that had the fewest black people in the union at the time. He had lived there for a decade before being encouraged to record a demo. "He was discovered when he was singing in country clubs in Montana of all places."
**RCA Contract and Cautious Approach (1965)** : Encouraged by Chet Atkins, a legendary country music producer at RCA, Pride made a demo, leading Atkins to offer him a recording contract in 1965. RCA, however, adopted a cautious approach, initially booking him for shows in different towns to "temperature check" audience reactions before scheduling further performances. "Atkins liked what he heard from Pride and offered him recording contract in 1965."
**Manager Jack Clement** : Charlie Pride's manager was Jack Clement, known for his unconventional and innovative approach in the music industry. "His manager was Jack Clement, who was known for thinking outside the box."
**Touring Demands** : Pride faced extensive touring demands early in his career, often spending long periods away from home. He once recounted being away for three months straight, longer than his military service. "I was away from home one time for three months and never got back home."
**Initial Obscuring of Race by RCA** : Early in his career, RCA avoided using Charlie Pride's photo on promotional materials. While his voice created demand, many listeners were unaware he was African American. "up to this point, RCA was not using Pride's photo on anything."
**Addressing His Race in Detroit (1966)** : At a show in Detroit in 1966, Pride confronted an audience that greeted him with silence upon realizing he was African American. He introduced himself, acknowledged his "permanent suntan," stated his Mississippi farming background, and declared his intention to entertain them. "friends, I realize it's a little unique me coming out here with a permanent suntan to sing country and western to you. But that's the way it is."
**Television Debut (1967)** : Charlie Pride made his television debut in 1967 on "The Lawrence Welk Show," where he performed his breakthrough song, "Just Between You and Me." "In 1967, he made his television debut on the Lawrence Welk show singing just between you and me."
**Grand Ole Opry Membership (1967)** : In 1967, Charlie Pride became the second African American to be granted membership to the Grand Ole Opry, and the first since harmonica player DeFord Bailey, who last played at the Opry in 1941. This marked a significant milestone for racial integration in country music's premier institution. "Charlie Pride became the second African American ever to be allowed to be a member of the Grand Ole Opera in the first since the harmonica player to Ford Bailey who had last played at the Opera in 1941."
**Extensive Chart Success** : Between 1969 and 1983, Charlie Pride recorded an impressive 24 number one country songs, establishing him as one of country music's most successful artists. "He went on to record 24 number one country songs between 1969 and 1983."
**Career Focus on Country** : Despite the pop crossover success of "Kiss an Angel Good Morning," Charlie Pride's other hits primarily charted on the country charts. He was content with this, viewing himself primarily as a country artist not seeking a pop career. "His other hits were on the country charts, which I think he was just fine with because he was a country artist who was not looking for a pop career."
**View on Country Music Diversity (1973)** : In 1973, Charlie Pride articulated a broad perspective on country music, arguing it is "many things" and the "basic music of America," encompassing elements of jazz, blues, and other categories. He believed that various genres were starting to "gel" into "one ball of wax." "Country music is just about time for it to stop being used as a whipping boy. It's good music. It's the basic music of America."
**Experience with Racism (Personal Account)** : Charlie Pride stated that he never experienced anyone yelling a slur at him or telling him to leave the stage. He attributed this to his belief in his identity as "Charlie Pride, the American singing musician," suggesting people might accept you as you believe yourself to be. "Pride said that there was never a time when anyone yelled to slur at him and shouted at him and told him to get off the stage."
**Inspiration for "Nashville" Character** : The character of Tommy Brown in Robert Altman's 1975 film "Nashville" was widely believed to be based on Charlie Pride due to the actor's resemblance in hairstyle and dress, and Pride's unique status as a prominent black country singer. Pride, however, denied any connection, stating that if it was meant to be him, it was "a lie." "The film director, Robert Altman, made a movie about the country music industry which came out in 1975 and he included a character that many people believed was supposed to be Charlie Pride."
**"You Look Like Them But Sound Like Us"** : Charlie Pride was told, "you look like them but sound like us," a comment highlighting his ability to appeal across racial lines in the country music genre. "Pride was told you look like them but sound like us."

## curiosities
**Career Shift to Country (after Charlie Pride)** : Originally from Mississippi, O.B. McClinton began his career as an R&B singer but struggled to gain traction and felt no real connection to the genre. Inspired by Charlie Pride, he transitioned to country music, finding "inner satisfaction" and a vocal style that better suited him. "he started as an R&B singer but he wasn't really able to break through there and didn't really feel any connection to it anyway. So he decided to give country a try after he heard Charlie Pride."
**Discomfort with R&B** : O.B. McClinton expressed that he did not feel authentic singing R&B, noting that his voice didn't blend well with the genre. In 1973, he articulated a sense of liberation in pursuing country music, stating he "turned the world out" to find satisfaction in it. "If you tried to listen to me sing rhythm and blues you wouldn't think it was the same person. My voice just didn't blend with that. It didn't sound good."
**Regina Taylor's "Star Category" (1975)** : In a 1975 article in the Terahot Tribune, Regina Taylor identified O.B. McClinton as one of three African Americans deserving of "star category" status in country music, alongside Charlie Pride and Stoney Edwards. "Obi-Miklinton was the third."
**Perspective on Race and Country Music** : When asked about his race and why he entered country music, O.B. McClinton deflected the question, likening it to asking "why am I black?" He believed that stereotypes, such as the "bibbed overalls" image, prevented more African Americans from engaging with country music as fans or performers. "He said I say don't ask me. I don't know. It's like saying hey man why am I black?"
**"The Chocolate Cowboy" Persona** : By the 1980s, O.B. McClinton was known as "the chocolate cowboy," an upbeat entertainer who often took requests and performed impressions of other singers in his shows. "He was a very upbeat entertainer by all accounts who by the 1980s called himself the chocolate cowboy."
**Stage Impressions (1984)** : In 1984, O.B. McClinton described his live shows, which included impressions of various country singers. He deliberately performed Charlie Pride impressions to highlight their differences, followed by Dolly Parton impressions to avoid being seen as a carbon copy of Pride. He also illustrated the contrast between country and R&B by singing the same song in the style of Johnny Cash and then Muddy Waters. "I do Charlie Pride just to make sure they hear that I'm different than I go right into an impression of Deli Parton just so they wouldn't say that I'm a carbon copy of her either."
**Death (1987)** : O.B. McClinton died of cancer in the mid-1980s at the age of 47 in 1987, cutting short a promising career. "McClinton got cancer in the mid 80s and he died far too young at the age of 47 in 1987."

**Fairy Tale (1974)** : Written by sisters Anita and Bonnie Pointer, "Fairy Tale" was inspired by Anita's heartbreak over a married DJ. Released as a single in the summer of 1974, it reached number 13 on the Billboard Hot Country Chart and also received Top 40 radio airplay. "Fairy Tale" earned the Pointer Sisters a Grammy Award for Best Country Performance by a Group or Duo in 1975, making them the only African American women to win a country Grammy until Beyoncé in 2024. "Made it to number 13. And so it was popular enough on the country charts that it got some airplay on top 40 radio too."

## curiosities
**Arkansas Roots and Country Upbringing** : The Pointer Sisters, hailing from Arkansas, grew up immersed in country music. They listened to it at home, on the radio, and in church, considering themselves "country people." "We were country people. Anita Pointer said."
**Inspiration for Country Music Entry** : Their venture into country music was sparked by heartbreak. Anita Pointer's relationship with a DJ ended when she discovered he was married, inspiring her and sister Bonnie to write "Fairy Tale" in the country genre. "Anita was dating a DJ and he neglected to tell her that he was married. Thus, their love that she thought they had was a fairy tale."
**Grand Ole Opry Performance (1974)** : The Pointer Sisters were invited to perform at the Grand Ole Opry in 1974, four years after Linda Martell's debut, following the success of their hit "Fairy Tale." "Four years after Linda Martell's appearance at the Grand Ole Opry, the pointer sisters were invited to play there."
**Experience with Racism in Nashville** : Upon arriving in Nashville for an event, the Pointer Sisters were mistakenly taken to the back of the venue, with staff assuming they were there to work in the kitchen. They were not there for work but to perform. "The staff thought that they were there to work the kitchen. Nope. They were not. They were there to sing this."
**Band's Refusal to Play Country** : The Pointer Sisters' own band refused to record "Fairy Tale," with their drummer stating, "I didn't join this group to play country music." This led the sisters to record the song with Nashville session musicians. "Their drummer got up and left and said I didn't join this group to play country music."
**Grammy Award for Country Performance (1975)** : The Pointer Sisters won a Grammy Award for Best Country Performance by a Group or Duo in 1975 for their song "Fairy Tale." They held the distinction of being the only African American women to win a country Grammy for 50 years until Beyoncé's win in 2024. "Fairy tale earned the pointer sisters a Grammy Award... for best country performance by a group or duo in 1975."

# genre - Country

## curiosities
**Origins and Influences** : The roots of country music are a blend of black and white influences. What was once known as Hillbilly music during the World War I era saw black and white musicians playing together and influencing each other. The banjo is an African instrument, and songs like "Will the Circle Be Unbroken" originated as African American spirituals, demonstrating the genre's diverse heritage. "The roots of country music are black and white."
**"White Man's Soul Music" (1991)** : Chris Christofferson described country music in 1991 as "white man's soul music," focusing on themes of drinking, cheating, and sex as subjects not typically found in pop music. This perspective, however, is considered problematic for implicitly excluding black performers and fans. "country is white man's soul music."
**Music Industry's Role in Racial Perception** : For about 100 years, the music industry has promoted the belief that country music is "conservative, red state, white music." This marketing strategy, driven by a desire for profit after World War I, led to the segregation of music and the promotion of white faces and voices as "real country music," effectively ingraining the idea that country music was exclusively white. "the music industry has been selling for about 100 years."
**Segregated Billboard Charts** : The racial division in the United States extended to the music industry, where even Billboard's music charts were segregated. This segregation reinforced the perception that country music was for white audiences and other genres for black audiences. "even billboards music charts were segregated."
**Industry Control and Profitability Motives** : Historically, the music industry in the United States, particularly concerning country music, has been controlled by predominantly white male executives. These executives believed it was more profitable to highlight white country western artists, which contributed to the genre being largely perceived as "mostly by and for white people" into the 1980s and beyond. "the music industry throughout the history of the United States was controlled by white executives, mostly white male executives, who believed it to be more profitable to showcase white country western artists."
