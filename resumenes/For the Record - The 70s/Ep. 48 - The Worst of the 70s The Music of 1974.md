## songs
**Seasons in the Sun (1974)** : This song was ranked #5 on a 2006 CNN poll of the worst songs of all time. According to Tom Harrison, music critic for The Province (Vancouver newspaper), in June 1983, with 11 million records sold, it "may be the fifth best selling single of all time." Tom Bryan of StereoGum described it as "sentimental mush." Terry Jacks' paper boy loved the song, even when Jacks' friends thought it "sucks," and the paper boy returned with friends to hear it again, which Jacks credited with launching its pop music history success. An uncredited review of the album in the Hamilton Ohio Journal News (March 10, 1974) attributed the single's success not to the performance, but to "the McEwen magical touch of schlock poetry and a hummelbo melody." The song went to number one on March 2, 1974, and sold 11 million copies. Rolling Stone magazine's "worst song of the 70s" poll ranked it at #7. Jacks explicitly stated that he liked Top 40 music, and to make the original composition a hit, he added a beat and tried to make the arrangement as interesting as possible. The lyrics were originally written by Belgian songwriter Jacques Brel, then adapted into English by Rod McKuen. Jacks heard Brel's original (about a man heartbroken over his wife's cheating) and then McKuen's version, making tweaks to it. He opted to remove the lyrics about the cheating wife, which McKuen had kept, and instead wrote about a friend who died of leukemia.
"with 11 million records sold, seasons in the sun may be the fifth best selling single of all time, but just try to find someone in Terry's hometown who'll admit to liking it."

## curiosities
**Beach Boys Collaboration Attempt** : Terry Jacks made some tweaks to "Seasons in the Sun" with the intention of giving it to The Beach Boys, who had asked him to write a song for them. However, "things got weird" with The Beach Boys, as Jacks believed Brian Wilson was attempting to steal the song. The process became too protracted, leading Jacks to ultimately pull the plug on the collaboration.
"Then Jacques thought that Brian Wilson was trying to steal the song and the whole process was getting dragged out way too long and so then Jacques just pulled the plug on that."

**Having My Baby (1974) - Controversial #1 Hit** : This song was ranked #1 on a 2006 CNN poll of the worst songs of all time, winning "in a landslide." It reached number one on the Billboard Hot 100 in the summer of 1974. The song immediately faced significant backlash, particularly from the women's liberation movement, which criticized lyrics implying male ownership ("my baby" instead of "our baby") and presenting childbearing as a demonstration of a woman's love. Paul Anka, who wrote the song when his first wife Ann was pregnant, responded to the criticism in a San Francisco Examiner interview, stating, "To hell with what they think there are people out there who like to go to bed and have babies." Peter Riley, writing for Stereo Review in 1974, called it "the worst," criticizing Anka's "grunts out the unforgettable lyrics" and "Elvis-like roar," yet admitted he found himself "vacantly humming it" exactly as he did with "Diana" years prior, suggesting Anka possessed a "powerful natural gift of communication." Odia Coates sang along with Anka on the track. As an adult, the song appears to contain a subtle message regarding abortion, acknowledging it as a legal option with lyrics such as "you didn't have to do it wouldn't put you through it I swept it from your life but you wouldn't do it." The song also topped "more than one... worst of the 1970s poll and maybe the worst of all time."

# artist - Bo Donaldson and the Haywoods

## members

## songs
**Billy Don't Be a Hero (1974)** : This 1974 song received votes in the 2006 CNN poll for worst songs and "consistently finds itself on the worst of lists." Maybe Green of Rolling Stone magazine associated it with "insipid 70s dribble," while Tom Bryan called it a "cheerily tragic, deeply dumb death song." Bo Donaldson himself initially did not want to record it, but his mother, who also managed the band, believed it would be a hit, a prediction that proved correct. The song tells the story of a young man named Billy who volunteers for a dangerous wartime mission, disregarding his fiancée's pleas not to be a hero, and subsequently dies. His fiancée learns of his death through a letter, presumably from the army, and discards it. Donaldson attributed the song's success to its narrative and catchy melody, alongside a "Vietnam element," even though no specific war is mentioned in the lyrics, and the song was purportedly about the Civil War. It became a number one hit in June 1974. Mike Gibbons, who sang lead on the track, later expressed that he felt the band had fallen into the "teen bubble gum machine" after being asked to cut their hair and shave to emulate The Osmonds' look.
"Bo Donaldson and the Haywoods Billy don't be a hero all released in 1974."

## curiosities
**Band Name Origin** : The band intentionally named themselves "The Haywoods" because they thought it sounded "kind of British," believing this would enhance their popularity. The band consisted of seven members.
"It was a seven man band that named itself the Haywoods because they thought that it sounded kind of British and it would help their popularity."
**Early Discovery and Teen Market Targeting** : Dick Clark's team discovered the band in Cincinnati almost a decade before their breakthrough. They opened for The Osmonds, gaining experience with screaming young audiences. The band later appeared multiple times on Clark's show "Action" (1973-1974), which was filmed on a Malibu beach and marketed the band as "fodder for Teeny Boppers" (today referred to as tweens).
"Dick Clark's people discovered the band in Cincinnati almost 10 years before they broke through."
**Bo Donaldson's 1974 Popularity** : Bo Donaldson was 20 years old when "Billy Don't Be a Hero" reached number one in June 1974. His handsome appearance attracted a significant crowd at a record signing event held at Montgomery Ward's in Fond du Lac, Wisconsin.
"Donaldson was 20 when the song went to number one in June 1974. He was a rather handsome young man and attracted quite the crowd at Montgomery Ward's in Fondelok, Wisconsin where he appeared at a record signing event."
**Stuck with Teen Image** : The band found it impossible to shed their "Teeny Bopper image" or distance themselves from the pervasive success of "Billy Don't Be a Hero," despite lead singer Mike Gibbons' belief that the band had more to offer musically.
"They were never really able to get out from under that whole Teeny Bopper image and from that song really."
**Band's Decline and Breakup** : Just a few years after achieving their biggest hit, the band was dropped by their record label. Although they subsequently signed with another label, Bo Donaldson and the Haywoods ultimately broke up in 1980.
"it was really just a couple of years later that their label dropped them. And even though then they were signed by a different one after that the band broke up in 1980."

# artist - Harry Chapin

## genres
Folk rock
Storytelling

## songs
**Cats in the Cradle (1974)** : This song reached number one on the charts in December 1974 and was inducted into the Grammy Hall of Fame in 2011. Tom Bryan critically described it as a "four-minute musical guilt trip" that oversimplifies the complexities of parenthood to the sole question of a father's physical or mental presence. He further characterized its tear-jerking coda, depicting an adult child having no time for his father, as "pretty dickish," arguing the father shouldn't demand his son's time when the son has a new job and a sick child. The song was inspired by a poem written by Chapin's wife, Sandra, combined with the real-life distant relationship between her ex-husband and his father. Sandra, in turn, was inspired by a country music song she heard on a long drive, which told the story of an old couple reminiscing about their grown children and grandchildren, although she could not recall the song's title or performer.
"Cats in the cradle, number one, it went to number one in December 1974"

## curiosities
**Rock Critic Disdain** : Harry Chapin reflected in 1978 that "the cool school of rock critics" consistently disliked his music, often making it clear that liking his songs was "not cool." He felt they would "get vicious" when discussing his work, criticizing his lyrics or storytelling, while at the same time meticulously analyzing single words in other artists' songs.
"The cool school of rock critics have never liked me. They make sure they tell everyone how it's not cool to like Harry Chapin. For some reason, they either hate my lyrics or storytelling or what I believe in or something. God, they can really get vicious when it comes to me."
**Song Inspiration from Family Life** : "Cats in the Cradle" was inspired by a poem penned by Harry Chapin's wife, Sandra, which was also influenced by the distant relationship between her ex-husband and his father. Sandra's poem itself stemmed from a country music song she heard during a long drive, describing an old couple's reflections on their children and grandchildren, though she could not recall the specific song or artist.
"Cats in the cradle was inspired by a poem that was written by his wife Sandra. It was that plus this real-life, distant relationship between her ex-husband and his father."

## curiosities
**Simon Frith's "Public Use" Theory** : Simon Frith, a former professor of music studies and a rock critic, proposed a theory stating that pop music is for "public use." This implies that the primary objective of pop music is to generate profit, which artists achieve by delivering what the audience already desires and expects, rather than challenging musical boundaries. This theory can help explain why pop songs frequently appear on "worst of" lists.
"Simon Frith, a former professor of music studies and a rock critic who stated that pop music is for public use. That means in part that the purpose of pop music is to make a profit. You do that as a pop music artist by giving people what they want, what they already know they want and not by pushing boundaries."

**US Cultural and Political Climate (1974)** : The United States in 1974 was described as a "weary nation." Most American troops had been withdrawn from Vietnam by 1973, though the fall of Saigon was still a year away. The Watergate scandal dominated headlines, leading to President Richard Nixon's resignation, the first and only time a US president has done so. The country also grappled with an energy crisis, long gas lines, and an unstable economy. This pervasive weariness contributed to a collective craving for nostalgia and a perception of "simpler times," as evidenced by the debut of the TV show "Happy Days" in 1974.

He explains that individuals have a physiological response to sound. Culturally, Gasser introduced the term "IntraCulture" to describe subcultures of affinity that exist within a larger culture, explaining how personal preferences for music subgenres are influenced by one's upbringing and the musical environments they are exposed to.
"Gasser says that there is science behind liking what we like. We have a physiological response to sound. It's not just science though. Culture plays a role too. Gasser said in a 2019 interview, I actually used the term IntraCulture to describe cultures that take place within a culture."
