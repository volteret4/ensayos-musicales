# artist - The Archies

## genres
Bubblegum
Pop

## labels
Kirshner Records (implied, created by Don Kirshner)

## members
Tony Dante (all vocals)

## songs
**Sugar Sugar (1969)** : This song, co-written by Jeff Barry and Andy Kim, was rejected by The Monkees, leading Don Kirshner to create The Archies as a fictional band to record it. Tony Dante sang all vocal parts, with his voice multi-tracked to create the illusion of a full band. Released around Memorial Day 1969, it initially struggled for radio play until Kirshner convinced a DJ in Texas to spin it, leading to a surge of requests. The song rapidly climbed the Billboard Hot 100, reaching number 41 in early August, then 24, 14, and finally number 3 by the end of August 1969, where it was behind Johnny Cash's "A Boy Named Sue" and The Rolling Stones' "Honky Tonk Women." "Sugar Sugar" then unseated "Honky Tonk Women" from the number one spot and remained there for a month, famously preventing Creedence Clearwater Revival's "Green River" from reaching number one. This track is described as having a good hook, simple lyrics, and a syncopated rhythm, and is noted for molding the taste of a generation of preteens. "Oh sugar sugar honey you are my candy girl I can't stop wanting you."

## curiosities
**Origins as a Fictional Band** : The Archies were a cartoon band, created by Don Kirshner specifically to record "Sugar Sugar" after The Monkees refused the song. The band was based on the Archie comics and had its own Saturday morning cartoon, ensuring they "could not say no" to Kirshner. "And that is partially how the Archies were born. A cartoon band based on the Archie comics that had its own Saturday morning cartoon could not say no to Don Kirschner when presented with sugar sugar."
**Impact on Public Taste** : The Archies, through their continuous presence on TV every Saturday morning from 1968 to 1978, profoundly influenced the musical tastes of a whole generation of preteens, teaching them to presume instant gratification as their birthright. "The Archies molded the taste of a whole generation of preteens before their runny little brains had a chance to gel."

# artist - Andy Kim

## member of

## songs
**Sugar Sugar (1969)** : Andy Kim co-wrote this song with Jeff Barry, which became a number one hit for The Archies after The Monkees rejected it. "Kirschner had a song though that was written by Jeff Barry and Andy Kim that he was sure would be a hit."
**Baby I Love You (1969)** : This song by Andy Kim was on the Billboard Hot 100 in late August 1969, reaching at least number 14, just one spot behind his co-written hit "Sugar Sugar" by The Archies. "And one spot behind Andy Kim's baby I love you. Yes, the same Andy Kim who co-wrote sugar sugar how weird is that."
**Rock Me Gently (1974)** : Andy Kim considered this to be the best song of his life. Despite his strong belief in the song, it took a year and a half to get radio play, requiring him to self-finance its release and create his own record label, Ice Records, as he only had enough money for one song (Side B of the 45 was an instrumental of the track). His perseverance paid off when it became a number one hit. Released in June 1974, it remained on the Hot 100 throughout the summer before finally reaching number one. Kim intentionally avoided a guitar or drum solo, opting instead for a "Stevie Wonderish" keyboard solo near the end of the song, performed by his keyboard player. "His perseverance paid off because he not only had recorded the best song of his life he had a number one hit."

## curiosities
**Appreciation for Pop Songwriting** : Andy Kim deeply loved and appreciated his pop songs, including "Sugar Sugar" and "Rock Me Gently," embracing his role as a pop songwriter rather than apologizing for the "bubblegum" label. He quoted Mark Twain, saying, "the two best days of your life are the day you were born and the day you find out why," in reference to finding his purpose. "It's cool to see him love his pop songs instead of apologizing for them."
**Physical Resemblance** : Andy Kim is noted for bearing a striking resemblance to Gene Simmons. "It's cool to see Andy Kim by the way dead ringer for Gene Simmons..."

## songs
**I Think I Love You (1970)** : David Cassidy initially resisted singing this song, preferring to pursue acting or rock music. However, producer West Farrell insisted on bubblegum records. Radio stations were reluctant to play it until a station in Iowa gave it a chance, after which it quickly became a number one hit. The song reached number one on November 21, 1970, shortly after *The Partridge Family* TV show debuted, and notably knocked "I'll Be There" by The Jackson 5 off the top spot. It was David Cassidy's first and only number one hit, and it was also the last song he performed live in his youth. "I think I love you. Hit number one November 21st and by the way it knocked out I'll be there by the bubble gum adjacent Jackson five."

## curiosities
**Teen Idol Image and Ambition** : David Cassidy was a major teen idol from 1970 to 1971. At 20 years old, he played the 16-year-old character Keith Partridge. Despite his success, he wanted to be an actor and a rock singer, not a teen idol singing bubblegum songs like "I Think I Love You." "Now David wanted to be an actor but not on a show like this. And he wanted to sing but not songs like I think I love you."
**Rolling Stone Interview and Image Control (1972)** : In a 1972 interview with Robin Green for *Rolling Stone*, Cassidy was initially hostile, fearing ridicule. He discussed his image and the expectations placed upon him two years into playing Keith Partridge, admitting his resistance to cutting bubblegum records. To challenge his bubblegum image and assert his desire to be a rock star, he posed nude for an Annie Leibovitz photo shoot for the magazine's cover. "He talked about his image and what was expected of him by that time two years into playing Keith Partridge. He told the interviewer Robin Green. When I first got in the studio, I said to the producer West Farrell, I don't want to cut bubble gum records and he said, no man, we're going to cut bubble gum records."
**Drug and Alcohol Use** : In his 1972 *Rolling Stone* interview, Cassidy openly discussed his drug and alcohol use, which sadly foreshadowed his future struggle with alcoholism. This admission was part of his effort to "poke holes" in his wholesome bubblegum image. "He also talked about his drug and alcohol use in that article which was an unfortunate foreshadowing of his life as an alcoholic."
**Love for Live Performance** : Despite his initial ambivalence towards the songs that made him famous, David Cassidy found immense joy and a "high" in performing live for his fans, cherishing the connection he felt with an audience who "would rather have me sing than anybody else in the world." "He told Rolling Stone interviewer Robin Green, it's a high going out on that stage. You look around and it's all there for you. People loving you like that. My friends are there with me. I'm doing what I love to do singing and I'm singing for people who would rather have me sing than anybody else in the world."
**Later Life Struggles and Legacy** : In his final months in 2017, David Cassidy struggled with dementia and other issues, which he publicly attributed to alcoholism. Before singing "I Think I Love You" one last time at BB King's Blues Club in New York, he reflected on his legacy, expressing a desire for it to be one of bringing "light and love" into the world through his music. "Dr. Filler someone asked me recently what do you want your legacy to be. I don't know I never thought about it. I guess the fact that everything I did in my life was to bring light and love into all the world that I was able to touch because you gave that to me 100,000 times."
**Tiger Beat Magazine Cover (1970)** : David Cassidy appeared on the October 1970 cover of *Tiger Beat* magazine alongside Bobby Sherman and Donnie Osmond. "Right now I'm looking at a cover of Tiger Beat magazine from October 1970 and on the cover are David Cassidy, Bobby Sherman and Donnie Osmond."

They also disliked being considered "opening act material" in the US and having audiences only want to hear their older bubblegum hits. "Andy Scott who played guitar for sweet said in a 1978 interview that that was intentional and that there was quote no way the band was going to come in tour because of Little Willie being a hit 18 months after the fact."

## curiosities
**Lester Bangs' Definition** : Rock critic Lester Bangs famously described bubblegum music as "the basic sound of rock and roll minus the rage, fear, violence and enemy that runs from Johnny Burnett to Sid Vicious." "the basic sound of rock and roll minus the rage, fear, violence and enemy that runs from Johnny Burnett to Sid Vicious."
**Definition and Target Audience** : Bubblegum music refers both to its sound, typically uptempo dance music with uncomplicated lyrics, and the age group it is marketed to, generally 12 to 16-year-olds, with slight variations. "The term refers as much to the age group that the music is marketed to as it does to the sound which is generally uptempo dance music with uncomplicated lyrics. The age group for marketing was roughly 12 to 16."
**Critical Reception** : Bubblegum music faced significant resistance from rock critics who often published scathing reviews, primarily due to concerns about a lack of "authenticity," especially when record labels intentionally targeted preteens. They were also bothered by "fake bands" scoring mega hits. "The fact though that an artist or a record label would intentionally seek the dollar of preteens who are presumed to have bad taste in music is exactly what turned off the vast majority of rock critics."
**Irony of Authenticity (Lester Bangs)** : Lester Bangs highlighted the "wonderful irony" that despite many bubblegum groups never truly existing, there was an "authentic tenderness" in their performance of sentimental lyrics. "The wonderful irony is that it worked and even if most of these groups never really existed. There was an authentic tenderness they meant when they sang, oh sugar sugar honey you are my candy girl I can't stop wanting you."
**Simon Frith's Perspective on Pop** : Simon Frith, a former professor of music studies and rock critic, stated that pop music is "for public use" and its purpose is partly to make a profit by giving people what they already want, rather than pushing boundaries. This formulaic approach doesn't necessarily make it "bad." "Simon Frith... has stated that pop music is for public use. That means in part that the purpose of pop music is to make a profit."
**Evolution into Teen Idol Market** : In the early 1970s in the United States, bubblegum music morphed into a phenomenon closely associated with teen idols, who also sold merchandise like *Tiger Beat* and *16 Magazine* posters and lunchboxes. While teen idols were "real" people unlike cartoon bands, from a musical standpoint, there was little to differentiate them from bubblegum acts. "So in the early 70s we start to see at least in the United States bubble gum morphs into the succession with teen idols who will also sell Tiger Beat or 16 magazine posters, lunch boxes."
**Marketing as "Product"** : Trade magazines like *Billboard* frequently used the term "product" when referring to the music of both cartoon bubblegum bands and real teen idols, emphasizing the heavy marketing aspect of this genre. "If you read some of these old trade magazines like Billboard, you will note that the word product is used when referring to the music of both the cartoon bands and the real teen idols because it's a lot about marketing."
**Demand for Teen Idols** : Ron Tepper, a marketer for a Los Angeles company, observed that demand for teen idols could not be manufactured; they either fit what teenagers wanted or they didn't. If they fit, merchandise and records sold; if not, "you could not give those records away." "Ron Tepper was a marketer for the law for a company in Los Angeles and he said that you could not generate demand for teen idols. They either fit the mold of what the teenagers wanted or they did not."
**Teen Idol Sexuality and Marketing** : Fred Rice, a promoter for The Jacksons, commented that the bubblegum audience required teen idols who were "not a sexual threat," suggesting that having a picture of David Cassidy or Donnie Osmond on a pillow was analogous to "sleeping with them" in a non-threatening way. "Fred Rice who was a promoter for the Jackson's said that the bubblegum audience needed teen idols that were not a sexual threat. To have a picture of David Cassidy or Donnie Osmond on their pillow was like sleeping with them."
**Industry Perspective on Quality** : Steve Wax, a public relations representative for Bell Records in the early 1970s, emphasized that "good and bad are relative terms" in bubblegum music, arguing that if people want a particular "product," it should be provided, regardless of personal taste. "Steve Wax who was a public relations representative for Bell Records in the early 70s said, bubblegum music you've got to remember that good and bad are relative terms people say to me how can you promote that stuff but I'm paid to do a job so what do my personal likes have to do with it if that's what the people want how can I deny it to them."
