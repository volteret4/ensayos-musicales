## curiosities
**Move to United States (1965)** : Joni Mitchell relocated to the United States in 1965, becoming an integral part of the Laurel Canyon music scene in Los Angeles, which was a hub for folk and rock musicians. "Joni Mitchell moved to the United States in 1965 and she was part of the whole Laurel Canyon scene in LA."
**Shift to Folk from "Vanilla" Rock** : Joni Mitchell explained that she gravitated towards folk music because the post-Chuck Berry, post-Elvis rock of her era had become "vanilla," lacking the depth or originality she sought. "Joni Mitchell who said she turned to folk when kind of post Chuck Berry post Elvis rock became as she described it vanilla."
**Early Musical Influences** : Growing up, Joni Mitchell's primary musical influence was country music. "But what she heard growing up was country"
**Early Ambition and Canadian Success** : In a 1979 interview with Cameron Crowe, Joni Mitchell recalled that early Canadian artists, including Neil Young, had modest aspirations, with Young reportedly aiming for enough money to buy "a purse and a chicken farm." She stated that none of them had "grandiose ideas" about the scale of success they eventually achieved, considering it "a long shot," particularly for a Canadian artist. "none of us had any grandiose ideas about the kind of success we received in those days it was really a long shot especially for a Canadian."

## songs
**American Woman (1970) - Original Intent** : According to lead singer Burton Cummings, "American Woman" was not originally intended to be anti-American or anti-war, despite being widely interpreted as such during the Vietnam War era. "According to Burton Cummings, the lead singer for the guest who at that time American woman was not intended to be anti-American or anti-war although many people interpreted both of these ways."
**American Woman (1970) - Original Meaning** : Burton Cummings stated that the song "American Woman" was actually about a Canadian man feeling intimidated by what he perceived as more assertive American women. "Cummings said it was really about a Canadian guy being intimidated by slightly more assertive American women."
**American Woman (1970) - Political Interpretation** : Despite Burton Cummings' stated intent, during the Vietnam War era, "American Woman" was widely interpreted by many listeners as a political statement due to lyrics referencing "war machine" and "ghetto scene." "given that this is the Vietnam War era, regardless of what Cummings said it was supposed to be, many people did interpret American woman as political."
**American Woman (1970) - Signature Guitar Riff** : The song "American Woman" features an instantly recognizable opening guitar riff that plays a crucial role in its distinct identity and overall impact. "Lyric's aside, the opening guitar riff makes the song instantly recognizable."
**American Woman (1970) - Riff Origin** : The iconic guitar riff for "American Woman" was created spontaneously on the spot by Randy Bachman, who played it to signal Burton Cummings, who was outside the curling rink trying to buy records, to get back on stage. "Cummings said that that riff was just made up on the spot by Randy Beckman."
**American Woman (1970) - Lyric Creation** : Upon hearing Randy Bachman's impromptu riff, Burton Cummings ran to the stage and, in what he described as a "Bob Dylan-esque stream of consciousness moment," began spontaneously spouting out lyrics, initially aiming just for rhymes. "Cummings hears that riff, runs up to the stage and what he describes as a Bob Dylan S. stream of consciousness moment. He just starts spouting out some lyrics."
**American Woman (1970) - Bootleg Recording Preservation** : Fortuitously, a kid in the audience was recording the show with a tape recorder to create a bootleg. The band convinced him to give them the tape containing their "American Woman" jam, thereby preserving a crucial moment in rock history that became a number one hit. "As luck would have it, there was a kid in the audience who had a tape recorder because he was going to make a bootleg tape of the show. And the band convinced him to give them the tape with their American woman jam on it."
**American Woman (1970) - Billboard #1 Hit** : "American Woman" achieved the number one position on the Billboard Hot 100 chart on May 9, 1970, marking a significant commercial success for the band. "This song American woman went to number one on the Billboard Hot 100 on May 9, 1970."
**American Woman (1970) - Reaction to #1 Hit** : Burton Cummings recounted that the band received the news of "American Woman" hitting number one on the charts while staying at a Holiday Inn in Milwaukee, Wisconsin, leading to such excitement that they jumped on the beds until they broke it. "Bert Cumming said that when they found out they were at a holiday in in Milwaukee, Wisconsin. Back then you found out on Wednesdays where your record was on the charts and Randy Beckman took the call, told the band and then they jumped up and down on the bed so hard that they broke it."
**American Woman (1970) - Displacing Jackson 5** : "American Woman" displaced "ABC" by The Jackson Five from the number one spot on the Billboard Hot 100. "The song by the way that they knocked out of the number one spot was ABC by the Jackson Five."

## curiosities
**Formation and Origin** : The Guess Who originated from Winnipeg and began making music in 1958. "The guests who was from Winnipeg and they started making music in 1958."
**House Band for CBC's Let's Go** : For a period, The Guess Who served as the house band for the television show "Let's Go," broadcast on CBC. "For a while they were the house band for the show Let's Go on CBC."
**Early Instability and Southern Ontario Shift** : The Guess Who experienced several name and member changes early in their career before settling in Southern Ontario, where they would eventually achieve music history with a hard rock hit. "The band saw a few name changes and a few changes in members before they found themselves somewhere in Southern Ontario and about to make music history with a hard rocking hit."
**Label's Preference for Ballads** : The Guess Who's record label expressed a preference for ballads, specifically seeking another song in the vein of "These Eyes," despite the band's inclination towards hard rock. "Their label wanted something like the song These Eyes, they wanted another ballad."
**White House Performance Myth** : It is a myth that The Guess Who were asked not to play "American Woman" when invited to perform at the White House by Richard Nixon. Although the band received criticism for playing there, their PR manager fabricated the story that Nixon requested them not to play the song, believing it would generate positive publicity. "not true that they were asked to not play American woman at the White House when Richard Nixon invited the band to play there. They did get a lot of crap for playing the White House and their PR manager said that it would be good publicity for them to say that Nixon said, hey, don't play that American woman song, but that did not happen."
**Randy Bachman's Mormon Conversion and Band Tension (1966)** : Randy Bachman's conversion to Mormonism in 1966 created significant tension within The Guess Who due to conflicts between his religious beliefs and the typical rock and roll band lifestyle. "when Randy Backman of the Guests Who became a Mormon in 1966, it led to tension in the band centering on differences between the stereotypical rock and roll band lifestyle and how that conflicted with Backman's religious beliefs."

## songs
**Let It Ride (1973)** : Following the success of this and "Take Care of Business", the band was looking for a strong follow-up single in 1974. "Let it ride"

**You Ain't Seen Nothing Yet (1974) - Lyrical Content** : "You Ain't Seen Nothing Yet" is explicitly about sex, a theme that Randy Bachman later ironically referred to as his "greatest mistake" given his strict rules for band members. "You ain't seen nothing yet. It's clearly about sex."
**You Ain't Seen Nothing Yet (1974) - Randy Bachman's Initial Disbelief** : Randy Bachman initially did not believe that "You Ain't Seen Nothing Yet" would become a hit single, having written it as an internal joke and not taking it seriously. "Backman did not think that you ain't seen nothing yet would be that song."
**You Ain't Seen Nothing Yet (1974) - Origin as a Joke** : Randy Bachman wrote the song as a "not very nice joke" targeting his brother, the BTO II's brother, who had a stutter, incorporating stammering into the vocals. "He wrote it, as I will say, not very nice joke to make BTO2's brother who had a bit of a stutter."
**Rolling Down the Highway (1974)** : This song from the album "Not Fragile" was initially liked by the head of the record label, Charlie Fach, but he sought a more compelling single from the album. "he kind of liked rolling down the highway"
**You Ain't Seen Nothing Yet (1974) - Label Head's Discovery** : During a review of the "Not Fragile" album, the band played "You Ain't Seen Nothing Yet" for Charlie Fach, the head of their label, despite Randy Bachman's dismissive attitude toward it. Fach immediately recognized its potential, declaring it "magic" and insisting it be on the album. "So the band plays, you ain't seen nothing yet. Charlie Fatsch took it out and heard it. He was the head of the label and he said, this is magic. I have an earthy thing like this on the radio. You must put it on the album."
**You Ain't Seen Nothing Yet (1974) - Randy Bachman's Reluctance** : Despite label head Charlie Fach's enthusiasm, Randy Bachman was highly reluctant to include "You Ain't Seen Nothing Yet" on the album, deeming it "nuts" because the guitars were out of tune, his vocals were stammering and sounded like "bad Van Morrison impressions," and he considered it an unfinished "blurting out lyrics" rather than a proper song. "I said, you're nuts. The guitars aren't even in tune. I'm stammering and doing bad van morse and worse in impressions. I don't know what I'm doing. This isn't even a song. I haven't even written it. I'm just blurting out lyrics."
**You Ain't Seen Nothing Yet (1974) - Fach's Conviction** : Charlie Fach, the label head, overruled Randy Bachman's objections, calling the raw, unpolished track "charming." He cited his past success discovering Rod Stewart's "Maggie May" (which was seven and a half minutes long and shouldn't have been a single) as proof of his intuition, confidently declaring "You Ain't Seen Nothing Yet" would be a "career song." "And he said, no, it's charming. I discovered Maggie May for Rod Stewart and that shouldn't have been a single because it was like seven and a half minutes long. Believe me, this would be a career song."
**You Ain't Seen Nothing Yet (1974) - Enduring Success Prophecy** : Charlie Fach accurately predicted the song's enduring impact, telling Randy Bachman that he would be "singing it every day of your life and you'll be grateful to sing it every day of your life," and that it, along with "Taking Care of Business," would be BTO's "career songs." "He said, you were singing it every day of your life and you'll be grateful to sing it every day of your life. That and taking care of business are going to be your songs."
**You Ain't Seen Nothing Yet (1974) - Inclusion on Album** : Despite his strong reservations, Randy Bachman "relented" and included "You Ain't Seen Nothing Yet" on the "Not Fragile" album after the insistence of label head Charlie Fach. "So I relented and put it on the album."
**You Ain't Seen Nothing Yet (1974) - Worldwide #1 Hit** : Contrary to Randy Bachman's expectations, "You Ain't Seen Nothing Yet" became a massive international success, reaching number one in 22 countries. "They went to number one in 22 countries."
**You Ain't Seen Nothing Yet (1974) - Million-Selling Single** : "You Ain't Seen Nothing Yet" was Bachman-Turner Overdrive's sole million-selling single, a significant commercial achievement that surpassed expectations. "There's BTOs, only single million seller."
**You Ain't Seen Nothing Yet (1974) - International Covers** : The song's global popularity led to it being covered in numerous languages, possibly "10 or 12 different languages," including German, Bavarian, and Finnish, and was even adapted into dance disco hits. "And got covered in maybe 10 or 12 different languages. German, Bavarian, Finland, everybody did this song. It's been covered many times as a dance disco hit."
**You Ain't Seen Nothing Yet (1974) - Randy Bachman's Gratitude** : Randy Bachman expressed eternal gratitude for "You Ain't Seen Nothing Yet" and "Taking Care of Business," acknowledging that they provided substantial financial benefits over the years, metaphorically buying "a lot of cornflakes for a lot of kids, a lot of gas for my cars." "And I'm eternally grateful to this song. You ain't seen nothing at the taking care of business because it's bought a lot of cornflakes for a lot of kids, a lot of gas for my cars over the years."
**You Ain't Seen Nothing Yet (1974) - Influences** : According to Tom Bryan, Randy Bachman has openly acknowledged that "You Ain't Seen Nothing Yet" incorporated several musical influences, including the cowbell from Free's "All Right Now," the beat from The Doobie Brothers' "Listen to the Music," the rhythm guitar from The Who's "Baba O'Riley," and a guitar medley from Dave Mason's "Only You Know and I Know." "Backman is very open about the fact that you ain't seen nothing yet is a song that is kind of pieced together from other influences like the cowbell from all right now by free and the beat from Listen to the Music by the Dubie Brothers. Certainly the rhythm guitar from Bobo O'Reilly by the Who and this guitar medley from only you know and I know."
**You Ain't Seen Nothing Yet (1974) - Chart & Award Success** : "You Ain't Seen Nothing Yet" reached number one on both the Billboard Hot 100 and Canada's RPM chart in the week of November 9, 1974. It later received a Juno Award in 1976 for being the best-selling single in Canada. "Number one on the Billboard Hot 100 and on Canada's counterpart RPM, the week of November 9th, 1974 and it won a Juneau Award for the best selling single in Canada in 1976."

## curiosities
**Formation (1970)** : Following Randy Bachman's departure from The Guess Who in 1970, he co-founded a new band, Bachman-Turner Overdrive, with former Guess Who member Chad Allan, his brother Robbie Bachman, and bassist Fred Turner. "He left the guest who in 1970 and with another former band member Chad Allen and Backman's brother Robbie and bassist red turner, they formed a new band."
**Band Name Origin** : The band's name, Bachman-Turner Overdrive (BTO), originated from a trucking magazine called "Overdrive" that they encountered at a steakhouse in Windsor, Ontario. "the name came from a trucking magazine that they saw at a steakhouse in Windsor, Ontario. The name of the magazine was Overdrive."
**Fred Turner's Role in Naming** : Fred Turner proposed the band name by writing "BTO" on a napkin, which the members found appealing because "Overdrive" aptly described their musical style. "Fred Turner, he took a napkin, he wrote BTO on it. He's like, what do you think about this? They liked it because Overdrive seemed like a good descriptor of their style of music."
**"Car Music" Description** : Randy Bachman described Bachman-Turner Overdrive's music as "car music," meaning the kind of music specifically designed for listening to while driving. "Backman said that they make car music, the type you want to listen to while you're driving."
**Strict Band Conduct and Tim Bachman's Firing** : Randy Bachman enforced strict behavioral rules for his new bandmates, including prohibitions on drinking, drugs, and pre-marital sex. This commitment to "straight-laced behavior" was so strong that his brother, Tim Bachman, was fired from the band, with strong belief that it was for not adhering to these regulations. "He was serious, though, about the type of straight lace behavior that he expected from his new bandmates so much so that he fired his brother Tim and it's strongly believed that Tim was fired for not adhering to these rules."

## curiosities
**Lack of Radio Friendliness (1970s)** : Rush was generally not considered radio-friendly throughout the 1970s, with "The Spirit of Radio" (1980) eventually helping them to break this barrier and gain broader airplay. "Rush was not radio-friendly until the 80s, I think, the spirit of the radio in 1980 actually helped break that barrier."
**Negative Critical Reception (Early Career)** : In their early career, Rush faced significant critical disdain, with many critics failing to understand their musical direction or finding their sound to be derivative and unoriginal. "A lot of critics could not stand them. We're not even sure what they were trying to do musically."
**John Mendelssohn's Harsh Review** : Music critic John Mendelssohn, known for his harsh reviews of Led Zeppelin, described Rush's sound as "quite deliberately... like Led Zeppelin with lobotomies." He characterized singer Geddy Lee's vocals as "Robert Plant at 33 and a third RPM" and suggested only "barbed out little twerps" would find their music exciting. "To put it as succinctly as possible, Rush sound quite deliberately one supposes. Like Led Zeppelin with lobotomies, Singer getty Lee, like Robert Plant at 33 and a third RPM."
**WMMS and Donna Halper's Role in US Breakthrough** : Rush's breakthrough in the United States was significantly aided by Cleveland rock station WMMS, where DJ Donna Halper began playing "Working Man." Halper was known for her open-mindedness toward new and Canadian bands and recognized the song's energy as suitable for her Cleveland listeners. "Well, enter Cleveland Rock Station, WMMS, a DJ, Donna Halper, started playing working man."
**Donna Halper's Open-Mindedness** : DJ Donna Halper, instrumental in Rush's US breakthrough, was noted for her open-minded approach to discovering and playing new bands, particularly Canadian ones. "Now she was known to be very open-minded about new bands, including Canadian bands."
**Initial Lack of Label Interest** : The package containing Rush's record sent to DJ Donna Halper included a note indicating that no record label, not even Bob Roper's own A&M Canada, had been interested in signing the band. "There was a note with a record that said no label, including his own, was interested in signing the band."
**Toronto Radio Landscape (1970s)** : Donna Halper observed that during the 1970s, Toronto radio was in a "wimpy mellow jello phase," resulting in very little airplay for hard rock music. "Toronto Radio was going through its wimpy mellow jello phase, and Hard Rock got little airplay."
**Immediate Airplay of Working Man** : Recognizing the potential of "Working Man," Donna Halper immediately approached the on-air DJ, who agreed to play the song without delay, leading to an instant positive reaction from listeners. "She went to the DJ who was on the air at that moment. He listened. She agreed, said this has to be played immediately. And so he played it."
**Listener Demand for Rush Record** : Following the initial airplay of "Working Man" on WMMS, listener demand for the Rush record rapidly escalated within days, a phenomenon significantly facilitated by DJ Donna Halper. "And within days, people were clamoring for the Rush record, and Donna helped that happen too."
**Neil Peart's Demeanor at Final Show (2015)** : Neil Peart was "pretty adamant" that the 2015 show was to be Rush's final performance, playing with that conviction and, uncharacteristically, left his drum throne to embrace his bandmates on stage, an act he had previously vowed never to do. "Neil was pretty adamant that it was the last show, and he played it like it was going to be the final show. And that's why he actually left the drum throne and came out and gave us a hug on stage which he swore he would never do."
**Alex Lifeson's Acceptance of Rush's End (2015)** : Alex Lifeson reportedly accepted the 2015 final show as the definite end of Rush more readily than other members. "I think Alex accepted it more as the end."
**Emotional Final Performance (2015)** : The final Rush show in 2015 became deeply emotional, particularly in the last 20 minutes, to the point where Geddy Lee found himself "choked up at a microphone" for the first time, suggesting a personal acknowledgment of the band's end. "it got really emotional in the last 20 minutes. That's the first time I ever got choked up at a microphone. So I guess a part of me knew."
**Juno Award for Most Promising New Group (1974)** : In 1974, Rush received a Juno Award, recognizing them as the "most promising new group in Canada." "The rush wanted Juno in 1974 as the most promising new group in Canada."
**ZZ Top Encore Incident (1974)** : Donna Halper confirmed an incident from 1974 when Rush opened for ZZ Top: the crowd demanded an encore, but someone associated with ZZ Top unplugged Rush's amplifiers, preventing them from performing further and denying the audience's request. "Helper also confirms the story that when rush opened for ZZ Top in 1974, the crowd was screaming for an encore, but someone with ZZ Top unplugged rushes amps, denying them and the crowd in an encore."
**Donna Halper's Relationship with Band** : Donna Halper fostered a strong, protective relationship with Rush, stating she felt like their "big sister," always ensuring they were not being taken advantage of. "She clearly had a great relationship with the band and said she always felt like they're big sister. She felt very protective of them, making sure they weren't being taken advantage of."

"Rick Emmett, who explained what he views as the differences between rush and Rick's band triumphant. He said that rush took more of an ensemble integration approach. "Canadians have this hereditary inferiority complex which translated into musical group ambitions means that they just want to be hometown heroes back from conquering the states."
**Evolution of Rock Touring** : In the late 1960s and early 1970s, the concept of touring for rock bands, which had been a "complete disaster" in the final years of The Beatles (leading them to stop touring entirely), was being reshaped into a more profitable model, as exemplified by bands like The Rolling Stones.
