# genre - Novelty Song

## curiosities
**Definition by Jim Stafford (1981)** : Musician Jim Stafford stated that novelty songs are almost impossible to define, but they should always be "new and refreshing." "It is almost impossible to define novelty songs but they should be new and refreshing."
**Definition by Joshua Clover** : Author and UC Davis English professor Joshua Clover described a novelty song as one with "something very exaggerated in its differences or difference from other songs of his genre," usually pop, but sometimes country or rock. This difference, he notes, dominates the song rather than merely distinguishing it. "A novelty song has something very exaggerated in its differences or difference from other songs of his genre."
**Humor as an Element** : Humor is considered an important element, with songs being successful if funny or entertaining enough. However, humor can become stale or offensive over time, which is why novelty songs with staying power often come from artists with staying power. "I think that if a song is funny enough or entertaining enough it can become a hit."
**Musically Interesting as a Requirement** : Jim Stafford emphasized that for a novelty song to "have legs" and be heard repeatedly, the music itself must be interesting enough to support the lyrics. This implies that while the lyrics are often central to the novelty, the musical quality contributes to its longevity. "The music has to be interesting enough to support the lyrics."
**Good Storytelling as a Requirement** : Jim Stafford highlighted that the singer needs to tell a good story within the song for it to be effective. He cited Kenny Rogers' "The Gambler" as an example of strong narrative in a song. "The singer needs to tell a good story."
**Performance Challenges** : Jim Stafford compared performing a musical joke to telling any other joke, noting that it's difficult if the audience already knows the punchline. His challenge was to write clever enough songs that people would want to listen to them more than once. "The problem about telling a musical joke is the same as telling any other joke you're dead if people stop you in the middle of it."

# curiosity

**Streaking Fad (1970s)** : Streaking emerged as a college campus fad, described as a "new sport" where the object was to run naked across campus with "nothing more than a smile." News reports from SMU in Dallas noted it drawing larger crowds than basketball games. It escalated into an unruly spectator sport at UNT and UT Arlington, even interrupting a feminist meeting. The motivations included relieving tension and offering "comical relief" from serious issues like Watergate, the economy, and inflation. Some perceived it as better than "dead bodies on campus," referencing the Kent State shootings. Opponents viewed it as immoral. "Streaking began as a college campus fad."
**Public Opinion on Streaking (March 1974)** : A poll by the *Date No Hyo* local newspaper in March 1974 revealed that pro-streeters outnumbered anti-streeters by approximately four to three. Reasons for support included tension relief and comical relief from the era's political and economic difficulties. Conversely, readers of the *Missoula* newspaper in Montana were more conservative, expressing opposition and horror at newspaper photos of streakers. "In this poll pro streeters outnumbered the anti-streeters by about four to three."
**Robert Opel - Professional Streaker** : Robert Opel, a comedian, gained notoriety as a "streaker for hire." Unlike most who streaked for fun, Opel used it to attract attention for causes such as peace and protested the closing of a nude beach by streaking at an LA council meeting. His most famous act was streaking nude across the stage at the Academy Award ceremony in the 1970s, interrupting David Niven's introduction of Elizabeth Taylor. "Robert opal was a streaker not just for the fun of it which is why the vast majority of people did it but he was a streaker for hire and he did it to attract attention for causes like for peace."
**Tonight Show Streaking Incident (March 28, 1974)** : On March 28, 1974, a writer for *The Tonight Show*, Pat McCormick, streaked past Johnny Carson during his monologue. Carson had coincidentally begun the show by addressing a rumor that they would be streaked, and McCormick's act caused a mixture of delight and horror for Carson, Ed, and Doc. "On march 28th 1974 a writer for the tonight show streaked past johnny carcin in the middle of johnny's monologue."
**Disaster Movies in the 1970s** : Disaster movies, featuring themes like sinking ships, crashing airplanes, burning buildings, epidemics, and menacing sharks, were a hugely popular film genre in the 1970s. This trend followed the original *Airport* movie in 1970. The cast of 1974's *The Towering Inferno* included Paul Newman, Steve McQueen, William Holden, Fred Astaire, Robert Wagner, Richard Chamberlain, and O.J. Simpson. "If there is a singular type of movie that we loved in the 1970s it was the disaster movie sinking ships and crashing airplanes and buildings on fire and epidemics and 25 foot sharks terrorizing beach communities."
**Cultural Context for Disaster Movies (1970s)** : According to a 1975 syndicated music column by a member of the Christian rock group The Domains, the popularity of disaster movies like *Jaws* reflected a national desire for escape. Americans were "tired of serious subjects" such as presidential judgment (Nixon and Watergate), war, economic woes, and oil company complexities, finding a "healing toy" in these less serious plots. This desire for escapism was also linked to the rise of dance music and disco. "It would appear that the desire for escape has arisen in a nation that is tired of serious subjects."
**Jaws (Movie) (1975)** : Based on Peter Benchley's 1974 novel, *Jaws* was directed by a 26-year-old Steven Spielberg and became a summer blockbuster. Originally budgeted at $4 million, it ended up costing $9 million due to problems with the 25-foot mechanical shark and potential actor strikes. Despite the challenges, the movie grossed $476 million. Its iconic score by John Williams and tension-building shots of the water, rather than frequent views of the unrealistic shark, created a terrifying experience for audiences, leading to an uptick in shark dart sales in California. "Jaws was based on the 1974 novel by peter benchley and the director was a 26 year old who had yet to direct a major motion picture steven spielberg."

"His son later said that he believes that his dad was the pioneer of the break in record like the one we just heard on the streak mixing in this this reporter and then though taking it to a different level than the streak adding in these snippets of current songs."
**Rolling Stone Magazine - Cover Significance** : In the 1970s, appearing on the cover of Rolling Stone magazine was considered the ultimate sign that a singer or band had "made it," more so than just having an article inside. The magazine, in its sixth year of publication in 1973, carried significant cultural ethos for many years. "In the 1970s this was a sign that you made it if you were a singer or if you were a band and it was not just about the article like yes that was great if there was an article about you in the rolling stone but if you were on the cover you made it."

This song was also quite famous, rivaling the recognition of "Werewolves of London." "poor poor pitiful me that Linda Ronstadt made a hit with in 1976 was also quite famous."
**Werewolves of London (1978)** : Released in January 1978, this song was Zevon's most famous and his one and only Top 40 hit, reaching number 21 on the Billboard Hot 100 in May 1978, indicating a long chart run. Zevon himself dismissed it as "a piece of shit" and "a dumb song for smart people," acknowledging it as a novelty song (though distinct from "straight comedy" like Steve Martin's "King Tut"). The song ostensibly describes a "murdering fashionable werewolf" and sparked a dance craze; however, Jackson Browne offered an alternative interpretation, suggesting it was "more about a ladies man." "Werewolves of London was released in January 1978 It made it to number 21 on the Billboard Hot 100 in May which tells you it was on the charts for a long time and it was Zeevon's one and only top 40 hit."
**Werewolves of London (Writing & Recording)** : The song's title is credited to Phil Everly, who drew inspiration from the 1935 movie "Werewolf of London." The song developed organically in a session with Zevon, Leroy Maranel, and Waddy Wachtel, with Wachtel creating the opening lyrics ("I saw werewolf with a Chinese menu in his hand walking through the streets of Soho in the rain he was looking for the place called Li Ho Fux going to get a big dish of beef Chowmain") based on a recent trip to London and a real Chinese restaurant. Zevon added the closing lines about "his hair was perfect." Zevon initially wanted the song to be "more heavy and less cute," leading him to recruit Mick Fleetwood to play drums and John McVie to play bass, who were recording Fleetwood Mac's "Tusk" in the same studio. "werewolves was literally a 15 minute song that none of us took seriously we did it as spontaneously as could be and look what happened."
**Werewolves of London (Cultural Impact)** : This song gained further cultural recognition through its inclusion in the movie "The Color of Money."
