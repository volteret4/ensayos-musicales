## curiosities
**Trust Fund Background** : Gram Parsons was a privileged white individual who received a significant annual income of $55,000 from a trust fund, stemming from his grandfather's Florida Citrus Empire. This financial independence meant he never had to worry about money, a fact that some critics argued placed him outside the "struggle" often considered an essential element of authentic country music. "He was a privileged white kid who lived off the 55,000 or whatever it was, a year that he got from his trust fund."
**Musical Influences and "Cosmic American Music"** : Parsons was deeply influenced by diverse American musical forms, including gospel, Elvis's early country-tinged rock, and particularly Ray Charles' 1965 album "Country and Western Meets Rhythm and Blues." He coined the term "cosmic American music" to describe his fusion of all types of American music, intentionally crossing genre and cultural boundaries, defining it as a "stew." "He said it's cosmic American music."
**Dislike for "Country Rock" Label** : Parsons strongly disliked the term "country rock" to categorize his music, famously referring to it as "a plastic dry fuck." He preferred his own descriptive, "cosmic American music," which better encompassed his broad artistic vision. "Parsons, no surprise, did not like the term country rock, which he referred to as, and I shall quote, a plastic dry fuck."
**Founding the International Submarine Band** : While a student at Harvard, Parsons co-founded the International Submarine Band with John Nuese, which is widely considered one of the earliest country rock groups, regardless of Parsons' personal dislike for the genre label. "Parsons and John News formed what many considered to be one of the earliest country rock bands, whether Graham liked the term or not, called the International Submarine Band."
**Nudie Suits and Image** : Parsons was known for his distinctive "nudie suits," which were custom-tailored and often featured elaborate designs. His specific suit included marijuana leaves, poppy flowers, and naked ladies on the lapel, reflecting a "psychedelic cow punk" image that contributed to his legendary status. "Graham suit with its marijuana leaves and poppy flowers. He's got these naked ladies on the lapel. It's definitely one of a kind."
**Death and Body Snatching** : Parsons died on September 19, 1973, from a drug overdose, embodying a "true 70s rock star fashion." He had expressed a wish to be cremated and have his remains scattered in the Joshua Tree desert, contrary to his family's desire for a burial in Louisiana. His manager, Phil Kaufman, stole Parsons' body from Los Angeles International Airport and performed a DIY cremation in the desert, an event that significantly contributed to his legend and popularity. "He died on September 19th 1973 in true 70s rock star fashion of a drug overdose."
**Musical Chemistry with Emmylou Harris** : Gram Parsons shared a unique and profound "musical chemistry" with Emmylou Harris, creating a "gorgeous duet partnership" that many consider unparalleled. Their strong emotional and possibly spiritual connection was evident in their collaborations, despite Parsons being married during their time together. "I'm not sure if there has ever been or ever will be a duet partnership that is as gorgeous as the one between Graham and Emileu."

"it comes down to knowing how to market a band that does not neatly fit into a radio format."
**November Single Release Rule** : According to Rusty Young, the general "rule of thumb" in radio was not to release new singles in November, as stations would be preparing to play Christmas music. "the rule of thumb was to not release new singles in november because radio stations were getting geared up to play Christmas music."
**1970s Political and Musical Climate** : The mid-1970s was a "dark time both politically and musically in America," characterized by "turmoil in Washington" and the rising popularity of disco music, which influenced artists like The Eagles to create music that captured "the spirit of the time." "it was a dark time both politically and musically in america there was term oil in washington disco music was starting to take off."
