## albums
**Station to Station (1976) - Critical and Commercial Success** : This album, released in January 1976, achieved both critical acclaim and commercial success. However, at the time of its release, Bowie was unable to enjoy this triumph due to severe drug addiction affecting his mental health. "David Bowie saca el disco Station to Station, el cual es un éxito tanto de crítica como de eventa, pero hoy el es incapaz de saborear el éxito."
**Low (1977) - The Berlin Trilogy Begins** : This album, Bowie's 11th, was recorded at Château d'Hérouville with producer Tony Visconti and the core band of Carlos Alomar, George Murray, and Dennis Davis. Initially, Bowie was not entirely satisfied with the sound, leading him to invite Brian Eno to join the recording sessions. Bowie considered it a very special, melancholic album reflecting his mental recovery, thus believing it wouldn't be commercially successful. He refused to promote it, choosing instead to tour as a keyboardist for Iggy Pop, aiming to stay in the background. Despite initial mixed reviews, it performed quite well commercially. "En lausa respira melancolía, Bowie lo considera muy especial, grabado en una época de recuperación mental donde plasmoso estado, por lo que creía que no iba a funcionar comercialmente."
**Heroes (1977) - Optimism in Berlin** : Recorded at Hansa Studios, just 500 meters from the Berlin Wall, this album saw Bowie in a better mental state, aiming for a more optimistic vibe. It featured producer Tony Visconti, crucial collaboration from Brian Eno, and the same band from *Low*. Robert Fripp later joined, surprising Bowie and Visconti with his enthusiasm and adaptability, helping to define the album's epic atmosphere and creating unique sound textures. Visconti considered it a more positive version of *Low*, maintaining the structure of a conventional song side and an ambient/instrumental side. It was better received than its predecessor. "En esa época, Bowie se encontraba en un mejor estado mental, por lo que quería una hire más optimista para el disco."
**The Rise and Fall of Ziggy Stardust and the Spiders from Mars (1972) - Tour Inclusion** : Five songs from this album were added to David Bowie's world tour promoting *Heroes* and *Low*. "a la que se lean a añadir 5 canciones del Sigistardas."
**Lodger (1979) - Creative Shift** : Recorded in Switzerland with final touches in the United States, this album was created during a break in the *Heroes* tour. Robert Fripp was not involved; Adrian Belew (a future King Crimson member) took his place. Brian Eno's Oblique Strategies were crucial, inspiring ideas like playing old Bowie melodies backward and musicians swapping instruments to foster spontaneity. The album abandoned the electronic and ambient sound of *Low* and *Heroes* for more conventional songs, combining New Wave, World Music, pop, and Krautrock influences. While there were no arguments during recording, the creative spark between Bowie and Eno from their previous two albums seemed to be fading. "El disco abandonó el lado más electrónico y ambiental de sus dos anteleles trabajos a favor de canciones más convencionales, por decirlo de alguna manera."

## songs
**Warszawa (1977)** : This track was composed by Brian Eno using his portable EMS AKS synthesizer while Bowie was at a trial in Paris. It made a great impression on Bowie and became the opening track of the B-side of the *Low* album, contributing to its atmospheric, desolate, and sci-fi soundtrack-like quality. "La pista causó una gran impresión en Bowie y sería el tema que abriría la carave."
**"Heroes" (1977)** : The titular single from the *Heroes* album, despite its current iconic status, initially went largely unnoticed upon its release. Its widespread popularity wasn't achieved until David Bowie's performance at Live Aid in 1985. "el single Heroes pasó bastante de saber si evidó su momento. No fue hasta su actuación en Live Fight en 1985 que la canción se volvería popular."

## curiosities
**Drug Addiction and Mental Health Issues (1976)** : In 1976, David Bowie was suffering from a severe drug addiction that was significantly impacting his mental health. This condition made him unable to enjoy the success of his album *Station to Station*. "Sufre una fuerte adicción a las drogas, las cuales están afectando a su salud mental."
**Move to Berlin for Recovery (1976)** : To recover from his drug addiction and escape the intense spotlight of Los Angeles, David Bowie decided to move away. After a temporary stop in Switzerland, he relocated to Berlin with his friend Iggy Pop. "Para recuperarse y escapar del foco de atención, decide huir de los ángeles que su amigo y hip-hop."
**Influence of Krautrock on Bowie** : During his residency in Berlin, Bowie became immersed in the local bands, particularly Kraftwerk and Neu!, who had already been a significant influence on his 1976 album *Station to Station*. Their music, along with Edgar Froese's *Epsilon in Malaysian Pale*, became his soundtrack during his period of rehabilitation. "impregna de las bandas locales, principalmente de Crawford y no hoy quienes ya habían sido una gran influencia para Station to Station."
**Collaboration with Brian Eno and the Berlin Trilogy** : During his recovery, Bowie also listened to Brian Eno's *Discreet Music*, which greatly influenced him. This led to their collaboration on Bowie's next three albums—*Low*, *Heroes*, and *Lodger*—which would become known as the Berlin Trilogy. "escuchado Discreet Music de Brian Inno, con quien crearía sus próximos tres discos, la que sería conocida como la trilogía de Berlin."
**Shared Flat in Berlin** : After recording *The Idiot* (Iggy Pop's first solo album), David Bowie and Iggy Pop found and shared a modest flat in Berlin. "han encontrado en un modesto piso en Berlin, el cual compartieron."
**Touring as Iggy Pop's Keyboardist (1977)** : Instead of promoting his album *Low*, David Bowie chose to tour as a keyboardist for Iggy Pop, deliberately staying in the background. "Se negó a promocionarlo, bien su lugar se fue de girar como teclista el Diggy Pop, intentando quedar siempre en un segundo plano."
**Randomness and Contrast in Music** : From *Low* onward, Brian Eno taught Bowie that not all songs need to tell a story. Randomness and contrast became recurring characteristics in the careers of both artists. "Y no también le enseño que no todas las canciones tienen por qué contar una historia. A partir de ese disco, la leatoriedad del contraste serían características recurrentes para la carrera de ambos."
**The Berlin Wall as a Metaphor** : During the recording of *Heroes* at Hansa Studios, the omnipresent Berlin Wall served as a powerful visual metaphor that complemented the album's visual and emotional atmosphere. "David Bowie y Breyánino encontraron en el muro omnipresente durante toda la grabación, una meta fuera visual que complementaba la atmósfera visual y motiva del álbum."
**Post-Berlin Trilogy Move to New York (1979)** : Following his productive period in Berlin, David Bowie moved to New York in 1979. "Después de su época en Berling, Bowie se trasladó a Nueva York en 1979."
**The White Duke's Legacy** : Bowie is visualized as "the White Duke," fading into the city he left behind, whistling melodies like a solitary hero who redefined his era's musical landscape, leaving an indelible mark. "Me encanta visualizar la figura del duque blanco, desbandeciéndose en la ciudad que dejó atrás, silbando alguna de esos melodías, como un héroes solitario que redifina el paisaje musical de su época."

## curiosities
**Role in David Bowie's *Low*** : Contrary to popular belief, Brian Eno did not fully produce David Bowie's album *Low*. His fundamental importance lay in structuring the album, contributing creative ideas, and utilizing electronic instruments, particularly on the B-side. "Pese a la credencia popular, le uno está producido integramente porino, en la cara de álbum apenas aportonada, en cambio sí que tuvo una importancia fundamental a la hora de estructurarlo, aportando ideas creativas y en los instrumentos electrónicos."
**Composition of "Warszawa"** : Brian Eno composed the track "Warszawa" using his portable EMS AKS synthesizer while David Bowie was attending a trial in Paris. "Y no se llevó su sintetizador portátil, MSEHKS, y aprovecho que Bowie estaba en un juicio en parís para componer Guarxiava."
**Teaching Bowie about Narrative in Songs** : Eno taught David Bowie that not all songs necessarily need to tell a story. This perspective, combined with an embrace of randomness and contrast, became a recurring characteristic in the careers of both artists from *Low* onward. "Y no también le enseño que no todas las canciones tienen por qué contar una historia."
**Enhanced Role in David Bowie's *Heroes*** : Brian Eno played a more significant role in the creation of David Bowie's *Heroes* album than he did on *Low*. He used his "Oblique Strategies" card deck to promote creativity and generate original ideas, acting as an "assistant director" to Bowie's overall vision. "Breyánino tuvo un papel más importante que Low, si usamos sus oblices, una carta creada por el para promover la creatividad y generar ideas originales, y tomó el papel de asistente de ese gran director que era David Bowie."
**Creative Spark Fading on *Lodger*** : While Brian Eno and David Bowie did not argue or fight during the recording of *Lodger*, it was noticeable that the creative spark present during their work on *Low* and *Heroes* was beginning to diminish. "El feeling entre Inno y Bowie y Anoral mismo no discutieron ni se pelearon durante la grabación del disco, pero se notaba que la chispacreativa de esos dos anteleles trabajos estaba extinguiéndose."

## curiosities
**Pioneering Harmonizer Use (1977)** : During the recording of David Bowie's *Low*, producer Tony Visconti used the iconic Harmonizer H910 multi-effects processor for the first time on the drums. He applied it to give the drums a futuristic touch, a technique that would later be widely adopted in New Wave music in the 1980s. "Besconti utilizó por primera vez el icónico procesador multi-efectos, Armonaiser H910 en la batería para darle un toque futurista, técnica que usaría la NiWave en los años 80."
**Capturing Ideas for *Heroes*** : During the recording sessions for David Bowie's *Heroes*, which were overflowing with constantly evolving ideas often at risk of being lost, Tony Visconti kept a magnetic tape recorder running continuously to ensure no creative moment was missed. "Visconti tenía una grabadora de cinta magnética y ciencia en día durante todo el proceso para que no se perdiese nada."
**View on *Heroes*** : Tony Visconti regarded David Bowie's *Heroes* as a more positive version of its predecessor, *Low*. "Visconti lo consideró como una versión más positiva de Low."

# curiosity

**The Berlin Trilogy (1977-1979) - Acclaim Over Time** : The three albums (Low, Heroes, Lodger) comprising David Bowie's Berlin Trilogy, created with Brian Eno and Tony Visconti, profoundly influenced music throughout the 1980s, leaving an indelible mark. These albums gained critical acceptance over time and are now considered among the best not only in David Bowie's discography but in the history of modern music. "La tecnología de Berlingress se lo profundamente a lo largo de los años 80, dejando una voy enborrable en la música desadecada. Estos tres discos con el paso del tiempo fueron ganando aceptación crítica. De hoy lista que no les incluyante los mejores discos ya lo solo dé Bowie, sino de la historia de la música moderna."
**The Berlin Trilogy Structure** : Both David Bowie's *Low* and *Heroes* albums followed a similar two-sided structure, featuring one side with more conventional songs and another side dedicated to more ambient, instrumental tracks. "Heroes sigue la estructura del anterior trabajo, con una cara con canciones más convencionales y una cara de con temas ambientales más instrumentales."
