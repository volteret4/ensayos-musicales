# curiosity

**The Ruta del Bacalao / Ruta Destroy - Global Clubbing Capital** : During the 1980s and early 1990s, Valencia transformed into the global capital of clubbing, giving rise to the Ruta del Bacalao, also known as the Ruta Destroy. This phenomenon was more than just a collection of discotheques along the coast; it was a cultural revolution that defined a generation, offering a unique space for freedom, creativity, and exuberance with a globally unparalleled coherent sound and aesthetic. "La conocida como ruta del bacalao no era solo un conjunto de discotecas esparcidas a lo largo de la costa, era un fenómeno que marcó una generación, un espacio de libertad, creatividad y desenfreno que definió la joventud de toda una edad, y todo ello con una coherencia sonora y estética única nivel mundial."

**Ruta del Bacalao - Modern Pilgrimage** : The Ruta del Bacalao was the largest clubbing movement in Spain, comprising a series of discotheques located along the Carretera del Saler. Young attendees would travel by car, scooter, or bus from one club to another in a journey that could last up to 72 hours, resembling a modern pilgrimage to temples devoted to dark, avant-garde, and eclectic music. "Consistía en una serie de discotecas ubicadas alrededor de la carretera del saler."

**Post-Dictatorship Youth Seeking Freedom** : The Ruta del Bacalao emerged shortly after Spain transitioned out of a dictatorship, with young people deeply eager for freedom and new experiences, especially in nightlife and music. This societal context fueled their desire to party and explore novel sounds, contributing significantly to the movement's fervent energy and widespread appeal. "Los jóvenes estaban ansiosos de libertad y se morían por salir de fiesta y vivir experiencias y sonidos nuevos."

**Immature Legislation and Legal Loopholes** : During Spain's transition period, nocturnal leisure legislation was underdeveloped due to the legacy of Francoism, containing numerous legal loopholes. Discotheque entrepreneurs skillfully exploited these gaps, allowing clubs on the Ruta to operate with extended hours and flexible regulations, which inadvertently facilitated the unprecedented growth of the clubbing phenomenon. "Durante el periodo de la transición, la legislación española, en todo lo relacionado al océo nocturno, era un muy madura debido al legado del franquismo y estaba llena de evacios legales que aprovecharon los empresarios de las discotecas."

**Stigmatization and Changing Perception of the Ruta** : For many years, the Ruta del Bacalao suffered from a stigmatized public image, primarily associated with chaos and sensationalist headlines. However, this perception has been evolving positively, thanks to recent documentaries and books such as "Bacalao: historia oral de la cultura de Valencia" and "En Éxtasis: Bachalao como contracultura de España," along with the A3 Media series "La Ruta." "Durante muchos años ha habido una visión estigmatizada de la Ruta del bacalao, relacionada con el caos y los titulares sensacionalistas."

**Barraca - Birthplace of "Música Blanca" (Opened 1965)** : Barraca, a discotheque located in a traditional Valencian house (barraca), had been open since 1965, originally hosting everything from jazz concerts to flamenco tablao. Its pivotal moment arrived in 1981 when DJs Juan Santamaría and Carlos Simó began spinning "música blanca," a sound characterized by artists like Depeche Mode, The Cure, Joy Division, and The Fall, which became so iconic that Valencians nicknamed it "música barraquera." "La discoteca ubicada en una barraca, la casa tradicional valenciana, llevaba abierta desde 1965 y al vergo desde conciertos de jazz a un tablo de flamenco."

**Chocolate - The Darkest Club (Opened 1980)** : Chocolate opened in 1980, located 200 meters from Barraca in a former dried rice warehouse, named for its dark brown facade. It was renowned as "the darkest, the most gothic club on the Ruta," with DJ Tony Vitale "El Gitano" spinning sinister bands such as Bauhaus, Cabaret Voltaire, and Throbbing Gristle, alongside guitar-driven acts like The Cramps and The Meteors, attracting punk and sinister subcultures. "Su fachada estaba pintada de color marrón oscuro, de ahí su nombre, y su sonido. Fue el club más oscuro, el más gótico de todas las discotecas de la ruta."

**Chocolate - Pioneer of Drag Queen Shows** : Chocolate was one of the pioneering venues on the Ruta to host drag queen shows, an innovative and truly groundbreaking initiative for its era. This progressive stance further cemented its reputation as a unique and avant-garde space within the Valencian club scene. "También fue uno de los primeros lugares en albergar shows de drag queens en una época en la que era algo realmente pionero."

**Chocolate - Concerts at 7 AM** : In its early days, Chocolate focused more on concerts, uniquely scheduled at 7 AM to attract patrons leaving Barraca. Stories recount how foreign artists, upon hearing the unusual early morning time, often believed it to be a mistake and would repeatedly inquire if the organizers actually meant 7 PM. "Cuentan que cuando les decían el horario de los artistas extranjeros, creían que se trataba de un error y siempre preguntaban si no se referían a la 7 de la tarde."

**Fran Lenaers' DJ Mixing Revolution** : Fran Lenaers revolutionized DJing in Valencia at Spook Factory by being one of the first to seamlessly mix records, a stark contrast to the previous practice where DJs would play an entire song and then, in the final seconds, cue up the next. His refined technique profoundly defined the sound of Spook Factory and, by extension, influenced the entire Ruta, pushing towards more electronic and avant-garde sounds. "Hasta entonces, en las discotecas, era muy raro que se mezclas en el psiquitrón en las canciones. Se solía dejar la canción entera y en los últimos segundos se ponía la siguiente."

**Spook Factory - The Spectrum Factory** : Spook Factory, known as "la discoteca del murcia lago, la fábrica del espectro" (the Murcia Lake discotheque, the spectrum factory), initially shared a dark sound akin to Chocolate or Barraca but differentiated itself by being more electronic. Under Fran Lenaers and DJ Tete, its musical direction shifted firmly towards techno and EBM, attracting a broader audience interested in electronic and avant-garde sounds, and focused more on vinyl than performance. "Vivienta también tenía un sonido escuro similar al de chocolate o barraca, el suyo era más electrónico."

**Spook Factory - One-Hour Legal Closure** : Spook Factory famously adhered to the law by closing for only one hour, from 5 to 6 AM, during which time the lights were turned on, cleaning commenced, music was stopped, and doors were closed, all while the crowd remained inside. Promptly at 6 AM, the lights were dimmed again, and the DJ resumed the party. "La discoteca tras solo cerraba por ley durante una hora, de 5 a 6, donde sentían las luces de la impiezas, paraban la música y cerraban las puertas."

**Espiral - "The Toughest Club of Levante" (Opened 1970)** : Espiral, open since 1970 and initially a disco club, achieved fame outside Valencia from 1976 onwards, partly due to DJ Jesús Brisas, earning it the moniker "la discoteca más cañera de levante" (the toughest club in the east). Known for its hard-hitting sound including EBM, Dark Wave, and Industrial, which collectively defined "música máquina," the club featured a pool often filled during Saturday sessions and had its own anthem, "El Mítico Dune Espiral." "Era conocida como la discoteca más cañera de levante."

**Puzzle - Refining Styles and Sunday Afters** : Puzzle distinguished itself by embracing new, "more colorful" musical styles such as synth pop, techno pop, and even house, hosting bands like New Order and Camouflage in its 80s sessions. It became renowned for its mythical Sunday sessions starting at 6 AM, drawing crowds from Spook, Barraca, and Chocolate, effectively becoming a major after-party spot, thanks to its resident DJs, Los Gemelos. "Pasó a abrir nuevos estilos musicales más coloridos, sin pop, techno pop o incluso house."

**ACTV - Architectural Icon on the Beach (Building 1918)** : ACTV was the sole Ruta discotheque located in the Valencian capital, housed in a beachfront building from 1918 that originally served as the Termas Victoria public baths. Designed by Kike Kompany, its powerful aesthetic generated mystery around its acronym, with theories ranging from "Arturo Controla Toda Valencia" to the official "Actividad Escultural de las Termas Victoria," or even "Corriente Alterna y Televisión" as suggested by its image creators. "La única discoteca de la ruta ubicada en la capital Valenciana. En un edificio a Pied de Playa Construido en 1918, el cual alojo unos baños públicos, las termas Victoria."

**ACTV - Superior Sound and Lighting** : ACTV was lauded for having one of the most powerful club designs ever seen, with a very clean electronic sound. Many considered it the club with the best sound and lighting on the Ruta, contributing significantly to its reputation for delivering an exceptional sensory experience. "Hay quien dice que era el club con mejor sonido e iluminación."

**N.O.D. - Inverted Name, Parking Culture (Previously Don Julio)** : N.O.D., formerly known as Don Julio, ingeniously derived its name by inverting the word "Don" and literally flipping the letters on its facade. Under DJ Kike Jaén, it became the primary instigator of the "Cultura de Parking," a phenomenon akin to today's "botellón" where patrons invested heavily in car sound systems for parking lot parties, which the club supported with free paellas and open-air sessions. "N.O.D es la palabra Don invertida, cuentan que aprovecharon las letras de la facha de las posicion del revés, de Aila en Invertida."

**"Cultura de Parking" - The Outdoor Clubbing Phenomenon** : The "Cultura de Parking" originated at N.O.D., driven by DJ Kike Jaén, where club-goers invested large sums in car sound systems to host parties in the parking lots, akin to modern "botellones." The clubs, seeing its appeal, supported this phenomenon with initiatives like free paellas and open-air sessions, leading to times when the parking lots drew more people than the discotheques themselves, spreading to clubs like Chocolate and Heaven. "Muchos de esos feligreses invertían grandes cantidades de dinero en equipos de música para sus coches."

**Heaven (Jiven) - Valencia's First After-Party** : Heaven, known locally as "Jiven," was a club where the party truly began when all others ended. Its legendary Sunday sessions, which started at 6 AM, earned it the reputation among many as Valencia's first after-party, attracting crowds from other clubs and cementing its place in the Ruta's vibrant nightlife. "En Jiven empezaba la fiesta cuando terminaban todas las demás."

**Pinedo - Thunderous Volume and Parking Parties** : Pinedo, situated almost on the beach, was renowned for possessing one of the most powerful sounds and, reportedly, the most "thunderous volume" on the entire Ruta. Its extreme loudness often led many patrons to prefer listening to sessions from the parking lot, where parallel parties (Cultura de Parking) would spontaneously erupt. "Pintanar hace un diubicada también casi en la playa, el club tenía uno de los sonidos más contundentes y, según dicen, el volumen más atronador de toda la ruta."

**Other Clubs of the Ruta del Bacalao** : Beyond the most famous venues, many other significant clubs emerged around the Ruta, including Villa de Lina, The Face, Arabesco, Kumanises, Sucre (later Amadeus), Isla, Distrito 10, El Torero (a bar on Spook's terrace), Galaxys (later known as Evento), Bananas, and El Templo. These clubs contributed to the extensive and diverse network that characterized the Ruta del Bacalao. "Al renedor de todas ellas empezaron a surgir muchas otras, como Villa de Lina, de Fays, Arabesco, Kumánises, Sucre, posteriormente Amadeus, Isla, Distrito 10, El Torero, el barubicado en la terraza de Spook, Galaxiq, conocida posteriormente como evento, Vananas, o el templo."

**Ruta del Bacalao's Lack of Foreign Media Recognition** : Despite its global significance, the Ruta del Bacalao received surprisingly little attention from foreign media, with critics like Simon Reynolds omitting it from his seminal work on electronic music, "Energy Flash." While Reynolds did acknowledge Ibiza's importance for the "summer of love" explosion in 1988 UK that sparked rave culture, he never referenced Valencia's club culture, which was arguably Spain's most important and a pioneer in electronic music. "Por ejemplo, Simon Reynolds no la mencionan sobre la música electrónica, Energy Flash."

**Musical Evolution in the 90s - Shift to Commercialism and Trance** : In the 1990s, the Ruta's music underwent a significant transformation as Acid House gained momentum and DJs started playing more commercial genres like Eurodance. The new wave of attendees prioritized pure partying and sought hypnotic, trance-like rhythms that fostered a true "cult to dance," marking a departure from the avant-garde experimentation and musical sophistication of the 80s. "La nueva aleada de asistentes ya no buscaba la experimentación van guardista ni la sofisticación musical de antes. Su prioridad era la fiesta en su forma más pura, optando por ritmos hipnóticos y sonidos que arrozaban el trance en un auténtico culto al baile."

**New Audience in the 90s - Escape from Routine** : The 90s brought a new demographic to the Ruta, predominantly composed of workers who viewed the weekend as an escape from their weekly routines. These individuals would dedicate their energy and savings to unleashing their passion during the weekend, recovering on Monday and Tuesday, only to repeat the cycle, finding music to be a crucial valve for escape from daily tedium and confinement. "Este nuevo grupo estaba predominantemente compuesto por trabajadores, personas que le quedaban sus rutinas semanas sobrevividas hasta el viernes, invertían su energía y sus ahorros en desatar toda su pasión durante el fin de semana y luego pasaban los primeros días de la semana recuperándose, sólo para repetir el ciclo una y otra vez."

**Decline of the Ruta del Bacalao - Negative Media Focus and Increased Control** : The Ruta del Bacalao's perception drastically shifted due to increased media focus on its negative aspects, including rising accident rates, more varied and lower-quality drugs, and a greater number of alcohol controls. This, combined with stricter club laws, reduced operating hours, increased police presence, heightened social awareness, political changes, and a diminished clubbing culture, made it virtually impossible for such a phenomenon to reoccur. "El crimen tal caser puso el foco de atención mediático a la cará negativa de la escena. Una cada vez mayor tasa de accidentes, drogas más variadas y de peor calidad, mayor número de controles de arcolemia, la percepción de la ruta cambió."

**Ibiza's Reinvention vs. Valencia's Decline** : While the Ruta del Bacalao faced decline, Ibiza, across the sea, managed to reinvent itself and sustain the "Valencian fantasy" that had once defined the Ruta. Ibiza's ability to adapt and prolong its allure stands in contrast to Valencia's inability to overcome the challenges that led to the Ruta's eventual demise. "Al otro lado del mar, en diviza se había hecho mejor. El sueño y viza se suporra inventar e hizo perdurable la fantasía valenciana."
