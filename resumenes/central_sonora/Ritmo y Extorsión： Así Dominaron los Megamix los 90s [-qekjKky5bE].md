# artist - Miguel DeGa

## member of
Max Music

## curiosities
**Co-founder of Max Music** : Miguel DeGa, a salesman who previously sold Italo Disco maxis in discotheques, decided with Ricardo Campoy to form their own company, Max Music, in 1964. They were central figures in the Spanish Megamix phenomenon and were known for their unscrupulous business practices. "Miguel de Gaid Ricardo Campoy, dos comerciales 20 a.C. que se ganaban la vida vendiendo Maxis de Italo Disco en discotecas, deciden que ha llegado el momento de formar su propia empresa y crean el sillo Max Music en 1964."
**Unscrupulous Business Practices** : Miguel DeGa and Ricardo Campoy, the duo behind Max Music, were noted for their lack of scruples in generating profit. Their methods included corporate espionage, pressuring distributors, and commissioning cover versions of songs to avoid paying rights for the originals. "El duo que formaban Miguel de Gaia y Ricardo Campoy no tenía lescrupulos para hacer dinero de cualquier manera."
**Exporting Megamixes to International Markets** : Miguel DeGa was instrumental in expanding the Megamix phenomenon beyond Spain. He opened an office in Miami and successfully exported the concept to countries like Germany, Argentina, and Mexico. "Miguel le agabrió una oficina en Miami y exportó al fenómeno Megamix al extranjero."
**Criminal Actions and Imprisonment** : Following Ricardo Campoy's departure from Max Music to found Vale Music, Miguel DeGa's response escalated into criminal activity. On September 3, 1998, DeGa hired 13 Mexican hitmen to kidnap Campoy. Due to a mistaken identity, José María Castells was kidnapped instead. DeGa was subsequently convicted and sentenced to prison, from which he escaped in 2005 during a temporary leave, and his whereabouts remain unknown. "El 3 de septiembre de 1998, el empresario contrató a 13 carios mexicanos para secuestrar al Ricardo Campoy."

## curiosities
**Winner of Max Music's Mixing Contest** : Mike Platinas won the contest organized by Max Music, which sought creative and ingenious individuals to mix songs, offering a prize of 100,000 pesetas and a record deal. # genre - Italo Disco

## curiosities

# label - Blanco y Negro Music

## curiosities
**Early Independent Record Label** : Blanco y Negro Music was an independent record label based in Barcelona that had just started its journey in the industry when it released "Studio 54 Connection." This album, mixed by Raúl Orellana, was the first mix record published in Spain. "El disco le editó Blanco en el Euro Music, una discográfica independiente de la ciudad con Dal que acababa de empezar su andadura en la industria."
**Creator of the Bolero Mix Series** : Seeing the success of the Max Mix series, Blanco y Negro Music decided to launch its own compilation series called "Bolero Mix." They permanently signed Raúl Orellana as their main Megamixer for this highly successful and long-running series. "en blanco y negro, viendo el éxito de la serie, deciden sacar la soya propia, a la que llaman Bolero Mix, fichando ya definitivamente Raúl Erellana, quien se convirtió en su principal Megamixer."
**Rival to Max Music** : Blanco y Negro Music, despite being an independent label, was one of the two main players (along with Max Music) that dominated sales in the Megamix market, even surpassing major labels like CBS, EMI, and Westpavogs. Its success rivaled that of Max Music, leading to intense competition, including Max Music's controversial hiring of Blanco y Negro's star DJ, Quique Tejada. "Pero blanco y negro, y en especial Max Music, dos discográficas independientes recién creadas estaban arrasando en ventas, superando a gigantes como CBS, Emmy, Westpavogs."

# label - Max Music

## curiosities
**Founding and Early Strategy (1964)** : Max Music was founded in 1964 by two salesmen, Miguel DeGa and Ricardo Campoy, who previously sold Italo Disco maxis in clubs. To kickstart their venture, they launched a contest for creative song mixers, offering a 100,000 peseta prize and a record deal, which was won by Mike Platinas. "crean el sillo Max Music en 1964."
**Pioneer of Spanish Megamixes and Name Registration** : Max Music was instrumental in popularizing the Megamix phenomenon in Spain, with its first "Max Mix" album (mixed by Mike Platinas and Javier Usia) being a sales success. The label even registered the name "Megamix" to refer to these types of compilations. "la discográfica registró el nombre de Megamix para referirse a esos trabajos."
**Unethical Business Practices** : Max Music was known for its "not entirely ethical" practices. These included forcing contest winners to choose between prize money or a record deal, failing to pay mixers for their work, engaging in corporate espionage, pressuring distributors to favor their products, commissioning cover versions of songs to avoid paying royalties, and flooding the market with compilations to undermine competitors. "se podría decir que las prácticas del sello no eran del todo éticas."
**Involvement in Criminal Activities** : The intense rivalry between Max Music and newly formed Vale Music (founded by former Max Music co-founder Ricardo Campoy) escalated dramatically. Miguel DeGa, head of Max Music, hired 13 hitmen to kidnap Campoy, an act that mistakenly led to the kidnapping and beating of José María Castells. DeGa was subsequently imprisoned and later escaped. "El 3 de septiembre de 1998, el empresario contrató a 13 carios mexicanos para secuestrar al Ricardo Campoy."
**End of an Era** : The departure of Ricardo Campoy, who took the Dream Team and other employees to form Vale Music, along with Miguel DeGa's subsequent criminal conviction, ultimately led to the end of Max Music. This marked a significant turning point in the Spanish music industry. "Lo que es cierto es que ese movimiento propició la creación de la nueva discográfica, Vale Music, y el final de Max Music."

# label - Vale Music

## curiosities
**Founding by Ricardo Campoy** : Vale Music was created following Ricardo Campoy's departure from Max Music, where he had been a co-founder. Campoy reportedly took a self-loan of 75 million pesetas and brought The Dream Team and other staff from Max Music to establish his new label. "ese movimiento propició la creación de la nueva discográfica, Vale Music"
**Post-Max Music Success and TV Collaborations** : After the tumultuous end of Max Music, Vale Music continued to release successful compilations such as "Disco Estrella" and "Dance Mix." The label innovated by collaborating with popular television programs like "Gran Hermano" and "Crónicas Marcianas" to release albums featuring music from those shows. "Siguieron sacando recuperatorios como Disco Estrella o Dance Mix y USA y empezaron a colaborar con programas televisivos de éxito y evitar ángomes de la música que sonaba en ellos, como son el caso de gran hermano o crónicas marcianas."
**Partnership with Operación Triunfo** : Vale Music secured a contract to release weekly albums for "Operación Triunfo," which became the biggest musical television success in Spanish history. Beyond these weekly releases, the label also took on the responsibility of managing the careers of the show's contestants. This partnership represented the last major commercial success for the Spanish record industry before the widespread impact of piracy. "Operación triunfo se convirtió en el mayor éxito televisivo musical de la historia del país."

# curiosity

**Megamixes as a Unique Spanish Phenomenon (1980s-1990s)** : During the 1980s and 1990s, while global pop artists dominated charts elsewhere, Spain experienced an unprecedented phenomenon where peculiar electronic music compilation albums, known as Megamixes, frequently topped sales charts. These mixes, characterized by frantic and skillful blending with scratches, brakes, and sound effects, were a unique cultural and commercial success. "en España ocurrió un fenómeno particular y inédito."
**From Clubs to Million-Dollar Revenue** : Record labels that initially focused on selling music directly in discotheques witnessed a dramatic shift, transitioning to generating millions in revenue through the success of these Megamix compilations. This transformed their business model and income streams. "Los sellos que evitaron estos discos pasaron de vender música directamente en discotecas a generar ingresos millonarios."
**Origins of Mixing in Late 1970s US Clubs** : The concept of mixing began in the late 1970s when some American funk and disco DJs started creating new songs by blending rhythmic parts from various tracks for playback in discotheques. These early experiments proved highly successful, setting the stage for the later Megamix phenomenon. "A finales de los años 70, algunos diges norteamericanos de fanque y disco empezaron a crear canciones mezclando partes rímpicas de varios temas para reproducirlos en discotecas, donde eran todo un éxito."
**Early Circulation of Pirated Cassettes** : The experimental mixes created by US DJs in the late 1970s quickly gained popularity, leading to the widespread circulation and brisk sales of pirated cassettes featuring these unique blends. This early informal market demonstrated a strong demand for mixed music. "Empezaron a circular casets piratas con esas mezclas, los cuales se vendían como churros."
**Definition and Techniques of a Megamix** : A Megamix is described as a musical and sound collage where the "megamixer" combines fragments of different songs to create a cohesive, unitary whole. Unlike conventional DJ sessions, Megamix production involved studio tools such as samplers and sequencers for precise cutting, tempo adjustment, and the addition of effects, utilizing techniques akin to scratching, sampling, and beat juggling, including frantic beat repetitions and original compositions. "Un megamix es eso, un colach musical y sonoro en el que el megamixer combina trozos de diferentes canciones para crear un conjunto anitario."
**Traditional Labels' Response to Megamix Success** : The unprecedented sales success of Megamixes, particularly "Max Mix 4" surpassing even Julio Iglesias's album, deeply affected traditional record labels. In response, they pressured for the creation of a separate, parallel chart specifically for compilations, aiming to prevent these albums from "staining" the chart positions of legendary artists. "presionaron para que si se une lista paralela específica de recopilatorios."
**Influence of "La Ruta y el Sonido Valencia"** : The cultural and commercial explosion associated with "La Ruta y el Sonido Valencia" (the "Valencia Route/Sound") significantly influenced the Megamix phenomenon. This led to Megamixes adopting faster and more forceful music styles, expanding beyond Italo Disco to incorporate genres like Acid House, New Beat, EBM, and later Máquina and OroDens. "Tras la explosión cultural y comercial que supuso la ruta y el sonido valencia, el fenómeno Megamix empezó a adoptar músicas más rápidas y contundentes que iban más allá delitalo disco."
**Market Saturation with Commercial Megamixes (1990s)** : By the 1990s, capitalizing on their popularity, the market became saturated with increasingly commercial Megamixes. Some notable examples included "Maquina Total" and "Lo Más Duro," which initially featured techno or EDM but later evolved to include accelerated covers, pachanga, and "cantaditas." "el mercado es el sobresatoro de Megamix es cada vez más comerciales."
**Club-Branded Megamixes** : In addition to record label releases, major discotheques across Spain also began issuing their own branded Megamixes, such as those associated with "Pole Position," "Scorpia," "Central del Sonido," "Chasis," and "Pont Aeri." These compilations further diversified the market. "Las principales discotecas del país también sacaban sus propios Megamixes, como fueron las hérias por letrón, escorpias central del sonido, chashi soponairi."
**"Bomba Zomix" Controversy and Exploitation** : Weeks after a failed car bomb attempt on José María Adnar (Aznar) by the terrorist group ETA on April 19, 1995, Max Music announced its new Megamix, "Bomba Zomix." This release was highly profitable for Miguel DeGa and Ricardo Campoy, who were deemed unscrupulous for profiting from such a sensitive national event. "Bomba Zomix fue un meme muy rentable."
**Documentary "Megamix Brutal"** : A documentary titled "Megamix Brutal" was recently released, delving into the entire Megamix phenomenon with a particular focus on the malicious business practices and unethical actions that characterized the era. "Hace poco se estén en el documental Megamix brutal. En el de següenta todo el fenomeno más centrado en estas prácticas maciosas."
**Spanish Megamix as a "Made in Spain" Phenomenon** : Despite attempts to export the Megamix trend to countries like Germany, Argentina, and Mexico, where it had relative success, the Spanish Megamix scene maintained its unique identity. Characterized by its creativity, distinct techniques, and sonic narrative, it remained largely a "Made in Spain" phenomenon, contrasting with the DJ sets and compilations popular in Anglo-Saxon markets like Ministry of Sound or Global Underground. "La escena del Megamix español, con su creatividad, técnica propia y enerrativa sonora, se mantuvo como un fenómeno Mating Spain."
**Impact of Piracy on Spanish Music Industry** : The era of Vale Music and "Operación Triunfo," which represented the last major commercial success for the Spanish record industry, was ultimately curtailed by the advent of widespread piracy. Piracy radically changed the landscape of the music industry in Spain. "antes de que la piratería llegase para cambiar radicalmente el panorama."
**Megamixes as Precursors to Modern Music Techniques** : The in-depth study of various Megamixes reveals that many of them incorporated techniques and concepts that would later be popularized by more innovative genres of modern music. This suggests that Megamixes, though perhaps overlooked, deserve a place in music history for their foresight and innovation. "en muchos de ellos he podido ver cómo se ha dado en técnicas y conceptos que más tarde popularizarían géneros más innovadores de la música moderna."
