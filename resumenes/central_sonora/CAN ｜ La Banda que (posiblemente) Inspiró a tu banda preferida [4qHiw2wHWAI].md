## albums
**Tomorrow Never Knows (Implied 1968)** : This was the band's initial recording attempt, but no record label was willing to release it at the time. It was eventually released years later, grouped with other recordings from 1968. "un disco que no encontró discográfica a dispuesto a editar lo que saldría años más tarde, junto a otras avaciones como D.L.I.1968."
**Monster Movie (1969)** : Can's first official album, described as a "table de boot" made more accessible to help the band secure a record deal. It featured three songs on one side and the 20-minute track "Yoo Doo Right" on the other, establishing a tribal mantra driven by Jaki Liebezeit's drums. This album was a clear statement of intent, blending rock, funk, and free jazz influences with a crucial emphasis on percussions over melodies, a dynamic that would define their future releases and the distinct sound of Kraut Rock. "una table de boot al cual hicieron un más accesible para que no tuviesen problemas a la banda de encontrar discográfica."
**Soundtracks (1970)** : This compilation marked Damo Suzuki's first work with the band, featuring songs recorded for films and quickly assembled to meet a record label's interest in their material. It also included two earlier songs with Malcolm Mooney, and notably expanded the band's lyrical scope to include Japanese alongside English, reflecting Suzuki's original, improvised approach to language. Though a "sobresaliente trabajo" with tracks like "Tel-Doc" and "Modern Sky," it veered towards a more pop-oriented and psychedelic sound, closer to Pink Floyd, and served as a good starting point for new listeners, despite the band's desire to shed Anglo-Saxon influences. "Este es un trabajo sobresaliente, con grandes canciones como Tel-Doc, Tango Whisky-Man o Modern Sky."
**Tago Mago (1971)** : Considered the band's second official album, named after a trip to Mallorca, this work allowed Can to pursue a more daring sonic path. Jaki Liebezeit's drums were in a "state of grace," acting as the sole anchor amidst a surrealist canvas of distortion, noise, electronic keyboards, and tape manipulation that created the band's signature musical landscape. This profoundly unusual double album showcased their unique compositional method, involving extensive improvisation sessions from which appealing segments were later cut and potentially enhanced with arrangements. The first vinyl focused on rhythm, concluding with the sublime 18-minute funk trance of "Halleluwah," which nearly anticipated drum and bass, while the second vinyl explored more abstract and cosmic themes with long tracks like "Aumgn" and "Peking O," revisiting the atonal spirit of Stockhausen. It is widely regarded as the band's most famous and acclaimed work, frequently appearing on lists of the greatest rock albums in history, offering a wilder, darker, and tenser form of psychedelia in contrast to Pink Floyd's meticulously constructed progressive approach, with its groovy rhythms and often dark sonic pollution serving as a clear reference for John Lydon's band PiL. "Este tal vez sea el trabajo más famoso y destacado de la banda, el cual aparece en todas las listas de los mejores discos de rock de la historia."
**Ege Bamyasi (1972)** : Another masterpiece that continued the avant-garde and experimental direction of "Tago Mago" but achieved greater accessibility due to the consistent rhythmic presence throughout the album, particularly highlighting Jaki Liebezeit's exceptional drumming. This album offered a funkier, extremely precise, and polished rhythm, continuously propelled by Holger Czukay's pulsating and repetitive bass. Damo Suzuki shone on this record as one of his generation's most unique and brilliant vocalists, with his singing on "Sing Swan Song" notably influencing Kanye West's track "Drunk and Hot Girls" and countless rock artists. "Este disco nos ofrece una rímica más funky, extremadamente precisa y pulida, se os tenía constantemente por el bajo pulsante, repetitivo de Chukai."
**Future Days (1973)** : Described as a "breath of fresh air" after the sonic intensity of "Tago Mago," this album moved away from darker sounds to explore a more atmospheric and cosmic aesthetic. It metaphorically takes the listener on a zero-gravity journey over the sea, drifting yet generating a constant sense of momentum, with Holger Czukay's magnetic tapes projecting luminous synthesizer notes. Considered the band's most balanced album, where no member overshadowed another, Damo Suzuki's contributions were less prominent and more discreet. This work profoundly influenced Brian Eno's ambient output and, indirectly, David Bowie's Berlin phase, serving as an excellent and luminous turning point for the band that anticipated the post-rock genre of the 90s. "Una obra excelente que aporta un giro hacia la luz a la banda y que se adelantó al por rock de los 90."
**Soon Over Babaluma (1974)** : This was the first album released after Damo Suzuki's departure, and while loved by the narrator, it is suggested to be somewhat underrated due to being overshadowed by the preceding "trilogy." The album is noted for anticipating darker and ambient techno sounds, predating their wider emergence. "Pero eso en el album enorme en el que se adelantan al techno más escuro y ambiental."

## curiosities
**Formation in Cologne and Inner Space Studio** : The band was formed in Cologne, described as the capital of contemporary music, and established their base of operations in Schloss Nörvenich, a castle near Cologne, which they named "Inner Space." This castle served as the recording site for their initial albums. "La banda se formó un colonia, capital de la música contemporánea y como va a ser de operaciones instalaron en el Oslo Bernish, un castillo cerca de colonia, al que llamaron inerospays. El ese castillo grabaron sus primeros discos."
**Name Origin - Communism, Anarchism, Nihilism** : According to band members in an interview, the name "Can" (or "Cannes, The English") was an acronym for Communism, Anarchism, and Nihilism, though this was presented somewhat ironically and was a point of divergence for founding member David C. Johnson. "a utilizar una banda como Cannes, la tan inglés, aunque según dijeron en una entrevista era un acrónimo de comunismo, anarchismo ni lismo."
**Early Concerts - Malcolm Mooney's Expressive Performance** : During early concerts where Can gained popularity in the German underground, each show was described as a "sound storm" that could last up to six hours and was a theatrical spectacle. Vocalist Malcolm Mooney was particularly expressive, famously reciting "Upstairs Downstairs" for nearly two hours after the band had finished playing, leaving him exhausted and barely able to move. "Muni era muy expresivo, en un concierto mientras la banda tocaba, el se dedico a recitar Upstairs Downstairs, y así siguió hasta casi 2 horas después de haber acabado el show, Exhaustu y sin apenas poder moverse."
**Composing Method - Long Improvisations and Editing** : Can employed a distinctive compositional process that involved engaging in lengthy improvisation sessions. From the resulting recordings, they would then meticulously cut out the most attractive segments, which could then be augmented with additional arrangements if desired. "Tocaban en largas sesiones de improvisación, para luego de las grabaciones resultantes, recortar los trozos más atractivos a los cuales les podían añadir o no algún arreglo."
**Suzuki Trilogy - Historic Consecutive Masterpieces** : Can is renowned for producing one of the "best consecutive trilogies" in music history, known as the "Suzuki trilogy," which includes "Tago Mago," "Ege Bamyasi," and "Future Days." This trilogy is poetically described as a natural cycle from the darkness of night to the rebirth and beauty of a new day, with each album being so exceptional that it deserved its own stylistic sequel. "Lo que está claro es que caen los de una de las mejores trilogías consecutivas de la historia de la música, de que ha pasado a los anales como la trilogía de Suzuki."
**Dark Side of the Moon Commercial Comparison (1973)** : In 1973, the same year "Future Days" was released, Pink Floyd's "Dark Side of the Moon" also came out. For every hundred copies of Pink Floyd's album sold, only one copy of Can's album was sold, starkly illustrating their differing commercial reach outside Germany. "Se vendió una copia en el disco de los alemanes, por cada haciéndolo en los ingleses."
**Post-Suzuki Vocal Approach** : After Damo Suzuki's departure, the band consciously gave less prominence to vocals. Although they tried out more vocalists, none were deemed suitable to permanently replace Suzuki, leading to vocal duties being shared between Michael Karoli and Irmin Schmidt. "A partir de ahora darían menos protagonismo a la voz, y si bien si que probaron a más vocalistas, nadie les convenció para sustituir a su suquí. El puesto arco dirían entre Carol and Smith."
**Evolution of Sound Post-Trilogy** : Following the "Suzuki trilogy," Can initially continued in the cosmic vein of "Future Days." However, with the introduction of new members, their sound gradually became somewhat "domesticated" over time, although they still produced significant works. "Tras esta trilogía la banda seguirá un tiempo en la línea cósmica de Fichord Days. Pero con la entrada del nuevo osmiembros, su sonido, cierta manera, será domesticando paulatinamente."
**Separation and Reunions** : The band maintained a schedule of releasing one album per year until the early 1980s, when they ultimately separated. They later reunited on a couple of occasions to perform shows and record another album. "La banda seguirá en su línea de disco por año hasta principios de los 80, cuando se separaron. Se reunieron eventualmente en un par de ocasiones para realizar algún show y grabaron un disco."
**Irmin Schmidt - Last Living Original Member** : Following the death of Damo Suzuki, Irmin Schmidt is noted as the only remaining living member of Can's original lineup. "Tras la muerte de damo, el único miembro vivo de la formación original es el teclista Hermin Smith."

# artist - Damo Suzuki

## members
Can

## curiosities
**Background as Street Musician** : Before joining Can, Damo Suzuki was a young, nomadic Japanese troubadour who earned his living by playing music on the streets of various European cities. "un joven trotado a montos japonés que se ganaba la vida tocando en las calles de diferentes ciudades europeas."
**Spontaneous Audition and Recruitment by Can** : Suzuki was discovered by Holger Czukay and Jaki Liebezeit playing outside a Munich cafe and was immediately invited to sing with Can that same evening. He accepted without hesitation, and despite knowing only a couple of chords, he improvised lyrics during a performance that reportedly horrified the audience, causing 500 people to leave, yet profoundly captivated the band, leading to his full-time recruitment. "Le proposeron cantar esa misma noche, a lo que Suzuki aceptó sin pensárselo. Sabiendo tan solo un par de acordes, improvisando la letra, hizo una performance que horroriza al público, dicen que mis 500 personas se fueron del show, pero enamoraron a la banda."
**Unique Vocal Style** : Damo Suzuki's approach to lyrics was characterized by being original and improvised, especially when he incorporated Japanese into Can's songs. He is recognized as one of the most singular and brilliant vocalists of his generation, with his singing on "Sing Swan Song" specifically noted for influencing Kanye West. "son foque original e improvisado para ir al lenguaje de la letra de piedra. ... Damos su suquibrillán este disco como uno de los vocalistas más singular y se brillantes de su generación"
**Departure from Can to become Jehovah's Witness** : After the release of "Future Days," Damo Suzuki, reportedly unhappy with his role as a vocalist and with the band's direction, decided to leave the group and become a Jehovah's Witness. "damos suquibrillán encantado con su faceta de vocalista y con la banda, abandonó el grupo para convertirse en testigo de Jehová."

## curiosities
**Student of Karlheinz Stockhausen** : Irmin Schmidt was a former German student of the renowned contemporary classical composer Karlheinz Stockhausen, an education that significantly shaped his early musical perspective. "un antiguo alumno alemán de Stochhaus en llamado Irmell Smith"
**Transformative New York Trip (1966)** : In 1966, Schmidt traveled to New York, where he immersed himself in the experimental music scene, meeting and spending time with prominent figures such as Steve Reich, La Monte Young, Terry Riley, and even interacting with John Cage, feeling a deep affinity for their work. During his stay at the mythical and bohemian Chelsea Hotel, he also encountered Andy Warhol and his circle, including The Velvet Underground. "En 1966, un antiguo alumno alemán de Stochhaus en llamado Irmell Smith hace un viaje en Nueva York. Allí conoce y pasa tiempo con Steve Reitch, la Montellon, Terry Ridley e incluso llegó a tratar con John Cage. Sentía una gran afinidad hacia esos músicos. Se hospedan el mítico y bohemio del Chelsea, donde conoce Andy Warhol y a su entorno, incluía la velvete ante the Ground."
**"Corruption" and Fascination with Rock** : Schmidt described his New York trip as having "corrupted" him, awakening an immense fascination for the possibilities of rock music. Prior to this, avant-garde music in his academic circle was synonymous with contemporary classical music, but after this experience, he perceived artists like James Brown, The Velvet Underground, and Pink Floyd as embodying a less elitist and more inclusive form of modernity. "Según sus palabras, ese viaje le corrompió, despertando en el una enorme fascinación por las posibilidades del rock. En su círculo, la música de Vanguardia era la música clásica contemporánea. Pero tras ese viaje, dio que el fan de Yes Brown, Walt Rocksico de elico de Pink Floyd representaba en una forma menos elitista y más inclusiva a lo moderno."
**Explanation of Kraut Rock's Distinct Sound** : As Can's keyboardist, Irmin Schmidt offered a unique explanation for the distinct sound of Kraut Rock, attributing it to the different educational environment Germans grew up in. He suggested that sincere expression from this background inevitably leads to a different "feeling" in their music, devoid of nationalist sentiment. He contrasted this with the melodic focus of American and British music, theorizing that music from lands surrounded by water is more influenced by water and air. "El 10.000 ismiz que se encargará de los tecladores de la banda, explicó el Kraut Rock, en la predilección de los alemanes por ese sonido de esta bonita forma. Los alemanes nos hemos educado en un ambiente completamente diferente, de modo que si nos expresamos de forma sincera, es inevitable que tengamos un feeling diferente, pero sin tener ningún tipo de sentimiento nacionalista. La música americana y británica está más relacionada con las melodías. Si estudias la música de todo el mundo, es que en las tierras rodean de agua, la música está influenciada más por el agua y el aire."
**Shared Vocal Duties Post-Suzuki** : After Damo Suzuki's departure from Can, Irmin Schmidt, alongside Michael Karoli, took on vocal responsibilities for the band, contributing to a period of less vocal prominence. "El puesto arco dirían entre Carol and Smith."

# genre - Kraut Rock

## curiosities
**Origin of the Term** : The term "Kraut Rock" was initially used in English media as a somewhat humorous or derisive moniker to refer to the diverse German music scene, with "Kraut" meaning "cabbage." Although many artists of the era disliked the term, it is no longer considered controversial by contemporary artists and has become a recognized genre. "El término Kraut Rock se usó por primera vez en los medios ingleses, como siguió morístico para referirse a la diversa escena alemana. Kraut significa coal, aunque a muchas artistas de la época no les gustaba el término, ya no es considerado controvertido por las artistas actuales."
**Artists' Preferred Term - Cosmiche** : The artists themselves, particularly those within the Kraut Rock movement, preferred to refer to their genre as "Cosmiche," suggesting a broader, more cosmic and less nationalistic identity for their experimental music. "El Kraut Rock, ellos le llaman Cosmiche"
**Defining Characteristics** : Kraut Rock encompasses experimental rock that emerged in Germany in the late 1960s and early 1970s. It is distinguished by its pioneering use of synthesizers and its adoption of recording and production techniques derived from concrete music. Key features include long improvisations and the consistent use of hypnotic, repetitive rhythms, most notably the "motorik" beat—a 4x4 rhythm that creates a sensation of constant movement, akin to driving on a highway. These elements set it apart from Anglo-Saxon psychedelia and the predominant verse-chorus structures of popular music at the time. "engloba el rock experimental que se desarrolló en la alemania finales de los años 60 y principios de los 70, y destaca por el uso de sintetizadores y técnicas de grabación y producción de la música concreta. Largas improvisaciones y el uso de rígmicas y hypnoticas y repetitivas, como el motoric, un ritmo 4x4 que da una sensación de movimiento constante, como el conduciendo por un autopista."
**Representative Bands** : The most representative bands of the Kraut Rock genre include Neu!, Kraftwerk, Faust, Harmonia, Ash Ra Tempel, Popol Vuh, and Amon Düül. "Sus bandas más representativas son Noy, Graffwood, Faust, Daniel Lindrim, Plaster, Ash Rattimpell, Popol Buh, Amondul y Armonia."

# curiosity

**Chelsea Hotel - Bohemian Hub (1966)** : The mythical and bohemian Chelsea Hotel in New York City served as Irmin Schmidt's lodging in 1966, where he encountered cultural figures like Andy Warhol and his circle, including The Velvet Underground, and experienced a pivotal moment that "corrupted" him with the possibilities of rock music. "Se hospedan el mítico y bohemio del Chelsea, donde conoce Andy Warhol y a su entorno, incluía la velvete ante the Ground."
**Schloss Nörvenich (Inner Space) - Can's Recording Studio** : Can established their operational base at Schloss Nörvenich, a castle situated near Cologne, which they affectionately named "Inner Space." This historic location functioned as the recording studio where their initial albums were brought to life. "como va a ser de operaciones instalaron en el Oslo Bernish, un castillo cerca de colonia, al que llamaron inerospays. El ese castillo grabaron sus primeros discos."
**Diverse Musical References for Can** : Can drew inspiration from a wide array of musical references beyond rock, including The Velvet Underground, "Modern Software in Pension" (a likely transcription error for Modern Lovers or Modern Jazz Quartet, but transcribed verbatim), the Jamaican dub productions of Lee "Scratch" Perry, and the free jazz of Albert Ayler. "También han nombrado como referentes a velvete ante the Ground o Modern Software in Pension, a la producción del dabjamaicano de Líperry o alfrichas de Albert Eiler."
**Irmin Schmidt's Theory on German Musical Expression** : Irmin Schmidt explained that Germans, having been educated in a fundamentally different cultural environment, inevitably express a distinct "feeling" in their music, which he emphasized was unrelated to nationalist sentiment. He theorized that while American and British music tends to be more melodic, music from lands surrounded by water, when observed globally, shows a greater influence from water and air. "Los alemanes nos hemos educado en un ambiente completamente diferente, de modo que si nos expresamos de forma sincera, es inevitable que tengamos un feeling diferente, pero sin tener ningún tipo de sentimiento nacionalista. La música americana y británica está más relacionada con las melodías. Si estudias la música de todo el mundo, es que en las tierras rodean de agua, la música está influenciada más por el agua y el aire."
**Can's Far-Reaching Influence** : Can's influence extended to a vast and diverse array of artists across different genres, including major figures like David Bowie, Swans, PiL, Tortoise, Superé Ubu, as well as electronic music producers and hip hop artists. Additionally, the narrator personally notes their impact on bands like Sonic Youth, Dinosaur Jr., Talking Heads, Massive Attack, The Stone Roses, Happy Mondays, The Fall, Spacemen 3, The Orb, Soulwax, Bullion & Vegedream, Stereolab, Portishead, and Neil Young. "Desde David Bowie a Swans, pasando por este rola, Pill, Torto y Soperé Ubu. ... Y ahora cuando vuelvo a ser incónde mi cerebro para recordar esas bandas tocadas por Can, veo a Sonic Youth, de sus amberechén, tarquing heads, masifatac, eston roses, Happy Mondays, de fall, specimen 3, de inhibición, suelmars, estereolab, búlico y vegetal y mítate, radio fed, muro, porteshead, ni el mar."
