## curiosities
**Decision to End Johnny Rotten Persona (January 14, 1978)** : John Lydon, previously known as Johnny Rotten and the primary face of punk, decided to "kill his character" at the Sex Pistols' last concert of their American tour, which also became their final show together. He was reportedly tired of playing the same accelerated three-chord Chuck Berry style and felt music could be more. "Los expuestos, la banda más grande del momento, acababa de perder a su vocalista."
**Tired of Manipulation and Fakery** : John Lydon was weary of Malcolm McLaren's manipulation to maintain the image of a destructive lunatic and tired of pretending to be a lout. In reality, he was a secret fan of English literature, passionate about Oscar Wilde, and a music lover with a very eclectic taste. "John y esta barto de que maglara el demanipular a mantener esa imagen del unático destructivo, esta barto de fingir que era un patán."
**Eclectic Musical Tastes Revealed (1977)** : In 1977, shortly after the Sex Pistols released "God Save the Queen," John Lydon was interviewed by a London radio station to play his favorite songs on a special program called "The Punk and His Music." While everyone expected the wildest punk, he, the supposed "Antichrist of punk," played reggae, Krautrock bands like Can, Tim Buckley ballads, and Captain Beefheart's wild hip-hop experimentation. This music, despised by the punk scene at the time, later became a significant reference for post-punk. "Todo el mundo esperaba escuchar el punk más albaje, pero en su lugar, el supuesto anticristo del punk, un setem más de Rigi, de bandas de Krauro como Cannes, Valadas de Tim Buckley, o la loquísima experimentación hip-hid de Captain Vithhart."
**Post-Sex Pistols Escape to Jamaica** : After the Sex Pistols, Rotten reclaimed his birth name, John Lydon, and his first decision was to escape. He packed his bags and spent time in Jamaica, where he mingled with prominent dub artists. This Jamaican genre, based on echo, empty space, and heavy bass, would become one of the many genres defining post-punk. "Su primera decisión fue escapar, hizo las malitas y pasó una temporada en Jamaica, donde se codeó con los grandes totes del lab."
**Views on Disco Music (Metal Box Era)** : During interviews for *Metal Box*, John Lydon began stating that the only contemporary music he liked was disco, a declaration considered absolute heresy by punk fans who still followed him. He argued that disco was functional, useful, and free from the "stupidities of conventional rock." "Laiton empezó a decirle a la entrevista que la única música contemporánea que le gustaba era la música disco."
**Critique of Punk Music (Metal Box Era)** : John Lydon contended that punk, despite its revolutionary origins, had become "something fascist." He championed Public Image Ltd as an entity that broke away from such limitations. "decía que el punk, en su origen, género revolucionario, se había convertido en algo fascista."
**Belief in No Future (Pre-Metal Box)** : Prior to *Metal Box*, Johnny Rotten famously chanted that there was "no future," reflecting the punk ethos. However, the subsequent release of *Metal Box* demonstrated that there was indeed a significant future for music, proving his earlier statement wrong. "Johnny Rote encantaba que no había futuro, pero como hemos visto, había mucho futuro, y poco después lo demostró con Metalbox."

## albums
**First Issue (1978) - 'Versyshu' (Debut Album)** : Public Image Ltd launched their first album in 1978, referred to in the transcript as "Versyshu," generating high expectations as it was Lydon's new band and he was still considered the face of punk by many. However, its design was far from punk, with members posing like a Vogue photoshoot. Its sound also surprised many, as it didn't completely break with punk rock tradition but included tracks with strong dub and dance music influences. "En 1978 lanzaron su primer disco, Versyshu."
**Metal Box (1979) - Unconventional Packaging** : Released in the winter of 1979, *Metal Box* was presented not as a conventional record but as a metallic 16mm film canister, devoid of band photos. This weighty, hard-to-store, and more expensive object was intentionally "hostile to the consumer," a deliberate choice by John Lydon. "Se encontraron con esto, una caja metálica de película de 16 milímetros, sin fotos de la banda, que pesaban montón, que era incómoda de almacenar en las estanterías y para colmo era mucho más caro que un disco normal."
**Metal Box (1979) - Symbol of Rupture** : The metallic box packaging of *Metal Box* served as a symbol of a radical break from the punk scene that had dominated two years prior. Its release marked the birth of something new, rejecting punk's rules, limits, and dogmas, and forging a new path for music. "esta caja metálica no era solo una lecdota, sino que era un símbolo, el símbolo de una ruptura con una escena que había arrasado dos años antes, el punk."
**Metal Box (1979) - Musical Masterpiece** : *Metal Box* is considered a masterpiece and one of the biggest ruptures in music history. It featured incredible bass lines from Jah Wobble, acting as both rhythmic pulse and melodic motif, creating physical and heavy lows described as "the human heartbeat." Keith Levene, freed from traditional rhythm and melody duties, played dislocatedly, producing crystalline and kaleidoscopic sounds. "Y ahora sí, hablemos de esta obra maestra, Metalbox."
**Metal Box (1979) - Studio as Instrument** : For *Metal Box*, Public Image Ltd fully embraced the methodologies of disco and Jamaican dub, using the recording studio as an instrument in itself. They radically processed sounds, employed echo chambers, and incorporated immense empty spaces, treating silence as equally important as noise. "Abrazaron del todo la metodología de la música disco y del tapjamaicano, o sea que usaban el estudio de grabación como si fueran instrumentos más."
**Flowers of Romance (1981) - More Radical** : Following *Metal Box*, Public Image Ltd released *Flowers of Romance*, an album described as even more radical than its predecessor. Subsequent albums by the band became somewhat more conventional. "Luego habían flowed esos romance, un disco así que ve más radical que Metalbox."

## songs
**"Fodderstompf" (1978) - The 'Ponderstown' Joke Track** : This rhythmic and repetitive song, referred to by the transcript as "Ponderstown," was created as a joke to extend the album and fulfill contractual obligations with Virgin. It was inspired by Donna Summer's "I Feel Love" and is notably one of their most "rock tourist" tracks, showcasing potent dub and dance music influences. Despite being a joke, its hypnotic dub-funk bassline and characteristic drum sound laid the rhythmic foundations for their subsequent album, *Metal Box*. "Esta especie de broma estaba inspirada en el afil love de Don Asamer, ya en corrigenariamente a eso, una broma, es con diferencia su tema más rock turista, es la que tiene la influencia del dub y la música de baile más latente."

## curiosities
**Conception as a "Communications Company"** : More than just a band, John Lydon presented Public Image Ltd as a "communications company," an anti-rock entity designed to dismantle everything. "Más que una banda, Leidon lo presentó como una compañía de comunicaciones, una entidad anti-rock diseñada para demodarlo todo."
**Pioneering New Musical Paths** : Public Image Ltd was at the forefront of opening interesting new paths for music, although they were not alone. They are seen as pioneers in the break from punk, mainly due to their strong connections to the original punk scene. "PIL estaba abriendo un camino muy interesante para la música, pero no estaban solos."
**Later Albums More Conventional** : After the experimental *Metal Box* and *Flowers of Romance*, Public Image Ltd's subsequent albums became "a bit more conventional." "sus siguientes discos, ya serán un poco más convencionales."

# genre - Punk

## curiosities
**Rejection by Post-Punk** : *Metal Box* by Public Image Ltd symbolized a profound break from the punk scene, rejecting its limits and dogmas to forge new musical paths. "Metalbox suponía el nacimiento de algo nuevo, algo que nunca java en las reglas del punk, que rechazaba sus límites y sus dogmas"
**Traditional Faction (1978)** : In 1978, punk was divided, with a traditional faction largely composed of working-class individuals. They believed music should be noisy, stripped of artistic pretensions, based on accelerated rock and roll, and function as a "megaphone for the street." This branch later led to scenes like hardcore and the Oi! movement. "por un lado estaban los pános más tradicionales, gente por lo general de clase obrera, que creía que la música debía ser ruidosa y estar casi despojada de pretensiones artísticas."
**Avant-garde Faction (1978)** : In 1978, another faction of punk emerged, described as "avant-garde." They believed the punk revolution could achieve more than just four-chord accelerated rock and roll songs, emphasizing that "form could be much more radical and revolutionary than the message." Unlike traditional punks who rejected the past, avant-garde punks looked to previous bands for inspiration. "Pero por otro lado, había otra facción ahora que vamos a llamar van guardista. Ellos me llan que la revolución punk podía servir para mucho más que hacer canciones con cuatro cordes acelerados de rock and roll."
**Impact on Music Industry** : The punk revolution transformed the music industry, and its commercial success in the UK and USA prompted major record labels to sign riskier bands. This led to the formation of independent labels with non-commercial aims. "Tuvo que venir la revolución punk para poner la industria patas arriba."

# genre - Post-punk

## curiosities
**Influences from Pre-Punk Avant-garde** : Post-punk drew heavily from music that was previously disliked by the punk scene, including reggae, Krautrock, Tim Buckley ballads, and Captain Beefheart's experimentation. "Música codiaba la escena punk, pero que poco después, serían una gran referencia para el post-punk."
**Not Just Dark and Gothic** : Post-punk is more than just a sound or a genre; it was not solely dark and gothic music, nor merely what followed punk. It represented "the greatest period of openness and creativity in music history." "Es por eso que el Pospang no es solo un sonido ni un genero, no fue solo música oscura y gotica... sino que supuso nada a la setra parte de mayor apertura y creatividad de la historia de la música."
**Defining Legacy** : Post-punk left a lasting legacy that defined electronic music, alternative music, and independent music for the following three decades, effectively completing the revolution initiated by punk. "El Pospang dejó un legado que definió la música electrónica, la música alternativa y la música independiente que vendría en las tres siguientes décadas."

# curiosity
