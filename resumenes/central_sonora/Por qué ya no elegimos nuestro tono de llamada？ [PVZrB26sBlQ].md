# label - Record Labels

## curiosities
**Control of the Music Industry - 1990s** : In the 1990s, a handful of record labels controlled the entire music industry, which was described as a "crystal palace." Unlike today's easy access to music online, music then was a physical object, primarily CDs. These labels held the "keys to the kingdom" and were unwilling to relinquish their control. "Un puñado de discográficas lo controlaban todo."
**Crisis due to Piracy - 2000s** : In parallel with the ringtone boom, the record industry faced its biggest crisis due to widespread piracy via platforms like Napster, Emule, and Kazaa. Instead of adapting to the changing landscape, labels responded with lawsuits, restrictions, and moralistic campaigns claiming "piracy killed music," clinging to an outdated model with abusive pricing. "En Paladero a esto la industria discográfica estaba viviendo la mayor crisis de su historia causa de la piratería."

# instrument - Mobile Phone

## curiosities
**Early Models - Status Symbol** : The first mobile phones were perceived as bricks with enormous batteries, primarily used for work, and were seen as a status symbol associated with executives. This perception began to change as brands like Motorola and Nokia commercialized smaller phones. "Esos primeros teléfonos eran ladrillos con baterías enormes que se usaban para trabajar, y se veían como un simbolo de estatus que se asociaban a los efectudivos."
**Nokia 2110 (1994) - Ringtone Pioneer** : In 1994, the Nokia 2110 introduced a revolutionary feature: several selectable melodies as phone tones, including "Type 7" (an extract of Francisco Tárrega's "Gran Vals"), which would later become the "Nokia Tune." This marked the beginning of technological personalization and transformed the phone from an anonymous tool into an extension of personality. "Pero en 1994, el Nokia 21.10 incluyó varias melodías en su teléfono que podías seleccionar como tono."
**Nokia 3210 (1999) - Integrated Composer** : Launched in 1999, the Nokia 3210, along with the later Nokia 3310 (2000), included a crucial personalization tool: an integrated composer. This allowed users to create their own ringtones, utilizing a protocol that enabled the exchange of small data packets, such as musical scores, via SMS. "El Nokia 32.10 lanzado en 1999 y posteriormente el indestructible 33.10 lanzado en el año 2000, incluyeron una herramienta clave en esta personalización, un compositor integrado que se permitía hacer tu propio tono de llamada."
**Nokia 3310 (2000) - Integrated Composer** : The "indestructible" Nokia 3310, launched in 2000, like its predecessor the 3210, featured an integrated composer that allowed users to create custom ringtones. This phone facilitated the exchange of musical scores via SMS, laying the groundwork for the commercial ringtone industry. "El Nokia 32.10 lanzado en 1999 y posteriormente el indestructible 33.10 lanzado en el año 2000, incluyeron una herramienta clave en esta personalización, un compositor integrado que se permitía hacer tu propio tono de llamada."
**Polyphonic Sound Enhancement - Mid-2000s** : With the advent of polyphonic ringtones, mobile phones significantly improved their sound chips. Unlike earlier monophonic phones that could only play one channel, these new devices could play several channels simultaneously, making music sound "something more real" and greatly amplifying the ringtone phenomenon. "Los teléfonos empezaran a mejorar su chid de sonido. Y ahora podían hacer sonal varios canales y multanios a diferencia de los monofónicos, que solo tenían uno."
**MP3 Playback and Smartphones - End of Ringtone Era** : The ability of phones to reproduce MP3 files, combined with the arrival of the first smartphones offering superior sound quality and access to a world of MP3s, marked the end of the dedicated ringtone industry. Despite expectations for extreme personalization to soar, the opposite occurred. "Y tras todo esto, los teléfonos empezaron a reproducir a MP3."
**Modern Default Ringtones - Apple Marimba, Xiaomi Melody** : In the smartphone era, despite having infinite access to millions of songs in perfect quality, the vast majority of users now leave their phones on the factory-default ringtone. Examples include Apple's Marimba and Xiaomi's melody, which have become anonymous and functional sounds, reflecting a paradoxical shift away from active musical personalization. "Las marínvas de Apple. La melodía de Xiaomi. No, más universal de todos."

# curiosity

**Ringtone Industry Emergence (Late 1990s - 2000s)** : The ringtone industry emerged in the late 1990s and boomed throughout the 2000s, reaching an annual turnover of 700 million euros in Spain alone, surpassing single sales. This industry, characterized by its "unethical practices," evolved from simple monophonic melodies to complex polyphonic sounds and eventually to hyper-commercialized noises. "Una industria que había desaparecido, una que tenía prácticas, digamos, poco éticas, y que llegó a factorar 700 millones de euros al año, superando incluso la venta de singles."
**Youth Identity Expression (1990s)** : Before social media, young people in the 1990s expressed their identity and tastes by filling folders with stickers, using magazine cutouts of bands, wearing band t-shirts, and decorating rooms with posters of popular artists. This era saw the "golden age" of urban tribes, where music, often on CDs, played a central role in personal expression. "Durante los años 90 se vivió una explosión de identidad, la gente joven quería mostrar en todo momento sus gustos y su forma de ser."
**2G Technology for Mobile Phones** : Motorola and Nokia commercialized increasingly smaller phones and introduced 2G technology. This advancement, while not directly sound-related initially, paved the way for the data capabilities that would enable ringtone personalization via SMS. "E introdujeron una tecnología que iba a reulocinar el mercado, el 2G."
**SMS Protocol for Ringtone Distribution** : Nokia phones, particularly the 3210 and 3310, introduced a protocol allowing small data packets to be changed within an SMS message. This groundbreaking technology enabled users to send and receive musical scores for songs, directly leading to the creation of the first digital catalogs for ringtones, predating Spotify and iTunes. "Estos teléfonos introdujeron un protocolo que permitían cambiar pequeños paquetes de datos dentro de un SMS, por ejemplo, la partintura de una de esas canciones que podías hacer."
**Early Digital Catalogs and Companies** : The ability to send ringtones via SMS sparked a "fever," leading to the overnight emergence of companies like Mobileys, Top, Proofset, and Hamba. These companies continuously bombarded television with advertisements and filled magazine pages with wallpapers and codes for popular songs, forming the first digital music catalogs. "Y así es como en hacieron los primeros catalogos digitales de la historia, mucho antes de Spotify y iTunes."
**Polyphonic Ringtones - Qualitative Leap** : The introduction of polyphonic ringtones marked a significant qualitative leap in mobile phone personalization. Recognizing users' desire for richer sound, phones improved their sound chips to play multiple channels simultaneously, making music sound "more real" and causing the ringtone phenomenon to explode. "Con la llegada de los politonos se dio un salto cualitativo, viendo que esta personalización sonora era algo que importaba a los usuarios."
**Hyper-Commercialization of Noise** : By the mid-2000s, ringtones ceased to be a form of personal expression and transformed into a commercial spectacle. People began paying for the most annoying tones rather than popular songs, leading to the hyper-commercialization of noise. This market generated up to 700 million euros annually in Spain alone. "Los tonos ya habían dejado de ser una expresión personal. Y se habían convertido en un espectáculo comercial."
**Piracy Era - Napster, Emule, Kazaa** : Around the same time as the ringtone boom, the music industry faced its biggest crisis due to widespread piracy through platforms like Napster, Emule, and Kazaa. Users could download not just albums but entire discographies, fostering a sense of "infinite abundance" and allowing discovery of music not played on radio, challenging the traditional music industry model. "Trepente todo el mundo se bajo de Napster, Emule o Caza. Ya no solo discos, sino discografías completas de extremo duro, de Nirvana o de Ophespring."
**Decline of Ringtone Companies (Late 2000s)** : By the late 2000s, most polyphonic ringtone companies began to disappear. This decline was attributed to market saturation, the proliferation of deceptive applications (promising X-ray vision or flashlight functions but delivering blank screens), and problematic subscription services that sent unsolicited, charged SMS with songs and made cancellations extremely difficult. "A finales de la década de los 2000, la mayoría de las empresas de polito nos empezaron a desaparecer."
**Bluetooth - The Final Blow to Ringtone Companies** : The advent of technologies like Bluetooth, which allowed free transfer of songs between devices, delivered the final blow to the struggling ringtone companies. This eliminated the need to pay for ringtones, further eroding their business model. "Finalmente, la llegada de tecnologías como el Bluetooth, que permitía pasar canciones entre dispositivos gratuitamente, acabaron de enterrar estas empresas."
**Paradox of Abundance (Post-Smartphone Era)** : With the arrival of smartphones offering superior sound quality and infinite access to MP3s, it was expected that extreme personalization would soar; however, the opposite occurred. Whereas in the cassette era, despite poor quality and high cost, people invested in personalized ringtones as a public declaration of identity, today, with abundant perfect quality music, most users stick to default ringtones. "El leer a de las casetas, cuando el sonido era de mala calidad y el proceso era caro y engorroso, todos nos esforzábamos en conseguir nuestro tono deseado."
**Homogenization of Music Consumption** : The paradox of abundance extends to general music consumption. Before the internet and algorithms, scarcity motivated people to actively seek and discover new music, creating a personal connection with each album. Today, despite having infinite music, algorithms tend to reaffirm existing tastes, leading to less variety in listening habits and the disappearance of urban tribes, making music consumption more homogeneous. "Este paisaje de abundancia infinita que prometía diversidad. Paradojecamente, la ha vuelto todo más homogéneo."
