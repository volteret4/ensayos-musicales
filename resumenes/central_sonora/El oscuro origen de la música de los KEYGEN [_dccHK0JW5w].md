# artist - Castello Varsky

## curiosities
**Ultimate Soundtracker Creator (1967)** : Castello Varsky created the Ultimate Soundtracker in 1967 for the Commodore Amiga, which was the first tracker software and only allowed four simultaneous audio channels. He also created the .mod format, which became the standard file format for piracy. The tracker did not export audio files like MP3s, but instead saved instructions and a "little box with loose samples" in a .mod file, which was then executed in real-time by the device. "El primer track fue el Ultimate Soundtracker, creado por Castelo Varsky en 1967 para el comodore amiga, que solo permitía cuatro canales de audios simultáneos. También creó el formato rey de la periatería, el punto mod."

# curiosity

**Music Sharing as Infrabalorated Love** : The cultural analysis of historical scenes suggests that, in the margins, new aesthetics and forms of expression emerge where sharing music is a fundamental, undervalued form of love. This perspective drives the exploration of the origins of musical scenes. "compartir música siempre ha sido, en el fondo, una forma de amor infrabalorada."
**1980s Youth Cultures** : The 1980s were a fertile decade for various youth subcultures, including the Goth scene, the Blitzkids, Hip Hop, and the explosion of Acid House. Additionally, other cultures like graffiti and skate also developed their unique soundtracks, showcasing a diverse musical and social landscape. "Si miramos atrás en los años 80, frontal vez en la década más fue la reciente para estas tribus."
**Digital Piracy Scene Emergence** : The 1980s also witnessed the rise of a more hermetic, illegal, and persecuted culture than any rave: the digital piracy scene. This scene, where technology and art merged, gave birth to the aesthetic and sound of digital piracy, differing from street-originated music scenes by its reliance on computing and code. "Hubo otra cultura en esa misma década de la cual que se enadía a hablar? Una escena mucho más hermética, ilegal y perseguida que cualquier rave."
**Video Game Copying Culture** : In the 1980s, before PCs were common, microcomputers like the ZX Spectrum and Commodore 64 used cassette tapes and floppy disks. It was common for young people in schools to exchange copies of games, often with written lists of what they had and what they wanted, much like trading cards. "Eran los colegios donde se intercambiaban estos juegos. Los chavales iban con listas escritas de que juegos querían y que juegos tenían para intercambiar. Vamos como si fueran cromos."
**Anti-Copy Systems** : The video game industry, displeased with rampant game copying, launched aggressive ad campaigns in specialized magazines to equate copying with theft. When ethical appeals failed, companies implemented technological anti-copy systems that detected duplicates, causing copied games to fail to load. "Las compañías empezaron en colorir sistemas anticópia que detectaban, ser software original, o en duplicado casero. Ahora, si el usuario grababa un juego, directamente ya no cargaba."
**The Cracker Figure** : The implementation of anti-copy systems led to the emergence of the "Cracker" figure. These talented individuals found ways to break copy protections, often signing their cracked software with an alias (e.g., "Cracked By") and even hacking game high-score tables to display their names, seeking prestige and recognition from peers rather than money. "Y es así como nación a nueva figura, el Cracker. De repente, empezamos a jugar a videojuegos que venían firmados. Antes de empezar la partida, en la pantalla de inicio del juego, veía su mensaje de Cracker By, seguido de un alias."
**Hacker Panic and Early Cyber Laws** : The rise of crackers coincided with a "hacker panic," where media and governments became alarmed by general news of teenagers penetrating banks, stealing military secrets, or collapsing networks. Cyberpunk literature and cinema fueled the myth of the dangerous, solitary computer criminal, leading governments to pass the first laws against computer crime. "Todo esto coincidó con la explosión del panico hacker. Los medios y los gobiernos empezaron a alarmarse."
**Cracktros - Digital Craftsmanship** : Cracktros were specific intro screens created by crackers, displayed before the pirated software launched. They featured signatures, animations, color effects, moving texts, greetings, pirate manifestos, threats to software companies, and distinctive music. Limited to less than a megabit on a floppy disk or cassette, cracktros were meticulously hand-coded, executing in real-time, often surpassing the technical quality of the games they accompanied. "Así nacieron las Crackers, más conocidas como Crackers. Ahora el Cracker ya no se lo competía por ver quién lo grabas al saltarse al sistema de copiantes, sino por ver quién tenía la mejor crack tro."
**Cracker Group Structure** : Crackers organized into groups (crews) with highly defined roles, similar to graffiti culture. The "cracker" focused solely on breaking protections, the "coder" handled visual effects for the intro, the "swapper" managed physical distribution and logistics, and the "musician" created the soundtrack. "Al igual que la cultura del Cigoo, los crackers organizaban en una especie de críos, en grupos con roles muy definidos."
**Cracker Codes of Honor** : The cracker scene operated under strict codes of honor. If a group signed a cracktro, no one else could modify or erase that intro, considering it sacrilege. Their motivation was prestige and fame, not money. Those who tried to sell pirated copies were derogatorily called "lamers." "Y el igual que la cultura del graffiti, esta cena tenía códigos de honor muy estrictos. Si un grupo enfermaba un Crack, nadie podía borrar ese intro. Eso era considerado un sacre elección."
**"Lamer" Term Origin** : The derogatory term "lamer," used to describe someone uncool or inauthentic, was adopted directly from skater culture and applied within the cracker scene to individuals who attempted to sell pirated copies for profit, contrary to the scene's ethos of prestige and anti-system romanticism. "De hecho, a quien intentaba vender las copias pirateadas se le llamaba la Imer. Un término de expectivos sacado directamente de la cultura Skater para definir a alguien cu tre o poca auténtico."
**Cracker Scene Communication** : The cracker scene had its own communication channels, similar to punk rock. These included paper fanzines (like "Alemania Ilegal" or "Iguana" from Britain) and digital magazines on floppy disks called "Diskmags" (such as "Sex and Crime"), fostering a sense of community and information exchange. "Al igual que la escena Pan, Correiz, tenía sus propios canales de comunicación. Elitaban fancines de papel, como la Alemania ilegal, o el británico iguana, incluso revistas digitales en discuet, las llamadas Dismarks, como Sex and Crime."
**Demoscene Origin from Piracy Scene** : Towards the end of the 1980s, as anti-piracy laws tightened and cracking became dangerous, many digital artists from the cracker scene decided to abandon piracy. Driven by legal pressures or a growing artistic interest, they began experimenting with the creative possibilities of microcomputers and early PCs, leading to the birth of the Demoscene. "Pero a finales de los 80, las leyes empezaron a endurecerse. La piratería dejó de ser una travisura y se empezó a convertir en un delito con penas de cárcel. Ser Cracker se estaba volviendo peligroso."
**Demoscene - Pushing Hardware Limits** : The Demoscene emerged as a movement where artists created "demos" – visual pieces that pushed the limits of microcomputers and early PCs, often outperforming commercial games technically. This movement was deliberately named "Demoscene" to distance itself from the criminal connotations of the cracker scene. "A estas piezas les llamaron de Mous, y para separarse de la connotación criminal de la escena del crack, a este nuevo movimiento lo votizaron como de Moussin."
**Demoscene Competitions and Innovation** : Demoscene fairs, like Assembly in Finland and Revision in Germany, became massive events where groups competed in categories such as "less than 64 kilobytes" to create the best demos and intros. This intense competition among groups like The Black Lotus, Fairlight, and The Future Crew led to significant innovations in animation and video game technology worldwide. "los lupos que participaban, muchos de ellos con vínculos aún con la cultura Cracker, competían por ver quienes creaban la mejor intro en distintas categorías, siendo una de las más populares la de menos de 64 kilobytes."
**Demoscene Legacy in Video Games** : Many achievements seen in modern video games originated from demoscene competitions. Numerous demoscene artists and hackers went on to found important companies in the video game industry, especially in Sweden and Finland, including DICE, Remedy Entertainment, IO Interactive, and Housemarque. "muchos de los logros que damos presentados en los videojuegos nacieron en estas competiciones. De hecho, muchos de estos artistas y hasta hackers acabaron fondando importantes empresas de la industria del videojuego, especialmente en suécia y finlandia."
**Tracker Music - Code-Based Soundtrack** : Tracker music became the official anthem of digital piracy, created with code to fit within the extremely limited file sizes shared alongside cracked software and games. Unlike traditional sheet music, tracker music was conceived by imagining a programmer placing events on a hexadecimal grid, storing instructions and samples in formats like .mod, which were executed in real-time. "Una música hecha con código que se iba a convertir en el imno oficial de la periatería hasta nuestros días."
**Tracker Music Technical Characteristics** : Due to memory and channel limitations (e.g., four simultaneous audio channels on the Amiga), tracker music developed distinctive techniques. Fast arpeggios were used to simulate synthetic chords on a single channel, deceiving the ear into perceiving multiple notes. Repeating a note at different volumes simulated a delay effect, becoming a signature of the genre and these scenes.
