## History of Recording Technology & Copyright

event @Thomas Edison **Phonograph Patent Filing (1877)** : Thomas Edison filed a patent for his "talking machine" (phonograph) on December 24, 1877, enabling the first capture, playback, storage, sharing, and analysis of audio.
curiosity @Thomas Edison **Initial Phonograph Uses** : Thomas Edison's original list of uses for the phonograph placed "reproduction of music" far down the list, prioritizing uses like preserving speeches, audio letters, dictation, talking clocks, telephone machines, and remote learning.
curiosity **Phonograph and Early Copyright Issues** : The introduction of the phonograph and Edison cylinders highlighted a gap in existing copyright laws, which were designed for the printed word, leading to the unauthorized recording and distribution of audio.
event **Gutenberg Printing Press (1457)** : Johann Gutenberg invented the printing press, offering the first book around 1457, which facilitated widespread book availability but also raised new questions about copying rights.
event **First Copyright Laws (Late 15th/Early 16th Century)** : The invention of the printing press led to the creation of the first copyright laws in the late 15th and early 16th centuries to define rights for copying intellectual property.
curiosity @William Shakespeare **Thomas Pavier Piracy** : William Shakespeare's works were pirated by printer Thomas Pavier, who sold unauthorized copies and even early drafts ("unfinished demos") without compensating the author.
event **English Copyright Law (1662)** : A major copyright law was passed in England in 1662, initially focused on the printed word.
event **Sheet Music Copyright (Late 18th Century)** : Sheet music came under copyright protection in the late 18th century.
curiosity **Copyright Law Unprepared for Phonograph** : Existing copyright law, primarily designed for the printed word and later sheet music, was "completely unprepared" for the advent of Thomas Edison's talking machine (phonograph) about a hundred years after sheet music copyright began.
curiosity **Sound Recording Copyright Recognition (Early)** : Written was the first country to recognize that sound recordings needed copyright protection.
event **US Sound Recording Copyright (1972)** : The United States did not properly address copyright protection for sound recordings until 1972.
curiosity **Public Domain Discrepancy (Sheet Music vs. Recording)** : A unique situation arose where a 1922 song's sheet music was in the public domain, but an audio recording of that same music from 1922 did not enter the public domain until 2022.
curiosity **Recording Contracts (Early 20th Century)** : Recording contracts, which emerged in the early 20th century, grant record labels exclusive rights to an artist's creations for marketing and sales, with profits shared.

## Bootlegging Culture & Definitions

genre **Bootlegging Industry Emergence** : The inadequacy of early copyright laws for audio recordings facilitated the birth of an unauthorized music industry, working in the shadows of legitimate record labels, music publishers, and performing rights organizations.
genre **Bootlegs - Evolution of Unauthorized Recordings** : Bootlegs, originally secretly recorded Edison phonograph cylinders, evolved to include reel-to-reel tape machines, unauthorized vinyl records, cassettes, CDs, and digital files, traded freely online.
curiosity **Bootlegs - Definition and Origin** : "Bootlegs" is the original name for unauthorized recordings, which began with secretly recorded Edison phonograph cylinders and evolved through various formats.
curiosity **Origin of "Bootleg" Term** : The term "bootleg" originated during the Prohibition era (early 20th century) when illegal alcohol was smuggled in curved flasks hidden in tall boots, and later applied to anything illegally manufactured, distributed, and sold, including music.
curiosity **Bootleg vs. Counterfeit Recordings** : Bootleg recordings differ from counterfeit or pirated albums; counterfeits are manufactured to look indistinguishable from official releases but are produced illegally to avoid royalties and undercut legitimate records.
curiosity **Organized Crime and Pirated Records** : Pirated records were a significant business for the mob in America for decades, particularly for stocking mob-controlled jukeboxes in the 1940s and 1950s.
curiosity **Bootlegging as Copyright Violation** : Bootlegging is defined as a third party violating an artist's recording contract by distributing and selling their music for profit without legal right, failing to compensate the artist or record label.
genre **Audience Recording (Bootleg Type)** : An "audience recording" is a type of bootleg made by someone in the audience with a microphone, capturing the raw, often low-fidelity sound and energy of a live gig.
genre **Soundboard Recording (Bootleg Type)** : A "soundboard recording" is a type of bootleg made by plugging a recording device directly into the PA system's soundboard, providing a clean capture of the on-stage performance without overpowering audience noise.
genre **Radio Broadcast Recording (Bootleg Type)** : From the 1970s to the late 1990s, another type of bootleg involved recordings of concerts routinely broadcast on FM Rock radio stations, including live studio performances, live feeds from venues, or professionally engineered distributed recordings.

## Early Bootlegging Figures

member @Lionel Mapelson **Godfather of Bootlegging** : Lionel Mapelson, the official librarian at the Metropolitan Opera in New York, is considered the "godfather of all bootlegging" and music piracy.
member @Lionel Mapelson **Metropolitan Opera Librarian** : Lionel Mapelson secured a job as the official librarian at the Metropolitan Opera in New York in the late 1800s.
member @Lionel Mapelson **Phonograph Obsession (1900)** : Lionel Mapelson documented his phonograph obsession in his diary on March 22, 1900, stating he was "a phonograph maniac always making or buying records."
curiosity @Lionel Mapelson **Met Opera Recording Technique** : Lionel Mapelson recorded Metropolitan Opera performances from a catwalk 40 feet above the stage, using his Batini phonograph.
curiosity @Lionel Mapelson **Cylinder Erasing Technique** : Lionel Mapelson would erase subpar recordings from wax cylinders by shaving off the grooves, allowing for reuse.
curiosity @Lionel Mapelson **Dispute Over Legality of Recordings** : There is historical dispute about whether Lionel Mapelson secretly made and sold his opera recordings or if he obtained permission from the Met and its musicians.
song @Lionel Mapelson **Tosca Recording (1903)** : Lionel Mapelson recorded a performance of the opera *Tosca* in 1903.
curiosity @Lionel Mapelson **Cylinder Swapping Technique** : To record an entire opera like *Tosca*, Lionel Mapelson had to manually swap out wax cylinders every two minutes.
curiosity @Lionel Mapelson **Number of Cylinder Recordings** : Lionel Mapelson made approximately 120 fragile cylinder recordings over a three-year period.
curiosity @Lionel Mapelson **Cessation of Recording Activity** : Lionel Mapelson stopped making recordings after about three years, likely because the Metropolitan Opera discovered his activities and wanted to produce their own official recordings.
member @Lionel Mapelson **Librarian Tenure** : Lionel Mapelson served as the Metropolitan Opera librarian for 52 years.
member @Lionel Mapelson **Death Date (1937)** : Lionel Mapelson died of a heart attack on December 21, 1937.
album @Lionel Mapelson **Mapelson Cylinders (Collection)** : A collection of Lionel Mapelson's historical recordings, known as "The Mapelson Cylinders," features about 100 different performances.
curiosity @Lionel Mapelson **Cylinder Conversion to 78 RPM (1939)** : In 1939, many of Lionel Mapelson's original cylinder recordings began to be converted into 10-inch 78 RPM records.

## Magnetic Tape & Cassette Era

instrument @Lionel Mapelson **Batini Phonograph** : Lionel Mapelson used a portable Batini phonograph, a brand utilizing Edison's technology, capable of recording up to two minutes of audio on wax cylinders.
instrument **Magnetic Tape and Reel-to-Reel Recorder** : Magnetic tape and reel-to-reel recorders were invented by the Nazis in the 1930s by a company that later manufactured poison gas, revolutionizing audio capture.
curiosity **WWII Capture of Tape Technology** : Magnetic tape technology was captured by the American Army at the end of World War II and brought to the US by Major Jack Mullin.
member @Major Jack Mullin **Ampex Founder** : Major Jack Mullin created the company Ampex after bringing magnetic tape technology from Germany to the United States.
artist @Bing Crosby **Magnetic Tape Adoption** : Major Jack Mullin sold the idea of recording to magnetic tape to Bing Crosby for use in his radio shows, leading to its widespread adoption in recording studios.
genre **Jazz Live Recording Culture (1930s-on)** : Jazz fans, frustrated by record labels' inability to meet demand for live recordings, began making their own reel-to-reel recordings in clubs as early as the 1930s.
curiosity **Early Jazz Tapers as Archivists** : Early jazz fans who made live reel-to-reel recordings were generally viewed as archivists, not bootleggers or pirates, helping to preserve jazz history.
instrument **Compact Cassette (1963)** : Philips, a Dutch electronics company, introduced the compact cassette in 1963, an invention led by Leo Oatens, miniaturizing reel-to-reel tape into a portable plastic shell, 4x2.5x0.5 inches.
curiosity **Compact Cassette and Audience Recordings** : The compact cassette's small size and portability made it ideal for sneaking into shows to make audience recordings.
member @Leo Oatens **Compact Cassette Inventor** : Leo Oatens led the team at Philips that invented the compact cassette in 1963.
genre **Tape Trader Culture** : The compact cassette facilitated the emergence of "tape trader culture," pre-internet underground networks of music fans who traded bootlegs via mail and print ads on a "pay-it-forward" basis, focused on archiving artists' legacies without monetary exchange.
curiosity **Tape Trader Etiquette (No Charging)** : In tape trader culture, it was considered "very uncool" to charge money for bootlegs; the practice operated on a "pay-it-forward" system.
instrument **Nakamichi 550 (Tape Deck)** : The Nakamichi 550 was a small, excellent tape deck frequently used by serious Grateful Dead tapers for capturing audio.
curiosity **Tape Trader Home Setup** : Tape traders would use multiple cassette machines simultaneously at home to copy recordings made at shows, often distributing them with personalized artwork.
curiosity **Tape Trader Skills and Etiquette** : Proficient tape trading required skills like finding optimal venue spots for optimal audio capture, managing audience noise, selecting quality tapes (e.g., Max L2's, TDK SA), and meticulous documentation of setlists, artwork, and gear used.
instrument **Maxell XLII & TDK SA (Cassette Tapes)** : Maxell XLII and TDK SA were mentioned as among the best cassette tapes used by tape traders for recording.

## Artists & Taping Culture

artist @Pearl Jam **Rebel Stance and Industry Resistance** : Pearl Jam maintained a "rebel stage," actively working outside the standard music industry system, a stance that influenced their approach to live recordings.
member @Eddie Vedder **Early Taping Habit** : As a child, Eddie Vedder would sneak tape recorders into concerts to document shows for personal enjoyment at home.
curiosity @Pearl Jam **Encouragement of Fan Taping** : Upon achieving success, Pearl Jam, particularly Eddie Vedder, not only allowed but encouraged fans to make recordings at their shows, aiming to provide fans with high-quality recordings.
album @Pearl Jam **Official Bootlegs Packaging** : Pearl Jam's "Official Bootlegs" were originally sold as CDs in plain, "illegal-looking" packaging, typically a brown cardboard sleeve.
curiosity @Pearl Jam **Billboard Top 200 Chart Record** : Pearl Jam set a record for having the most albums simultaneously on the Billboard Top 200 chart with their "Official Bootlegs" series.
album @Pearl Jam **Official Bootlegs Sales Figures** : Pearl Jam sold almost 14 million copies of their "Official Bootlegs" releases since their inception in the fall of 2000.
artist @Charlie Parker **Live Recording Preservation** : Much of Charlie Parker's legacy would have been lost without audience recordings.
artist @The Grateful Dead **Tape Trader Obsession** : The Grateful Dead were the most famous obsession of tape traders, due to their limited radio presence, lack of official live releases for jams, and unique performances at each show.
curiosity @The Grateful Dead **Deadhead Tape Exchanges (1972)** : By 1972, dedicated "Deadheads" (fans of The Grateful Dead) established tape exchanges globally to share live recordings.
curiosity @The Grateful Dead **Initial Disapproval of Taping** : The Grateful Dead initially "frowned on" fans recording their shows.
curiosity @The Grateful Dead **First Taping Section (1984)** : The Grateful Dead became the first band to create dedicated "taping sections" where fans could set up their gear to record shows, beginning on October 27, 1984, at the Berkeley Community Theatre.
artist **Artists Allowing Taping** : Many artists officially allow taping of their shows, including Pearl Jam, Dave Matthewspan, Fugazi, Iron Maiden, Fish, Weezer, Metallica, Sonic Youth, Fiddy Sents, Black Cros, Blues Traveler, and Radiohead.
curiosity **Smartphone Impact on Live Taping** : The advent of smartphones rendered efforts to prohibit live concert recordings largely "moot," as concertgoers easily sneak phones into shows.
curiosity **Artist/Label Opposition to Bootlegs** : Many artists and record labels opposed live bootlegs due to concerns about documenting subpar performances, loss of control over recordings, and the commercial sale of these recordings (copyright infringement) outside the tape-trader community.
curiosity **Taper Evasion Techniques** : Before smartphones, concert tapers developed elaborate methods to evade security, including disassembling gear, hiding components on multiple people, assembling equipment in bathrooms, and pre-hiding gear in venues.
curiosity **Concert Ticket Warnings** : Concert tickets historically included warnings like "no photographic or recording devices allowed" to deter live taping.
member @Kurt Cobain **Health and Mental State (1994)** : During Nirvana's March 1, 1994, Munich show, Kurt Cobain was struggling with bronchitis, laryngitis, drug addiction, and mental health issues.
curiosity @Kurt Cobain **Final Live Performance (1994)** : Kurt Cobain's final live stage performance occurred on March 1, 1994, in Munich, Germany, a month before his death, preserved by a smuggled tape recording.
curiosity @Elton John **Radio Broadcast Bootleg Impact** : Elton John's November 17, 1970, WABC-FM broadcast was widely bootlegged by thousands of listeners, prompting his record company to issue an official version of the show months later.
album @Elton John **Official Live Album Release (Post-1970 Bootleg)** : Elton John's record company was compelled to issue an official live album based on his November 17, 1970, WABC-FM performance due to its widespread bootlegging.
curiosity @David Bowie **Radio Broadcast to Official Release (1972/2008)** : David Bowie's October 20, 1972, Santa Monica performance, originally an FM radio broadcast, became a famous bootleg, then a semi-legal release with litigation, and finally an official release on June 3, 2008.
