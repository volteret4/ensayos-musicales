## Foundational Concepts & Theories
event **James Burke's Connections TV Show (1970s)** : A BBC television series premiered in the 1970s, starring science historian James Burke, which illustrated unexpected interconnections between scientific achievements, discoveries, and historical events. This model is explicitly applied to music history in the podcast.
curiosity **Biological/Evolutionary Basis of Music Appreciation** : The human brain has a specialized part dedicated to music, which leads to understanding sounds as special, storing musical memories distinctly, and secreting "feel good hormones" similar to an orgasm. This phenomenon was even confusing to Charles Darwin.
curiosity **Darwin's Music-Mating Call Theory** : Charles Darwin theorized that human appreciation for music might be linked to the mating calls of birds and other creatures, suggesting the brain's music centers are related to the need for sex and the species' drive to reproduce.
curiosity **Lullabies as Survival Mechanism Theory** : An anthropological theory proposing that lullabies, universally used across all known societies to calm babies, function as a survival mechanism to prevent predators from being alerted to the presence of vulnerable young.
curiosity **Early Humans' Collective Voice Music Theory** : A theory suggesting early humans utilized music as a collective voice to unite individuals during times of celebration, sadness, rituals, and warfare, thereby fostering tribal cohesion.
curiosity **Music as Ancient Story Preservation Method** : A theory positing that early humans, lacking written language, used music to preserve and transmit important cultural information like myths, folk tales, and histories across thousands of generations, starting from migrations out of Africa.

## Ancient Music & Tuning Systems
artist @Pythagoras **Architect of Mathematical Music Theory (c. 570 BC)** : A Greek mathematician (c. 570 BC) renowned for the Pythagorean theorem, he is also considered foundational to music history for using math and science to organize and define musical sounds, distinguishing melody from noise.
genre **Pythagorean Tuning - Mathematical Basis of Western Music** : A system developed by Pythagoras (c. 570 BC) based on mathematical ratios (e.g., 3:2) derived from string experiments, where a string half the length produces double the pitch. This system became the primary basis for intonations, scales, instrument tuning, and composing Western music until the early 1500s.
genre **Blue Notes - Origin in Blues Music (US South, 1870s)** : Notes found between accepted tones (e.g., on a piano keyboard), also known as "bent notes," that are outside conventional scales. They originated in blues music from the US South (starting c. 1870s) and were intentionally excluded by Pythagorean tuning due to their "natural imperfections."
instrument **Knife/Broken Bottle as Guitar Slide** : Early blues players utilized a knife or a broken bottle on the fretboard of a guitar to bend strings, effectively producing "blue notes" (notes outside conventional scales) that were a deliberate departure from the discrete notes favored by Pythagoras.
influence **Blue Notes' Spread to Jazz, R&B, Rock** : The use of "untunings" and "blue notes" spread from early blues music to jazz, then rhythm and blues (R&B), and subsequently became an integral and widespread part of rock music and other modern genres.

## Early Composers & Women in Music
artist @Enheduanna **First Named Composer & Singer-Songwriter (Sumer, c. 2300 BC)** : A royal Sumerian high priestess in the city-state of Ur, living around 2300 BC, identified as the earliest known composer by name. She was a poet whose hymns to Sumerian gods were set to music and some performed by her, though her compositions were religious in nature (some with "dirty and sexy" themes).
curiosity **Ancient Music Subject Matter Restrictions** : In ancient times, music was primarily restricted to three themes: religious rituals, storytelling (including lamentations), and praising great men, with love songs being uncommon and considered "unwise" until figures like Sappho.
artist @Sappho **Early Lyric Poet from Lesbos & LGBTQ+ Symbol** : A poet from the Greek island of Lesbos who composed lyric poetry intended to be sung in company with a lyre. She is recognized as a symbol of love and desire between women, contributing to the origins of the words "sapphic" and "lesbian."
curiosity @Tegan and Sara **Contemporary Women in Music Mention** : Tegan and Sara are mentioned as contemporary female artists during a discussion of the historical contributions of women to music, specifically following the segment on Sappho.
instrument **Ancient Women Drummers (3000+ Years Ago)** : Historical evidence from carvings, paintings, and sculptures (dating back approximately 3,000 years ago) shows women were humanity's first formalized drummers in regions like Egypt, Mesopotamia (Sumeria), Ancient Greece, and Ancient Israel, where they used drums to keep beats for music designed to induce trance states.
curiosity **Drums' Transition from Women to Men/Military Use** : Over centuries, the role of drumming shifted from women to men, as the instrument became masculinized and was increasingly adopted for military purposes, such as leading armies into battle.

## African American Music & The Great Migration
event **American Civil War Ends & 13th Amendment (1865)** : The American Civil War concluded, leading to the ratification of the 13th Amendment on December 6, 1865, which abolished slavery and set in motion profound social and cultural shifts in the United States, particularly impacting the former slave population.
event **The Great Migration (1910-1940)** : A significant demographic shift between 1910 and 1940 where approximately 2 million African Americans moved from the US South to Northern and Midwestern cities (e.g., Memphis, St. Louis, Chicago, Indianapolis, Cleveland, Detroit), driven by racial discrimination (Jim Crow laws), poverty, and enabled by railroad expansion. This movement facilitated the spread of African American culture, including music like work songs, spirituals, gospel, and African influences.
genre **Ragtime - Early African American Genre (St. Louis, late 1800s)** : An African American musical genre originating in St. Louis in the very late 1800s, characterized by its "unsmooth," syncopated, or "ragged" rhythms, which significantly influenced the development of jazz.
genre **Jazz - Birth and Cultural Dominance (New Orleans, early 1900s)** : A musical genre that emerged from an offshoot of Ragtime in New Orleans, migrating North with African Americans during the Great Migration. Jazz became the dominant form of American popular culture until the mid-1950s.
genre **Blues - Southern Roots and Simplistic Style (c. 1870s)** : A musical genre with roots in the US South possibly as early as 1870, becoming widely heard by 1901-1902. It was characterized by simple arrangements, often just a guitar and voice, and was performed in venues known as "Juke joints."
venue **Juke Joints & The Chitlin' Circuit - Blues Performance Venues** : Juke joints were informal gathering places for African Americans in the US South where blues music was played, danced to, and consumed. The Chitlin' Circuit was a touring network connecting these venues, extending from Florida to major cities like New York (Apollo Theater, Cotton Club).
influence **Blues' Fusion with Country, Western, Hillbilly Music** : The blues genre mixed with country music, Western music, and hillbilly music in the US South, contributing to the musical foundation that would eventually lead to rock and roll.
genre **Big Bands - Jazz Ensembles (1920s-1930s)** : Large jazz ensembles popular in the 1920s and 1930s, featuring up to 20 or more musicians playing horns, guitars, piano, double bass, drums, and often vocalists. Their high cost of maintenance, the Great Depression, and World War II contributed to their decline.
event **US Musician Strike (1942-1944)** : A significant strike in the United States between 1942 and 1944 that largely brought the recording industry to a standstill, impacting big bands and contributing to the downsizing of musical groups.
genre **Bebop - Post-WWII Jazz Style** : A less danceable form of jazz that developed as big bands were forced to downsize after World War II due to economic pressures, musician shortages, and the recording strike.
genre **Rhythm and Blues (R&B) - Origins from Jazz Downsizing (late 1940s)** : A genre that emerged in the late 1940s as downsized big bands began to emphasize rhythm and beat. R&B combined with country, Western, hillbilly, and traditional blues elements to create the conditions for the birth of rock and roll in the 1950s.
influence **Great Migration's Role in Rock and Roll Birth** : The Great Migration of African Americans (1910-1940), stemming from the abolition of slavery, is identified as the foundational event that ultimately led to the birth of rock and roll in the 1950s through the development and blending of jazz, blues, and R&B.

## The Great Metric Shift
curiosity **Human Preference for 120 BPM & 4/4 Time** : Studies indicate humans generally prefer music played at a tempo of 120 beats per minute and written in 4/4 time, which is common in dance, rock, and disco music.
curiosity **Pre-1900 Dominance of 3/4 (Waltz) Time** : Prior to 1900, 3/4 time (waltz time) was the most popular time signature for songs in Western music, exemplified by national anthems like "The Star-Spangled Banner" or traditional songs like "Take Me Out to the Ball Game."
event **The Great Metric Shift (post-1910)** : A fundamental change in Western musical rhythm that occurred after 1910, where the dominant time signature shifted from 3/4 to 4/4. This shift is primarily attributed to the influence of ragtime and its subsequent development into jazz.
artist @Scott Joplin **"Maple Leaf Rag" (1899) & Ragtime's Metric Impact** : An African-American pianist who composed "Maple Leaf Rag" in 1899, a piece often in 2/4 time that was considered radical for its era. Joplin's ragtime compositions were instrumental in initiating the "Great Metric Shift" away from 3/4 time.
curiosity **1900 Music Magazine Condemnation of Ragtime** : A music magazine in 1900 expressed severe concern about ragtime, describing it as "virulent poison" and a "malarial epidemic" that threatened the sanity of youth, reflecting cultural and potentially racist anxieties surrounding the new genre.
influence **Ragtime and Jazz's Role in 4/4 Time Dominance** : Ragtime, initially in 2/4 time, evolved into jazz, which expanded to 4/4 time. As jazz became a primary cultural driver, 4/4 time became dominant, now representing over 90% of all popular music.

## The Shellac Crisis & Vinyl Revolution
event **Japan's Invasion of Mele Peninsula (December 8, 1941)** : On December 8, 1941, Japan's invasion of the Mele Peninsula (encompassing modern-day Malaysia, Thailand, Myanmar, and Singapore) cut off the world's primary source of shellac, a crucial raw material for phonograph records, causing a significant industry crisis.
instrument **Shellac - Primary Material for Pre-Vinyl Records** : A waxy, resin-like material secreted by the female lac bug, predominantly sourced from the Mele Peninsula. In the 1940s, shellac comprised one-third of the material (alongside minerals, cotton fibers, and carbon) used to manufacture 78 RPM phonograph records.
event **The Shellac Crisis (1940s)** : A period during the 1940s when the Japanese Imperial Army's control of the Mele Peninsula halted the global supply of shellac, a key component for records, leading to a severe shortage and forcing the recording industry to develop substitutes; new records could only be obtained by trading in old ones.
artist @Dr. Peter Goldmark **Columbia Records Engineer & Vinyl Pioneer** : Chief engineer at Columbia Records who was tasked with finding a shellac substitute during the 1940s crisis. His team developed polyvinyl chloride (vinyl) as a superior material for records.
instrument **Polyvinyl Chloride (Vinyl) - Revolutionary Record Material** : An artificial substance, initially used for sewer pipes in the 1920s, that Dr. Peter Goldmark's team at Columbia Records adapted for phonograph records. Vinyl was more durable and allowed grooves to be cut closer together, holding five times more music per side than 78 RPM shellac discs.
album **12-inch 33 1/3 RPM Vinyl Album - Industry Game Changer** : A new record format introduced after the development of polyvinyl chloride, which, along with the 45 RPM single, revolutionized the recorded music industry by offering greater durability and significantly increased storage capacity, leading to a multi-billion dollar business.
album **7-inch 45 RPM Single - Industry Game Changer** : A new record format introduced alongside the 12-inch 33 1/3 RPM LP after the development of polyvinyl chloride, which helped transform the recorded music industry into a multi-billion dollar enterprise.
