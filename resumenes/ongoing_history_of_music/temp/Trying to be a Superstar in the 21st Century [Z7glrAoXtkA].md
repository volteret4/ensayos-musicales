## General Observations on Music Industry & Superstardom

curiosity @ : **Superstar Rock Act Definition (Traditional)** : An act that sells music by tens of millions of units, draws tens of thousands to concerts where people will "crawl over broken glass" for tickets, creates a deep catalog of hits released over years, and is deemed great by a consensus of millions of people.
curiosity @ : **21st Century Superstar Rarity** : The 21st century has seen fewer and "smaller" superstar rock acts compared to previous decades, with "stars" getting "fewer and further between."
curiosity @ : **Aging Rock Acts (21st Century)** : Many of the biggest rock acts in the world are not young; examples include Green Day (mid-40s), Dave Grohl (creeping up on half-century mark), Trent Reznor (52), Pearl Jam (early to mid-50s average), U2 (upper 50s average), Bruce Springsteen (68), and Paul McCartney (75).
curiosity @ : **Music Industry Transformation (Post-2000)** : The music industry has changed drastically since 2000, rendering 20th-century metrics like album sales and chart positions less relevant for defining superstardom.
curiosity @ : **Album Sales Decline (General)** : Album sales have been dropping at a double-digit rate for years, with digital albums dropping at 25% per year for the last couple of years, leading to the belief that 20 million-selling albums will never be seen again.
curiosity @ : **End of Traditional Gatekeepers** : The traditional cultural gatekeepers (record labels, radio, printed media) that tightly controlled access to music and manufactured consensus in the past are now largely gone due to the internet, shifting power to the individual music fan.
curiosity @ : **Consumer Choice and Consensus (21st Century)** : Unlimited and unfettered consumer choice in the 21st century has led to more disagreement or less agreement on which acts should receive public attention, causing public attention to be spread thinner.
curiosity @ : **Impact of Heritage Acts' Longevity** : The continued presence and touring of "first and second generation" rock bands (e.g., Rolling Stones, Pink Floyd members, Eagles, The Who, Bob Dylan, Paul McCartney) mean new acts must compete for attention and money in a more crowded marketplace.
curiosity @ : **Shrinking Artist Royalties & Touring Necessity** : Royalty checks from older album sales, once considered like retirement annuities, have shrunk significantly, forcing heritage acts to continuously tour to maintain cash flow and lifestyle.
curiosity @ : **Generational Musical Tastes Shift** : Unlike past generations where kids avoided parents' music, parents and their children today have more musical commonality, especially in rock.
curiosity @ : **Less Tribal Music Fans (New Millennium)** : Younger generations (who came of age musically in the new millennium) are far more ecumenical and less tribal in their tastes, easily switching between genres like rock, hip hop, pop, classic rock, dance, and EDM.

## Albums & Music Consumption

curiosity @ : **"Long, Slow Death of the Album"** : The concept of the rock album, defined as a collection of songs carefully recorded, sequenced, and meant to be consumed as a whole, is gradually dying.
album @The Beatles: **Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band (1967) – Industry Game Changer** : This 1967 album was a game changer, shifting the music industry's main focus from low-margin singles to high-margin albums as the basis of everything in music.
curiosity @ : **Record Label Strategy (Pre-Internet)** : Record labels gradually phased out singles, coercing consumers to buy full albums even if they only wanted one song, a move described as arrogant and cynical.
event @ : **Napster and File Sharing – Album Era's Beginning End** : The emergence of Napster and other original illegal file-sharing programs offered music as an "ala carte buffet," marking the beginning of the end of the album era.
event @ : **iTunes Music Store Launch (January 9, 2001)** : The introduction of the iTunes Music Store on January 9, 2001, allowed legitimate and legal purchase of individual tracks, further contributing to the decline of album sales.
curiosity @ : **Streaming and Album Tracks** : In the streaming world, album tracks generally receive almost zero traction, with playlists dominating consumption, leading to questions about the point of recording non-singles.
curiosity @ : **Pop Artists Album Strategy (21st Century)** : Pop artists' albums are often structured as collections of songs intended to be released as a parade of singles, with the album acting merely as a "wrapper."
curiosity @ : **Hip-Hop Artists Release Strategy (21st Century)** : Hip-hop stars maintain a steady drip of new material, appearing on other artists' records, dropping mixtapes and remixes, to keep fans constantly occupied and their names top-of-mind.
curiosity @ : **Rock Artists Album Strategy (21st Century)** : Rock acts and their fans are still conditioned to long waits between albums, which does not align with current music consumption trends.

## Streaming's Impact

curiosity @ : **Streaming as Primary Revenue Source** : Streaming has become the greatest source of revenue for record labels, accounting for over half of their earnings by mid-2017, generating $150,000 US per minute.
curiosity @ : **Streaming Payouts to Artists** : A single stream pays artists and composers only a fraction of a cent, significantly less than the sale of a digital file or CD, leading to a shrinking amount of money flowing to musicians.
curiosity @ : **Decimation of Musician Middle Class** : Low streaming payouts have decimated the middle class of musicians, traditionally a breeding ground for superstars, creating a gap between huge acts and a struggling underclass.
curiosity @ : **"Recording and Touring Treadmill"** : Musicians, particularly in the middle class, are forced into a constant cycle of recording and touring (as playing live is one of the few steady revenue streams), leading to creative, physical, and mental exhaustion.
curiosity @ : **Lack of Rock on Streaming Charts** : Rock artists' songs are very rarely found in the top 200 most streamed songs weekly, which are overwhelmingly dominated by pop, R&B, and hip-hop (e.g., Drake, Ed Sheeran, The Weeknd, Justin Bieber).
curiosity @ : **Record Label Marketing Focus Shift** : Due to streaming revenue concentration in pop, R&B, and hip-hop, record labels focus marketing dollars there, leaving anything with a guitar with less or no marketing support.
curiosity @ : **Streaming's Effect on Songwriting** : Streaming alters the nature of music by forcing artists to retain attention in the first 30 seconds (due to Spotify's payout threshold), leading to shorter instrumental intros (under 5 seconds, down from 17 seconds in the 80s), more hooks jammed into the first half-minute, and front-heavy songs with familiar samples.

## Genre & Cultural Shifts

genre @ : **20th Century Jazz Dominance** : Jazz, in all its forms, dominated the first half of the 20th century, pushing Western culture forward.
genre @ : **Rock and Roll's Mid-20th Century Rise** : Rock and roll emerged in the 1950s, pushing jazz aside to become the leading form of music for the next 50 to 60 years.
genre @ : **Hip Hop's 21st Century Cultural Dominance** : Hip hop has eclipsed rock as the current driving force in popular culture, calling the shots on a major scale culturally, though rock and jazz remain important.

## Influences (Implicitly, the influence of industry on artists)

curiosity @ : **Spotify 30-Second Payout Rule** : Spotify does not pay out royalties for a stream until 30 seconds have passed.
curiosity @ : **Spotify Skip Rates** : Spotify monitors listening habits, showing that 25% of all songs are skipped in the first five seconds, 29% in the first 10 seconds, and 35% are skipped before 30 seconds pass.
curiosity @ : **Songwriting Evolution (Shorter Intros)** : The average instrumental lead-up to vocals in pop songs has shortened from around 17 seconds in the 80s to less than five seconds today, which is a "really bad thing for rock songs."
curiosity @ : **Front-Heavy Song Production** : Producers have reverse-engineered the listening habits of pop fans, writing songs that are front-heavy with more hooks jammed into the first half-minute, often starting with the chorus or familiar samples, to cater to impatience and short attention spans.
