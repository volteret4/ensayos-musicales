## Music Genres & Eras
genre **Rock - Traditional Instrumentation** : For the longest time, sounds of rock were made with voice, guitars, bass, drums, and keyboards like piano and organ.
genre **Progressive Rock (Prog Rock) - Electronic Integration** : In the 1970s, electronic sounds worked their way into Prog Rock.
genre **Punk Rock - Scene & Technology Adoption** : At the very end of the 1970s, technology (synthesizers) became cheap enough for young musicians in the last months of the original punk rock scene to adopt music-making machines.
genre **New Wave - Overview (1978-1984)** : An era extending roughly from 1978 to 1984, divided into two periods: an early period of jerky, twitchy, fun power pop based largely on guitars, bass, and drums, and a later synthesizer-driven period called Technopop.
genre **Post-Punk - Defining Term** : A blanket term for all new music made possible by punk, emerging immediately after the punk rock explosion of the 1970s. Did not sound like punk but couldn't have happened without it.
genre **Technopop (Synth Pop/Electro Pop) - Emergence & Characteristics** : Emerged in earnest around 1979 as the synthesizer-driven second half of the new wave era. Defined as post-punk music where electronic keyboards and related gear (like drum machines) were the dominant instruments, with real guitars and drums often absent.
genre **Early Technopop - Vibe & Lyrical Themes** : Often robotic, cold, deliberately artificial, emotionally sterile, and intense, with a "Blade Runner-ish" feel. Shared nihilism with guitar-driven punk. Lyrically, spoke of futurism, alienation, isolation, and a dystopian view of life.
genre **New Romantic - Offshoot of Synth-based Music** : A subgenre of synth-based music rooted in British clubs like Billies and The Blitz Club, which served as a breeding site for groups like Spandau Ballet, Visage, and Culture Club.
genre **Industrial Music - Future Exploration** : Described as one of the most intense, hard, and heavy forms of alternative music, stratified from punk, new wave, and Technopop, to be explored in a future podcast episode.

## Instruments & Technology
instrument **Synthesizer - Early Development (1940s-1960s)** : Work began in the 1940s, accelerated in the 1950s with the advent of the transistor. Early machines were gigantic, filling entire rooms (one required a rail car for transport). By the 1960s, engineers like Bob Moog and Don Buchla built smaller, though temperamental, hard-to-program, and expensive units.
instrument **Korg 700S - Early Synthesizer** : A specific synthesizer model purchased and used by Daniel Miller in November 1978 to record "Warm Leatherette."
instrument **Mini-Moog - Synthesizer Discovery** : The synthesizer model Gary Numan stumbled upon in a studio in spring 1979, leading him to abandon recording a punk album for electronic music.
instrument **EMS VCS III - Primitive Synthesizer** : An early synthesizer without a keyboard, used by Pink Floyd on the instrumental track "On the Run" from *Dark Side of the Moon*.
instrument **Polyphonic Synthesizers - Advancement (1980-1981)** : Newer synthesizers emerging around 1980-1981 that allowed playing multiple notes simultaneously, making it possible to build chords without layering individual notes on separate tracks, unlike older monophonic synths.
instrument **FM Synthesis - Sound Generation Technology** : A new method for taming electricity into notes that emerged around 1980-1981, described as being far better than the old oscillators.
instrument **Drum Machines - Electronic Instrument** : Related gear to electronic keyboards that became dominant instruments in Technopop. Part of the early 80s music-making technology explosion.
instrument **Samplers - Early Music Technology** : Part of the early 80s music-making technology explosion, alongside polyphonic synths and drum machines.
instrument **MIDI (Musical Instrument Digital Interface) - Communication Standard** : Originated from an engineering paper called "Universal Synthesizer Interface." Became the new worldwide standard for allowing electronic instruments to communicate, enabling a single cable to carry 16 channels of information and connect up to 16 different devices, allowing a single played note to trigger events on multiple devices.

## Influences & Collaborations
influence **Punk Rock - Influence on Technopop Aesthetic** : The emerging Technopop was part of New Wave's evolution, a subgenre created by the same attitude as punk. The punk sensibility of self-taught musicians and prioritizing attitude over ability transferred to early Technopop practitioners.
influence **Kraftwerk - Influence on David Bowie** : David Bowie was fascinated by Kraftwerk's work and immediately dove into synthesizers after observing their experiments.
influence **Bob Moog - Wendy Carlos Collaboration** : Wendy Carlos worked with Bob Moog on his synthesizer, notably in creating the all-synth soundtrack for *A Clockwork Orange*.

## Curiosities & Anecdotes
curiosity **Synthesizer Scale & Transport** : An early synthesizer model was so large it had to be transported on a rail car.
curiosity **Daniel Miller - DIY Success** : As The Normal, he recorded "Warm Leatherette" in his bedroom with rudimentary gear (Korg 700S, four-track reel-to-reel machine) and no musical experience, yet sold 30,000 copies in November 1978.
curiosity @Paul Humphries **Music Aptitude Report** : His mother received a school report from his music teacher stating, "Paul Humphries has absolutely no aptitude for music whatsoever," despite his later success with OMD.
curiosity @Gary Numan **"Are 'Friends' Electric?" - Lyrical Inspiration** : The song is based on Gary Numan's vision of a future where machines come to your door to provide services, with the "friend" in the song being a prostitute.
curiosity **UK Musicians Union - "Loonies" Label** : The Musicians Union's idea to ban synthesizers was met with strong opposition from the Union of Sound Synthesists and was dismissed by the music press and fans as "the product of a bunch of loonies."
curiosity **Technopop Decline - Changing Perception of Synths** : By the mid-1980s, synthesizer technology improved to the point where it became harder to distinguish between real and synthesized instruments, and synths became so common they transitioned from being "new and futuristic" to "the common present."
