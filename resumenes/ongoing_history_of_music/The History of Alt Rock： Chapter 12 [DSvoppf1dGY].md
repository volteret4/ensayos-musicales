## albums

The album itself sold over 500,000 copies in the two weeks following the single's release. "Country House from Blur's fourth album, The Great Escape, Outsold, Roll With It from Oasis by about 55,000 copies."
**Blur (1997) - Non-Britpop Sound** : Their self-titled 1997 album marked a departure from the Britpop sound, adopting a low-fi, indie-ish style perceived as American. This shift contributed to the decline of the Britpop movement. "And then Blur's Celtyle 1997 album didn't really sound very Britpop at all. In fact, it sounded really American in its low-fi indie-ishness."

## songs
**Park Life (1994) - Title Track** : The title track from their 1994 album "Park Life" featured Cockney bits provided by actor Phil Daniels, known for playing Jimmy the mod in the movie Quadrophenia. **Country House (1995)** : Released on August 15, 1995, this single from "The Great Escape" became central to the "Battle of Britpop," famously outselling Oasis's "Roll With It" by approximately 55,000 copies. ## curiosities
**Early History and Manchester Association - 1989** : Blur first appeared around 1989, originating from Colchester, East of London. Despite their geographic origin, they were often mistakenly associated with the Manchester scene due to their early sound, which featured "Manchester-ish bits" in songs from around 1991. "They had first serviced back in 1989 and although they were from Cold Chester, which is East of London, they were lumped into what was happening in Manchester in the Northwest, some 300 kilometers away."
**Early Struggles with Alcohol** : After a strong start, Blur faced early difficulties, stumbling "hard in a haze of alcohol" before getting their act together. "Blur came out of the gate hard, but then they stumbled hard in a haze of alcohol."
**Rivalry with Oasis - "Death Match"** : As Blur cleaned up their act, they became pitted "head to head with Oasis as the leading British band," leading to a fierce "death match" rivalry. This was one of the most vicious rivalries in British music history. "By the time Oasis got rolling, blur had cleaned up their act to the point where they were pitted head to head with Oasis as the leading British band. And the result was nothing short of a death match."
**Damon Albarn's Motivation for British Sound** : After a particularly bad tour of America, Damon Albarn was determined to make British music distinctly British again, reacting to a UK music scene dominated by American sounds. This resolve directly led to the creation of "Park Life." "Damon Alborn of Blur had a lot to do with this. After a particularly bad tour of America, he came home to a country that was a wash in American music and he became determined to make British music British again."
**Initiation of "Battle of Britpop" - August 15, 1995** : Damon Albarn deliberately escalated the rivalry with Oasis by moving the release date of a new Blur single ("Country House") to August 15, 1995, the exact same day Oasis was releasing their single ("Roll With It"). This move triggered a massive media frenzy and the iconic "Battle of Britpop." "Damon Alborn started it. When he found out when Oasis was going to release the first single from their What's the Story Morning Glory album, he moved the release date of the new Blur single up to the very same date, August 15th, 1995."

## curiosities
**Origin from "Rain" and Noel Gallagher's Takeover** : Noel Gallagher, formerly a roadie for Inspiral Carpets and later an employee in British Gas's parts department, launched a "coup d'état" on his younger brother Liam's band, then called "Rain." He renamed it Oasis and took over all songwriting responsibilities. "Nole Gallagher launched a coup d'état on his younger brother's band, which was called Rain. He renamed it Oasis and took over. Everything, all the songwriting."
**Musical Influences and Punk Attitude** : Oasis's debut album combined new and familiar sounds, incorporating elements of The Beatles, David Bowie, T-Rex, The Jam, The Stones, and The Stone Roses. Their songs were delivered with an intense, almost punk attitude, creating guitar pop as loud as the Sex Pistols but with more groove and melody. "There were bits of Beatles, it's a Bowie and T-Rex and the Jam and the Stones and the Stone Roses in there."
**Media Sensationalism and Public Behavior** : The band provided abundant material for the music press, including "stupid quotes, drug use, public drunkenness, fist fights" both at home and abroad. This behavior generated "great sensational headlines," contributing to their fame. "Everything they did made for great sensational headlines."
**Rivalry with Blur - "Death Match"** : Oasis became one of the leading British bands, engaged in a fierce "death match" rivalry with Blur, which was one of the most vicious in British music history. "By the time Oasis got rolling, blur had cleaned up their act to the point where they were pitted head to head with Oasis as the leading British band. And the result was nothing short of a death match."
**"Battle of Britpop" Single Release - August 15, 1995** : Oasis's single "Roll With It" was deliberately released on August 15, 1995, the same day as Blur's "Country House," due to Damon Albarn's strategic move. This generated massive media attention, with even tabloids covering personal lives, the Financial Times running profiles, and the BBC covering it like a major news event. "Damon Alborn started it. When he found out when Oasis was going to release the first single from their What's the Story Morning Glory album, he moved the release date of the new Blur single up to the very same date, August 15th, 1995."
**"Ultimate Winner" of Britpop War** : Despite "Country House" outselling "Roll With It" in the immediate "Battle of Britpop," Oasis was considered the "ultimate winner" because their second album, "What's the Story Morning Glory," successfully broke them worldwide. This made them a global phenomenon, unlike many British bands before them. "But the ultimate winner in this war was Oasis. For the first time in years, a British band and a band with Indypop credentials to boot broke it open worldwide."
**"Biggest Band on the Planet" Status** : Oasis achieved global dominance with "What's the Story Morning Glory," selling over 10 million copies. For a period, they were considered "the biggest band on the planet." "And for a while, Oasis was the biggest band on the planet, selling more than 10 million copies of morning glory."

# genre - Britpop

## genres
Indiepop (credentials)

## curiosities
**All-Encompassing Social Phenomenon** : At its peak, Britpop was an all-encompassing social phenomenon in the UK, fostering a renewed sense of pride across the island and making London the perceived "most swinging place on earth." "At its height, Brit Pop was an all-encompassing social phenomenon in the UK."
**Counterinsurgency Against Grunge** : Britpop emerged as a direct "counterinsurgency" launched by the British against the rise of grunge and the alternative nation in North America, promoting homegrown talent and sounds. "This show is about the counterinsurgency that the British launched against that."
**Early Nomenclature - "New Wave of New Wave"** : Initially, the new music that would become Britpop was sometimes referred to as "the new wave of new wave," but this term was deemed too cumbersome. "at first people called this new music the new wave of new wave, but that was kind of a mouthful."
**Coined Term from The Face - May 1994** : The term "Britpop" originated from "the B word," an abbreviation for British pop, published by The Face magazine in May 1994. By the end of 1994, the term was widely adopted for numerous "unabashedly British" bands. "Then a magazine called The Face published the B word in May of 1994 as an abbreviation of British pop."
**Peak Years - 1994-1996** : The period from 1994 through 1996 represented the peak years for Britpop, characterized by a resurgence of British pride, homegrown talent, and songs reminiscent of classic British acts like The Beatles and The Stones. "1994 through to 1996 were the peak years for Brit Pop."
**Distraction from Bosnia Conflict** : Britpop, particularly the media's focus on the rivalry between Oasis and Blur, served as a significant distraction for the British media from larger European issues, such as the growing problems in Bosnia. "Even though much of Europe's attention was focused on the growing problems in Bosnia, much of the British media spent their time chronicling the battle between Oasis and Blur."
**Era in British History - Mid-90s** : In the mid-90s, Britpop transcended music to become an entire era in British history, marked by a vibrant cultural, social, political, and economic atmosphere in London. It coincided with the rise of a young Prime Minister, Tony Blair, and a renewed sense of British pride among young people. "In the mid-90s, Britpop was more than just music. It was an era in British history."
**Peak and Inevitable Decline - Summer 1995** : Britpop reached its peak in the summer of 1995, followed by an "inevitable decline." The momentum initially masked the cracks, but signs like BBC Radio 1's policy shift and the University of Leeds symposium on Britpop reflected its widespread influence before the downturn. "The peak of Britpop was the summer of 1995. After that came the long, slow, inevitable decline."
