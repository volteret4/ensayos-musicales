# genre - Alt-rock

## curiosities
**1980s Culture** : In the 1980s, the alt-rock scene, including bands, managers, labels, DJs, record stores, and fans, embraced an "outsider-ness," desiring no connection with the mainstream, and fostered a "very tribal" environment. "People involved in the scene, the bands and the managers, the labels and the DJs, the record stores and fans, reveled in their outsider-ness."

**1980s Radio Diversity** : Commercial alt-rock radio stations in the 1980s exhibited staggering breadth in their playlists, featuring techno pop, industrial, various forms of punk, early rap and hip hop, goth, British indie, remixes, ethereal dream pop, and guitar rock, with each station having a distinct local flavor due to a lack of centralized charts. "This kind of variety was typical for alt rock stations all across North America. No two stations were exactly the same, each had its own local flavor."

**Golden Age (1980s)** : In retrospect, some consider the 1980s to be the "golden age of commercial alt-rock radio" due to the immense musical variety and the passionate, mission-driven staff dedicated to delivering "good music to the masses." "But in retrospect, some consider this to be the golden age of commercial alt-rock radio."

**Grunge's Impact (1990s)** : With the rise of grunge in the early 1990s, particularly bands like Pearl Jam, Soundgarden, Alice in Chains, and Nirvana, alt-rock began to "coalesce around hard guitar rock," becoming less diverse and eventually supplanting the mainstream. "Alt Rock began to coalesce around hard guitar rock."

**Homogenization of Sound (1990s)** : Grunge's immense popularity led to the homogenization of alt-rock radio playlists, as program directors across North America began to follow the trends of larger stations like KROQ in Los Angeles, largely squeezing out the varied sounds of the 1980s in favor of big guitars and screaming vocalists. "The result was an unstoppable march towards a homogeneity in sound."

**Post-Grunge Slump (Late 1990s)** : After the initial grunge bands "died off" and the "grunge well basically went dry," the late 1990s experienced a period where "nothing concrete to replace it" emerged, leading to "Dreck-like New Metal" and a grim outlook for the genre. "The original Grunge bands died off and we were left with Dreck-like New Metal."

**Current State (21st Century)** : In the 21st century, alt-rock radio has "loosened up" and "weaned itself at least a little bit from Grunge guitars," experiencing an explosion of indie rock and a greater variety of sounds, though some argue there are now "too few big guitar bands." "Happily, things have loosened up and Indy Rock has exploded. Alt Rock Radio has weaned itself at least a little bit from Grunge guitars."

**80s Alt-Rock Radio Culture** : In the 1980s, commercial alt-rock radio stations were unique, with no industry-wide charts or airplay tracking; each station curated its music based on local audience preferences, leading to a "staggering breadth of music" on playlists that varied significantly between cities like Toronto (CFNY), Boston (WFNX), and Los Angeles (KROQ). "Each station simply served up the music it thought its local audience would like. And that's why my station, C.F. and Y. and Toronto, sounded so much different than, say, W.F. and X. and Boston or K. Rock and Los Angeles."

**Radio Announcer Leeway (Pre-Grunge)** : In the pre-grunge era of alt-rock radio, announcers had "a tremendous amount of leeway in choosing the music" played, often preferring extended mixes over album or single versions because their longer duration provided practical benefits like time to file records, answer request lines, and plan the next song. "Back then, the announcer had a tremendous amount of leeway in choosing the music that we played, and many of us found these extended mixes to be better than the album with the single mix, that they were longer to help."

**Commercial Radio Economics** : Commercial radio stations operate on revenues dependent on ratings, which require appealing to the largest number of people for as long and as often as possible; this reality means that while hardcore music fans exist, "billions of people who just want songs they can sing along to in the car." "And since the radio stations' revenues depend on ratings, higher ratings, equals more revenues, commercial alt-rock stations struggled along for month to month, always with the specter of a format change, looming overhead."

**"Heartbreak Beats" Extended Mix** : An extended mix of the song "heartbreak beats" was mentioned as a track favored by alt-rock radio staff in the pre-grunge era, not only for its artistic merit but also for providing practical time buffers during on-air shifts. "The second other first is heartbreak beats, the extended mix."

**Shift in Radio Programming (1990s)** : The proliferation of alt-rock stations and the rise of grunge led to industry magazines compiling charts and monitoring airplay, causing program directors in smaller markets like Des Moines, Iowa, to base their playlists on what larger, influential stations like KROQ in Los Angeles were playing, resulting in "a homogeneity in sound." "A quick check of the charts, and suddenly Des Moines was following LA instead of trying to come up with something that would work in Des Moines."

**Impact of Technology on Playlists (Late 1990s/Early 2000s)** : As listeners gained control over their music with iPods, MP3s, torrents, and eventually streaming, commercial alt-rock radio responded by "tighten[ing] their playlists more and more each year" to maintain ratings and revenues, reinforced by nationally and internationally published charts. "And as listeners empowered themselves with iPods and MP3s and torrents and eventually streaming, millions tightened their playlists more and more each year to keep those ratings and revenues up."

**Demise of 80s Alt-Rock Radio** : The unique era of 1980s commercial alt-rock radio is considered "an air in media whose time has passed," with public listening habits and demands having changed irrevocably over the last 40 years, making a return to that format unlikely. "Commercial Alt Rock Radio of the 80s was an air in media whose time has passed."

**Youth Music Criticism (Historical)** : The phenomenon of older generations complaining that the music of young people is worse than music from "their day" is a historically recurring argument that "you will never win," with similar complaints documented since the days of Aristotle concerning the youth of ancient Greece. "We all need to remember that every generation has the right to believe that the music of their youth is the greatest music of all time. This is an argument that you will never win, even though we've heard the same complaints about the music of young people since, and I'm serious about this, since the days of Aristotle, who was always complaining about the music of the youth of ancient Greece."

**21st Century Superstars Decline** : The 21st century has seen a decline in the ability to create new "superstar acts" of comparable magnitude to previous eras, partly because the enduring popularity of 1990s music and artists like Green Day, Pearl Jam, and Nirvana continues to monopolize airplay and fan attention, making it harder for new artists to break through. "We still have superstars, but they aren't anywhere as big as they used to be."

**Legacy of 90s Bands** : Music from the 1990s was so popular that many acts from that era, including Green Day, Blink-182, Pearl Jam, and even defunct bands like Nirvana and Stone Temple Pilots, continue to be "extremely popular with fans," making it challenging for new artists to emerge and capture attention in the 21st century. "That music, in that decade, was so good and so popular that audiences kept demanding to hear it. And so many of the acts from that era never went away."

**Decline of Traditional Gatekeepers** : The music industry underwent significant changes with the decline of physical sales and the rise of streaming in the 2000s, leading to the diminished power of "old cultural gatekeepers" such as record labels, radio stations, record stores, music magazines, and video channels, as they could no longer "create consensus among the public about what was good music." "The old cultural gatekeepers, record labels, radio stations, record stores, music magazines and video channels saw their powers of persuasion greatly diminished."

**Streaming's Power Shift to Individuals** : With the advent of streaming in the 2000s, power shifted "away from the star-making machinery behind the popular song to the individual," as each person became their "own music directors" with instant access to over 70 million songs on their phones. "when streaming took hold in the 2000s, all the power swung away from the star-making machinery behind the popular song to the individual."

**Increased Competition for New Artists** : Current artists face immense difficulty breaking through and achieving superstardom in the 21st century due to increased competition from "the entire musical history of the human race" available via streaming, coupled with the inability to achieve the kind of public consensus about music that existed in the pre-streaming era. "Current acts now have to compete with the entire musical history of the human race."

**Streaming's Impact on Music Consumption** : Streaming has fundamentally changed how people interact with and value music, liberating it from physical limitations but also altering listening behaviors; unlike physical formats that encouraged repeated listening and financial investment, streaming offers access and convenience, leading to frequent skipping. "Streaming is not about possession. It's about access and convenience. And this has resulted in some very different interactions with music."

**Streaming Skip Rates and Payment Threshold** : Data analysis indicates that 24% of streaming listeners skip a song within the first five seconds, 29% within ten seconds, and 35% within 30 seconds, a critical threshold because streaming services do not pay artists until a song has been listened to for at least 30 seconds. "This is important at 30 second number because a streaming service does not pay out for a song until 30 seconds have passed."

**Songwriting Adaptation to Streaming** : The 30-second payment threshold and the impact of skips on algorithms have forced producers and songwriters to "throw everything they can at the first 30 seconds of a song" to retain listener attention, leading to "shorter intros, putting the choruses up front, adding as many sugar high hooks as possible" to ensure payment. "This means that producers and songwriters now throw everything they can at the first 30 seconds of a song in order to hold our attention."

**Song Length Trends (2000 vs. Today)** : Driven by streaming algorithms favoring shorter songs (as people stick with them longer), the average song length on the Billboard Hot 100 has decreased from 4 minutes in 2000 (with none under 2.5 minutes) to 3 minutes and 30 seconds today, with an increasing number of songs under 2.5 minutes. "In 2000, the average length of a song on the Billboard Hot 100 was 4 minutes and none were under 2.5 minutes. Today, the average song on the same chart is 3 minutes and 30 seconds, so a decrease of half a minute."

**Streaming's Focus on Singles** : With streaming, the emphasis has shifted entirely to "individual songs, specifically singles," with album tracks receiving significantly less attention, leading to a question about the future relevance of recording full albums. "With streaming, it's all about individual songs, specifically singles, album tracks. They don't get much love at all."

**Future of the Album** : While albums remain the "currency of the recorded music industry" for contracts and awards, and physical formats like CDs and vinyl still sell, signs suggest the album is fading as artists increasingly adopt a "drip, drip, drip of new songs" strategy (like in hip hop) to keep fans continuously engaged, replacing the traditional album-recording-and-touring cycle. "First of all, albums are still the currency of the recorded music industry. Contracts are based on albums."

**Album vs. Single Historical Shift (Pre-1965)** : Before 1965, rock and roll and pop music primarily existed as 7-inch 45 RPM singles, with albums generally reserved for "proper music" like classical, jazz, and soundtracks. **Album as Artistic Medium Emergence (Post-1965)** : Around 1965, artists like The Beatles and Bob Dylan began to view the album as a creative "palette," leading them to compose longer, more complex, and carefully sequenced collections of songs meant to be experienced as a whole, a shift solidified by releases like 'Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band' in 1967. "But then things began to shift around 1965. The Beatles, Bob Dylan, and a few others looked at the album and thought, I can work with this."
