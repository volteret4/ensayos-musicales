# genre - Alt Dance

## curiosities
**Emergence from Post Punk** : Alt Dance is a style of music that emerged in the years immediately following the punk rock era of the 1970s, specifically from the "post-punk explosion." It signifies a period when dancing became cool again for "cool kids" after the stigma associated with disco had subsided. "It's part four of the post-punk explosion, and it's all about what we'll call alt dance."
**Revival of Dance Culture** : Following the "death of disco" by 1980, alt dance emerged as a "cool underground dance music" that welcomed outsiders and "weirdos," contrasting sharply with the exclusionary glitzy disco clubs like Studio 54. This movement helped restore dancing's status as a socially acceptable and cool activity. "This was cool underground dance music that welcomed outsiders and weirdos."
**Underground for the Underground** : While alt dance music grew substantially, especially in major cultural centers like the UK, Ibiza, Italy, New York, Chicago, LA, and Toronto, very little of it made it to mainstream radio. This underground nature was precisely how its fans preferred it, cultivating a unique subculture. "But very little of this music made it to mainstream radio, which is just how fans liked it."

# genre - Punk Rock

## curiosities
**Ethos of Freedom** : The punk rock era of the 1970s fostered an ethos of "do whatever the hell you like," which unleashed music to fracture and evolve in many directions simultaneously. This freedom led to thrilling results and was a catalyst for the emergence of various alternative genres. "music, freed by the do whatever they held you like, ethos of punk, was able to ride off in all directions at once, with some very thrilling results."
**Dancing and Anti-Fashion** : In the 1970s, dancing was generally considered "uncool" by some rock fans, with the exception of pogoing or slam dancing performed at punk band shows. The punk scene also had an austere anti-fashion attitude, against which the New Romantics reacted with elaborate styles. "Dancing was seen as uncool, unless you were pogoing or slam dancing to a punk band."

# genre - Disco

## curiosities
**Ubiquity and Backlash (Late 1970s)** : In the late 1970s, disco music was ubiquitous, especially on the radio, and its cultural dominance lasted for a couple of years, notably aiding the liberation of gay and certain minority groups. However, it also became associated with decadence, drugs, sex, and exclusive clubs that often excluded those not considered "pretty enough" or "dressed in the right high end clothes." "disco was so huge that it drove the culture for a couple of years."
**Monotony and Economic Disparity** : Despite its positive aspects, disco was also perceived as monotonous and dull. The stark contrast between the decadent, fun-seeking "rich and beautiful people" in exclusive disco clubs and the wider population struggling with a global recession and over 20% interest rates in the late 1970s fueled a significant backlash. "And everyone else was worrying about paying the mortgage with interest rates over 20%."
**Industry Over-saturation and Crash** : The record industry became excessively invested in disco, leading to the production of many "bad records" and an intense backlash. This over-saturation, culminating in events like the "Disco Demolition Night" in Chicago in summer 1979, caused the disco craze to crash and burn by 1980, sending the entire recorded music industry into a deep crisis. "There was too much disco and the backlash was intense."

# genre - New Romantics

## curiosities
**UK Scene and Clubs (Late 1970s)** : The New Romantics scene emerged in the UK in the late 1970s, centered around clubs in London, Birmingham, and Liverpool, such as the Blitz Club, Billies, and Erics. This movement was marked by its distinctive fashion and musical styles. "a new scene called New Romantics took hold in a few clubs in London, Birmingham, and Liverpool in the late 1970s."
**Fashion-First Movement** : New Romantics originated as a fashion-first movement, serving as a backlash against the "austere anti-fashion attitude" of punk. Adherents adopted "way over the top dress," makeup, wild hairstyles, and engaged in gender bending, drawing inspiration from glam rock and late 18th/early 19th-century Dandy styles. "It's important to understand that this sound started as a fashion first, and as a backlash against the austere anti-fashion attitude of the punks."
**Musical Origins** : Although initially a fashion movement, some New Romantics adherents eventually ventured into music, leading to the formation of influential groups like Duran Duran, Culture Club, Spandau Ballet, and A Flock of Seagulls. The sound was greatly influenced by David Bowie and Roxy Music. "Just so happens that some of the adherents decided to get into music, and this was the origin of such groups as Durand Ran, Culture Club, Spandovalet, and a flock of Seagulls."
**Rapid Mutation and Decline (Early 1980s)** : The New Romantics scene mutated quickly, with fashion evolving from outrageous to sharp suits. Despite peripheral associations with groups like Ultravox, Japan, and Adam Ant, the rapid changes led to the scene largely burning out by 1982. "The New Romantic scene mutated rapidly. Fashion moved from the outrageous to sharp suits."
