# artist - The Clash

## members
Joe Strummer

## labels
CBS

## albums
**The Clash (1977) – Debut Album Recording** : The band recorded their self-titled debut album at CBS Studio 3, featuring songs based on real people and events from their experiences in London during the punk years. The back cover of the album features a photograph depicting the events of the Notting Hill riot from August 30, 1976. "When they entered CBS Studio 3 to record their self-titled debut album, they were armed with a bunch of songs based on real people and real events."

## songs
**White Riot (1977)** : This song was inspired by the Notting Hill riot on August 30, 1976, where 1,600 police officers clashed with residents, resulting in 300 injured officers, 35 overturned police vehicles, property damage, and 66 arrests. Joe Strummer was fascinated by the fortitude of the Black West Indian community who fought back against police harassment and questioned why white people didn't show the same resolve. The band stopped playing the song by the end of 1979 due to misinterpretation, with some calling it racist or taking it as an invitation to cause trouble, but it remained a Clash classic. "But that didn't stop it from becoming a clash classic."

## curiosities
**Early Inspiration – London Punk Years** : In late 1976 and through 1977, The Clash drew heavy inspiration from the social and economic turmoil in London during the punk rock era. The English economy was in shambles with strikes, high unemployment, rolling blackouts, and a pervasive class system. The band sought to document the experiences of being young, poor, and desperate in Britain during this period. "Inspiration was taken from what they saw around them in London during the punk years."

# artist - The Boomtown Rats

## members
Bob Geldof

## songs
**I Don't Like Mondays (1979)** : This song was written by Bob Geldof of The Boomtown Rats after he heard news of the Cleveland Elementary School shooting on January 29, 1979, while at a college radio station in Atlanta. The shooting was committed by 16-year-old Brenda Spencer, who killed principal Burton Wag and a custodian, injured eight children, and when asked why, stated, "I don't like Mondays. It livens up the day." Brenda Spencer is still imprisoned at the California Institution for Women in Chino, California. "It's based on the true story of a school shooting by Brenda Spencer in San Diego on Monday, January 29, 1979."

## songs
**Sunday, Bloody Sunday (1983)** : This song, featured on U2's *War* album, is not directly about the January 30, 1972 Derry massacre known as Bloody Sunday, but rather about the band's dilemma and conflict during the 1982 St. Patrick's Day Parade in New York City. U2's manager Paul McGinnis had secured them a spot, but they refused to march upon learning that IRA members would be participating, feeling it was wrong to export the conflict beyond Ireland. The song expresses the sentiment of questioning how long such hate will continue. "It's really more about the dilemma and the conflict you too had during the 1982 St. Patrick's Day Parade in New York City."

# artist - R.E.M.

## songs
**What's the Frequency, Kenneth? (N/A)** : This song is inspired by the October 1986 assault on CBS News anchor Dan Rather in New York City, where two men repeatedly attacked him while yelling, "Kenneth, what's the frequency?" While initially attributed to mentally ill William Tager, who believed the media beamed signals into his head, another theory suggests a connection to Texas writer Donald Bartholome, who featured characters named Kenneth and a newsman named "Mr. Lather" (portrayed as evil) in his stories. Bartholome and Rather both worked in Houston journalism in the late 1950s/early 1960s, leading to speculation of a long-standing grudge. "all of which help to culminate in one of REM's best-known songs."

# artist - Pearl Jam

## members
Eddie Vedder
Joseph Dell

## songs
**Jeremy (1991)** : This song became a major hit for Pearl Jam and was inspired by the true story of 16-year-old Jeremy Wade Dell, who committed suicide in front of his English class on January 8, 1991, in Richardson, Texas. Eddie Vedder was fascinated by the story, partly due to a personal experience in school where a classmate shot a fish tank with a sawed-off shotgun. Jeremy Dell's father, Joseph Dell, later expressed deep anguish over the song's popularity and the resulting attention from fans who visited his son's grave, left notes, and created websites, often idealizing Jeremy's actions and expressing adoration for Eddie Vedder. "My anguish is just as deep with each call, note or email."

## songs
**Polly (1991)** : Originally titled "Hitchhiker," Kurt Cobain wrote the first version of this song in June 1987 after reading a newspaper story about a 14-year-old girl who was kidnapped, raped, and tortured by Gerald Frant but managed to escape. The song was first recorded during the "Blue EP" sessions in 1989, refined during the "Nevermind" sessions, and the album version is a remix of that demo. During a 1991 *Nevermind* tour performance of "Polly," two individuals in the audience sexually assaulted a girl, leading Kurt Cobain to refer to them as "two wastes of sperm and eggs" in the *Incesticide* album liner notes. "Paulie based on a true story of kidnapping, rape and torture."

# curiosity

**The Troubles in Northern Ireland** : The period known as "The Troubles" involved intense fighting, clashes, and numerous deaths between Irish Protestants and Catholics in Northern Ireland, marked by shootings, nail bombs, and sniper fire involving the Irish Republican Army and the British Army. "It was the time of the troubles fighting between Irish Protestants and Catholics."
**Irish Republican Army vs. British Army Conflict** : The conflict in Northern Ireland during "The Troubles" involved violent clashes between the Irish Republican Army and the British Army, characterized by shootings, nail bombs, and sniper fire. "Shootings, nail bombs, sniper fire, the Irish Republican Army versus the British Army."
**Bloody Sunday (January 30, 1972)** : On January 30, 1972, during a banned march of up to 15,000 protesters in Derry, Northern Ireland, British soldiers opened fire after stones were thrown, resulting in 26 people shot, 13 dying at the scene, and one dying later. This event is known as the Bogside Massacre or Bloody Sunday. "The day became known as the Bogside Massacre to some and Bloody Sunday to others."
**Dan Rather Assault (October 1986)** : In October 1986, CBS News anchor Dan Rather was assaulted on Park Avenue in New York City by two well-dressed white men who repeatedly punched and kicked him while yelling, "Kenneth, what's the frequency?" The men disappeared and were never caught, initially leading to accusations that Rather fabricated the incident, though a doorman witnessed it. The attacker was later identified as William Tager, a mentally ill man who believed media, specifically Rather and CBS News, were beaming signals into his head. "Suddenly two well-dressed white men get in his way. The tall one with a mustache looks at Rather and says, Kenneth, what's the frequency?"
**School Shooting Precedent (1991)** : The suicide of Jeremy Wade Dell in front of his classmates on January 8, 1991, predated the Columbine High School massacre by seven years and was considered "absolutely insane" for a student to bring a gun to school to kill himself. "It was seven years before Columbine, and the idea of a kid bringing a gun to school to kill himself was absolutely insane."
**"Polly" Inspiration: Gerald Frant's Crimes** : The Nirvana song "Polly" was inspired by the June 1987 kidnapping, rape, and torture of a 14-year-old hitchhiking girl ("Polly") by Gerald Frant, a serial rapist paroled in 1980. Frant tortured the girl with whips, razors, and a blowtorch at his trailer, but she escaped from his van during a gas stop. Frant was arrested a day later and is serving two consecutive 75-year sentences in Eastern Washington state. "Kurt Cobain wasn't much for reading the newspaper, but one day in June of 1987 he came across a story about a 14 year old girl who had been kidnapped at gunpoint by a freak, ironically named Gerald Frant."
**David Milgaard Wrongful Conviction** : David Milgaard, a Manitoba man, was wrongfully convicted at age 16 for the 1970 rape and murder of 20-year-old Saskatoon nursing assistant Gail Miller, based on false testimony from two friends. He was sentenced to life in prison on January 31, 1970, and served 21 years before his release, eventually being exonerated and cleared of all crimes two years later. Inquiries into the miscarriage of justice led to Larry Fisher, the landlord of one of the false witnesses, being arrested, convicted, and imprisoned for Miller's murder, where he died in 2015. "Although he's never mentioned by name, the tragically hip song Weep Kings is about David Milgard, a Manitoba man who was convicted for the rape and murder of a 20 year old Saskatoon nursing assistant named Gail Miller."
**R. Budd Dwyer Suicide (January 22, 1987)** : R. Budd Dwyer, the Pennsylvania state treasurer, was convicted of accepting a $300,000 bribe from CTA, a company selected to resolve a state tax problem. The morning before his sentencing on January 23, 1987, Dwyer called a press conference, reiterated his innocence, and then fatally shot himself in the mouth with a .357 Magnum, an event captured on video by at least five cameras. The broadcast of this footage sparked widespread debate on appropriate news coverage. "The whole thing was caught on video by at least five people."
**Mount Vesuvius Eruption (79 AD) Inspiration** : The eruption of Mount Vesuvius in 79 AD, which destroyed Pompeii, served as inspiration for songs like "Pompeii" by Bastille and "Cities in Dust" by Siouxsie and the Banshees. "Both Pompey by Bastille and City's Indust from Suzy and the Banshee's are about the eruption of Mount Vesuvius in 79 AD."
**Fall of the Berlin Wall (1989) Inspiration** : The fall of the Berlin Wall in 1989 is recalled in the Jesus Jones song "Right Here, Right Now." "Right here, right now from Jesus Jones, recalls the fall of the Berlin Wall in 1989."
**1993 IRA Bombing Inspiration for "Zombie"** : The song "Zombie" by The Cranberries was written in response to a 1993 IRA bombing that caused the deaths of two individuals. "Zombie from the Cranberries is about a 1993 IRA bombing that killed two people."
**LA Riots (April 29, 1992) Inspiration** : The Los Angeles riots, which began on April 29, 1992, after the verdict in the Rodney King trial, directly inspired the Sublime song "April 29, 1992." "And April 29, 1992 from Sublime is all about the LA riots that erupted after the Rodney King trial."
