## albums
**Evil Empire (1996) – Second Studio Album** : Released on April 16, 1996, nearly four years after the band's debut record. The album debuted at number one on the album charts and sold millions of copies, with some of its songs being nominated for Grammy Awards. The lengthy gap between the debut and this album was due to extensive touring, non-album projects like movie soundtracks, and significant internal politics and dissension within the highly political band. An earlier attempt to record the album in Atlanta in late 1994, where 20 songs were written over four to five months, was aborted due to "tensions and creative differences." Recording eventually resumed in Hollywood and Melbourne, Australia. "Evil Empire came out in April 1996 and debuted at number one on the album charts."
**The Battle of Los Angeles (1999) – Third Studio Album** : Released on November 2, 1999, one day short of the seventh anniversary of their first record. Like their previous album, it debuted at number one on the album charts, notably preventing Mariah Carey's album from reaching the top spot. This album showcased Tom Morello's unique guitar playing style, characterized by the use of various effects and the powerful "space between the notes." "The third Rage Against the Machine album was called The Battle of Los Angeles. It came out on November 2nd 1999... And like the last album, it debuted at number 1 on the album charts."
**Renegades (2000) – Covers Album** : This album was a collection of covers released when the band did not manage to write any new original songs for a potential fourth studio album. It included a cover of Bruce Springsteen's "The Ghost of Tom Joad," which had been a staple in Rage's live performances for years and was also featured on a 1997 video collection. "The result was a collection of covers called Renegades. It featured a cover of a Bruce Springsteen song that became one of Tom Morello's all-time favorites."
**Live Album (2000) – Recorded at Olympic Grand Auditorium** : This live album was recorded at the Olympic Grand Auditorium in Los Angeles on September 13, 2000. This concert ultimately marked the band's last show for a significant period of time, as Zach de la Rocha announced his departure a month later. "In the end, Rage went it alone, recording a live album at the Olympic Grand Auditorium in Los Angeles. The date was September 13th, 2000, and this turned out to be the last ever show Rage would ever do for a long, long time."
**Rage Against The Machine with Chris Cornell (Bootleg CD)** : A bootleg CD featuring early recordings of the members of Rage Against the Machine (Tom Morello, Brad Wilk, Timmy C) jamming with Chris Cornell before they officially formed Audioslave. The first track on this bootleg was titled "Take It Out On Me," which was later released as "Cochise" on Audioslave's debut album. "I have a bootleg CD at home called Rage Against The Machine with Chris Cornell. The first track is called Take It Out On Me."

## curiosities
**Early Career Momentum and Perception (1993)** : After their Lollapalooza 1993 performance, Rage Against the Machine was perceived as a "new, young, angry band" whose debut album and live reaction indicated they were "destined to be the biggest band in the world." However, this potential did not fully materialize as expected due to various internal and external challenges. "Their debut album was so good and the reaction to what they played and said and performed was so positive that it seemed that they were just destined to be the biggest band in the world."
**Four-Year Album Gap Explanation (1992-1996)** : The significant four-year delay between Rage Against the Machine's debut album and their second record, Evil Empire, was attributed to three main factors: extensive touring, side projects (like recording movie soundtracks), and internal political dissension. This delay was unusual for a band whose debut was considered a "game changer," as maintaining momentum typically required a second album within 18 months. "First they toured and toured and toured. Second they got sidetracked on some non-album projects like recording songs for movie soundtracks. And third, internal politics."
**Band's Political Nature and Internal Dissension** : As a "highly political, super socially conscious band," Rage Against the Machine was seen as more than just a musical group, but a "political unit" aiming for "political, social and economic change." While their goals were admirable and standards high, the difficulty in achieving global change led to internal disillusionment and dissension, contributing to creative differences and tensions. "See here's a problem with a highly political, super socially conscious band like rage. You're more than just a band. You're a political unit using the system to drive political, social and economic change."
**Zach de la Rocha's Fan Engagement and Disillusionment** : Zach de la Rocha often cultivated strong relationships with fans by holding political discussions in venue parking lots after shows with groups of kids. However, he felt disillusioned when only a small fraction (e.g., 15 kids out of a 5,000-person crowd) stayed, questioning if their political message was truly getting through or if fans were merely pretending to engage. "For example, it was an uncommon to find Zach D. Leroka in the parking lot of the venue after the show holding a political discussion with 15 kids."
**Tom Morello's Songwriting Philosophy** : In an interview with Alternative Press magazine, Tom Morello articulated his view on songwriting, stating his disinterest in merely "good songs" that prompt toe-tapping. He aimed to write "great songs" that could "destroy cops and set fire to the suburbs," reflecting the band's confrontational political stance. "A good song should make you want to tap your toes and get with your girl. A great song should destroy cops and set fire to the suburbs. I'm only interested in writing great songs."
**Contradiction with Major Record Label** : Rage Against the Machine faced an "inherent contradiction" by being a left-wing collective while operating under a "multinational major record label." This arrangement created tension, as the band was expected to fulfill contractual obligations like touring, promotion, interviews, and MTV appearances, which often included editing out expletives from singles for radio and video play. "Complicating matters was the fact that they were a left-wing collective working for a multinational major record label."
**Attempt to Record Second Album in Atlanta (1994)** : In late 1994, Rage Against the Machine attempted to record their second album in Atlanta, where they moved into a house, jammed, and wrote 20 songs over four to five months. This effort was "aborted" due to escalating tensions and creative differences. During this period, they also fired their manager, reportedly because he "wasn't buying into the revolution." "They wrote 20 songs over the four or five months that they stayed in Atlanta, but tensions and creative differences got so crazy that everything was aborted."
**Recording Locations for Evil Empire** : After the failed Atlanta sessions, the band relocated to California to cool off. They then developed new songs in Hollywood, which were subsequently recorded in Melbourne, Australia, for their second album, Evil Empire. "New songs were fleshed out in Hollywood, and then recorded in all places, Melbourne, Australia."
**Meeting Subcomandante Marcos in the Mayan Jungle** : Rage Against the Machine accepted an invitation from the Zapatistas, a Libertarian Marxist group led by Subcomandante Marcos, who had declared war on the Mexican government in 1994 and used the internet to reach out to like-minded left-wing bands. Despite some media portraying the Zapatistas as terrorists, Rage met with their leader in the Mayan jungle, simultaneously condemning American politicians and military personnel as war criminals, showcasing their "very bold, very confrontational stuff." "While certain elements of the media portrayed the Zapatistas as terrorists, there was Rage Against the Machine, meeting with Subcomundente Marcos in the Mayan jungle."
**Tour with Wu-Tang Clan and Law Enforcement Issues** : Rage Against the Machine's decision to tour with the "very political hip-hop outfit" Wu-Tang Clan caused alarm among law enforcement authorities. Some police departments sought to block gigs due to fears of "civil insurrection and violence and riots." Additionally, the two groups eventually realized their politics were not fully aligned, leading to rumors of a backstage fight. "This combination scared certain law enforcement authorities along the itinerary, which created more difficulties."
**Saturday Night Live Incident (1996)** : During a performance on Saturday Night Live, Rage Against the Machine protested guest host Steve Forbes, a billionaire Republican presidential candidate, by hanging an American flag upside down as a sign of disrespect. They were only allowed to play one song and were swiftly escorted from the building. It was rumored that Zach de la Rocha intended to publicly denounce General Electric (NBC's parent company) as a manufacturer of weapons and perpetrator of war crimes during the First Gulf War. The band subsequently accused GE and NBC of censorship. "Rage didn't get a chance to play a second on that night because he was rumored that Zach was going to call out General Electric, the parent company of NBC, as manufacturers of weapons and perpetrators of war crimes in the first Gulf War."
**Advocacy and Support for Causes** : Rage Against the Machine consistently engaged in political activism, playing benefits and political rallies, and supporting various causes. These included the case of Mumia Abu-Jamal, a journalist on death row for a 1982 murder of a police officer in Philadelphia, and participating in the first Tibetan Freedom Festival, organized by the Beastie Boys. "This included the case of Mumaia Abu Jamal, a journalist who was on death row for killing a cop in Philadelphia in 1982, and included the first Tibetan Freedom Festival, which was organized by the Beastie Boys."
**Self-Perception as Cultural Revolutionaries** : The band attempted to reconcile their status as "major label rock stars" by presenting themselves as "soldiers on a cultural battlefield" and "evolutionaries" building a bridge between music and "the movement." "All the while, they tried to reconcile their position as major label rock stars by portraying themselves as soldiers on a cultural battlefield."
**Tom Morello's Guitar Style** : By the release of The Battle of Los Angeles, Tom Morello had developed a highly unique guitar playing style. He was known for using numerous effects to create diverse sounds, but a key characteristic highlighted was "the space between the notes," where "short gaps of nothing between the notes often say more than riffs that don't have gaps," creating a powerful effect. "By this time, he had developed a very unique playing style. He used lots of effects to get lots of different sounds. But when you listen to Tom's playing, the thing that really gets me is the space between the notes."
**Songs in Video Games and Movie Soundtracks** : Rage Against the Machine's music was featured in various media, including video games and movie soundtracks for films such as Godzilla and The Matrix. "There were songs and video games and songs on soundtracks for movies like Godzilla and the Matrix."
**Rhyme and Reason Tour Cancellation (2000)** : In the summer of 2000, Rage Against the Machine planned a 30-date "Rhyme and Reason Tour" with the Beastie Boys, featuring activist messages and a promise to donate a dollar from every ticket to charity. However, the tour was entirely canceled after Beastie Boys member Mike D dislocated his shoulder in a mountain bike accident, rendering him unable to perform. "But then Mike D drove his mountain bike into a pothole and dislocated his shoulder. Turned out to be so bad that the whole tour had to be canceled."
**Zach de la Rocha's Departure and Band Breakup (2000)** : On October 18, 2000, Zach de la Rocha announced his departure from Rage Against the Machine, stating that the band's "decision-making process has completely failed" and no longer met the collective aspirations or his "artistic and political ideal." His exit marked the end of the band's initial run, attributed to the toll taken by competing philosophies and ideologies within the group. "On October 18th, 2000, Zach D. LaRocca released a statement, I feel that it is now necessary to leave Rage because our decision-making process has completely failed."
**Rage's Lifespan Aligns with Don Letts' Theory** : Rage Against the Machine formed in 1991 and broke up in 2000, lasting approximately nine years. This timeframe aligns with British DJ and filmmaker Don Letts' theory that the average lifespan of most bands is seven to nine years, after which they typically "explode" due to internal pressures like creative differences and diverging lifestyles. "They formed in 1991, got big real fast, and right on schedule, they broke up in 2000, torn apart by a series of internal conflicts."
**Zach de la Rocha's Unsuccessful Solo Sessions** : After Rage's breakup, Zach de la Rocha attempted solo projects. He worked with DJ Shadow on a planned "silhouette" project that never materialized. Later, sessions with Trent Reznor were also unsuccessful, with Reznor stating, "those tapes will never see the light of day." Although a few of Zach's songs were eventually released, a full solo album never emerged from these efforts. "He worked with DJ Shadow with the hopes of making a silhouette, but that didn't work. Then he hooked up with Trent Rezner. But those sessions were a disaster too."
**Reunion for Coachella (2007) Driven by George Bush** : Despite significant animosity rumored between Zach de la Rocha and the other band members, and numerous rejected offers for reunion, Rage Against the Machine eventually reunited for the Coachella Music Festival in April 2007, following rumors in late 2006. According to Tom Morello, the catalyst for their reunion was the political climate under President George Bush, as the band felt compelled to "stand up and oppose what was going on with the U.S. domestically and internationally." "Well according to Tom Morello, it was George Bush. The time had simply come for a band like Rage to stand up and oppose what was going on with the U.S. domestically and internationally."
**Post-Reunion Activities and No New Albums** : Since their 2007 reunion, Rage Against the Machine has toured, engaged in "rabble rousing at various political conventions," and generally continued "stirring the pot" regarding political issues. However, there has been no indication that the band plans to record any new studio albums together, primarily focusing on live performances and activism. "However, there were never any indications that there would be a new album."
**Future Recording Potential** : Despite the band's current focus on live gigs and activism without new recordings, the possibility of future material is left open. The idea is posed that if "circumstances are right" and they feel a need to deliver a new message, they might record again, though the value of preserving their legacy of three "influential and revolutionary" studio albums is also considered. "If circumstances are right and they feel they need to get together to deliver a new message, I can see them doing it."

## curiosities
**Formation of the Band** : After Zach de la Rocha left Rage Against the Machine, the remaining instrumentalists (Tom Morello, Brad Wilk, Timmy C) considered finding a new singer for Rage but were ultimately connected with Chris Cornell by producer Rick Rubin. During their first rehearsals, the immediate chemistry led to the writing of 21 songs in 19 days, including "Exploder," "Light My Way," and "Bring 'Em Back Alive." The members were committed to forming a full band, not just a one-off project, aiming for their new work to be "the best thing" they had ever done. "The first time we got together, we just jammed together and we decided, well, let's just try writing some songs. And I think the first three days of rehearsal with Chris, we wrote, exploder, light my way, and bring them back alive."
**Chris Cornell's Initial Reaction to Jamming** : Chris Cornell arrived at the initial rehearsal with a "pretty cavalier attitude," expecting to know within 10 minutes if the collaboration would work. He realized it would be great almost immediately but stayed for an hour and a half before confirming his commitment to forming a band, despite the others wanting to continue jamming. They quickly developed seven songs within a couple of days. "Within 10 minutes, I knew that it would be great. But I stuck in there for maybe an hour and a half. And then they were ready to just keep going and keep going."

# concert - Rhyme and Reason Tour

## curiosities
**Canceled Joint Tour (2000)** : The Rhyme and Reason Tour was a planned 30-date tour in the summer of 2000 featuring Rage Against the Machine and the Beastie Boys. It was intended to carry activist messages and donate a dollar from each ticket to charity. However, the tour was entirely canceled due to Beastie Boys member Mike D dislocating his shoulder in a mountain bike accident. "Rage announced that they were going to do a 30-date tour with the Beastie Boys called the Rhyme and Reason Tour... the whole tour had to be canceled."

# concert - Olympic Grand Auditorium (Los Angeles, Sep 13, 2000)

## curiosities
**Rage Against the Machine's Last Show for a Long Time** : Rage Against the Machine recorded a live album at the Olympic Grand Auditorium in Los Angeles on September 13, 2000. This concert proved to be the band's final performance for an extended period, as Zach de la Rocha announced his departure a month later, leading to the band's initial breakup. "The date was September 13th, 2000, and this turned out to be the last ever show Rage would ever do for a long, long time."
