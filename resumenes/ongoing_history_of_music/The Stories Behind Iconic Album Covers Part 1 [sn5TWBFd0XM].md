## albums
**Ramones (1976) – Self-Titled Debut** : Released on April 23, 1976, this album features a simple black and white photograph of the four band members in ripped jeans and leather jackets, posed against a graffiti-covered brick wall. This aesthetic was intended to represent the band as a unified "gang." The photo was taken by Roberta Bailey for *Punk Magazine* and was chosen after the band's initial preference for a style resembling The Beatles' *Meet the Beatles* (1964) was rejected by Sire Records art director Tony Wadler. The image was taken in Albert's Garden, a private community garden in New York City, and manager Danny Fields approved of its perfection. The cover helped solidify a distinctive punk-fashion look and has since become one of the most imitated album poses, even becoming part of the permanent collection of the Museum of Modern Art in New York. "This is a perfect representation of the band as a gang aesthetic."

## curiosities
**Iconic Cover Photography Fee** : Photographer Roberta Bailey was paid a total of $125 for capturing the image used on the Ramones' self-titled debut album, which went on to become one of the most iconic images in rock and roll history. "Roberta Bailey was paid a grand total of $125."

# artist - Sex Pistols

## members
Steve Jones

## labels

## albums
**Never Mind the Bollocks, Here's the Sex Pistols (1977)** : Released on October 28, 1977, this album's cover features a solid day-glow background, with the full title "Never Mind the Bollocks Here's the Sex Pistols" spelled out in a mix of different fonts and upper/lower case letters, resembling a ransom note. Two versions exist: one with a pink background and another with yellow. The artist, Jamie Reid, developed this ransom note style while working for *Suburban Press*. The title was changed in mid-1977 from an initial concept of "God Save the Sex Pistols" after guitarist Steve Jones heard fans using the phrase "Never Mind the Bollocks," meaning "Stop Talking Rubbish." "The first three lines are different fonts, while the band's name is styled in a mix of upper and lower case letters, reminiscent of a ransom note made out of cut up letters from a newspaper."

## curiosities
**"Bollocks" Obscenity Controversy** : The word "ballocks" on the album cover was deemed obscene by some upon its release, leading to significant problems for record stores and radio stations. In London, police visited Virgin Record stores displaying the album poster, threatening prosecution under the 1889 Indecent Advertisements Act. This led to managers removing posters and at least one store manager being arrested. The controversy escalated when the story hit newspapers before the album's release, prompting ads like "The album will last, the sleeve may not." A court case on November 24, 1977, involved expert witnesses, including an English professor, testifying that "ballocks" was an old English term for priest or, in context, meant "nonsense." Everyone involved was ultimately found not guilty, and the album became highly influential. "The word ballocks was deemed obscene by some, which caused all sorts of issues for record stores in radio."

# artist - Depeche Mode

## members
Alan Wilder

## albums
**Music for the Masses (1987)** : Depeche Mode's 1987 album cover features a beige background with the band's logo and album title in stark block letters. The central image depicts three red megaphones mounted on a pole, set in an isolated location without an audience, literally "in the middle of nowhere" in England's Peak District. This concept was developed by long-time collaborator Martin Atkins, who favored megaphones and the idea of speakers in an unconventional setting, disconnected from actual "masses." The band and label embraced the concept, using megaphones throughout the album's marketing. "The image features three red megaphones, mounted on a pole, ready to broadcast to, well, apparently no one."

## curiosities
**Rejected "Music for the Masses" Cover** : An alternative cover for Depeche Mode's *Music for the Masses* was created, featuring a stylized megaphone in a white and orange motif with drawn sound waves. Approximately 135 copies were prepared for distribution to stores before the band and label had a change of heart, recalling and destroying nearly all of them. About a dozen copies remained with retailers. In 2011, keyboardist Alan Wilder auctioned off his copy, fetching around $4,000, and it is now considered extremely rare, with Discogs reporting no sales of this version. "About 135 copies were prepared for shipping to stores, but then the band and the label had a change of heart and had all those copies recalled."

# artist - Red Hot Chili Peppers

## members

## albums
**Mother's Milk (1989)** : Released on August 16, 1989, the cover of *Mother's Milk* features a black and white photograph of a giant, topless woman, Don Lane, cradling all four band members in her arms, with a rose covering her left nipple. The original idea came from Anthony Kiedis, inspired by a 1970s Sly and the Family Stone poster he had as a child. Guitarist John Frusciante proved difficult during the photoshoot, rejecting all but one photo of himself laughing. Although Ioni Skye, Anthony Kiedis's girlfriend at the time, was also photographed, Don Lane's image was ultimately chosen. "A black and white photo of a giant, topless woman, cradling all four members of the band in her arms with her left nipple covered by a rose."

## curiosities
**"Mother's Milk" Nudity Lawsuit** : The cover of *Mother's Milk* sparked controversy due to its depiction of Don Lane, a topless woman cradling the band members. Lane claimed the band did not ask for her permission to use her image. Several record store chains, including Walmart, refused to stock or display the record due to perceived female nudity, despite the minimal exposure. Walmart specifically opted for a censored version where band members were enlarged to cover more of Lane. Compounding the issue, limited promotional posters were distributed with Lane's breasts completely exposed, again without her knowledge. Lane subsequently sued the band and was awarded a $50,000 settlement. "She sued the band and won a settlement of $50,000."

## albums
**Licensed to Ill (1986)** : This monster album became the first rap record to reach number one on the Billboard charts, setting high expectations for the Beastie Boys' follow-up. "a monster album that became the first rap record to reach number one on the Billboard charts."
**Paul's Boutique (1989)** : This album, released in 1989, was the highly anticipated follow-up to *Licensed to Ill*. It was initially a commercial flop because it diverged significantly from expectations, being ahead of its time. The packaging is noteworthy, best viewed in its vinyl gatefold form, which unfolds from 12x12 inches to 12 inches by three feet, revealing a panoramic photo of Ludlow Street on Manhattan's Lower East Side, specifically from the perspective of 99 Ludlow Street. While MCA's alias Nathaniel Hornblower was credited, the real photographer was Jeremy Chattan, the Beastie Boys' original bass player from their punk era, who took about 30 rolls of film, including some lost bird's-eye shots from 101 Park Avenue. "Instead, they released something so ahead of its time that the record was initially a commercial flop."

## curiosities
**"Paul's Boutique" Store Fictionality** : The titular "Paul's Boutique" store depicted on the album cover never actually existed. The name was conjured up by Mike D, and the actual store photographed at 99 Ludlow Street was Lee's Sportswear, where the band hung a temporary sign. Today, neither Lee's Sportswear nor the adjacent Ben's Shoes remains at the location, with Google Street View showing a bank in its place. "There was no such store as Paul's boutique. That was the name Mike D. conjured up."

## albums
**Dirt (1992)** : Alice in Chains' second album, *Dirt*, released in 1992, represented a full pivot to their grunge sound and image, having evolved from their earlier hair metal style. Given the album's dark themes, photographer Rocky Schenk aimed for a "hellish" and disturbing cover. The artwork features a nude woman, Mariah O'Brien, half-buried in what appears to be a cracked, dry desert floor, leaving her fate ambiguous. Layne Staley revealed his input for the cover was inspired by "a certain person who basically buried my ass so the woman on the cover is kind of the portrayal of that person getting sucked down into the dirt instead of me." The "desert" was actually a set built in Schenk's Hollywood studio using miniatures for mountains and foam core/clay for the ground. "So what we have is a cover featuring a nude woman half-barried in what looks like a cracked and dry desert floor."
**Music Bank (1999) – Box Set** : This 1999 box set includes additional photos from the *Dirt* album cover photoshoot. "If you have the 1999 box set Music Bank, it contains a few."
**Black Gives Way to Blue (2009)** : The booklet for *Black Gives Way to Blue*, the first Alice in Chains album featuring new singer William DuVall, includes a photo of Mariah O'Brien holding her heart. "And that's her holding her heart inside the booklet of Black Gives Way to Blue, the first Ellison Chains album with new singer William DuVall."

## curiosities
**Mariah O'Brien's Cover Shoot** : Mariah O'Brien, known for appearing on Spinal Tap's "Bitch School" single, posed for the *Dirt* album cover on June 14, 1992. She lay in the body-shaped hole in the studio's "desert" set for eight hours while photographer Rocky Schenk took hundreds of shots and adjusted lighting. O'Brien later recalled having to hold her urge to urinate during the lengthy session. It was clarified that O'Brien, not singer Layne Staley's then-girlfriend Demri Parrott, is the woman on the cover. Today, Mariah O'Brien works as an interior designer in Los Angeles. "Mariah had to lie in that hole for eight hours as Schenk took hundreds of shots, adjusting the lighting countless times."

# artist - Nirvana

## members
Kurt Cobain

## albums
**In Utero (1993)** : The cover artwork for Nirvana's 1993 album *In Utero*, designed by Art Director Robert Fisher, features a winged figure on a pale, cracked background. This angelic figure is depicted as ripped open, exposing bare bones and organs, reflecting Kurt Cobain's fascination with human anatomy as art, creating an image that is both peaceful and disturbing. The figure is actually a "transparent anatomical mannequin," a life-sized, anatomically correct female model used for teaching anatomy, which was found at a medical supply store in the Mall of America in Minneapolis. Nirvana used several such mannequins as stage props for the *In Utero* tour. The back cover, photographed by Charles Peterson, displays various items from the medical store and Kurt's personal collection, including plastic fetuses, body parts, a turtle shell, orchids, and lilies, all laid out on his living room floor. These items incorporate symbols from *The Woman's Dictionary of Symbols and Sacred Objects*, aligning with Kurt's interest in artwork representing themes of sex, women, birth, and death, which also hints at the raw music within the album. "The angelic figure is ripped open. We see bare bones and exposed organs, but not to Kurt s fascination with human anatomy as art. It's both peaceful and disturbing."

## songs

## curiosities
**"In Utero" Title Evolution** : Kurt Cobain initially wanted to title Nirvana's 1993 album "I Hate Myself and Want to Die," claiming it was a joke. However, the title was ultimately changed to *In Utero* due to concerns that the original title could encourage dangerous behavior and potentially lead to lawsuits. Another rejected title was "Verse Chorus Verse," which was also the original name of the song later known as "Sappy." The final title, *In Utero*, was derived from a poem written by Courtney Love. "In the end, the title In Yudero was chosen because everyone realized that I hate myself and I want to die could encourage people to do some dangerous things and perhaps result in some lawsuits."

# artist - Pearl Jam

## members
Jeff Ament

## labels

## albums
**Ten (1991)** : This album achieved significant success, prompting high anticipation for Pearl Jam's subsequent release. "How would Pearl Jam follow up the glories of the ten record?"
**Vs. (1993)** : Pearl Jam's highly anticipated album, released in stages in October 1993, features an Angora goat on its cover, not a sheep. The photograph was taken by bassist Jeff Ament at Lifeline Farm in Victor, Montana. The goat, described as friendly and curious, was unaware it was destined for slaughter, a metaphor Jeff Ament used to express the band's feeling of being "slaves to the star making machinery of the popular song," which also explains the original working title, "Five Against One," and the final title, "Vs." Guitarist Stone Gossard stated that the title represented the struggles of making a record and maintaining one's independence and soul "Versus Everyone Else's." Other panels of the album feature symbolic images of human feet walking in mud, representing the band. "The goat, by the way, wasn't angry at all. It was very friendly, very curious and completely oblivious to the fact that it was soon to be slaughtered."

## curiosities
**"Vs." Anti-System Release Strategy** : Pearl Jam deliberately released *Vs.* in stages, with the vinyl edition first appearing in the UK on October 11, 1993, followed by North America on October 12. The CD and cassette versions were delayed until October 18 and 19, respectively. This staggered release was a conscious effort by the band to "mess with the system" and "stick it to the label," Epic Records, which they felt was trying to kill off vinyl in favor of more expensive and profitable CDs. In 1993, none of the band members even owned a CD player, and Jeff Ament preferred a cassette version as his primary listening format. Further challenging the label, the vinyl edition was released without the album title or band name printed on it, forcing Epic to add a sticker to the shrink wrap. "Why? Phil Jim just wanted to mess with the system. So they did. In their mind, it was another way to stick it to the label."
**"Vs." Unauthorized Image Controversy** : Early pressings of Pearl Jam's *Vs.* album included a picture of an elderly woman who reportedly never granted permission for her image to be used. This led to later pressings featuring a different woman, and the liner notes humorously stating, "the new and improved woman behind the counter." "She apparently never gave permission for her image to be used."

## albums
**Siamese Dream (1993)** : The Smashing Pumpkins' second album, *Siamese Dream*, was released on July 19, 1993, in the UK and a week later in North America. Billy Corgan initially envisioned an "outsider artist" creating an avant-garde, untrained, but passionate cover that might not be widely understood or liked, but this idea was rejected by the label. Instead, a photograph by Melody McDaniel featuring two seven-year-old child models, Ali Langer and Los Angeles Roberts, was chosen. The girls, who had never met before but displayed instant chemistry, were photographed in Los Angeles. They were kept smiling with popsicles from a passing ice cream truck and only settled down when promised they could keep the shoes they were trying on. "The label rejected the idea and a photo of two little girls was selected."

## curiosities
**"Siamese Dream" Cover Models Reunion** : The two young girls featured on *The Smashing Pumpkins'* *Siamese Dream* album cover, Ali Langer and Los Angeles Roberts, were child models who met for the first time at the photoshoot in Los Angeles. They are not Siamese twins, as the title might imply. In 2018, Langer and Roberts, now a nurse and an IT professional respectively, reunited to help promote a reformed Smashing Pumpkins and their tour. It has been confirmed that one-time Smashing Pumpkins bassist Nicole Fiorentino is not one of the girls on the cover, despite a claim she made that even fooled Billy Corgan. "In 2018, Ali and Los Angeles got back together to help promote a reformed smashing pumpkins and a tour."

# curiosity

**Vinyl Resurgence and Appeal** : Vinyl records have experienced an unexpected comeback, now outselling CDs in countries like Canada, the US, the UK, and Australia. Many consumers are purchasing vinyl even without a turntable, drawn to its tangible and physical nature as a representation of music. Fans often stream music while holding the vinyl, appreciating the physical ownership, liner notes, lyric sheets, and artwork. "Vinyl is once again outselling CDs and a surprising number of people are buying vinyl, even if they don't have a turntable."
**Evolution of Album Artwork Presentation** : The glory of album artwork faced decline with the advent of digital formats. Initially, it was shrunk to CD size, then reduced to a postage stamp-sized image on platforms like iTunes, and became almost non-existent with streaming services. In pre-digital and pre-CD days, artwork was crucial for the listening experience, mystery, mystique, and marketing of music, sometimes even prompting purchases solely based on a cool-looking cover. Album artwork was once highly revered, inspiring coffee table book collections like *The 100 Best Album Covers* and *The Album Cover Album*, which treated rock and pop album covers with respect and offered insights into both the musical artists and the artists who created the artwork. "For a while, we were in real danger of seeing the glories of album artwork disappear."
**1990s Album Art Production Budget** : During the 1990s, there were significant budgets allocated for the creation of album artwork, allowing for elaborate physical sets and detailed photography, in contrast to modern methods like AI-generated artwork. "Now keep in mind this was the 90s when there was a budget for this sort of thing and we didn't have AI-generated artwork."
