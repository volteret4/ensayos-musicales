# instrument - Synthesizer

## curiosities
**Early Challenges (1940s-1950s)** : Synthesizer technology dates back to at least the 1940s. Early models, like one at Columbia University, filled entire rooms. The first truly "portable" synths still required transport on rail cars, ran very hot, consumed immense electricity, and were incredibly complex to program. "Synthesizer technology goes back to at least the 1940s. A special electronic lab was created in Cologne, West Germany in 1951. Columbia University in New York also had a synthesizer that filled an entire room."
**1960s Miniaturization and Cost (1960s)** : Engineers like Don Buchla and Bob Moog began manufacturing smaller, solid-state synthesizer units. However, these were still expensive and "insanely untangable," often regarded more as "nerdy novelties" than serious musical instruments. "That began to change in the 1960s when engineers like Don Bukhla and Bob Mogue began to manufacture small solid state units."
**Golden Age Factors (Mid-1970s)** : The "Golden Age of synthesizers" was ushered in during the mid-1970s, characterized by synths becoming rapidly cheaper, smaller, and more portable. This increased accessibility, coupled with the punk rock explosion, fundamentally changed musical composition and direction. "First of all, synth gear was rapidly coming down in price and keyboards were getting smaller and more portable. The second reason was the punk rock explosion."
**Monophonic vs. Polyphonic Challenges (1970s-Early 1980s)** : Early monophonic synths like the Roland SH-1 were difficult to program live, requiring frantic patch setup between songs with flashlights. Polyphonic keyboards, which allowed for playing chords, arrived around 1980-81 (e.g., Prophet 5, Roland Jupiter-8), but still required reprogramming between every song, making live performance challenging. "The programmeable synthesises when we moved away from the presets, they were still little monophonic ones like the Roll and S8 ones."
**Distinctive Manufacturer Sounds (1980s)** : During the era of classic synthesizers, each manufacturer developed a unique sonic character. Musicians often gravitated to a specific brand (e.g., Korg, Roland, Oberheim, Yamaha) because their instruments offered distinct sounds and features. "Each manufacturer had a sound. Yeah, they did. So you were a corg person. You were a rolling person. You were an over-hime person. You were a Yamaha person and they were all different."
**Vintage vs. Soft Synths (Contemporary)** : Older, vintage synthesizers, despite their finicky nature, weight, and tendency to drift out of tune, are now highly collectible and expensive (e.g., a Jupiter-8 on eBay can cost £2000). Many contemporary musicians prefer modern "soft synths" in computers for their reliability, consistent tuning, and ease of use, despite nostalgic purists. "The funny thing is now, you know, a lot of youngsters are looking to collect these things. You try and get a Jupiter 8 on eBay now. It's like £2000 or something."

# instrument - Mellotron

## curiosities
**Tape-Based Operation (Unspecified)** : The Mellotron is an antique, tape-based instrument where each key triggers a specific piece of tape, which plays for approximately 11 seconds. When the key is released, a spring retracts the tape. Sustain is limited to the tape's length, and changing sounds requires manually unscrewing the top and replacing entire looms of 36 tapes. "Okay. So basically, there is a tape. It's not a tape loop. It's a piece of tape that lasts about 20 seconds. No, it's 11. Oh, is it? Yeah."
**Live Performance Unreliability (Unspecified)** : Mellotrons were notoriously difficult to use in live settings, especially with unstable power sources. Voltage fluctuations could cause pitch issues, and the instrument was prone to breakdowns, requiring the player to "chase the pitch wheel" or wait for stable power conditions (e.g., waiting for people in a French town to stop cooking). "He's, you know, which just happened to us a few times. I said, he's chasing the pitch wheel, trying to, it's going slow, it's going slow. I'm losing the tune."
**Early Sampling Device (Unspecified)** : The Mellotron is considered an early form of sampling device. Bands could commission custom tapes with their own unique sounds and special effects, allowing them to reproduce specific audio from their records in a live setting that couldn't be achieved otherwise. "But you know, at the time, they were basically the first sampling device. And a lot of bands would actually, you could get your own tapes made up."
**Famous Examples in Music (1967-1970s)** : Notable uses include the flute sound at the beginning of The Beatles' "Strawberry Fields Forever," Procol Harum's "A Whiter Shade of Pale," a violin sound in OMD's "Made of All" (often mistaken for bagpipes), Lynyrd Skynyrd's "Tuesday's Gone," and extensive use by Pink Floyd. "The flute on strawberry fields is probably the most famous melaton."
