## An Exploration of Musical Superlatives and Curiosities in New Rock and Alternative Music

### Introduction: The Quest for Musical Superlatives

The human desire for recognition and legacy often manifests in extraordinary feats, from the bizarre, like Monsieur Mauchtoute (Michel Lotito) consuming an entire Cessna 150 airplane due to a uniquely thick stomach lining, to the groundbreaking in the arts. This drive for "world records" extends deeply into the world of new rock and alternative music, revealing fascinating insights into artists, their creations, and the culture surrounding them. This document delves into some of these remarkable achievements and oddities, collected from the annals of music history.

### Artistic Evolution and Enduring Influence: Lou Reed

*   **The Longest-Serving Figure in Alt-Rock:** Lou Reed is identified as the longest continuously recording and touring musician in the alt-rock/new rock sphere, maintaining a career spanning over 50 years. This longevity surpasses even iconic figures like Iggy Pop (who began in 1965) and David Bowie (whose first record, "Liza Jane," was released in 1964).
*   **Early Career Roots in Doo-Wop:** Reed's earliest foray into music was in 1958, at the remarkably young age of 14. He was part of a doo-wop group called The Shades, formed during his high school years on Long Island.
*   **First Recorded Moments:** The Shades recorded a single in 1958, featuring the song "So Blue." This recording represents Lou Reed's first-ever documented musical output.
*   **Profound Stylistic Divergence and Influence:** The sound of "So Blue" stands in stark contrast to the experimental and gritty aesthetic that would define Reed's later work with The Velvet Underground and his solo career. Despite these humble, early beginnings in doo-wop, Reed's subsequent artistic evolution would profoundly influence a vast array of future alt-rock luminaries, including David Bowie, Iggy Pop, R.E.M., and The Killers, among many others. His early career thus serves as a powerful example of an artist's transformative journey.

### Key Works and Production Details: Lou Reed's "Walk on the Wild Side"

*   **Iconic Status:** Released in 1972, "Walk on the Wild Side" remains Lou Reed's most widely recognized and celebrated song.
*   **Bowie's Production Hand:** The track was produced by none other than David Bowie, highlighting a significant collaboration between two pivotal figures in alternative music.
*   **Unexpected Pedagogical Connection:** The distinctive saxophone part on the track was performed by the very musician who had taught David Bowie how to play the saxophone when Bowie himself was 14 years old, creating a unique lineage in its production.
*   **Session Musician Contributions:** The bass lines were laid down by Herbie Flowers, a session musician who was reportedly paid a mere $30 for his contribution, illustrating common practices in recording sessions.

### Artistic Philosophy and Unique Production: Kid Carpet

*   **"Casio Best (Failed World Record Attempt)":** This 2008 track by Kid Carpet (real name Ed Patrick) was conceived as an ode to the Beijing Olympics.
*   **Embracing Lo-Fi Aesthetics:** Kid Carpet's artistic philosophy champions the use of accessible and unconventional instruments, specifically favoring "cheap keyboards and Fisher Price guitars." This approach reflects a DIY ethos prevalent in certain corners of alternative music.
*   **Ambitious (and Foiled) Vocal Plans:** Patrick intended to use a Furby, the popular electronic toy, for backing vocals, showcasing a playful and experimental attitude towards sound creation.
*   **Anecdotal Misfortune:** This unique vocal plan was unfortunately thwarted when the Furby was stolen at a gig, an amusing anecdote that underscores the unpredictable nature of live performances and the fragility of unusual artistic tools.

### Performance Records and Instrumental Extremes

*   **Largest Drum Set:** Contrary to popular belief which often points to Neil Peart of Rush, the Guinness Book of World Records recognizes Chad Smith of the Red Hot Chili Peppers for performing with the largest drum kit.
    *   **Scale of the Kit:** Smith utilized an astonishing 308-piece drum set for this record.
    *   **Context of the Performance:** This monumental setup was used for a special, one-off drum-clinic performance in 2006, arranged as a favor to a friend who owned a music store and for Pearl Drums, the manufacturer Smith endorses. It was not a touring kit.
*   **Record-Breaking Live Concert Attendance and Gross:** The Red Hot Chili Peppers' Hyde Park concerts in London, held over three nights in the summer of 2004, attracted an audience of 245,000 people and grossed $17 million, setting a significant record for live music events of its kind.
*   **Fastest Rapper:** Rebel XD holds the record for rapping 852 syllables in just 42 seconds, a feat achieved at a competition in Chicago on July 27, 2007.

### Mass Participation Musical Events

*   **"Smoke on the Water" Rendition:** In 2006, Kansas City, Missouri, witnessed 1,721 people simultaneously playing the iconic riff from Deep Purple's "Smoke on the Water."
*   **"Knockin' on Heaven's Door" Rendition:** This record was later surpassed on October 26, 2007, in Shalong, Mejhalaya, India, where an astounding 5,730 people played Bob Dylan's "Knockin' on Heaven's Door" in unison, showcasing a remarkable display of collective musical performance.

### Rare Collectibles and Music Archaeology

*   **The Former Pinnacle: Sex Pistols' "God Save the Queen" A&M Single:** For a significant period, the most valuable alt-rock collectible was the Sex Pistols' "God Save the Queen" 7-inch single released on A&M Records in 1977.
    *   **Context of Rarity:** The Sex Pistols were notorious for their unstable record deals, signing with EMI, then A&M for a mere five days before being fired due to objections from other artists on the label.
    *   **Accidental Creation of Rarity:** During their brief tenure at A&M, approximately 25,000 copies of the single were pressed. Following their dismissal, these records were ordered to be destroyed.
    *   **Survival and Value:** A few dozen copies were "liberated" from the pressing plant, some given to A&M executives as gifts, and others ended up with band members (Steve Jones was known to sell copies for cash). These surviving singles became immensely valuable, with one selling for $24,000 at auction at the time of the podcast.
*   **The New Record Holder: The Velvet Underground's Scepter Studios Sessions Acetate:** This unique and historically significant item eventually claimed the title of the most valuable alt-rock collectible.
    *   **Accidental Discovery:** In September 2002, Warren Hill of Montreal discovered the acetate in a box of records at a street sale in Chelsea, New York City, purchasing it and two other records for just 75 cents.
    *   **Physical Peculiarities:** The record was unusually pressed on brittle aluminum and bore the enigmatic label "Velvet Underground, 425-66 Attention in Dulf."
    *   **Historical Significance:** It was identified as a one-of-a-kind, undiscovered acetate pressing of early sessions from Scepter Studios that would later contribute to The Velvet Underground's seminal debut album. This find offered a direct auditory link to the formative stages of a band widely regarded as the first true alt-rock group.
    *   **Auction Saga:** The record's journey to sale was tumultuous: an initial eBay bid reached $155,401 but was not honored. It was eventually sold in December 2006 for $25,200.
    *   **Fragility and Preservation:** Due to its brittle aluminum composition, the acetate could only be played approximately two dozen times before degradation. Recognizing its immense historical value, someone had the foresight and daring to rip it to MP3, allowing its crackly, lo-fi, but authentic sound (an alternate take of "Heroin") to be preserved and heard by others.

### Art as Commodity: The Thurston Revival

*   **Highest Price for a Debut Single:** Dan O'Donnell, performing as The Thurston Revival (a Vancouver-based artist), holds the record for the highest retail price ever set for a debut single.
    *   **"Somewhere There's An Angel":** In 2007, O'Donnell released 100 vinyl copies of this song, each priced at $200.
    *   **Art Project Concept:** The high price was deliberately set as an "art project," serving as a critical statement on the commodification and perceived devaluation of music in the public consciousness.
    *   **Creative Inspiration:** The song itself took two years to write and was inspired by Arthur Miller's classic play, *The Crucible*.
    *   **Collaborative Artistic Element:** To further enhance its artistic value, ten acclaimed British artists each designed ten unique sleeves for the record, turning each copy into a distinct piece of art, many of which were subsequently exhibited in galleries.

### Pioneering Sounds and Genre Records: Moby

*   **"Thousand" – The Fastest Dance Song:** Moby, the "T-Drinking, vegetarian" artist, achieved a Guinness World Record for the fastest dance song with his 1992 track "Thousand," found on the B-side of the single "I Feel It."
    *   **Extreme Tempo:** The song reaches an astonishing 1000 beats per minute (BPM).
    *   **Context of the Record:** While technology has since enabled the creation of music reaching up to 15,000 BPM (known as "Extra Tone"), these tracks have generally not achieved commercial success as hit singles. Moby's "Thousand" notably reached number 38 on the British charts, cementing its status as the fastest *hit* dance record.
    *   **Tempo Reference Points:** For context, typical rock and hip-hop tempos range from 80 to 100 BPM, house music between 120 and 135 BPM, and extreme techno genres like Gabar and Speedcore exceed 200 BPM. Moby's achievement significantly pushed these boundaries within popular music.

### The World's Largest Private Music Collection

*   **Paul Mooney's Unrivaled Collection:** Paul Mooney of Pittsburgh amassed what is recognized as the world's largest private record collection.
    *   **Decades of Collecting:** The collection began 50 years prior to 2008 with a single Frankie Laine 45-single.
    *   **Immense Scale:** As of early 2008, it comprised over 3 million records and 300,000 compact discs.
    *   **Genre Diversity:** The collection is comprehensive, encompassing virtually every imaginable genre: rock, country, R&B, comedy, Broadway show tunes, soundtracks, children's records, bluegrass, folk, new age jazz, and more.
    *   **Format Inclusivity:** It spans an extensive range of physical formats, including LPs, EPs, 78s, 45s, cassettes, 8-tracks, CD singles, and full albums.
    *   **Logistical Challenges:** When the collection reached 160,000 items, Mooney's wife insisted it be moved out of their home, leading him to acquire a 16,000-square-foot climate-controlled warehouse to house it.
    *   **Attempted Sale and Valuation:** Due to age, health concerns (diabetes), and the sheer cost of maintenance, Mooney attempted to sell the entire collection on eBay. The minimum reserve bid was $3 million, a figure estimated to be significantly below its true value. Appraisers suggested the collection, with its numerous rarities, was worth closer to $50 million. For comparison, acquiring an equivalent number of songs (approximately 6 million) on iTunes would cost over $5.9 million.
    *   **Unsuccessful Auction:** Despite its incredible scope and value, the eBay auction ultimately fell through, leaving Paul Mooney as the reluctant, yet still record-holding, owner of this monumental archive.
