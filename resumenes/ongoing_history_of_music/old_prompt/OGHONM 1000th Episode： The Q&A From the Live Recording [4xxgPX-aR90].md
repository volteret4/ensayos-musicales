As a music historian and critic, this text from Alan Cross's *Ongoing History of New Music* offers a rich tapestry of insights into the world of music journalism, artistic interaction, cultural shifts, and the profound impact of music on society. Beyond mere facts, it delves into the philosophy, anecdotes, and technical evolution that underpin a career dedicated to dissecting the soundtrack of our lives.

Herein lies a comprehensive summary of the extracted information, organized for detailed analysis:

## The Philosophy of Music Curation and Journalism

Alan Cross’s approach to music journalism, particularly with *The Ongoing History of New Music*, is rooted in a deep-seated mission to provide depth and context often lost in contemporary music consumption.

*   **Enriching the Listener Experience:** Cross's primary goal is to "tell you stuff that you don't know," unearthing facts and stories that evoke a "wow, that's cool" reaction, even from a seasoned music professional. This pursuit aims to make music listening a richer, more meaningful experience.
*   **Combating "Organized Noise":** He perceives streaming services as delivering "organized noise" because they lack the human element to provide context—details about the song, artist, album, scene, sound, history, or recording process. His role is to fill this void, making music more interesting by revealing its origins and the artist's journey.
*   **The Power of Validation:** Cross understands that providing interesting trivia empowers listeners. When they share these "wow, that's cool" facts with friends, it gives them validation, fostering a grateful connection to the source of information.
*   **Emotional Investment:** Every script is infused with his "heart and soul," ensuring that any emotion conveyed in his voice is genuine. He selects topics and angles that allow for "maximum emotional impact" with a "minimum number of words."
*   **Proxy for the Audience:** In interviews, Cross sees himself as a proxy for every listener who wishes they could be in the room. His objective is to elicit unique, never-before-told stories from artists.
*   **The Four-Step Interview Process:** Cross employs a structured methodology for interviews:
    1.  **Propriety:** Behaving properly, avoiding over-familiarity, dismissiveness, or rudeness.
    2.  **Competence:** Demonstrating thorough knowledge of the artist, their work, and the interview's purpose.
    3.  **Comfort:** Once propriety and competence are established, the artist begins to relax, lowering their guard.
    4.  **Trust:** Comfort leads to trust, at which point artists are more likely to share intimate or unique details. This process, though sometimes challenging, has no shortcuts.

## Notable Artist Encounters & Anecdotes

Cross's career is punctuated by a myriad of interactions with music legends, providing invaluable insights into their personalities and the dynamics of celebrity interviews.

*   **Bono (U2):** A master of charisma, Bono makes the interviewer feel like the "only person in the world that matters." He is an effusive conversationalist, possessing "the soul of an Irish poet," and will continue talking even as he is physically escorted away when interview time expires.
*   **David Bowie:** Bowie shared Bono's intense charisma. Cross recounts a 1990 solo interview with Bowie during his Tin Machine era. Overwhelmed by Bowie's iconic presence (referencing Ziggy Stardust and the Thin White Duke), Cross forgot to unpause his tape recorder, resulting in a completely unrecorded interview. Years later, confessing this to Bowie, the artist simply replied, "Well, that was stupid, wasn't it?" This remains a significant "lost tape" in Cross's memory.
*   **Leonard Cohen:** A famously disastrous early interview in 1988 highlights Cross's initial lack of experience. During a promotional push for Cohen's *I'm Your Man* album (where he was being marketed as an alternative artist by IRS Records), Cross, then unfamiliar with Cohen's gravitas, asked if he could call him "Lenny." Cohen, in black attire and dark sunglasses, took a drag from a French cigarette, blew smoke in Cross's face, and, leaning into the microphone, delivered a resonant "Don't." The interview was brief and profoundly awkward.
*   **Gord Downie (The Tragically Hip):** Described as "deep and enigmatic," Downie's standard response to questions about song meaning was always, "What does it mean to you?" In a surprising display of humility, Downie once arrived early for an interview and offered to fetch Cross a Starbucks latte.
*   **Noel Gallagher (Oasis):** Known for being precisely "how you want Noel Gallagher to be," Noel is portrayed as a rich source of Britpop anecdotes.
*   **Liam Gallagher (Oasis):** Similarly, Liam is described as "a jerk, just like you want him to be," reaffirming his public persona.
*   **Angus Young (AC/DC) & John Paul Jones (Led Zeppelin):** Both legendary figures offered Cross tea during early career interviews, illustrating a general trend that "the bigger the artist, the better they are at interviews" as they understand it's part of their professional duty.
*   **The Beautiful South:** Recalled as Cross's "worst interview." The band arrived at the radio station directly from the airport, tired, somewhat inebriated, and visibly annoyed at missing a football match. During a live, on-air segment, they sat far from the microphones, offering only silence or terse responses, leading to a painfully short, 90-second segment.
*   **The Pixies:** Cross was present for one of their early Toronto performances where they "almost tore down the concert hall," underscoring their raw live energy.
*   **Shane MacGowan (The Pogues):** An anecdote reveals MacGowan's legendary reputation for drinking. Cross was scheduled to introduce The Pogues but couldn't locate MacGowan, who was "out drinking someplace." He was eventually "hustled" onto the stage, smelling distinctly of alcohol from 15 feet away, yet performed flawlessly, knowing all the lyrics. Cross plans a future episode to explore The Pogues' pivotal role in integrating Irish Celtic folk traditions into punk rock.

## The Impact of Landmark Releases: Nirvana's *Nevermind*

The release of Nirvana's *Nevermind* serves as a focal point for discussing a seismic shift in musical landscape and cultural impact.

*   **Synchronized Release and Immediate Resonance:** The CD single for "Smells Like Teen Spirit" dropped on August 27, 1991, coincidentally the same day as Pearl Jam's *Ten*. The full *Nevermind* album followed on September 24, 1991, alongside Red Hot Chili Peppers' *Blood Sugar Sex Magik*.
*   **First Canadian Airplay and Explosive Reaction:** Alan Cross believes he was the first in Canada to play "Smells Like Teen Spirit" at 11:38 AM on August 27, 1991. The phone lines at the radio station "melted down" instantly with listeners demanding to know "What is this?" This compelled the station to play the song repeatedly throughout the day.
*   **Rapid Cultural Penetration:** Within two days, by a Thursday night alternative dance event in Burlington, attendees already knew all the lyrics, demonstrating the song's incredibly swift and deep impact on popular consciousness.
*   **Commercial Juggernaut:** Initially, Warner Brothers projected sales of only 50,000 to 100,000 copies. However, *Nevermind* rapidly soared to 300,000 sales per week. In January, it famously dethroned Michael Jackson from the #1 spot on the Billboard album charts, a symbolic victory for alternative music.
*   **Defining an Era:** This moment signaled the arrival of alternative music into the mainstream, ushering in a "wild ride" era between 1991 and 1995. This period was characterized by a constant influx of "new sound, new song, new scene," encompassing grunge, American indie, the Lollapalooza generation, gangster rap, and early Britpop.

## Evolution of Music Consumption & Cultural Gatekeepers

Cross provides a compelling analysis of how music consumption, discovery, and cultural significance have transformed from the pre-internet era to the age of streaming and algorithms.

*   **The Pre-Internet Era: Scarcity and Consensus:**
    *   **Gatekeepers:** Music discovery was controlled by five main "cultural gatekeepers": radio stations, record stores, record labels, music magazines, and eventually, video channels.
    *   **Ubiquity and Social Importance:** This scarcity of access led to a strong consensus around popular artists. Bands like KISS could achieve universal omnipresence, fostering a shared musical experience.
    *   **Identity Formation:** Music played a crucial role in self-identification during youth (ages 14-24), allowing individuals to project their identity as "punk kids," "metal kids," or "goth kids" to the world. This made the music of that period profoundly important as "comfort food" for life.
    *   **Information Flow:** Before the internet (pre-1996), radio was a vital source of real-time music information. Journalists relied on subscriptions to numerous magazines (Cross personally spent $3,000 annually), press clippings from record labels, and direct contacts from places like the UK, enabling stations like CFNY to be "weeks ahead" of Canadian music magazines.
    *   **Toronto's Role:** Toronto served as a key "staging ground" for British acts to break into the North American market, with CFNY receiving credit from numerous bands from 1984 onwards for their North American success.

*   **The Streaming Era: Abundance and Fragmentation:**
    *   **"Organized Noise":** With over 120 million songs accessible, streaming creates a vast "panoply of music" but often lacks the contextualizing information provided by human curators.
    *   **Loss of Consensus:** Even mega-stars like Taylor Swift, while immensely popular, do not achieve the same level of ubiquity or consensus across society as past legends like The Rolling Stones or Led Zeppelin. Her lyrics are not as universally known as "Satisfaction" or "Smells Like Teen Spirit."
    *   **Algorithmic Bubbles:** Streaming algorithms, by recommending "more of what you've already listened to," create "bubbles" that limit genuine musical exploration and prevent listeners from discovering new, exciting sounds.
    *   **Ecumenical Youth Tastes:** Modern 15-year-olds, with access to the entire history of recorded music, exhibit highly "ecumenical" tastes, listening to contemporary pop alongside '90s grunge, '70s rock, '60s classics, and even jazz artists like Billy Holiday. This broad influence will shape future music in unpredictable ways.
    *   **The "Heroin Addict" Analogy:** Cross likens the experienced music fan to a heroin addict, needing ever-increasing doses (or novelty) to achieve the same "high" from new music. By their 40s, many either retreat to the music of their youth or explore artists from that formative period they initially missed.
    *   **Recommendations for Discovery:** To combat algorithmic bubbles and rediscover excitement, Cross advises listening to online radio stations from other parts of the world (for diverse perspectives) and attending live music.

*   **Dominant Genres and Cultural Drivers:**
    *   **Global Shift:** Globally, hip hop has surpassed rock as the dominant musical form, driving culture.
    *   **Canadian Anomaly:** In Canada, however, rock remains the #1 genre, particularly in concert ticket sales, followed by pop, then hip hop and R&B. Rock is "not dead" and still vibrant, as evidenced by its prevalence in places like Edinburgh.
    *   **Youth-Driven Innovation:** Music is consistently "driven by youth" and acts as a "downstream" reflection of societal changes.

## The Production Journey of "The Ongoing History of New Music"

The evolution of *The Ongoing History of New Music* from its inception in 1993 to its current multi-platform presence highlights significant technological and procedural shifts in broadcasting.

*   **Early Days (Pre-Digital, first 250 shows, 1993 onwards):**
    *   **Labor-Intensive Process:** Alan would write his scripts by Wednesday. Recording took 30-45 minutes on quarter-inch tape.
    *   **Manual Editing:** Editing was a painstaking, real-time process using a razor blade, tape, and a grease pencil, often taking double the recording time.
    *   **Real-time Music Integration:** Music was "laid in" on multi-track tape and mixed down to two-track tape for radio playback, all in real-time.
    *   **Technical Limitations:** Early shows "sound like shit" due to the lack of audio processors available at the time.
*   **Modern Digital Production (Post-2000s):**
    *   **Home Studio & Collaboration:** Alan now records his voice tracks using Pro Tools in his home studio.
    *   **Remote Workflow:** Voice tracks are uploaded to Dropbox for producer Rob Downs, who edits them on a laptop, even while traveling (e.g., on a plane at 35,000 feet).
    *   **Music Integration:** Alan supplies music files or Rob uses existing extensive hard drives.
    *   **Podcast Adaptation (Launched 2017/2018):** To navigate complex copyright and rights management issues, full songs cannot be played on podcasts. Instead, they are "telescoped down" to 15-second clips. This podcast format has significantly increased the show's "longevity."
*   **Workload Evolution:**
    *   Initially, 48 shows were produced annually.
    *   Currently, the schedule is reduced to 33-34 shows per year, with summer months used for reruns, allowing listeners to catch up on missed episodes.
    *   The transition from two shows a week with analog tape to three shows every two weeks digitally reflects improved efficiency.
*   **Failed Early Podcasting Attempt:** In 2000, an attempt was made to launch a podcast, but it "never gained traction." Concerns about cannibalizing radio listenership and a lack of market demand at the time prevented its continuation until 2017/2018.
*   **Expanded Distribution:** Beyond traditional radio (including overnight slots on AM stations in Canada and a podcast-only station in the UK), the show is available early and ad-free on Amazon Music for Prime subscribers.

## Early Career & Musical Genesis (Alan Cross's Background)

Alan Cross's personal journey from a small-town "rockhead" to an influential alternative music curator is a testament to serendipity and the transformative power of diverse musical exposure.

*   **Small-Town Roots:** Growing up in a small town outside Winnipeg, his early musical diet was exclusively Top 40 radio.
*   **The "Dazed and Confused" Rockhead:** The arrival of an album rock station in 1978 converted him into a passionate rock fan, immersed in Deep Purple, Van Halen, Led Zeppelin, and AC/DC. He played drums in a metal trio, boasting a "Neil Peart-sized drum kit," and recounts an amusing anecdote about his singer/bassist friend misremembering Van Halen lyrics on stage.
*   **First Glimpse of Alternative:** His job at KX96 in Brandon, Manitoba, in 1983, exposed him to a more eclectic mix, including The Jam, The Clash, Aztec Camera, English Beat, and The Specials, broadening his musical horizons.
*   **Discovery of CFNY and Conversion:** While working at an unfulfilling contemporary station in Winnipeg, he applied to CFNY in Brampton, a station he knew through Rush's "The Spirit of Radio" (whose lyrics were dedicated to CFNY) and its reputation for alternative music. Upon joining, he was initially unfamiliar with bands like The Smiths, Depeche Mode (beyond "People Are People"), and New Order. However, after a week exploring CFNY's extensive library, he experienced a revelation: "Where has this music been all my life?" This marked his complete conversion from a "metalhead rockhead to an alternative kid."
*   **"Mission from God":** The early CFNY staff, despite low pay and long hours, were united by a shared passion, seeing themselves "on a mission from God" to educate the world about great music.
*   **The CFNY Vinyl Library Heist:** In 1996, when CFNY moved to new premises, the station intended to discard its extensive vinyl library, deeming it obsolete in the nascent internet age. Cross, predicting vinyl's future value, rented a U-Haul truck and "stole" the entire collection (from 1977-1996), now housed in his basement. These records bear the handwritten notes of iconic CFNY DJs. He has a pact with Iver Hamilton that the last one living inherits the other's record collection.

## Controversial Perspectives

Cross discusses specific topics he approaches with caution or, in retrospect, regrets having covered.

*   **"Me Too" Allegations:** He explicitly avoids covering "Me Too" cases, especially those with allegations but no court convictions. He believes it's not his role to adjudicate such cases and doing so carries significant legal risks.
*   **Kurt Cobain Murder Conspiracy:** Cross expresses regret for having explored the "Kurt Cobain was murdered" theory on his show, now labeling it "conspiratorial garbage." Spending time with Courtney Love ultimately convinced him that the conspiracy theories were unfounded. (He notes that while the specific conspiracy angle is regrettable, the topic could fit a true crime format from a different perspective in his new podcast, *Uncharted*.)

## The "Ongoing History" Theme Song

The iconic theme song, a staple of the show, has its own unique backstory.

*   **Accidental Creation:** The theme was created in 1993 by imaging producer Amin Batia, initially for a show Alan was assigned "against his will."
*   **Compositional Elements:** It's a blend of synthesizers (played by Batia), production library sounds, and effects.
*   **Symbolic Opening:** The opening sound is a DAT tape "scrubbing backwards," designed to symbolize a "rewind through time," aligning with the show's historical premise.
*   **Mysterious Female Voice:** A distinctive "beautiful female voice thing that slides by" within the theme remains an enigma; its exact origin from the myriad production libraries of the era is unknown.
*   **No Full Version:** The theme is not a complete, standalone song but rather a meticulously crafted combination of elements.

## Related Projects & Future Endeavors

Cross continues to expand his influence and output through various projects that delve into music history and beyond.

*   **CFNY Documentary:** A film documentary, currently in final editing, explores "The Spirit of Radio CFNY years" (1977-1992). This project investigates how the station impacted the growth of alternative music in North America, featuring interviews with prominent artists like Jim Kerr (Simple Minds) and Peter Hook (New Order/Joy Division), with segments filmed at Abbey Road Studios and across North America.
*   **"Uncharted: Crime & Mayhem in the Music Industry":** Debuted in October 2023, this new true crime podcast delves into the intersection of music and crime, covering murders, robberies, disappearances, the mob, and other dark incidents. Episodes are released bi-weekly.
*   **"Mr. Obituary":** Due to his extensive knowledge, Cross is frequently consulted by Canadian (and occasionally international) news networks for commentary on deceased musicians (e.g., Jimmy Buffett, Gordon Lightfoot, Gord Downie). These events often inspire "What's the Big Deal About" episodes, aimed at educating new audiences about the significance of these artists.
*   **AI Version of "Ongoing History":** An intriguing future development mentioned is an upcoming AI version of *The Ongoing History of New Music* in the new year.
*   **aJournalofMusicalThings.com:** Cross maintains a personal website, updated daily since 2011, providing music news, information, and recommendations, along with a free daily newsletter.
