### The Slumak Legend and Its Enduring Mystery

*   **The Genesis of a Curse:** The central myth revolves around an indigenous prospector named Slumak (also referred to as Sloomox). Before his execution in 1891, he is said to have uttered a potent curse in the Chinook language: "Nika Memlos, mine Memlos," translating to "When I die, the mine dies." This warning was directed at anyone who dared to search for his secret gold mine.
*   **A Century and a Half of Enigma:** The story of Slumak's gold is described as a 150-year-old mystery, steeped in legends of people disappearing, dying, or never returning from their quest for the hidden treasure. The very mention of the Pit Lake region evokes a sense of ancient, untouched wilderness, a "Jurassic" land that time forgot, suggesting a powerful, almost preternatural connection to the curse.
*   **The Lure of Immense Riches:** Rumors began to circulate shortly after Slumak's death about a "lucrative and hidden stash of gold in the mountains," estimated to be worth "billions." This immense value elevates the legend beyond mere folklore to a significant cultural and economic driver, fueling relentless searches.
*   **Personal Encounters with the Curse:** The host, Crue Williams, recounts a vivid, sensory dream during his expedition, where he felt a "cold, sandy, wind-blown finger pressing on my lips." This intensely personal experience made the curse "real, very real," suggesting its psychological power and the possibility of spiritual or ancestral warnings against disturbing the hidden treasure. The reluctance to share details implies a deeply unsettling, almost taboo nature to the experience.

### Historical Context: British Columbia's Gold Rush Era

*   **The Fraser River Gold Rush (1858):** The narrative anchors Slumak's story within the broader historical context of British Columbia's first major gold rush. This event saw an influx of approximately 30,000 gold seekers to the Fraser River banks, transforming the region.
*   **Colonial Establishment and Control:** The British government swiftly established the colony of British Columbia on August 2nd, 1858, from the non-sovereign territory of New Caledonia. This act was driven by a strategic intent to avoid the lawlessness seen in the California and Australian gold fields and to prevent the territory from falling into American hands, highlighting the geopolitical stakes of the gold rush.
*   **New Westminster: A Frontier Hub:** New Westminster is portrayed as a bustling "gold rush staging area" on the Fraser River, serving as a transient hub where prospectors, including Slumak, would arrive to spend their fortunes.
*   **Indigenous Lands and European Colonization:** Prior to 1858, the population of New Caledonia was primarily indigenous, estimated between 40,000 and 50,000 people. The text notes how traditional indigenous trading trails became "overgrown with brush in the wake of European colonization," illustrating the displacement and disruption caused by settler expansion.

### Slumak's Life and Allegations

*   **Mysterious Origins and Lifestyle:** Slumak is depicted as a fiery, young indigenous prospector from the "mountains north of the Pit Lake." He would appear in New Westminster with "gold nuggets the size of walnuts," spending lavishly on "booze, gambling, and women," before disappearing for weeks, his comings and goings shrouded in mystery. He never revealed the source of his gold.
*   **"Hunter Without Equal":** Slumak's survival skills and intimate knowledge of the rugged Pit Lake wilderness are emphasized. He was known as a "hunter without equal," possessing a nature "vicious in the extreme" and "without fear," traits that allowed him to endure the harsh, life-threatening conditions of the forest, particularly in winter.
*   **A Pattern of Alleged Violence:** Beyond the immediate murder of Louis B, Slumak was "credited by the Indians for another murder committed years ago and under similar circumstances." Newspaper reports suggest he "committed four or five murders during the last 25 years," including an incident six years prior where he allegedly disappeared for a year before returning as if nothing happened. These allegations portray him as a serial offender in the eyes of the colonial press and perhaps some indigenous community members.
*   **Fears and Stereotypes:** The description of Slumak as having the "countenance and resemblance of that of an incarnate demon" by a witness, coupled with terms like "desperate character" and "bloodthirsty old villain" by newspapers, reflects the prevalent stereotypes and fears surrounding indigenous people at the time.

### The Murder of Louis B and the Manhunt

*   **The Crime (September 8, 1890):** Slumak was accused of the "deliberate murder" of Louis B, described as a "half-breed" (a derogatory term for someone of mixed Indigenous and European ancestry), by shooting him through the breast while fishing at Pit River. The victim died instantly from a single bullet to the heart and lungs.

*   **Attempted Concealment:** Slumak allegedly attempted to dispose of Louis B's body by placing it in a canoe and intending to drop it "overboard in deep water" in Pit Lake, indicating a calculated attempt to hide the crime. The body was later recovered after authorities ordered the river to be dragged.
*   **The Manhunt Leader: Warden William Morseby:** The search for Slumak was led by Warden William Morseby, an English-born constable who had served as governor of the New Westminster jail for 13 years. Morseby is portrayed as a ruthless and determined figure, vowing to bring Slumak in "dead or alive" and not allowing "possible risk" to interfere.
*   **Destruction as a Tactic:** Morseby's tactics were harsh and punitive. He burned Slumak's "meager shack to the ground" and ordered his men to destroy Slumak's canoe, his only means of transportation, in an attempt to cut off his resources and force him out of the wilderness.
*   **Forced Relocation of a Community:** In a drastic measure, Morseby ordered the entire population of Slumak's village to pack their belongings and move to the Coquitlam Indigenous Reserve, about 20 kilometers away. This act of forced relocation, resisted angrily by the community (with Slumak's brother even attempting to fight Morseby), demonstrates the state's power and its willingness to punish entire communities for the actions of individuals, seeing them as complicit or obstructive.
*   **Slumak's Resistance and Surrender:** Slumak evaded capture for over six weeks, using his knowledge of the bush to survive. He communicated with relatives using "grass fire signals" and engaged in a close encounter with a constable, highlighting his resourcefulness and determination not to be taken alive. Ultimately, he surrendered on October 25th, 1890, "starving and weather beaten," "emaciated," with no ammunition and his clothes in rags. His surrender was reportedly influenced by community members, who had been convinced by the Indian agent that it was their "duty and in their best interest" to deliver Slumak to authorities.
