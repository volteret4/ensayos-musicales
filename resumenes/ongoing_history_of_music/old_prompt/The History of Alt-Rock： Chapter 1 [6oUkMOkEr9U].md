## The Complete History of Alt-Rock: Chapter 1 - The Genesis

### The Core Concept: Re-evaluating Musical History

The podcast series "The History of Alt-Rock" aims to re-examine the entire history of alternative music, a project not undertaken since the late 1990s. This re-evaluation is crucial because history is a "moving target"; new details emerge, interpretations shift, and what was once deemed cool can become cheesy, while the weird becomes familiar. The true significance of events often isn't understood until years later, requiring time for their effects to "stew and simmer." Periodically revisiting musical history offers a deeper appreciation for both contemporary sounds and their predecessors.

### The Genesis of "Alternative": Early Rock's Radical Roots

*   **"All Rock Was Alternative"**: In its infancy, rock and roll was inherently alternative. When songs like Jackie Brenston's "Rocket 88" (featuring Ike Turner on vocals) emerged in the early 1950s, the sound was considered "a little frightening."
*   **"The Devil's Music"**: This new genre was a strange offspring of rhythm and blues (then known as "race records" of the American South) and hillbilly/country music. It genuinely scared "decent folks" of the era.
*   **Teenage Rebellion and Primal Appeal**: A newly classified demographic—teenagers—were drawn to this music due to its energy, primal nature, and raw primitivism. Crucially, its desirability was amplified by the fact that parents and pastors vehemently hated it, making it wonderfully dangerous.
*   **Beyond the Beat**: While many teenagers cited "the beat, the beat, the beat" as their primary reason for liking rock and roll, the music also possessed its own secret language and codes. The phrase "rock and roll" itself was an old African-American slang term for sex, a hidden meaning appreciated by "hip kids."
*   **Racial and Societal Anxieties**: The notion of white kids listening to Black music, especially with its "jungle drums," was deemed "indecent" and "way too weird" for many, reflecting the "uptight, let's face it, racist views" prevalent in society at the time. This cultural clash positioned early rock and roll as a subversive force against mainstream norms.

### Elvis Presley: The First Alternative Star?

*   **Breaking Barriers**: Elvis Presley, a young white truck driver who famously "sounded like he was black," began to fundamentally alter the musical landscape. He is widely recognized as the first true rock and roll star.
*   **Mainstream Alternative**: If rock and roll itself was the alternative to the mainstream in the 1950s, then Elvis, as its primary icon, could be considered the first alternative music star.
*   **Technological Catalysts**:
    *   **The Transistor Radio**: This new invention made rock and roll portable, liberating listeners from the communal living room console where parents could monitor their choices. Teenagers could now listen anywhere, "safely away from people who might disapprove."
    *   **The 7-inch Single**: The low cost of producing these records contributed significantly to the rapid spread of rock music.
*   **The Search for Further Subversion**: As Elvis's star ascended and he transitioned into a more mainstream persona, a new wave of artists began to emerge, actively seeking to "subvert things even further," indicating a continuous push for the "alternative" edge.

### Proto-Punk Pioneers: Pushing the Boundaries of Early Rock

*   **Billy Lee Riley's Extreme Performance**: Recording for the legendary Sun label in Memphis (like Elvis), rockabilly singer Billy Lee Riley took the nascent rock and roll sound to radical extremes. Witnesses at his recording sessions recalled him literally swinging upside down from rafters, screaming into the microphone, distinguishing him as "certainly different" even within rock's already radical framework.
*   **The Wild Men of Rock and Roll**:
    *   **Little Richard**: Described as "weird and scary," his flamboyant style and explosive performances were far from conventional.
    *   **Jerry Lee Lewis**: A close relative of preacher Jimmy Swaggart, Lewis was known for pounding pianos "to splinters" and sometimes setting them on fire on stage. His scandalous marriage to his 13-year-old cousin further cemented his image as a dangerous renegade.
*   **Darker Aesthetics**:
    *   **Gene Vincent (1957)**: Introduced a darker sound and image, exuding a tough, greasy, leather-jacketed persona.
    *   **Eddie Cochran**: His songs, particularly those addressing teenage boredom, later found an influential audience among early punk acts, with one notably being covered by "Sedvishes" (Sid Vicious) of the Sex Pistols.

### The Surf Sound: Technical Innovation and Sonic Power

*   **Southern California Craze**: By the early 1960s, surf music gained traction in Southern California, predating the Beach Boys' mainstream success.
*   **Dick Dale's Guitar Mastery**: Dick Dale was a significant innovator, playing his guitar left-handed and upside down. He "cooked so many new sounds" out of his instrument that he became a consultant for the Fender Company, testing amplifiers and portable reverb units. Although he never achieved a Top 40 hit, anyone who heard him play never forgot "that sound of power."

### Garage Rock: Rawness and Rebellion from the Suburbs

*   **DIY Ethos**: By 1963, teenagers worldwide were eager to make their own music. Crucially, musical proficiency wasn't a barrier; "not being able to play wasn't really that much of an issue," with the emphasis on "just to go ahead and do it."
*   **The Kingsmen and "Louie Louie"**:
    *   **Budget Recording**: Their "slop-rock anthem" was recorded for a mere $50.
    *   **Unintelligible and Scandalous Lyrics**: The song's notoriously "completely unintelligible lyrics" were widely rumored to be pornographic, leading to a six-month sound analysis test by the FBI, which ultimately failed to prove the allegations. The singer's heavy dental braces and the crude recording setup (singing into a single overhead microphone) were later cited as reasons for the poor enunciation, suggesting no deliberate attempt to hide explicit content.
    *   **Launching an Era**: The Kingsmen effectively launched the "American garage band" era, characterized by groups that were often "rough and ragged" and occasionally explored "risque" material.
*   **Question Mark & The Mysterians and "96 Tears"**: In 1966, this band released a song originally titled "69 Tears." Fearing the provocative connotations of the number "69," their record company successfully persuaded them to change the title.
*   **Proliferation and Aesthetic**: Throughout the mid-1960s, garage bands like The Seeds, Count Five, The Barbarians, The Standells, and The Troggs emerged across North America. Some adopted a rebellious "James Dean" look with leather jackets, leading observers to label them "punks."
*   **Underground Appeal**: While a few garage bands achieved hit singles, most remained "underground," popular with a specific audience that favored music that was "rough and raw and real."
*   **Stratification of Rock**: By its tenth birthday, rock music had already begun to segment. There was the "acceptable mainstream stuff at the top" and then the music "that wasn't ready or interested in prime time." This dynamic signaled the birth of a new musical subculture, laying groundwork for future alternative movements.

### The Unlikely Trio: Origins of Future Icons

The text introduces three individuals whose combined influence would prove "incalculable" for the development of alternative rock:

*   **David Jones (Later David Bowie)**:
    *   **Early Aspirations**: A young saxophone player with a fondness for Little Richard, Jones's early career was marked by a search for identity.
    *   **Public Eccentricity**: His first notable public appearance was on a BBC News program in November 1964 as the founder, president, and spokesman for the "International League for the Preservation of Animal Filaments," an organization dedicated to normalizing long hair for young British men.
    *   **First Single**: In June 1964, he released his first single, "Liza Jane," with his group The King Bees.
    *   **Name Change**: In 1966, he adopted the stage name David Bowie to avoid confusion with Davy Jones of The Monkees, a cute English pop star on TV.
    *   **Early Struggles**: By 1967, Bowie was still "trying to find himself," experimenting with images, styles, and sounds without much success. His 1967 song, "The Laughing Gnome," was a "desperate attempt to land a song on the charts" that failed. He also explored dancing and even believed there was a career in being a mime.
    *   **Day Jobs**: To support himself, he wrote music for BBC TV shows, performed in a coffee house trio called Feathers, and worked at Legastat Document Copiers, copying legal papers. His escape from a "life of photocopying and mime abuse" was famously attributed to the American Space Program, setting the stage for future developments.

*   **Jimmy Osterberg (Later Iggy Pop)**:
    *   **Avoiding Vietnam**: In Detroit, Osterberg reportedly faked being gay or insane at the Army Recruiter's office to avoid being sent to Vietnam, a story that highlights his early anti-establishment leanings.
    *   **Musical Beginnings**: He worked as a drummer for hire, occasionally playing on Motown records, and also performed with his own band, The Iguanas, in 1964.
    *   **Nickname Origins**: He acquired the nickname "Iggy" from his time with The Iguanas. The "Pop" moniker came after he shaved off his eyebrows post-heavy drinking, leading someone to remark that he resembled a local drug addict known as "Jim Pop."

*   **Lewis Allen Reed (Later Lou Reed)**:
    *   **Early Trauma and Musical Outlet**: Born in Brooklyn, Reed started playing in bands in the late 1950s, a period punctuated by his parents sending him for electroshock therapy to "cure him of what they believed to be homosexual tendencies," a stark reminder of 1950s societal norms. His earliest band, The Shades, focused on doo-wop.
    *   **Academic and Professional Pursuits**: He later studied film, journalism, and creative writing at Syracuse University, while also working as a part-time DJ.
    *   **Staff Songwriter**: By September 1964, Reed was in Manhattan, working as a staff songwriter at Pickwick Records for $25 a week. His job was to "churn out songs for budget albums" that sold for a dollar in department and furniture stores, creating what was described as "prefabricated crap."
    *   **"The Ostrich"**: In November 1964, his band The Primitives (with Reed on vocals) recorded "The Ostrich," an attempt to capitalize on popular dance crazes like the Twist and the Mashed Potato. This was around the same time Bowie was defending long hair on TV and Iggy Pop's band was opening for The Shangri-Las.

### The Velvet Underground: Defining the "First Alternative Rock Band"

*   **Formation and Radical Lineup**: A few months after "The Ostrich" was recorded, Lou Reed and Welsh-born bandmate John Cale began forming what is now considered "the first ever alternative rock band," The Velvet Underground. The lineup solidified by December 1965, featuring Reed, Cale, guitarist Sterling Morrison, and drummer Mo Tucker.
*   **Mo Tucker's Revolutionary Drumming**: Tucker was a radical female drummer who defied conventions. She played standing up, used bass drums turned on their sides, and "whacked everything with mallets instead of sticks," contributing significantly to the band's distinctive sound.
*   **Name Origin**: The band's provocative name was derived from a "trashy book on S&M sex by Michael Leigh," signaling their transgressive themes.
*   **Early Gigs**: Their first paying gig was on December 11, 1965, at a New Jersey high school, earning them $75. In spring 1966, they secured a residency at The Café Bizarre in Greenwich Village.
*   **Andy Warhol's Patronage and Production**: Pop artist Andy Warhol heard their "raw and gritty approach" and immediately integrated them into his "Factory" circle, hiring them to play his famously "freaky parties." Warhol became their "mentor and chief patron," encouraging them to recruit German model Nico for "visual interest" and even securing producer credit for their 1967 debut album. Despite the credit, Warhol "didn't really know much about making records"; his role was to "inspire and encourage them," without touching anything technical.
*   **The Debut Album: *The Velvet Underground & Nico* (1967)**:
    *   **Scandalous and Shocking Content**: The album was groundbreaking for its noisy, angry sound and its explicit themes of sex, drugs, street life, S&M, and the "awesomeness of just getting wasted."
    *   **Unprecedented Candor**: Unlike even the "most daring hippie bands of the day" who merely alluded to sex and drugs, Lou Reed was "actually describing it," as exemplified by the song "Heroin," which was "unemphasize how shocking that song was for 1967."
    *   **Commercial Failure, Profound Influence**: The album's rawness and realism proved too much for the mainstream; it peaked at number 199 on the Billboard Top 200 chart before quickly disappearing. However, for a burgeoning audience seeking an alternative to "sugary pop" and the "acid wash of psychedelic rock," The Velvet Underground represented "something new and important."
    *   **Impact on Future Icons**: David Bowie became a fan, particularly of "Waiting for the Man," shortly after the album's release. Iggy Pop also loved the record and saw them perform live in April 1967, an experience that heavily influenced his next steps.

### Iggy Pop and The Stooges: Performance Art and Confrontation

*   **Velvet Underground's Influence**: Following his exposure to The Velvet Underground, Iggy Pop disbanded The Iguanas by summer 1967 and formed a new group, The Psychedelic Stooges.
*   **Chaotic Beginnings**: Their first performance was a private party on Halloween 1967, which was "not very good," leading their manager to quit and return to teaching.
*   **Public Debut as Avant-Garde Spectacle**: Their first proper public performance in March 1968 was unforgettable:
    *   **Unconventional Opening Act**: The show began with a food processor (a blender with water and raw meat) run through the PA system for 15 minutes.
    *   **Iggy's Bizarre Attire**: Iggy appeared in a nightshirt, golf shoes, white makeup, and a large afro wig made of tinfoil.
    *   **Primitive Drumming**: Drummer Scott Asheton kept time by banging on 50-gallon oil drums with hammers instead of traditional sticks.
*   **Extreme Stage Antics**: The Stooges gained notoriety for Iggy's increasingly extreme stage behavior, which was unprecedented in rock:
    *   **Physicality**: Stage diving, self-mutilation, covering himself in glitter dust, using broken glass.
    *   **Visceral Performances**: Infamously, Iggy would smear peanut butter or raw meat all over his bruised and bleeding body.
    *   **Contrast with Mainstream**: These antics were completely alien to mainstream bands like The Beatles or The Rolling Stones, and even hippie groups avoided such confrontational displays.
    *   **Police Encounters**: Police officers, who "continually busted Iggy for indecent exposure," were perplexed, having "never seen anything like it" and "didn't know what to do with them."
*   **Raw Sound**: A live recording of "Raw Power" from 1973, performed for a "very violent and angry biker gang," captures the intensity, with "bottles smashing on the stage."

### MC5: Political Fury and Anti-Establishment Stunts

*   **Detroit's Radical Voice**: The MC5, another Detroit-area group from the mid to late 1960s, were known for their loud, profane, and "highly political in a left wing super radical kind of way" sound.
*   **Political Affiliation**: In 1968, around the time they signed a major record deal, they appeared at the ill-fated Democratic National Convention in Chicago as guests of the White Panther Party, solidifying their radical image.
*   **Record Label Conflict and Street Cred**: Despite their major deal, they managed to release only one album before being dropped. This occurred after they sent a "vulgar letter on company letterhead" to record stores that refused to stock their album. Such stunts, however, "only enhanced their streetcred."
*   **Debut as a Live Album**: Their debut record, *Kick Out The Jams* (1968), was a live album, a remarkable and unusual choice for the time, prompting the question, "Imagine doing that today."

### Captain Beefheart: Avant-Garde Extremism and Enduring Influence

*   **The Un-listenable Masterpiece**: By October 1969, California sculptor-turned-musician Captain Beefheart released *Trout Mask Replica*, an album "so strange that it immediately became an underground classic."
*   **Polarizing Reception**: Most who heard it found it "absolutely completely and undeniably un-listenable." Yet, many influential figures, including the future Johnny Rotten of the Sex Pistols, bought and played it precisely *because* it was un-listenable.
*   **Party Clearing Power**: The album was notorious for its ability to "clear the room at any party" and remains "tough to listen" to even today.
*   **Opening New Possibilities**: Despite its difficult nature, the album's 28 songs "simply opened the eyes of a few to new possibilities," serving as a crucial absorption point for those who would later go on to "do cool things." The text emphasizes that its unconventional sound ("This is supposed to sound like this") was intentional, highlighting its radical departure from musical norms.

### The Alt-Rock Aesthetic: Beyond Mainstream and Hippie Culture

*   **Emergence of a Distinct Audience**: By the end of the 1960s, a growing segment of music fans expressed boredom with "conventional rock and pop."
*   **Rebellion of Taste**: These individuals "reveled in being different and liking different things," with a clear preference for tastes that were "more disagreeable... to the mainstream and to hippie culture." This deliberate divergence from established norms underscored the nascent alternative aesthetic, valuing distinctiveness and challenge over popular appeal.
