## albums
**Autobahn (1974)** : This album, released in 1974, marked Kraftwerk's shift to an all-electronic sound, which was unprecedented at the time. Side 1 features a 23-minute title track, an homage to a Sunday drive on Germany's superhighways, incorporating instruments like the Mini-Moog, EMS Synthi, ARP Odyssey, and Florian Schneider's self-built Robovox. "In 1974 they released an all-electronic album. Nothing had ever sounded like this."
**Trans-Europe Express (1977)** : Released in 1977, this album is noted for its influential beats, which were foundational in creating hip hop music. Its distinctive rhythms were famously sampled and adapted by artists like Afrika Bambaataa for "Planet Rock." "Craftwork beats, especially from their 1977 album Trans-Europe Express, were influential in creating the foundations of hip hop."
**Computer Love (1981)** : This 1981 track by Kraftwerk served as an inspiration for Coldplay's 2005 hit. Coldplay deliberately echoed Kraftwerk's style, leading to Kraftwerk receiving songwriting credits for the Coldplay song, which was a major hit single and contributed to the "X&Y" album selling over 15 million copies. "Here's Craftwork with computer love in 1981."

## curiosities
**From Organization to Power Plant** : Kraftwerk emerged from the ashes of the experimental group Organization. Its name, "Kraftwerk," is German for "power plant." Founding members Ralf Hütter and Florian Schneider, originally classical music students, developed a keen interest in synthesizers and began experimenting with synth and drum machine music, leading to their unique all-electronic sound. "Organization, remember that band we talked about? It became Kraftwerk, which is German for power plant."
**Influential After 1977** : Kraftwerk is considered by some to be the most influential band in the world next to The Beatles, with their impact traceable in music starting anytime after 1977. Their influence spans EDM, techno (including Detroit techno and Daft Punk), ambient and chillout music, synth and sample-constructed music (like Simple Minds and Depeche Mode), early 80s techno pop (OMD, Soft Cell), and industrial music (Nine Inch Nails). "Pick any starting point in music anytime after 1977. And you will somehow be led back in some fashion to Craftwork."
**Kling Klang Studio** : Kraftwerk's studio in Dusseldorf, known as Kling Klang, was the site of endless hours of experimentation that led to a string of important albums released over seven years following their 1974 album, "Autobahn." This studio became synonymous with their unique electronic sound. "resulting from the endless hours of experimenting at their studio in Dusseldorf, called Kling Kling."
**Robovox Creation** : Florian Schneider, a founding member of Kraftwerk, personally built an instrument called the Robovox. This custom-made device was used to treat vocals in a distinctive electronic way, contributing to the band's innovative sound, particularly on their 1974 "Autobahn" album. "something called a Robovox, which Florian Schneider built himself so he could treat vocals in a cool electronic way."
**"Fahr'n, Fahr'n" vs. "Fun, Fun, Fun"** : On their "Autobahn" album, Kraftwerk's lyrics "Fahr'n, Fahr'n" (German for "driving") were a deliberate homage to driving, distinct from any notion of copying The Beach Boys' "Fun, Fun, Fun." This highlights their focus on a uniquely German cultural experience. "They were saying, Ferrin's Ferrin, which is German for driving. Not fun, fun, fun."
**Volkswagen Beetle Samples** : Some of the unique sounds on Kraftwerk's track "Autobahn" were sourced from Ralf Hütter's Volkswagen Beetle. These sounds were captured by a tape recorder hanging out the window during a drive down the A555 autobahn, which links Bonn and Cologne, a major center for electronic music experimentation at the time. "Some of the sounds on this track are taken from Ralph Hooder's Volkswagen Beetle, captured by a taper quarter hanging out the window on a way down their highway called the A555."

## songs
**Autobahn (1974)** : This track, featured on Kraftwerk's 1974 album of the same name, is a 23-minute homage to a Sunday drive on German superhighways. # genre - Krautrock

## curiosities
**Origins of the Name** : The name "Krautrock" was coined by the British, initially as a pejorative term derived from "Sauer Kraut," a slur for Germans dating back to World War II. It began as an insult, evolved into a joke, and then, as the music gained traction, it stuck and became highly respected, despite many Germans finding it insulting and an unnecessary throwback to the war years. "But it was the Brits who came up with a name that stuck, Crout Rock."
**Post-War Cultural Rebirth** : Krautrock's story is deeply intertwined with Germany's post-World War II context, serving as a political, demographic, social, and artistic effort by Germany to rebuild after the Nazi past. The genre wouldn't exist without the foundational events of WWII and the subsequent desire for a new cultural identity. "Craftrock's story is especially interesting because of its origins as a political, demographic, social, and artistic attempt by Germany to rebuild after a Nazi past."
**Influence on Post-Punk** : Krautrock began to make serious inroads into the UK after British labels like Virgin Records signed numerous bands, viewing it as a form of prog rock. This exposure significantly changed post-punk music in the country, influencing its sound with icy coldness, hypnotic rhythms, and electronic elements. "This is how crop rock started to make serious inroads into the UK, inroads that changed post-punk music in the country."
**Characterized by Specific Elements** : Krautrock is identifiable by its unique sound, which includes a distinct beat, the pervasive use of electronics, an "icy coldness," a sometimes droning approach, tasteful improvisations, and a deliberate departure from rock's traditional rhythm and blues roots. These elements allowed it to work its way into many forms of rock. "The beat, the use of electronics, the icy coldness, the sometimes use of a drony approach, the tasteful improvisations, and above all, a move far, far away from rock's traditional roots, which of course is rhythm and blues."
**Unpopular in Germany, Popular Abroad** : For a considerable period, Krautrock bands struggled to gain recognition or commercial success within Germany itself, with German labels hoping for a "German version of The Beatles." However, these acts garnered significant attention outside the country, particularly in the UK, where labels eagerly signed them. "One of the interesting things about crop rock is how, for the longest time, none of these homegrown bands could get arrested in Germany."

# curiosity

**Post-World War II Germany** : Following World War II in the spring of 1945, Germany was left in ruins—cities, industries, infrastructure, and culture were all devastated. The country was divided, with West Germany emerging into Democratic Europe and seeking to rebuild from scratch, including its cultural identity. "Everything starts in the spring of 1945. With Hitler and the Nazis finally defeated, everything about Germany was in ruin."
**Rejection of Nazi-Tainted German Music** : Young German musicians after WWII were determined to create a form of rock that was explicitly different from traditional German music, which they felt had been irrevocably "tainted by the Nazi legacy." This sentiment fueled a desire for something new and entirely distinct. "And they certainly didn't want anything resembling traditional German music. It had been tainted by the Nazi legacy."
**West German Counterculture (Late 1960s)** : In West Germany, a vibrant counterculture movement emerged in the late 1960s, driven by students, protesters, and hippies. This movement was fundamentally rooted in a desire to transcend the nation's dark Nazi past and propel the country into a modern future. "Like everywhere else in the Democratic West, there was a counterculture movement in Germany powered by students, protesters, and hippies."
**Government-Owned Avant-Garde Studios** : Post-war Germany embraced experimental music, providing government-owned studios that were accessible to anyone interested in avant-garde sounds, particularly new electronic music. This state support for experimentation fostered a unique musical environment. "Germany had also embraced certain experimental approaches to music. There were a number of studios, some owned by the government, that were available to anyone interested in avant-garde sounds, especially new electronic ones."
**Shah of Iran State Visit and Protests (June 1967)** : In June 1967, the state visit of the Shah of Iran to West Germany became a flashpoint. On June 2nd, a student named Beno Ohnesorg was tragically shot dead by police during a protest, an event that deeply outraged many across the country and contributed to escalating social unrest. "In June 1967, the Shah of Iran traveled to West Germany for a state visit. On June 2nd, a student named Beno Orasag was shot dead by police during a protest."
**Rudi Dutschke Assassination Attempt (April 11, 1968)** : Almost a year after the Ohnesorg shooting, on April 11th, 1968, student activist leader Rudi Dutschke survived an assassination attempt. Both this and the Ohnesorg incident significantly triggered violent reactions from both the far left and far right in West Germany, intensifying political tensions. "On April 11th, 1968, almost a year later, a student activist leader named Rudy Dutschke survived an assassination attempt."
**Baader-Meinhof Gang** : The Baader-Meinhof Gang, an extreme-left terrorist group in West Germany, engaged in violent guerrilla attacks, including bombings, kidnappings, hijackings, and killings, as a reaction to the political climate of the late 1960s. They were also noted fans of the art collective Amon Düül. "On the extreme left, there was the Bodder-Minhoff Gang, who engaged in violent guerrilla attacks within the country and without."
**German 1968 Protests and Fascism** : The global protests of 1968, which addressed issues like authoritarianism, nuclear annihilation, and rights, were particularly radical in Germany. German youth explicitly aimed their protests at what they called "fascism," acutely sensitive to their nation's Nazi past, especially as many people in charge of the newly rebuilt Germany were seen as connected to the Nazi regime. "These protests were aimed toward what participants called fascism. And Germany is particularly sensitive to fascism because of their experience with Hitler and the Nazis, the greatest fascists of them all."
**Rejection of Nazi Legacy by Youth** : Young Germans in the late 1960s actively confronted their parents' and grandparents' Nazi past, refusing military service and condemning political institutions. This post-war intellectual thinking, centered on preventing future atrocities, permeated all corners of West German society and fueled their desire for a new cultural identity. "Young people were trying to come to terms with the Nazi past of their parents and grandparents. They refused to join the military. They condemned political institutions."
**Desire for Original German Music (1968-1977)** : A generation of post-war young people, Germany's baby boomers, sought to create a new musical identity, specifically between 1968 and 1977. They aimed to develop a form of rock that was distinct and separate from the music coming out of the UK and North America, believing German music needed to be rebuilt from a blank slate to reflect a reborn Germany. "All these people, especially those involved in German-created music between 1968 and 1977, were out to create a form of rock different and separate from what was happening in the UK and North America."
**Zodiac-free Arts Lab in Berlin** : Following the International Essen Song Festival in 1968, some German musicians continued their experiments in "song labs" like the Zodiac-free Arts Lab in Berlin. These spaces fostered the emergence of new sounds, contributing to the development of "Cosmic Music." "A few who began further experiments in the aforementioned song labs like the Zodiac-free Arts Lab in Berlin."
**Space Exploration Influence on Culture (Late 1960s)** : The widespread cultural fascination with space in the late 1960s, driven by the space race, moon landings, and plans for space stations, significantly influenced German musicians. They, along with others, saw space as a direction for the future, embracing its "techy cool" aesthetic in their music. "Now, speaking of which, the idea of space permeated all culture in the late 1960s. The space race between the Americans and the Soviets, the moon landings, the plans for space stations."
**Autobahns and Freedom (Hitler's Project)** : Germany's extensive network of motorways (autobahns) with no speed limits and long, straight sections symbolized freedom and movement, particularly relevant to the Motorikbeat. However, it's acknowledged that the highway project was initiated by Hitler, adding a layer of historical complexity to this symbol of freedom. "The open road promised freedom, even though the highway project was started by Hitler."
**Cologne as Electronic Music Hub** : The city of Cologne was a significant center for experimentation with electronic music during the time Kraftwerk was recording "Autobahn." The A555 autobahn, linking Bonn and Cologne, featured prominently in the creation of sounds for the song, highlighting the city's role in this musical development. "Cologne being the center of so much experimentation with electronic music at the time."
**Conny Plank (Producer)** : Conny Plank was a German record producer deeply involved in the creation of new German music. His dedication extended to building his own 56-channel recording console to facilitate these unique sounds, marking him as a pivotal figure behind the Krautrock movement. "A German record producer named Connie Plank was very much involved in creating this music."
**John Peel and the "Krautrock" Name** : While the exact origin of the term "Krautrock" is uncertain, it's speculated that BBC DJ John Peel may have coined the name, or perhaps a writer in the Melody Maker, or an anonymous fan. Regardless, the name, initially an insult, eventually stuck and became respected. "Maybe it was BBC DJ John Peel who came up with a name. It could have been a writer and the melody maker, or maybe it was just some anonymous fan."
