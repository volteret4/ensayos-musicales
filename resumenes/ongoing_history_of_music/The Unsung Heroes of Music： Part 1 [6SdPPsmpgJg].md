His investment provided crucial capital for the development and commercialization of tape recording technology.

**Tape's Broader Impact Beyond Music** : The same tape recording technology that revolutionized music production also made videotape possible. # instrument - Fuzz pedal

## curiosities
**Invention of the Maestro FZ-1 Fuzz-Tone** : The first-ever fuzz pedal, the Maestro FZ-1 Fuzz-Tone, was developed after an accidental equipment failure in late 1960. Session guitarist Grady Martin plugged his baritone guitar into a faulty recording console at Nashville's Quonset Hut studio, resulting in a fuzzy, distorted sound on Marty Robbins' hit "Don't Worry." Gibson Guitars, recognizing the sound's popularity, commissioned Martin, engineer Glenn Snottie, and WSMTV engineer Revis Hobbs to design a circuit that could replicate it on demand. The resulting foot pedal prototype led to Gibson manufacturing the Maestro FZ-1, released with a demonstration record in 1962.

**Features of the Maestro FZ-1 Fuzz-Tone** : The Maestro FZ-1 Fuzz-Tone was described in a 1962 demonstration record as a built-in foot pedal switch that attached to an amplifier with a handy jack. It was designed to create various effects with the touch of a toe, without interfering with regular guitar or bass sounds when turned off. It featured both a volume control and an attack control, was completely transistorized, and had its own self-contained power supply. Demonstration examples included a "Sousa Fone effect" and a "tuba sound" when used with an electric bass.

**Impact and Popularity After "Satisfaction"** : The Maestro FZ-1 Fuzz-Tone gained immense popularity after The Rolling Stones' "(I Can't Get No) Satisfaction" became a hit. Despite Keith Richards initially intending the distorted guitar riff to be a placeholder for a horn section and disliking the fuzz sound himself, the rest of the band loved it. Its success cemented the distorted guitar's place as a central element in rock music, leading to a surge in demand for the Maestro FZ-1 and inspiring other manufacturers to create their own versions.

**Other Notable Fuzz Pedals** : Following the success and influence of the Maestro FZ-1 Fuzz-Tone, other manufacturers began developing their own distortion pedals. Prominent examples that emerged included the Tone Bender, the Fuzz Face, and the Electro-Harmonix Big Muff. These pedals further diversified the range of distorted sounds available to guitarists.

# instrument - Synthesizer

## curiosities
**Bob Moog's Influence** : Bob Moog is recognized as the inventor of the synthesizer, and his pioneering machines fascinated Malcolm Cecil and Robert Marguliffe in the late 1960s, inspiring them to build the monumental Tonto.

**Creation and Capabilities of Tonto** : Tonto, short for "The Original New Timbral Orchestra," was constructed by Malcolm Cecil and Robert Marguliffe as the world's largest modular synthesizer. This colossal instrument featured gear from Moog, Oberheim, ARP, EMS, Roland, and Yamaha, along with custom-made analog synth modules, keyboards, patch bays, and joysticks. It enabled the creation of entirely new sounds and the simultaneous layering of drum tracks, bass lines, and multiple melodies—a novel capability at the time.

**Physical Description of Tonto** : Tonto was a massive instrument, installed in a series of wooden cabinets arranged in a 25-foot diameter semicircle, standing 6 feet tall. Weighing approximately one ton, its immense size was a defining characteristic. It also made a cameo appearance in the movie "Phantom of the Paradise," visible in the background of the studio where Winslow Leech worked.

**Later Life and Restoration of Tonto** : After its influential period with Stevie Wonder and other artists in the mid-1970s, Tonto's immense size became a disadvantage as smaller, cheaper, and more powerful synthesizers emerged. It was moved between studios and eventually became "unwanted and unloved," even residing for a time in Mark Mothersbaugh of Devo's studio in Los Angeles. In 2013, Tonto was put up for sale, purchased, and subsequently restored by the National Music Center in Calgary, where it now forms a significant part of their keyboard collection.

**Tonto's Impact on Synthesizer Technology** : Tonto's groundbreaking capabilities and its use by prominent artists like Stevie Wonder played a significant role in driving synthesizer manufacturers to advance their technology throughout the 1970s. This pressure led to the development of smaller, faster, and cheaper electronic keyboards, which in turn fostered the creation of new music genres like Techno Pop.
