**MS Diagnosis (April 2017)** : Art Alexakis began noticing symptoms in 2015, including stumbling and forgetting lyrics, which some mistook for drinking or drug use. He dismissed it as tiredness. After a car crash in April 2017 and a subsequent arm twinge, an MRI, arranged by his daughter, revealed lacerations on his brain indicative of multiple sclerosis. He kept the diagnosis quiet for four years. "Then in April 2017, he was on his way to pick up his daughter at school. It was raining, and he crashed into a jeep. Now nobody was hurt, but then two weeks later, he had a twinge in his arm that he thought was left over from a snowboarding crash or maybe the jeep crash. His daughter set him up with an MRI, and that's when he learned the news. They found lacerations on Art's brain indicative of multiple sclerosis."
**Managing Multiple Sclerosis** : Initially viewing his MS diagnosis as a death sentence, Art Alexakis's prognosis improved after working with doctors who prescribed Copazone. He takes three injections a week, avoids inflammatory foods like sugar, and has been told by his doctor he could have another 30 years if his condition remains stable. While he can no longer run and has taken up swimming, his guitar playing remains unaffected, and he doesn't expect to be confined to a wheelchair. "The prognosis isn't as bad as he thought it might be, and Art realizes that he may need a cane later in life, but he shouldn't be confined to a wheelchair. He hopes. He gives himself three injections a week, yes to stay away from inflammatory foods like sugar, and if things continue the way they are, Art's doctor says he could have another 30 years."

**Aaron Solowoniuk's MS Diagnosis (1998)** : Drummer Aaron Solowoniuk was diagnosed with multiple sclerosis in 1998 at age 24, before the band's rise to fame, while working on an assembly line at a Chrysler factory. His initial symptoms included buzzing legs, difficulty walking, optic neuritis in his left eye resulting in vision loss, and general confusion. He managed the condition for over a decade with medication and a positive outlook. "What was 24? What were the symptoms? Buzzing legs from my hips down. I could barely walk to my job at Chrysler. I had an optic neuritis in my left eye, so I lost a lot of vision in my eye, and just being confused and trying to figure out what all that meant was really difficult."
**Relapse and Stepping Back from Recording (January 15, 2016)** : In a YouTube message posted on January 15, 2016, Aaron Solowoniuk announced he was experiencing an MS relapse that had slowed him down over the past 6-7 months, making even simple tasks like climbing stairs daunting and drumming very tiring. Due to his health, he decided he couldn't play drums on the band's upcoming record and needed to focus on getting healthy. "On January 15th, 2016, Aaron posted a message on YouTube. Just wanted to let everybody know what is going on in Billy Talent World right now. The album is being recorded as we speak... Over the past six or seven months. I've been dealing with a relapse."
**Exploring Alternative MS Management** : Aaron Solowoniuk decided to stop taking all prescribed drugs for three months, as newer medications had not worked and made him feel worse. He is now pursuing a regimen of healthy eating and exercise, hoping to manage his MS symptoms naturally, with a plan to explore newer therapies if this approach proves ineffective. "Right now I've stopped taking all of my... I'm three months into no drugs at all. I'm going the eating healthy and exercising route to see how that works because the drugs that I've been taking, the newer ones, haven't really worked for me and they kind of made me feel worse."
**Band Support and Charitable Work** : Despite Aaron Solowoniuk's ongoing health challenges and reduced ability to play, Billy Talent continues to support him. The band also engages in numerous charitable initiatives with MS groups, demonstrating their commitment to raising awareness and funds for the disease. "It's been tough for Aaron and he hasn't been able to play with the band as much as he'd hoped but he's still with the guys and the guys are still supporting him, including with numerous charitable initiatives with MS groups."

**Ian Curtis's Epilepsy Diagnosis (January 23, 1979)** : Ian Curtis began experiencing seizures in the fall of 1978, which rapidly became frequent. "Ian started having seizures in the fall of 1978. They quickly became frequent. On January 23rd, 1979, the diagnosis was confirmed. And the diagnosis was grim. His case was really bad."
**Initial Openness and Later Withdrawal on Epilepsy** : Initially, Ian Curtis was very proactive about his epilepsy, joining the British Epilepsy Association and openly discussing his condition. However, he soon ceased being open, eventually refusing to talk about it altogether. "At first, Ian was very proactive, joining the British Epilepsy Association and was willing to talk to anybody about what he faced. But he soon stopped being so open about his condition and eventually refused to talk about it."
**Worsening Condition and Lifestyle Factors** : Despite trying various anti-convulsant drugs, Ian Curtis's epilepsy showed little improvement. His lifestyle, including touring, late nights, drinking, smoking, and drug use, was detrimental, leading to increasingly frequent and stronger seizures throughout 1979 and early 1980. "Meanwhile, many different anti-convulsants were tried with little effect. Turing, the late nights, the drinking, the smoking, and the drugs did not help. And the seizures became more frequent and stronger through 1979 and early 1980."
**Medication Side Effects and Personal Impact** : The medication Ian Curtis was taking for his epilepsy caused wild mood swings. The fear of having a seizure while holding his baby daughter made him hesitant to embrace her, and he was known to suffer at least two severe seizures weekly. "The men, he was on, caused wild mood swings. When his wife gave birth to a baby daughter, Ian was afraid to hold her or less the seizure begin and he actually hurt her. He could be counted on to have at least two giant seizures a week."
**On-Stage Seizures and Collapse** : Ian Curtis's epilepsy frequently affected his live performances. During one studio session, he fell, hit his head on a sink, and lay unconscious. Strobe lights at a show once induced a seizure, requiring him to be carried off stage, an event that was not isolated, as he collapsed regularly during gigs. "One night in the studio, he fell and hit his head on the sink and laid unconscious on the floor. During a show that featured strobe lights, a seizure was induced and he had to be carried off stage. And that wasn't the first time. Ian collapsed during gigs almost regularly."
**Depression and Personal Turmoil (April 1980)** : By April 1980, Ian Curtis was severely depressed, a state compounded not only by his epilepsy but also by a failing affair with a Belgian fan, the impending split from his wife, and dire financial difficulties that forced them to give away their family dog. He desired solitude but was ill-equipped to live alone. "By April 1980, Ian was very, very depressed. Not just as a result of the epilepsy either. He was having an affair with a Belgian fan. He wanted to be alone but really wasn't equipped to live alone."
**Suicide (May 18, 1980)** : After previous suicide attempts, Ian Curtis took his own life by hanging himself from a clothesline in his kitchen in the early morning hours of May 18, 1980. This tragic event occurred on the very day Joy Division was scheduled to depart for their first-ever tour of North America. "There had been some suicide attempts. That Monday was the day joy division was supposed to leave for their first ever tour of North America."
