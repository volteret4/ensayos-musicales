# genre - Ska

## curiosities
**Definition and Structure** : Ska is defined as a choppy form of music in 4/4 time, characterized by an emphasis on the second and fourth beat of each bar, augmented by the guitar hitting on the first and third, and sometimes fourth beat. The guitar technique used is called the "Ska upstroke," contributing to its very danceable quality. The bass line typically plays steady quarter notes up and down the scale. Brass instruments like trombone or saxophone are often included for added effect. "SCA is a choppy form of music in 4x4 time. The key is the timing of the emphasis on the beat. It's on the second and fourth beat of each bar. That's augmented by the guitar, hitting on the first and third, and sometimes on the fourth beat."
**Tempo and Evolution** : Ska can be played at various tempos. If slowed down to around 80 beats per minute, it transforms into "rock steady," and slowing it down further results in "reggae." "SCA can be played at a variety of tempos, although if you slow it down too much, it actually morphs into a new sound called rock steady at around 80 beats per minute. And you slow it down and you have something called reggae."
**Birth in Jamaica (Late 1940s)** : Ska originated in Jamaica in the years following World War II, influenced by a mix of Western European music, North American radio (Dixieland, jazz, blues, R&B), and indigenous Caribbean sounds like mental music and calypso. These influences combined to create "shuffle," which was popular throughout Jamaica in the 1950s and early 1960s, eventually evolving into Ska. "Well at least since the late 1940s. SCA was born in Jamaica in the years following World War II."
**First Wave (1962-Late 1960s)** : The "first wave" of Ska began in 1962 when Cecil Bustamenta Campbell and guitarist Jerry Jha shifted the beat emphasis from the one and three to the two and four, stressing the offbeat. This coincided with Jamaica's independence from Britain, making Ska the semi-official sound of the Independence Movement. The Skatalites were a key studio band during this era. "The big change happened in 1962 when one of Coxen's buddies, Cecil Bustamenta Campbell, changed the beat. Cecil and his guitarist Jerry Jha moved the beat emphasis from the one and the three to the two and the four. So in other words, they began to stress the offbeat. And this is pretty much when Scott was born."
**Naming Origins Debate** : The origin of the term "Ska" is debated. Theories include Cluett Johnson's made-up word "Scavuvi" used to describe his band's sound, or Byron Lee's potential coinage. Another theory suggests it came from Don Drummond's band, which by 1964, was known as The Skatalites. "That's up for debate. Some say credit should go to Cluit Johnson. In the late 50s and early 60s, he let a band called Clu J and his blues blasters."
**Dominance and Decline of First Wave** : Ska became the dominant music in Jamaica through the mid-1960s, inspiring a national dance called Skanking and producing international hits like Desmond Dekker's "The Israelites." However, by the end of the 1960s, Ska was supplanted by its slower descendant, Reggae, marking the end of the first wave. "Despite all this, SCAT became the dominant music in Jamaica through the mid 1960s. Even a national dance called Skanking emerged."
**Second Wave (Late 1970s-1985)** : The "second wave" of Ska emerged in the UK in the wake of punk, fueled by Jamaican immigrants who moved to Britain after Jamaica's independence due to a post-war labor shortage. It was initially known as "Bluebeat" and coexisted with R&B, Soul, and Reggae, embraced by British youth subcultures like mods, rockers, teddy boys, and early punks. "The second wave came in the wake of punk. Jamaica became an independent nation in 1962, so no more British colony."
**Second Wave Characteristics** : The second wave mixed older Ska/Reggae sounds with punk attitude, leading to bands like The Specials, Madness, The Selector, and The Body Snatchers. Jerry Dammers formed the Two Tone record label, which was central to this movement. This era involved covers of original 1960s Jamaican Ska. "These older sounds got mixed in with the new ones coming out of the punk scene. And one of the first guys to really figure it out was Jerry Damors, an artsy guy from Coventry."
**Second Wave Decline** : Despite its positive, multiracial appeal and message of unity, the second wave scene was infiltrated by racist organizations like the National Front, causing violence at gigs. The talent pool became diluted due to too much Ska too quickly, and the music grew stale. Two Tone records faced financial issues, and the British Music Press moved on, causing the second wave to largely end by 1985. "Despite its positivity, its multiracial makeup and appeal, and its message of unity, the scene was eventually infiltrated by racist organizations, such as the National Front, which started creating problems at SCAT gigs. Lots of violence."
**Third Wave (Late 1980s-Early 2000s)** : The "third wave" of Ska emerged in the late 1980s, primarily from California, with bands like Fishbone. It became massive during the time of grunge and the Alt Rock explosion in the early 1990s. This wave retained original Jamaican sounds but featured more prominent guitar riffs and liberal use of bigger horn sections. "If we really dig into this we can see the stirrings of this third wave in the very late 1980s through the rise of Fishbone and California as well as a few bands from the East Coast."
**Third Wave Influences** : American punk underground fans, enchanted by the British Ska revival, fused Ska with hardcore influences for the third wave. Early examples include The Mighty Mighty Bosstones from Boston, and later Rancid. Other bands included Voodoo Glow Skulls, Reel Big Fish, Less Than Jake, Buck-O-Nine, and Dance Hall Crashers. "Given their background they inevitably fused SCAT with hardcore influences. So we're going to start on the East Coast in Boston with the Mighty Mighty Boss Tones."
**Third Wave Flavors and Global Reach** : Third wave Ska, also known as "Ska Punk," had several distinct flavors, with some bands leaning more punk/metal (like Rancid) and others exploring new wave sounds (like No Doubt). While California was the epicenter, third wave Ska became a global phenomenon with scenes in Canada, Spain, Germany, Japan, Argentina, and Russia. It began to melt away by the early 2000s but remains popular. "SCAT Punk, as it became known, had several flavors. Take this group out of Orange County, who struggled through two albums, before something really got weird, with a 1995 album called Tragedy Kingdom."
**Fourth Wave (2002-Present)** : The "fourth wave" began to emerge between 2002 and 2004, driven by a new generation of Ska bands and the continued activity of second and third-wave groups (e.g., The Specials reuniting, Rancid and Reel Big Fish finding new fans, Fishbone still active). This wave respects Ska traditions but introduces new elements, sometimes called "progressive Ska." "Now for there to be a 4th wave, we had to have cooperation from both 2nd and 3rd wave bands. The specials that Key Band from the 2nd wave got back together 40 years after they last recorded together."
**Fourth Wave Characteristics and Examples** : Fourth-wave bands include Streetlight Manifesto (leaning punk), and California's "Beatska" or "Hippie-skaw" (mixing reggae, hip hop, and punk, like Slightly Stoopid). Other examples include The Steady 45s, The Bandulus, and The Soul Radics, with The Interrupters currently leading the movement. "Some of these groups refer to what they do as progressive SCAT. Others are happy with just 4th wave. They respect the traditions of SCAT, yet try to do something a little different."
**Resilience of Ska** : Ska has proven to be one of the most resilient and enduring forms of music in the modern era, having undergone three distinct revivals. "SCAT has proven to be one of the most resilient and unkillable forms of music of the modern era, with three revivals."

These revivals offer music a chance to renew, regenerate, and evolve. "Music revivals can be defined as social movements, where segment of the population decides that a specific era or musical system needs to be restored for whatever reason. **Clement "Coxsone" Dodd - Downbeat Sound System** : Clement "Coxsone" Dodd spent time in the US South in the 1950s absorbing blues and R&B. Upon returning to Jamaica, he started playing records he acquired in America from his parents' shop, calling this operation the Downbeat Sound System, which proved very popular. He would periodically return to the States for more records, hiring Lee "Scratch" Perry to manage the sound system in his absence. "He spent some time in the US South in the 1950s, soaking a blues and RMB. And when he got back home to Jamaica, he started playing some of the records he got in America in his parents' shop. He called this the Downbeat Sound System."
**Cecil Bustamenta Campbell and Jerry Jha - Ska Beat Innovation** : In 1962, Cecil Bustamenta Campbell, a colleague of Coxsone Dodd, along with his guitarist Jerry Jha, changed the musical beat by moving the emphasis from the one and three to the two and four, thereby stressing the offbeat. This innovation is largely credited with the birth of Ska. "The big change happened in 1962 when one of Coxen's buddies, Cecil Bustamenta Campbell, changed the beat. Cecil and his guitarist Jerry Jha moved the beat emphasis from the one and the three to the two and the four. So in other words, they began to stress the offbeat. And this is pretty much when Scott was born."
**Jamaican Independence and Ska** : The birth of Ska coincided with Jamaica's transition from a British colony to an independent nation in 1962. This new music became the semi-official sound of the Independence Movement, symbolizing a new national identity. "Not really began to catch on at the same time as Jamaica moved from a British colony to an independent nation. And Scott, this new music, became the semi-official sound of the Independence Movement."

**Skanking Dance** : Skanking emerged as a national dance in Jamaica during the mid-1960s, reflecting the widespread popularity and cultural impact of Ska music. "Even a national dance called Skanking emerged."
**UK Post-War Labor Shortage** : Following World War II, the United Kingdom experienced a labor shortage. In response, the UK invited citizens of the Commonwealth, including Jamaicans, to immigrate to alleviate the problem. This migration played a crucial role in bringing Ska music to Britain and sparking the "second wave" revival. "Meanwhile though, the UK was suffering through a post-war labor shortage. Jamaica had the choice of tuffing it out at home through a period of grinding poverty, or immigrating to the UK and take their chances there."
**Coventry as Immigration Hub** : Many Jamaican immigrants who moved to the UK to address the post-war labor shortage settled in the industrial city of Coventry in Central England, naturally bringing their music, including Ska (then known as Bluebeat), with them. "a lot of Jamaicans went for the offer, many settled in the industrial city of Coventry in Central England. Naturally, these new citizens brought their music with them, and that meant SCAT."
**Jerry Dammers - Creative Rebellion** : Jerry Dammers, an artsy individual from Coventry and the son of a minister, loved older music but also embraced the attitude of punk. He channeled this into creating a unique sound for his band, The Specials, as a way of rebelling against his bleak, working-class upbringing. "He was also the son of a minister, so all this was a great way of rebelling against how he grew up in this bleak working class existence."
**Rude Boys - Jamaican Petty Criminals** : "Rudy" refers to "rude boys," who were petty criminals found in places like Kingston, Jamaica. Over the years, "rude boys" was shortened to "Rudy," and the term was popularized in the second wave Ska scene, notably by The Specials' cover of "A Message to You Rudy." "Well, rude boys, those petty criminals that could be found in places like Kingston, Jamaica, and over the years, rude boys were shortened to just Rudy."
**National Front Infiltration of Ska Scene** : Despite the second wave Ska scene's positive, multiracial makeup and message of unity, it was eventually infiltrated by racist organizations such as the National Front. This group created problems at Ska gigs, leading to significant violence and contributing to the decline of the second wave. "the scene was eventually infiltrated by racist organizations, such as the National Front, which started creating problems at SCAT gigs. Lots of violence."
**Talent Dilution and Stale Music** : The rapid increase in Ska music production during the second wave led to an oversaturation of the market, causing the talent pool to become diluted and the music to grow stale, which contributed to the scene's decline. "Meanwhile there was too much SCAT to quickly. The talent pool became diluted and the music started to get stale."
**British Music Press Boredom** : The British Music Press eventually grew bored with the second wave Ska movement and shifted its focus to other genres, specifically Rockabilly, which further contributed to the decline and end of the second wave by 1985. "And then the British Music Press got bored of the whole thing and moved on to something else. In this case, it was another revival. Rockabilly."
**California as Third Wave Epicenter** : California was identified as the primary epicenter for most of the third wave Ska movement, although it also had a global reach with scenes in various other countries. "California was the epicenter of most of this, but third wave SCAT was a global thing, with scenes taking hold in Canada, Spain, Germany, Japan, Argentina, and even Russia."
**Fourth Wave Origins** : The roots of the fourth wave revival of Ska can be traced back to the period between 2002 and 2004, when a new generation of Ska bands began to emerge, building on the legacy of previous waves. "If you dig into this, the roots of this revival go back to some time between 2002 and 2004, when a new generation of these SCAT bands began to emerge."
