## songs
**Love Me Do (1963)** : This song first hit Canadian airwaves on February 18, 1963. Initially, it sold only 170 copies in Canada, as reported by an executive for Capitol Records Canada, indicating a limited early interest. However, it was later reissued due to a massive surge in public demand following the success of "She Loves You." "All white, the executive for Capital Records Canada said the Beatles only sold 170 copies in total."
**Please Please Me (1963)** : This single sold a modest 280 records in Canada upon its initial release, reflecting the early lack of Canadian enthusiasm for The Beatles' music. Its sales, much like "Love Me Do," saw a significant boost and reissue after "She Loves You" became a breakthrough hit. "Please please me would sell 280 records."
**From Me to You (1963)** : With an initial sale of 300 copies in Canada, this song further demonstrated the lukewarm reception The Beatles received from Canadian audiences in their early career. It was subsequently reissued to meet the public's renewed clamor for the band after their popularity soared with "She Loves You." "from me to you sold 300."
**She Loves You (1963)** : Co-written by John Lennon and Paul McCartney, this song, released in September 1963, marked The Beatles' significant breakthrough in Canada. It rapidly gained immense popularity, selling 100,000 copies and causing such a huge spike in demand that their earlier songs had to be reissued to meet the public's newfound interest. "Written by John Lennon and Paul McCartney, the song quickly exploded in popularity, selling 100,000 copies, and due to a huge spike in demand, the earlier songs were reissued, as the public now clenored for the Beatles."
**All My Love (N/A)** : This song was uniquely available in Canada nearly a year before The Beatles gained widespread recognition in the United States. Due to this Canadian availability, Capitol Records Canada was responsible for manufacturing and then exporting at least 100,000 copies (with some estimates reaching 350,000) of this record to the American market. "songs such as All My Love and Roll Over Bay, Tovon and Twist and Shout were only available in Canada."
**Roll Over Bay, Tovon (N/A)** : Another song that was exclusively available in Canada almost a year prior to The Beatles' breakthrough in the United States. Its demand in Canada and subsequent export to the US by Capitol Records Canada highlighted the band's earlier embrace north of the border. "songs such as All My Love and Roll Over Bay, Tovon and Twist and Shout were only available in Canada."
**Twist and Shout (1963)** : The Beatles' famous rendition of this song was a cover based on The Isley Brothers' version, released on their first UK album, *Please Please Me*, with John Lennon on lead vocals. It served as the opening song for their Vancouver concert on August 22, 1964, and was notably available in Canada almost a year before the group's significant breakthrough in the United States. "The song was a cover based on the Isley Brothers version, the Beatles' rendition of Twist and Shout was released on their first UK album, Please Please Me, and John Lennon provided lead vocals."
**I Want to Hold Your Hand (N/A)** : Despite being one of their biggest songs at the time, "I Want to Hold Your Hand" was conspicuously absent from the 11-song setlist performed by The Beatles at their Vancouver concert on August 22, 1964. "which oddly didn't include I want to hold your hand one of their biggest songs at the time."

## curiosities
**First North American Tour Stop in Winnipeg (1964)** : On a warm, cloudy day in mid-August 1964, The Beatles were flying from London to San Francisco for their inaugural major North American tour, making an unplanned refueling stop in Winnipeg. This stop unexpectedly turned into a major event when CJAYTV's Bob Burns announced their arrival on local radio, leading to the airport being swarmed by frenzied fans. "The band landed on August 18, 1964 at 2.5pm."
**Bryce Decker's Airport Escapade (1964)** : On August 18, 1964, local high school student Bryce Decker took a daring chance at Winnipeg airport during The Beatles' refueling stop. He ran across the runway, climbed the plane's stairs, and nearly entered the aircraft before being restrained by security, an escapade that was captured in a series of photographs. Decker later recalled seeing the band members chuckling as security wrestled with him, having "just did it for a bit of fun" without realizing its significance. "After arriving at the airport, he ran across the runway, up the stairs, and nearly got into the plane before he was grouted by security."
**Paul McCartney Greets Winnipeg (1964)** : Following the incident with Bryce Decker at the Winnipeg airport on August 18, 1964, Paul McCartney exited the plane, yelled "Hello, Winnipeg," and descended the stairs, where he was immediately swarmed by a throng of reporters, including Bob Burns. This brief appearance was the band's only direct interaction with the Winnipeg crowd, as they never returned to the city. "Oh, and the man who greeted the crowd was none other than Paul McCartney."
**Ringo Starr's Gregarious Nature** : Bob Burns, the CJAYTV reporter who announced The Beatles' arrival in Winnipeg and interviewed John Lennon, later stated that Ringo Starr was the most gregarious member of the band and appeared to be the most mature. "He would later state that Ringo Star was the most gregarious of the band and seemed the most mature."
**Greatest Musical Group in History** : The Beatles are widely considered and continue to be regarded as the greatest musical group in history, a statement presented in the transcript as an undeniable fact. Their global tours in the 1960s brought their music to millions, establishing them as an irresistible force. "They are considered and still are the greatest musical group in history, and I'm sorry, but there's no debate about that."
**Early Canadian Disinterest (1963)** : Despite their eventual global success, The Beatles initially struggled to gain traction in Canada in early 1963. Capitol Records Canada reported very low sales for their early singles: "Love Me Do" sold 170 copies, "Please Please Me" sold 280, and "From Me to You" sold 300, suggesting Canada was initially uninterested. "It seemed that Canada was not interested in the Beatles, but that all changed with the release of She Loves You in September 1963."
**Canada's Early Embrace (1963-1964)** : The Beatles were embraced in Canada nearly a year before their breakthrough in the United States, with songs like "All My Love," "Roll Over Bay, Tovon," and "Twist and Shout" exclusively available there. Capitol Records Canada played a crucial role by manufacturing these records north of the 49th parallel and then exporting at least 100,000, and potentially up to 350,000, copies of each song to the US. "Although the United States gets a lot of attention because it's where the Beatles made their TV debut, it was in Canada where they were embraced first."
**1964 North American Tour & Ed Sullivan Appearance** : By the spring of 1964, The Beatles had achieved massive popularity not only in their home country but also across North America. This included performances in New York, Washington, and Miami in February, alongside their famous appearance on the Ed Sullivan Show, all generating immense buzz. "In February, the group had played New York, Washington and Miami, had appeared on the Ed Sullivan show, and they were attracting the same sort of buzz they had at home."
**First Official Canadian Performance in Vancouver (1964)** : The Beatles' first official Canadian concert and performance took place on August 22, 1964, at the Pacific National Exhibition (PNE) in Vancouver, British Columbia. The PNE is an annual 15-day summer fair and amusement park. "And the concert was held at the Pacific National Exhibition in Vancouver, British Columbia."
**Vancouver Concert Delay and Paperwork (1964)** : The Beatles' Vancouver concert on August 22, 1964, was scheduled for 8 pm, but a significant delay occurred because the pilot of their plane forgot to sign a required document. This forced the band to return to the airport to complete the paperwork, while 20,000 fans impatiently waited at the stadium. "The show was supposed to start at 8pm, but the pilot of the plane bringing the Beatles to Canada forgot to sign a document, and the band was forced to return to the airport to finish the paperwork and then head back to the stadium."
**John Lennon's Clash with Red Robinson (1964)** : Halfway through the Vancouver concert on August 22, 1964, as the crowd neared rioting, local radio host Red Robinson was instructed to go on stage to calm the fans. John Lennon, unaware of the situation, confronted Robinson, telling him, "Get off our stage, nobody interrupts the Beatles," before relenting after Robinson explained that manager Brian Epstein had sent him. "John looked down at Brian Epstein, the band's manager who gave him the okay. John turned Robinson and said, Okay, carry on mate."
**Brief Vancouver Performance (1964)** : The Beatles' concert in Vancouver on August 22, 1964, despite the massive anticipation and crowd frenzy, lasted only 27 minutes. They played 11 songs, notably omitting "I Want to Hold Your Hand," one of their biggest hits at the time. Immediately after the show, the band was whisked away to Vancouver International Airport and flown straight to Los Angeles, marking their one and only performance in Western Canada. "Then just 27 minutes after they arrived on stage, they left."
**Chaotic Toronto Airport Arrival (1964)** : On September 6, 1964, over 10,000 young people stormed Toronto's international airport to catch their first glimpse of The Beatles. The frenzied crowd jostled behind a 60-meter chain-link fence, belting out verses from their chart-topping tracks and screaming in a way the city had never heard before. "Behind a 60 meter chain link fence, the frenzy crowd jostled for position and belted out verses from chart crushing tracks such as, Please, please me, love, be, do, and she loves you."
**King Edward Hotel Frenzy (1964)** : Upon their arrival at the King Edward Hotel in downtown Toronto on September 6, 1964, a frenzy of fans greeted The Beatles, leading to Paul McCartney having his shirt torn and Ringo Starr being separated from John and George. Although police quickly restored order on the street, Beatlemania continued inside, where a 14-year-old girl was found hiding in the linen closet of their royal suite. "Paul would have a shirt torn open by a fan, wrangle with separated from John and George, but thankfully police were able to quickly restore order on the street and get the band into the hotel."
**Mayor Phil Givens Snubbed (1964)** : On September 6, 1964, Toronto mayor Phil Givens and his wife attempted to visit The Beatles at the King Edward Hotel at 1:30 pm but were turned away by a "blonde woman." She claimed two of the Beatles were sleeping and two were with relatives, an incident that led to the Daily Star publishing an article with the headline, "Beatles Blonde, Snob is Mayor." "The next day, the daily star released an article with the headline, Beatles Blonde, Snob is Mayor."
**Hotel Invasion by Fans (1964)** : During The Beatles' stay at the King Edward Hotel in Toronto in September 1964, teens relentlessly bypassed security, creating an "infestation" for which the hotel seemed to have "no repellent." At 3 am, radio journalist Larry Cain, traveling with the band, encountered a teen in an oversized housekeeping uniform knocking on his door, asking for soap and then if he could take her to The Beatles. "Teens kept getting past security. It was as if the hotel was dealing with an infestation for which they had no repellent."
**Massive Police Deployment in Toronto (1964)** : The Beatles' first visit to Toronto in September 1964 necessitated an extraordinary police response, with more than 850 officers from the city, the Ontario Provincial Police, and the counties called in. By noon, 4,000 police officers and auxiliaries were on duty around Maple Leaf Gardens, dividing the crowd into smaller sections, with majestic horses used to push back waves of "deranged youth." "The band's first visit to Toronto required more than 850 officers from the city, the Ontario provincial police, and the counties."
**Concert Logistics at Maple Leaf Gardens (1964)** : For the September 6, 1964, concerts at Maple Leaf Gardens, a detailed plan was devised to get The Beatles safely to the venue. Limousines served as a decoy, drawing fans while the band slipped out the back door of the King Edward Hotel and into a paddy wagon, which smuggled them into the Gardens. A five-block area around the venue was sectioned off 12 hours prior, transforming downtown Toronto into "Beedleland." "Limos were to pull up at the King Edward Hotel, as expected, as fans pounded at the vehicles like zombies the band would dug out the back door and into a patty wagon that would smuggle them into the gardens."
**Extreme Conditions at Toronto Concert (1964)** : The atmosphere inside Maple Leaf Gardens on September 6, 1964, was intensely hot and muggy, with temperatures around 30 degrees Celsius outside and even higher inside. Soda and ice cream vendors ran out of supplies, and water fountains barely functioned, yet the fervent fans' dehydration and exhaustion were no match for their enthusiasm. "It was a hot, muggy, late summer day with temperatures at around 30 degrees Celsius. Inside the venue it was even hotter, and soda and ice cream vendors were running out."
**Opening Acts Booed by Impatient Crowd (1964)** : At the Maple Leaf Gardens concert in Toronto on September 6, 1964, the opening bands, which included Bill Black's Combo, The Exciters, Clarence "Frogman" Henry, and Jackie De Shannon, were met with boos from the crowd. The audience was impatient and made it clear they wanted only one band: The Beatles. "As the opening bands, which included Bill Black's combo, the exciders Clarence Frogman Henry, and the Celterie Jackie De Shannon hit the stage to crowd-bood, they only wanted one band, and one band only."
**Mass Hysteria at Maple Leaf Gardens (1964)** : Upon The Beatles' appearance on stage at Maple Leaf Gardens on September 6, 1964, flash bulbs went off, blinding everyone, and the roar of 16,761 screaming voices made it impossible to hear the music, blinding and deafening the band. Items like bras and lipsticks rained down, leading to 109 fans fainting at the venue, described as "mass hysteria" rather than just a rock show. "It's unclear if they could even hear their own voices over the roar of 16,761 streaming voices, nor do we know if they could see anything as bras, lipsticks, and other items rain down on them."
**Post-Concert Activities and Press Conference (1964)** : After their first concert at Maple Leaf Gardens ended around 6 pm on September 6, 1964, The Beatles were quickly moved to a dressing room. There, they took photographs with local DJs, fan club presidents, and Miss Canada, followed by a press conference where they appeared somewhat bewildered but maintained their charming demeanor and "savage wit" towards journalists. "They then had a press conference where they looked a little bewildered, but as usual they were charming to admirers, and unleashed their savage wit on journalists."
**Identical Second Toronto Concert (1964)** : The Beatles' second concert at Maple Leaf Gardens in Toronto, which began at 10 pm on September 6, 1964, was virtually identical to the first. It featured screaming fans, blinding flash bulbs, raining lipsticks and bras, resulting in continued frenzy and fainting among the audience, echoing the "mass hysteria" of the earlier show. "If you've seen footage from a Beatles concert, you can imagine that the second concert was identical to the first."
**Peaceful Departure from Toronto (1964)** : The day after their chaotic concerts, just before noon on September 7, 1964, The Beatles headed back to the Toronto airport. The sun was shining, the crowd was considerably smaller than the previous day, and the mood was distinctly different. The band signed autographs, posed for photos, waved to their admirers, and then boarded their plane, marking the end of their first Toronto visit. "The sun was shining, and the crowd was considerably smaller. The mood was also different. This was the end."
**Montreal Arrival and Death Threats (1964)** : The Beatles' arrival at Dorval Airport for their only visit to La Belle province mirrored the scenes at other tour stops, with hordes of screaming fans. However, their time in Quebec was significantly marred by death threats from French Canadian separatists, specifically the FLQ, leading to the presence of police sharpshooters at the Montreal Forum on St. Catherine Street for their two concerts. "Their arrival at the Dorval Airport mirrored what happened at every stop on their tour. Hordes of screaming fans at the airport throngs of fans lying the streets, clutching magazines and singing their favorite Beatles songs."
**George Harrison on Ringo's Death Threat (1990)** : In 1990, George Harrison recounted a specific death threat against Ringo Starr during The Beatles' 1964 Montreal visit. He stated that a Montreal newspaper reported someone intended to kill Ringo, possibly because they disliked his nose or because he was perceived as "the most British of the Beatles." "A Montreal newspaper reported that somebody was going to kill Ringo because they didn't like his nose or something, because he was probably the most British of the Beatles."
**Ringo Starr's Worry Over Montreal Threats (1964)** : Ringo Starr expressed genuine worry about the death threats received during The Beatles' 1964 Montreal concerts. He recalled that some individuals targeted him "as an English Jew," despite him not being Jewish, noting that while threats were often taken "in stride," this particular incident, despite more police presence, was one of the few times he was truly concerned. "Some people decided to make an example of me as an English Jew. The one major fault is, I'm not Jewish. Threats we took in stride. I mean, suddenly there'd be a few more cops, but this was one of the few times I was really worried."
**Brief Montreal Stay (1964)** : The Beatles played two concerts at the Montreal Forum on September 8, 1964, performing a 12-song set for 9,500 fans at 5:20 pm and another show for 11,500 fans at 8:30 pm. They chose not to stay in Montreal, instead flying directly to Jacksonville, Florida, having been in the city for only eight hours. "The band stayed in Montreal for only 8 hours."
**Return to Toronto and Fading Beatlemania (1965)** : On August 17, 1965, The Beatles returned to Canada, flying in from New York to Pearson Airport (then Toronto International Airport), where fans again rushed them, met by Toronto police. They stayed at the King Edward Hotel, where "smart fans" booked dozens of rooms. However, the enthusiasm had waned slightly, as newspaper coverage shifted from front-page headlines to inside pages, with many suggesting that Beatlemania was fading and the band's popularity might soon disappear. "Unlike the previous year when the band made front page news in Toronto, this year, they were on the inside pages, with many starting to say that Biddlemania was fading, and the band would soon disappear."
**Last Canadian and Touring Concerts (1966)** : The Beatles returned to Canada on August 17, 1966, for their final two concerts ever on Canadian soil in Toronto. This tour also marked the last time the band would ever play live, consisting of only eight shows over 12 days before they stopped touring forever to focus exclusively on studio music. "Just a year later in 1966, the band returned for their last two concerts ever on Canadian soil. The tour was also the last the band would ever play."
**Final Toronto Concerts (1966)** : On August 17, 1966, The Beatles performed their last Canadian concerts in Toronto, with 15,000 fans attending the first show and 17,000 for the second at 8 pm. Bill J. Nelson served as MC, and the routine chaos continued, with 50 audience members treated for shock and screaming volumes making the music barely audible. "That number only went up to 17,000 fans for their second concert at 8pm."
**Departure from Canada (1966)** : After their final concerts in Toronto on August 17, 1966, The Beatles stayed overnight before flying out to Boston on August 18th. This departure marked their definitive farewell to Canadian stages, as they never toured again. "This time, they left forever."
**Individual Members' Solo Careers** : Following The Beatles' decision to stop touring in 1966 and their eventual breakup, individual members — John Lennon, George Harrison, Paul McCartney, and Ringo Starr — all went on to play solo shows in Canada. "And while the group would never play Canada again, the country would still see individual members over the next few years."

## curiosities
**Montreal Bed-In for Peace (1969)** : John Lennon and his partner Yoko Ono held a week-long non-violent "bed-in for peace" protest in Montreal from May 26 to June 1, 1969. Intended as a novel way to promote peace against the Vietnam War, this event followed an earlier bed-in in Amsterdam, and was planned for Montreal after Lennon was denied entry to the United States due to a 1968 cannabis conviction and a brief, uncomfortable stay in the Bahamas due to heat. "On May 26th, June 1st, 1969, he held a bed in for peace protest in Montreal."
**US Entry Denied (1969)** : John Lennon was barred from entering the United States for a planned second bed-in for peace in New York due to a 1968 cannabis conviction. This travel restriction was a key factor in his decision to host the bed-in in Montreal instead. "But due to a 1968 cannabis conviction, Lennon was not allowed in the United States."
**Bahamas Attempt (1969)** : Prior to the Montreal bed-in, John Lennon and Yoko Ono intended to host the event in the Bahamas. They flew there on May 24, 1969, but only spent one night because of the oppressive heat, leading them to seek an alternative location in Canada. "They then intended to host the event in the Bahamas, but after flying in on May 24th, 1969, they only spent one night there because of the heat."
**Canadian Visa and Hotel Choices (1969)** : After the Bahamas attempt, John Lennon and Yoko Ono landed in Toronto and were granted a 10-day visitor visa, staying at the King Edward Hotel. They ultimately chose Montreal over Toronto for their bed-in because of its proximity to New York, which would facilitate easier American press coverage. "The choice was between Montreal and Toronto, and ultimately they chose Montreal because it was close to New York, which would make American press coverage much easier."
**Queen Elizabeth Hotel Stay (1969)** : John Lennon and Yoko Ono flew to Montreal on May 26, 1969, for their bed-in, staying in rooms 1738, 1740, 1742, and 1744 at the Queen Elizabeth Hotel. Room 1742 was where the anthem "Give Peace a Chance" was recorded on June 1st. "John and Yoko flew to Montreal on May 26th and stayed in the room 1738, 1740, 1742, and 1744 at the Queen Elizabeth Hotel."
**Notable Visitors During Bed-In (1969)** : During their week-long bed-in in Montreal, John Lennon and Yoko Ono received visits from several prominent figures. These included Timothy Leary, Tommy Smothers, Dick Gregory, Murray the K, Al Capp, Alan Ginsburg, and notably, then-Prime Minister Pierre Trudeau. "During the week in Montreal, the couple was visited by Timothy Leary, Tommy Smothers, Dick Gregory, Murray the Kay, Al Cap, Alan Ginsburg, and of course, Prime Minister Pierre Trudeau."
**Hotel Suite Renamed and Renovated (1991, Recently)** : In 1991, the Queen Elizabeth Hotel received permission from Yoko Ono to begin renting room 1742 in John Lennon's name. More recently, all four rooms (1738, 1740, 1742, and 1744) where the couple stayed were combined into one large suite, featuring two bedrooms, a dining room, two lounges, two bathrooms, a pantry, and virtual reality goggles to experience the view from Lennon's perspective. Today, this renovated suite is known as the "John Lennon and Yoko Ono suite." "Years later, in 1991, the Hotel received permission from Yoko Ono to begin renting room 1742 in Lenin's name."
