# genre - Christian Rock
## curiosities
**Growing Music Segment** : Christian Rock is highlighted as one of the rare segments within the music industry that is actively growing and increasing in profitability. "It's one of the few segments of the music industry that's actually growing and becoming more profitable."
**Built-in Audience** : The genre benefits from a substantial pre-existing audience, estimated to include over 180 million Americans who are potential consumers. "Christians have a built-in audience of over 180 million Americans."
**Mainstream Industry Misunderstanding** : The mainstream music industry often struggles to comprehend and manage Christian bands and their values, being more accustomed to promoting themes of rebellion, sex, and violence. "Most of the mainstream music industry doesn't know what to do with these bands and their values. It's made billions by pushing songs about rebellion and sex and violence and infidelity and immorality and even criminal behavior."
**Parallel Universe Existence** : For an extended period, Christian rock existed within its "own parallel universe," largely separate from the mainstream music scene, though an increasing number of bands are now crossing over. "Until now, Christian rock has existed in its own paradol universe."
**Early Condemnation (1950s)** : In the 1950s, upon the advent of rock and roll, religious leaders of all denominations universally denounced it as "the music of the devil," deeming it immoral and evil. Some churches even conducted public record burnings, with artists like Elvis and The Beatles being primary targets. "When rock and roll first came on the scene in the 1950s, leaders of all denominations and creeds declared it to be the music of the devil. Rock and roll was condemned from pulpits everywhere as immoral and evil."
**Oxymoronic Concept Historically** : For decades, the very idea of "Christian rock" was considered contradictory, or "oxymoronic," because "Good Christians simply did not rock," and electric guitars were not seen as appropriate for hymns. "There was no such thing as Christian rock for decades. The concept was oxymoronic. Good Christians simply did not rock."
**Toughening and Growth (1980s)** : Throughout the 1980s, Christian rock experienced growth and became "toughen up," despite a prevailing stereotype that it was "weak and watered down" and involved "well scrub preaching from behind a guitar" in the eyes of mainstream fans. "Christian rock continued to grow and toughen up throughout the 1980s but for most people Christian rock was still stereotyped as well scrub preaching from behind a guitar."
**Infrastructure Development (1990s)** : By the late 1990s, the Christian rock scene had significantly broadened, with an increase in new groups, louder guitars, and heavier beats, including the emergence of Christian speed and thrash scenes in California. A sophisticated infrastructure was built to support this music, including Christian-owned clubs, record labels, and bookstores. "By the late 1990s Christian rock had really opened up. More groups were formed. The guitar got louder and the beads got heavier."
**"Christian Underground"** : The self-sustaining ecosystem of Christian bands performing in Christian-owned clubs, signing with Christian record labels, and selling CDs through Christian bookstores was often referred to as the "Christian Underground." "Some called it the Christian Underground."
**Evolving Stereotypes (Late 1990s)** : Towards the end of the 1990s, the negative stereotypes associated with Christian rock began to diminish. Bands increasingly attracted fans based on their sound and musical quality, rather than solely their message. Their music became positive and current, less preachy, and visually and sonically resembled mainstream bands, albeit with a noticeable absence of typical rock and roll themes like sex and drugs. "By the end of the decade the various stereotypes began to fall by the wayside. Instead of weak and safe imitations of the real thing these bands began to attract fans for their sound and not their message."
**Youth Group and Church Support** : In the mid-1990s, the Christian rock market expanded rapidly, partly due to youth groups and churches organizing shows. These events were considered "the safest type of concert any parent could hope to find," helping to spread word-of-mouth for many bands. "The market for Christian rock grew quickly in the middle of the 90s. Youth groups and churches began to put on shows which is about the safest type of concert any parent could hope to find."
**Internal Schisms and Judgmentalism** : The Christian underground experienced "a series of schisms." Bands crossing into the mainstream worried about alienating their Christian fanbase by appearing too negative or straying into territory that could be interpreted as immoral, offensive, or anti-God. Some factions of Christian fans were highly judgmental, evaluating bands based on "Gods Per Minute" (GPMs)—the number of times "God" was invoked in lyrics—to determine a band's "holiness." "There were a series of schisms within the Christian underground. Some of the bands we've heard on this program are worried about alienating the Christian fans as they cross over into the mainstream."
**Fear of Mainstream Perception** : Conversely, Christian rock bands also expressed concern about being perceived as "too wholesome" by the secular world, fearing they would be dismissed as "sermonizers and proselytizers" and thus "shot down based on preconceived notions." "At the same time, these bands are worried about being too wholesome for the rest of the world. That they'll be perceived as sermonizers and proselytizers."
**Persistent "Evil Rock" Belief** : Despite the evolution and growth of Christian rock, the belief that "rock music is evil" remains prevalent among some individuals. These critics argue that Christian bands, regardless of their presentation, are "perverting Christianity," often quoting scripture to support their condemnation of groups like Creed, Jars of Clay, and MXPX. "The concept that rock music is evil is still alive and well. Some people believe that these bands, no matter what their veneer, are perverting Christianity."

# curiosity
**Religion and Music's Love-Hate Relationship** : Historically, religion has maintained a complex "love-hate thing" with music. While music has been integral to ritual and prayer for millennia across various faiths, religions have also often prescribed what types of music are deemed acceptable. "Religion has always had this love-hate thing with music. I mean, any religion I can figure out is music as part of ritual and prayer. It's been that way for millennia."
**The Tritone - "Devil's Interval"** : A few years after the Nicene Council in 323 AD, the early Christian church identified the augmented fourth, or tritone, as a "corrupting force" and an "evil" musical interval. It was perceived as "hideous" and "awful," sounding like something "Satan himself might have composed." This led to the belief that those who played or were influenced by this chord were "in league with the devil" and deserving of "immediate burning at the stake." "It was a cord. Today we'd call it the augmented fourth, a type of musical interval known as the tritone. If you've taken music theory at some point, you'll know what I'm talking about. To the ears of the church, this cord sounded evil."
**Pope Gregory's Musical Organization (Late 6th Century)** : By the late sixth century, Pope Gregory undertook the task of organizing church music into a system of eight distinct modes, all of which he declared met with "God's approval," establishing a foundational structure for sacred music. "By the late sixth century, Pope Gregory had to create the music he organized into series of eight modes, all of which he said, met with God's approval."
**Catholic Church's Role in Major/Minor System** : Approximately a thousand years after Pope Gregory's reforms, the Catholic church played an influential role in promoting the transition to the major and minor system of keys and chords, which forms the basis of much of the music we use today. "A thousand years later, the Catholic church helped encourage the transition to the major and minor system of keys and cords that we use today."
**Rock and Roll Condemnation (1950s)** : When rock and roll emerged in the 1950s, leaders from various denominations and creeds universally declared it "the music of the devil," condemning it from pulpits as immoral and evil. Some churches went as far as holding public record burnings, with Elvis and The Beatles frequently targeted. "When rock and roll first came on the scene in the 1950s, leaders of all denominations and creeds declared it to be the music of the devil. Rock and roll was condemned from pulpits everywhere as immoral and evil."
**"Gods Per Minute" (GPMs) Metric** : A satirical metric, "Gods Per Minute" (GPMs), was used by some judgmental Christian rock fans to assess a band's perceived "holiness." It referred to "the number of times the word God is invoked every minute in the lyrics," with insufficient GPMs leading to a band being considered "no more holy than motley crew." "That means the number of times the word God is invoked every minute in the lyrics. You know, GPMs, God's per minute."
**Mainstream Success Driven by Song Quality** : The primary reason for the mainstream success of bands like Creed, P.O.D., and Evanescence is likely attributed to their ability to write "great songs," rather than solely their origins within the Christian music scene. "Did the majority of people buy their records because they came from the Christian scene? Or because they wrote great songs? I suspect that that's the real reason."
