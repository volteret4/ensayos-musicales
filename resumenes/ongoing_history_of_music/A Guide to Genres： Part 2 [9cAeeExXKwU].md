# genre - Art Punk

## curiosities
**Art Punk – British Art School Tradition; Fall, Wire, XTC, Television, Patti Smith, Art Brut** : Art Punk is rooted in the British art school tradition — institutions that parked creative kids who weren't going to make it in university, focused on graphic design, visual arts, theater, and fine art. Art Punk is like Garage Rock (raw, rugged, intense) but with a more sophisticated twist: more educated cleverness, sometimes incorporating theater and performance art, less working class. Key bands include the Fall, Wire, XTC, Television, Yeah Yeah Yeahs, Suicide, and Patti Smith. Art Brut (part British, part German) took their name from a form of visual art made by outsiders like prisoners and the mentally ill. "Art Punk bands include the Fall, Wire, XTC, Television, the Ye-Yah-Yah-Suicide and Patti Smith."


# genre - Baggy / Madchester

## curiosities
**Baggy / Madchester – Born from Phil Sacks' Cheap Bell-Bottoms; Happy Mondays, Stone Roses, Inspiral Carpets** : The Baggy/Madchester sound began when a Manchester clothing stall owner named Phil Sacks received a cheap shipment of bell-bottom trousers, which local musicians bought as rebellion against tight jeans. Sacks began managing his customers, who became the Happy Mondays. By the late 1980s, a new generation of groups were mixing loose psych-inspired music with house and acid house from clubs like the Hacienda, plus ecstasy and acid. A holy trinity emerged: the Happy Mondays (the Rolling Stones), the Stone Roses (the Beatles), and the Inspiral Carpets (the Kinks or the Who). The term "Madchester" was coined by Philip Shotten, a video director for Factory Records. "The term baggy became interchangeable with Mad Chester, which is a word that can be credited to a guy named Philip Shotten."


# genre - Britpop

## curiosities
**Britpop – Direct Descendant of Madchester; British Musical Nationalism; Beatles, Stones, Kinks, Who, Glam** : Britpop was the major UK genre of the 1990s, the direct descendant of the Madchester/Baggy sound, adding British musical nationalism — embracing the influences of the Beatles, the Stones, the Kinks, the Who, Glam, and the Mod era. Key bands included Blur, Suede, Supergrass, Pulp, Menswear, and — biggest of all — Oasis. "It took what madchester was all about and added in some British musical nationalism, embracing the influences of the Beatles and the Stones and the Kinks and the Who and the Glam era and the Mod era."


# genre - C86

## curiosities
**C86 – NME Cassette, May 1986; Young Raw Indie UK Bands; "C" from Cassette Capacity Designations** : In May 1986, NME included a special cassette with one of their issues featuring young, recently signed bands from various independent British labels. Because blank cassettes had numerical designations (C60 = 60 min, C90 = 90 min), the NME called their 1986 cassette C86. All bands on the tape were young, the music was raw and sometimes primitive, and nothing came from a major label. C86 became shorthand for young, raw, primitive indie rock in the UK. "If you know your cassette culture, you'll know that blank cassettes had special numerical designations."


# genre - Dreampop

## curiosities
**Dreampop – Coined by Alex Elluley of AR Kane; Atmospheric, Textured; Lana Del Rey as Minimalist Example** : Dreampop is a very atmospheric and highly textured form of alt rock with DNA from certain corners of Goth and psychedelic music. The term was apparently coined by Alex Elluley, part of an experimental British band from the mid-1980s called AR Kane. The sound is meant to immerse the listener in mood and vibe — often highly produced with effects-laden guitars and synths, or achieved through a special kind of minimalism. Dreampop often features breathy vocals, male or female. Lana Del Rey's 2012 work is cited as minimalist Dreampop. "As far as we can tell, the term Dreampop was coined by a guy named Alex Elluley."


# genre - Chillwave

## curiosities
**Chillwave – "Summer of 2009"; Coined Ironically by Hipster Runoff Blog; Washed Out "Feel It All Around"** : Chillwave is more atmospheric than Dreampop, with lots of reverb, very mellow, using vintage synthesizers for a retro-ish sound seemingly rooted (but not quite) in the 1980s. The word appears to come from the blog Hipster Runoff, and the intent was ironic — calling something "chillwave" was not a compliment. The internet turned it into a real thing. The summer of 2009 is still referred to as the summer of chillwave. It was one of the first alt-rock sub-sub-genres to bubble up almost exclusively from the internet. Artists include Ariel Pink, Neon Indian, and Washed Out (Ernest Green, Athens, Georgia). "The intent was ironic. Calling something chill wave was not meant as a compliment, but then the internet took all as it does and turned it into a thing."


# genre - Industrial

## curiosities
**Industrial Music – Throbbing Gristle Founded It; "Industrial Music for Industrial People"; Ministry and NIN Biggest Sellers** : Industrial music's roots go back to the 1970s with Throbbing Gristle, who founded a label called Industrial Records with the motto "Industrial Music for Industrial People." The sound moved to Europe first, then was imported into the US in the early 1980s via a Chicago label named Wax Tracks, where it caught on with the corner of the alternative crowd seeking something powerful, angry, and brutal. Industrial fragmented into body music, power electronics, Japanoise (some of the most extreme music ever made), power noise, industrial metal, martial industrial, witch house, dark ambient, industrial hip-hop, and industrial techno. The two best-selling industrial bands are Ministry (which started as a synth-pop band) and Nine Inch Nails, whose Trent Reznor has done more than anyone to bring industrial music to the masses. "The two best-selling industrial bands of all time are Ministry, which started out as its fashion mode like synth band, and Nineteen Snails, headed up by Trent Resner."


# genre - Psychobilly

## curiosities
**Psychobilly – The Cramps from NYC Punk; Real Home Now Europe (Especially Post-Soviet States); Canadian Bands: The Brains, Gutter Demons, The Creepshow** : Psychobilly combines one part punk, one part rockabilly (country-fied early rock and roll from the 1950s), and one part schlocky sci-fi and horror movies. Tip further toward horror and you get Gothabilly; drop country elements and you get Horror Punk. The Cramps emerged from the New York punk stew of the 1970s as the kings of psychobilly, but psychobilly's real home has become Europe — first the UK, then eastern Europe (East Germany, Poland, Bulgaria, and former Soviet satellite states). Canadian contributions include The Brains, the Gutter Demons, and The Creepshow, the last of which mines horror movie themes. "Most of these flavors also have themes of sex and violence in common, and more than a few ditch electric-based guitarists for upright double bases."


# genre - Paisley Underground

## curiosities
**Paisley Underground – LA Early 1980s; Beatles Pop + California Psych; Named After Red Paisley Dress; Bangles Biggest Group** : The Paisley Underground came out of California in the early 1980s, taking Beatles-like pop and mixing it with 1960s California psychedelia. The name came from a comment by a member of a band called The Three O'Clock who found a red paisley dress in a thrift store. The scene featured breezy pop songs with lots of melody, and the name helped record labels discover these bands. The biggest group to emerge from the Paisley Underground was the Bangles, who before their top-40 success were a highly regarded garage pop band. "The biggest group to come out of the whole Paisley Underground was the bangles."


# genre - Twee

## curiosities
**Twee – Innocence, Sentimentality, Naivety; Belle and Sebastian "Boy with the Arab Strap" (1998) as Defining Example** : Twee is a minor subsidiary division of indie pop that also has attributes of chamber pop (strings, horn sections, piano). It projects innocence, sentimentality, vulnerability, and naivety cut with harmless playfulness — almost like the thoughts of 12-year-olds brought to life. It is post-punk but definitely not punk. Glasgow's Belle and Sebastian, led by singer-songwriter Stuart Murdoch, is considered the defining Twee band. Their 1998 track "The Boy with the Arab Strap" — despite being about a certain type of sexual device — exemplifies the sound. "Tweet is all about projecting innocence, sentimentality, vulnerability, and naïtivity, cut with some harmless playfulness."
