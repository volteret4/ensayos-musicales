# artist - David Bowie

## genres
rock

## albums
**Ziggy Stardust and The Spiders from Mars** : This album was a huge seller in England through late 1972 and 1973, complete with all the required hit singles. It marked an extraordinary 18 months in British rock history, establishing Bowie's immense popularity. "His album Ziggy Stardust and The Spiders from Mars was a huge seller, complete with all the required hit singles."

## concerts
**Hammersmith Odeon (July 3rd, 1973) – Ziggy Stardust Retirement** : This infamous London concert, captured by documentary filmmaker DA Pennebaker, was David Bowie's "quits show." It marked the last show of the tour and the final performance of the Ziggy Stardust persona, concluding an extraordinary period in British rock. "The infamous Bowie quits show at the Hammersmith Odeon in London July 3rd 1973."

## curiosities
**Ziggy Stardust Alter-Ego** : Ziggy Stardust was one of Bowie's first and most enduring alter-egos and stage characters. Described as a "bisexual alien dressed up all in glitter," this persona was the primary driver of Bowie's immense popularity in England during late 1972 and 1973, with the entire country discussing him. "Ziggy Stardust, one of Bowie's first and most enduring of all his alter-egos and stage characters, had been the ticket."
**Theatrical Retirement of Ziggy Stardust** : Despite the overwhelming success of the Ziggy Stardust character, Bowie decided the persona had run its course and sought to retire it. He chose to do so in a highly theatrical manner, bowing out at the peak of his fame at a concert in one of London's most famous rock venues. "Bowie had decided that his Ziggy character had run its course and it was time to get out."

# artist - Ramones

## genres
punk rock
US punk rock

## members
Joey Ramone
Johnny Ramone

## albums
**Debut Album (1976)** : The Ramones' debut album had been released a few months prior to their pivotal London gigs in July 1976. Its release had already clued in a select few in the UK to the nascent "punk rock" phenomenon. "Their debut album had come out a few months earlier and a select few in the UK were in the loop above this new thing that had been dubbed punk rock."

## concerts
**The Roundhouse (July 4th, 1976, London)** : This concert served as England's "first proper exposure to US style punk rock." Despite their European record company almost cancelling the entire tour, the Ramones delivered an "explosive show" to an audience of approximately 2,000, which included many future figures in the British punk scene. "But the Ramones busted out with an explosive show that left many people very excited."
**Dingwalls (July 6th, 1976, London)** : Two nights after their Roundhouse performance, the Ramones played at Dingwalls. These two London gigs are credited with teaching "just about every single person who would become a major player in the British punk rock explosion" a crucial lesson, transforming their understanding of music. "Two nights later, the Ramones played a show at a club called Dingwalls."

## curiosities
**Queens Origin and Appearance** : The Ramones were described as a "scruffy bunch of leather and denim weirdos from Queens." Their distinctive look and raw sound were highly influential, particularly during their groundbreaking 1976 gigs in London. "the Ramones, this scruffy bunch of leather and denim weirdos from Queens, were showing a bunch of brits how it should be done."
**Catalyst for British Punk Explosion** : The Ramones' two London gigs in July 1976 are considered foundational for the British punk rock explosion. Major figures like Johnny Rotten, Sid Vicious, Joe Strummer, Chrissy Hynde, The Damned, and Billy Idol were in attendance and were profoundly "transformed by what they saw and heard," inspiring them to form their own bands. "Over the course of those two nights, July 4th and July 6th 1976, just about every single person who would become a major player in the British punk rock explosion were taught a lesson by the Ramones."
**Backstage Anecdote at The Roundhouse** : Joe Strummer recounted an incident at The Roundhouse where he, John Z, and some of the Sex Pistols threw a rock at Johnny Ramone's backstage window to get in. Johnny Ramone eventually stuck his head out, and the future punk icons were pulled up through the window in a "human chain," a memorable "punk rock moment." "I think Johnny Ramones stuck his head out and went, and we met, hey, this is the clash and this is the business and we needed to get in and so they kind of formed a sort of human chain and pulled us up through this window and that was the first time we met him and it was just a really great punk rock moment."

## concerts
**Birmingham University (May 2nd, 1980) – Final Gig** : This recorded concert marked Joy Division's last performance. During the song "Decades," singer Ian Curtis fainted on stage due to an epileptic seizure, initially mistaken by the audience for an exaggerated part of his "unique herky jerky way of dancing." "The final Joy Division gig was held on May 2nd, 1980 at Birmingham University."

## curiosities
**Band's Rising Fortunes Amidst Singer's Struggles** : By spring 1980, Joy Division's career was on an upward trajectory with two critically acclaimed albums and a booked American tour. Simultaneously, singer Ian Curtis was battling severe personal issues, including drug and alcohol abuse, an affair, worsening epilepsy, and profound depression, leading him to consider quitting the band and divorcing his wife. "Meanwhile, Joy Division's fortunes as a band were looking up. The first album had been a critical success and there were indications that the just-release second album was also going to do well. A tour of America had been booked and expectations were high, very high."
**Ian Curtis's Last Stage Appearance and Suicide** : At Joy Division's final concert on May 2nd, 1980, Ian Curtis suffered an epileptic seizure on stage but returned to perform the encore. This proved to be his last public performance, as he died by suicide less than two weeks later, making the gig a tragic premonition. "This would be Ian's last appearance on stage because in less than two weeks he'd be dead. A suicide."

# artist - The Jesus and Mary Chain

## members
William Reed
Jim Reed

## genres
Indie music

## songs
**Never Understand (1985)** : This single, released in March 1985, generated considerable excitement among "the cool kid" audience. Its popularity contributed to the high attendance at their North London Polytechnic concert, which later became infamous. "The Jesus and Mary chain had a single called Never Understand which had the cool kid pretty excited."

## concerts
**North London Polytechnic College (March 15th, 1985) – The Great Riot** : This concert is known as "the great Jesus and Mary chain riot." The band, famous for their unconventional and provocative live shows, kept an oversold, impatient crowd waiting an hour. Their 20-minute set, largely comprised of "squalling feedback" and performed with their backs to the audience, combined with an earlier incident involving the opening band, ignited widespread violence, stage invasion, and gear destruction, garnering significant British music press attention. "The Jesus and Mary chain provocateurs of the great riot at North London polytechnic on March 15th 1985."

## curiosities
**Unconventional Concert Style** : The Jesus and Mary Chain were known for their radical departure from traditional concert formats. Their sets were typically brief, lasting only 10 to 20 minutes, predominantly featuring "squalling feedback," and they often performed with their backs to the audience. This confrontational approach was perceived as more of a "provocation" than a conventional musical performance. "They really didn't believe in staging traditional concerts. Sets ran anywhere from 10 to 20 minutes... They sort of played songs but spent most of the time immersed in squalting feedback and more often than not they'd play with their backs to the audience."
**The Great Jesus and Mary Chain Riot** : Their concert at North London Polytechnic on March 15th, 1985, escalated into a riot. Factors contributing to the chaos included oversold tickets, a prolonged wait, and the band's deliberately provocative, short, feedback-heavy performance. The resulting stage invasion, gear smashing, and crowd violence were extensively covered by the British music press, cementing its place in rock history. "this gig has gone down in Indiraq history as the great Jesus and Mary chain riot."

"The Stone Roses with something from their 15 song set played at Spike Island on May 27th 1990."

**Biggest UK Indie Band (Early 1990s)** : At the beginning of the 1990s, The Stone Roses were recognized as the biggest indie band in the UK. "At the beginning of the 1990s the biggest Indie band in the UK was the Stone Roses."
**Madchester Sound Pioneers** : The Stone Roses were a prominent band within the "Madchester sound," a genre that was described as "all the rage" and came to a climax with their Spike Island concert. "The Mad Chester sound featuring bands like the Roses and the Happy Mondays and the Charlotons and the Inspiral Carpets was all the rage"

## curiosities

"This was the site of Britain's first official punk rock festival."

Despite starting slowly and encountering significant mid-set technical problems (stage monitors, blinding spotlight on Thom Yorke), the band's confidence grew. Thom Yorke's decision to have the light turned on the crowd seemed to resolve the monitor issues and create a "magic" moment, solidifying the gig's legendary status. "There are people who maintained that Radiohead's Glastonbury gig on June 28, 1997 was simply the best show of all time ever."

# curiosity

**Sarajevo Cafe and World War I Trigger (April 1914)** : In April 1914, a newspaper clipping in a letter arrived at the Salat Number Runa cafe in Sarajevo, detailing Archduke Franz Ferdinand's upcoming visit on June 28th. This information prompted a terrorist group to plan his assassination, which Gavrilo Princip carried out, triggering World War I and fundamentally shaping the 20th century. "That assassination was the trigger for World War I and everything in the 20th century that followed."
**Significance of "Hinge Points"** : Academics define "hinge points" as singular historical events that profoundly alter everything that follows, often with clear understanding in hindsight. The fall of the Berlin Wall is cited as a modern example of such an event with long-lasting effects. "History is full of what academics call hinge points. These are singular events that changed everything that came after."
**Rock Concerts as World-Changing Events** : The concept of rock concerts having the power to change the world is explored, with high-profile examples like Woodstock, Altamont, and Live Aid cited for their lasting political, social, and artistic implications. The discussion then focuses on "lower profile gigs" that also had subtle but long-term musical effects. "can something like a rock concert change the world? Well, I think the answer might be yes."
