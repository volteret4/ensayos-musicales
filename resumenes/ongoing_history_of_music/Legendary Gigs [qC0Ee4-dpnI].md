# artist - David Bowie

## albums
**Ziggy Stardust and The Spiders for Mars (1972) – Seminal Album** : This album was a huge seller in England through late 1972 and into 1973, complete with major hit singles, propelled by Bowie's Ziggy Stardust alter-ego.

## venues
Hammersmith Odeon

## curiosities
**Ziggy Stardust Alter-Ego** : Ziggy Stardust was one of Bowie's first and most enduring alter-egos and stage characters, described as a bisexual alien dressed in glitter, who captivated England in late 1972 and 1973.
**Bowie Quits Show (July 3, 1973)** : Bowie announced the "last show that we'll ever do" at the Hammersmith Odeon in London, declaring the Ziggy character had run its course and he was exiting "on top" in a theatrical manner.
**Filmed by D.A. Pennebaker (July 3, 1973)** : The "Bowie quits show" at Hammersmith Odeon was captured by documentary filmmaker D.A. Pennebaker.
**False Retirement (1973)** : Despite his on-stage announcement on July 3, 1973, that it was his last show ever, Bowie returned to the stage less than two months later for a TV special and continued recording and touring thereafter.

# artist - The Ramones

## members

Johnny Ramone

## genres
US style punk rock

## venues
Roundhouse
Dingwalls

## curiosities
**Debut Album Release (Early 1976)** : Their debut album was released a few months prior to their pivotal London gigs in July 1976.
**London Gigs (July 4 & 6, 1976)** : The Ramones played at the Roundhouse on July 4th and Dingwalls on July 6th, 1976, gigs that introduced US-style punk rock to England and transformed many future major players in the British punk rock scene.
**Impact on British Punk Scene (July 1976)** : Their two London gigs in July 1976 were instrumental in exposing British musicians, including Johnny Rotten, Sid Vicious, members of The Clash, Chrissie Hynde, The Damned, and Billy Idol, to US punk rock, inspiring the British punk explosion.
**European Tour Cancellation Scare (July 1976)** : Their European record company connection had canceled almost all their tour gigs and was going to pull them from the Roundhouse show, but the band proceeded.
**Backstage Entry Anecdote (July 4, 1976)** : Joe Strummer and Simo Jones of The Clash, along with some Sex Pistols members, gained backstage entry at the Roundhouse by throwing a rock at Johnny Ramone's window and forming a human chain to be pulled inside.
**"End of the Century" Documentary** : The Ramones documentary "End of the Century" features accounts from Joey Ramone and Joe Strummer about their impactful London shows.

# artist - Joy Division

## members
Ian Curtis

## albums
**First Album (Undated) – Critical Success** : The band's first album was a critical success by spring 1980.
**Second Album (1980) – High Expectations** : Their second album was just released in spring 1980 and was also expected to do well, with high expectations for a booked tour of America.

## venues
Birmingham University

## songs
**Decades (1980 Live Version)** : Ian Curtis fainted on stage during a live performance of "Decades" at the final Joy Division gig at Birmingham University on May 2nd, 1980, due to an epileptic seizure.

## curiosities
**Band's "Death Throes" (Spring 1980)** : By spring 1980, the band was described as being in its "death throes," amidst personal struggles of singer Ian Curtis.
**Ian Curtis's Struggles (Spring 1980)** : Singer Ian Curtis was battling drugs, alcohol, an affair with a Belgian woman, severe and worsening epilepsy (with frequent seizures and ineffective medication), and deep depression, leading him to consider quitting the band, moving to the continent to open a bookshop, and divorcing his wife.
**Final Gig Performance (May 2, 1980)** : The final Joy Division concert was held on May 2nd, 1980, at Birmingham University and was recorded.
**Ian Curtis's On-Stage Seizure (May 2, 1980)** : During the final gig at Birmingham University on May 2nd, 1980, Ian Curtis fainted on stage during the song "Decades" due to an epileptic seizure; the audience initially mistook it for part of his unique "herky jerky" dancing.
**Ian Curtis's Last Appearance (May 2, 1980)** : Despite a seizure during the show, Ian Curtis recovered backstage and returned to perform an encore, which marked his last appearance on stage.
**Ian Curtis's Suicide (May 1980)** : Ian Curtis died by suicide less than two weeks after the final Joy Division concert on May 2nd, 1980.

# artist - The Jesus and Mary Chain

## members
William Reid
Jim Reid

## genres
British indie music

## venues
North London Polytechnic College

## songs
**Never Understand (1985)** : This single was released in March 1985 and generated excitement among "cool kids," leading to a highly anticipated gig.

## curiosities
**Non-Traditional Concert Style (Pre-1985)** : The band was known for not staging traditional concerts, with sets lasting only 10 to 20 minutes, featuring squalling feedback, and often playing with their backs to the audience, acting more as a "provocation."
**The Great Jesus and Mary Chain Riot (March 15, 1985)** : A concert at North London Polytechnic College on March 15, 1985, descended into a riot due to over-sold tickets, a hot and cramped venue, police departure, an opening band (Meat Whiplash) throwing a bottle into the crowd, and J&MC making the crowd wait an hour before playing a 20-minute set and leaving. The frustrated crowd then threw cans, invaded the stage, and smashed all the gear.
**British Music Press Attention (March 1985)** : The riot at North London Polytechnic College received significant attention from the British music press due to their widespread presence at the event.

# artist - The Stone Roses

## genres
Madchester Sound
Indie music

## venues
Spike Island

## albums
**The Stone Roses (1989) – Self-Titled Debut** : Released in May 1989, this album yielded five major hit singles and established the band as the biggest indie band in the UK by the early 1990s.

## curiosities
**Spike Island Outdoor Gig (May 27, 1990)** : The band played a legendary outdoor gig at Spike Island, a peninsula jutting into the Mersey River, surrounded by chemical factories and power plants, on a site that was a reclaimed chemical dump.
**"Gig of the Year" Hype (May 1990)** : The Spike Island show was immediately proclaimed the "gig of the year" upon its announcement.
**Meeting of Minds (May 27, 1990)** : Spike Island served as a significant meeting point for kids from the Madchester scene and rave culture, facilitated by the use of ecstasy, fostering a sense of shared community.
**Performance Quality (May 27, 1990)** : Despite its legendary status, the band's performance at Spike Island was generally considered average or even below average, with the band seeming nervous and tentative, and the sound system being suspect, except for the last ten minutes.
**Biggest Crowd (May 27, 1990)** : The Spike Island concert attracted between 28,000 and 30,000 people, representing the largest crowd The Stone Roses had ever managed to attract.
**British Music Press Hype (May 1990)** : The British music press heavily hyped the Spike Island event, proclaiming "the 90s begin now," contributing to its legendary status despite the performance quality.
