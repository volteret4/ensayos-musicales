## albums
**Alexis on Fire (2002) – Self-Titled Debut** : This was the band's first record, a self-tutorial release that came out on Halloween 2002. It was released on Distort Records, a label started by Greg, who was a champion of aggressive music. The band secured a small publishing deal advance from EMI Music Publishing, which allowed them to fund a van and record this album for free after hours in EMI's writing room. "Here was a sample from that first record, the self-tutorial release that came out on Halloween 2002."

**Watch Out (Year Not Specified, after 2002)** : The band's second album, which they continued to tour heavily for. While working on this record and during its subsequent touring, the band made music with the stage and crowd interaction in mind. Songs like "Accident" and "Get Fighted" were created to elicit large crowd participation with clapping and singing along. "We had a song like, we had a song like, Accident, which had a huge crowd participation. We had songs like, get fighted, crowds clapping and singing along."

**Crisis (2006)** : Released on August 22nd, 2006, this album went straight to number one on the Canadian album chart, selling over 20,000 copies in its first seven days, a success that caught the industry by surprise. The album was made with a "pop sensibility" as the band got better at writing songs that listeners could imagine singing back to them, informed by their extensive touring for the previous album. "On August 22nd 2006, Alexis on Fire released their third album and it went straight to number one on the Canadian album chart, selling more than 20,000 copies in his first seven days."

**Old Crows / Young Cardinals (Year Not Specified, after 2006)** : This was the band's fourth album, which achieved massive success and received significant radio play. The band spent considerable time touring for "Crisis" (approximately two years, 2006-2008) and Dallas Green was also heavily involved with City and Colour during this period. They took more time writing the songs for "Old Crows / Young Cardinals," recording demos in a rented backroom of an installation factory in St. Catharines, and experimenting with different singing styles. "We took a little bit more time putting the songs for Old Crow's and Young Cardinals together."

**Dogs Blood EP (Year Not Specified, after Old Crows)** : This was planned as the band's fifth release, starting as an EP. The band began writing songs for it, but the process was halted when Dallas Green made the decision to leave the band. They ultimately decided to finish the four songs they had already developed for the EP. "Well, they're, the fifth, I guess dogs blood when we started writing the songs for the dogs blood EP, which would come out after all crows. We started writing those songs and then that's kind of when I had made the decision to leave the band."

## curiosities
**Early Niagara Music Scene** : The band members met by playing in various other local bands around Niagara, southern Ontario. The scene was characterized by "incestuous sort of stuff" with musicians frequently moving between groups. Shows were often kid-promoted in 200- or 150-capacity clubs, or rented halls like Columbus Hall, Masonic temples, and Canadian Auto Workers Union Halls for a couple hundred bucks. Bands would sometimes play at places like the Kingwalk Court, a Chinese food restaurant in St. Catharines, and be paid in beer, which was beneficial given their underage status. "A lot of kid promoted shows. Like people who are like in high school, they rent out a... A pole, a shop. A pole, a shop. Nice Columbus Hall, Masonic temples, Canadian auto workers union hall."

**Wade McNeil's Promotion Skills** : Even at 15 years old, Wade McNeil was adept at negotiating and promoting shows. He was known for organizing events and was responsible for booking Alexis on Fire's first American tour by connecting with people in internet chat rooms. "Wade was one of those kids. Wade can promote a show. Wade at 15 years old is Negoshi."

**Band Name Origin** : The name "Alexis on Fire" originated from a Discovery Channel segment about a contortionist stripper named Alexis Fire, where the segment itself was titled "Alexis on fire." The band members quickly adopted it, not expecting their band to gain widespread recognition. They later received and ignored cease and desist letters regarding the name and deliberately chose to write it as one word, which often led to mispronunciations like "Alex's on fire." "And in one of the segments, they did a segment on a girl who's a stripper, who was also a contortionist, and she amongst other things, which we would come to find out. But her name was Alexis Fire and the segment was called Alexis on fire."

**Watch Out Album Artwork** : The artwork for the "Watch Out" album features two Catholic schoolgirls engaged in a knife fight. This imagery was inspired by a long-standing joke where the band would describe their sound on flyers as "two Catholic schoolgirls in a knife fight." Dallas Green photographed his then-girlfriend and her friend, who were actual Catholic high school students at the time and wore their own school uniforms for the shoot. "So when you make flyers for a show, back in the day you'd always have like the name of the band and then underneath it in parentheses or whatever, we'd have a little description of what the band was. And it was always something like, you know, crushing buffalo metal core or like, you know, whatever."

**Early Career Aspirations** : The band initially had modest aspirations, not expecting mainstream success or radio play. Their highest goal was to play to about 200 people at venues like The Cathedral in downtown Toronto, believing their type of music would lead to obscurity. "The top of the, the top of the heap would have been playing to 200 people at the cathedral downtown Toronto. Yeah. That was as big as the aspirations."

**MuchMusic Impact** : MuchMusic played a crucial role in Alexis on Fire's rise to prominence. Managers Joel and Trisha, label head Greg, and EMI's Michael, along with George Stroumboulopoulos (who wore their t-shirt and brought them on MuchMusic before they had recorded anything), championed the band. MuchMusic's ability to connect listeners across Canada, from St. John's to Yellowknife, allowed their music to resonate with a wider audience who felt a connection to their sound. "Much music back in the day is that it linked a kid in St. John's Newfoundland with a kid in Yellowknife. And you could turn it on and see an Alexis on Fire video and go, that sounds like I feel."

**Connoisseurship vs. Consumption** : The band's rise occurred in an era where music connoisseurship was more prevalent than instant consumption. Before streaming and widespread internet access, fans had to actively seek out music by attending shows, knowing people, or acquiring physical records. This created a deeper investment in the music that resonated with them. "But it's more about connoisseurship than it is consumption. Because back then before streaming before, you know, the internet, you had to seek out this stuff."

**Right Place, Right Time** : The band attributed much of their success to a combination of luck and seizing opportunities at the right moment. They acknowledge that many equally talented bands fall by the wayside or never achieve the same level of recognition, often due to not being in the "right exact spot at the right exact time." They also noted a tendency in Canadian culture to promote "palatable" music over potentially "best" cultural output. "We just happened to be the band that got that video and got, we had a bunch of open doors and yeah, in the right time."

**Touring Challenges** : The band experienced significant disparity in audience numbers during their early tours. For example, they played a successful show at the Croatian Cultural Center in Vancouver to a thousand people with Billy Talent, only to cross the border into Bellingham, Washington, and play for only the bar staff, with Dallas Green not even performing due to voice issues. Later, attempts to "hammer it" in America during the "Old Crows" era, including a "disastrous" Warped Tour and long unfulfilling American tours where only 40-60% of shows were good, became mentally exhausting. "We played for the bar staff. I don't even think I played the show. You didn't. I didn't. I took a T-shirt. Because your voice was bothering you."

**Pop Music Shift** : The rise of Alexis on Fire and similar aggressive bands coincided with a shift in pop music in the mid-2000s. Fans who had grown up with pop acts like Backstreet Boys, N'Sync, Christina Aguilera, and Britney Spears from the mid-90s were aging and seeking music that expressed anger, fears, and frustrations, creating an opening for bands like Alexis on Fire and Billy Talent. "But during that time, we were seeing the end of the spike in pop music, you know, the back street boys and instincts and Christine Aguilar is in Britney Spears and that group of people who had been into pop since the middle 90s with spice girls and whatever, they were now getting older and they were getting angry and they were looking for something that spoke to that anger and their fears and their frustrations."

**Resilience and Delivery** : The band members emphasized their resilience and commitment to "deliver" whenever given an opportunity. They never "botched anything," successfully performing at major events like the MuchMusic Video Awards, despite the pressure of a gigantic stage. "I will say this in full humble, in full humbleness, Alexis on Fire f*** delivers. When it comes down to like the moment, like if we need to do something, don't people draw a drawing that please."

**Post-Crisis Plateau** : While "Old Crows / Young Cardinals" was successful, it was perceived as a step down from the monumental success of "Crisis" and did not reach number one. This marked the first time the band saw a drop in ticket sales in some locations, leading to a feeling that they had hit a ceiling and caused doubts among the members after years of consistent progress. "And as much as it was a success, it was a step down from crisis. And I think that, in some way, it just, I think until that point, it was just step up, after step up, after step up, like we were always making a little bit of progress."

**Dallas Green's Departure** : Dallas Green decided to leave the band not for solo success, but due to creative differences, feeling he could no longer write aggressive music and did not want to force it. He attempted to continue both Alexis on Fire and City and Colour but found he couldn't write aggressive material naturally anymore. The breakup was amicable, and it was acknowledged that he "probably should have left Alexis on Fire four years earlier" as City and Colour had begun to eclipse Alexis on Fire in popularity. "I left the band solely because I had creatively I wanted to do something else."

**Post-Breakup Lives** : Following the band's breakup, members pursued different paths: Dallas Green continued with City and Colour, George Pettit became a firefighter, Wade McNeil became a radio DJ, Jordan Hastings became a fill-in drummer for Billy Talent and played in other bands, and Chris Steele was last heard to have gone for a "truck on the Himalayas." "I mean, Dallas Green is off doing a city and color thing. Vocalist George Pettit became a firefighter. Wade McNeil is doing a bunch of stuff, including working as a radio DJ. Drummer Jordan Hastings is filling it with Billy Talbot and playing in some other bands. And I'm not even sure where bass player Chris Steele is."

**The Breakup's Positive Impact** : The band members ultimately view their breakup as a positive development, allowing each individual to pursue other aspects of their lives and careers without growing to resent each other. It prevented the band from reaching a point where they were "at each other's throats" and ultimately enabled their reunion under better circumstances. "No, the dust is settled too. I mean like the breakup was the best thing to happen to all of us. It really was."

**Reunion Motivation** : The reunion was driven by the members remaining close, enjoying playing together, and sensing strong public demand. They initially played sporadic shows as a "vacation," but Dallas Green's move back to Toronto led to more regular practices and the decision to write new music, rather than just playing old hits. Despite the challenges of balancing music with family life and other aspirations, the "neural pathway" carved through years of playing live made returning feel natural and joyful. "It just became, it became no excuse for us to not play. Yeah. That's really what it was."

# concert - Warped Tour

## curiosities
**Disastrous American Tour** : Alexis on Fire's participation in the Warped Tour during the "Old Crows" era was described as a "broke event" and a "disaster," contributing to a period of unfulfilling American tours that took a toll on the band. "I remember like during that war, we're doing that full warp tour was, you got a broke event. It was a disaster doing that."

# concert - Croatian Cultural Center (Vancouver)

## curiosities
**Successful Canadian Show** : Alexis on Fire played an incredibly successful show at the Croatian Cultural Center in Vancouver with Billy Talent, drawing a thousand people. This event was considered one of the best nights of their lives and showcased the strong demand for aggressive music in Canada. "Literally play the Croatian Cultural Center in Vancouver with Billy Talent to a thousand people and it'd be one of the best nights of our life."

# curiosity

**Vinyl Distros at Shows** : In the early days of the aggressive music scene, attending shows at local halls often involved "vinyl distros." People would bring bins of their own records and set up flea market-style stalls to sell music, which was a primary way for fans to discover and acquire new and niche music. "Back in the day, when you go and see a show at a hall, too, there'd be vinyl distros. People would come with bins of their own records, and they'd set up like almost like a flea market, and they'd sell records."
