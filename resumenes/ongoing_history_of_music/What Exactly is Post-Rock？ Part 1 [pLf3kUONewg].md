# genre - Post Rock

## curiosities
**Definition and Implication of "Post"** : The term "post rock" is baffling because the prefix "post" implies that this music comes after rock, suggesting rock is finished, yet rock is still very much present. It describes music that is different enough from "normal" rock to stand apart. "The use of the prefix post implies that this music comes after rock. Rock is over done finished and this is what comes next."
**Characteristics** : Post rock is characterized by non-traditional song structures, importance of texture and atmospherics, unusual subject matter, and the experimental, unconventional use of rock instruments, often without typical riffs. This type of music would historically not have been played on the radio. "Song structure is definitely non-traditional. Texture is important. Atmospheric are paramount. And the subject matter is unusual."
**Early Usage of the Term** : The term "post rock" dates back to 1967, appearing in a Time Magazine article about The Beatles' "Sargent Pepper" album, but it was popularized later by music writer Simon Reynolds. "The term had been around since, get this, 1967 in a Time Magazine article about the Beatles greatly forward with the Sargent Pepper album."
**Simon Reynolds' Popularization** : Music writer Simon Reynolds helped popularize the term "post rock" in the 1980s and first used it in print in 1993 to describe Bark Psychosis's album "Hex." "Reynolds helped popularize it in the 1980s. The first time he used an imprint was in 1993, when he was searching for a way to describe an album entitled Hex by a group called Barke Psychosis."
**Instrumentation for Non-Rock Purposes** : Simon Reynolds defined post-rock groups as "using rock instrumentation for non-rock purposes," such as employing guitars for texture and atmospherics rather than for riffing and power chords. "Groups that, and these are Simon Reynolds words, using rock instrumentation for non-rock purposes."
**Retrospective Application of the Term** : Once "post rock" entered the music vernacular, people began to retrospectively identify earlier groups that met its criteria, including My Bloody Valentine, Cocteau Twins, early industrial music bands like Throbbing Gristle and Einstürzende Neubauten, and Talk Talk. "People began to look back to see if there were any other earlier groups who met this criterion. And yes, there were."
**UK Experimental Scene (Late 80s/Early 90s)** : The UK harbored an experimental scene in the late 1980s and early 1990s that included bands like Talk Talk, Stereolab, Lika, Seefeel, Moonshake, and Disco Inferno, characterized by jazz-style rhythms, spacious production, atmospherics, and dynamics. "Talk Talk was part of an experimental scene in the UK through the late 80s and early 1990s."
**Chicago School of Post-Rock** : This distinct school focused on instrumentation, tight performances, clean recordings, and the use of instruments such as Fender Rhodes, electro-pianos, marimbas, and vibraphones, representing music that required rock to have existed for its own development. "The focus here was on instrumentation, type performances, clean recordings, and use of things like fender roads, electro pianos, marimba's, and vibraphones."

# genre - Krautrock

## curiosities
**German Origins (1970s)** : Krautrock emerged in West Germany during the 1970s, as German musicians sought to distance themselves from their country's Nazi past by creating a distinct German rock style, different from UK and American music. "The next step on the post rock ladder is probably the music that came out of West Germany in the 1970s."
**Influences and Approaches** : This music blended psychedelic rock and electronic music, bravely exploring approaches not rooted in the traditional blues and R&B of standard rock. "They liked psychedelic rock and electronic music. And they were brave enough to try approaches that weren't necessarily rooted in the blues and R&B traditions of standard rock."
**Nomenclature: "Kajshmi music" vs. "Krautrock"** : Germans initially called it "Kajshmi music" (cosmic music), but a "cheeky Brit" coined the name "Krautrock," which ultimately stuck. "The Germans called it Kajshmi music, cosmic music. But then some cheeky Brit decided it should be called Kratrock and that name stuck."
**Varieties and "Motorik" Rhythm** : Krautrock encompassed various styles, from electronic-focused (like Kraftwerk) to noise-preferred material with a driving, hypnotic 4/4 rhythm known as "motorik," which evoked driving on the Autobahn. "There are many different varieties of Kratrock. Some went all in with electronics like craft work. There's like noise preferred material with a driving hypnotic, 4-4-rhythm known as motoric, which evoke driving in a car in the Autobahn."

# genre - Post-Punk

## curiosities
**Definition by Simon Reynolds** : Music writer Simon Reynolds defined post-punk as "music that wasn't punk, but you could tell by listening to it that punk had to have happened," signifying music that emerged in the wake of the original punk rock explosion. "The best definition of post-runk that I've ever read and I think this comes from music writer Simon Reynolds is music that wasn't punk, but you could tell by listening to it that punk had to have happened."
**Umbrella Term for Late 1970s Rock** : Post-punk served as an umbrella term for rock music of the late 1970s, characterizing a period where musicians, freed by punk's stripping down of rock, built things up again with new tools and attitudes. "Post-punk was an umbrella term for rock music of the late 1970s."
**Avant-Garde and Broadened Sounds** : This genre delved into "deeper waters" than punk, pushing beyond traditional rock norms with an avant-garde style. It incorporated synthesizers, world music, funk, jazz, and dance music, drawing ideas from non-musical arts like cinema, literature, politics, and fashion. "It fished in deeper waters than punk. It went outside the norms of traditional rock. It was more avant-garde in style and thinking."
**Key Artists and Styles** : Artists and styles considered post-punk include David Bowie's "Low" album, goth, dark wave, dream pop, jangly guitar bands like R.E.M., spiky groups like Wire, shoegaze, and Brian Eno's solo ambient experiments. "David Bowie's low album from 1977 ticked all those boxes. [...] Goth, dark wave, dream pop, the jangly guitars, bands like R.E.M., the spiky stuff of groups like Wire and Shoegay's were, and in some cases still are, artists in those buckets that we can consider to be post-punk."
**Duration of the Era** : Post-punk was prevalent from approximately 1979 through the mid-1980s, before the music scene became too splintered to be encompassed by a single umbrella term. "Post Punk was a thing from about 1979 through to the mid 1980s, before things got splintered and segmented so much that we needed a whole bunch of new categories."

# genre - No Wave

## curiosities
**Late 1970s New York Scene** : No Wave was a short-lived, very avant-garde live scene in New York in the late 1970s, characterized by its deliberate non-commercialism and intent to be difficult to listen to. "Before we go any further in this history of post-rock, we have to loop around to New York for a very short live scene called No Wave. This happened in the late 1970s."
**Characteristics: Dissonance and Noise** : The genre featured lots of dissonance, noise, and guitar chords and sounds that "just did not make sense." It occasionally incorporated elements of free jazz, funk, and disco. "Lots of dissonance, lots of noise, and a lot of guitar chords and sounds that just did not make sense. Elements of free jazz and funk and disco were sometimes incorporated."
**Rejection of Conventions** : No Wave artists rejected all rock conventions, including those expressed in punk and new wave, aiming to create an effect and elicit a response, even anger. It was nihilistic, pessimistic, and confrontational, yet wrapped in an artsy guise that included cinema, fashion, and visual art. "All No Wave artists professed to reject all conventions of rock, even as it was expressed in punk and new wave. It was nihilistic, it was pessimistic, it was confrontational, but it was also wrapped in an artsy guise that included cinema, fashion, and all manner of visual art."
