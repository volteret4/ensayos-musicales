## albums
**Billy Talent (2003) - Self-Titled Major Label Debut** : This album marked the beginning of the band's major label career in the fall of 2003. It was recorded in Vancouver with local Toronto producer Gavin Brown. The album featured songs that had been worked on for "years and years," though some, like "River Below," were written more quickly. The band's distinctive sound, particularly the singing and guitar playing, made it stand out on Canadian radio. Singles like "Try Honesty" and "River Below" were released. The album's success in Canada was boosted by high rotation on MuchMusic and an increase in the Canadian content requirement for radio to 35%. However, in the US, after "Try Honesty" did well, the label chose not to release another single, marking "the beginning of our open battle with America." "Billy Town is nothing to lose from their self-tapped major label debut album."
**Billy Talent 2 (2006) - Second Studio Album** : This album was released on June 27, 2006, three years after their debut. The band initially had a different title but ultimately settled on "Billy Talent 2" at Ian's suggestion, who thought two bars next to the name would look cool graphically. It was recorded in Vancouver at The Warehouse at Brown Island Studio with Gavin Brown producing, and Ian also became more involved in the production. The album aimed to move beyond the "screamy thing" of the first album, incorporating more melody and expanding the band's sound with songs like "Surrender" and "Pins and Needles." Despite an A&R representative from Atlantic Records stating they didn't hear a "Green Day single" on it, the album became a "fan favorite" and produced some of their biggest singles, including "Devil in a Midnight Mass," "Red Flag," and "Fallen Leaves." The album's success came after intense touring following the first album, driven by a desire to "outdo yourself" and prove themselves on the critical second record. "Devil and a midnight mass, the first single from that second album, cleverly entitled, Billy Talent 2."
**Billy Talent 3 (2009) - Third Studio Album** : Released in 2009, this album featured "Rusted from the Rain" as one of its singles, which was the band's first charting number one. The album was produced by Brendan O'Brien, recorded in Atlanta and Toronto, and took 40 days for recording and mixing in Atlanta. The label (Warner US) initially sought an "A-level" producer like Butch Vig or Brendan O'Brien for the album. The band felt it was a "rock record" compared to their first two "punk" records. Despite the band's confidence, the album was leaked three months before its release and Warner US decided not to release it two months before, forcing them to quickly sign with Roadrunner Records. This last-minute change and the leak "sabotaged" the record's release, though it still performed well in Canada and Germany. It's described as a "polarizing record" for fans, often considered a "grower." "Billy Talent was arrested for the rain, one of the singles from their 2009 album, Billy Talent 3."

## curiosities
**Early Years and Major Label Signing (2003)** : The band toiled in obscurity for "eight, nine years" before their major label career began in the fall of 2003. Their break came when producer Gavin Brown and Mike McCarty saw them, leading to demo recordings and a publishing deal. Warner Canada then became interested due to their "first right of refusal" on the demos. The band experienced a "bidding war" involving Atlantic, Elektra, and Warner Brothers (US) over a month in August, with them being flown to New York for fancy dinners and performances. They were a "working class" band, all around 26 years old at the time, which made the experience surreal. "we went from toiling an obscurity for eight, nine years to then all of a sudden, literally within two weeks being flown back and forth to New York for a whole month, the whole month of August."
**Signing with Atlantic Records** : The band ultimately signed an 80/20 split deal with Atlantic Records for the world and America, while Warner Canada retained rights for Canada. This decision was influenced by the legendary status of Atlantic, known for artists like Led Zeppelin. They even met Ahmet Ertegun, one of Atlantic's co-founders, in his office on the day of signing, where they saw Jimmy Page's bow from "The Song Remains the Same" on the wall. "And so we decided to go with Atlantic records, which was the originally in our guys that we had seen that came up."
**Vocal Style Development** : Ben, the vocalist, initially spent the first five years of the band trying to emulate other singers like Eddie Vedder, Scott Weiland, and Zack de la Rocha. He eventually realized he couldn't sound like them and developed his own distinctive style. This unique vocal approach, combined with Ian's guitar playing, helped the band stand out from other Canadian bands of the era like Moist, Treble Charger, and The Tea Party. "I, the first five years of being in a band, you know, I was trying to sound like other people, you know, I was trying to sound like Eddie Vetter. I was trying to sound like Scott Watt. I'm trying to sound like Zack Delroch. You know, like, I'm trying to do that. And then I realized I can't do that, you know, so I just kind of ended up being me."
**Unique Guitar Playing Style** : Ian, the band's guitarist, developed a unique, self-taught playing style that combines rhythm and lead parts due to being the sole guitarist. He achieves this by playing in drop D tuning, which allows him to play bar chords with one finger while doing leads with the others, describing it as "almost like piano playing." He was influenced by clean players like Andy Summers and Mark Knopfler, as well as Tom Morello and Kim Thayil, and consciously avoided traditional playing. His style is considered "the most unique in the world." "I ended up trying to do both when I became the sole guitar player in this band. And by doing that, I found like dropping to D, I could play, I could play bar chords with one finger and then do a bunch of leads with the other."
**Challenges with US Labels** : The band faced persistent difficulties with US labels and management. After the initial success of "Try Honesty," Atlantic Records decided not to release another single from their debut album, citing commitments to "bigger artists" and the approaching Christmas season. In 2004, Warner America, facing financial trouble due to fear of Napster and declining CD sales, dumped around 200 artists. The band was nearly dropped, but their second record's sales (around 60,000 units, exceeding the 50,000 threshold for dropping) saved them, though they "wanted to get dumped" due to lack of support. "And in the States, we released Tri-Honesty and it did quite well and then the label had a choice to release another single and they didn't. And that was kind of the beginning of our open battle with America."
**Label Staff Turnover Impact** : A significant challenge for the band in the US was the frequent turnover of label staff, where their "champions" (A&R reps who believed in them) were repeatedly fired. This left them working with new personnel who had no vested interest in the band, leading to a lack of sustained support and preventing them from reaching the "next level." This occurred "four times in the States." "all the people that we had met and all the people that were our quote unquote champions to like help us, they all got fired."
**Success in Europe (Germany and UK)** : Despite US struggles, Billy Talent achieved significant success in Europe, particularly Germany and the UK. This was attributed to extensive touring in small clubs on their first album, opening for bands like The Beatsteaks, and strong label support that ensured radio and MTV play. They prioritized investing their time and energy in Europe, playing festivals there due to a strong "festival culture," rather than "half sold rooms" in the States. "we put in the time there. We played every small club in Germany on the first album and we got the right tours opening up for the one of the best punk bands there called the Beat Stakes. And it just took off organically."
**Production Involvement of Ian** : During the recording of "Billy Talent 2" at The Warehouse at Brown Island Studio in Vancouver, Ian, the main songwriter, became more involved in the production process, recognizing his ability to contribute to that aspect of the music. "it's also where Ian became more involved in the production as well, because he's always been the main songwriter in the band. It's when Ian kind of first went, oh, I can kind of, I get this and I want to kind of dip this feet in the production waters."
**Creative Drive for Second Album** : The band approached their second album with intense ambition, viewing it as their "big shot" where a "crappy second album" could end their career. This drive led them to make the songs "the best they can possibly be" and expand their sound, despite constantly being on the road for a year and a half to two years. "it's the wanting to outdo yourself and you know that your second album is that that's your big shot."
**Songwriting Process** : Ben and Ian collaborate on lyrics, which typically begin after the music is completed. For their first record, many songs were years in the making, while others, like "River Below," were written more quickly. Ian often uses "little recorders" and acoustic guitar to work on tunes while on the road. "Ian and I have always done it together you know so we would always wait till and still to this day when the music is done that's when the lyrics kind of would start."
**"Billy Talent 3" Label Rejection and Leak** : The release of "Billy Talent 3" was marred by significant issues. The album was leaked online three months before its scheduled release. Two months prior, Warner US, who the band had signed with after leaving Atlantic, decided they no longer wanted to release it, with Tom Wally pulling the plug. This left them without a US label just before a worldwide release. They ultimately signed with Roadrunner Records last minute, but the leak and delayed US release "sabotaged the record," leading labels to "pull back on the whole release." "it got leaked and then two months before it was supposed to come out Warner US decided that they didn't want to release it."
**Aaron's Battle with MS** : The band has collectively supported Aaron, the drummer, through his battle with Multiple Sclerosis (MS) "from day one." "we'll talk about Aaron's battle with MS something that has been a group effort from day one."
**Tom Wally's Unusual Meeting** : When Warner Brothers President Tom Wally wanted to meet Billy Talent, he refused to leave his New Jersey beach house. The band was flown to New Jersey, played a "s***ly little session" in a rented airplane hanger with unfamiliar gear, while Wally sat looking at the floor, preoccupied with his house. After the performance, he still wanted to sign them but only allowed them to eat on his patio during a family birthday, providing fried chicken and beer. "Tom Wally had, he wanted to see us. He was the president of Warner Brothers. And he didn't want to leave his beach house in New Jersey to come to the city. So they took us to New Jersey, rented an airplane hanger. We played the s***ly little session in front of him."

# label - Warner US

## curiosities
**Mass Artist Purge (2004)** : Around 2004, Warner US faced financial difficulties, driven by fears of Napster, piracy, and declining CD sales. They conducted a "big cull of artists," dumping approximately 200 artists in one week. The company had a policy to drop any artist who sold less than 50,000 units in America. Billy Talent, having sold around 60,000 units of their second record, narrowly avoided being cut, though they initially "wanted to get dumped" due to lack of label support. "I remember 2004, ish, Warner had a big call of artists. They dumped a lot of people because they were so afraid of Napster."
**Rejection of Billy Talent 3 Release** : After Billy Talent left Atlantic Records to sign with Warner US for their third album, "Billy Talent 3," the label ultimately decided not to release the record two months before its scheduled worldwide release. This decision was made by President Tom Wally, who "pulled the plug" after the album had been leaked. This left them without a US label just before a worldwide release, forcing them to find another label (Roadrunner Records) last minute. "two months before it was supposed to come out Warner US decided that they didn't want to release it and we had it was scheduled all around the world to be released except now we didn't have a label in the States to release it."

# label - Roadrunner Records

## curiosities
**Last-Minute Release of Billy Talent 3** : Following Warner US's decision to drop "Billy Talent 3" after it was leaked, Roadrunner Records stepped in to release the album. Ron Berman, a long-time fan, facilitated this last-minute deal. However, the quick turnaround meant they didn't have sufficient time to properly set up the release, and it came out "months after the release date," contributing to the record being "sabotaged." "so we ended up going to now roadrunner records because Ron Berman was a fan for a long time and they put out the record and it was like a last minute thing so they didn't have a lot of time to set it up right."

# curiosity

**Napster's Impact and CD Sales Peak (Early 2000s)** : Billy Talent's major label career began in late 2003, "just after Napster explodes" and "just as CD sales peak." This timing meant there was still significant money in the music industry, and labels were "willing to invest in bands, new bands, and take a chance." However, by 2004, fears of Napster, piracy, and declining CD sales led to financial troubles for labels like Warner America, resulting in mass artist purges. "Just after Napster explodes. And just as CD sales peak. Yeah. So there was still money in the industry."
**Canadian Content Requirement Increase** : Around the time of Billy Talent's initial success, the Canadian content requirement for radio stations increased to 35%. This policy change created "more demand for Canadian material on the radio" and is cited by the band as a factor that "benefited" them, helping to "sustain a career now." "the Canadian content requirement for radio went up to 35%."
**Evolution of Music Industry (2006)** : By 2006, when "Billy Talent 3" was being conceived, the music industry was "evolving very rapidly," with sales "really start[ing] to go down." Concerns about piracy persisted, and the era of streaming, iPods, and iPhones had not yet arrived, creating an insecure environment for musicians and songwriters. "We're seeing sales really start to go down music industry is panicking because revenues are falling people are still talking about piracy and we haven't got to the era of streaming yet."
**Musician Insecurity** : Despite their success, members of Billy Talent express a persistent "underlining insecurity" as musicians and songwriters. They believe that while they put their "best foot forward," there is "never a confidence like it's going to work," highlighting the inherent uncertainty of a career in music. "as a musician I think there's always a underlining insecurity yeah and all that kind of I don't think you're ever feel secure."
