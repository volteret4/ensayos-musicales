# genre - Rock

## curiosities
**Declining Popularity Declarations (1957-1997)** : Rock music has been declared "dead" multiple times throughout its history, including in 1957 when Elvis Presley joined the army, in 1970 with The Beatles' breakup, in 1977 due to the rise of disco and ironically the birth of punk, in the early 1980s with the advent of synthesizer bands, in 1989 when it was claimed to have run its course, and in 1997 with the popularity of boy bands, girl singers, and hip hop. In each instance, rock eventually experienced a rebirth. "Rock had been declared dead in 1957 when Elvis went into the army in 1970, when the Beatles broke up."
**12-Year Rock and Pop Cycle Theory** : Since the 1950s, a theory suggests a predictable 12-year cycle for the rise and fall of rock and pop popularity, where rock ascends for approximately six years, then declines for six years as pop rises, driven by demographic and cultural forces. This cycle dictates that rock reaches a peak, new ideas become exhausted, and the generation pushing rock ages out, leading to a decline before pop resurgence. "Since the 1950s, we've been able to track the rise and fall in the popularity of rock versus pop."
**Struggle in the 2010s** : Rock music struggled throughout much of the 2010s not due to a lack of quality, but because it had difficulty attracting the same cultural attention it once held, facing fierce competition from pop, hip hop, and EDM. This competition, combined with changes in music consumption, contributed to the breakdown of the traditional 12-year cycle. "I don't think I'm saying anything controversial when I say that rock struggled through much of the 2010s."
**Slower Streaming Adoption** : Rock fans, along with country fans, were slow to adopt streaming in a significant way during the 2010s, preferring to stick with traditional physical formats like CDs and vinyl. This slower adoption meant less engagement with the new technology that was increasingly driving the music industry. "Rock fans as well. We're slow to adopt streaming in a big way, sticking with CDs and vials."
**Limited Marketing and Algorithm Suppression** : In the streaming era, as record labels increasingly invested marketing and promotional dollars into pop and hip hop due to their higher streaming numbers, rock music received far less attention. This, combined with algorithms that tamped down rock songs because they weren't streamed as much, contributed to the genre's struggle. "Meanwhile, rock and country got far less marketing and promotional attention than they used to contributing to their struggle."
**Challenge for New Bands in Streaming Era** : Unless rock bands had an established history predating streaming services, it became a significant challenge for new rock acts to break through in the 2010s. The shift from traditional gatekeepers to individual listener curation and algorithm-driven discovery made it harder for rock to gain traction. "Rock wasn't dead. It's just that unless you had a history that predated streaming, it was a challenge for new rock bands to break through."
**Inability to Rebound Post-2000** : The recorded music industry has largely failed to create new superstar rock acts since the turn of the century, contrasting with previous decades when numerous rock bands could fill arenas and stadiums. This decline is attributed to the breakdown of old industry models, which prioritized physical music sales and allowed for longer artist development. "The recorded music industry has done a terrible job of creating new superstar acts since the beginning of the century."
**Impact of Guitar Sales Decline** : A significant drop in electric guitar sales during the 2010s, reported by the Washington Post in 2017 to be down by a third to about a million per year in the US, raised questions about whether fewer young people were interested in making rock music. Major manufacturers like Gibson and Fender faced debt, and Guitar Center filed for bankruptcy in 2020. "Did rock struggle for attention in the 2010s because fewer people are interested in making it?"
**Slight Uptick in Guitar-Based Rock** : Towards the end of the 2010s, there was some positive news for rock music, with electric guitar sales picking up and a slight increase observed in guitar-based rock, suggesting a potential future rebound for the genre. "Towards the end of the decade, sales of electric guitars picked up. And there seemed to be a slight uptick in guitar based rock."

**Rise and Dominance in Early 1990s** : Triggered by a terrible recession and the First Gulf War in 1990, and the coming of age of Generation X, grunge and alternative music culture rapidly rose to dominance in 1991, clearing out the old guard of pop and hair metal. This music resonated with Gen X's feelings and anger, enjoying widespread popularity until about mid-1996. "In about nine months, the old guard and the pop cohort were cleared out by the rise of grunge and alternative music culture."
**Decline in Mid-1990s** : Grunge's dominance began to decline around mid-1996 as Generation X aged and moved on, the artists defining the era lost momentum or disappeared, and an improving economy made the "dour sounds" of grunge less resonant. This decline paved the way for the resurgence of happy pop music. "Gen X grew up and moved on, the artists that defined the early nineties either disappeared or lost their momentum, the economy improved so the dour sounds of grunge no longer resonated as much."

# label - Spotify

## curiosities
**Founding and Global Rollout** : Spotify was founded in 2006 and began its online rollout country by country in 2008. It launched in the US in July 2011 and in Canada in 2014, becoming a major player in the global streaming music market. "Spotify was founded in 2006 and began going online country by country in 2008."
**Dominance and Skip Data** : Spotify became a dominant force in music streaming, providing access to over 110 million songs by the mid-2010s. In 2016, data revealed that 24% of its listeners skip a song in the first 5 seconds if they don't like it, 29% skip within 10 seconds, and 34% are gone by 30 seconds, indicating shortened attention spans. "In 2016, it was revealed that 24% of Spotify listeners will skip a song in the first 5 seconds if they don't recognize it or decide they don't like it."
**Untouched Music Library** : It is estimated that 20% of the songs in Spotify's library, which contains over 110 million tracks, have never been streamed by anyone even once. This equates to more than 20 million unheard songs, raising questions about lost music and missed opportunities for discovery. "On Spotify alone, it's estimated that 20% of the songs in the library have not been streamed by anyone even once."

# curiosity

**Decades as Momentum-Driven Periods** : Decades don't simply end when the calendar flips to a new year; they often carry momentum or a "hangover" that extends their influence for one to three years. For instance, the 1950s arguably continued until 1963, and the 1960s' influence stretched into 1972-ish. "Decades have momentum, sometimes a hangover, that carries things forward for a year or two or even three afterwards."
**Historical Decades Transition Markers** : The shift between decades is often marked by significant historical events rather than just calendar changes. The assassination of JFK in 1963 is noted as kicking off the 1960s; the death of disco, a recession, and Ronald Reagan's election ended the 1970s; the First Gulf War, another recession, and the rise of alternative music ended the 1980s by 1991; and 9/11, the dot-com bubble burst, and the iPod's introduction concluded the 1990s in 2001. "It took the assassination of JFK to really kick off the new decade."
**2010s Defining Characteristics** : The 2010s decade began on time and was characterized by prosperous economic growth, the rise of authoritarianism, and a massive surge in technology adoption, including smartphones, the explosion of social media, and cord-cutting, significantly impacting how music was consumed. "For the next 10 years, we saw everything from prosperous economic growth to the rise of authoritarianism and technology."
**Republican Presidency and Rock Music Resurgence Theory** : Historically, the election of a Republican president, especially a polarizing one, has often correlated with young people responding through loud, angry rock music, as seen during the Nixon (1968), Reagan (1981), George H.W. Bush (1991), and George W. Bush (2001) administrations. However, this trend did not significantly materialize following Donald Trump's election in 2016, with only isolated examples. "usually whenever there's a Republican in the White House and the more polarizing and controversial, the better. Young people have traditionally responded to that, to that political and social circumstance with a lot more loud angry rock."
**Streaming as Cheaper and More Convenient Consumption** : Streaming music services revolutionized music consumption by offering a cheaper alternative to buying downloads and a far more convenient method than purchasing physical media. By the mid-2010s, it became evident that streaming represented the future of music consumption. "Streaming was cheaper than buying downloads and far more convenient than traveling through time and space to buy your music on a piece of plastic."
**Erosion of Cultural Gatekeepers by Streaming** : Prior to streaming, entities like record labels, radio stations, and print media acted as cultural gatekeepers, filtering music for quality and commercial viability, thus fostering a general consensus about popular music. Streaming, by offering access to millions of songs on demand, transformed individuals into their own gatekeepers, leading to a fragmented listening landscape where a broad consensus is rare beyond a few superstars. "For decades and decades and decades, music had to go through a series of cultural gatekeepers, record labels, record stores, radio stations, print media."
**Impact of Too Much Music on Attention Spans** : The overwhelming availability of over 110 million songs on streaming services contributes to shortened attention spans and increased anxiety among listeners, leading to quick "skipping" if a song isn't immediately engaging. This constant search for "something better out there" often results in music becoming "organized noise" lacking context and deeper meaning. "There's just too much music."
**Global Music Expansion via Streaming** : Streaming services have "flattened out the world" of music, making global genres like K-pop massively popular, leading to major growth in Bollywood songs from India, and an explosion of interesting Latin music worldwide. This vast global library far surpasses the inventory of traditional record stores. "The world has flattened out. K-pop is massive. One genre that saw major growth in the decade was Bollywood songs from India. Plus, there's been an explosion in interesting Latin music all over the world."
**Lack of Context and Emotional Attachment in Streaming** : The digital nature of streaming, without physical album covers or accompanying liner notes, leads to listeners knowing less about songs, albums, artists, producers, and the stories behind the music, dulling its meaning. Furthermore, the essentially free access to music diminishes the emotional and financial investment listeners once felt. "It's delivered without context, and lack of context can dull the meaning of music."
**Record Industry's Emphasis on Songs vs. Albums** : The recorded music industry, struggling with the breakdown of old physical sales models, shifted focus to individual songs and their placement on playlists rather than nurturing artists over multiple albums. This "WAM-BAM Thank You MAM" speed makes it harder for new acts to develop, affecting all genres. "There's been a bigger emphasis on individual songs and where they fit on a Spotify playlist than there is for an album that might be promoted over two or three or even four years."
**Decline of Independent Live Music Venues** : Many independent live music venues closed during the 2010s due to high rents, gentrification, or an inability to generate sufficient revenue. This reduction in performance spaces meant fewer opportunities for new bands to develop their sound and gain live experience. "Many, many independent live music venues closed during the 2010s because owners were unable to make enough money to keep the lights on, squeezed up by higher rents or became victims of gentrification."
