**James T. Russell's Digital Recording Invention (1966)** : In 1966, American inventor James T. Russell devised a method to record digital information, specifically data reduced to patterns of zeros and ones, onto a transparent foil sheet. This foil was illuminated from behind by a halogen lamp, converting the digital patterns into light. "The technology that went into CDs goes as far back as 1966 when an American inventor named James T. Russell figured out a way to record digital information, that is data that had been reduced to patterns of zeros and ones, onto a sheet of transparent foil."

**CD Playback Direction and Speed Changes** : Unlike vinyl records, which play from the outside in, compact discs are recorded in a spiral and play from the inside out. Digital speeds change during playback; Track 1 plays at 480 RPM, and as the laser moves towards the outer edge, the rotational speed gradually slows to approximately 210 RPM. "Information is recorded in a spiral, just like a vinyl record, except that CDs play from the inside out. Digital speeds changed as the disk played. Track 1 plays at 480 RPM. As the laser moves towards the outer edge, rotational speeds slows to about 210 RPM."
**CD Audio Specifications: Sampling Frequency and Resolution** : The compact disc standard set the sampling frequency at 44.1 kHz, meaning the recording technology captured real-world analog audio 44,100 times per second. The resolution was established at 16 bits, which aligned with the data storage capabilities of computers during that era. "It was decided that the sampling frequency would be 44.1 kHz. This means the recording technology grabbed real-world audio and a log audio. 44,100 times a second. Resolution was set at 16 bits, which is related to how the computers of the day store data."
**Standard CD Diameter and Capacity (12 cm)** : The ultimate standard diameter for a compact disc was set at 12 centimeters, which was 5 centimeters larger than Philips' original prototype. This 10% increase in diameter translated to a 20% increase in storage capacity, allowing a disk to hold between 74 and 75 minutes of music. "The diameter was ultimately set at an even 12 cm. Five cm bigger than the original Philips prototype. That 10% increase in diameter represented a 20% increase in capacity."
**Myth of Beethoven's Ninth Symphony and CD Capacity** : A popular, but false, story claims that the 12 cm CD diameter was chosen because a Sony executive insisted the new format must be able to store his favorite performance of Beethoven's Ninth Symphony. The actual reason for the size increase was more practical and related to basic geometry and physical design considerations. "Do not believe any stories that this increase in size was because an executive at Sony demanded that the new technology have enough capacity to store his favorite performance of Beethoven's ninth symphony. **First Public CD Demonstration (1981 on "Tomorrow's World")** : The compact disc technology was publicly demonstrated for the first time on the BBC show "Tomorrow's World" in 1981. "The first public demonstration of the technology, apparently was on a BBC show called Tomorrow's World in 1981. It's a long playing record from here to here, 60 minutes of digital music."
**Music Industry Recession (Early 1980s)** : The early 1980s saw the recording industry experiencing a severe recession, with revenues declining for the first time since the Great Depression. This economic downturn made record companies hesitant to invest in new and expensive technologies like the compact disc. "The recording industry was still suffering from a terrible recession in the early 1980s. For the first time ever, first time since the Great Depression, revenues in the industry had gone down. **Record Store Resistance to CDs** : Record stores were initially resistant to the introduction of CDs due to significant logistical challenges. They already had extensive shelving for vinyl LPs, 7-inch singles, and were still accommodating 8-tracks, while also grappling with the explosive popularity of pre-recorded cassettes driven by devices like the Sony Walkman. Retailers were reluctant to install more shelving for another format, especially one requiring customers to purchase expensive new playback machines, and expressed concerns about the CD's small size making it easily shoplifted. "Record stores also weren't too happy about CDs. They already had miles and miles and miles of shelving for vinyl LPs. Plus, they had to devote space to 7-inch singles. People were still coming in to buy music on 8-track, and they were grappling with the explosive rise in the popularity of pre-recorded cassettes, thanks to this new thing called the Sony Walkman and similar portable music devices."
**CD Shoplifting Problem and Longbox Solution** : The compact size of CDs made them highly susceptible to shoplifting, a concern for retailers. This problem was addressed through the introduction of the "Longbox," a paper or plastic package measuring approximately 6 inches wide and 12 inches long. Its larger dimensions prevented easy concealment and allowed it to be displayed in existing 12-inch vinyl record bins. "Another reason retailers were skeptical had to do with the CD size. They were so small that they were very easily shoplifted. Those problems were solved by a special sort of packaging called the Longbox."
**Slow Initial CD Sales and High Prices (1983)** : Initially, CD sales were sluggish and largely confined to audiophiles. In 1983, only about 350,000 CD players were sold. CDs themselves were expensive, typically costing $16 to $25 for a domestic release, which was almost double the price of a vinyl record or cassette. A $20 CD in the early 1980s would be equivalent to approximately $45 today. "At first, sales were slow and confined to audio files. Only about 350,000 CD players were sold in 1983. And CDs themselves were expensive, usually running anywhere from $16 to $25 for a domestic release, making them almost double the price of a vinyl record or cassette."
**Decline of Vinyl Quality (Mid-1970s to Early 1980s)** : The oil crisis of the mid-1970s significantly impacted vinyl record quality. Petrochemical products, including vinyl, became more expensive, leading manufacturers to produce thinner records and recycle more vinyl. This recycling introduced impurities that resulted in new pressings sounding worse, with increased clicks, pops, low-frequency rumble, and greater susceptibility to scratches and damage, ultimately pushing consumers towards CDs. "Petrochemical products like vinyl became more expensive. To save money, records became thinner. Lots of vinyl was recycled, which introduced impurities that caused new pressings to sound worse, with clicks and pops and low-frequency rumble, and they were easily scratched and damaged."
**Expansion of CD Manufacturing Plants (1984-1988)** : Following the initial two plants, Sony opened the first US-based CD manufacturing plant in Terre Haute, Indiana, in October 1984. **Cumulative CD Sales Reaching Half a Billion (1989)** : By 1989, the cumulative sales of compact discs had reached an impressive half a billion units worldwide, demonstrating the rapid growth and adoption of the new format. "And by 1989, half a billion disks had been sold."
**Decline of 8-Track Format (1997)** : The 8-track format was the first of the older music formats to be phased out, with the last major label release on 8-track being Fleetwood Mac's "Greatest Hits" in 1997. "When it came to old formats, eight tracks were the first to go. The last major label release on eight track was Fleetwood Max Greatest Hits in 1997."
**CDs Outselling Vinyl (1988)** : Compact discs surpassed vinyl records in sales by 1988, indicating a significant shift in consumer preference and market dominance for the new digital format. "Then the bottom dropped out of the vinyl market with CDs outselling the old format by 1988."
**CDs Overtaking Cassettes (1991)** : In 1991, the compact disc overtook the cassette as the biggest-selling format for pre-recorded music. The cassette had previously held this position for a period, but the CD's rapid growth led to its new market leadership. "In 1991, the cassette, which for a while, had been the biggest selling format for pre-recorded music, was overtaken by the CD."
**Compact Disc as Fastest Growing Entertainment Product** : The compact disc was, at the time, recognized as the fastest growing entertainment product in history. Sales figures illustrate this rapid expansion: 61 million CDs sold in 1985, 140 million in 1986, 1 billion by 1992, and 2 billion by 1993. "The CompactDisc was quite simply the fastest growing entertainment product in the history of the universe. 61 million CDs were sold in 1985, 140 million in 1986, and then the numbers just kept getting bigger and bigger and bigger. 1 billion in 1992, 2 billion in 1993."
**High CD Profit Margins (1990s)** : Despite new manufacturing plants coming online annually and increasing sales volumes, CD prices remained stubbornly high in the 1990s, often $14, $15, $16, or more. While the manufacturing cost per CD dropped to about $1 for labels, they maintained high retail prices, leading to substantial profit margins. "Labels had brought the cost of manufacturing a CD down to about $1. Yet the retail price remained $14, $15, $16 or more. So talk about a fat profit margin."
**Declining CD Player Costs (Late 1990s)** : While CD prices remained high, the cost of CD players steadily decreased throughout the 1990s. By the end of the decade, a wide range of players was available, from ultra high-end audiophile models costing thousands of dollars to affordable options priced around $100 or even less. "And while the price of music in this format stayed stubbornly high, the cost of buying a CD player kept coming down and down and down. By the end of the 90s, there was a CD player for everyone from ultra high-end audio file models costing thousands of dollars to some that sold for around $100, or even less at the local drugstore."
**Mercedes-Benz First In-Dash CD Player (1984)** : In 1984, Mercedes-Benz became the pioneering auto manufacturer to offer an in-dash compact disc player in its vehicles. Other car manufacturers subsequently followed suit, and by the end of the 1990s, CD players were practically standard in cars, often including multi-disc changers. "In 1984, Mercedes-Benz became the first auto manufacturer to offer an in-dash CD player."
**Lexus SC430 Last Model with Factory Cassette Player (2010)** : The 2010 Lexus SC430 was the last car model available to offer a factory-installed cassette player, marking the end of an era for integrated cassette playback in new vehicles. "The last model to offer a factory cassette player was the 2010 Lexus SC430."
**Flaws of CD Jewel Cases** : Compact disc jewel cases had several recurring design flaws, including hinges that frequently cracked and broke, preventing the case from staying closed, and the central hub that secured the disc often breaking, which put the CD at risk of scratches or falling out. "The hinges on the jewel case always cracked and broke, making it impossible for the case to stay closed. Or that little thing in the center of the CD tray broke, putting your CD at risk of getting scratched or falling out of the case."
**CD Sales Peak (2000)** : The peak of compact disc sales occurred in 2000, with 943 million units sold in the United States alone and a global total of 2.44 billion units. "The high watermark of the CD came in 2000 when 943 million were sold just in the United States. Worldwide, 2.44 billion."
**CD Sales Death Spiral (2000-2018)** : Following its peak in 2000, CD sales entered a "death spiral." Global sales plummeted from $25 billion in 2000 to approximately $5 billion in 2018, representing a 90% drop from its all-time highs. "Global sales went from 25 billion in 2000 to around 5 billion in 2018. That drop was 90% from its all-time highs, 90%."
**Music Industry's Self-Inflicted Wounds in CD Decline** : Several factors contributed to the "great CD die-off" after 2000, some of which were self-inflicted by the recorded music industry. These included the failure to lower CD prices as promised and the cynical decision to eliminate CD singles, forcing consumers to buy entire albums for just one desired song. "After 17 years of consecutive growth, CD sales peaked in 2000. But then a number of things conspired to create the great CD die-off. And some of the reasons were self-inflicted."
**Elimination of CD Singles (Late 1990s)** : The recorded music industry made a strategic decision to eliminate the CD single, which was the digital equivalent of the traditional 7-inch single. This move was perceived as cynical, aimed at compelling consumers to purchase entire albums even if they only desired one specific song. "The recorded music industry made the cynical decision to eliminate the CD single, which was the digital equivalent of the old 7-inch single. The attitude seemed to be, what, you want just that one song on the album? Too bad, no more singles for you. You buy the whole album."
**Types of CD Singles (5-inch and 3-inch)** : Two main types of CD singles existed: the standard 5-inch CD, which typically contained 1 to 5 songs, often including bonus tracks unavailable elsewhere; and the 3-inch mini CD (or CD3), introduced in 1987, which could hold up to 20 minutes of music. The smaller CD3s were largely gone by 1985 (likely meant later than introduced) and were problematic with self-loading CD players without an adapter ring, sometimes becoming unplayable or trapped. "There were two types of CD singles. The first one was the same size as a regular CD, but had between 1 and 4, maybe 5 songs on it. The actual single plus a couple of bonus tracks that often weren't available anywhere else. The second type of CD single was just 3 inches in diameter, which was introduced in 1987."
**Early Internet Music Trading (Early 1990s)** : People began trading music online in the early 1990s, though the process was initially slow and buggy. The music industry was aware of this activity but did not consider file trading a serious threat, focusing instead on the rise of home CD burners. "People had been trading music online since the early 1990s, but it was a slow buggy process. The music industry knew all about it, but they really didn't take file trading seriously."
**Music Industry's Misjudgment of CD Burners vs. Digital Files** : The music industry perceived home CD burners as the primary threat, fearing widespread burning and selling of bootleg album copies. They failed to grasp that consumers would be more interested in individual songs and the convenience of digital files, and struggled to comprehend how digital files could transcend physical formats. "Their big concern was the rise of CD burners in home computers. That was the big threat. This illegal piece of plastic that you could hold in your hands. The industry was positive that this would lead to people burning bootleg copies of legitimately released albums and selling those. They honestly didn't get that people would be more interested in individual songs and the convenience of digital files."
**Rise of MP3 and University Student Adoption (Late 1990s)** : Towards the end of the 1990s, the MP3 format began gaining significant traction, particularly among university students who leveraged their schools' high-speed internet connections for file sharing. "Then, towards the end of the 1990s, a new thing called the MP3 was seen to be picking up steam. University students glomed onto the runaway using their school's high speed internet connections."
**Napster Launch (June 1, 1999)** : On June 1, 1999, Napster, an easy-to-use software program, appeared, simplifying MP3 file trading. This ushered in a new era of music consumption, where users could obtain individual songs for free from their home computers, challenging the traditional model of buying full-priced CDs. "And then, on June 1st, 1999, a new easy-to-use software program called Napster appeared. This made trading MP3 files a snap and it ushered in a whole new way of doing things."
**Emergence of Kazaa and BitTorrent (2001)** : Following legal challenges against Napster that ultimately led to its demise, two other file-sharing programs, Kazaa and BitTorrent, were introduced in 2001, further fueling the growth of online music piracy. "But then in 2001, just as Napster was being sued out of existence, two other programs were introduced into the wild. There was Kazah and BitTorrent."
**Music Industry's Failed Response to Piracy (2003)** : By 2003, CD sales had fallen 20% from their peak. The music industry's attempts to regain control through litigation and by launching its own legal digital services proved ineffective, as it was "way too late" to reverse the trend. "By 2003, CD sales had dropped 20% from their all-time highs. The industry was not panic. So they tried to litigate themselves back into control, but it was way too late. They tried to innovate themselves back in control with legal digital services, but nothing they did worked."
**iTunes Software Introduction (January 2001)** : Apple introduced iTunes in January 2001. Initially, it served as music management software for Mac users to organize CDs ripped to their hard drives or other collected digital music. It also allowed users to burn compilation CDs, analogous to old cassette mixtapes. "In January 2001, iTunes was introduced. This was originally Music Management Software for people with Macs, who had ripped CDs to their hard drive or had collected music from somewhere online. You could also use iTunes to burn compilation CDs, the digital equivalent of the old cassette mixtape."
**iPod Introduction (October 2001)** : The iPod was introduced in October 2001 and, despite not being the first digital music player, was significantly superior to existing alternatives. Its arrival quickly made portable cassette and CD players seem outdated. "In October 2001, the iPod was introduced. This wasn't the first digital music player, but it was way, way better than anything else out there. An overnight portable cassette and CD players seemed to be about as cool as wind up gramophones."
**Steve Jobs' Digital Music Proposition to Labels** : Steve Jobs approached record labels, arguing that piracy was devastating their business and that they needed to transition to digital. He presented iTunes as his solution, and the labels, having struggled to find their own effective digital strategy, had little choice but to agree. "Steve Jobs went to all the record labels and basically said, look, piracy is killing you. You have to go digital. You can't do it right, so here is my solution. And the labels really had no choice but to agree."
**iTunes Music Store Launch (April 28, 2003)** : The iTunes Music Store officially opened for business on April 28, 2003. It quickly dominated the digital music market, eventually capturing up to 70% of it, a development that record labels disliked due to their loss of control over distribution and pricing. "And on April 28, 2003, the iTunes Music Store opened for business. iTunes would eventually have up to 70% of the marketplace for digital music, something that the labels absolutely hated because they'd lost control."
**Failed High Fidelity Digital Disk Attempts (HD CD & Super Audio CD)** : The industry attempted to update CD technology, which was based on 1970s/80s standards, with two incompatible high-fidelity digital disk formats: the HD CD and the Super Audio CD. "There were two attempts at high fidelity digital disks. Remember that CD technology is still rooted in standards that were created in the late 1970s and early 80s. So let's update that. There was the HD CD and the Super Audio CD. Two standards that were, yep, not compatible."
**DataPlay Disk (Tiny Format)** : Another attempted format was DataPlay, a disk so minuscule that it was considered a choking hazard, indicating an ill-conceived design. "There was data play, a disk so tiny that was actually a choking hazard."
**CD Malware Controversy and Lawsuits** : Record labels engaged in misguided attempts to deter copying by embedding malware, specifically a rootkit, onto some CDs. If a user tried to rip such a CD, the rootkit would install itself on their machine, preventing copying. This unethical practice led to a series of lawsuits filed by music consumers. "And then there were misguided attempts of putting malware on CDs. If you tried to riff this CD from a major record label, something called a root kit would install itself on your machine, preventing you from copying anything. And that ended really, really badly with a bunch of lawsuits being filed by and on behalf of music consumers."
**CDI Discs and Anti-Ripping Features** : CDI (Compact Disc Interactive) discs, like The Cranberries' "Doors and Windows," combined music with multimedia content. While playable in both computers and CD players, some had features that prevented ripping to a computer, and Track 1 on the Crenbury's disc could produce ear-piercing static if played in a regular CD player without caution. "This is a CDI disc, meaning that it contains all sorts of multimedia content as well as music. This one could be played in a computer and a CD player. But you need to turn down your CD player for Track 1 because it just might read it as ear piercing static. Want to rip it to your computer? Uh-uh, wouldn't let you."
**YouTube's Impact on CD Sales (2005)** : The launch of YouTube in 2005 led to another significant drop in CD sales, as consumers increasingly turned to the platform to find and consume music online. "Then, in 2005, YouTube went online and there was another big drop as people started looking for music that way."
**Decline of Physical Music Retail and CD-ROM Drives** : As CD sales continued to fall, record store chains began going out of business, and big box stores largely stopped stocking CDs. Concurrently, new computers ceased coming with built-in CD-ROM drives, and the availability of standalone CD players drastically decreased, even disappearing from new cars. "Record chains began to go out of business because fewer people were buying discs. Big box store stopped carrying CDs. Computers stopped coming with built-in CD ROM drives and the number of CD players you could actually go out and buy had dropped drastically."
**Streaming's Dominance and CD Market Collapse (2008-2018)** : By the time Spotify launched in 2008, the CD market had already shrunk by at least two-thirds, and by 2018, it had plummeted by 90% from its peak. This "total and complete collapse" of the CD market was driven by the overwhelming convenience of digital streaming, which became the primary way people accessed music. "By the time Spotify came along in 2008, the CD market had dropped by at least two thirds. By 2018, that drop was 90% from its all-time highs, 90%. And it's not done falling yet. It has been a total and complete collapse as we all have collectively abandoned the CD for the unbelievable convenience of digital."
**Streaming Revenue Surpasses Physical Media (April 2018)** : The music industry, no longer concerned about declining CD sales, began to make substantial revenue from streaming. In April 2018, revenue generated from streaming services first surpassed that from physical media sales. "Revenue from streaming first eclipse that of physical media in April of 2018. The industry still isn't making as much as it did in the glory days of the CD but the bleeding has stopped. They're actually making cash."
**CD's Lack of Emotional Cachet Compared to Vinyl** : Compact discs, unlike vinyl records, are noted for not possessing the same "emotional cache." There is a current trend indicating that vinyl may once again outsell CDs for the first time since the mid-1980s. "CDs don't have the same emotional cache as vinyl. We're heading toward a time when vinyl may just outsell CDs again for the first time since the middle 1980s."
**Global Niche CD Markets (Japan, Germany, South Africa, South Korea)** : While global CD sales have plummeted, specific countries such as Japan, Germany, South Africa, and South Korea still maintain relatively strong CD markets. However, even these markets are beginning to experience erosion due to the increasing adoption of streaming music services. "Japan, Germany, South Africa and South Korea have very solid CD markets. But even those countries are starting to see erosion in CD sales, largely because of the adoption of streaming music services."
**Future of CD as a Niche Format for Curation** : The compact disc is not expected to completely disappear but will evolve into a niche format, similar to vinyl, catering to individuals who prioritize the physical possession and careful curation of music. This caters to "personal music curation" as distinct from the convenience of broad "music consumption" via streaming. "Will the CD disappear? No. But it will become a niche format for people who want to possess music, just like those who like vinyl. I think we're looking at a split in the way we consume music."
**Higher Fidelity of Physical Formats (CDs and Vinyl)** : For the time being, physical formats like compact discs and vinyl records continue to offer higher fidelity and better sonic quality compared to standard digital files. However, this is gradually changing with the advancement of high-resolution audio formats. "For the time being, these formats, compact discs and vinyl, deliver higher fidelity and better sonic quality than we can get from digital files. Now, that's slowly changing with formats like high resolution audio, which are fantastic, by the way."
**Ritual of Physical Music Consumption** : There is a unique ritualistic aspect to consuming physical music, such as taking a CD or vinyl record from a shelf, holding it, and engaging in the process of playing it. This experience is distinct from simply accessing a digital file. "But a digital file is still not the same as taking something down from the shelf, holding it in your hands and going through the ritual of playing it."
