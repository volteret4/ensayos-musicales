# label - Fraunhofer Society
## curiosities
**MP3 Algorithm Development** : The Fraunhofer Society, a publicly funded research organization in Germany, was home to Carl Heinz Brandenburg and his team of approximately a dozen individuals who developed the software algorithm that became known as MP3. This algorithm was eventually chosen as the international standard for compressed audio on CD-ROMs, though initially met with disinterest. "He worked at the Fraunhofer Society, a publicly owned research organization based in Germany, and he too had a vision."
**Initial Patent Attempt (Denied)** : Professor Dieter Sitzer, who mentored Carl Heinz Brandenburg, filed a patent application for transmitting speech and music over copper wires. However, his application was denied, with the examiner deeming the concept "impossible." The Fraunhofer group later succeeded in acquiring a patent for their audio compression technology, building upon the foundational work that Sitzer had pioneered. "He filed a patent on it, but was denied because the person examining the patent application told him, this is impossible and we don't issue patents for impossible things."
**High Quality Low Bitrate Audio Coding Research (1987)** : In 1987, the Fraunhofer group intensified its research into what they termed "high quality low bitrate audio coding." Their objective was to compress a full-sized audio file from a compact disk (WAV file) to one-twelfth its original size while preserving as much audible quality as possible, with the initial focus on creating files for personal computer storage rather than streaming. "Research intensified in 1987 into something that became known as high quality low bitrate audio coding."
**Psychoacoustics Experiments** : The Fraunhofer team conducted an extensive series of experiments grounded in the principles of psychoacoustics. They meticulously tested ten-second audio clips from CDs, compressing them and then using highly expensive headphones to identify any audible errors or glitches, such as "Muddy" or "Hiss," and worked to mitigate artifacts like double speak and pre-echo. "Starting with the theories of psychoacoustics, the Fraunhofer team embarked on a long series of experiments."
**Failed Standards Competitions** : The Fraunhofer team's Layer 3 algorithm consistently lost against Musicam's Layer 2 in multiple standards competitions. Despite Layer 3 offering superior sound quality, it was perceived as too complex, leading to Layer 2 being adopted for various applications including digital FM radio, interactive CD-ROMs, video compact disc, digital audio tape, HDTV, and audio on DVDs. Fraunhofer recorded a 0 for 7 record in these competitions. "So Fraunhofer was 0 for 6 in standards competitions."
**NHL Broadcast Breakthrough (1995)** : The Fraunhofer team achieved its first major breakthrough when Steve Church of Tell Us licensed Zeffer boxes, which ran on MPEG Layer 3 technology, to the National Hockey League. Beginning on January 20, 1995, these boxes provided significantly superior and more cost-effective audio quality for play-by-play broadcasts than previous methods, leading to their installation in every NHL arena. "It was the break the front-hover team was looking for."
**Free MP3 Encoder Distribution (1994-1995)** : Between 1994 and 1995, the Fraunhofer team distributed thousands of 3.5-inch floppy disks containing a free Layer 3 encoder for personal computers at various trade shows. This strategic move was instrumental in enabling everyday consumers to create their own Layer 3 files, especially as Intel's powerful Pentium chips became widely available, providing the necessary processing power. "Thousands of those 3.5-inch floppy disks were distributed by front-hofer at trade shows through 1994-1995."
**First Experimental MP3 (Funky.mp3) (1994)** : In 1994, to avoid copyright issues during their experiments, the Fraunhofer team utilized an original piece of music created by one of their own research team members, Yergen Hare, titled "funky.mp3." This file was distributed with a strict 30-day license, permitting only compression and decompression for experimental purposes. "So at first, and again, this is 1994, they used a piece of music written by one of their own research team members named Yergen Hare. And he called it funky.mp3."
**Public Website and Shareware (1995)** : In late 1995, the Fraunhofer Institute launched a public website specifically for their new MP3 technology, offering free downloads of the MP3 encoder as shareware for DOS, Windows, and Linux operating systems. This initiative perfectly coincided with the public's increasing engagement with the Internet, allowing early adopters to easily download and share music. "In late 1995, the Fraudhofer Institute created a public website dedicated to their new MP3 technology."
**Cessation of MP3 Support (2017)** : In early 2017, the Fraunhofer Institute formally announced its decision to cease support and licensing for MP3 technology, acknowledging its status as an outdated format. The organization stated it would no longer pursue improvements for the MP3 and highlighted the availability of superior audio codecs, including AAC and MP8, signaling its intent to allow better technologies to supersede it. "In early 2017, it announced it was stopping support and licensing of MP3 technology."

"Do you realize what you've done? Yes, Brandenburg. You've killed the music industry."

"News groups started by early adopters of the Internet were discussing the potential of layer 3 software when it comes to encoding music. **Psychoacoustics Principle** : The principle of psychoacoustics, based on research by Eberhard Zwicker and further developed by Carl Heinz Brandenburg, explains how the human ear and brain perceive sound, noting that louder sounds can mask weaker ones. This understanding was crucial for creating compression algorithms that could remove imperceptible audio data, thereby reducing file size without a noticeable degradation in perceived quality. "This involves learning how the ear and brain process sound. We don't perceive 100% of the sound we hear because sounds overlap and stronger sounds very weaker ones."
**MPEG Layer 1, Layer 2, Layer 3 (1991)** : Following a protracted competition, the Moving Pictures Expert Group (MPEG) finalized several audio encoding iterations in April 1991: Layer 1, Layer 2 (representing the Musicam process), and Layer 3 (the more complex Fraunhofer algorithm). This established a format war within the nascent digital audio market, with Layer 2 initially garnering greater industry support. "Many iterations were singled out. Moving pictures expert group Lair 1, there was Lair 2 and Lair 3."
