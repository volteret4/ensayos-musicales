# artist - Pearl Jam

## curiosities
**Ticketmaster Legal Challenge (1994)** : Pearl Jam launched a legal action against Ticketmaster in 1994, aiming to tour with tickets priced below $20 and a service charge no more than $1.80. Ticketmaster refused, stating they needed to charge at least $2 to cover costs. Despite a significant fight, testimony before the U.S. Congress, and an attempt to circumvent Ticketmaster's network, the case accusing Ticketmaster of being anti-competitive collapsed in 1995. The band now uses Ticketmaster for their ticketing. "Despite a good fight, testimony before the U.S. Congress and an attempt to circumvent Ticketmaster's ticket selling network, it didn't work."

# artist - U2

## curiosities
**Vertigo Tour Ticket Scalping (2005)** : During U2's 2005 Vertigo tour, a company called Wiseguy exploited the ticketing system. They spent $200,000 to purchase 5,000 memberships in U2's fan club, which offered early access to discounted tickets. Instead of reaching actual fans, Wiseguy scalped these tickets, leading to them hauling in $2.5 million from the tour. This practice was a major reason for the outcry from fans who were unable to secure coveted spots inside the inner stage ring. "Wiseguy started using this kid's software and was soon gobbling up tickets to big shows all over the continent."

# curiosity

**Early Concert Ticket Sales (Pre-Computerization)** : In the past, buying concert tickets involved visiting physical box offices, often located within theaters, arenas, or department stores, like the "Attractions Box Office" (ABO) or "Celebrity Box Office" (CBO). Buyers would pay cash for pre-printed paper tickets. For high-demand shows, long lines, sometimes extending for days, were common. There was no guarantee of tickets, and individuals might hire others to stand in line to purchase the maximum allowed. The distribution of pre-printed tickets across multiple box offices meant choosing a location was a gamble for securing good seats. "For the longest time the rule for buying concert tickets was, stampede and those who survived got tickets."

**Ticketron's Introduction of Electronic Box Offices (1969)** : Ticket Reservation Systems Inc., later known as Ticketron, was a pivotal player in modernizing concert ticket sales. Established in 1969, it pioneered the use of electronic box offices. This innovation eliminated the need for pre-printed tickets that were previously randomly distributed to various physical box office locations, marking a significant step towards computerized ticketing. "They were the first to use electronic box offices that eliminated the need for pre-printed tickets that were then randomly distributed to physical box offices."

**Ticketmaster's Dominance and Monopoly (Established 1976)** : Ticketmaster, established in Arizona in 1976, has grown through expansion and acquisition to become the largest primary seller of tickets globally, including acquiring Ticket Fraud in 1991. Its near-monopoly on concert sales has led to numerous legal challenges, most notably by Pearl Jam in 1994, although that case collapsed. Now owned by Live Nation, the world's largest concert promoter, Ticketmaster's control over the market has intensified. "Through expansion and acquisition, Ticketmaster has become the largest primary seller of tickets in the known universe."

**Ticket Pricing Mechanics and Responsibility** : Concert ticket pricing is a complex process involving economic projections, estimations, and considerable guesswork. An artist's manager negotiates with a promoter on a show-by-show basis, considering market demand, available inventory, and the desired earnings from the tour. "Hard costs" for a stage show typically account for 30-50% of the ticket price, with the remaining revenue split approximately 85% to the act and 15% to the promoter. Artists may receive an advance or a guaranteed amount per show or tour to cover startup costs. Ticketmaster, as the agent for venues, adds a "service fee" to cover its costs and a small profit. Additionally, a "venue fee" for electricity, security, and staffing, and various taxes (including special amusement taxes) are added. The actual base ticket price is set by the promoter and artist, not Ticketmaster, yet Ticketmaster often receives all the blame for high prices, acting as a "deflector of criticism" to insulate artists from fan wrath. "Ticketmaster has almost nothing to do with the price of a concert ticket other than their service fee. Yet it actually gets all the blame."

**Concert Ticket Allocation and Holdbacks** : Not all tickets for a concert are released for sale simultaneously; promoters may release blocks over time, adjusting prices based on demand. A significant portion of tickets, known as "holdbacks," are reserved for specific purposes before general public sale. These include allocations for fan club presales, credit card offers (like American Express's Front of the Line), radio station and media contests, record label requests, the band's and venue's guest lists, and sponsors. The number of these holdback tickets is dynamic. Consequently, for a venue with a stated capacity of 15,000 seats, perhaps only half or less (e.g., 5,000 to 7,000) may actually be available to the general public when tickets officially go on sale. "So in the end, how many of those 15,000 tickets are actually available for sale when the clock hits 10 o'clock on Friday morning and the general public can get in on the action? Maybe half, but probably less."

**Scalper Bots and Early Exploitation (Early 2000s)** : Scalper bots are software programs designed to purchase thousands of concert tickets exponentially faster than humans, exploiting computerized ticketing systems' ability to process many transactions per minute. Ken Losen, founder of Wiseguy, identified this opportunity in the early 2000s, hiring a 17-year-old Bulgarian programmer to develop such software. These early bots bypassed security measures like CAPTCHA challenges by discovering that Ticketmaster used a limited number of character combinations (around 30,000). Wiseguy's team copied these as JPEG files into a database, enabling their bots to break through codes using brute force. Ticketmaster did not update these CAPTCHA codes for years, allowing bots to acquire millions of tickets. "Louson and others discovered the ticket master only used about 30,000 combinations."

**Ken Losen and Wiseguy's Bot Operations** : Ken Losen, recognizing the shift to online ticket sales in the early 2000s, founded Wiseguy to acquire hard-to-get tickets. He hired a 17-year-old Bulgarian programmer to develop bot software that could outpace human buyers. Wiseguy successfully used this software to purchase large quantities of tickets across North America, selling them to clients or brokers at significantly inflated prices. For instance, in 2005, Wiseguy generated $2.5 million from a U2 tour by buying 5,000 fan club memberships for $200,000 and scalping the discounted tickets. The company was eventually busted by the FBI, but the bot software concept persisted and became cheaper and more widespread. "He found a 17-year-old programmer in Bulgaria who knew that computers could be much faster than humans when it came to following the online purchase prompts."

**The Modern Secondary Ticket Market** : The secondary ticket market is where tickets are resold after the primary sale, often after venues declare a "sell-out." Bot operators frequently act as wholesalers, selling large blocks of tickets to brokers, who then resell them to the public at marked-up prices. Companies like StubHub (owned by eBay, not Ticketmaster) facilitate this market, promoting themselves as online ticket exchanges. This market can serve as an "insurance policy" for buyers, allowing them to recoup costs or even profit if their plans change, especially for shows sold far in advance. There are theories that promoters might use this market to sell their own holdback tickets for extra profit. Speculative buying, where sellers offer tickets before they are officially on sale, is also common, though risky for buyers. Prices are dynamic, fluctuating based on demand and the artist's popularity, with about 50% of buying activity occurring in the 48 hours before a show. "The secondary market is where you go to buy a concert ticket after the venue says that it's sold out."

**StubHub's Role in the Secondary Market** : StubHub is a prominent online ticket exchange company in the secondary market, allowing individuals to buy and sell tickets they no longer need or want. Contrary to a common urban legend, StubHub is not owned by Ticketmaster; it is a subsidiary of eBay. The platform provides a mechanism for fans to potentially recover their investment or profit from tickets, acting as a form of insurance, particularly for shows booked far in advance. Unlike illicit underground markets, StubHub offers some consumer protections for both buyers and sellers. "StubHub is owned by, wait for it, eBay."

**Speculative Ticket Buying** : Speculative buying occurs in the secondary market when an entity advertises and offers tickets for sale even before those tickets have officially gone on sale to the public. These sellers are essentially promising that they will acquire the tickets. While buyers might ultimately receive the advertised tickets, they could also receive different tickets, or be completely scammed and lose their money. This practice is described as "fishing for suckers." "The entity placing that ad is merely offering a promise that such a ticket will be available."

**Ticket Guardian and Concert Ticket Insurance** : Ticket Guardian is a company that has proposed an innovative solution to the complexities of the secondary ticket market by offering concert ticket insurance. For a small fee, such as three dollars per ticket, this insurance remains active until the show's start time. If a ticket holder cannot use their ticket for any reason, they can enter its number into the Ticket Guardian app. The ticket is then invalidated, the buyer receives a full refund credited to their credit card, and the promoter regains the ticket, which can then be resold at its current market rate. "Their idea is to offer concert ticket insurance."

**Challenges with Anti-Bot Laws** : Governments globally, including in Canada, the US, Australia, Ireland, and the UK, are attempting to combat scalper bots with anti-bot laws. However, the effectiveness of these laws is significantly hindered by the borderless nature of the internet. Bot operators can simply relocate their operations to jurisdictions with less stringent regulations, enabling them to continue attacking ticketing systems in other countries from the outside. This makes comprehensive enforcement extremely difficult. "But because the internet knows no borders, operators can just move to places where they can attack those jurisdictions from the outside."

**Artist Reluctance to Price Tickets at True Market Value** : Artists and promoters are often hesitant to price concert tickets at their true market value, even when the secondary market demonstrates a much higher willingness to pay. This reluctance stems from a desire not to appear "greedy" or to be seen as "gouging fans." They aim to cultivate a lifelong fan base, recognizing that charging new, young fans hundreds of dollars for a ticket early in their career could deter them. Instead, they often allow Ticketmaster and scalpers to absorb the criticism for high prices. "Artists and promoters are loathed to come across as greedy to their fans."
