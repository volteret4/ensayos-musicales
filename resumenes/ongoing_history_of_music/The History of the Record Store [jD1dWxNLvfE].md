## curiosities
**Early Career and Rendezvous Records Partnership (Late 1940s - 1951)** : Alan Freed, a radio announcer at WAKR in Akron, Ohio, developed a friendship with Leo Mintz, who operated Rendezvous Records, a prominent record store in Cleveland. Mintz observed that a significant number of white teenagers were purchasing R&B records sourced from the US South and suggested Freed feature these on his radio show. When Freed moved to Cleveland for a position at WJW, Mintz bought airtime on the station for Freed's show, which premiered on July 11, 1951, playing records supplied by Rendezvous. "Fried hosted, playing R&B records supplied by rendezvous records."
**The Moondog and Pioneering Rock Concerts** : Alan Freed achieved considerable fame, adopting the moniker "the Moondog" because his radio program aired after midnight. He subsequently began promoting live shows for the artists featured on these R&B records, naming these events "Moondog Balls." These concerts are historically recognized as the first-ever rock concerts. "And looking back, these were the first ever rock concerts."
**Coining the Term "Rock and Roll"** : Freed famously started referring to the music he played on his radio show as "Rock and Roll." This change in terminology occurred after a New York street musician, who claimed prior usage of the name "Moondog," threatened legal action. One theory suggests Freed derived the term from the 1951 recording "60 Minute Man" by Billy Ward and the Dominoes, which included the lyric "I rock them, roll them all night long," known as African American slang for sexual intercourse, a meaning Freed was likely aware of. "Fried also famously eventually started calling the music he played on his radio show, Rock and Roll."

# label - HMV (His Master's Voice)

## curiosities
**Origins and First Store (1921)** : HMV, an acronym for "His Master's Voice," originated in the UK as a record label owned by the Gramophone Company. The very first HMV store opened in 1921 on Oxford Street in London, capitalizing on the surging demand for records that followed the gramophone's popularity during and after World War I. "And the first store opened on Oxford Street in London in 1921."
**Merger Leading to EMI (1931)** : A decade after its initial store opening, the Gramophone Company, HMV's parent entity, merged with another recorded music manufacturer. This union resulted in the formation of Electrical and Musical Industries, which subsequently became known as EMI. "Ten years later, there was a merger between the gramophone company and another recorded music manufacturer, resulting in electrical and musical industries, henceforth known as EMI."
**Global Expansion and Flagship Store (1990s)** : By the 1990s, HMV had evolved into a global franchise, operating 320 stores at its peak. Its flagship location at 383 Oxford Street in London was recognized as the world's largest record store, known for hosting regular, decent-sized in-store performances. The chain extended its reach across continents, with stores in Australia, Ireland, Singapore, Hong Kong, India, and Italy. "At its peak in the 1990s, HMV owned 320 stores."
**US Market Challenges (Closed by 2004)** : HMV maintained a limited presence in the United States, primarily establishing stores in Manhattan. However, these American ventures did not perform well and all US locations were closed by the end of 2004. "The American store is closed by the end of 2004."
**Canadian Operations and Customer Loyalty (Pre-2010)** : HMV Canada was notable for its customer-centric policies, including a no-hassle returns policy and a popular loyalty program. This program allowed customers to receive an 11th CD for free after purchasing ten, which was particularly appealing given that CDs retailed for $17-20 each at the time. "First, they had a no-hassle returns policy. And secondly, they had a loyalty program by 10 CDs, Get the 11th Free, which was really good when you consider that CDs at the time were running 17, 18, 19, 20 dollars each."
**Financial Decline and Bankruptcies (2010s)** : The entire HMV group began facing severe financial problems by 2010. While vinyl sales saw a rise, they were insufficient to offset the steep decline in CD sales. Concurrently, operational costs such as rents increased while customer foot traffic dwindled. A strategic pivot towards selling more music merchandise also failed to reverse the trend. Following a particularly difficult Christmas season compounded by adverse weather, HMV initiated store closures in January 2011. Exactly two years later, in January 2013, HMV UK declared bankruptcy, and subsequent revival attempts by Hillcoe also failed, leading to more closures and another bankruptcy. "By 2010, the entire HMV group was running into financial problems."
**Canadian Subsidiary Closure (2017)** : HMV Canada, operating as a subsidiary of the parent company, also encountered significant financial difficulties, ultimately leading to bankruptcy. All HMV stores in Canada were permanently closed by April 30, 2017. "Bankruptcy Beckons and all the stores were closed by April 30, 2017."
**Acquisition by Sunrise Records and Revitalization (2017 onwards)** : Following HMV Canada's closure, Canadian chain Sunrise Records acquired the leases for 70 HMV locations across Canada. Subsequently, Sunrise also purchased Hillcoe's interest in HMV UK and Ireland, thus keeping the HMV brand alive. While some stores were closed, others reopened, including the new HMV Vault flagship store in Birmingham in 2019. "However, Canada's Sunrise Records, a chain formerly with just a handful of locations, moved in and snapped at the leases for 70 HMV locations across Canada."

**Peak Sales and Decline (Early 21st Century)** : During its most successful period, Tower Records achieved impressive sales, independently selling a billion dollars worth of CDs. # curiosity

**Early On-Demand Music Acquisition** : For over a century, the primary method for individuals to listen to music on demand was through ownership, typically by purchasing physical media like a "piece of plastic" for a set price. This transaction represented both an emotional and financial investment, encouraging listeners to extract maximum enjoyment from their purchase. "for over a hundred years, the only way you could hear music on demand was to own it."
**The Record Store Experience (Pre-Internet Era)** : In the era before widespread internet access, acquiring music involved a significant experience: leaving home, traveling to a record store, and physically browsing shelves. This process offered opportunities for discovery, interaction with new music, special ordering specific titles, and social engagement with friends or store clerks who often provided recommendations. "In order to acquire this music, you had to leave your home, find your way to a record store, and search through all the shelves hoping to find something."
**Decline of Traditional Record Stores** : While record stores continue to exist, their numbers have significantly dwindled compared to their "glory days" from the 1960s to the late 1990s. Many legendary stores and chains have permanently disappeared, reflecting a shift in how music is acquired and consumed. "Record stores are still with us, but there were fewer and fewer of them."
**Early Recorded Music Sales Venues (Late 1880s)** : When pre-recorded music, in the form of Edison cylinders and Berliner discs, was first introduced in the late 1880s, it was primarily sold in furniture stores. These establishments also sold phonographs and gramophones, which were initially regarded as pieces of fine furniture, making their sale alongside records a logical pairing. "When pre-recorded music was introduced in the late 1880s, these Edison cylinders and Berliner discs were sold at furniture stores, the same places that sold phonographs and gramophones, which made sense, given that these first units were looked upon as fine furniture."
**Extensive Early Record Dealer Network (1906)** : By 1906, the United States boasted an extensive network of 25,000 record dealers, most of which were furniture stores. This widespread distribution system was established and supported by the major record companies of the era, Victor and Edison. "In 1906, there were 25,000 record dealers just in the United States."
**Early Demonstration Recordings (1906)** : In 1906, demonstration recordings, such as "I am the Edison hologram," were utilized in stores by salespeople to introduce customers to the novel concept of recorded audio. These recordings aimed to showcase the entertainment capabilities of phonographs and records, highlighting their ability to provide melody, tales, laughter, and music for dancing or evoking memories. "Salespeople would play this in the store as a way of introducing customers to this new idea of recorded audio."
**Demonstration of Double-Sided Records (1910)** : A demonstration recording from 1910 promoted the innovative "Columbia Double Disc Records," which featured different selections on each side. These records were explicitly not for sale but served to illustrate the "double value" proposition, offering two recordings for only a few cents more than a single-sided record, playable on any disc machine, including Columbia Graphophones or Victor Talking Machines. "The purpose of this record is to demonstrate the Columbia Double Disc Records."
**Diverse Early Retailers for Records** : Beyond dedicated record stores and furniture shops, records were sold in a variety of other retail outlets, including department stores such as Sears, Kresge's, Kmart, and Woolworths, as well as independent stores and even drug stores. These establishments typically featured small displays with four to six bins, holding around thirty records each, with no more than two copies per title. "Department stores had them. Sears, Cresgies, Cammart, Woolworths."
**Early Radio Stations and Record Store Collaboration** : In the early days of radio, experimental stations like XWA in Montreal, one of the world's first, and KDKA in Pittsburgh, relied on local record stores for their programming. Engineers would obtain phonograph records from shops like Layton Brothers record store, which provided them for free in exchange for on-air announcements acknowledging their source. "When engineers got bored with speaking, they grabbed some phonograph records from the Layton Brothers record store down the street."
**Record Store Growth and Great Depression Impact** : New record stores emerged throughout the 1920s, indicating a period of growth. However, this expansion was severely interrupted by the Great Depression, which caused an astonishing 95% drop in record sales between 1930 and 1932. The market eventually recovered, albeit slowly. "There was a big setback with a great depression, which saw sales dropped by an astounding 95% between 1930 and 1932."
**Oldest Existing US and Canadian Record Stores** : The title of the oldest existing record store in the US is disputed, with claims for Ryan Hart's Music and Video, a piano store that began selling records in 1901, and Georgia Sound Shop, which opened in 1932. In Canada, Wilson and Lee in Oshawa, founded in 1922, was a significant contender but unfortunately closed permanently in the fall of 2019. "The oldest existing record store in the US is either Ryan Hart's Music and Video, a piano store that started selling records in 1901, or it could be Georgia Sound Shop, which opened for business in 1932."
**Core Business of Record Stores** : A record store's fundamental business model involves securing physical space, amassing inventory, employing staff, and undertaking all necessary activities to sell recorded music on behalf of record labels. This venture inherently carries substantial risk, as predicting sales success beyond major superstar releases is difficult. "It acquires space, a masses inventory, hires people, and does whatever it has to do to sell recorded music on behalf of record labels."
**Format Transitions and Retailer Disagreement (Early 1980s)** : In the early 1980s, a period of disagreement arose between record stores and labels as stores were asked to adopt the new compact disc (CD) format. Retailers, having already invested heavily in shelving for 45s, albums, 8-tracks, and cassettes, were reluctant to incur further costs. The issue was resolved when it was demonstrated that two CDs could be stacked in the same space as a 12-inch album, and the struggling industry needed the CD to revitalize sales during a deep recession. "But when it was pointed out that you could stack two CDs side by side in the same space as a 12 inch album, they got the point."

**Record Store Day Founding and Impact (2007)** : Record Store Day originated in 2007 from a meeting of independent record store owners in Baltimore who sought to revitalize their struggling businesses. Inspired by "Free Comic Book Day," they developed an event featuring special products and appearances to draw customers back to stores. The first Record Store Day in 2007 successfully spread worldwide, with hundreds of thousands of independent stores participating annually. Record labels recognized the marketing opportunity and joined with special, limited-edition vinyl releases, leading to the saving of numerous small shops and a significant increase in vinyl sales. "This led to the first record store day in 2007."
**Record Store Day Scheduling and 2020 Changes** : Record Store Day is typically held on the third Saturday of April, with a secondary event scheduled for Black Friday in November. In 2020, due to the COVID-19 pandemic, the schedule was adjusted to include three Record Store Days in addition to the Black Friday sale. "Normally, record store day is held on the third Saturday of April with a secondary event scheduled for Black Friday in November."
**Contemporary Record Store Experience** : Despite changes in the music industry, record stores remain valuable spaces for browsing, shopping, and cultural engagement. Visitors can learn by exploring the racks, observing other patrons, and conversing with staff, who often work for the love of music rather than profit. "Although record stores aren't what they used to be, there are still fantastic places to hang out, browse, and shop."
