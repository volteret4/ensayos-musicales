# label - Ticket Reservation Systems Inc. (TRS)

## curiosities
**Founding and Early Operations (1965)** : Founded on May 4, 1965, TRS was the first computer-controlled communication service for ticket sales, conceptualized by Harvey Dubner and funded by Edgar M. Brompton of Seagrams. Its initial aim was to sell tickets for Broadway shows remotely, using Control Data 1700 machines with a rewritten operating system. "It was called Ticket Reservation Systems Inc. or TRS. It was founded on May 4, 1965."
**Early Distribution Model** : TRS installed its electronic box office terminals in banks, travel agencies, supermarkets, and department stores. These locations earned a commission on tickets sold, with the goal of attracting customers to their businesses. "At first they installed these terminals at a couple of banks and some travel agencies. Then came some supermarkets and department stores."
**Challenges and Prejudices** : Despite its innovative approach, TRS faced software glitches, printer malfunctions, and inventory management struggles. Additionally, many retail outlets were unwilling to sell rock concert tickets, viewing rock fans negatively and often placing terminals in inconvenient store locations to avoid direct interaction. "TRS did reasonably well, although there were software glitches, printers that wouldn't print and struggles with handling the inventory of tickets in the system."
**Early Service Charges** : TRS implemented service charges, a key revenue stream to cover its operational costs, starting at 25 cents per ticket. Half of this charge was returned to the venue, a practice that set a precedent for future ticketing companies. "Oh, and men TRS did have service charges. It was 25 cents per ticket, although half of it was kicked back to the venue."
**Rivalry with Compute Ticket** : TRS engaged in a "bitter rivalry" with Compute Ticket, competing for clients such as theaters, arenas, and retail locations. Ultimately, TRS prevailed as Compute Ticket ceased operations by 1970. "TRS and Compute Ticket became really bitter rivals, fighting for clients like theaters, narinas, retail locations, and grocery stores."
**Acquisition of Ticketron Name and Renaming (1969)** : In July 1969, TRS acquired the "Ticketron" name from another defunct computer ticketing company and officially rebranded itself as Ticketron, solidifying its position in the emerging market. "TRS, meanwhile, bought the name Ticketron from another company that crashed in burn trying to use computers to sell tickets. And in July 1969, TRS officially became Ticketron."

# label - Ticketron

## curiosities
**Emergence from TRS (1969)** : The company Ticketron officially emerged in July 1969 when Ticket Reservation Systems Inc. (TRS) acquired the name from another struggling computer ticketing company and rebranded itself. Ticketron quickly became a major player in the ticket-selling industry. **Technical and Client Service Struggles (Late 1980s)** : Despite achieving significant scale, selling tens of millions of tickets and deploying nearly a thousand terminals across the US, Canada, and Europe, Ticketron struggled with client relations and system capacity. "The company just couldn't handle the volume of requests on its machines. **Inventory Allocation Issues** : A significant problem for Ticketron was its inability to secure full ticket allocations for shows, often receiving only about 50% of the available inventory. Fans discovered this limitation, bypassing Ticketron to purchase directly from venue box offices for better seats, which severely impacted the company's revenue. **Failed Diversification Attempts** : Ticketron explored diversification into other ventures, including selling lottery tickets and enabling horse betting through its terminals. These attempts, however, did not succeed in bolstering the company's failing business model. "Ticketron also had this idea that they could sell lottery tickets through their terminals. **Demise and Acquisition by Carlyle Group (1990)** : By 1990, Ticketron's operational and financial difficulties became insurmountable, forcing the company to sell. # curiosity

**Definition of a Concert Ticket** : A concert ticket is defined as a contract involving the attendee, the performing act, the promoter, and the venue. It grants admission to a specific event at a stated time and place, and can be either purchased or, in some cases, provided free for attendance tracking. "A concert ticket is a contract between you, an act, a promoter, and a venue that allows you admission to a specific event at a stated time and place."
**Physical and Electronic Ticket Varieties** : Concert tickets exist in various forms, including pre-printed card stock, machine-printed tickets, home-printable barcodes or QR codes, and digital e-tickets stored on phones. They may incorporate security features like holograms and can be linked to the credit card used for purchase. "A concert ticket can be pre-printed on card stock. It can be printed by a machine the moment you buy it. It can be a barcode or QR code on a piece of paper that you print out at home."
**Historical Problem of Event Access Control** : The challenge of managing who could access an event has existed for centuries, with issues arising at the Greek Theater of Dionysus in 325 BCE. This historical context highlights that ticketing complexities are not a modern phenomenon. "Managing who can get into an event has been a problem for centuries and centuries."
**Birth of Ticketed Events and Scalping (Ancient Times)** : The practice of selling tickets emerged as a way for performers and venues to generate revenue and control attendance. Simultaneously, the phenomenon of scalping was born, with individuals selling tickets at inflated prices outside sold-out events, as seen even in ancient Greek and Roman theaters. "Selling tickets was how the performer and the venue made money and it also limited attendance to any given show. And thus, both the ticketed event and the scalper was born."
**First Modern Concerts (17th Century)** : Concerts as they are recognized today originated in the 17th century, initially at European universities. This period marked the beginning of public, admission-charging musical performances. "The first concerts as we know them today began in the 17th century at European universities."
**Early Pre-Computer Ticketing Methods** : Before computers, concert tickets were acquired primarily through three methods: purchasing pre-printed hard tickets directly from a venue's box office, mailing a letter and check to receive a voucher exchangeable for tickets, or buying from official external ticket agencies that charged a fee of about $1.50. "In those early pre-computer days, there were three ways to buy a concert ticket."
**"Diggers" and Scalping in Traditional Ticketing** : "Diggers" were individuals hired by scalpers to stand in line for hours or even days at box offices to buy the maximum number of tickets, which were then resold at inflated prices. This practice was common in the pre-computer era, exploiting the inefficiencies of physical ticket queues. "Well, they were hired by scalpers to scoop up as many tickets as they could. The term for those placeholder people were diggers."
**"ICE" - Box Office Corruption** : "ICE" was the term for payoffs made by scalpers to box office managers in exchange for early access to the best printed tickets. This form of corruption was rampant, with estimates suggesting over $10 million annually paid for Broadway shows in New York City in the early 1960s. "The payoff to the box office manager was called ICE."
**Drawbacks of Traditional Box Office Ticketing** : Lining up for physical tickets was problematic: it excluded those unable to wait for extended periods (e.g., people with disabilities, out-of-town fans), lacked transparency regarding seat inventory across multiple outlets, and was highly susceptible to scalping and corruption. "Lining up for physical tickets was awful and flawed and open to all kinds of corruption."
**Promise of Computerized Ticketing (1968)** : A newspaper article from 1968, referencing Ticket Reservation Systems Inc. (TRS), proclaimed the radical potential of computerized ticketing, stating that "pre-printed tickets are a thing of the past" and that "seat inventories exist only in the memory banks of our computers." It envisioned a future where anyone could easily purchase tickets for events anywhere. "pre-printed tickets are a thing of the past. Seat inventories exist only in the memory banks of our computers. A West Coast musical enthusiast can buy tickets for the Broadway show he planned to see on his next trip with as much ease as a native New Yorker."
**Mid-1970s Ticketing Landscape** : By the mid-1970s, concert tickets were sold through a mix of traditional and nascent computerized methods: hard tickets at box offices, scalpers and ticket brokers, phone orders with credit cards for box office pickup, and computerized terminals from companies like Ticketron, Select-A-Seat, and BAS. "Contra tickets are being sold in several ways. Number one, same old, same old way with hard tickets at a box office. Number two, scalpers and ticket brokers. Number three, sometimes you could call the ticket seller, give them your credit card number, and then pick up tickets at the box office. And number four, computerized terminals via ticket-tron, select a seat and BAS."
**Challenges in Concert Promotion and Financial Settlement** : As concerts grew in scale, promoters like Bill Graham faced complex challenges: deciding between general admission or seated formats, managing inventory across various venue types (theaters, arenas, stadiums), knowing which outlets had best seats, reconciling unsold tickets, and accurately settling payments with bands at the end of the night. "How do you accurately settle up with the gate receipts at the end of the night? The band's going to want to get paid. How much do you pay them?"
**Public Backlash Against Service Charges (1970s)** : The introduction of service charges, even as low as 50 cents per ticket (around 8% of a $6 ticket face value in the 1970s), led to widespread public dissatisfaction and accusations of price gouging, with many screaming "rip off." "50 cents represents about 8% added on to the cost of the ticket. And many people were not happy about this. They would scream, rip off."
**Numbered Wristband Innovation** : Bill Graham pioneered a system of numbered wristbands to manage lines and reduce scalping for popular shows. Attendees received a wristband at the box office, and a random number was drawn to determine the order of entry, providing a fairer queuing system. "Graham, as far as we know, was also the first to try a new policy of numbered wristbands."
**Importance of Radio Advertising for Concerts** : To ensure shows were well-attended, extensive advertising on local AM and FM radio stations was crucial. Spreading the word about concerts and tours was key to attracting larger audiences. "So it was very important to get the word out on these concerts and these tours by advertising very, very heavily on local AM and FM radio stations."
