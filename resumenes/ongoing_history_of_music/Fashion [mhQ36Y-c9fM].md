# genre - Punk

## curiosities
**Safety Pin Aesthetic** : The punk fashion aesthetic was significantly influenced by New York punk Richard Hell, who famously held his decomposing clothes together with safety pins. Malcolm McLaren, inspired by this look, and Vivienne Westwood, his partner, subsequently introduced this distinct style in their clothing shop, shaping the early visual identity of punk. "Malcolm McLaren had been inspired by how a New York punk named Richard Hell held his decomposing clothes together with safety pins. **Protest Fashion and Doc Martens** : Punks adopted Doc Martens as a form of "protest fashion" because these boots were seen as unpretentious, working-class footwear, providing a stark contrast to the Birkenstocks favored by hippies. Their practical steel toe was also useful in confrontations, fitting perfectly with the punk uniform of jeans, jackets, and mohawks. "Punk's and skinheads adopt the shoe as a protest fashion thing. These were unpretentious working-class boots, the exact opposite of the Birken stops worn by hippies. And they went perfectly with a punk uniform of jeans instead of jackets and mohawks. The steel toe was also handy in a fight."

# genre - Ska

## curiosities
**Distinctive Dress Code** : Fans of Ska music are known for adhering to a particular dress code, which often involves a standardized uniform. This uniformity was significantly influenced by figures like Jerry Dammers of The Specials. "fans of, say, ska dress another."
**UK Ska Fashion Descent** : The clothing styles adopted by the UK Ska scene were directly descended from the original Ska clothes worn in Jamaica. This tradition emphasized sharp, fitted suits in dark colors such as black, navy blue, and chocolate, maintaining a formal yet distinct look. "The clothes for UK SCA descended from the SCA clothes of Jamaica. Sharp suits and dark colors like black, navy blue and chocolate were required."
**Specific Outfit Details** : The Ska uniform was highly specific, requiring fitted single-breasted jackets, white and crisp shirts, and narrow, tapered trousers that were cuffed at least 3 inches above the floor. Clark shoes were considered the best footwear choice, and the entire ensemble was often topped off with a pork pie hat. "The single breasted jackets were fitted. The shirts were supposed to be white and very crisp. And the trousers had to be narrow and tapered, cuffed at least 3 inches above the floor. Clark shoes were best, and the whole thing needed to be topped up with some kind of a hat, preferably pork pie. They did work then, and you know what? It still works today."

# genre - Mod

## curiosities
**Mid-1960s British Youth Cult** : Mod fashion originated in mid-1960s Britain as a distinct youth cult, possessing its own unique philosophy and lifestyle, vividly portrayed in the film "Quadrophenia." "This look had its roots in the mid 1960s and Britain. The mods were kind of a youth cult with their own philosophy and lifestyle. If you've ever seen the film Quadrofemia, you'll know exactly what I'm talking about."
**R&B Music and Scooter Culture** : Mods were fervent enthusiasts of rhythm and blues (R&B) music, drawing inspiration from artists like James Brown from the US and local bands such as The Who, The Kinks, and Small Faces. Their signature mode of transportation was a heavily accessorized and highly customized Vespa T-S or Lambretta GT200 motor scooter. "The mods loved rhythm and blues, R&B music, people like James Brown from the States and locally the Who and the Kinks in the Small Faces. Their mode of transportation was a heavily accessorized and highly customized Vespat-TS or Lambreta GT200 motor scooter."
**Regulated Uniform and Attention to Detail** : The Mod uniform was meticulously regulated and policed, emphasizing a sharp appearance and rigorous attention to detail. This included ensuring pants were hemmed to the exact right length, with a cuff allowing for pant swing, maintaining no more than a 1-inch gap between the pant leg and the top of the shoe. "And they wore a uniform which was highly regulated and policed. See the duty of the mod was to look sharp and pay attention to detail. For example, it was very important to have your pants hemmed at exactly the right length. And the cuff had to allow for the swing of your pants so there was never more than a 1-inch gap between the end of your pant leg and the top of your shoe."
**Specific Clothing Items** : Mod fashion favored slim-fitting suits, Ben Sherman shirts featuring narrow button-down collars, three-button mohair jackets, and parkas often adorned with distinctive Union Jack flags or target symbols. "Mods favored slim-fitting suits featuring Ben Sherman shirts with narrow button-down collars. Jackets were three button mohairs and there were lots of parkas, often featuring union jacks or targets."
**Revival and Enduring Influence** : Mod fashion experienced a significant revival in the 1970s, spearheaded by The Jam, and despite the band's eventual demise, Mod culture and fashion re-emerged, albeit in an altered form, during the Britpop era of the mid-1990s. In the 21st century, pockets of Mods continue to thrive globally, proudly carrying on the fashion attitudes of their predecessors, including driving updated versions of their iconic scooters. "There was a mod revival in the 1970s and was led by the jam. ...Mod culture and fashion survived the demise of the jam and rose again in a somewhat altered form during the Brit pop era of the middle 1990s. And here in the 21st century pockets of mods are thriving around the world with all of them carrying on the proud fashion attitudes of their forefathers. Which includes driving updated version of those cool little scooters."

# label - 2 Tone

## curiosities
**Founder and Racial Harmony Symbolism** : Founded by Jerry Dammers, the leader of The Specials, 2 Tone was a Ska-based record label whose name was inspired by the two-toned suits worn by skinheads and Mods in the 1960s. The black and white color scheme was intentionally chosen to symbolize racial harmony. "Jerry Damors, the founder and leader of the specials, one of the great SCA bands of all time, and the founder of a SCA bass record label called Tuton. In the name of the label was fashion based. Damors dubbed the company after the two toned toned suits worn by skinheads and mods back in the 1960s. The black and white color scheme was supposed to symbolize racial harmony."
**Walt Japsco Mascot** : Jerry Dammers, drawing from his art school days, created a checkerboard pattern featuring a black and white rendering of a rude boy, which resembled a photo negative. This image, based on an old photo of reggae star Peter Tosh, became Walt Japsco, the unofficial mascot for both the 2 Tone label and the broader Ska movement. "Damors was fascinated by this look. He had sketchpads full of designs, one of which was this checkerboard pattern that Jerry had come up with in his art school days. He made that checkerboard with a black and white rendering of a rude boy that kind of looked like a negative of a photo. In fact, it was based on a photo, a very old picture of reggae star Peter Tosh. This guy became the unofficial mascot of Tuton and the SCA movement in general. Even at a name, he was called Walt Japsco."

**Origin and Early Use** : The Doc Marten shoe was invented by Dr. Klaus Martens, who, after sustaining a skiing accident in 1945, collaborated with his engineer friend Herbert Funk to design a comfortable, shock-absorbing shoe incorporating air cushions. For its initial two decades, the footwear primarily served as work boots and orthopedic wear, manufactured in Seeshaupt, a small German town in Bavaria. "Now there was a doctor Martin. And when he had his skiing accident back in 1945, who knew where his invention would lead? His full name was Dr. Klaus Martens. And after he messed up his foot while skiing, Dr. Martin and an engineer friend named Herbert Funk worked on a shoe with a comfortable shock absorbing soil. Their idea was to build in air cushions. ...For the first couple of decades, docks were used as work boots and as orthopedic wear. ...all of which were made in Seas helpt, a small German town on Lake Starnberg in Bavaria."
**Transition to Fashion Accessory** : Initially introduced in the UK in 1960 as a medical aid, Doc Martens rapidly evolved into a significant fashion accessory by the early 1970s. Punks and skinheads adopted them as a form of protest fashion, valuing their unpretentious, working-class aesthetic, which starkly contrasted with the Birkenstocks favored by hippies, and whose steel toe also proved practical in altercations. "These shoes were first introduced into the UK as a medical aid in 1960, but by the early 1970s, the boots had become a fashion accessory. Punk's and skinheads adopt the shoe as a protest fashion thing. These were unpretentious working-class boots, the exact opposite of the Birken stops worn by hippies. And they went perfectly with a punk uniform of jeans instead of jackets and mohawks. The steel toe was also handy in a fight."
**Peak Popularity and Sales in 1990s** : Doc Martens reached their zenith of popularity in the early 1990s, becoming an indispensable accessory for participants in the "Lollapalooza nation." The classic 1460 model, featuring eight holes, was highly sought after, and despite requiring significant breaking-in time, the boots were one of the few fashion labels to which alt-rock fans pledged allegiance. By 1994, the company was selling 125,000 pairs monthly in the US and 10 million worldwide annually. "Docks reached the peak of popularity in the early 1990s as they became an essential accessory for anyone who was part of the Lava Polluzination. The black boots with their yellow stitching went well with the Grunge flannel and jeans. The classic model is the 1460 with eight holes, although there were many variations on that. They needed a lot of breaking in and blisters were common, but now is besides the point. Docks were one of the few fashion labels to which alt-rock kids pledged allegiance. By 1994, the company was selling 125,000 pairs a month in the US and 10 million a year worldwide."
**Post-Grunge Decline and Evolution** : Following the decline of grunge and the rise of pop music in the mid-1990s, the Doc Marten company experienced a downturn but subsequently underwent significant "Darwinian evolution." While the classic boot remains a staple, the company now produces a diverse range of dress and casual shoes, alongside updated versions of the 1460, offering as many as 70,000 variations from three-eyed shoes to 20-eyed boots. "But then the bottom fell out in the middle 1990s after the demise of Grunge and the rise of pop music. Since then, the company has undergone some serious Darwinian evolution. The classic boot is still sold, but the company also makes a variety of dress and casual shoes in addition to an updated version of the 1460. There are 70,000 variations of docks from a three-eyed shoe up to a 20-eyed boot."

# curiosity

**Rock and Fashion Symbiotic Relationship** : Rock music and fashion have shared a symbiotic relationship since their inception, with fashion serving as a crucial component of rock's identity as both show business and theater. The visual aspect is deemed highly important, as it suggests that one must "look good if you're going to sound good." "Rock and fashion have had a symbiotic relationship from the very beginning. After all, rock is also equal part show business and theater, so it's important to look good if you're going to sound good."
**MTV's Impact on Music Fashion (1981)** : The arrival of MTV in 1981 dramatically altered the music industry, elevating the importance of visual presentation to the point where looking good and trendy almost overshadowed sounding good. This shift led to an explosion of a "billion different looks and styles and trends" in new rock fashion. "And then MTV arrived in 1981, which meant that looking good and trendy almost became more important than sounding good. And from there, fashion and new rock splint to a billion different looks and styles and trends."
**Fashion as Tribal Affiliation** : A fundamental function of music fashion is its ability to signify tribal affiliation, allowing individuals to visually express their connection to specific subcultures, musical tastes, or social groups. This is evident in how punks dress distinctively from ska fans. "One of the most important functions of music fashion is that it can be used to signify your tribal affiliation, which brings us to our first concept, the concept of the uniform."
**The "No Image" Image of the 1990s** : In the 1990s, the concept of standardized uniforms in music fashion fell out of favor, with many musicians adopting a deliberate "we don't have an image, image." This approach marked a period where a lack of overt visual branding became a statement in itself. "The concept of the standardized uniform fell into disfavor for the 1990s when most people adopted the, we don't have an image, image. That was the way of thinking."
**Return of Band Uniforms in the 21st Century** : In recent years, the practice of bands dressing uniformly has experienced a resurgence, possibly fueled by a boredom with the "we don't have an image" attitude prevalent in earlier decades. Current musicians are drawing inspiration from the structured looks of punk and new wave eras, as well as the early Beatles and Kraftwerk, embracing a formal, clean-cut style as a means to differentiate themselves. "But in the last few years, the idea of a band dressing the same has come roaring back. ...So what is this deal with this new love of uniforms? Maybe it's because the current generation of musicians are bored with the, we don't have an image attitude. They're looking back at the punk and new wave errors with admiration. They're even looking back at the early days of the Beatles with their matching narrow jackets. They look at the clean lines of a band like Kraftwerk, which became an art statement in itself. These generation Y bands are emulating that formal, clean cut nicely pulled together look. There was so big 20 and 25 years ago. In other words, it's currently a way to stand out from the crowd, which is what it's all about, right?"
**Celebrity Endorsement for Fashion Brands** : Celebrity endorsement is a highly effective strategy for fashion companies, particularly those with limited advertising budgets. The example of Ugg Boots demonstrates how giving free products to highly photographed celebrities, such as Pamela Anderson, can lead to exponential sales increases and make a product a seasonal fashion must-have. "Earlier on, we talked about how good it is for business, for celebrity to be spotted wearing your stuff. Take the case of the Ugg Boot. This is a great study in this whole thing. The company had no money to advertise their boots in the traditional sense. So they simply gave away a few pair to the most photographed celebrities in the world. And they started with Pamela Anderson. And every time she turned up in People or Us magazine wearing a pair of Uggs, sales went through the roof. Uggs became the fashion boot for his season. And the company made a fortune, all for the price of a couple of pairs of free boots."
**Designers Dressing Musicians** : The practice of designers collaborating with musicians to create their wardrobes has a long history, dating back to the 1960s with John Stephen designing for The Who and Tommy Nutter creating suits for Mick Jagger. This trend continued with Stephen Sprouse dressing Deborah Harry of Blondie and Alexander McQueen partnering with David Bowie, underscoring the fashion industry's understanding of celebrity influence. "English designers in the 1960s knew this, which is why a guy named John Stephen designed clothes for the who, and a guy named Tommy Nutter made suits for Mick Jagger. ...Meanwhile, David Bowie paired with Alexander McQueen. In New York, Stephen Sprouse dressed up as neighbor, Debra Harry of Blondie."
**Luxury Brands Benefiting from Celebrity Wear** : Luxury brands like Prada have seen their fortunes rise significantly in specific market segments when influential musicians such as Beck and Dave Grohl started wearing their products. This phenomenon highlights how young audiences are highly attentive to the fashion choices of celebrities, often driving purchasing trends. "Later, Prada, so their fortunes rise in certain segments after guys like Beck and Dave Grohl started wearing their stuff. They know that young people pay attention to what the stars are wearing when they see those pictures in music magazines and tabloids."
**Musicians Owning Fashion Companies** : The creation of fashion is not exclusive to professional designers; many musicians have successfully launched their own fashion and accessory companies. Notable examples include Bono (Edun), the Beastie Boys (X-Large skate clothing), and Travis Barker (American Dream belt buckles and DC Shoes), demonstrating artists' entrepreneurial ventures beyond music. "Creating fashion is not restricted to designers. Bono of you, too, runs a fashion company with his wife called Eden... The Beastie Boys have a skate clothing company called X-Large. Travis Barker of Blink 182 has a hot belt buckle company called American Dream and a shoe company called DC Shoes."
**Fashion and Music Inseparability** : Rock music and fashion are profoundly interconnected and almost inseparable, primarily because rock inherently functions as a form of showbiz. To effectively connect with an audience, a compelling visual presentation is deemed essential, making fashion an integral part of the rock experience. "Rock and fashion are so tightly connected that they're pretty much inseparable. After all, rock is showbiz and if you want to connect, you need to look good, right?"
