**Formation in Athens, Georgia (Spring 1980)** : R.E.M. came together in Athens, Georgia, in the spring of 1980, marking the beginning of their 31-year career. "They came together in Athens, Georgia in the spring of 1980."
**Departure of Bill Berry (Year Unspecified)** : Bill Berry, the band's original drummer, quit R.E.M. to become a hay farmer. The band continued as a three-piece for 14 years after his departure, but their success was mixed, and they were "never the same" without him. "Bill Berry, their original drummer, quit to become a hay farmer."
**Major Label Deal with Warner Bros. (1988)** : R.E.M. made the strategic decision to transition from an indie alternative/college rock band to a major label, feeling exhausted by the constant recording and touring demands. Following a bidding war, Warner Brothers signed them in 1988 for an unprecedented $6 million, an exceptionally large sum for an indie band at that time. Eight years later, they secured another deal worth $80 million, demonstrating their significant market value and influence. "Warner signed them in 1988 for $6 million, an insanely huge number for an indie band at that time."
**Pioneering Indie-to-Major Label Transition** : Despite accusations of "selling out" when they signed with Warner Brothers, R.E.M. maintained artistic integrity and continued to operate without corporate interference. Their ability to do so served as a crucial precedent, demonstrating to other indie bands like Hüsker Dü, Sonic Youth, and Nirvana that it was possible to sign with a major label without compromising their art. "they showed other indie bands that they could make the jump to a major without compromising their art."
**Final Breakup (September 21, 2011)** : After fulfilling their contractual obligations to Warner Brothers by 2010 and becoming free agents, R.E.M. attempted to record new material independently but found themselves "out of gas." Consequently, they officially announced their decision to disband on September 21, 2011, stating their desire to leave the music business on their own terms. Since their breakup, the members have resisted all offers for a reunion, with Mike Mill famously quipping in 2024 that it would take "an impending destruction at birth by a comet" to bring the band back together. "So on September 21, 2011, they officially announced that they were calling it a day."

Along with synthesizers, drum machines, and digital recording, it opened up "entirely new vistas of unimaginable sound and creativity," leading to the rapid proliferation of genres and sub-genres. "Add a new technology like the synthesizer, the drum machine, the sampler, and digital recording technology, an entirely new vistas of unimaginable sound and creativity opened up."

# curiosity

**Evolution of Music in the 21st Century** : The first 25 years of the 21st century have seen immense change in music, with transformations happening consistently and being widely adopted. The rapid turnover of news and information makes it easy to lose perspective on the extent of these changes since January 1st, 2000. "Now that the century is one quarter done, it's a good time to look back on what happened in music."
**Rise and Fall of MP3 Players (2001-2011)** : After legal settlements regarding MP3 players in the late 1990s, numerous companies rushed into the market. Apple's iPod, launched on October 23, 2001, became dominant, especially after becoming Windows compatible on October 1, 2002. Microsoft's later attempt with the Zoon on November 14, 2006, failed to compete, being seen as a "bad imitation" that ceased hardware production by 2011, largely due to the rise of the iPhone integrating iPod features. "Apple was an early adopter jumping in with the iPod on October 23rd, 2001."
**Microsoft Zoon Failure (2006-2011)** : Microsoft launched its digital music player, the Zoon, on November 14, 2006, as an attempt to compete with Apple's iPod. Despite features like Wi-Fi, FM radio, wireless song sharing, and later an OLED screen and HD radio tuner, the Zoon was plagued by issues such as bulkiness, a confusing point system for music purchases, restrictive WMA format, limited country availability, and being seen as a "bad imitation" of the iPod. Its ultimate demise by 2011 was largely accelerated by the iPhone's integration of iPod features, rendering dedicated MP3 players obsolete. "Zoon's stuck around for a few years."
**Music Piracy in the Mid-2000s** : By 2005, music piracy was rampant, leading to a "free fall" in CD sales as people exchanged billions of songs through illegal file-sharing programs like Blind Wire, KaZaA, Audio Galaxy Satellite, and Bear Share. Record labels were at a loss on how to combat this, resorting to lawsuits, digital rights management, and eventually the controversial Sony BMG Rootkit. "By 2005, music piracy was rampant."
**Streaming and Genre Classification (21st Century)** : Streaming services have turbocharged the explosion of new genre classifications due to their reliance on algorithms. To organize music and provide recommendations, streaming companies continuously invent names for new styles, sometimes weekly, leading platforms like Spotify to have around 2500 different genre tags. "The thing that really turbocharged the explosion in new genre classifications was streaming."
**Cyclical Nature of Musical Styles** : Musical styles often follow a cyclical pattern: a genre emerges, gains popularity, declines, disappears, then is rediscovered for a "resurrection and a renaissance," with the cycle typically running between 12 and 15 years. The 2000s and 2010s demonstrated several revival periods, including indie rock, punk, emo, shoegaze, psychedelic music, and 80s synth-pop. "Once you've been around long enough, you begin to notice cycles in musical styles."
**Decline of 24-Hour Music Video Channels (1990s-2020s)** : The era of dedicated 24-hour music video channels like MTV (launched 1981) and MuchMusic (launched 1984) began to decline as early as 1992, when MTV shifted to lifestyle and reality programming ("The Real World") to boost revenue. Pressure from the internet in the late 1990s, followed by the rise of YouTube and Vevo in the mid-2000s, further eroded their relevance, leading most channels to cease music video rotations by the early 2010s and some, like MTV Canada, to shut down completely by late 2024. "MTV was the first moving to lifestyle and reality programming in 1992 with shows like The Real World in order to broaden appeal and increase advertising revenues."
**Evolution and Monetization of Music Videos (21st Century)** : While the 24-hour cable music video channel is largely "extinct," music videos themselves continue to evolve as an art form and promotional tool, shifting from television to smartphones, tablets, and social media platforms like TikTok and Instagram. Budgets are smaller, and they no longer primarily sell CDs, but they are now monetized through platforms like YouTube and still set cultural and fashion trends, with awards like the MTV Video Music Awards and Canada's Prism Prize acknowledging their excellence. "The music video, however, does live on as an art form and a promotional tool."
**Sony BMG Rootkit Scandal (2005-2006)** : In 2005, Sony BMG controversially implemented a Rootkit (malware called XCP and MediaMax) on its music CDs to prevent copying, which surreptitiously installed on users' computers without consent, compromising system security. Discovered by security researcher Mark Russinovich in October 2006, the scandal led to widespread outrage, class-action lawsuits, and millions in damages, further eroding public trust in record labels while failing to curb piracy. "This was the unauthorized installation of malicious code, a malicious software program on private computers that granted unauthorized access to that system by an outside party without your consent."
**User Control and Privacy in Digital Music (2014)** : The U2 "Songs of Innocence" album giveaway by Apple in 2014, where 500 million copies were pushed to iTunes users without an opt-out, highlighted a critical issue: users' demand for 100% control over their digital devices and personal space. This event, much like the Sony Rootkit controversy, underscored the importance of transparency, respect for user autonomy, and privacy, even when intentions are perceived as good. "users wanted 100% control over what went on their devices."
