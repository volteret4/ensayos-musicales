## albums
**The Real Thing (1989) - Third Studio Album** : Released on June 20, 1989, this album initially saw slow traction with its first two singles. However, the release of its third single, "Epic," in January 1990 propelled the album to massive success, establishing Faith No More as a significant crossover act. "At first, it just kind of sat there through its first two singles."
## songs
**We Care A Lot (Early, 1985)** : This single was released early in Faith No More's career, featuring singer Chuck Mosley. Its unique sound, characterized by crunchy metallic guitars, heavy beats, and a rap-style vocal delivery, stood out significantly in 1985, as nobody else was doing that kind of music at the time. "Early Faith No More featuring singer Chuck Mosley and We Care A Lot."
**Epic (1990) - Breakthrough Single** : Released in January 1990 as the third single from "The Real Thing," this song was a "stomping mix of hard rock, metal, and rap." Its accompanying video, featuring a poor little fish, was shown extensively on MTV, turning it into a massive hit, reaching the Top 10/Top 40 in America and introducing rap to white kids in the suburbs. "It was a stomping mix of hard rock, metal, and rap."
## curiosities
**Formation and Early Following (1982-1985)** : Faith No More formed in San Francisco in 1982 and developed a substantial cult following in the Bay Area by 1985, despite initial difficulties in finding a stable singer. "They were formed in San Francisco in 1982, and by 1985 they had a rather substantial cult following in the Bay area."
**Courtney Love's Brief Tenure** : For a period, Courtney Love served as the band's front person, an interesting historical note given her later prominence. "A woman named Courtney Love was their front person for a time."
**Pioneering Genre Fusion** : By the mid-1980s, Faith No More was developing a distinctive sound combining "crunchy metallic guitars, heavy beats, and a rap style vocal delivery," a unique blend for its time that contributed to breaking down musical barriers. "By the mid-80s, they had a real thing going on, crunchy metallic guitars, heavy beats, and a rap style vocal delivery."
**Suburban Rap Introduction** : Along with the Beastie Boys, Faith No More played a key role in making rap accessible to white suburban kids through their successful crossover songs like "Epic." "And just as we saw with the Beastie Boys, it found purchase with white kids in the suburbs."

## albums
**License to Ill (1986) - Chart-Topping Debut** : Released on November 15, 1986, this album was a monumental success, becoming the first rap album ever to top the charts. It combined punk, rock, rap, funk, and hip hop into a globally successful formula and featured Kerry King of Slayer on guitar, introducing countless millions, especially white suburban kids, to hip hop. "And of course, things completely exploded with their license to Il Album which came out on November 15th in 1986."
## songs
**Rock Samples and Big Beats (1986)** : Around the same time Faith No More was developing its sound, the Beastie Boys were "using rock samples and big beats behind their three way raps" in 1986. This was part of the formula that spun punk, rock, rap, funk, and hip hop into a globally successful phenomenon. "Around the same time Faith No More was telling us how much they cared, the Beastie Boys were using rock samples and big beats behind their three way raps."
## curiosities
**Punk Roots and NYC Influence** : The Beastie Boys initially began as a punk band but evolved their sound after becoming fascinated with the music emerging from the projects and streets of New York clubs. "The Beastie Boys originally started as a punk band, but as they spent more time in the clubs of New York, they became fascinated with the music that was coming from the projects in the streets."
**Pioneering Rock-Rap Fusion** : By 1986, the Beastie Boys had perfected a formula that successfully blended punk, rock, rap, funk, and hip hop, proving that there could be "plenty of common ground between metal and rap." "And in 1986, they hit on the formula that spun punk, rock, rap, funk, and hip hop into a globally successful thing."
**Impact on Suburban Youth** : The Beastie Boys were crucial in bringing rap and hip hop to a new, broader audience, including white kids in the suburbs, many of whom were previously inclined to form metal bands. "But the bottom line is that the Beastie Boys brought rap and hip hop to a whole new audience, including all these white kids out in the suburbs, the ones most prone to say, forming metal bands."
**First Rap Album to Top Charts** : "License to Ill" holds the distinction of being the first rap album to reach number one on the charts, a significant milestone, even if it was noted as "a bit weird that society had to wait until three white rappers came along to make rap mainstream." "What many people forget is that this record was the first rap album to top the charts."

## albums
**The Black Album (1991) - Breakthrough & Repositioning** : Released in the summer of 1991, "The Black Album" marked a significant shift for Metallica. Produced by Canadian super producer Bob Rock, it saw the band's sound slow down and become more polished, moving away from their previous raw speed and power. Despite purist objections, it sold over 15 million copies, a "huge breakthrough" that introduced Metallica to a wider audience and repositioned the band as music for outsiders and misfits, aligning them with an "alternative" identity. "Metallica released their famous black album... Bob Rock got them to slow down a bit and polished up their sound."
## curiosities
**Shift from Raw Speed to Polished Sound** : Before "The Black Album," Metallica was known for "raw speed and raw power." However, with the 1991 album, producer Bob Rock guided them to "slow down a bit and polished up their sound," a move that angered speed metal purists but significantly broadened their appeal. "Now up until this record, Metallica was all about raw speed and raw power."
**Brand Repositioning** : "The Black Album" initiated a "careful repositioning" of the Metallica brand. While remaining metal, they began emphasizing their identity as music for "outsiders and misfits," allowing them to "kind of qualifies as alternative" and access a broader audience akin to Nirvana, Soundgarden, and Pearl Jam. "It was also the start of a careful repositioning of the Metallica brand."
**Groomed for Mass Consumption** : At the start of the 1990s, Metallica was "being groomed in package for mass consumption," indicating a deliberate strategy to expand their mainstream appeal, which culminated with the success of "The Black Album." "Metallica is being groomed in package for mass consumption."

# genre - Rap
## curiosities
**Early Polarization and Dismissal** : When rap and hip hop began entering the mainstream in the mid-1980s, it immediately polarized people. Many aggressively dismissed it as "crap" and "bad poetry over, beat stolen from another record," refusing to acknowledge it as legitimate music. "Those who didn't or refused to get it were aggressively dismissive of what rap brought to the table."
**Initial Embrace by Alternative Scene** : Upon its emergence, rap and hip hop were "so new and so different that it was originally embraced by some quarters of the alternative scene." Its non-mainstream, weird, and sometimes annoying qualities ironically made it qualify as alternative music, leading to acts like Run DMC or Rakim being played alongside Depeche Mode on alternative radio. "When rap and hip hop first emerged, it was so new and so different that it was originally embraced by some quarters of the alternative scene."
**Rebellious Appeal Enhanced by PMRC** : The Parental Music Resource Center (PMRC) inadvertently boosted rap's appeal by pushing for "parental advisory stickers on rap albums and demanding that record labels censor these artists." This parental disapproval and attempt at censorship made rap even more attractive to kids seeking rebellious, anti-establishment music. "Everything will get a kid into a form of music faster than by by telling them they can't listen."
**Growing Mainstream Power** : By the 1990s, hip hop and rap had become a "very powerful musical and cultural force," eventually surpassing rock as the genre driving culture. This growth involved co-opting parts of the rock scene to create hybrid sounds. "by the time we got into the 1990s, hip hop and rap was becoming a very powerful musical and cultural force."
