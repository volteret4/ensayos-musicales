# artist - Pearl Jam

## curiosities
**1992 Seattle Free Show Ticketing Dispute** : In May 1992, Pearl Jam planned a free show in Seattle for approximately 30,000 people and approached Ticketmaster to handle the ticketing, as city requirements mandated monitoring the crowd size. Ticketmaster quoted costs of $1 to $1.50 per ticket, which would amount to $45,000 for the band to absorb. Pearl Jam ultimately decided not to use Ticketmaster for this event, a decision that marked the beginning of their significant conflict with the ticketing giant. "In the end, for this particular show, Pearl Jam elected not to use ticket master, but the experience somehow left a bad taste in their mouth. And this was the beginning of a fight."
**1994 Tour Price Cap Initiative** : For their 1994 tour, Pearl Jam actively sought to keep ticket prices as low as possible, a significant stance during the "grunge era." They instructed all promoters to set a ticket face value of $18, with a strict limit of 10% on service charges, meaning the total "all-in" ticket price had to remain under $20. "When it came time to two or in 1994, Pearl Jam told all promoters that they were going to keep ticket prices as low as possible."
**Exploiting Contract Loophole** : Pearl Jam attempted to circumvent Ticketmaster's service charges by utilizing a loophole in their contracts that allowed promotional, charity, and private shows to be ticketed independently. This strategy enabled the band to play several shows without Ticketmaster's involvement, even in venues that typically had exclusive Ticketmaster contracts. "Pearl Jam tried to get around the service charge issue entirely by exploiting a loophole in ticket master's contracts that said promotional concerts, charity gigs and private shows could be ticketed in any way the act wanted."
**Dispute Over Ticket Price Split** : Pearl Jam sought an "all-in" ticket price of $25 but wanted Ticketmaster to reduce its service charge from $4 to $2, which would increase the band's take from $21 to $23 per ticket. Ticketmaster refused this demand, instead suggesting that Pearl Jam could achieve their $25 ticket price if they agreed to accept less for the base price of the ticket. "But then Pearl Jam said, no, no, no, no, you charge $2 so we could make $23 for ticket and ticket master would refuse saying that the band could have their $25 ticket if they agreed to take less."
**1994 Tour Cancellation** : Following the death of Kurt Cobain, Pearl Jam's enthusiasm for their 1994 tour waned, leading to the cancellation of the remaining dates. This event occurred amidst their ongoing ticketing dispute with Ticketmaster and shortly before the US Justice Department initiated contact. "Then Kurt Cobain died. Pearl Jam's heart wasn't in it anymore and the rest of that particular tour was canceled."
**US Justice Department Antitrust Inquiry (1995)** : In the period after their tour cancellation, the US Justice Department contacted Pearl Jam, having monitored Ticketmaster since its 1991 merger with Ticketron and being aware of Pearl Jam's disputes over service charges. The Justice Department reportedly encouraged Pearl Jam to file an antitrust complaint, rather than a lawsuit, initiating an investigation into Ticketmaster's practices. "It appears that the Justice Department may have encouraged Pearl Jam to file a complaint, not a lawsuit, asking for an antitrust investigation into ticket master's practices."
**Antitrust Hearing and Inconclusive Outcome (June 1995)** : A hearing was scheduled for Thursday, June 30, 1995, in Washington D.C., where members of Pearl Jam's organization and supportive music industry figures presented their case against Ticketmaster. Despite intense media coverage, the government's investigation was closed later in 1995 without any substantial action, with only a promise for continued monitoring of the ticketing industry. "But in the end, nothing came of it. The government investigation was closed in 1995 without anyone doing anything."
**Post-Investigation Tour with ETM** : Following the inconclusive government investigation, Pearl Jam embarked on a subsequent tour, explicitly vowing to use non-Ticketmaster venues and partnering with a ticketing company called ETM. However, this venture proved largely unsuccessful, with "more miss than hit" results, ultimately leading to the cancellation of a portion of the tour. "Meanwhile, Pearl Jam went ahead with their next tour, vowing to use non-ticket master venues with a ticketing company called ETM."
**Return to Ticketmaster for Business Reasons** : After their prolonged battles and attempts to use alternative ticketing, Pearl Jam eventually reverted to using Ticketmaster. This decision was driven by changing economic realities in the music industry, as album sales significantly declined by the late 1990s, with revenues dropping from $15-$20 million annually, and illegal file sharing further eroding sales. To compensate for lost album revenue, maximizing income from live touring became essential, which necessitated playing in major venues, most of which had existing ticketing contracts with Ticketmaster. "Well, it was simply the right business decision. In the early 90s, Pearl Jam albums were selling like crazy. So the income stream was fantastic. They were making $15, $20 million a year."

# curiosity

**Ticketmaster's Emergence and Strategy (Early 1990s)** : Ticketmaster emerged as the dominant computerized ticket seller by 1991, outlasting rivals like Ticketron. Its success stemmed from its approach as a ticket service utilizing computers, rather than a computer company dabbling in tickets, and its reputation for a "rock-solid backend" in technical operations. "We finished the last episode in 1991 with ticket buster emerging as the dominant seller of concert tickets."
**Ticketmaster's Free Hardware/Software Model** : Ticketmaster provided its clients (venues, promoters) with ticketing software and hardware for free. This model was financially sustainable through the imposition of service charges on every ticket sold, which covered Ticketmaster's costs and generated profit without taking a cut of the ticket's face value. "Like some of its predecessors, Ticketmaster gave its clients the software and hardware for free."
**Service Charge Evolution (1990s)** : In the 1990s, service charges on concert tickets began to increase aggressively, encouraged by Ticketmaster, and became more uniform across different types of events, whereas previously, tickets for events like circuses had lower charges than concerts. Portions of these service charges were often rebated to the promoter and the venue. "However, in the 1990s, these charges started to creep up and the industry, encouraged by Ticketmaster, was pretty aggressive in boosting these charges for its services."
**Exclusive Ticketing Inventory Deals** : Ticketmaster secured exclusive deals with venues where it was granted the entire inventory of tickets for a given show, rather than sharing it with multiple sellers. This system benefited fans by providing a single point of purchase and venues by ensuring consistent business relationships over several years, similar to exclusive concession deals for beverages. "Instead of sharing the inventory of tickets with multiple ticket sellers, Ticketmaster signed deals where they were given the entire inventory for a given show."
**Ticketron's Role in Ticketing History** : Ticketron was a predecessor in the computerized ticketing space that eventually became defunct and was later absorbed by Ticketmaster. It was characterized as a "computer company that sold tickets," contrasting with Ticketmaster's identity as a "ticket service that made use of computers." "Ticketmaster was the former, the defunct Ticketron was the latter."
**US Justice Department's Antitrust Scrutiny (1991-1995)** : The US Justice Department began monitoring Ticketmaster following its 1991 merger with Ticketron. Their interest intensified with Pearl Jam's public disputes over service charges, leading to an encouraged antitrust complaint in 1995, though the investigation ultimately closed without significant government action, with a promise to continue monitoring the industry. "They'd been keeping tabs on ticket master since the 1991 merger with Ticketron."
**Paul Allen's Investment in Ticketmaster (1990s)** : Paul Allen, co-founder of Microsoft, recognized the growing business of concert ticket sales and invested $325 million USD to acquire an 80% stake in Ticketmaster. His involvement, providing access to the latest Microsoft technology, significantly contributed to Ticketmaster's early lead in online ticketing. "Paul Allen, the co-founder of Microsoft, recognized this and he spent $325 million US dollars to acquire an 80% stake in the company."
**Ticketmaster Acquisitions (1998-2000)** : Ticketmaster expanded its market presence through strategic acquisitions. In 1998, it absorbed City Search, and in 2000, it purchased Ticket Web, further consolidating its position as the internet became increasingly prevalent for ticket sales. "Ticket master absorbed a company called City Search in 1998. Another ticketing company called Ticket Web was bought in 2000."
**Ticketmaster Website Launch (June 1995)** : Ticketmaster launched its first website in June 1995, initially serving as a searchable database of events without direct purchasing capabilities. Users still had to call a phone line or visit a physical outlet to buy tickets. "Ticketmaster's first website was launched in June 1995."
**First Online Ticket Sale (September 11, 1996)** : Online purchasing became possible on Ticketmaster's website starting September 11, 1996. The very first online ticket sale on that day was for a Seattle Mariners game. When Ticketmaster contacted the buyer, his motivation was simply "I don't like people." "For the record, the first online ticket sale took place on that day, September 11th, 1996, and it was one ticket to a Seattle Mariners game."
**Ticketmaster's Vertical Integration Strategy (2008-2009)** : Ticketmaster pursued vertical integration within the concert industry, aiming to own key elements of the music business to become stronger. This strategy led to the acquisition of artist management company Frontline Management in 2008 and the major merger with Live Nation, the biggest concert promoter, in February 2009, forming Live Nation Entertainment. "Plus, ticket master began to see the possibilities of vertical integration within the concert industry."
**Live Nation Entertainment Formation (February 2009)** : In February 2009, Ticketmaster, the largest ticket seller, merged with Live Nation, the largest concert promoter, to form Live Nation Entertainment. This merger significantly enhanced vertical integration by allowing the combined entity to promote shows nationally and internationally, own venues (amphitheaters), and steer managed artists (via Frontline Management) into venues with Ticketmaster contracts. "In February 2009, ticket master, the biggest seller of tickets merged with live nation, the biggest concert promoter. The new company was called Live Nation Entertainment and this resulted in even more vertical integration."
**Dominance of Live Nation Entertainment** : Live Nation Entertainment, formed by the merger of Ticketmaster and Live Nation, is estimated to sell approximately 70% of all concert tickets worldwide, including for most major acts. Despite opposition and antitrust scrutiny, the company has maintained its structure. "Ticket master sells maybe 70% of all concert tickets worldwide, including for most of the biggest acts in the world."
**Historical Concert Ticket Underpricing (Pre-Internet)** : Before the advent of the internet, concert tickets were generally "woefully underpriced" relative to the quality and spectacle of the live experience offered. This historical undervaluation meant that artists, managers, and promoters later realized they could significantly raise prices. "In the days before the internet, concert tickets were woefully underpriced relative to the spectacle that you received for the money."
**Concert Ticket Price Influences: Inflation and Touring Economics** : Concert ticket prices have significantly increased over the last three decades due to general inflation and a fundamental shift in touring economics. Historically, tours promoted albums, with record sales covering costs. Today, new records often serve as a pretext for tours, as most artist revenue now comes from live performances. "First, inflation, everything costs more. It's just the way things work. Second, the economics of touring have changed."
**Concert Ticket Price Influences: Industry Pricing Power** : A third reason for rising concert ticket prices is the realization by artists, managers, promoters, and venues that audiences were willing to pay higher prices for shows. This understanding led to a concerted effort within the industry to increase ticket costs, moving towards "fair market value." "And third, prices went up because the people in the industry, artists, managers, promoters, venues, knew that they could raise prices and we would still pay them."
**Concert Ticket Price Influences: Competition Among Promoters** : Counterintuitively, competition among promoters for popular acts drives concert ticket prices up. Promoters bid against each other by offering increasingly larger guaranteed payments to artists, which are minimum amounts paid regardless of ticket sales. The winning promoter then recoups these higher guarantees and other costs by raising ticket prices, which ultimately trickle down to the fans. "When a band is ready to go on tour, their agent lets promoters in various cities and territories know if there is more than one promoter in a certain city or territory, those who want the show will bid on it."
**Concert Ticket Price Influences: Merchandise Sales Markup** : Bands set profit targets for merchandise sales, and because venues typically take a significant cut (20-50%) of merch sales, artists often compensate by charging higher prices for t-shirts and other merchandise. This strategy contributes to the overall cost for fans attending concerts. "Merch sales go mostly to the band. Although the venue will take a cut of sales, maybe 20, 25%, I've heard as high as 50% in some cases."
**Concert Ticket Price Influences: Fair Market Value Realization** : Artists and managers have belatedly recognized that concert tickets have historically been underpriced compared to other entertainment events like sports and theater. They are now actively seeking to price tickets at their "fair market value," reflecting the true worth of the performance and demand. "Artists and managers have finally realized that they've been underpricing their concert tickets for years relative to other events like sports and theater."
**"Facility Fee" for Venues** : In addition to service charges, a "facility fee" may be levied on concert tickets, with the revenue going to the venue to help cover its costs. While venues also generate income from concessions, parking, and alcohol, this fee is an additional way for them to make money from ticket sales. "There may also be a service charge that goes to the venue for the same reason. This became known as a facility fee in some territories."
**Cup Holders in Venues for Increased Sales** : The widespread presence of cup holders in amphitheaters, arenas, and stadiums serves a purpose beyond convenience: they are primarily designed to enable attendees to purchase and hold two drinks simultaneously. This encourages greater alcohol sales, as fans can store a second beer while consuming their first. "They're there so you can buy two drinks at once and have some place to put your second beer while you drink your first."
**Tiered and VIP Pricing** : Concert ticket pricing has evolved to include tiered pricing, where seats closer to the stage cost more, and VIP sections that offer additional benefits like soundcheck access or meet-and-greets for even higher prices. This allows artists to maximize revenue from higher-demand seating and unique fan experiences. "First, there was tiered pricing. Better seats closer to the stage cost more. Believe it or not, that wasn't always the case."
**Percentage-Based Service Charges** : Since the 1990s, service charges have shifted from flat fees to being calculated as a percentage of the ticket's face value. This change means that as ticket prices increase, the service charge also rises significantly, impacting the final cost for consumers. "And remember, service charges are often set as a percentage of the face value of the ticket."
**Concert Ticketing "Holdbacks"** : A significant portion of concert tickets are held back from the general public sale for various purposes: fan clubs (1,000 tickets in a 15,000 capacity venue example), VIP seating (1,000 tickets), credit card VIP programs (2,000 tickets), artist's use for friends/family (100 tickets), venue promotional use (100 tickets), radio station contests (400 tickets), record label use (100 tickets), and seat licenses (500 tickets for season ticket holders). This can reduce the publicly available pool dramatically. "The first category is called holdbacks. These are tickets reserved for specific purposes."
**Deals with Ticket Brokers/Scalpers** : Promoters, ticket sellers, and venues often have existing deals with ticket brokers, sometimes referred to as scalpers, who resell tickets on the secondary market. This practice allows the primary sellers to unload tickets, transferring financial risk to the secondary market, and records those tickets as sold, even though they haven't reached the end consumer yet. "The promoter, the ticket seller, and the venue may have existing deals with ticket brokers, companies that resell tickets on the secondary market."
**Bots and Instant Sell-outs** : Online ticket sales are heavily targeted by scalpers using automated software programs called "bots." These bots, combined with the high-speed processing capabilities of ticketing systems like Ticketmaster, can hammer online box offices with thousands of requests per minute, causing a limited public ticket inventory to sell out within 60 seconds. "Plus we have scalpers using software buying programs called bots to hammer the online box office with requests for tickets."
**Leakage of Holdback Tickets to Secondary Market** : The system of ticket "holdbacks" is prone to leakage, where tickets intended for fan clubs, seat license holders, venues, or even the artists themselves are immediately resold to scalpers on the secondary market for profit. This practice contributes to the scarcity of tickets for the general public and drives up prices. "Meanwhile, the whole back situation is very leaky. Members of the fan club get tickets and might immediately flip them to scalpers to make some extra money."
**Online Ticketing Shift (Circa 2000)** : Around 2000, concert ticket sales began transitioning from physical box offices and phone lines to online platforms. This shift, driven by technological improvements and increased efficiency, allowed for faster sales of greater ticket volumes but also required significant capital investment in hardware and software, making it difficult for new players to enter the market. "That all began to change around 2000 when concert tickets began to be sold online."
**All-in Pricing vs. Drip Pricing** : Artists have the option to implement "all-in pricing," where the displayed ticket price includes all fees and charges. However, studies have indicated that fans are just as annoyed by a single, higher all-in price as they are by the "drip, drip, drip" of multiple added fees, leading ticket sellers like Ticketmaster to continue absorbing blame for fragmented pricing to deflect negativity from the artists. "Now here is a weird thing. The artist could ask for all in pricing. That means the price you see includes all the charges for the ticket rolled into one."
**Dynamic Pricing Mechanism** : Dynamic pricing, similar to that used for hotel rooms and airline tickets, adjusts concert ticket prices up or down based on real-time demand. Algorithms aim to find the "fair market value" where artist popularity meets fan willingness to pay. Unlike scalping, the increased revenue from dynamic pricing directly benefits the artist, not a secondary seller. "This is where dynamic pricing comes into play. Just like hotel rooms and airline tickets and Uber rides, go up and down with demand."
**Dynamic Pricing Controversy and Price Moderation** : Dynamic pricing often causes initial controversy when tours go on sale, especially for popular acts, as high demand can lead to exorbitant initial prices (e.g., $5,000 for nosebleed seats). However, demand typically moderates after the first few days, and algorithms adjust, causing prices to fall, a fact rarely reported in the news. "This causes controversy during the first couple of days the tour goes on sale, especially for a super popular act."
**Optimal Ticket Purchase Time for Fair Value** : Studies suggest that the "fair market value" for a concert ticket, balancing demand and willingness to pay, is often achieved 24 to 48 hours before the showtime, regardless of the seller. This indicates that waiting closer to the event may result in better prices as demand moderates or secondary sellers try to unload inventory. "Study show that the fair market value of a concert ticket, that balance between demand and what we're willing to pay, is usually achieved 24 to 48 hours before showtime."
**Fan Entitlement vs. Market Reality** : There's a common misconception among fans that they "deserve" to buy front-row seats at a low, fixed price, rather than accepting that concert tickets, like other high-demand goods or services, are subject to market forces and supply-and-demand pricing. "You do not deserve To buy a front row seat for 50 bucks just like you can't get a first class airline ticket to Singapore for a hundred bucks You pay the going rate."
