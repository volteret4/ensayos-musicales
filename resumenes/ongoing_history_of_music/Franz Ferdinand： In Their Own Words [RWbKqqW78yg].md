## albums
**Franz Ferdinand (2004)** : The band's debut album, released on March 9, 2004. It achieved massive success, selling a million copies in the US and winning numerous awards, including two Brit Awards and the Mercury Prize. The album's artwork and sound were described as "geometric," "chopped," "solid," and "angular," reflecting a precise and bold aesthetic. "This debut record was a hugely successful album, selling a million copies in the US, winning awards from magazines and MTV."
**You Could Have It So Much Better (2005)** : This second album was released on October 5, 2005, approximately 18 months after their debut. The band felt they had "unfinished business" and more ideas to explore from their first record, leading to its quick release. Its visual imagery drew heavily from the Soviet Communist era, characterized by bold, geometric, and blocky designs, even influencing the font choice, to match the music's clean lines, preciseness, and boldness, as well as the "cut and paste" song structures. "And that's why the second record came out so quickly after it was like, come on, this is unfinished business here, we need to do something well with this."
**Tonight (2009)** : The band's third album, released on January 26, 2009, was recorded after a significant break following their 2006 tour. The visual imagery departed from the Soviet theme, instead adopting a "nighttime record" theme based on photographic inspirations from Cindy Sherman and Weegee. The album's creation involved a lot of improvisation and the use of old Soviet technology, specifically Polyvox synthesizers, to achieve a unique sound. "The theme of this record is the night. It's a nighttime record."

## songs
**This Fire (2004)** : This was one of the singles from Franz Ferdinand's 2004 debut album. "Franz Ferdinand with this fire that was one of the singles from their debut record from 2004."
**Take Me Out (2004)** : The first single from the band's debut album, released on March 9, 2004. This track is a prime example of Franz Ferdinand's experimental approach to tempo, featuring a dramatic slowdown about a minute into the song. The verses were originally upbeat (around 125 BPM), but the choruses sounded "ridiculous" when played at that speed, leading the band to separate the fast verses from the slow choruses to create an unconventional but effective song structure. "France, Verde, with Take Me Out, the first single from the first album which was released on March 9th 2004."
**Do You Want To (2005)** : This song is from their second album, You Could Have It So Much Better, released in October 2005. "Friends for a Dan with Do You Want to, from the second album, you could have it so much better, which came on on October 5th, 2005, about 18 months after the first record."
**Ulysses (2009)** : The first single from the album Tonight. The song is inspired by the ancient Greek epic of Ulysses and its modern adaptations, such as James Joyce's book and the film O Brother, Where Art Thou? It reflects the theme of embracing being lost and viewing it as an adventure, drawing parallels to the band's experiences while touring. "It's mainly about the Greek guy all those years ago."
**Live Alone (2009)** : A track from the album Tonight, notably featuring the distinct sound of ancient Soviet-era Polyvox synthesizers. "Franz Herdnand using some ancient Soviet era synthesizers on a song called Live Alone from the Tonight album."

## curiosities
**Early Band Formation and Delays** : The band members, Alex Kapranos, Paul Thompson, Bob Hardy, and Nick McCarthy, played in various bands in Glasgow during the 1990s before forming Franz Ferdinand. There was confusion regarding the band's exact formation and first rehearsal dates, with Alex initially suggesting January 2002 or April 2003 for the first show, while Paul insisted it was 2002, citing the recording of their first album in summer 2003 after a year of playing together. "First show, I think, was in April 2003. No, I was 2002."
**Band Name Origin - Archduke Franz Ferdinand** : The band's name was chosen by its artsy members, who viewed it as a "mission." It refers to Archduke Franz Ferdinand, a member of the Austro-Hungarian Royal Family whose assassination on June 28, 1914, in Sarajevo by Gavrilo Princip triggered World War I, marking the beginning of the 20th century's widespread carnage. The band found the name appealing for its sound, rhythm, and historical significance, aspiring for their music to have a similar pivotal impact. "But also the theory behind the name was that he was this guy and when he was shot everything turned on that moment, nothing was quite the same afterwards."
**Encounters with Archduke's Descendants** : The band has met descendants of Archduke Franz Ferdinand who were curious to meet them. In one instance at a show in Vienna, two children, identified by local papers as his great-great-grandchildren, were asked to pose with the band while holding an etching of the Archduke's assassination, which Alex Kapranos found morbid. "The weirdest one was when we played in Vienna one time and there were these two kids who came down to the show and the local papers there as well, they were saying, right, okay now we're going to pose to the band and one of the kids to hold this etching of the assassination of the Archduke."
**Death Threat Incident** : Alex Kapranos recounted receiving a death threat from someone who claimed to be a "star one" and threatened to murder the band. "only did get a death threat once, didn't they remember? Like some guy said he was going to come and murder us because he was a, I don't know they had good stuff."
**"The Chateau" Rehearsal Space** : The band's initial rehearsal space was an abandoned warehouse they creatively named "The Chateau." This venue also hosted their gigs and art events, but it was eventually raided by the police, forcing them to relocate. "That track came together in the rehearsal space, which was an abandoned warehouse that they called the Chateau, a little artistic irony there."
**Victorian Jail Rehearsal Space** : Following a police raid on their previous rehearsal space, "The Chateau," the band moved their operations to a building that had once served as an old Victorian jail. This move continued their pattern of choosing ironically artsy locations for their creative endeavors. "The cops weren't so sympathetic though. They raided the place, necessitating a move to a building that was once an old Victorian jail."
**Unconventional Song Structures** : Franz Ferdinand is known for experimenting with tempo changes and unconventional song structures, a characteristic Alex Kapranos attributes to a desire for bravery and experimentation in their music. He noted that their approach to "Take Me Out," with its contrasting fast verses and slow choruses, arose from a need to make both sections work effectively while maintaining a strong emphasis on melody within an experimental framework. "I guess it's being brave enough to use an unconventional song structure, you know, a laylite, sort of being a little bit experimental."
**Alex Kapranos's Food Writing** : Alex Kapranos began writing about food for The Guardian after an editor approached him. Despite initial skepticism about his qualifications as a musician, his past experience as a chef and the opportunity to travel globally, trying unusual foods like deep-fried locusts and bulls' testicles, provided a unique perspective. He appreciated the "naivety" his lack of a food critic's vocabulary brought to his writing. "The idea was my idea, I've got to be totally honest about that, it was one of the editors at the Guardian, approached me and said, what do you want to write about food and my immediate reaction was like, well of course I can't write about food, I'm a guy in a band, I don't know anything about it."
**Three and a Half Million Records Sold** : Following the success of their first two albums, Franz Ferdinand sold a total of three and a half million records, a significant achievement that afforded them the luxury of taking more time to record their third album. "That's what happens when you sell three and a half million records. You get to take your time."
**Alex Kapranos's Activities During Band Break** : During the band's break after touring in late 2006, Alex Kapranos went to Vancouver to produce The Cribs' album and recorded his own album with a friend named Danny, released by Domino Records under the character name Victoria and Albert. The band regrouped in February 2007. "I went to Vancouver and produced the crib's last record. They went to New York and mixed that. I recorded an album, I hear my friend Danny, that's a character, which Domino put out this year actually."
**Artwork Inspired by Cindy Sherman and Weegee** : For the artwork of their third album, Tonight, Franz Ferdinand's visual imagery shifted to a "nighttime record" theme, inspired by photographers Cindy Sherman and Weegee. They created a series of improvised nighttime "crime scenes," similar to Sherman's untitled film stills and Weegee's flash-bulb crime scene photography, inviting fans to imagine the narrative. "And they're based around the ideas of two photographers, Cindy Sherman and Ouija."
**Soviet Technology Experimentation** : For their third album, the band experimented with old Soviet technology, specifically Polyvox synthesizers, to achieve a distinctive sound. Alex Kapranos compared these instruments to Lomo cameras or Octavia microphones, noting that they were reverse-engineered versions of Western technology (like Moog synthesizers or Leica cameras), which, while "kind of wrong," produced a "kind of good" and cool sound, characterized by aggressive filters. "One of the things the band experimented with for this record was some old Soviet technology."
**Physical Media Philosophy** : Alex Kapranos views records and CDs as containers for music, acknowledging that the music itself is transferable online. He believes that to sell physical containers, they must be desirable, appealing particularly to "vinyl nuts" who demonstrate devotion to music by purchasing and actively listening to vinyl. He differentiates sound quality: vinyl is like cinema, CD is like HDTV, and MP3 is like YouTube. "All the record is or a CD is it's a container for the music."
**John Varvatos Ad Campaign** : Franz Ferdinand's "slim, stylish look" attracted fashion designer John Varvatos, who signed them to front his 2009 ad campaign. This collaboration meant that Alex Kapranos's new wardrobe would be featured extensively in fashion magazines. "Their slim, stylish look caught the eye of designer John Vervardo's. They were signed to front his 2009 ad campaign."

# instrument - Polyvox Synthesizers

## curiosities
**Soviet Reverse Engineering** : Polyvox synthesizers are examples of old Soviet technology, designed through reverse engineering to approximate Western instruments like the MOOG, without the engineers ever having seen the original. An electronic engineer in Kiev, inspired by the sound of the MOOG, built the Polyvox based solely on theory. The resulting instrument, while "kind of wrong," produced a "kind of good" and cool sound, characterized by its extreme and aggressive filter, and an appearance resembling a Soviet tank's control panel. "But there was an electronic engineer in Kiev I think he was. And he wanted to build a MOOG. I loved the sound of the MOOG. He knew the theory of how a MOOG was built. Had heard what they sound like but had never seen one. And so he built this thing and it looks like the control panel of a Soviet tank."
**Used by Franz Ferdinand on "Tonight"** : Franz Ferdinand notably experimented with and incorporated Polyvox synthesizers into the recording of their third album, Tonight, particularly on the song "Live Alone," highlighting its distinct sound. "Franz Herdnand using some ancient Soviet era synthesizers on a song called Live Alone from the Tonight album."

# curiosity

**Bands with Artistic Sensibility** : Several notable bands are cited as possessing an inherent artistic sensibility without becoming pretentious. These include The White Stripes, Trent Reznor, U2, The Cure, Marilyn Manson, Roxy Music, the Sex Pistols, and Blur. This trait is often attributed to members having attended art school, studied art, or being art collectors. "You too, the cure, Marilyn Manson, Roxy Music, the Sex Pistols, Blur, they all get it without going over the top, mainly because each one of them either A went to art school, or B studied art, or C collect art."
**Synesthesia** : Synesthesia is a neurological condition where the brain perceives the world in different, cross-sensory ways. Bob Hardy of Franz Ferdinand has a form of synesthesia where he can "see smells," which he considers a gift rather than a handicap, and uses this perception as inspiration for his painting. Some people with synesthesia can also "see music." "Bob has a condition known as synesthesia. It's really hard to explain, but Bob's brain, his actual brain, is wired in a way that he can see smells."
**Glasgow School of Art** : Bob Hardy studied painting at the Glasgow School of Art and frequently exhibits his artwork. His background in art, combined with Alex Kapranos's interest in art, helped forge their connection before forming Franz Ferdinand. "He studied painting at the Glasgow School of Art and often exhibits his creations."
**Bob Hardy's Creative Pursuits** : Beyond his role as bassist for Franz Ferdinand, Bob Hardy is a multidisciplinary artist. He writes graphic novels, with one notable project being based on the development of viaducts. He also collaborated on an almanac with his aunt, Françoise Hardy, who is an astrologer and musician. Additionally, he is deeply involved in video games and served as a consultant for the development of the game Rock Band. "Bob also writes graphic novels. Last I heard he was working on one based on the development of Viaducts."
**Physical Media Sound Quality Comparison** : Alex Kapranos offers a vivid analogy to describe the difference in sound quality across various music formats. He likens listening to vinyl to watching a film in a cinema, a CD to watching a film on an HDTV, and an MP3 to watching a film on YouTube, emphasizing the "huge difference" in quality that makes vinyl a distinct and devoted listening experience. "The way I think about it is listening to vinyl is like going to see a film in the cinema. Listening to a CD is like watching a film on an HDTV. And listening to an Mb3 is like watching a film on YouTube. That's the difference in the quality."
