## albums
**The Smiths (Debut Album)** : This self-titled first album was a hit with critics and a surprisingly large number of fans in Britain, demonstrating strong initial reaction from the public and critical acclaim. "After coming out of the gate so well with their critically acclaimed debut record..."
**Hat Full of Hollow (Collection Album)** : Released hastily by the record label due to strong demand after their debut album, this collection comprised singles, B-sides, and outtakes. Its release was intended to buy the band time to record another proper studio album, and it performed very well commercially. "their record label had to rush release a second album of singles and B-sides and outtakes called Hat Full of Hollow, just to buy the Smiths some time to record another proper studio album."
**Meat Is Murder (1985)** : Released on Valentine's Day 1985, this was considered the band's typical difficult second album and is often cited by fans as their least favorite. The band attempted to expand their sound and subject matter, but with mixed results, leading to a "confused" reception. This record marked Morrissey's outspokenness on animal rights and vegetarianism, featuring a sample of cattle hoof beats on the title track. "The Smiths' second proper album came out on Valentine's Day 1985, was called Meet Is Murder. This was the typical, difficult second album, and usually the one cited by Smith's fans as their least favorite."
**The Queen Is Dead (1986)** : Released in mid-June 1986, this album is considered the Smiths' masterpiece, despite being born amid controversy and significant problems with their record company, Rough Trade. It marked a huge improvement over "Meat Is Murder" in all aspects—musically, lyrically, and performance-wise—and featured a great collection of pop songs filled with wit, satire, emotion, criticism, and carefully crafted self-pity. "In mid-June 1986, the Smiths released what is considered to be their masterpiece."
**Strangeways, Here We Come (1987)** : This was the band's final studio album, released on May 19, 1987, amidst internal turmoil and the eventual disintegration of the band. The title "Strangeways" refers to an infamous British prison, hinting at the difficult circumstances surrounding its creation. "in the midst of all this turmoil, the band was able to record one final studio album. It was called Strange Ways Here We Come, Strange Ways Being an Infamous British Prison."

## songs
**Please, Please, Please, Let Me Get What I Want (1984)** : Released in August 1984, this song is described as one minute and 50 seconds of naked emotion and unbearable sadness, considered by some to be the perfect Smiths song, while others found it very wimpy. "The Smiths from August 1984 and please, please, please, let me get what I want."
**Hand in Glove (1983)** : Released in May 1983 as the band's first single, this song explicitly dealt with homosexuality, marking a stark contrast to the dominant heavy hard rock and synthesizer dance beat music of the time. It exemplified the band's unique jangly pop sound with roots in 50s and 60s music. "Here's that first single, May 1983. This is Hand in the Globe."
**Reel Around the Fountain** : This song, typical of The Smiths' early thematic content, was about child molestation. Its controversial subject matter was part of what made the band stand out from other contemporary music. "Another song called Real Around the Fountain was about child molestation."
**Pretty Girls Make Graves** : This song touched upon the dark theme of murder, another example of Morrissey's lyrics exploring taboo or emotionally intense subjects. This thematic choice contributed to the band's unique and often somber appeal. "Pretty girls make graves was about murder."
**This Charming Man (1983)** : Released in late 1983, this song became one of The Smiths' signature tracks, largely due to Johnny Marr's unique guitar style. Marr's tasteful, minimal, and jangly approach to guitar playing was a fresh sound at a time when power chords and fast solos dominated. "This charming man from late 1983, which would become one of their signature songs, thanks largely to Johnny Mars guitar style."
**How Soon Is Now** : Initially considered a throwaway B-side to a 12-inch single that "nobody cared about," this song has since become one of the great retro hits and an alt-rock classic of all time. It is compared to iconic songs like "Smells Like Teen Spirit," "Anarchy in the UK," and "Ziggy Stardust" for its enduring impact. "Since then, though, this song has become one of the great retro hits ever."
**That Joke Isn't Funny Anymore** : This track is presented as a sample from the album "Meat Is Murder," showcasing the band's lyrical depth and emotional range during that period, despite it being considered a difficult second album. "This is a sample of Media's murder. It's called That joke isn't funny anymore."
**Frankly, Mr. Shankly** : Featured on "The Queen Is Dead," this song critiques The Smiths' own record company, Rough Trade. The name "Mr. Shankly" is a thinly veiled reference to Jeff Travis, the head of the label, demonstrating the band's willingness to "bite the hand that fed them." "This is called, frankly, Mr. Shankly. And wherever you hear the name, Mr. Shankly, substitute the name Jeff Travis."
**Girlfriend in a Coma (1987)** : One of The Smiths' last singles from "Strangeways, Here We Come," this song inspired the Canadian author Douglas Coupland to write a book titled "Girlfriend in a Coma" in 1986, illustrating the widespread influence of The Smiths' music on popular culture. "Here was one of their last singles. Girlfriend in a coma from Strange Ways Here We Come."

## curiosities
**Book - Songs That Saved Your Life** : A book about The Smiths is titled "Songs That Saved Your Life," reflecting the profound personal impact the band's music had on many fans, particularly sensitive and introspective types. "you might have a book on the Smiths, which is honest to God called Songs That Saved Your Life."
**Cult of Personality around Morrissey** : The Smiths' cult of personality was heavily centered around Stephen Patrick Morrissey, who was perceived as a romantic loner. Fans worshiped him, and he fostered an image of being an outspoken and enigmatic lead singer. "The Smiths Cult of Personality was centered around Stephen Patrick Morrissey, a kid from Manchester, who fancied himself to be a romantic loner."
**Morrissey's Early Life and Influences** : In the 1970s, Morrissey devoured music magazines, wrote letters to the editor criticizing critics, and became a big fan of Oscar Wilde, a flamboyant Victorian-era writer known for his love of fresh flowers and controversial lifestyle. Morrissey also worked for the tax department and a hospital morgue before pursuing music. "Morrissey became a big fan of Oscar Wilde, the flamboyant and controversial Victorian era writer who loved fresh flowers and apparently other men, something for which he served two years in prison."
**Band Name Origin** : The name "The Smiths" was deliberately chosen because Morrissey and Marr couldn't think of anything more plain or boring, making a statement against the fancy names of other Alt Rock bands of the early 80s like Orchestral Manoeuvres in the Dark and Depeche Mode. It was also a homage to New York poet Patti Smith, one of Morrissey's heroes, and possibly a nod to a 1960s Manchester nightclub called Mr. Smiths. "They chose the name the Smiths because they couldn't think of anything more plain or anything more boring. That was very deliberate."
**Live Debut (October 4, 1982)** : The Smiths made their live debut on October 4, 1982, at The Ritz, a club in Manchester. The lineup eventually settled with Morrissey, Marr, Mike Joyce, and Andy Rourke by the time their first single was released the following spring. "The Smiths made their live debut on October 4th, 1982 at a Manchester club called The Ritz."
**Unique Musical Style and Thematic Content** : In 1983, The Smiths distinguished themselves from the dominant heavy hard rock and synthesizer dance music. Their sound was jangly pop with roots in 1950s and 60s music, featuring simple, stark arrangements without big solos or excessive overdubs. Morrissey's vocal style was a croon, likened to Bing Crosby, and his lyrics explored taboo subjects like homosexuality, child molestation, and murder, alongside life and love, but with a unique sense of doomed romanticism and emotional agony. "The Smiths were neither. The music was jangly pop with its roots clearly in the 50s and 60s."
**Visual and Performance Aesthetic** : During live gigs, The Smiths often featured a gay go-go dancer. Morrissey surrounded himself with fresh flowers and sometimes wore a clunky 1950s-era hearing aid for aesthetic effect, contributing to the band's distinctive image. "Their live gigs featured a gay gogo dancer. Morrissey surrounded himself with fresh flowers, and he often wore a clunky 50s-era hearing aid for some kind of aesthetic effect."
**Morrissey's Public Outspokenness** : Morrissey became highly outspoken about various issues, including animal rights (leading to his "self-righteous vegetarian indignation" on "Meat Is Murder"), Margaret Thatcher's conservative government, the state of music, and the music industry. He was also critical of other stars, making controversial statements about Madonna, reggae music, and even the Live Aid initiative. "This is the record where Morrissey began to be very outspoken about animal rights and meat eaters in general."
**Problems with Rough Trade Records** : The band experienced significant problems with their record company, Rough Trade, which worsened after "The Queen Is Dead" was released. These issues were so prominent that they were even satirized in the song "Frankly, Mr. Shankly." "The problems with rough trade got worse."
**Internal Band Conflicts and Disintegration** : After "The Queen Is Dead," The Smiths faced numerous internal issues, including problems with management. Bass player Andy Rourke was temporarily fired due to a heroin addiction. Johnny Marr was involved in a suspicious car accident on November 12, 1986. Craig Gannon, who had joined as a fifth member, was fired weeks later. Johnny Marr grew tired of Morrissey's dictatorial tendencies, self-centeredness, and public tirades. These factors led to the band's breakup. "Base player Andy Warck was temporarily fired due to a massive heroin addiction. Johnny Marr was nearly killed in a suspicious car accident on November 12, 1986."
**Official Breakup (September 12, 1987)** : After recording their final studio album, "Strangeways, Here We Come," The Smiths officially broke up on September 12, 1987. Their mystery and influence, however, have endured despite their short career. "The album came out on May 19, 1987. And on September 12 of that year, it was official. The Smiths had broken up."
**Influence on Technopop and Britpop** : Despite existing for only about four years, The Smiths became the definitive and most influential British indie band of the 1980s. Their success and impact are credited with hastening the decline of Technopop and laying the foundations for the Britpop sound that would dominate British pop for nearly a decade thereafter. "Not only did their success and their impact hasten the death of Technopop, it also laid the foundations for what would become Britpop a few years later."
**Morrissey-Marr Songwriting Alliance** : The collaboration between Morrissey and Johnny Marr was a "fine, fine songwriting team" that helped reintroduce the pleasures of the two- and three-minute pop song. Their combined talents contributed to Morrissey's lyrical wit, sense of doomed romanticism, and penchant for drama. "While they were together the Morrissey Mar Alliance was a fine, fine songwriting team."
**Empathy for Alienated Fans** : The Smiths, much like Depeche Mode and The Cure, provided comfort and solace to troubled, sensitive, shy, and alienated music fans. Morrissey's ability to not only understand but also articulate their pain was a key factor in the band's deep connection with its audience. "Morrissey not only understood their pain, but could articulate it like no one else."
**Unlikely Reunion** : A reunion of The Smiths is deemed unlikely due to significant bad blood among Morrissey, Johnny Marr, and the other band members. An "ugly court battle" resulted in Morrissey having to pay one million pounds in back royalties to other members. Morrissey also claims no promoter has ever made a big money offer for a reunion tour. "Will there ever be a Smith reunion? Unlikely given that there's a lot of bad blood between Morrissey Johnny Mar and the rest of the band, there was a ugly court battle that resulted in Morrissey having to pay out one million pounds in back royalties to other members of the band."
**Recommended Books** : Two essential books for understanding The Smiths are "Morrissey and Marr, the severed alliance" by Johnny Rogan, and "The Smiths, songs that saved your life" by Simon Goddard. "If you're a fan, you will love it. If you're not a fan, stay away."

# curiosity

**Golden Age of Rock Music** : An individual's "golden age of rock music" is defined as the period between their middle teens and early twenties. This is when one has the most time, energy, and desire to invest in music, both physically and financially, and when music is emotionally and spiritually central to their life. The music discovered and adopted during this time tends to stay with them forever. "Your golden age of rock was that period between your middle teens and your early twenties."
**1983 Music Industry Context** : In 1983, musicians generally fell into two camps: those playing heavy, hard rock, or those focused on synthesizers with dance beats. This binary landscape made The Smiths' unique jangly pop sound, with its 50s and 60s roots, stand out as a refreshing alternative. "If you were a musician in 1983, you were in one of two camps. A, you were into heavy, hard rock, charged up and testosterone, or B, you were into synthesizers with dance beats."
**Johnny Marr's Innovative Guitar Style** : In 1983, Johnny Marr's guitar playing introduced a "new and fresh sound" that contrasted sharply with the prevailing trends of power chords and fast solos. His style was characterized by its tasteful, minimal, and jangly qualities, which became a signature element of The Smiths' music. "Nobody was playing the guitar like Johnny Mars back in 1983. For everyone else, it was all about power chords and fast solos, not this really tasteful, minimal, jangly stuff."
**Reintroduction of Two- and Three-Minute Pop Songs** : The songwriting alliance of Morrissey and Marr within The Smiths played a significant role in helping to "reintroduce the pleasures of the two and three minute pop song," a concise and impactful format for popular music. "They helped reintroduce the pleasures of the two and three minute pop song."
**Music as Salvation for Sensitive Souls** : The Smiths, alongside bands like Depeche Mode and The Cure, provided profound comfort and a sense of understanding for troubled, sensitive, shy, and alienated music fans. Morrissey's ability to empathize with and articulate their pain made their records a source of solace. "The Smiths gave troubled, sensitive, shy and alienated music fans something to hang on to."
