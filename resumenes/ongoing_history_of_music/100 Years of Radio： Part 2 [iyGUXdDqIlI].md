# curiosity

**Top 40 Radio – Invented in Omaha by Todd Storz After Observing Jukebox Repetition** : The origin of top 40 radio traces to Todd Storz and Bill Stewart's efforts to rescue a failing Omaha AM station called KOWH. One story holds that around 1951 they observed bar patrons repeatedly playing the same songs all night on a jukebox, inspiring the idea of doing the same on radio. A more documented version: Storz ran a New Orleans station and, competing against a rival show called "The Top 20 at 1280," launched "The Top 40 at 1450" one hour earlier. When he moved to Omaha, he expanded the concept to 24 hours. KOWH went from a 4% to a 45% market share within six months. The format spread worldwide and dominates commercial radio to this day. "You can thank or blame Todd Storz for top 40 radio."

**"Radiohead" Band Name – Came from a Talking Heads Song About Radio** : The band name Radiohead derives from "Radio Head," a track from the Talking Heads 1986 album *True Stories* about someone's experience listening to the radio. "That is where the band got their name."

**Transistor Radio (1954) – Portable Device That Made the Teenage Cultural Revolution Possible** : Until 1954, radios were large pieces of furniture that provided communal family listening experiences. The transistor radio changed everything: pocket-sized, portable, and — crucially — equipped with an earphone that allowed private listening. It arrived at precisely the same moment as rock and roll and the newly constructed social category of the teenager. Young people could take their forbidden music anywhere, out of sight and out of earshot of parents. "The transistor radio and rock and roll arrived at exactly the same time. This new social and economic construct called a teenager could then take his or her music with them everywhere."

**Border Blaster Mexican Radio Stations – 500,000-Watt Signals Covering the Continent** : While US and Canadian AM stations were capped at 50,000 watts, Mexican stations just across the US border operated at 100,000, 250,000, or even 500,000 watts — powerful enough for signals to reach across North America and sometimes Europe. Birds approaching transmitters were literally cooked by the electromagnetic radiation. The most famous was XERF AM in Ciudad Acuña (across from Del Rio, Texas), associated with Wolfman Jack, which could reportedly be heard continuously from New York to LA. XERB near Tijuana claimed 500,000 watts while branding itself as "the mighty 1090 in Hollywood" to seem like a US station. "Animals coming too close to the transmitters would be literally cooked to death."

**FM Surpassed AM in 1978 – AM Kept Alive by Talk Radio; Canada Had Unique Diversity Rules** : AM radio dominated broadcasting from its invention in the 1920s all the way to 1978, when FM listening finally exceeded AM in the United States for the first time. AM survived primarily through right-wing talk radio and sports radio. In Canada, unique regulations protected AM by legally prohibiting FM stations from replicating AM programming formats: no more than 49% of songs could be hits (top 40 songs), no song could be played more than 18 times per week, and stations were required to run programming spanning multiple genres by law. FM stations also faced extensive spoken word quotas. These rules were dropped in the 1990s but left a lasting imprint on how Canadian radio sounds compared to its American counterpart. "In Canada, AM stations playing music continued to be very successful well into the 80s."

**First Radio Commercial (August 1922) – WEAF New York, $50 for 10 Minutes; First Jingle Was Wheaties, 1926** : WEAF, an AM station in New York City with no parent company to subsidize costs, pioneered the modern radio commercial in August 1922 by selling 10 minutes of air time to the Hawthorne Court apartment complex in Jackson Heights for $50 — the first paid radio advertisement. By October 1922, the station had made $550 in ad sales. The first radio commercial jingle — a singing advertisement — was broadcast by Wheaties breakfast cereal in 1926. By 1930, approximately 90% of American radio stations were running commercials. "As far as we can tell, the modern radio commercial was born in August 1922."

**Car Radio Ban Attempt (1930s) – Massachusetts Registrar Tried to Outlaw In-Car Radios** : The first car radio appeared in 1930 as the Motorola 5T71, a delicate vacuum-tube device that could add as much as $120 to a $600 car. George A. Parker, the registrar of motor vehicles in Massachusetts, campaigned to ban radios in cars on distracted driving grounds, coming close to having his way. The public rose up against him and shut him down; Parker redirected his efforts toward drunk driving. "The good citizens of Massachusetts rose up as one and slathered him down."

**DAB Digital Audio Broadcasting – Norway 99.7%, Canada and US 0%** : Digital Audio Broadcasting is an entirely separate set of frequencies broadcasting MP2 digital signals, developed in Europe as a replacement for analog AM and FM. While Norway has achieved 99.7% population coverage, Switzerland 99.5%, and Germany 98%, the United States refused to adopt the European standard (citing interference with military frequencies), meaning Canada and the US have zero DAB coverage. European countries satisfied with DAB have begun shutting down their AM and FM transmitters entirely, leaving North America technologically behind. As a compromise, the US developed "HD Radio" — a trademark (like Kleenex) for digital signals piggybacked on existing FM broadcasts, standard in cars since around 2012 but attracting negligible public awareness. "Ask a person on the street about HD radio and they'll go, what?"
