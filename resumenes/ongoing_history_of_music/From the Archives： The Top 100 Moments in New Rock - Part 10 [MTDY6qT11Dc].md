# artist - Nirvana

## albums
**Nevermind (1991) – Industry-Changing Release** : Released on September 24th, 1991, initial shipments totaled 46,251 copies, with industry expectations of only 50,000 sales if lucky. This album arrived during a stagnant period for rock music, dominated by classic rock, unamusing hair metal power ballads, and "crappy phony pop stars." It resonated deeply with Generation Xers who were seeking an emotional voice amidst recession and the Gulf War, providing them with music that spoke to their anger and fear. *Nevermind* swiftly became a monumental success, changing the entire rock landscape and shifting alternative music into the mainstream. "Never mind was one of those records that changed everything."

## songs
**Anarchy in the UK (1976) – Debut Single** : Released on Friday, November 26th, 1976, this was the Sex Pistols' first single, following their first gig on November 6th, 1975. The band recorded many versions with different producers before settling on the final mix produced by Chris Thomas, known for his work with Roxy Music. The recording sessions in Fall 1976 involved five straight takes, with Thomas splicing together halves of different takes, rigging a tape delay to mask a snare drum sync issue, Johnny Rotten laying down vocals around 7 PM, and Steve Jones overdubbing 22 layers of guitars a couple of days later to fill out the sound. Initially, it had "little impact outside the punk community" in its first week but saw a massive sales surge after their *Today* show interview. "In the first week that song was in the stores in November of 1976, it had little impact outside the punk community."

## curiosities
**First Gig (1975)** : The Sex Pistols' first public performance took place on November 6th, 1975. The band had been together for just over a year before releasing their debut single, "Anarchy in the UK." "The first gig had been on November 6th of 1975."
**"Today" Show Interview (1976) – National Controversy** : On Wednesday, December 1st, 1976, the Sex Pistols appeared on the popular UK tea-time chat show *Today*, hosted by Bill Grundy. They were a last-minute replacement for Queen, who had to cancel due to Freddie Mercury's throat infection. The band and their entourage consumed a bottle of Blue Nun in the limo and more liquor in the green room before going on live, unaware the show was unedited. Grundy's provocative questioning led to the band using profanity on air, which resulted in Grundy's two-week suspension, the producer and director's firing, and widespread media outrage with headlines like "the filth and the fury." This 117-second incident, despite being accidental, massively boosted punk rock's profile in the UK, causing "Anarchy in the UK" sales to jump from 10,000 copies in a week to 10,000 copies a day. "The impact of that 117 second interview was immediate."

**Pre-Punk Scene Hub (Early 1970s)** : In the early 1970s, before punk rock was a defined genre, the Mercer Art Center served as a significant hangout for the "outrageous weird stuff" happening in New York's nascent music scene. Located at 240 Mercer Street within the Broadway Central Hotel, it featured multiple rooms allowing up to five simultaneous performances, and hosted some of the New York Dolls' very first gigs. **Collapse and Aftermath (1973)** : On a Friday in August 1973, the hotel portion of the Broadway Central Hotel, which housed the Mercer Art Center, suddenly collapsed. This catastrophic event killed four people and injured 19, and could have been much worse if it had happened a few hours later when 1,500 people might have been inside. The destruction of this key venue left New York's "weird music freaks" without a gathering place, directly contributing to the rise of CBGB as the new hub for the burgeoning punk scene. "But then something went terribly wrong one Friday in August of 1973. The building started to creak and make all kinds of strange noises. And then suddenly, and without warning, the hotel portion collapsed."

# instrument - Electric Guitar

## curiosities
**Invention of the Electric Guitar (1890s-1950s)** : The invention of the electric guitar is attributed to several individuals. George Breed, a US naval officer, laid the groundwork for the guitar pickup in 1890. Lloyd Law, an acoustic guitar designer, patented a "quasi-electric" in 1934. Adolf Rickenbacker's company introduced an electric lap guitar popular with country performers in the mid-1930s. George Bochamp designed the "frying pan," the first electric guitar built in significant numbers. Les Paul created his experimental "log" by cannibalizing guitars in 1941 but couldn't find a manufacturer. Fender began working on a solid-body electric guitar in 1949, releasing the Telecaster two years later, while Gibson issued a Les Paul-endorsed model a year after that. Without this instrument, "rock and roll would have never had a chance." "So who really invented the electric guitar? Like I said, it depends on who you ask, but this much we know. Without it, rock and roll would have never had a chance."

**Invention and Introduction of the MP3 (1989)** : MP3 technology originated from the Froenhofer Institute, a German government research group, with Carl Hein's Brendan Berg as the key figure. He started developing the idea in 1977, aiming to send music over phone lines by digitizing and compressing files. By 1989, his team developed the necessary algorithms to shrink music files to one-tenth their original size, releasing the software as shareware for free use. Initially overlooked in Germany, it gained immense traction in Silicon Valley in 1997, leading directly to the creation of Napster and profoundly impacting the music industry and music consumption worldwide. "All this current turmoil in the industry can be traced back to the father of the MP3, Carl Heinz, Brendan Berg."
**First Legal Pay-Per-Download Music Service (1995)** : Sonic Net, a New York-based company, launched in 1995 as the first to legally offer pay-per-download music singles. "Sonic Net was the first, and look at where we are today."

**Invention of Recorded Sound (1877)** : Thomas Edison invented the phonograph, initially aiming to preserve the human voice for dictation or as an answering machine for the telephone. His first design in 1877 used a rotating cylinder covered in tin foil, where shouting into a horn moved a needle to cut grooves. Playback involved placing the needle back and turning a crank. The first sounds ever recorded were Edison reciting "Mary had a little lamb." After a decade, he refined the machine, replacing tin foil with wax. This invention fundamentally changed music consumption by allowing it to be enjoyed "wherever and whenever anyone wished." "Without recorded sound, all music would have to be enjoyed as it was being made. A live performance would be the only way to experience music."
