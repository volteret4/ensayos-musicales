## albums
**Pills, Thrills, and Bellyaches (1990)** : This album, released in 1990, marked a period of massive success for the band, both at home in England and internationally. The band considered this their "LA album," describing it as encompassing "everything, whatever you can get." The album's commercial performance contributed to Factory Records flying high. "The Happy Mondays in Loose Fits from the 1990 album Pills and Thrills and Belly Eggs."
**Bummed** : This album was characterized by the band as their "ecstasy album," indicating its connection to the drug culture of the Madchester scene. "I think, Bund was the, was the excessy album."
**Yes Please (1992)** : This album, intended as a follow-up to "Pills, Thrills, and Bellyaches," was recorded at Eddie Grant's studio in Barbados. The recording sessions were chaotic, marked by members destroying "more than nine cars," drinking local rum, self-medicating with local herbs, and turning sun lounges into a crack den. Shaun Ryder's inability to provide lyrics and vocals meant the band had to record most of the music without him, only for him to typically write lyrics later. Despite these difficulties and being described as "a collection of... songs" rather than a cohesive album, it surprisingly received good reviews and reached number 3 on the charts. Bez reportedly considers it his favorite Happy Mondays album. Its release is considered to have marked the end of the Madchester era. "The Happy Mondays and Stinking Thinking, from the Yes Please album, the record that pretty much marked the end of whatever Madchester was."

## curiosities
**Factory Records Contract** : Contrary to a common perception that Factory Records bands lacked formal contracts, the Happy Mondays confirmed they did have a contract. The label, particularly Tony Wilson, granted them considerable artistic freedom, allowing them to "do what we want." "No, we did. We had a concert, but they would pray. They would let us do what we want."
**Relationship with Factory Records Staff** : Apart from Tony Wilson, who "loved" the Happy Mondays and supported them, many other bands, managers, and staff at Factory Records "hated" them or "didn't really take tools." "Tony Loved Us, everyone hated us."
**Factory Records Bankruptcy Myth** : The band refutes the myth that they were responsible for bankrupting Factory Records. Instead, they assert that the label went under due to "bad management" and, upon its disbandment, Factory Records still owed the Happy Mondays a significant amount of money. "No, we didn't. That's another myth. We didn't."
**Barbados Recording Session Chaos - Sam Lord's Castle Incident** : During the recording of their "Yes Please" album in Barbados, the band stayed at Sam Lord's Castle. On their first night, after a long flight, the keyboard player, PDR, got drunk, became annoyed by a cabaret band called "Spice" or "Spice Boys," got on stage, and was subsequently escorted away by the police, leading to the band being kicked out of the castle. "He'd got so annoyed that I hated the cabaret band that he'd got up stage and it's like one of them. That's how it all started."
**Barbados Recording Session Chaos - Car Destruction** : While recording in Barbados, the band was involved in numerous car accidents, with "more than nine" cars reportedly destroyed due to the poor road conditions, particularly on the less touristy east side where Eddie Grant's studio was located. "I think it was about, I thought it was nine, but then someone told me it was more."
**Barbados Recording Session Chaos - Shaun Ryder Crashes into Bar** : One night, while Gazz Wheeling was drinking at a small, shack-like bar, Shaun Ryder drove a car straight through the front of the establishment, asking for Gazz. The bar owner, a former West Indian cricketer, was wealthy and calmly stated he would pay for the damage, as did the band. "This car comes through the front, the front bit of the bar... And everyone jumped up and then show mocks instead of heard you was here."
**Barbados Recording Session Chaos - Bez's Jeep Incident** : During the Barbados sessions, Bez had a Jeep and drove it into the marshlands near the studio's pool. The Jeep ended up upside down, crushing Bez and resulting in him having "all these pins and his arm." Approximately two weeks later, Bez broke his arm again attempting to jet ski. "this Jeep upside down and he was crushed and he had all these pins and his arm and then two days, I know, I know about two weeks after he was, you know, trying to try to jet ski in the yellow, you know, and broke it again."
**Barbados Recording Session Chaos - Crack Den at the Studio Pool** : Studio staff, including a man named Mark from New York, discovered that the band had transformed the sun lounges by the studio pool into a makeshift crack den, a situation Gazz Wheeling found "hilarious." "I went up to the pool and they turned the sun lounges... they turned and they made a track down out of it."
**Band Entourage in Barbados** : The Happy Mondays' recording session in Barbados involved a large entourage, swelling to about 30 people at one point as families, including Gazz Wheeling's wife, came to stay near the studio. "We were part of about 30 people at one point."
**Acknowledging the End of Their Peak** : During the chaotic "Yes Please" recording sessions in Barbados, the band members realized they had "peaked" and were merely "milking it," recognizing that their level of "goofiness" signaled the approaching end of their initial success. "So at some point, we knew he was ending... we've peaked. Yeah. And now we're just milking it."
**Reunions and Shaun Ryder's Persona** : The Happy Mondays reunited only once after an initial break, not multiple times for "tax reasons" as sometimes suggested. Gazz Wheeling clarified that Shaun Ryder used the "tax reasons" excuse as a "safety net" to maintain a "real Manchester finger" persona, where not "enjoying it" was considered "cool" and a reflection of a working-class English identity. "No, that was, that was just Sean saying that to kind of ever, as a bit of a, a safety net in case it all went pear-shaped."
**Gazz Wheeling's Departure** : Gazz Wheeling ultimately left the Happy Mondays because of Shaun Ryder's behavior, which included cutting live sets down to 20 minutes (even at festivals expecting 1.5 hours) and having to be paid to show up for recording sessions for a new album. Gazz felt the new material wasn't as good as their early work, and Shaun's actions made continued collaboration difficult. "I knew I had to leave one to come straight on the other and they got to the point where Sean, you know, was cutting the sets."
**Shaun Ryder's Shyness and Insecurity** : Despite his wild public persona, Shaun Ryder is described as being "shy" and "insecure." This hidden shyness, combined with the band's collective insecurities, was suggested to be a driving force behind some of their behavior, rooted in a working-class English mentality of "not getting above your station." "No, Sean was kind of, a little more Sean, yeah, it's shy, Sean, shy. So that's why he did it, you know, he was shy, basically, and we were kind of all kind of insecure."

# genre - Madchester

## curiosities
**Origin and Era** : Madchester was a "crazy and wildly popular scene" that emerged in Manchester, England, flourishing in the late 1980s and early 1990s. It is described as music that "set the stage for much of the British indie music that was to come for the next 15 years," with its impact still felt today. "In 1990, commercial reign for the Inspiral Carpet, one of the bands that formed the Holy Trinity of madchester, that crazy and wildly popular scene in Manchester, England that flourished in the late 80s and early 90s."
**Holy Trinity** : The scene was famously led by a "Holy Trinity" of bands: Inspiral Carpets, Happy Mondays, and The Stone Roses. "one of the bands that formed the Holy Trinity of madchester."
**Duration and Decline** : The Madchester phenomenon "burned very brightly, very hard for about four years," roughly ending around 1992-1993. Its decline was attributed to bands burning out and public interest moving on, leading to a period of lull before the rise of Britpop. "So, Madchester... comes to an end somewhere around what he figured, 92, 93."
**Cultural Impact and Travel** : During its peak, Madchester clubs were so popular that people would travel extensively from London via coaches and trains, arriving at Piccadilly Station on Fridays and Saturdays to spend the weekend in Manchester. This was unusual for Londoners to travel to "provincial cities." "the first time ever you'd been a club and you'd hear people would come up from London, get a coach... All these Londoners piling off to go, you know, and staying in Manchester for the weekend."

# label - Factory Records

## curiosities
**Artistic Freedom Policy** : Factory Records was known for allowing its artists, such as the Happy Mondays, considerable artistic freedom, letting them "do what we want" despite having formal contracts. "They would pray. They would let us do what we want."
**Tony Wilson's Role** : Tony Wilson, the founder, was described as a "great guy" and primarily "an artist and a supporter of the artist," rather than a shrewd businessman. He was particularly supportive of the Happy Mondays, even when others at the label or other bands disliked them. "Tony wasn't the greatest businessman. He was an artist and a supporter of the artist."
**Bankruptcy Cause** : Factory Records' eventual disbandment and financial collapse are attributed to "bad management," not solely to the Happy Mondays' excessive spending as widely believed. The label still owed the Happy Mondays a substantial amount of money when it went under. "Bad management broke them."

# curiosity

**Manchester's Enduring Musical Legacy** : Manchester has consistently been a significant center for music, producing a wide array of influential artists and bands across various genres, from the Bee Gees and David Jones (The Monkees) to The Smiths and Simply Red. This rich musical history persists, with the city's identity deeply intertwined with music, football, and fashion. "Manchester's always been, I don't know why Manchester's been this great musical set."
**Manchester's Cultural Pillars** : Historically, and particularly during the Madchester era, Manchester's identity was built around three equally important cultural pillars: music, football, and fashion. These elements were deeply ingrained in the working-class culture of the city and were seen as interconnected. "there's three things, it's the older son, it's a, it's music, it's football, it's fashion, then three things are really important."
**English Working Class Mentality of "Knowing Your Place"** : A strong "class thing in England," especially within the working class, dictates a mentality of "you don't get above your station." This concept implies that even when successful, individuals are expected to "know your place" and not appear overly enthusiastic or self-important, a trait exemplified by Shaun Ryder's "not enjoying it" persona. "It's a class thing in England. So working class thing, you don't get above your station."
**Manchester's Urban Transformation** : Manchester has undergone gentrification and significant development, evolving into a "very European" city often referred to as "the capital of the north" or "the London of the north." It boasts three universities (Manchester, Manchester Met, Salford), the largest gay community outside London, and the largest student population in Europe. "it's got three universes, Manchester, Manchester, Met and Salford, it's had the biggest gay community outside London, biggest student population in Europe."
**Manchester's Political and Economic Identity** : Manchester is characterized as a "very left wing working class city" with considerable wealth, despite the decline of its industrial age, setting it apart from cities like Liverpool which "got left behind." "it's a very, it's a very, or left wing working class city."
**Historical Football Allegiances in Manchester** : Historically, football club allegiances in Manchester were geographically and socially divided: Western and North Manchester supported United, while South and East supported City. Manchester United was originally the Catholic team and the "working man's club" supported by trade unions, whereas Manchester City was the Protestant team and initially the "rich club." These distinctions have largely dissolved in modern times due to changes in ownership and the influx of money. "United was originally the Catholic team, city was the, the post-it team, city initially was the rich club, United was the working man's club to sell by the trade unions."
