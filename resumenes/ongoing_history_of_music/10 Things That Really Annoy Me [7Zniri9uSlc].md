# artist - The Clash

## albums

**London Calling (1979) – Recording Anecdote** : In the summer of 1979, The Clash entered the studio to record this album with producer Guy Stevens. During a warm-up session where the band covered an old blues song by American singer Vince Taylor, Stevens secretly hit record. Despite the band's concerns that their performance was sloppy and the tempo was wrong, Stevens accepted the take, asserting that "all rock and roll speeds up." The resulting energy, as heard in tracks like "Brand New Cadillac," contrasts with modern, digitally corrected recordings. "The clash was bewildered. We can't use that. It was sloppy. The tempo was all wrong. We listened to how we speed up through the song."

## songs

**Brand New Cadillac (1979)** : This song, featured on the "London Calling" album, serves as an example of The Clash's raw, uncorrected recording style from 1979. The band's tendency to play faster and faster as the song progressed, a natural imperfection, adds energy and was embraced by producer Guy Stevens, who stated, "all rock and roll speeds up." Such an organic tempo change would likely be "fixed" in modern production using tools like Pro Tools. "Did you notice how the band kept playing faster and faster throughout the song?"

# artist - Suzanne Vega

## songs

**Tom's Diner (1981) – MP3 Reference Track** : This song was penned by Suzanne Vega in 1981 while at Tom's Restaurant, located at 2880 Broadway in New York City, a location also famously featured on the television show "Seinfeld." Its unique characteristics—being a pure a cappella recording with almost no reverb—made it an ideal reference track for the Fraunhofer Institute in Germany during the development of the MP3 compression algorithm. Engineers on the project meticulously listened to the song "tens of thousands of times" as they worked to compress the digital audio file to one-tenth its original size without introducing audible glitches, ultimately achieving a very good result. "Now I played that song because it was used as a reference track when the Fraunhofer Institute in Germany was working on creating the MP3."

# artist - Rage Against the Machine

**Rage Against the Machine (1992) – Debut Album Recording** : The band's powerful debut record, released in 1992, was produced by Canadian Garth Richardson. To capture a live sound, Richardson set up the band in a large room, routed all their audio into a concert PA system, and then mic'd those speakers, instructing the band to play. This recording method is credited with contributing to the album's distinct live feel. "He set the band up in a big room and routed all their audio into a concert PA system. He then mic those speakers and told them to play."

**Californication (1999) – Loudness Wars Example** : Released in 1999, this album is frequently cited as one of the worst-sounding records affected by the "loudness wars" due to excessive compression during mastering. The heavy compression resulted in audible clipping, distortion, and a near-zero dynamic range, leading to a comparatively unsatisfactory listening experience. An unauthorized "Unmastered Edition" circulates online, offering a superior sonic experience with enhanced clarity in Chad Smith's cymbals, subtle keyboard lines, improved background vocals by Flea, greater separation in Flea's bass notes, and more openness in John Frusciante's chords. "There is so much compression used in the mastering that you can hear all kinds of clipping and distortion."

# curiosity

**Pro Tools Software Overview** : Pro Tools, a digital audio workstation, emerged as the standard recording software in most studios, originally conceived by students at the University of California at Berkeley in 1989 and launched in 1991. Initially costing $6,000 for a four-track system, it now offers extensive capabilities, including waveform visualization, cutting, pasting, and infinite sound tweaking, enabling precise correction of vocal errors, rhythmic lags, or incorrect notes. It has made recording significantly easier, though it can also serve as "compensation for a lack of talent," allowing for less precise initial performances. GarageBand, Audacity, and Adobe Edition are its technological descendants. "Most studios use pro tools, which is pretty much the standard for recording software."
**Digital Trickery and Lack of Talent** : Digital recording and editing technologies, particularly sophisticated software like Pro Tools, can be used to mask a performer's lack of talent. While beneficial for production, this capability means artists are not required to be as "spot on" during recording sessions, as "glaring mistakes" can be digitally corrected in post-production. This practice is criticized for leading to "unreal and unnatural recordings," contrasting with the analog era where artists had to perfect their parts and imperfections were sometimes valued for their beauty. "If you could fix it in the mix, it absolves you of being spot on during recording."
**Auto-Tune Technology Origins** : Auto-tune's core technology originated in the 1990s from Andy Hillbrand's work at Exxon, where he developed methods for interpreting seismic data to locate oil and gas deposits. Hillbrand later realized that the same underlying technology, which could detect, analyze, and modify seismic data, was transferable to audio files, allowing it to "detect, analyze, and modify the pitch of audio files," thereby creating real-time pitch correction for music. "Back in the 1990s, a guy named Andy Hillbrand was working for Exxon. His job was to develop techniques for plumbing the depths of the earth to locate deposits of oil and gas."
**Auto-Tune in Live Concerts** : Auto-tune is not confined to studio use; it can also be deployed in live concert settings. This real-time pitch correction technology allows a performer's off-key notes, whether slightly flat or sharp, to be instantly corrected, making it sound as though the artist on stage is "nailing every note perfectly" and ensuring a flawless vocal performance during a show. "Auto-tune can be used in a concert setting, making it sound like the person on stage is nailing every note perfectly."
**Auto-Tune's Robotic Effect** : When auto-tune is applied at extreme levels, it ceases to be a subtle pitch correction tool and instead creates a distinctive, "robotic effect" on the vocals. This artificial sound is a direct result of the technology being "turned up all the way to absurd levels" and is recognizable in many recordings. "That robotic effect? That's auto-tune turned up all the way to absurd levels."
**iTunes Software Deterioration** : Introduced on January 9, 2001, after Apple acquired Sound Jam, iTunes software began as a simple and functional tool. However, "over the years, though, it has become bloated and dysfunctional and tasked with doing too many things." It struggles as a combined music store, iPhone/iPad/iPod device manager, often exhibiting buggy syncing, archaic library management, high memory consumption, slow performance, difficulty with large libraries (leading to seizing or crashing), and lack of support for high-resolution audio files like FLAQ, performing "awful on Windows machines." "Over the years, though, it has become bloated and dysfunctional and tasked with doing too many things."
**MP3 Psychoacoustic Compression** : MP3s employ the science of psychoacoustics to compress digital audio files, primarily by "stripping out all the data that's massed by material in the foreground," meaning data that the human ear is less likely to perceive due to masking effects. Despite its efficiency, this compression algorithm introduces audible artifacts: a "shrillness, a dissonance on the high end" and bass sounds that are "unformed, little mushy and indistinct." "The algorithm shrinks the digital file by stripping out all the data that's massed by material in the foreground."
**MP3 Development and Hockey Recordings** : During the development of the MP3 at the Fraunhofer Institute in Germany, engineers faced significant challenges with compression artifacts. To address this, they unexpectedly used recordings of a hockey game as a reference for testing. The distinct sounds of "skates on ice and sticks on pucks and pucks on boards, and the cheer of the crowd" proved exceptionally effective at revealing glitches in early versions of the MP3 encoding software, helping them refine the algorithm to near perfection. "To get MP3s to sound as good as they do, engineers used recordings of a hockey game."
**MP3s and Dopamine Delay** : Neuroscientists suggest that when listening to an MP3, the brain "somehow knows that some of the music has been stripped out." As the brain attempts to internally "fill in the gaps" of missing musical information, there is a delay in the secretion of dopamine, the body's natural "feel good hormone." Consequently, listening to an MP3 "might not feel as good" or as quickly satisfying as an analog recording which retains all musical data. "Neuroscientists believe that when we hear an MP3, the brain somehow knows that some of the music has been stripped out."
**Decline in High-Fidelity Sound Pursuit** : Since the turn of the millennium, there has been a societal shift away from prioritizing "pure, clear sound," with many now content with the fidelity offered by cheap earbuds or laptop speakers. This contrasts sharply with the 1970s, 80s, and 90s, when a passionate pursuit of "perfect sound" (big, tight bass; accurate mid-range; bright, smooth highs) led many—predominantly men—to spend "thousands upon thousands of dollars" on elaborate home stereo equipment and car audio systems, engaging in detailed comparisons of technical specifications and brands. "But sometime after the turn of the millennium, society shrunk away from pure, clear sound."
**The Loudness Wars Phenomenon** : Over the past two decades, the music industry has seen "the loudness wars," a trend driven by record labels, producers, managers, and artists insisting on maximizing the intrinsic loudness of recordings during the mastering process. This is achieved through aggressive audio compression, which "squeezes in as much musical data as you can," resulting in "really squished music" with significantly reduced dynamic range, making quiet passages sound as loud or louder than intended. Modern CDs can be up to "20 decibels louder than they were back in the 80s and 90s," which flattens subtleties, increases perceived distortion, and leads to "listener fatigue." This practice is based on the unproven belief that louder recordings stand out more and are more successful, and has been exacerbated by the rise of MP3s and low-quality earbuds. "The trend has become that to make the default loudness, the default force by which the music seems to come out of the speakers and headphones, to be as great as possible."
**Cassette Technology's Flaws** : Cassettes are described as an "old, inferior technology" whose attempted resurrection is misguided due to inherent flaws. They deliver poor sound quality, are prone to jamming, and restrict mix tape creation to real-time. Over time, the glue binding magnetic particles to the tape dries out, leading to degradation and the "forever irreparable" loss of music information. Additionally, their plastic boxes have fragile hinges, and cassettes frequently end up damaged in car footwells or melt on dashboards, further compounded by the absence of factory cassette players in modern automobiles. "Cossets are an old, inferior technology that served us well in their day and serve no purpose whatsoever."
**Fan Abandonment of Popular Artists** : A common phenomenon where a segment of early fans will "blow off" a band or artist once they achieve widespread popularity, often perceiving this success as a "personal betrayal" because the artist is no longer their "little secret treasure." This behavior, termed "the opposite of bandwagonism," occurs despite most musicians' desire for their art to reach a broad audience and regardless of whether the band continues to produce quality music. "Fans who abandoned a group just because they become popular and then blame the band as if this was some kind of personal betrayal."
**Record Store Day Gougers** : "Record Store Day gougers" are individuals who exploit the annual event by lining up early, or paying others to do so, to purchase limited edition stock specifically created for the day. Their intent is not personal enjoyment but rather to resell these items "for inflated prices on eBay," demonstrating a form of opportunistic entrepreneurship that disrupts the spirit of the event for genuine collectors. "These are the people who line up early on record store day or pay people to line up for them and then buy up all the limited edition stock created just for that day and then sell it all for inflated prices on eBay."
**Misconceptions About Streaming Music Payouts** : Complaints that streaming music companies (e.g., Spotify, RDO, Slack, DZR) "are ripping off artists by paying out so little money" are often misplaced. These payouts are not arbitrary but are "negotiated with a bunch of people in the middle," including record labels, music publishers, and copyright boards. A music stream is a one-time listen, comparable to radio play (where artists receive a fraction of a cent per play), distinct from purchasing a download. Streaming services pay "billions in fees," representing "70% of what they bring in," but the opaque process by which these fees are distributed to artists by intermediaries is the core issue, as no standalone streaming service is currently profitable. "These payouts are not the doing of the streamers."
**Music Industry's "Rule of 30"** : Historically, in periods of industry prosperity, a "rule of 30" often governed record labels' financial model: "If a label released 30 records, 25 would lose money, 3 or 4 would break even, and 1 or 2 would become massive enough to pay for all the others." This illustrates the critical role of highly successful bands in generating the profits necessary to subsidize the development of new acts and cover the losses from less successful releases, maintaining the overall music industry ecosystem. "If a label released 30 records, 25 would lose money, 3 or 4 would break even, and 1 or 2 would become massive enough to pay for all the others."
