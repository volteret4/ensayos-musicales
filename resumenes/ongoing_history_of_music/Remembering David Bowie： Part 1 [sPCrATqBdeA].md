## albums
**The Man Who Sold the World (1970) - Controversial Gown Cover** : This album featured Bowie on the cover wearing a flowing gown designed by British fashion maven Mr. Fish (Michael Fish). This look, displaying androgyny, was not encouraged in 1970, leading to certain stores in the US refusing to display the record and hurting sales until new editions with different covers were rushed out in places like the US and Germany. Inside the album, new songs indicated a significant creative opening for Bowie. "But Bowie didn't care, even as the controversy hurt sales."
**Hunky Dory (1971) - Beginning of the Bowie Effect** : Released 13 months after "The Man Who Sold the World", this album marked a significant turning point in Bowie's career, indicating that "something was happening" and setting the stage for his major impact on music and style. The single "Changes" is from this album. "And here's where it begins to get interesting when it comes to the Bowie effect."
**The Rise and Fall of Ziggy Stardust, and The Spiders from Mars (1972) - Alt Rock Classic** : Described as a classic album essential for all music fans, it introduced the iconic Ziggy Stardust persona. "That's a classic album that every alt rock fan needs in their collection."
**Aladdin Sane (1973) - First Album as Genuine Rock Star** : Released on April 13th, 1973, this was Bowie's first album as a "genuine rock star" and further developed the Ziggy persona into a "higher realm." The title itself, when separated as "A Lad Insane," hinted at Bowie's mental state and was influenced by his half-brother Terry's struggles with mental illness. "The next album, also called Aladdin Sane, was Bowie's first as a genuine rock star."
**Pin Ups (1973) - Collection of Covers** : This album, a collection of cover songs, was also released in 1973 and was described as "Ziggy Karaoke," indicating its continuity with the Ziggy Stardust era and persona. "The pin-ups album, a collection of covers also from 1973, could be called Ziggy Karaoke."
**Diamond Dogs (1974) - Orwellian Concept Album** : Bowie's fifth album in just two years and four months, "Diamond Dogs" was loosely based on George Orwell's novel "1984," demonstrating Bowie's ambitious and unconventional approach to album themes. This prolific period of album releases, each distinct and event-like, greatly contributed to his rise to stardom. "Write an album loosely based on George Orwell's novel 1984? Come on, nobody did that kind of thing, but Bowie did and he pulled it off."
**David Live (1974) - Double Live Album** : This double live album was recorded in October 1974, capturing performances from the ambitious Diamond Dogs tour. Its release meant Bowie had put out five studio albums and one live record in just two years and ten months. "That fall, October 1974, a double live album was taken from the tour that made for five studio albums and one live record in just two years and ten months."
**Young Americans (1975) - Blue-Eyed Plastic Soul Debut** : Released on March 7th, 1975, this album marked a new musical direction for Bowie, showcasing his interest in American soul and R&B, resulting in smoother and funkier sounds. It was his sixth album in just three years and two months and featured the collaboration with John Lennon on the track "Fame." "The result was Young Americans released on March 7th 1975."

## songs
**The Laughing Gnome (1967)** : Released on April 14, 1967, this song represents Bowie's experimental phase in the 1960s where he was trying to find his musical identity. Bowie would later express regret for this particular musical endeavor. "That's the laughing gnome released April 14, 1967."
**Space Oddity (1969)** : This song emerged almost fully formed after Bowie saw Stanley Kubrick's "2001, A Space Odyssey" in 1968, with the character David Bowman potentially inspiring "Major Tom." It was initially rejected but then demoed for RCA Records. Its success was linked to the Apollo moon missions, with the BBC using it for their coverage of the Man Moon Landings. The song reached number five on the charts in the UK, but only number 124 in North America initially, before being re-released in 1973 and reaching number 15 in the US and number 16 in Canada. "That song became David Bowie's first ever hits."
**Changes (1971)** : A single from Bowie's 1971 album "Hunky Dory," this song is highlighted as a point where the "Bowie effect" began to get interesting, signaling his evolving impact on music. "Changes, a single from Bowie's 1971 album, Hunky Dory."
**The Jean Genie (1973)** : This song was partially written about Bowie's "even crazier friend" Iggy Pop, and also drew inspiration from Jean Genet, the French writer, poet, and political activist. "The Jean-Gene, written in part about his new even crazier friend Iggy Pop, and also inspired by Jean-Gene, the French writer, poet and political activist."
**Fame (1975)** : Co-written with John Lennon in about 20 minutes, this song originated from a conversation Bowie and Lennon had about the difficulties of being famous. It features Bowie's lead vocals and John Lennon's background vocals, where Lennon is heard echoing the word "fame" in the verses. Despite Bowie not liking the song, it was a last-minute addition to the "Young Americans" album, released as a single, and became his first ever number one hit in America. "And even though Bowie didn't really like the song it was tacked onto the album at the last minute and then it was released as a single and then it became Bowie's first ever number one hit in America."

## curiosities
**Death and Public Reaction (2016)** : David Bowie died suddenly and surprisingly from cancer in 2016, a fact that was initially met with disbelief and led to weeks of global mourning. His death was unexpected as he had released a critically acclaimed surprise album in 2013 and another acclaimed album just three days before his passing. "David Bowie had suddenly, surprisingly, passed away."
**Broad Cultural Influence** : Bowie's influence extends beyond music, impacting style, image, concert performance, fashion, film, stage, painting, museums, and even career management. His work significantly affected various music genres like rock, punk, new wave, goth, industrial, electronic music, dance, grunge, and indie rock. He is considered one of the most important figures for gay people in pop culture, with tremendous social and political implications, especially in the UK. "Bowie's influence can be found all over rock and punk and new wave, and goth, and industrial, and electronic music, dance, grunge, indie rock."
**Early Career Struggles as David Jones** : Before adopting the name Bowie, David Jones was a saxophone player inspired by Little Richard and American R&B. He was in several unsuccessful bands and struggled to find a style that suited him throughout the 1960s. "Bowie, or David Jones, as he was known back then, which is his real name, tried really, really hard to make something happen with music."
**1964 TV Appearance - League for the Protection of Animal Filaments** : On November 12th, 1964, at the age of 17, David Jones appeared on television as a spokesman for a fictitious group called the "League for the Protection of Animal Filaments." This was a scam designed to get his friends on TV and earn about five pounds each, ostensibly supporting men with long hair. "He appeared as a spokesman for the league for the protection of animal filaments, a totally fictitious support group for men who chose to wear their hair long."
**Name Change to David Bowie** : David Jones changed his name to David Bowie due to the existence of other notable David Joneses, including a poet/painter and Dave Jones of The Monkees. He was inspired by the character Jim Bowie, a real-life American pioneer, soldier, smuggler, and slave trader famous from the Battle of the Alamo, played by Richard Widmark in the movie "The Alamo." The correct pronunciation is "Bowie," not "Bowie." "So henceforth, David Jones began calling himself David Bowie, not Bowie, but Bowie."
**Early Experiments and Mime Training (1967-1969)** : Between 1967 and 1969, Bowie extensively experimented with various endeavors to find his niche. He lived in a Buddhist monastery, took dance lessons, learned mime, worked in a printing shop, and even appeared in an ice cream commercial directed by the then-unknown Ridley Scott. "He experimented with all kinds of different things."
**Inspiration for "Space Oddity" - Stanley Kubrick & Garden Gnomes** : Bowie was heavily influenced by Stanley Kubrick's "2001, A Space Odyssey," which he saw while "stoned out of his mind" in 1968, leading to the creation of "Space Oddity." The character name "Major Tom" might also have been inspired by Tom Major, the father of future British Prime Minister John Major, who had a second career making garden gnomes. "But it's possible that the name of the character in the song was inspired by the story of a failed British trapezoid artist named Tom Major."
**Androgynous Style and Gender Bending** : Building on his early 1960s long hair, Bowie embraced gender bending as a way to gain attention, notably wearing a flowing gown on the cover of "The Man Who Sold the World" in 1970 and in public. His subsequent Ziggy Stardust persona further pushed boundaries with makeup, costumes, and a unique haircut, feminizing the macho male rock and roll star image. "If his long hair of the early 1960s was enough to get him on TV, his style of dress, or more specifically, his dress, Bowie might have a chance at getting some attention."
**Glam Rock Transformation - Haircut and Fashion** : Inspired by Marc Bolan, Iggy Pop, Lou Reed, and The Legendary Stardust Cowboy, Bowie went "all in" on glam rock. A key element was his haircut, replicated and modified from a model in a magazine wearing Kansai Yamamoto clothes, razor-cut at the sides and back, backcombed into a "puffy fluff ball," and dyed reddish-orange at Yamamoto's suggestion. This, combined with makeup and costuming, transformed him into the "elfin cute boy in the dress" to something "never before seen in music." "Bowie fun to picture of a model in a magazine."
**Ziggy Stardust - Theatrical Rock Star Persona** : Bowie created Ziggy Stardust as a "doomed pansexual alien," using his acting and mime lessons to embody this character fully. This allowed him to sing, say, and do things he couldn't as "plain David Bowie." Ziggy's impact on rock in 1972 was immense, challenging the prevailing jeans and T-shirts aesthetic with makeup, costumes, and theatrical concerts featuring choreography, transforming rock into a full, immersive experience. "This idea of coming up with a pre-packaged synthetic ultra glamorous, ultra theatrical rock star."
**"Coming Out" Statement and LGBTQ+ Impact (1972)** : In January 1972, Bowie gave a "flippin' quote" to a British music paper, stating he had "always been gay." This statement, unthinkable for a celebrity at the time, given the legal context of homosexuality in Britain (decriminalized in England/Wales only in 1967, still illegal in Scotland/Northern Ireland for years, and military ban), became a bomb. Despite Bowie later recanting claims of being gay or bisexual, this declaration resonated with thousands who were afraid to come out, giving them courage. "This coming out struck a chord with thousands of people who wanted to do the same thing but were too afraid."
**Influence on British Punk - Sex Pistols Connection** : Bowie's "outsider-ness," short, sharp songs, wild fashion sense, and defiant attitude endeared him to alienated working-class youth who would become the first generation of British punks. His friendships with Iggy Pop and Lou Reed (of The Velvet Underground) also made him "cool" in their eyes. Famously, on July 2nd, 1973, after Bowie's final Ziggy Stardust tour show at the Hammersmith Odeon, a young Steve Jones (later guitarist for the Sex Pistols) stole microphones and amps from the venue, gear that would be used to start his new band. "A young thief named Steve Jones somehow got inside and helped himself to whatever he could carry out, mostly some microphones and some amps."
**Killing Off Ziggy Stardust (1973)** : At the end of the 1973 Ziggy Stardust tour, Bowie announced to the audience that it would be "the last show that we'll ever do." While the public thought he was retiring, Bowie intended to retire the Ziggy persona to make way for a new one, Aladdin Sane. This willingness to "kill himself off" and constantly reinvent was admired by punks. "What he really meant though was that he was killing off Ziggy."
**Aladdin Sane Persona and Half-Brother's Struggles** : The "Aladdin Sane" persona (a play on "A Lad Insane") reflected Bowie's mental state, influenced by the struggles of his adored half-brother, Terry. Terry suffered from manic depression and schizophrenia, requiring hospitalization, and later tragically committed suicide by lying down in front of a train a little over a decade later. "Not only had the Ziggy experience been crazy for everybody involved, but he also had a half brother he adored named Terry, who was suffering terribly with mental issues."
**Prolific Output and Reinvention (1973-1975)** : Between 1973 and 1975, Bowie released an astonishing six albums in just three years and two months, including "Aladdin Sane," "Pin Ups," "Diamond Dogs," "David Live," and "Young Americans." Each record was different, making every release an event and keeping audiences guessing what Bowie would do next. "Album after album, single after single character after character two or after two or it was absolutely incredible."
**Groundbreaking Diamond Dogs Tour (1974)** : The tour supporting the "Diamond Dogs" album was one of the most ambitious and expensive rock had ever seen. It featured elaborate sets, costume changes, choreography, and a cherry-picker boom that took Bowie out over the audience during "Space Oddity." This pushed the basic rock and roll show into unprecedented theatrical territory. "It was hugely expensive and very complex for its day and no one had ever seen anything like it."
**Transition to "Blue-Eyed Plastic Soul"** : During the "Diamond Dogs" tour, Bowie began transitioning from his "spaceman" image to a more elegant, sophisticated look with shorter hair and tailored suits. This paralleled a shift in his musical interests towards American soul and R&B, leading to the smoother, funkier sound of "Young Americans." This endorsement of black music encouraged many of his fans to explore those genres. "Bowie became less space man and more elegant, shorter hair, tailored suits, an air of sophistication."
**Mainstream Crossover Success (1975)** : By 1975, with the "Young Americans" album, Bowie had become a global superstar whose music transcended genre boundaries. His songs were played on both Top 40 and rock radio stations, and he was considered cool in punk clubs. Tracks like "Fame" or "Young Americans" were even mixed in among dance music in disco clubs. "He was one of the biggest stars in the world."
**Personal Struggles with Fame and Substance Abuse** : Despite his immense success, Bowie's personal life in the mid-1970s was fraught with heavy drinking and a powerful cocaine addiction. He also experienced paranoia, worrying about witches and UFOs, which contributed to his increasing mysteriousness and public allure. "But personally Bowie was in terrible shape. He was drinking a lot and it developed a powerful appetite for cocaine and he was going a little insane too."

# curiosity

**Early 1970s Rock Aesthetics** : In 1972, mainstream rock was characterized by a casual aesthetic of jeans, T-shirts, and long hair. Dressing like a hippie was the typical way for youth to annoy their parents. This context made David Bowie's Ziggy Stardust persona, with its makeup, costumes, and elaborate hair, radically new and impactful. "In 1972, rock was about jeans and t-shirts and long hair."
**Homosexuality Laws in Britain (Pre-1972)** : Until 1967, being homosexual was a crime in Britain, punishable by charges and imprisonment under the Sexual Offenses Act. Even after decriminalization, new conditions applied only to people over 21 in England and Wales, remaining a crime in Scotland until 1980 and Northern Ireland until 1982. Being a gay soldier was also a crime, and arrests for "gross indecency" tripled after 1967, perpetuating fear despite legal changes. "Until 1967, it was a crime to be a homosexual and Britain."
**Apollo Moon Missions and Cultural Impact (Late 1960s)** : The era of the Apollo moon missions provided a unique cultural backdrop for David Bowie's song "Space Oddity." The BBC's decision to use the song as a theme for their coverage of the Man Moon Landings significantly contributed to its initial chart success in the UK. "This was the era of the Apollo moon missions."
