# curiosity

**Nirvana Nevermind Cover (1991) – Swimming Baby Spencer Elden; Underwater Birth Idea; Photoshopped Fish Hook; Dad's Handprint Visible** : Kurt Cobain came up with the Nevermind cover concept inspired by a documentary on underwater births he and Dave Grohl watched during recording sessions, combined with his memories of supervising preschooler swims as a YMCA lifeguard in Aberdeen, Washington. A stock photo was found but the photographer demanded $7,500 per year while the album remained in print — so a photographer named Kurt Wettle (ex-Navy, previously had experience blowing things up with dynamite, also a mail-order minister who had married the baby's parents) was hired instead. Multiple babies were brought to the Pasadena Aquatic Center and thrown into the pool while Wettle shot pictures. Four-month-old Spencer Elden (son of Rick and Renata Elden of Eagle Rock, California) was selected. The fish hook with a dollar bill was added later via Photoshop — Kurt's idea. If you look at the right side of Spencer's rib cage, you can see his dad's handprint from where he was being held before the toss. Spencer was paid $150 for his troubles. At last word he still lives in the LA area, has a platinum award for Nevermind over his bed, and is not much of a Nirvana fan — the White Stripes are his thing. "Spencer, he was paid $150 for all his troubles."

**U2 The Joshua Tree Cover (1986) – Anton Corbijn; 20-Minute Rest Stop in Mojave Desert; Joshua Tree Died and Fell ~2000** : While finishing The Joshua Tree, U2 hired rock photographer Anton Corbijn and flew to Los Angeles in December 1986 to shoot in the Mojave Desert near Death Valley, driving around in a hired bus and staying in crappy motels. When someone asked the bus driver about the strange cactus-like plants, he said they were called Joshua trees — Bono thought it was a great name for a tree and they stopped for 20 minutes to take photos. When examining the contact sheets, those 20-minute rest-stop shots were judged the best, somehow capturing and encapsulating everything the band was about. U2 never revealed the specific tree's location to prevent vandalism. Despite precautions, the tree died and fell over sometime in fall 2000, about 200 years old. Intrepid fans have since found its remains; some leave a ring of stones in the shape of a heart. "When it came time to examine the contact sheets of all the photos that have been taken, the shots from that 20 minute rest stop were judged to be the best."

**The Killers Sam's Town Cover (2006) – Anton Corbijn; Dead Stuffed Longhorn Sheep; Damaged Beauty Queen Production Assistant** : The Killers hired Anton Corbijn again for Sam's Town. Corbijn, put in a similar desert headspace as with U2, wanted a dead sheep for the shoot — the Longhorn is the official sheep of Nevada, and a live one would be too camera-shy and vain. He rented a dead, stuffed Longhorn from the local museum. Corbijn also wanted someone who looked like a "damaged beauty queen" and found production assistant Felice LaZay at the Las Vegas recording studio where the Killers recorded the album. He took Felice and the dead stuffed sheep to an abandoned mobile home in the blazing desert and photographed them. The best shot of the dozens taken became the album cover. "So he rented one, a dead one, a stuffed one from the local museum."

**Hypnosis Design Group – Pink Floyd Dark Side of the Moon, Led Zeppelin, Genesis; Storm Thorgerson's Repeating Motif** : Hypnosis was a British design company that became famous for album artwork including Pink Floyd's Dark Side of the Moon (the prism), Led Zeppelin's Houses of the Holy, Presence, and In Through the Out Door, plus Genesis, Procol Harum, Paul McCartney, and many others. One of the three principals was Storm Thorgerson, who created the Dark Side of the Moon cover (widely considered the greatest piece of album artwork ever). When the company broke up in 1983, Thorgerson went solo and designed artwork for everyone from Anthrax to the Offspring to Ween. His signature "repeating image motif" — a beach with a silhouetted door leading through the ground into an alternate universe — appeared on Ian Thornley's Come Again album (2004). "Storm was the guy who came up with the Dark Side of the Moon cover, which many people still consider to be the greatest piece of album artwork ever."

**Peter Saville – Joy Division Unknown Pleasures (Pulsar CP1919 Signal); New Order Blue Monday (Lost £9,000 on Every Copy)** : Peter Saville from Manchester became the official go-to designer for Factory Records. His inspirations were classical arts, religious imagery, and the industrial world. Joy Division's Unknown Pleasures album cover shows 100 consecutive pulses from pulsar CP1919 — a dying star in the center of the Dumbbell Nebula in the constellation Vulpecula, essentially screaming radio waves. The Blue Monday 12-inch (1983) was designed to look like a computer floppy disk, with no band name or song title visible, and a series of colored blocks encoding a message that could only be cracked with a key included in New Order's next album Power Corruption and Lies. The code was supposed to say "Blue Monday" but due to an error it actually reads "X96." The sleeve was so expensive to produce that Factory Records lost 5p (about £0.05 / 30p accounting for 1983 values) on every single copy sold — and Blue Monday went on to sell 3 million copies, the biggest-selling 12-inch single ever, generating a net loss of approximately £9,000 on the artwork alone. "New Order and Factory actually lost 30 pints on every single copy they sold."

**Coldplay X&Y Cover (2005) – Baudot Code; Supposed to Say "X and Y" but Actually Reads "X96" Due to Encoding Error** : Coldplay hired design company Tappin Gofton for X&Y after liking their work on the Chemical Brothers' Push the Button. Tappin Gofton researched Baudot code — a binary telegraph code invented in 1874 by Emil Baudot as International Telegraph Alphabet Number One — and designed the cover using colored blocks representing Baudot encoded letters. The colors were their addition; original Baudot code was black and white. The design was intended to spell out "X and Y." However, somebody got the encoding wrong — to a Baudot expert, the cover actually reads "X96." "To a Baudoe code pro, the album is actually apparently called X96."

**Beastie Boys Paul's Boutique Cover (1989) – Photo Is at 99 Rivington Street in Manhattan, Not Brooklyn; Lee's Sportswear, Not Paul's Boutique** : The cover of Paul's Boutique, photographed by Nathaniel Hornblower (Adam Yauch's alter ego), shows a shop supposedly in Brooklyn — but the photo was actually taken at 99 Rivington Street at the corner of Ludlow on the Lower East Side of Manhattan, close to where the Beastie Boys first rehearsed at 59 Christie Street near Hester and Canal. The store in the photo is actually called Lee's Sportswear. You can spot an Al Green record, a record by the Modern Lovers, and Dwight Gooden's New York Mets jersey hanging in the image. Today a coffee shop a few feet away is called Paul's Boutique — named after the album, not the other way around. "The shop in the photo is actually a store called Lee's Sportswear."

**Album Cover Art History – Plain Brown Wrapper to Art Form; First Color Record Jacket in WWII; Sgt. Pepper as Milestone** : Early record covers were plain brown wrappers whose sole purpose was to identify contents. The first color record jacket appeared during World War II. By the late 1950s–early 1960s, most LP records featured portraits of the artist. As the 1960s progressed, artwork concepts became more ambitious, culminating with the Beatles' Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band package. The album cover became essential, spawning design companies like Hypnosis. The 12x12-inch LP gave designers an enormous canvas; with gatefold sleeves, designers had 6–8 square feet to work with. "From the middle 1960s, album art just exploded."

**Death of Album Art – CD Shrank Canvas from 12-Inch to 5-Inch; Now Digital Means No Physical Canvas at All** : The 12x12-inch canvas of the LP shrank to the 5x5-inch booklet of the CD, though CDs allowed multi-page booklets to compensate. Now, as music goes fully digital and album sales continue to decline, the physical canvas for album art is essentially gone. Hundreds of billions of dollars that once went to music now go to DVDs, video games, streaming TV, smartphones, and other entertainment, leading to a dramatically declining need for album cover art. "We could be witnessing the death of an art that extends back nearly a century. And that would be sad."
