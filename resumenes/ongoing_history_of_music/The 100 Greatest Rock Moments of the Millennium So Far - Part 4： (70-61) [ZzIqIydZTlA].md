# concert - Polaris Music Prize

## curiosities
**Establishment and Purpose (2006)** : The Polaris Music Prize was established in Canada in 2006, mirroring international awards like Scandinavia's Polar Music Prize and the UK's Mercury Prize. Its goal is to identify the "best album of the year" without considering genre or commercial success, aiming for a true meritocracy focused solely on musical merit. A jury of music professionals outside the label system nominates hundreds of albums, which are then narrowed down to a long list of 40, then a short list of 10. In September, a sequestered jury determines the winner, who receives $50,000, while short-listed artists get $3,000. It intentionally generates controversy and encourages listeners to discover new music. "Since 2006, we've had the Polaris music prize in Canada. The goal of each of these music prizes is to determine the best album of the year without any regard to genre or commercial success."

# curiosity

**90s Music as Top Nostalgia Driver** : Music from the 1990s has emerged as the number one source for nostalgia, surpassing the public's fascination with the 80s, 70s, and 60s. This trend is so strong that even young music fans who were not alive in the 90s have fiercely embraced the music from that era, making it "fresh and top of mind" for them. "It's been so long since the 90s, for example, that music of that era is number one for nostalgia. It clipsing the public's fascination with the 80s, the 70s, and the 60s."
**Streaming Surpassing Physical Sales (2016)** : In 2016, streaming officially became the leading revenue generator for the American recorded music business, surpassing all other forms of music distribution combined for the first time. This marked a watershed moment following years of disruption from illegal file sharing and the rise of iTunes, as streaming offered a convenient, low-cost, and legal way to consume music. By 2023, streaming accounted for 67% of the industry's total revenue, signaling an irreversible shift away from physical products, though vinyl has seen a comeback with "nice fat margins." "In 2016, there was a tipping point. For the first time, streaming generated more money for the American recorded music business than all other forms of music distribution put together."
**Concert Nostalgia Boom and Legacy Acts** : The resurgence of interest in "heritage acts" or "legacy bands" from the 1960s, 70s, and 80s has led to a "Concert Nostalgia Boom." These artists now tour in front of very large audiences, with fans willing to pay high prices to see them. This phenomenon is partly driven by the recorded music industry's failure to cultivate a comparable number of new arena- and stadium-filling acts, forcing promoters like Live Nation and AEG to rely on older acts who appeal to an older audience with more disposable income. "Promoters like Live Nation and AEG have had to turn to these older acts who appeal largely to older audiences. We have more disposable income and who are more than willing to relieve their youth one more time."
**Democratization of Music Creation and Distribution Leading to Glut** : The barrier to entry for releasing music has drastically lowered due to cheap and powerful home recording equipment and easy uploading to streaming platforms, YouTube, and TikTok. This offers "instant global distribution" and cuts out traditional "cultural gatekeepers" like record labels, radio, and magazines, which previously filtered new music, allowing only about 2,500 new albums per year. While promoting democratization, this ease of entry has resulted in a massive "glut of music," with over 100,000 songs uploaded daily. By early 2025, approximately 202 million songs will be available, but an estimated 25% (over 50 million) have never been heard, illustrating a diluted and cheaper music consumption experience. "Today, though, the barrier to entry for anyone wanting to release music is so low that anyone can do it. Home recording equipment is cheap and powerful, and uploading your music to the streaming music platforms can be done with a push of a button."
**The Loudness Wars (Early 2000s Peak)** : The "loudness wars" was a regrettable trend, peaking in the early 2000s, where songs were mastered with excessive compression to make them sound louder and "pop more" on radios and speakers. This technological race in the digital era flattened the dynamic range of original recordings, boosting quiet parts to be as loud as the loudest parts, creating an illusion of volume but resulting in distortion, lack of clarity, and significant loss of detail, leading to listener fatigue. Fortunately, pushback from organizations like Dynamic Range Day and streaming platforms (Apple Music, Spotify, YouTube) normalizing playback levels has reduced the incentive for loud mastering, though some "audio atrocities" like CD editions of Californication and Metallica's St. Anger remain. "The loudness wars refers to mastering songs with tons of compression, making them sound louder than they actually are."
**Mass Extinction of Big Chain Record Stores** : Starting around 2000, the recorded music industry experienced a "mass extinction event" with the widespread closure of big chain record stores globally. This was driven by the public's shift from physical media to online music access. Major chains like Tower Records (closed all but 1-2 Japan stores by 2006), the Musicland Group (including Suncoast and Sam Goody, bankrupt in 2006), Sam The Record Man (defunct by 2007 except one store), and Virgin Megastores (gone by 2009) disappeared. Record sections also vanished from Best Buy and department stores. The only "bright spots" were HMV's resurrection by Canadian entrepreneur Doug Putnam (who also bought Sunrise Records) and Rough Trade Records maintaining several locations. Most physical record retailing now belongs to independent stores. "This really was a mass extinction event as people moved away from physical media over to online access for music."
**Music Industry's Environmental Reckoning** : The music industry, recognized as a major polluter and energy consumer (through tours, non-biodegradable plastics, and the carbon footprint of streaming), has slowly begun an "environmental reckoning" and is moving towards more eco-friendly practices. This includes the emergence of "green records" made from sustainable materials, green platforms aiming to reduce energy consumption, and recording studios adopting high-efficiency gear, carbon-neutral methods, and sustainable energy sources. Remote collaboration now reduces travel needs. Concerts and festivals are increasingly powered by renewable energy, encourage waste reduction, and use digital tickets. Artists are selling merchandise made from organic/recycled fabrics with non-harmful dyes, engaging fans on environmental issues, and organizing benefit concerts. The touring industry is adopting biodiesel/sustainable fuels, electric vehicles, carbon offsets, and local sourcing to minimize its environmental impact. "The music industry is a major polluter and a major devourer of energy. The number of pollutants created by a single tour is incredible."
**De-rockification of Major Music Festivals** : Major music festivals like Lollapalooza and Coachella have significantly evolved away from their original rock roots, particularly starting around 2008. Lollapalooza, initially a full-blown touring festival of alternative and rock bands in the 1990s, is now primarily a pop and hip-hop event. Similarly, Coachella, which began in 1999 with a focus on rock, shifted towards pop and hip-hop headliners due to a cultural shift in America, where these genres became dominant among the festival-going demographic. While both festivals still include token rock appearances, their primary lineup strategies cater to youth-driven preferences for pop and hip-hop to ensure ticket sales. "The evolution of two major major music festivals away from their rock roots. When La La Palosa was first conceived in 1990, it was a full-blown touring festival of Weirdos, featuring the best in left-of-center and alternative bands."
**The Me Too Reckoning in the Music Industry** : The music industry has undergone a slow but significant "Me Too reckoning" since around 2006, addressing decades of unchecked predatory behavior, sexual assault, and misogyny. Historically, the industry's "sex, drugs, and rock and roll" ethos allowed abusers to operate with impunity, shielded by wealth, privilege, and "fixers." The Me Too movement, which gained traction on MySpace around 2006 and spread across social media, empowered women to share their stories and expose abusers. This led to perpetrators being held accountable through legal action, "cancellation" of careers, increased attention to challenges faced by female performers, and efforts to create safer spaces. The proliferation of smartphones and video recording also made bad behavior riskier. While significant progress has been made compared to earlier eras, further work is still needed to dismantle deeply ingrained systemic issues. "It's the slow, me too reckoning within the music industry. For decades, predators, sexual assaults, and other awful people got away with the craziest and most vile things when it came to the treatment of women, stuff that goes far beyond, you know, usual misogyny."
