# artist - John Philip Sousa

## songs
**The Liberty Bell (1893)** : This march, composed by American John Philip Sousa, is recognized as the theme from Monty Python's Flying Circus. It represents military music, which was highly popular in its era, characterized by big brass instruments and two-four rhythms with multiple snare and bass drums, making it "powerful, rousing music." "So, yeah, that's the theme from Monty Python's Flying Circus, but it's actually a march composed in 1893 by American John Philip Susa."

**Marches (Composed over 130)** : John Philip Sousa was the composer of over 130 marches, along with many other musical pieces that continue to be used today. His work exemplifies the popular military music style of the late 19th and early 20th centuries. "Susa was the composer of over 130 of these marches, along with many other pieces of music that are still being used today."

## curiosities
**Belief in Live Music Only** : John Philip Sousa strongly believed that music should only be experienced live. He considered the new talking machines of the turn of the 20th century, such as gramophones and phonographs that played recordings, to be "nothing short of evil." "And he believed that music was something that should only be experienced live."

**"The Metis of Mechanical Music" Article (1906)** : In 1906, John Philip Sousa wrote an article for Appleton's magazine titled "The Metis of Mechanical Music." In it, he expressed his strong disapproval of mechanical music devices, stating that they "substitute for human skill, intelligence and soul" and predicted a "marked deterioration in American music and musical taste." "In 1906, he wrote an article for Appleton's magazine, and the title of the article was The Metis of Mechanical Music."

**Opposition to Talking Machines** : John Philip Sousa actively campaigned against recording technology, advocating for a ban on these machines and the recordings they played. He even appeared before the U.S. Congress to argue that talking machines would "ruin the artistic development of music in this country," and expressed concern that they would eliminate the "vocal court." "He even appeared before the U.S. Congress to make his argument. The talking machines are going to ruin the artistic development of music in this country."

**Prediction of Musical Deterioration** : Sousa foresaw that recorded music would discourage people from learning music and lead to widespread unemployment among musicians. He also believed that a frozen recorded performance would become the standard, ending improvisation and evolution in music, making it "stale and boring." "I foresee a marked deterioration in American music and musical taste, an interruption of the musical development of the country and a host of other injuries to music in its artistic manifestations."

**Refusal to Conduct Recordings** : For many years, John Philip Sousa proudly refused to conduct any orchestras in recording situations. He famously stated, "I have never been in the Gramophone company's office in my life," demonstrating his steadfast opposition to the recording industry until his death in 1932. "And for years, Susa refused to conduct any orchestras in any recording situations, proudly saying that I have never been in the Gramophone company's office in my life."
