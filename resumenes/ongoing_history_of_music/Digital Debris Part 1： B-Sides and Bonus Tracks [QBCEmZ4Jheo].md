## curiosities
**Stance Against Inferior B-sides** : The Beatles consciously chose not to put inferior material on the B-sides of their singles. They aimed to provide fans with maximum value, leading to the creation of what became known as double A-sides, where both tracks were considered equally important. "The Beatles, for example, came to issue putting something inferior on the B side of their singles. They wanted to give fans as much value as they possibly could."
**Total Double A-sides (26)** : The band released a total of 26 double A-sides during their career. Each side of these singles achieved chart hit status independently, demonstrating the quality and importance placed on both tracks. "The Beatles released a total of 26 double A sides and each side was a chart hit on its own."

# label - Columbia Records

## curiosities
**Pioneering Double-Sided Discs (1908)** : Columbia Records was the first record company to introduce double-sided discs in 1908, a revolutionary development in the early recorded music industry. This innovation significantly increased the audio capacity of flat discs compared to earlier cylinders. "in 1908, Columbia Records became the first record company to offer double-sided discs. This was radical and revolutionary."
**Involvement in Format War (Late 1940s)** : In the late 1940s, Columbia Records was a key player in a "format war" with RCA. Columbia introduced the 12-inch 33 1/3 RPM long-playing (LP) album, designed to hold many songs per side, in contrast to RCA's 7-inch 45 RPM single format. "This is when we encountered the format war between Columbia's 12-inch 33- to 3rd RPM long-playing album that held lots of songs per side."

# curiosity

**Digital Music Dominance and Industry Shift** : The music industry has transitioned deeply into a digital world, where virtually every song is instantly available via streaming services like Spotify, Apple Music, YouTube, SoundCloud, and Bandcamp. This shift has eliminated the complex and expensive overheads of manufacturing, warehousing, transporting, and distributing physical products, making digital distribution significantly more efficient and profitable. "Now with streaming, there is almost, now with streaming, there's no physical product. All the expensive overhead and those big fixed costs are gone. Digital distribution is so much more efficient and profitable on every single level."
**Decline of Physical Music Formats** : The demand for physical music formats such as records, CDs, and tapes is shrinking, becoming a niche market. Generations of music listeners have grown up without ever visiting a record store or handling physical media, experiencing music as ephemeral digital files. "While there will always probably be a demand for music on physical formats, it's going to shrink and shrink until it's just a very nichey thing."
**Invention of Recording Cylinder (Late 19th Century)** : The recorded music industry began in the late 19th century with recordings on cylinders, invented by Thomas Edison. These cylinders, about the size of a Coke can, could hold approximately two minutes of audio. "When the recorded music industry began in the late 19th century, recordings came on cylinders but the size of a Coke can. It could hold around two minutes of audio and this was the invention of Thomas Edison."
**Invention of Flat Rotating Disc (Late 19th Century)** : Emil Berliner introduced the flat rotating disc, which significantly advanced recorded music technology. It offered advantages over cylinders, including easier mass production, greater audio capacity (over three minutes per side compared to two), and crucially, two playable sides, enabling over three times the audio of an Edison cylinder. "Everything changed when Emil Berliner came up with the idea of a flat rotating disc. It had several advantages over the cylinder."
**Obsolescence of Cylinders (1914)** : By the time World War I began in 1914, cylinders had largely become obsolete as a recording format. This was primarily due to the flat disc's ability to hold more music, a key advantage that drove its adoption. "By the time World War I broke out in 1914, cylinders were obsolete, largely because discs could hold more music."
**Format War of the Late 1940s** : The late 1940s saw a significant "format war" in the music industry between Columbia Records' 12-inch 33 1/3 RPM long-playing album, designed for multiple songs per side, and RCA's 7-inch 45 RPM record, which typically featured one song per side. "This is when we encountered the format war between Columbia's 12-inch 33- to 3rd RPM long-playing album that held lots of songs per side, and RCA's 7-inch 45 RPM record, which stuck with the standard one song per side."
**Radio's Shift to Pre-Recorded Music (Post-TV Rise)** : With the rise of television, radio experienced a programming deficit as many stars and shows transitioned to TV. To save money and compete, radio stations began playing more pre-recorded music, predominantly 7-inch singles, which became the standard currency for radio airplay. "So to save money and to find a way of competing with television, radio started playing more and more pre-recorded music. In other words, records. And the records they chose to play most often were the 7-inch single."
**Evolution of A/B Side Designation** : Initially, 7-inch singles treated both sides equally, labeled A and B merely for serial numbering (e.g., 2830-A, 28030-B) with no preference. By the early 1960s, as 45 RPM singles outsold albums, record labels began designating the song with the greatest commercial potential as the A-side for radio airplay priority, while the B-side became a placeholder. "But as the relationship between the recorded music industry and radio developed, the label started choosing which songs they wanted to be played. And by the early 1960s, which is when RPM singles far outsold albums, the song that was to be given priority by the record company and hopefully by the radio station, because it had the greatest commercial potential, was increasingly dedicated as the A side."
**B-Side as Placeholder or Orphan Song** : The B-side, also known as the flip side, was typically considered to have less commercial potential than the A-side. It might be a deep album cut, an orphan song with no other home, or simply a track chosen to fill the available space on the record. "The other side of the record, the B side, also known as the flip side, was usually, but not always, just a placeholder, something to fill the available space."
**"B/W" and "C/W" Terminology** : The abbreviations "B/W" (Backed With) and "C/W" (Coupled With) were used on record labels to indicate the B-side track of a single. These were alternative ways of specifying the secondary song on a two-sided record. "That B Stroke W stands for backed with. It's another way of saying this is the B side. You might also see in rare instances C Stroke W, which stands for coupled with, which makes the same thing."
**Stereo/Mono 7-inch Singles** : In the 1960s, as stereo recordings grew popular, some 7-inch singles featured the same song on both sides: a stereo version on the A-side and a mono version on the B-side. This dual-format release catered to listeners with different record player capabilities and allowed for servicing both mono AM and stereo FM radio stations with a single physical product. "The A song was the stereo recording and the B side was the mono version for those who had yet to upgrade their record players. This also served the purpose of servicing both mono AM radio stations and stereo FM stations with the same piece of plastic."
**Singles as Promotional Tools (Early 1970s)** : By the early 1970s, albums surpassed singles as the dominant selling format. Record companies then repurposed 45 RPM singles primarily as promotional tools to boost album sales. B-sides were often chosen specifically to be unsuitable for radio play, ensuring DJs focused on the designated A-side ("single," "consensus track," or "plug track"). "By the time we got to the early 1970s, albums had overtaken singles as the biggest selling format. This prompted record companies to change tactics. 45 RPM singles were used as promotional tools for selling albums."
**Cassette Singles (1980s-1990s)** : Cassette singles, the tape equivalent of 7-inch 45 RPM vinyl singles, were prevalent in the 1980s and 90s. These often adhered to the A-side and B-side tradition, mirroring their vinyl counterparts. "Not Cassette singles, the tape version of the 7 inch 45 RPM single that was around for a while in the 80s and 90s, often had an A side and a B side in keeping with the vinyl tradition."
**12-inch Singles / Maxi Singles** : 12-inch singles were records the size of an album, played at either 33 1/3 RPM or 45 RPM. Their larger surface area allowed for the inclusion of multiple songs on the B-side, beyond just the main hit on the A-side. These releases were sometimes referred to as Maxi singles. "Then we have 12 inch singles. These were records the size of an album. They might play it 33 in a third RPM or 45 RPM. The A side would feature what the label hoped would be the hit. Well the B side featured one or more songs that were there. You had 12 inches of violins instead of 7. Why not throw it up? Some refer to these releases as Maxi singles."
**Emergence of Bonus Tracks** : The concept of a "bonus track" evolved from the B-side, particularly with the advent of 12-inch singles that offered more space for additional songs. These tracks were considered extra content beyond the main single and sometimes included valuable, non-album material. "But it was also a new kind of B side, something that we began to call a bonus track."
**CD Singles (Mid-1980s)** : CD singles emerged in the mid-1980s as CDs gained popularity, functioning similarly to 7-inch vinyl singles as counterparts to full albums. They could hold significantly more music than vinyl singles and came in two sizes: the standard 5-inch (CD5) and a smaller 3-inch version that often required an adapter and largely disappeared by the mid-1990s. "CD singles came in two sizes. The most common was the same size as a standard compact disc and was sometimes called a CD 5 because they're 5 inches in diameter."
**UK CD Single Market** : CD singles were particularly popular in the UK, largely due to the high price of full compact discs compared to other markets. Megastores like Virgin and HMV on Oxford Street in London stocked numerous CD singles, some containing 3-6 extra songs, blurring the lines with EPs but sold as singles. "CD singles were especially big in the UK because the price of a full compact disc was really, really high compared to other markets around the world."
**Music Industry's Strategy to Kill CD Singles (Late 1990s)** : In the late 1990s, the music industry actively worked to suppress the CD single format. The strategy was to force consumers to buy full albums, even if they only desired one song, leading to the "full price or nothing" mentality. "in the late 90s, the music industry conspired to kill the CD single. They did not want to put up music this way anymore. The thinking became, what, you want this one song? And buy the full CD."
**Impact of File Sharing (Napster Era)** : The music industry's attempt to eliminate CD singles and compel full album purchases contributed significantly to the rise of file-sharing programs like Napster. Consumers, unable to easily acquire individual songs legally, turned to digital platforms for single-track acquisition. "And that was also a very big reason people jumped into file sharing when programs like Napster came along."
**Digital Music as the "Major Killer" of CD Singles** : The increasing consumer preference for listening to and acquiring individual songs digitally is identified as the primary reason for the decline in sales of all compact discs, including CD singles. Most labels abandoned the CD single format completely around 2010. "But the major killer of the CD single has been digital music. Consumers are more and more concerned about listening to and to a lesser extent acquiring individual songs. That is killing sales of all compact discs, including the CD single."
**Metaphorical A-sides and B-sides in Digital Releases** : Even in the digital era, some artists and labels, such as The Smashing Pumpkins and MGMT, metaphorically refer to concurrent digital track releases as A-sides and B-sides. This often involves releasing two songs simultaneously, with a subtle hint that one track is deemed slightly more important or commercially viable than the other. "And there is still some momentum from the old days with artists and labels referring to releases as A-sides and B-sides in a metaphorical way more than anything else."
**Fading Music Discovery Through B-sides** : The traditional method of discovering hidden gems by flipping over a record's B-side or finding bonus tracks on imported CD singles is disappearing in the all-digital, streaming world. This shift represents a loss of a specific type of unexpected musical discovery. "There's no more finding a song like Yellow Lead Better from Pearl Jam, which was originally a bonus track on an imported version of the Jeremy CD single."
