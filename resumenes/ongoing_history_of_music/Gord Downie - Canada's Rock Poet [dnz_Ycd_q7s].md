## albums
**Coke Machine Glow (2001) - Debut Solo Album & Poetry Book** : This record was released as a package alongside a book of poetry with the same name. This two-for-one deal made the book one of the best-selling poetry books by a Canadian, selling 10,000 copies in the first 36 hours. When the book and album were later separated, the book sold another 10,000 copies on its own. "It sold 10,000 copies in the first 36 hours."
**The Grand Bounce (2009) - Solo Album** : This solo record included the song "The East Wind," which Gord Downie recorded with the band The Country of Miracles. A track-by-track preview of the album highlighted its rich lyrical content, filled with references and tributes to figures like Al Purdy, Alexander Solzhenitsyn, and even a "Custerian" in the Battle of Little Bighorn. "It's also on a solo record entitled The Grand Bounce from 2009."
**Secret Path (2016) - Last Album Released While Alive** : This project was announced immediately after Gord Downie's cancer diagnosis and consisted of an album, a graphic novel, and an animated film. It told the harrowing story of 12-year-old Chanie Wenjack and the residential school system. Downie performed the album in full at Roy Thompson Hall in Toronto on October 21, 2016, which was his last full concert performance. "It was an album, Gord's last while he was alive, a graphic novel, and an animated film that told the story of Cheney and the residential school system."

The second verse incorporates a quote from Walter van Tilburg Clark, author of "The Ox-Bow Incident," which says, "night is like a room. It makes the little things inside your head too important." ## curiosities
**Early Life and Education** : Gord Downie was the fourth of five children, born to Edgar (a traveling salesman turned real estate developer) and Lorna, in Amherst View, Ontario, just west of Kingston on the other side of Collins Bay. He attended Kingston Collegiate Vocational High, where former students included Johnny McDonnell, Robertson Davies, and Don Cherry. He later enrolled at Queens University, studying political science and film studies, and developed a deep interest in reading poetry. "Gord was from Amherst View, Ontario, just west of Kingston on the other side of Collins Bay."
**Hockey Goalie and Harry Sinden Connection** : Downie was a goalie for Amherst View hockey teams that were good enough to win a provincial title one year. His godfather was Harry Sinden, a Stanley Cup-winning coach for the Boston Bruins and head coach for Team Canada in the 1972 series with the Soviets. This connection, stemming from Sinden's real estate partnership with Downie's father, Edgar, explains why Gord wore number four (Bobby Orr's number) when he played hockey and provided background for The Tragically Hip's song "Fireworks." "His godfather was Harry Sinden, a Stanley Cup winning coach for the Boston Bruins, which explains why Gord wore number four, Bobby Horace's number when he played hockey."
**Poetic Influences and Reading List** : Gord Downie deeply engaged with poetry, reading works by Leonard Cohen, Al Purdy, and Irvin Layton. His novel reading list included books by Margaret Atwood and he also read Northrop Frye, a highly influential 20th-century Canadian literary critic. "He also really got into reading poetry. Leonard Cohen, L. Perty, Irvin Layton."
**Lyric Writing Process** : Downie carried a notebook filled with fragments of conversations, observations, and ideas. When The Tragically Hip jammed, he would flip through these notes to find lines that resonated with the band's groove and rhythm. He often left the meaning of his lyrics open to personal interpretation, viewing this as a hallmark of a true poet, believing they should be open to all kinds of personal interpretation and pathways to certain truths. "When the band jammed, Gord flipped through these books until he found a line or two that felt really good with what the band was playing in terms of groove and rhythm."
**Hugh MacLean Reference in "Fully Completely"** : In a track from the 1992 album "Fully Completely," Downie paraphrases a passage from Canadian author Hugh MacLean's 1957 novel, "The Watch That Ends the Night." MacLean, an English professor at McGill, was known for being the first Canadian writer to attempt to describe the national character. Downie picked up the book during The Hip's 1991 "Road Apples" tour, and the referenced passage describes a central character's regret for not proposing to the love of his life due to pride, depression, and mostly a lack of courage. "In the song, Gord paraphrases a passage from MacLean's 1957 novel, The Watch That Ends the Night, a book that he picked up to read between gigs on the road apples tour in 1991."
**Bill Barilko and the "50-Mission Cap"** : The "Fully Completely" album features a song referencing the legend of Bill Barilko's plane crash and the Stanley Cup curse on the Toronto Maple Leafs, bizarrely mentioning a "50-mission cap." This cap, a baseball-type worn by WWII fighter pilots, symbolized a seasoned pilot who had survived at least 50 missions. In the song, Downie uses it as a metaphor for people, like his character, who feign experience by distressing their cap, much like he admits to quoting a hockey card (specifically card #340 from the 1991 NHL Pro Set Hockey Cards about Barilko) to appear more knowledgeable. "Barilco's story is just a metaphor for the image Gord's character in the song wanted to portray."
**Collaboration with Al Purdy** : Downie frequently returned to the work of Canadian poet Al Purdy. In 2002, he appeared in a five-minute short film based on Purdy's poem "At the Quinte Hotel," with Purdy himself also providing a voice. Downie also performed a recitation of the poem during a MuchMusic performance and appeared in a 2015 documentary on Purdy, which included a soundtrack featuring songs and poems inspired by Purdy, to which Downie contributed "The East Wind." "In 2002, Gord appeared in a five minute short film based on the poem."
**Environmental Activism** : Downie and The Tragically Hip were deeply involved in environmental causes, opposing clear-cutting of old growth forests in BC and advocating for clean water. Downie served as a board member of Lake Ontario Waterkeepers, an organization that fought against companies discharging sewage and planned tire burning (in Bath, Ontario, near The Hip's studio). They also pressed the city of Toronto to explain public beach closures due to pollution. In 2009, Downie and the band formed part of the Swim Drink Fish Music Club, raising funds for clean rivers and lakes. "Gord was a board member of Lake Ontario waterkeepers, which fought to keep the great lakes base and clean."
**Advocacy for Indigenous People** : Downie was a prominent supporter of Canada's Indigenous people, with his activism dating back to at least 2012 when the band participated in the Great Moon Gathering, an annual educational conference held by Indigenous communities along James Bay. He later helped publicize the horrors of the residential school program, particularly through the story of Chanie Wenjack. His work in this area, including the "Secret Path" project, became a major part of his legacy and quest for reconciliation. "Gord will also be remembered for his work and support of Canada's Indigenous people."
**Chanie Wenjack Project - "Secret Path"** : With his brother Mike, Downie discovered a February 1967 article in McLean's magazine titled "The Lonely Death of Charlie Wenjak." This article recounted the tragic story of 12-year-old Chanie Wenjack, an Anishinaabe First Nations boy who died of exposure and hunger in October 1966 while attempting to walk 600 kilometers home from the Cecilia Jeffrey Indian Residential School. Downie's "Secret Path" album, graphic novel, and animated film brought Wenjack's story to national and international attention, galvanizing resistance against the residential school system. "This told the story of 12-year-old Cheney Wenjak, a young boy from the Inish Nabi First Nations, who was sent to the Cecilia Jeffrey Indian Residential School in Canora, Ontario."
**Call to Action to the Prime Minister** : During The Tragically Hip's final show on August 20, 2016, in Kingston, Downie directly addressed the then-Prime Minister, who was in attendance, making an urgent demand for him to deal with longstanding problems faced by Indigenous people. "Gord called out the Prime Minister by name who was there, suddenly demanding, okay, not so subtly, that he'd deal with these longstanding problems."
**Indigenous Recognition** : On December 6, 2016, Downie was awarded an Eagle Feather at the Assembly of First Nations and was given an honorary Aboriginal name, Wacapi Omane, which means "man who walked amongst the stars" in Lakota. He continued to speak out for Indigenous people, appearing in Ottawa on July 2, 2017. "On December 6, 2016, he was awarded an Eagle Feather at the Assembly of First Nations and given an honorary Aboriginal name, Wacapi Omane, which is Lakota for men who walked amongst the stars."
**Brain Cancer Diagnosis and Death** : On May 24, 2016, it was announced that Gord Downie had been diagnosed with Gleoblastoma, an aggressive form of brain cancer. He passed away on the morning of October 17, 2017. Despite the short time remaining after his diagnosis, Downie dedicated his remaining months to tackling issues "so much bigger than him," particularly his advocacy for Indigenous people. "The news of Gord's death came out on the morning of October 17, 2017, because, as we all know, was Gleoblastoma, an aggressive form of brain cancer."
**Poet Laureate of Ontario Act** : On December 17, 2017, a few weeks after Downie's death, a bill was introduced in the Ontario legislature to create the Poet Laureate of Ontario Act in his memory. Two years later, this bill became law, establishing an official position to carry on Downie's love of poetry. "It was the Poet Laureate of Ontario Act in memory of Gord Dowdy."

## curiosities
**Formation and Name Origin (1984)** : The Tragically Hip formed in 1984 in Kingston, Ontario, with Gord Downie (20 years old at the time), Gord Sinclair, Robbie Baker, Johnny Faye, and sax player Dave Manning (about 15 years older). They took their name from a skit in a 1981 VHS tape called "Elephant Parts," produced by video pioneer Michael Nesmith (formerly of The Monkees). The phrase "tragically hip" also appears in Elvis Costello's 1982 song "Town Cryer" from the "Imperial Bedroom" album. "Things started coming together in 1984 when Gord, then 20 years old, the other Gord, Robbie, Johnny Faye, and a sax player, about 15 years older than everyone named Dave Manning, formed the tragically hip, taking their name from a skit in a 1981 VHS tape making the rounds called Elephant Parts."
**Early Gigs and Sound** : The band's first gig was in November 1984 at a small room in the Kingston Artist Association. They then started playing wherever they could in Eastern Ontario, including the Tarapin Tavern and the Lakeview Manor (which also hosted strippers). Their early setlists largely comprised blues rock standards, covering material from The Rolling Stones, The Doors, The Yardbirds, Marvin Gaye, Otis Redding, and Van Morrison. "Their set consisted largely of blues rock standards. Early stuff from the stones, material from the doors, the yard birds, Marvin Gaye, Otis Redding, Ben Morrison, that kind of thing."
**Lineup Change and Stability (1986)** : In 1986, the same year Gord Downie graduated from Queens University, founding sax player Dave Manning left the band. He was replaced by Paul Langlois, who had first met Gord Downie in grade 11 and was the son of the school's gym teacher and football coach. With Langlois joining, the lineup of The Tragically Hip remained unchanged for the next 31 years. "In 1986, the same year Gord graduated from Queens with a bachelor's degree, Dave Manning bowed out of the band and he was replaced by Paul Langua, who first met Gord when they were both in grade 11."
**Big Break and Management Deal** : The Hip got a significant break when a friend shared their demos with Hugh Seagull, a former senator from Ottawa. Seagull, in turn, passed the demos to conservative pollster Alan Greg, who was a partner in a management company with Jake Gold. Impressed by both the demos and a live performance, Greg and Gold signed on to manage the band. "The hip got a big break when a friend gave a copy of their demos to Hugh Seagull, a friend and former senator in Ottawa."
**Record Deal with MCA Records** : The band's debut EP, which served as their calling card, brought them to the attention of Bruce Dickinson, the newly appointed president of MCA Records. Dickinson was tipped off by a song he heard on a compilation CD. He flew to Toronto from New York and was impressed by a two-song set the band performed at Massey Hall, leading to their record deal and the reissue of their debut EP. "This is how they came to the attention of Bruce Dickinson, the brand new president of MCA Records."
**Environmental Activism** : The Tragically Hip were involved in various environmental programs and activities, including opposing the clear-cutting of old growth forests in British Columbia and advocating for clean water. Their activism led them to the Great Moon Gathering, an annual educational conference held by Indigenous communities along James Bay. In 2009, the band was part of the Swim Drink Fish Music Club, raising money to preserve clean rivers and lakes. "Gord and the hip were involved in many programs and activities, from opposing the clear-cutting of old growth forests and BC to making sure that we all have clean water."
**Final Show (August 20, 2016)** : The band played their final show on August 20, 2016, in Kingston, their hometown. During this concert, Gord Downie notably called out the then-Prime Minister, who was present, to address the longstanding issues facing Canada's Indigenous people. "When the hit played their final show on August 20, 2016 in Kingston, Gord called out the Prime Minister by name who was there, suddenly demanding, okay, not so subtly, that he'd deal with these longstanding problems."

# artist - Al Purdy

## curiosities
**Unofficial Poet Laureate of Canada** : Al Purdy had a career spanning 56 years, publishing 39 books of poetry, two memoirs, a novel, and four collections of letters. He was widely regarded as Canada's unofficial poet laureate and a master storyteller. "Perdi was considered to be Canada's unofficial poet laureate, a great teller of myths and stories."
**"At the Quinte Hotel" Poem** : For those looking to explore Purdy's work, the poem "At the Quinte Hotel," set in Belleville, Ontario, is recommended. It's a satirical piece that discusses beer, the relationship between art and commerce, and humorously depicts a bar fight where the author eventually wins over the crowd with his verses. "look up a poem called at the Quente Hotel, which is set in Belleville, Ontario."
**Influence on Gord Downie** : Gord Downie frequently returned to Purdy's work, appearing in a short film based on "At the Quinte Hotel" (2002), reciting the poem during a MuchMusic performance, and featuring in a 2015 documentary about Purdy. Downie also contributed to the documentary's soundtrack, which included songs and poems inspired by Purdy. "Gord Danny would return to Albert E. Time and Time again."

**National Mourning for Gord Downie (October 2017)** : When Gord Downie passed away in October 2017, the country experienced profound grief. A widely shared tweet of the day declared, "Canada closed, death in the family," reflecting the national sentiment. The subsequent week saw extensive efforts to explain to the rest of the world how a rock band singer could bring an entire nation, including its Prime Minister, to tears. "When Gord left us in October 2017, it was really rough. The best tweet I saw that day was Canada closed, death in the family."
**Definition of a Poet in Rock** : A true poet, particularly in rock music, is characterized as someone who weaves words in a way that is open to diverse personal interpretations and leads to various truths, speaking to individuals uniquely. This ability to evoke deep meaning without explicit explanation is considered the mark of a poet. "Someone who weaves words in a way that's open to all kinds of personal interpretation and pathways to certain truths."
**"Elephant Parts" VHS Tape (1981)** : This 1981 VHS tape, produced by video pioneer and former Monkees member Michael Nesmith, contained a skit from which The Tragically Hip took their name. The tape was "making the rounds" when the band formed in 1984. "taking their name from a skit in a 1981 VHS tape making the rounds called Elephant Parts."
**"The Watch That Ends the Night" Novel (1957)** : Written by Canadian author Hugh MacLean, this 1957 novel contains a passage paraphrased by Gord Downie in a song from The Tragically Hip's "Fully Completely" album. Downie read the book during the 1991 "Road Apples" tour, and the passage describes a central character's regret over missed opportunities in love due to pride, depression, and a lack of courage. "Gord paraphrases a passage from MacLean's 1957 novel, The Watch That Ends the Night, a book that he picked up to read between gigs on the road apples tour in 1991."
**"50-Mission Cap" Symbolism** : A "50-mission cap" was a baseball-style cap worn by World War II fighter pilots. A worn cap indicated a seasoned pilot who had survived at least 50 missions. In the context of a Tragically Hip song, Gord Downie used it metaphorically to describe individuals (including his lyrical persona) who intentionally distressed their caps to project an image of having more experience than they genuinely possessed. "A 50-mission cap was a baseball-type cap thing worn by fighter pilots in World War II. If you had one with lots of wear, it meant that you were a seasoned pilot who had survived at least 50 missions."

**Lake Ontario Waterkeepers Environmental Organization** : Gord Downie served as a board member for Lake Ontario Waterkeepers, an organization dedicated to keeping the Great Lakes basin clean. They actively fought against companies discharging sewage into public waters and once opposed a company in Bath, Ontario (where The Hip's studio was located) that intended to burn tires. In 2007, they compelled the city of Toronto to explain public beach closures due to pollution. "Gord was a board member of Lake Ontario waterkeepers, which fought to keep the great lakes base and clean."

**Residential School Program** : This was a Canadian government program designed to forcibly assimilate Indigenous children into Western society. The program's horrors were brought to national attention by Gord Downie, particularly through his "Secret Path" project which recounted the story of Chanie Wenjack, a 12-year-old Anishinaabe boy who died attempting to escape one such school. "To recap, this was a government program designed to assimilate Indigenous children into Western society."
**The Lonely Death of Charlie Wenjak (February 1967)** : This article in McLean's magazine, discovered by Gord Downie and his brother Mike, detailed the tragic story of 12-year-old Chanie Wenjack. Chanie, an Anishinaabe First Nations boy, ran away from the Cecilia Jeffrey Indian Residential School in Canora, Ontario, on October 16, 1966, hoping to walk 600 kilometers home. He died of exposure and hunger after walking for about 36 hours in late October, and his body was found by a passing train engineer. "This told the story of 12-year-old Cheney Wenjak, a young boy from the Inish Nabi First Nations, who was sent to the Cecilia Jeffrey Indian Residential School in Canora, Ontario."
**Poet Laureate of Ontario Act (December 17, 2017 / 2019)** : Weeks after Gord Downie's death, a bill was introduced in the Ontario legislature on December 17, 2017, titled the Poet Laureate of Ontario Act, specifically in his memory. Two years later, it became law, establishing an official provincial poet laureate to continue Downie's legacy and love for poetry.
