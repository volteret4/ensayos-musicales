## albums
**Young Americans (1975) - Imperial Phase** : This album marked a period in Bowie's career where songs and albums were released continuously. Over approximately three years, Bowie released six albums during this "imperial phase." "Bowie was still in his imperial phase. That part of his career where the songs and albums just kept coming."
**Low (1977) - Experimental Berlin Album** : Recorded at Hansa Studio in Berlin, this album was experimental and often very electronic, reflecting Bowie's fascination with German electronic music, specifically Kraftwerk. It was a colder-sounding record, made while Bowie was trying desperately to kick his cocaine addiction and experiencing physical and psychic pain. Side 1 featured short song fragments (none longer than 3.5 minutes), while Side 2 was entirely instrumental and included significant contributions from Brian Eno. Initially confusing to critics, it later became recognized as a bold work that expanded the reach of synthesizer music beyond its German origins, influencing post-punk bands like Joy Division. "Low, an experimental and often very electronic album that was part man who fell to earth, part station to station and part whatever was happening in Berlin at the time."
**Heroes (1977) - New Wave Precursor** : Recorded less than ten months after "Low" at Hansa Studio, this album continued Bowie's exploration of "crowd rock" and electronic sounds. Its title is a tribute to a song by the German band Neu!, and it includes "V2 Schneider," a nod to Florian Schneider of Kraftwerk. The record captured the nascent elements of what would become New Wave music, with Bowie once again ahead of trends. It is widely considered one of his all-time best records. "Heroes has gone down in history as one of Bowie's all time best records."
**Lodger (1979) - Berlin Trilogy Conclusion** : This album, the third in Bowie's "Berlin trilogy," was not recorded in Berlin but in Switzerland and New York, though it retained the style and feel of his German experience. Brian Eno contributed significantly to its sound, helping Bowie explore his artistic direction, though Tony Visconti remained the producer. "Lodger" was the least commercially successful of the trilogy and initially puzzled critics, but it gained appreciation over time and is considered by Bowie officials to be perhaps his most underrated album. "It was called Lodger and it was the least commercially successful of these albums and critics really didn't know what to make of it at first."
**Scary Monsters and Super Creeps (1980) - Post-Punk Breakthrough** : This record marked a perfect end to the 1970s and a welcoming of the 1980s, finding a new balance between post-punk, art rock, and pop elements after Bowie's time in Berlin. Its centerpiece, "Ashes to Ashes," revisited the character of Major Tom and featured a groundbreaking, expensive music video that tapped into the emerging New Romantics scene in Britain. The album was hugely influential, particularly on artists like Morrissey and Johnny Marr of The Smiths. "Scary monsters and super creeps. The next record was great, the perfect way to end the 1970s and to welcome in the 1980s."
**Let's Dance (1983) - Commercial Peak** : This album was conceived as a more commercially sounding record after Bowie's $17.5 million deal with EMI Records. Recorded with a new band of session players in just 17 days, including mixing, it was a massive and unexpected success, selling over six million copies and becoming Bowie's best-selling album of all time. Its hybrid rock dance funk sound was considered innovative for 1983. The album's success, however, caused Bowie to feel disconnected from his audience during the subsequent Serious Moonlight Tour. "Let's Dance was a massive success for Bowie and drew 2.6 million people to the serious Moonlight Tour as it wound through 16 countries."
**Tonight (1984)** : This album, a rushed follow-up to "Let's Dance," was intended to capitalize on Bowie's newfound mainstream audience but was largely unsuccessful. Reviews for the record and its accompanying tours were generally unkind. "Maybe the less said about the Tonight album and that never let me down album the better."
**Never Let Me Down (1987)** : This album, like its predecessor "Tonight," was a rushed effort that failed to resonate with critics or audiences. It received poor reviews and was part of a period where Bowie felt disconnected from his audience and direction. "Maybe the less said about the Tonight album and that never let me down album the better."
**Tin Machine (1989) - Band Debut** : This self-titled album was the debut of the four-piece rock band Tin Machine, formed by Bowie as its lead singer. The band drew inspiration from artists like Jimi Hendrix, Cream, and the Pixies, and their sound was distinctly different from earlier David Bowie records. It received harsh reviews, with some critics viewing Bowie as an "old man trying to be hip again." "Tin Machine from their self-titled Davy album and a single entitled Under the God."
**Black Tie White Noise (1993) - Return to Form** : This album, released after Bowie had resolved his legal issues with a former manager and was free to make music on his own terms, was generally acknowledged as his best work since "Scary Monsters" thirteen years earlier. Despite its critical reception, it did not produce any hit singles. "In 1993, a record called Black Tie White Noise didn't result in any hit singles, but it was generally acknowledged to be his best works in Scary Monsters 13 years earlier."
**Outside (1995) - Concept Record with Eno** : This album was a perhaps overproduced concept record, created in collaboration with Brian Eno. It was based on a paranoid end-of-the-millennium short story titled "The Diary of Nathan Adler." "That was followed by outside, a perhaps overproduced concept record with Brianino, which was based on a paranoid end of the Millennium Short Story called The Diary of Nathan Adler."
**Earthling (1997)** : This album featured the song "I'm Afraid of Americans," for which a creepy stalker video was made, featuring Trent Reznor. The album itself garnered plenty of positive reviews and was supported by a successful world tour. "Plenty of paused reviews and lots of love on the world tour that went along with it."
**Hours (1999) - Cyber Song Contest Inclusion** : This album featured a song by Alex Grant, the winner of Bowie's cyber song contest, showcasing Bowie's early interest and involvement in internet technology and new methods of music creation. "And a guy by the name of Alex Grant-Won."
**Heathen (2002)** : This album marked a return to consistent musical output for Bowie in the early 2000s, suggesting he had no intention of stopping his recording career. "Meanwhile, Bowie continued to make records. There was heathen in 2002."
**Reality (2003)** : Following "Heathen," this album further demonstrated Bowie's continued commitment to making music into the 21st century. It seemed to indicate that he planned to keep recording indefinitely. "And then reality from 2003, and it looked like Bowie had every intention of never stopping."
**The Next Day (2013) - Secret Recording** : This album was secretly written and recorded over a period of two years, with no one involved leaking information. Its existence was announced on Bowie's 66th birthday, January 8th, 2013, along with a new song. The ability to record such an album in total secrecy during the internet age was considered a "miracle," attributed to the loyalty of his associates and Bowie's low-key life in New York. "The first the world heard of this record was on January 8th, 2013, Bowie 66th birthday. All of a sudden, there was a new song and the announcement of a new album called The Next Day."
**Blackstar (2016) - Final Album** : This album was written and recorded by Bowie after he received a diagnosis of liver cancer in summer 2014, knowing he likely had time for only one more album. He worked at The Magic Shop on Crosby Street, with a daily routine, finishing the album before news from his doctors in November 2015 confirmed his cancer was terminal. The album was released on January 8th, 2016, his 69th birthday, and contained tracks with ominous videos and lyrics reflecting his mortality, such as "Lazarus." "And then, that a new album called Blackstar would be released on January 8th, 2016, Bowie 69th birthday."

## songs
**TVC-15 (1976)** : This song is about a hallucination experienced by Iggy Pop during one of his drug sessions with Bowie, where Iggy believed Bowie's TV was eating his girlfriend. Bowie subsequently wrote the song about a holographic television with the model number TVC-15. "For example, this song is about a hallucination, his even crazier friend, Iggy Pop, had during one of their drug sessions."
**Heroes (1977)** : The second verse of this iconic song was inspired by Bowie witnessing his producer, Tony Visconti, and a backup singer sharing a quick kiss by the Berlin Wall from the control room window of Hansa Studio. Bowie had told Visconti to take a walk so he could think alone to finish the lyrics for the song. "From the control room window Bowie saw Visconti and his girlfriend steal a quick kiss by the Berlin wall. And that's where the second verse of this song came from."
**Ashes to Ashes (1980)** : This song was the centerpiece of the "Scary Monsters and Super Creeps" album and picked up the story of Major Tom, a character Bowie had introduced ten years earlier in "Space Oddity." The accompanying music video was incredibly expensive for its time, costing £250,000, and was made before MTV existed, primarily for TV shows like "Top of the Pops" to allow artists more control over their public image. "The centerpiece of the record was ashes to ashes, a song that not only picked up the story of Major Tom from Space Audity, a character that Bowie had introduced ten years earlier, but it was also made into a hideously expensive music video that cost 250,000 pounds."
**Under the God (1989)** : This was a single from Tin Machine's self-titled debut album. The track marked a departure from David Bowie's solo work, fitting the band's more raw, four-piece rock sound. "Tin Machine from their self-titled Davy album and a single entitled Under the God."
**I'm Afraid of Americans (1997)** : This song from the "Earthling" album had a creepy stalker music video that featured Trent Reznor of Nine Inch Nails, a diehard Bowie fan. The video's style was compared to Martin Scorsese's "Taxi Driver" and gained plenty of attention. "Not only did they tour together, but Bowie got Trent to appear in the creepy stalker video for this song from 1997 called I'm Afraid of Americans, which looks like something straight out of Martin Scorsese Taxi Driver."
**Lazarus (2015)** : Released shortly before Christmas 2015, this track was part of Bowie's final album, "Blackstar," and featured an ominous video and lyrics that seemed deeply preoccupied with his mortality. The song also lent its name to a musical Bowie attended in early December 2015, just weeks before his death. "Listen to the lyrics of Alaseros."

## curiosities
**Early Career Impact - The Most Discussed Rock Star** : For over 40 years of his six-decade career, David Bowie was recognized as the most discussed, photographed, imitated, worshiped, and admired rock star in history. No other rock and roll performer had a more profound effect on music, with much of today's alt rock tracing its roots directly or indirectly to his work. "He was the most discussed, photographed, imitated, worshiped, and admired rock star in history."
**January 10, 2016 - Passing Away** : David Bowie passed away on January 10th, 2016, just two days after his 69th birthday. His death marked the end of a career that spanned six decades and profoundly influenced music and culture. "David Bowie, who passed away on January 10th, 2016, just two days after his 69th birthday."
**Shape-Shifting Persona and Identity** : Bowie embraced being different offstage from his onstage personas, unlike many rock artists who strive for authenticity. He curated characters like Ziggy Stardust, Aladdin Sane, Halloween Jack, the plastic soul man, and the Thin White Duke, frequently changing his image and character to capture public imagination and drive his artistic evolution. "It's very hard to convince people that you can be quite different offstage in rock and more than you are onstage."
**Mid-70s Drug Addiction and Paranoia** : In the mid-1970s, Bowie struggled with heavy drinking and tremendous amounts of cocaine, often eating little more than milk and peppers. This period led to erratic behavior, such as storing urine in his fridge out of fear that "unspecified witches" would steal his "vital essence," and touring with a telescope, convinced that aliens in an orbiting mothership were coming for him. He later stated he had no recollection of anything that happened in 1976. "But the truth was the boy was drinking heavily and using tremendous amounts of cocaine while eating little more than milk and peppers."
**The Thin White Duke Persona - Thomas Jerome Newton Influence** : This amoral, hollow, damaged, and emotionless character, with his signature white shirt and waistcoat, emerged from an autobiography Bowie began while filming "The Man Who Fell to Earth," in which he played the doomed space alien Thomas Jerome Newton. Bowie loved the character and asked to keep the costuming, directly influencing his look for the next couple of years. "This character came from a sort of autobiography Bowie had begun while filming the man who fell to Earth, the movie where he played a doomed space alien named Thomas Jerome Newton."
**Move to Berlin - Cold War Artistic Hub** : In an effort to escape the negative influences surrounding him in L.A. and kick his cocaine addiction, Bowie and Iggy Pop moved to Berlin, Germany. During the height of the Cold War, Berlin was a divided city, yet its unique artistic and bohemian culture provided the perfect environment for Bowie's musical evolution, where he immersed himself in German electronic and crude rock sounds. "He and Iggy Pop packed up and left for Berlin. Now Berlin. Nobody went to Berlin. This was the height of the Cold War."
**Berlin Residence and Studio Location** : In Berlin, Bowie and Iggy Pop found a place at Hopstras 155 in the Schöneberg area, located above a gay bar they frequented. They recorded at Hansa Studio, specifically Studio 2, situated about 500 yards from Potsdamer Platz, where sections of the Berlin Wall ran through the city. "They found a place at Hopstras 155 in the Schunenberg area of the city. Right below was a gay bar where they used to drink and not far from the Kreutzberg area, which is a good place for bars and clubs and funky shops."
**"Ashes to Ashes" Music Video - £250,000 Cost** : The music video for "Ashes to Ashes" was exceptionally expensive for its time, costing £250,000. Made before MTV existed, its primary purpose was for distribution to TV shows like "Top of the Pops" to allow artists greater control over their public image, a major gamble for Bowie who had just emerged from a costly legal battle with a former manager. "The centerpiece of the record was ashes to ashes, a song that not only picked up the story of Major Tom from Space Audity, a character that Bowie had introduced ten years earlier, but it was also made into a hideously expensive music video that cost 250,000 pounds."
**New Romantics Influence - "Ashes to Ashes" Video** : The "Ashes to Ashes" music video tapped into the burgeoning New Romantics scene emerging from London clubs, a stylish and futuristic form of post-punk electronic pop. Several actual kids from that scene appeared in the video alongside Bowie. "But it tapped into this new scene coming out of Britain, the new Romantics, a stylish and futuristic form of post-punk electronic pop coming out of the London clubs."
**Prolific Output in the 70s/80s** : From "Hunky Dory" onward, Bowie released 11 studio albums and two live albums in just over eight and a half years. During this intense period, he also collaborated with artists like Iggy Pop, survived a deep cocaine addiction, and expanded his artistic endeavors into film and theater, taking on projects that regular rock stars typically avoided. "I mean, after all, if we take Hunky Dory as the first album where Bowie got his groove, he had released 11 studio albums in just over 8 and a half years."
**Acting Career - "The Elephant Man" and Film Roles** : Bowie embarked on a significant acting career, notably performing 157 times as John Merrick in the stage production of "The Elephant Man," including a stint on Broadway. Throughout the 1980s, he appeared in films such as "Christine F," "Ball" (TV film), "Merry Christmas, Mr. Lawrence," "Yellow Beard," "The Hunger" (where he played a vampire, endearing him to goth fans), "Into the Night," "Labyrinth," "Absolute Beginners," and "The Last Temptation of Christ." "The acting thing would continue for the rest of the 80s. There was Christine F, a TV film called Ball, Merry Christmas, Mr. Lawrence, really good movie, Yellow Beard and The Hunger, all in 1983."
**EMI Records Deal - $17.5 Million** : In the 1980s, Bowie signed a new record deal with EMI Records worth $17.5 million, one of the biggest deals in the music industry at that time. This substantial contract influenced his decision to create a more commercially oriented album like "Let's Dance." "And then there was the new 17.5 million dollar record deal with EMI Records, one of the biggest deals in the industry to that time."
**"Just David Bowie" Persona** : After nearly a decade of assuming distinct characters for each album, Bowie decided to present himself as "just David Bowie" for the "Let's Dance" album. Ironically, this decision itself represented a new persona that audiences had not seen before, shifting from a cult artist to a bigger-than-life rock star. "So after nearly a decade of assuming roles of different characters for each album, he decided to be just David Bowie."
**Post-"Let's Dance" Creative Struggles** : The immense success of "Let's Dance" unexpectedly confused Bowie, making him feel detached from his audience and uncertain of their expectations. This "confused headspace" initiated a creative downturn for the rest of the 1980s, as his subsequent work was unfairly judged against the commercial peak of "Let's Dance." "I suddenly felt very apart from my audience and it was depressing because I didn't know what they wanted."
**Sound + Vision Tour - Retiring Hits** : To shed the "baggage" of his past successes and start fresh, Bowie undertook the Sound + Vision Tour, during which he promised to play his old hits like "Space Oddity," "Ziggy Stardust," "Rebel Rebel," "Young Americans," "Heroes," and "Let's Dance" one last time before retiring them forever. His intention was to clear the decks and move forward creatively. "The first was something called the Sound Invision Tour where Bowie promised to play his old hits one more time and then retire them forever."
**Marriage to Iman (Man Muhammad Abel Majid)** : Bowie married supermodel Iman, whose full name is Man Muhammad Abel Majid, in Switzerland on April 24, 1992. Both had been married before (Iman twice), but this relationship provided a stability that had often been lacking in their lives, leading Bowie to prioritize new aspects of his life. "He had a new wife, the man Muhammad Abel Majid, a man the Supermodel for short. They were married in Switzerland on April 24, 1992."
**Early Internet Innovator - Website and Gaming** : Bowie was one of the first artists to establish a proper website in the 1990s, a time when web design was still nascent. His company became so proficient that it even secured a contract to design and maintain the website for the New York Yankees. He also created the soundtrack for the computer game "Omicron," which featured virtual characters of both himself and Iman. "He was one of the first artists to set up a proper website. This was back in the 90s when few people were into web design."
**Bowie Bonds - Financial Innovation** : In 1997, Bowie collaborated with New York investment banker David Pullman to create "Bowie Bonds," a package of securities backed by the future projected royalties of 25 Bowie albums (287 songs recorded before 1990). Bowie received $55 million upfront, using the money to buy back rights he had lost to his former manager. This was a pioneering use of intellectual property to secure a financial instrument. "In 1997, a New York investment banker named David Pullman put together a package of securities backed by the future projected royalties of 25 Bowie albums, a total of 287 songs that were recorded before 1990."
**Bonds Downgraded Due to Internet Sales Decline** : Despite their innovative nature, Bowie Bonds were downgraded to junk status by 2004, approximately seven years after their creation. This downgrade was a direct consequence of declining music sales brought on by the rise of the internet, an irony given Bowie's own prophecy about music becoming "cheap as water" due to internet distribution. "By 2004, about seven years in, those bonds were downgraded to junk status. Why? Well, because of declining music sales brought on by the rise of the internet."
**Heart Attack in 2005 - Career Slowdown** : In June 2005, while on stage at a festival in Germany, Bowie suffered chest pains, initially misdiagnosed as a pinched nerve. It was later discovered to be a heart attack caused by a blocked coronary artery, requiring emergency angioplasty. This event led to a significant slowdown in his musical output and public appearances, as he became largely invisible, living a quiet life in New York. "In June 2005, it was on stage at a festival in Germany when he started suffering chest pains. It was first diagnosed as a pinched nerve. Later though, it was discovered to have been a heart attack, a blocked coronary artery."
**Secret Recording of "The Next Day"** : Bowie managed to record his album "The Next Day" in complete secrecy over two years in the internet age, a feat considered miraculous. This was achieved through the unwavering loyalty of his associates, who were sworn to secrecy, and by Bowie living a remarkably low-key life in New York City with Iman. He was often seen walking, grocery shopping, and attending social events, but people were accustomed to his flamboyant personas and simply didn't notice "David Jones." "How could one of the most famous musicians in the history of the universe manage to record an album in total secrecy in the age of the internet? It was a miracle."
**Liver Cancer Diagnosis - Final Album** : In the summer of 2014, Bowie began feeling unwell and was diagnosed with liver cancer, which was initially treatable. Knowing he likely had time for only one more album, he began writing songs that would serve as his own "eulogy" or "requiem," leading to the creation of "Blackstar." "Bowie had been feeling unwell. From what we know, the diagnosis was liver cancer. Bad, but treatable, at least at first."
**"Blackstar" Recording Process at The Magic Shop** : Bowie recorded his final album, "Blackstar," in secrecy at The Magic Shop on Crosby Street in New York City, located just 283 steps from his Lafayette Street building. His daily routine involved starting work around 10 AM with a double macchiato from La Colombe coffee shop, and finishing by dinner to be home with his wife and daughter. "He started work around 10 o'clock each day with a double macchiato from a coffee shop called La Colomb. And he finished up at a decenar of the day so he could be home for dinner with his wife and daughter."
**Terminal Cancer Diagnosis - November 2015** : In November 2015, Bowie received the news from his doctors that his chemotherapy was no longer effective and his cancer was terminal, leaving him with a limited time to live. By this point, the "Blackstar" album was already finished. "In November 2015, the news came from his doctors. The chemo was no longer working. The cancer was terminal. It was just a matter of time."
**Collapse Backstage at "Lazarus" Musical** : In early December 2015, after attending a performance of his musical "Lazarus" and meeting with the director, Bowie collapsed backstage. He subsequently regained some strength and was seen on the streets of New York as late as his 69th birthday, January 8th, 2016. "In early December 2015, Bowie attended a performance of his musical Lazarus. After meeting with the director, he collapsed backstage."
**Cremation and Ashes in Bali** : Following his death on January 10th, 2016, Bowie's body was cremated. In his will, he requested that his ashes be scattered in Bali and stipulated that there should be no funeral. "His body was cremated, and he asked in his will that his ashes be scattered in Bali. He left instructions that there be no funeral."
**Cultural Impact - Redefining Rock Stardom** : David Bowie profoundly changed culture by subverting the traditional meaning of a rock star, pushing music into new realms of theater and spectacle. His continuously shape-shifting style was widely imitated by artists like Madonna and Lady Gaga, and he served as a crucial inspiration for punk, new wave, post-punk, electronic, goth, industrial, and alternative movements, including bands like the Pixies, Nirvana, Nine Inch Nails, and Arcade Fire. "David Bowie irrevocably changed our culture. He began by subverting what it meant to be a rock star."
**Pioneer of Sexual Ambiguity and Gay Icon** : Bowie was candid, whether truthful or not, about his sexual orientation, introducing sexual ambiguity into mainstream culture. In doing so, he became a powerful gay icon, opening doors for countless gay performers and helping to launch a gay activist element within rock and pop music. "He introduced sexual ambiguity into mainstream culture, and in doing so became a powerful gay icon, opening the doors for hundreds if not thousands of gay performers, thereby launching a gay activist element in rock and pop."
**Sales and Enduring Cult Status** : Throughout his career, Bowie sold over 140 million records worldwide. Beyond commercial success, he cultivated one of the biggest and most enduring cults in the history of music, driven by his unique artistry and image control. "The entire time he made music that not only sold 140 million plus records, but also created one of the biggest and most enduring cults in the history of music."
**Refusal of Knighthood in 2003** : Bowie was offered a knighthood in 2003 but publicly turned it down, stating that he had no intention of accepting such an honor. He expressed skepticism about its purpose and maintained that it was not what he had worked for in his life. "He was offered a nighthood in 2003, but he turned it down. I would have never had any intention of accepting anything like that. I seriously don't know what it's for. That's not what I spent my life working for."
**Educator and Champion of Obscure Artists** : Through his collaborations with Iggy Pop and Lou Reed, and his early championing of The Velvet Underground, Bowie acted as an educator in music, helping to spread awareness and appreciation for these otherwise obscure performers. "Through his work with Iggy Pop and Lou Reed, and through his early championing of the Velvet Underground, Bowie acted as an educator, a person who helped spread the word on these otherwise obscure performers."
**Honorary Degree from Berkeley in 1999** : Bowie received an honorary degree from Berkeley in May 1999, recognizing his diverse contributions as a video pioneer, highly regarded actor (in both movies and on stage), internet innovator, and Wall Street dealmaker. "It's no wonder he received an honorary degree from Berkeley in May of 1999."

## albums
**Tin Machine (1989)** : This self-titled album was the debut record of the four-piece rock band formed by David Bowie, who served solely as the lead singer. # curiosity

**Berlin Wall - Inspiration for "Heroes" Song** : The Berlin Wall served as a poignant backdrop and inspiration for a key moment in David Bowie's song "Heroes." He witnessed his producer, Tony Visconti, and a backup singer share a quick kiss by the wall from his studio window, which directly inspired the second verse of the song. "From the control room window Bowie saw Visconti and his girlfriend steal a quick kiss by the Berlin wall."
**Bowie Bonds - Intellectual Property as Financial Instrument** : In 1997, New York investment banker David Pullman pioneered the concept of "Bowie Bonds," securities backed by the future projected royalties of 25 David Bowie albums recorded before 1990. This innovative financial instrument secured $55 million upfront for Bowie, allowing him to buy back his song rights from a former manager, and marked the first time intellectual property was used to collateralize bonds, inspiring similar deals for other songwriters. "And as far as anybody can tell, this was the first time anyone had ever used intellectual property to secure a financial instrument such as bonds."
**David Bowie's Internet Music Prophecy** : In 1997, David Bowie foresaw the future of music distribution via the internet, prophesying that music would become "cheap as water coming out of a tap." This prediction proved prescient, as declining music sales due to internet proliferation eventually led to the downgrade of his own Bowie Bonds to junk status by 2004. "In 1997, he already saw where music was heading with the internet. He prophesized that with the distribution of music promised by the internet, music would become cheap as water coming out of a tap."
