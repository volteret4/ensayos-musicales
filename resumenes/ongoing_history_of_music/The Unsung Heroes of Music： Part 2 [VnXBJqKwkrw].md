## curiosities
**Fashion Impact of Long Hair** : The Beatles were credited with making long hair fashionable and cool for men in the modern era, significantly changing not just fashion but society. Their hairstyles became a symbol of rebellion against conformity and a mark of membership in the 1960s counterculture, provoking strong reactions from the establishment. "The Beatles, it leased in the modern era, were really the people who made it fashionable and cool for guys to have long hair."
**Indonesian Government Reaction to Beatles' Look** : President Sukarno of Indonesia was so angered by young men imitating the Beatles' look that he declared it against the country's moral values, calling their music "Nek-Nek-Nyok" (random noises). He made Beatles haircuts illegal, ordered barbershops not to provide them, and empowered police to forcibly trim or shave bald anyone caught with such a haircut. "He called their music Nek-Nek-Nyok, which means random noises, yes."
**Normalization of Long Hair** : What began with The Beatles eventually led to long hair on men becoming a completely normal and accepted rock and roll look that persists globally today without a second thought. "Nobody gives it a second thought, and it all begins with the Beatles. They're the ones who made it okay."
**Origin of Mop Top Haircut (Yurgen Volmer)** : In 1961, Paul McCartney and John Lennon met Yurgen Volmer in Paris, who was a friend of Astrid Kircher and Stu Sutcliffe. Volmer had long hair parted and worn over his forehead, which "entranced" Lennon and McCartney, who still wore their hair slicked back. They asked him to cut their hair in his style, giving birth to the first version of the Beatles' mop top. "So he cut their hair, and so the Beatles' mop top was born, or at least the first version of it."
**Developing Mop Top Haircut (Astrid Kircher)** : About a year after the initial cut by Yurgen Volmer, during The Beatles' extended residency in Hamburg, Astrid Kircher regularly cut their hair into this new look. She first applied the cut to Stu Sutcliffe, then George Harrison, and finally John Lennon and Paul McCartney. Within two years, as The Beatles became the biggest band globally, their haircuts were nearly as famous as their music, solidifying long hair on men as a lasting trend. "She first gave the cut to Stu, and then George, and then finally John and Paul."

Gregory Coleman died homeless and broke in 2006, unaware of his profound impact on music, and band leader Richard Spencer died in 2020. "Not as the case with so many samples, neither Coleman nor the Winston's have seen any royalties from this, despite the fact that this piece of music has become part of so many other songs, some of which, many of which have become major hits."

"There are several theories as to why this happened, race and gender being one."

# instrument - Tiatrophone

## curiosities
**Early Wired Music Streaming (1881)** : Invented by Clement Adler and demonstrated in 1881, the Tiatrophone was a system that used 80 telephone transmitters placed around Parisian theater stages to transmit live symphony and opera performances via telephone lines to private homes for a subscription fee. It even offered a form of stereo sound, 70 years before stereo was widely adopted. "In 1881, a French engineer named Clement Adler demonstrated a system called the Tiatrophone at the International Exhibition of Electricity in Paris."
**Expansion and Commercial Use** : Following its debut, Tiatrophone services expanded across Europe, appearing in the UK as the "Electrophone" and even demonstrated in New York. It was also installed in public venues like restaurants and clubs, where for 50 centimes (50 cents), listeners could enjoy five minutes of music through ear tubes. "Tiatrophones were also installed in restaurants and lounges and private clubs, where for 50 times, which was around 50 cents, you got five minutes of listening through a couple of tubes that you stuck in your ears."
**Decline due to Radio** : The Tiatrophone service lasted until 1931, but its eventual demise was caused by the advent of radio (referred to as "the wireless" in Britain). Radio offered music transmission without the need for extensive wiring to individual homes, making it a more convenient and accessible technology. "This lasted a long time, too, all the way to 1931, in fact, and the thing that killed it, radio, which required no wires running all over the place."

Artists from Slipknot to Oasis have utilized this ubiquitous drum break. **Digital Jukebox Concept** : Professor Dieter Selzer, an expert in psychoacoustics and advisor to Carlheinz Brandenburg, envisioned a "digital jukebox" in the 1980s. This system would store all the world's music on a central server, accessible by anyone with a telephone line, a concept that was initially rejected as impossible for a patent. "Selzer had this dream of what he called a digital jukebox. A system whereby all the music in the world would be stored on a central server which then could be accessed by anyone with a telephone line."
**Professor Dieter Selzer's Patent Rejection** : Professor Dieter Selzer's 1980s patent application for his "digital jukebox" concept, which involved storing and transmitting all music via telephone lines, was rejected. The examiner deemed the proposal "impossible," stating, "It'll never work." "Selzer applied for a patent, but it was rejected on the grounds that the examiner decided that what he proposed was impossible."
**MP3 Development** : Carlheinz Brandenburg and his team at the Fraunhofer Institute for Digital Media Technology developed the algorithms for MP3 compression after considering Professor Selzer's rejected patent. Their breakthrough allowed digital audio files to be reduced by 10 to 12 times, making transmission over standard telephone lines feasible. "After much experimentation, Heinsb team figured it out. The algorithms used made a digital audio file 10 to 12 times smaller, which was enough for transmission down old-fashioned telephone lines."
**MP3 Compression** : The core innovation of MP3 technology was its ability to compress digital audio files significantly, typically by 10 to 12 times. This compression was crucial for enabling the transmission of music over limited bandwidth connections, such as old copper telephone lines. "The algorithms used made a digital audio file 10 to 12 times smaller, which was enough for transmission down old-fashioned telephone lines."
**MP3 Milestones** : MP3s became the worldwide standard in 1993, evolving from files with the ".BIT" extension. The technique was formally named Motion Pictures Expert Group 1, Layer 3 (MP3). On July 4, 1994, the Fraunhofer Society released the first MP3 encoder, followed by the first MP3 player software on September 9, 1995. "MP3s were made the worldwide standard in 1993."
**MP3 Impact on Music Industry** : Carlheinz Brandenburg, co-creator of the MP3, was once told that his team had "destroyed the music industry" because the technology facilitated widespread CD ripping and illegal file sharing. This unintended consequence dramatically reshaped how music was consumed and distributed. "Someone once asked Brandon Berg if he realized what his team had created. He looked at them and said, no, what? And that person replied, you've just destroyed the music industry."
**CD Ripping** : The advent of MP3 technology and the subsequent release of its source code by a hacker led to a surge in "CD ripping." This allowed individuals to easily convert physical CDs into digital files, which could then be shared freely over the internet, further disrupting the traditional music industry. "Things could even worse when a hacker found the source code. He created an even better version, set it free on the internet, and well, CD ripping went wild."
**Digital Jukebox to Streaming Evolution** : The concept of the digital jukebox, initially proposed by Professor Selzer, evolved into the modern streaming music service as internet exchange of songs became widespread. This progression allowed for on-demand access to music libraries, fulfilling the original vision. "As people got into the idea of exchanging songs over the internet, we really didn't have much further to go, at least in a tactical sense, before we went back to Professor Seltsor's idea of the digital jukebox."
**Definition of Streaming** : Streaming is defined as the ability to listen to songs transmitted to a device over the internet on demand. Full-featured streaming services allow access to any song on any number of devices, anytime and anywhere, exemplified by platforms like Spotify, Apple Music, and Deezer. "This is the ability to listen to songs transmitted to your device over the internet on demand."
**Real Audio Development** : Rob Glazer, an ex-Microsoft employee, founded Progressive Networks in 1994, viewing the internet as a new broadcasting system. His team created Real Audio, a proprietary system designed to stream audio in real-time and from dedicated servers, pioneering internet audio delivery. "He and his team created a proprietary system called Real Audio, which was designed to stream audio in both real-time and from a dedicated server."
**First Internet Stream (Baseball Game)** : Rob Glazer, a passionate Yankees fan, set up one of the very first internet audio streams: a baseball game between the Yankees and Seattle on September 5, 1995. This event marked a significant early application of his Real Audio technology. "Glazer was a huge Yankees fan, so one of the very first streams he set up was a game between them in Seattle on September 5, 1995."
**Rhapsody Development** : In 1999, a team created a program for Tune2.com, a customizable internet radio station, and developed a prototype of a "celestial jukebox" called Aladdin. In 2001, Listen.com purchased this technology, transforming Aladdin into Rhapsody, which launched on December 31, 2001, as the first modern on-demand streaming service offering an "all-you-can-eat buffet" of music for a flat monthly fee. "In 2001, all that tech was purchased by a company called Listen.com. And from there, Aladdin was transformed into the first modern on-demand streaming service, which was called Rhapsody."
**Rhapsody Acquisition and Rebranding** : After its launch, Rhapsody was acquired by Real Networks (formerly Progressive Networks, founded by Rob Glazer). In November 2011, Rhapsody acquired the intellectual property of Napster. By 2016, Rhapsody fully rebranded itself as Napster, operating under that name today. "And then it was bought by Real Networks, which was, of course, the new name for Rob Glazer's progressive networks. Nobody still exists. Except that in November 2011, it bought what was left of Napster, all the intellectual property. And in 2016, Rhapsody completely rebranded itself as Napster, which is how it operates today."
**Early Stereo System (Tiatrophone)** : The Tiatrophone system, demonstrated in 1881, featured a rudimentary form of stereo sound decades before it was officially "invented." It achieved this by placing 40 telephone transmitters on the left side of a stage and another 40 on the right, providing listeners with a spatialized audio experience in their homes. "And in stereo, too, this is like 70 years before stereo was invented because you had 40 transmitters put along the left side of the stage and another 40 along the right."
**Tiatrophone Expansion and Decline** : The Tiatrophone, or Electrophone in the UK, saw services roll out across Europe and was even demonstrated in New York. It operated as a subscription service for live music until 1931, when it was ultimately rendered obsolete by the advent of radio, which offered "wireless" transmission. "The Tiatrophone services rolled out across Europe. It appeared in the UK under the name Electrophone. And the technology was even demonstrated in New York."
**Radio's Impact on Wired Music Services** : The invention of radio ultimately led to the demise of the Tiatrophone/Electrophone service in 1931. Radio's ability to transmit audio wirelessly, without the need for extensive physical telephone lines running to individual homes, made it a superior and more convenient technology for accessing music. "This lasted a long time, too, all the way to 1931, in fact, and the thing that killed it, radio, which required no wires running all over the place."
**Yurgen Volmer's Origin of Beatles Haircut** : Yurgen Volmer, a friend of Astrid Kircher and Stu Sutcliffe, met John Lennon and Paul McCartney in Paris in 1961. Volmer wore his hair long and over his forehead, a style that "entranced" Lennon and McCartney, who asked him to cut their hair this way, initiating the Beatles' iconic mop top look. "There is some photographic evidence to suggest that Volmer had been wearing his hair long and in a part, something that Paul described as a long-haired Hitler thing."
**Astrid Kircher's Role in Beatles Haircut** : Astrid Kircher, Stu Sutcliffe's girlfriend, regularly cut The Beatles' hair into their distinctive new look during their long residency in Hamburg, about a year after Yurgen Volmer's initial cut. She first applied the style to Stu, then George, and finally John and Paul. "About a year later, when the Beatles had their long residency in Hamburg, Astrid Kircher cut their hair regularly into this new look."
**Gregory Coleman's Unsung Status** : Despite playing the "Amen Break," sampled in over 5,309 songs, drummer Gregory Coleman of The Winston's died homeless and broke in 2006, unaware of his profound impact on music history and without receiving any royalties. He remains an archetypal "unsung hero." "Gregory Coleman actually died homeless and broke in 2006, not knowing about what he'd done for music."
**Clyde Stubblefield's Recognition and Support** : Unlike Gregory Coleman, Clyde Stubblefield received some recognition, including various awards and a set of autographed sticks in the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame, despite not getting credit or royalties for his "Funky Drummer" break. Prince even paid tens of thousands for his healthcare, and he received an honorary doctorate before his death in 2017. "Clyde fared a lot better than Gregory Coleman. He received no credits on the James Brown song, nor did he get any royalties from the hundreds of times his playing was sampled."
**Clyde Stubblefield's Unsung Status** : Despite significant recognition from within the music community, including praise from artists and induction into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame for his influential "Funky Drummer" break, Clyde Stubblefield's widespread public recognition remained limited. He shaped music profoundly, "albeit unintentionally and very much uncompensated." "Yet you got to wonder how many people outside of the music community know of Clyde's Stubblefield and the incredible effect he had on shaping music with that one drum brick, albeit unintentionally and very much uncompensated."
**Jordan Mooney's Early Life and Fashion** : Born Pamela Ruck in Seaford in 1955, Jordan Mooney found herself a misfit in her small British hometown due to her unconventional fashion sense. After being expelled from school for her red and pink haircut (inspired by David Bowie), she moved to London. "She was born Pamela Ruck in 1955 in a British town called Seaford."
**Jordan Mooney's Role at Sex Boutique and Fashion Influence** : Jordan Mooney, after changing her name, worked at Malcolm McLaren and Vivian Westwood's "Sex" boutique on King's Road, London. She became a muse, modeling daring, unconventional clothes (wild hair, makeup, see-through items, bondage gear) custom-made by McLaren and Westwood. She acted as a "walking billboard" for the shop, which boosted business and attracted the "kids who didn't fit in," including those who would form the Sex Pistols. "She became something of amuse from McLaren and Westwood who began tannering clothes for Jordan who would then wear them everywhere."
**Jordan Mooney's Influence on British Punk** : Jordan Mooney was a crucial figure in the original British punk rock scene, influencing its fashion sense through her work at the Sex boutique and her association with the Sex Pistols, whom she followed everywhere as part of the "Bromley contingent." Andy Warhol photographed her and featured her in a film. Although she wasn't a musician, she served as a "magnet and then as glue" for an important part of the scene, potentially even contributing to the Sex Pistols' formation. "Jordan wasn't a singer, she didn't play any instruments but she did act as a magnet and then as glue for an important portion of the original British punk rock scene."
**Bromley Contingent** : The Bromley Contingent was a group of fans, including Jordan Mooney, who closely followed the Sex Pistols in their early days. Their presence and shared fashion sensibilities contributed to the development of the British punk rock scene. "Jordan followed the pistols everywhere as part of a pack of fans known as the Bromley contingent and wherever she went she influenced the fashion sense of punk rock."
**Jordan Mooney's Later Life** : After her job at the Sex boutique ended, Jordan Mooney married Kevin Mooney of Adam and the Ants. She later struggled with drug use, moved back to the seaside, and spent three decades working as a veterinarian's assistant after raising Burmese cats. "She fell into drugs and then moved back to the seaside. She raised Burmese cats which led to a job as a veterinarian's assistant that lasted for three decades."
**Tina Bell's Racial and Gender Barriers** : One theory for Tina Bell and Bam Bam's lack of recognition in grunge history is that audiences and critics might have been unable to accept a black woman fronting a "punky rock band in a man's world." This suggests that race and gender played a role in their being overlooked. "I mean, a black woman, fronting this kind of punky rock band in a man's world. Were audiences and critics unable to grasp such a person doing such a thing? Possibly."
**Tina Bell's Posthumous Recognition Efforts** : At the time of Tina Bell's death in 2012, efforts began to recognize her legacy, including a documentary and memoir titled "Conversations with the Grunge Queen." Her son, TJ, an Academy Award-winning documentary co-producer, is actively involved in these efforts to ensure her contributions are acknowledged. "At the time of Tina's death in 2012, there was an effort to finally recognize her legacy with a documentary in memoir called Conversations with the Grunge Queen."
