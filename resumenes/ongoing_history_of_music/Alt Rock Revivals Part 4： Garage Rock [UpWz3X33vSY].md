# artist - The Kingsmen

## members
Joe Eli (singer)
Lynn Easton (drummer)

## genres
Garage Rock
R&B (original song genre)

## curiosities
**House Band at Producer's Club** : The Kingsmen were the house band at an all-ages club owned by the local DJ from Portland radio station 91 Kissin, who also produced their famous recording of "Louis Louis." "He owned an all-ages club that featured the Kingsman, which is the band we're talking about here, as the house band."

## songs
**Louis Louis (1963)** : This track, considered an all-time classic and one of the most famous Garage Rock anthems, was recorded on April 6, 1963, in Portland, Oregon. It cost only $50 to produce and was a cover of an obscure 1957 R&B song by Louisiana singer Richard Berry. The session took place at Northwestern Motion Pictures and Recording, produced by a local DJ. The band blasted it out in a single take, meaning the released version is their only studio performance that day. The arrangement changed the original beat, and singer Joe Eli, wearing heavy braces, sang directly into a single boom microphone, contributing to the unintelligible vocals. The song is famous for a mistake: after the guitar solo, Eli comes in early, stops, and drummer Lynn Easton plays an extra drum fill to cover the error; this mistake is now intentionally replicated in modern performances. It became a regional, then national, hit, and rumors of pornographic lyrics led to its ban from radio stations and entirely in the state of Indiana. "On April 6, 1963, a bunch of dudes from Portland, Oregon spent 50 bucks to record a cover of an obscure 1957 R&B song called Louis Louis, originally done by a Louisiana singer named Richard Berry."

# genre - Garage Rock

## curiosities
**Definition** : Garage Rock is defined as basic, raw, loose, ragged rock and roll, primarily featuring guitar, bass, drums, vocals, and often a thin-sounding Farfisa organ. It is typically unsophisticated, primal, and primitive, built around power or bar chords, and can sometimes be aggressive, played in informal settings like basements, rec rooms, or garages. "It's the basic, raw, loose, ragged rock and roll you and your buddies play for no reason other than it feels good."
**Origins and Original Era (1964-1968)** : The roots of Garage Rock trace back to the early 1960s, sparked by inspiration from The Beatles and the British Invasion. Initially, it was simply called "Rock," not "Garage Rock." Its golden age was between 1964 (especially after The Beatles' Ed Sullivan appearance) and around 1968, when rock became more polished. An estimated 184,000 Garage bands formed in the US during those four years alone. "The roots of Garage Rock began in the early 1960s, when bands inspired by the Beatles and the rest of the British invasion started springing up like mushrooms."
**Regional Diversity** : Garage Rock lacked a single, unified sound, instead exhibiting strong regional variations across different areas. For instance, Surf Rock developed distinctly on the American West Coast. "There was no one sound for Garage Rock bands. Everything was very regional."
**Decline of Original Era (c. 1968)** : The original Garage Rock era concluded around 1968, largely influenced by technological advancements in recording studios that led to more sophisticated recordings. Rock bands themselves became more refined in their live performances, songwriting, and overall presentation. The rise of the album format also contributed, as Garage Rock had primarily found success through 7-inch singles and struggled to adapt to albums, which became the preferred format with the shift from AM to FM radio. "The original garage rock era ended around 1968, largely because of technology."
**First Revival: Punk Rock (Mid-1970s)** : The punk rock explosion of the mid-1970s is recognized as the first revival of Garage Rock. This new incarnation was essentially the same fundamental sound, but typically louder, sometimes angrier, occasionally political, and often much faster, while maintaining its core DNA. Bands like the Sex Pistols, The Clash, and The Damned are considered Garage Rock at their core due to their back-to-basics approach. "So the punk rock explosion of the middle 70s was the first ever revival of garage rock."
**Minor Revival: Garage Punk (Late 1980s/Early 1990s)** : After the punk explosion and its subsequent diversification in the 1980s, a minor revival emerged in the late 1980s and early 1990s, labeled "Garage Punk." These bands, such as The Headcoats, The Mighty Caesars, and The Mummies, drew more heavily from The Stooges than The Kingsmen, resulting in a louder and faster sound compared to the original Garage Rock acts. "After the punk explosion and its segmentation and stratification into many different forms throughout the 1980s, there was a minor revival in the late 80s and early 1990s."
**Biggest Revival (Late 1990s/Early 2000s)** : The most significant Garage Rock revival since punk occurred at the end of the 1990s and into the early 2000s. This resurgence was spurred by a disillusionment with the state of alternative rock, the burnout of grunge, the polarizing nature of nu-metal, and the dominance of pop music. It began in New York, with bands like The Strokes and The White Stripes, and coincided with a post-9/11 shift in public taste and youth seeking music with "more teeth, more bite." "This is what happened with Garage Rock at the very end of the 1990s in the early 2000s."
**Persistent Underground Presence** : Garage Rock is characterized as an enduring underground phenomenon that consistently resurfaces into the mainstream at unexpected times and in unforeseen ways. Its long history suggests it is a lasting genre, always "bubbling up" and awaiting its next revival. "Garage Rock seems to be an underground thing that is always there. Something that's always bubbling up into the mainstream in unexpected ways at unexpected times."

# genre - Surf Rock

## curiosities
**West Coast Emergence** : Surf Rock developed on the American West Coast, becoming a significant musical trend in certain circles within Southern California. It was largely pioneered by Dick Dale and the Deltones and initially began as an instrumental genre. "For example, on the American West Coast, something that became known as Surf Rock popped up."
**Intentional Guitar Distortion** : A defining characteristic of Surf Rock was the intentional distortion of electric guitars, achieved through heavy reverb and by physically modifying guitars and amplifiers in ways that voided warranties. This practice was necessary because dedicated guitar pedals and other outboard effects were not yet available. "What we had here was electric guitar, as with lots of reverb. And unlike just about everyone else, the guitars were intentionally distorted."

# genre - Punk Rock

## curiosities
**Origin as Garage Rock Revival** : Punk Rock, emerging in the mid-1970s, is identified as the first formal revival of Garage Rock. The term "punk" itself had even been previously used to describe some original 1960s Garage Rock bands. This new form maintained the basic DNA of Garage Rock but was generally louder, angrier, faster, and sometimes more politically charged. "The popularity of the Nuggets collection, combined with the rise of Iggy Pop and the studious and a few other like-minded bands, prompted the first garage rock revival, except now that it was being called punk."
**Influence of Nuggets Bands** : Many of the artists featured on the "Nuggets" compilation became heroes and direct inspirations for the musicians who would go on to create the punk rock movement. "Many of these so-called Nuggets bands became heroes to the people who would soon create this new thing called punk rock."

# instrument - Electric Guitar

**Surf Rock Distortion Technique (Pre-Pedals)** : In early Surf Rock, before the widespread availability of guitar pedals, guitarists achieved intentionally distorted sounds for their electric guitars by "screwing with their guitars and amps."
