## curiosities
**First Public Performance (1975) - Art School Debut** : The band's first show was on November 6, 1975, at an art school. Despite the venue, the student organizer was so repulsed by the performance that he cut the electricity to the stage after just 10 minutes. "Their first show was on November the 6th, 1975."
**Formation and Naming** : The band's manager, Malcolm McLaren, assembled the group as a "living, breathing advertisement" for his store called Sex. The band's name, Sex Pistols, derived "Sex" from the store and "Pistols" because it sounded dangerous and "emish." "He called the band Sex Pistols, Sex after the store and pistols because, well, that sounded dangerous and weirdly as an emish."
**Signing with EMI (1976) - Subversive Victory** : On October 8, 1976, the Sex Pistols signed a deal with EMI records for £40,000, which was considered a fortune at the time. This move was admired by the punk community, who saw it not as selling out, but as a "major subversive victory" for managing to "swindle such a huge amount of money from a gullible multinational major record label." "And the home of the Beatles no less. This was considered to be a major subversive victory for the punk rock cause."
**Infamous "Today" TV Appearance (1976) - National Scandal** : On December 1, 1976, the Sex Pistols were booked as last-minute substitute guests on the bland "Today" chat show after Queen cancelled due to Freddie Mercury needing a dentist. Lured to the studio with champagne and beer, the band members were "heavily toasted" upon arrival. The two-minute interview, which featured multiple instances of swearing on live television, including the word "f**k," immediately became a gigantic national scandal, leading to headlines condemning "punk rock scum" and "the filth in the fury." "This two minutes has gone down in history as one of the most legendary rock and roll television appearances ever."
**EMI Contract Termination (1977) - "Toxic" Band** : Following their infamous TV appearance and alleged public vomiting episodes, including one at Heathrow Airport (exaggerated by tabloids), the Sex Pistols became "toxic." EMI Records, the label of The Beatles and owner of Abbey Road studios, could no longer tolerate the controversy and paid the band £40,000 to terminate their contract. "So they paid the pistols 40,000 pounds to just go away."
**A&M Records Deal and Termination (1977) - Six-Day Contract** : Two months after being dropped by EMI, A&M Records signed the Sex Pistols. However, a party immediately following the signing at the label's offices resulted in significant damage, and guitarist Steve Jones was reportedly "having sex with a staffer in the bathroom." Facing questions from head office in Los Angeles and complaints from other A&M artists like The Carpenters, the label dropped the Sex Pistols after just six days, providing a buyout worth £75,000. "They were on A&M for just six days."
**Behavior and Reputation** : The Sex Pistols were linked to violence at their gigs, with stories of them spitting on the audience and vice-versa. Some accounts alleged their "favorite trick" was to vomit in public. These incidents led to their shows being picketed, promoters being spooked, and the group being banned from "just about everywhere," which ironically only made them more famous. "Some said that the pistols favorite trick was to vomit in public."

## albums
**Ramones (1976) - Debut Album** : Recorded at a studio in Radio City Music Hall, the album sessions began on February 2, 1976, and were completed by February 19, 1976, costing a total of $6,400. Legend states the master tape was so loud it irreparably damaged the pressing equipment, "blowing the cutter head clean off the machine." Upon its release on April 23, 1976, it was largely treated as a joke due to its fourteen two-minute songs, 28-minute length, and lack of guitar solos. However, it was also understood by some as embodying "the unpretentious joy of making rock and roll" with raw "energy, enthusiasm, the passion, and the realness of it all." "When the album was released on April 23rd, 1976, almost everyone treated this record as some kind of joke."

## songs
**Blitzkrieg Bop (1976) - First British Single** : Released as the group's first British single five days before their July 4, 1976, London show, this song was inspired in part by the chanting of the Bay City Rollers' hit, "Saturday Night." "The Ramones, with Blitzkrieg Bop from 1976, a song inspired in part by the chanting of a big hit of the day, was by the Bay City Rollers."

## curiosities
**Early Demos and Label Search (1974)** : The Ramones recorded their first demos in late 1974, costing about $2,000. They sent cassettes and press clippings to every record company they could find, but no one was interested except Sire. "They had recorded their first demos in late 1974, cost them about $2,000."
**Sire Records Signing - Trailblazers** : Joey Ramone stated that Sire Records "understood us best" and that the band wouldn't get "lost in the shuffle" by signing with them, despite later interest from bigger labels. The Ramones considered themselves "sort of the trailblazers," conducting the first national tour and inspiring other bands to get signed after witnessing their success. "We decided to go with Sire because we felt the seamless style understood us best and that we wouldn't get lost in the shuffle being on a major company."
**First British Show (1976) - Catalyst for UK Punk** : The Ramones played their first British show at the Roundhouse in London on July 4, 1976, the date of the American Bicentennial. This concert, which was attended by members of the Sex Pistols and The Clash, was a major catalyst in the development of British punk rock, with the band being treated "like Royal Teal" and inspiring a new generation of musicians. "And from that night on, the UK music scene was really never the same. The Ramones became a major catalyst in the development of British punk rock."

# artist - The Damned

## labels
Stiff Records

## albums
**New Rose (1976) - First British Punk Single** : Released on October 22, 1976, on the new label Stiff, this 45 rpm single had a budget of just £50. It holds historical significance as the "first made in Britain punk rock record." Beyond its chronological first, it was crucial for demonstrating a "second way to go" for punk rockers: making records "cheap, do it dirty, and then release it through one of the many independent record labels." "The Damned and New Rose, the seven-inch single that has gone down in history as being the first made in Britain punk rock record."

## curiosities
**Pioneering DIY Approach** : The Damned's release of "New Rose" proved that bands could bypass major labels and "make a record ourselves" with a minimal budget, championing a "do it yourself" (DIY) approach that convinced many groups to be more proactive in recording and releasing music. "This DIY, this do it yourself approach, preached by the Damned, convinced many groups to take a much more proactive approach when it came to recording and releasing music."

# genre - Punk Rock

## curiosities
**Social Significance (1970s Britain)** : Punk rock in Britain emerged from a deeply troubled society in the mid-1970s, characterized by the Arab oil embargo, an endless recession, widespread strikes, high youth unemployment, and a rigid class system. It provided a genuine social outlet, a "blow-up" for the frustration, anger, and energy of a generation that felt there was "no future." "There is a genuine social point to punk rock."
**DIY Ethos** : Punk rock championed the idea that it was no longer necessary to be an accomplished musician to make music, liberating and empowering new bands to form almost hourly. The "do it yourself" approach, demonstrated by bands like The Damned and Buzzcocks, encouraged groups to proactively record and release their own music cheaply through independent labels, making independent recordings prevalent by 1977. "Liberated and empowered by the fact that it was no longer necessary to be an accomplished musician in order to make music, new bands were formed almost hourly."
**Growth and Organization (1976)** : By September 1976, hundreds of punk rock bands had formed across Britain, attracting regular followings and playing gigs wherever possible. This growth led to the organization of larger events, such as the 100 Club Punk Rock Festival, to assess the burgeoning scene. "Still, by September of 1976, there were hundreds and hundreds and hundreds of these punk rock bands all over Britain."
**UK vs. New York Styles** : British punk was distinct from its New York counterpart. While American punk was described as "artsy, quoting French poets," UK punk was fueled by the stark realities of "inequities of the British class system, a failing economy, a crumbling empire, and a new generation of unemployed young people." "But British punk wasn't just a copy of what was happening in New York."

**Britain's Mid-1970s Crisis** : In the mid-1970s, Britain faced severe economic and social turmoil, including the Arab oil embargo, a persistent recession, widespread strikes by various workers (from coal miners to gravediggers), and high unemployment, particularly among young people. **Malcolm McLaren's Fashion Influence** : Malcolm McLaren, owner of the King's Road shop "Sex" in Chelsea, London, was impressed by Richard Hell of Television's improvised fashion of holding torn clothes together with safety pins due to poverty. McLaren considered this a "brilliant fashion statement" and took the "safety pin look" back to England, immediately offering similar clothes for sale at his store. "Malcolm thought this was a brilliant fashion statement and he took the safety pin look, some look back to England where he immediately began offering similar clothes for sale at his shop in the King's Road in the Chelsea area of London."
**DIY (Do It Yourself) Movement in Music** : The "do it yourself" (DIY) approach to recording and releasing music gained significant traction in Britain, particularly after The Damned's "New Rose" demonstrated that groups could make records cheaply and release them through independent labels. This philosophy encouraged bands to take a proactive stance in music creation, leading to a surge in independent recordings by 1977. "This DIY, this do it yourself approach, preached by the Damned, convinced many groups to take a much more proactive approach when it came to recording and releasing music."

"January 3rd, three guys named Steve Jobs, Steve Wozniak and Ronald Wayne formed a company called Apple Computers."
