**Parallel Universe** : At the end of the 1980s, alt rock fans lived in a "parallel universe," a "petri dish" of like-minded individuals who shared musical views and sensibilities, deliberately avoiding mainstream offerings like hair metal, classic rock, New Kids on the Block, and Top 40 radio.

# instrument - Singing Arc

## curiosities
**First Purely Electronic Instrument (1899)** : Invented by physicist William Duddell in 1899, the Singing Arc was discovered when the coil and capacitor of a carbon arc lamp produced sound. Duddell learned to control the oscillations in the circuit with a keyboard to alter its pitch, making it the first purely electronic musical instrument.

# instrument - Theremin

## curiosities
**Touchless Electronic Instrument (Early 1920s)** : Invented by Soviet physicist Leon Theremin 20 years after the Singing Arc, this instrument could be played without being touched. Coils inside its box generated an electrical field; waving one hand through this field produced a tone, and waving the other hand changed its pitch. When played correctly, it mimics a "warbling female opera singer" and was notably used for the theme in the sci-fi film "The Day the Earth Stood Still." It was experimented with by groups like The Beach Boys and Led Zeppelin in the 1960s.
**Contribution to Rhythmicon (1930s)** : Leon Theremin also helped develop the Rhythmicon in the 1930s.

# instrument - Synthesizer

## curiosities
**Early Room-Sized Models (1950s)** : In the 1950s, Columbia University in New York built a synthesizer that filled an entire room. It could only be programmed by making hundreds of connections with patch cords.
**Rock and Roll Debut (1967)** : The synthesizer made its debut in rock and roll in 1967 with a quick, twenty-second solo in The Monkees' song "Star Collector."
**Initial Skepticism in Rock** : Few rock musicians initially took the concept of purely electronic music seriously, considering synthesizers suitable only for "the odd effect."
**Commercially Feasible Size (1960s)** : Dr. Robert Moog, with support from Columbia, Princeton, and the University of Toronto, managed to shrink the synthesizer to a commercially viable size within two years, making it much more accessible than room-filling machines.
**Pioneering German Use (1970s)** : By the turn of the 1970s, German musicians had already been exposed to almost 20 years of electronic sounds, leading many young Germans to adopt synthesizers as a way to forge a musical identity distinct from British and American rock and roll.
**Ubiquity in the 1980s** : By the beginning of the 1980s, synthesizers were "everywhere," largely due to Kraftwerk demonstrating their meaningful application in pop music.

**Pre-Programmed Beats (Mid-20th Century)** : Following the Rhythmicon, a series of rhythm machines emerged that were pre-programmed to play standard beats such as waltzes, tangos, and foxtrots.
**Evolution into Beatboxes (1950s)** : By the end of the 1950s, these beatboxes were integrated into organs, like the old Yamaha Electones or Wurlitzer models.

# instrument - Drum Machine

## curiosities
**Evolution from Rhythm Machines (1960s-1970s)** : Rhythm machines evolved into drum machines in the 1960s and 1970s, with companies like Roland, Multivox, and Bentley selling increasingly sophisticated devices that were popular with new German electronic bands.
**First Album Use (1972)** : The first rock band to use a drum machine for an entire album was Kingdom Come in 1972.
**Flesh and Blood Drummers' Concerns (1980s)** : The proliferation of drum machines in the 1980s, especially after the introduction of the Roland TR-808, made "flesh and blood drummers really nervous."
**On-Stage Use (Techno Pop)** : In techno pop, rhythm tracks were often pre-recorded and played back on stage using reel-to-reel tape machines, or the drum machines themselves were used on stage, as with Echo & The Bunnymen and The Sisters of Mercy.

# instrument - Roland TR-808

## curiosities
**Analog Drum Machine (1980s)** : Roland unveiled the TR-808, an analog drum machine with a unique sound that inspired many musicians, contributing to the nervousness of human drummers in the 1980s.

# instrument - Sequencer

## curiosities
**Device to Control Other Devices** : The sequencer was a significant technological development that allowed musicians to program other devices (like synthesizers and drum machines) to perform specific actions at precise times.

# instrument - Roland MC-4

## curiosities
**Early Useful Sequencer (1978)** : Roland introduced the MC-4 in 1978, which was one of the first truly useful sequencers. It could store approximately 12,000 notes, though they had to be entered one by one. Once programmed, the data was stored on a cassette tape. Despite being "absolute hell to operate," it allowed for the sequencing of synthesizer events without further human intervention once started, enabling groups like The Human League to perform live outside the studio.

# instrument - MIDI (Musical Instrument Digital Interface)

## curiosities
**Industry Standard for Communication (June 1983)** : Introduced in June 1983, MIDI provided a common language and industry standard for electronic musical instruments. This allowed synthesizers, drum machines, and sequencers from different manufacturers to communicate with each other, making it possible for a single musician to produce the sound of an entire orchestra on stage.

# instrument - Fairlight Computer Musical Instrument

## curiosities
**Pioneering Digital Sampler and Synthesizer (Australia)** : An Australian company developed the Fairlight CMI, a device capable of synthesizing sounds, sequencing events, and, significantly, digitally sampling sounds for the first time. It allowed users to record, isolate, and manipulate any sound. Samples appeared as waveforms on a black and white monitor and could be manipulated with a light pen.
**High Cost and Studio Use ($30,000+)** : Initially selling for $30,000 and up, the Fairlight was primarily affordable only for high-end recording studios. Its capabilities were crucial for creating sounds like those in Frankie Goes to Hollywood's "Relax."
**Comparison to Akai S-1000** : By 1988, the Akai S-1000 was 100 times more powerful than the original Fairlight and one-tenth of its price.

# instrument - Synclavier

## curiosities
**Competitor to Fairlight** : The Synclavier was mentioned as a competitor to the Fairlight, offering similar advanced sampling and synthesis capabilities.

# instrument - EMU Systems gear

## curiosities
**Sampler for Michael Jackson's "Thriller"** : EMU Systems manufactured gear, including a sampler that cost $10,000 and could sample anything up to two seconds in length. Michael Jackson used some of this gear on his "Thriller" album.

# instrument - Magnetic Recording Tape

## curiosities
**Enabler of Early Sampling (1940s)** : The advent of magnetic recording tape in the 1940s made sampled sounds possible in music. This included everything from standard sound effects to manipulated shortwave radio interference.

# instrument - Akai S-900

## curiosities
**Affordable Sampler Breakthrough (1986)** : Released by Akai in 1986, the S-900 was a significant breakthrough in making sampling more accessible. This "little sampler" could store a 12-second sample and cost only $1,000, representing a shift towards more powerful, smaller, and cheaper technology.

# instrument - Akai S-1000

## curiosities
**Powerful and Affordable Sampler (1988)** : By 1988, Akai was selling the S-1000, a sampler that was described as 100 times more powerful than the original $30,000 Fairlight, yet sold at one-tenth of its price. This made advanced sampling capabilities widely available.

# instrument - Turntables

## curiosities
**Musical Instruments for DJs** : In the hands of skilled DJs, turntables and mixers transcended their original purpose to become genuine musical instruments, capable of creating entirely new music from spare parts, akin to an orchestra but without the need for expensive studio equipment like the Fairlight. This technique is now known as "turntablism."

# curiosity

**Early Mechanical Music** : Historically, all music was made mechanically, requiring physical actions such as hitting, strumming, plucking, or forcing air through instruments.
**Emergence of Electricity in Music (Early 1980s)** : By the early 1980s, electricity had been "tamed" to not only power new musical instruments but also to generate musical energy itself. This led to experiments in making anything into music, or chopping up existing music for reassembly.
**Mechanical vs. Digital Sound Manipulation** : Early experiments in sound manipulation involved "old ways" like mechanically chopping sounds with turntables and tape machines, while new methods utilized computers and samplers.
**Late 1980s Musical Landscape** : By the end of the 1980s, music was being made by machines, orchestrated by computers, and programmed by punks, fundamentally changing the musical landscape.
**West German Radio Electronic Studio (1950s)** : In the 1950s, West German radio established an experimental electronic studio, exposing German musicians to electronic sounds nearly two decades before the 1970s. This early exposure was crucial for the development of a distinct German electronic music identity.
**Pre-recorded Rhythm Tracks in Techno Pop** : In the techno pop era, rhythm tracks were frequently pre-recorded and then played back on stage using prominently displayed reel-to-reel tape machines.
**Frankenstein Monster Analogy for Sampling** : Sampling is likened to "constructing a cool Frankenstein monster," where new music is created by assembling a collection of used pieces—isolated sound bits from various sources—in creative ways, rather than necessarily using all new parts.
**Sampling as a Surgical Excision** : Sampling is defined as a composer surgically excising a sound, from a fraction of a second to any desired length, from any source for use in another composition. Once isolated, the sample can be used as is or further manipulated with computers or specialized devices like the Fairlight, Synclavier, or EMU Systems gear.
**Found Sound Philosophy** : Some musicians adopted the philosophy of "found sound," integrating the sounds of everyday life into their compositions.
**Technology Democratization (Samplers)** : The high cost of early sampling technology (like the $30,000 Fairlight) initially restricted its use to high-end studios. However, Japanese companies like Akai revolutionized accessibility by introducing more powerful, smaller, and cheaper samplers, such as the Akai S-900 ($1,000, 12-second sample) in 1986 and the S-1000 (100 times more powerful than the original Fairlight at one-tenth the price) by 1988.
**Turntablism** : The technique of chopping records into sounds that are then manipulated into something totally new, as performed by skilled DJs using turntables and mixers, is now called turntablism. These DJs made their equipment into genuine musical instruments, creating new music from spare parts without needing expensive samplers.
**Bridging Indie and Dance Music** : New Order's "Blue Monday" was a pivotal record that successfully bridged the gap between cool indie music and dance music, helping to revitalize the concept of dancing as cool again after the era of Disco.
**Popularity of 12-Inch Remixes** : Following "Blue Monday," the 12-inch remix format became a popular business for many bands, including Depeche Mode, The Cure, The Human League, Simple Minds, The Cult, Killing Joke, Erasure, The Psychedelic Furs, and Madness.
**Resurrection of the DJ and Club Culture (1980s)** : The 1980s witnessed the resurrection of the DJ and the rise of club culture, which became intimately tied to the evolving music technology and sound manipulation techniques.
**DJ as Performer in Rave Culture** : In the UK's rave culture, which emerged in the late 1980s, the DJ ascended to the role of performer and star, often remaining anonymous in the booth while creating mixes that made people dance, shifting the focus away from traditional bands or songs.
**Summer of Love (UK, 1988)** : The year 1988 in the UK was dubbed "The Summer of Love," marking the beginnings of a new dance and rave culture, coinciding with the widespread adoption of the drug Ecstasy.
**UK Government's Anti-Rave Legislation (1994)** : By 1994, the UK scene had grown so large and prominent that the government attempted to ban raves through the Criminal Justice and Public Order Act.
**Alternative Music Preferences (Late 1980s)** : By the late 1980s, fans of non-mainstream music explicitly rejected genres such as hair metal, classic rock, and popular acts like New Kids on the Block, as well as anything found on Top 40 radio, preferring to keep "good music" to themselves and their friends.
