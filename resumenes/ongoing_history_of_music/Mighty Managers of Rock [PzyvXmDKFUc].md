# artist - David Bowie

## albums
**Hunky Dory (1971) – Earnings Dispute** : This album's earnings, dating back to 1971, were included in the financial settlement terms when David Bowie sought to terminate his contract with manager Tony DeFries and Main Man in April 1975. Main Man was to receive half of Bowie's earnings from this album through an upcoming live album.
**Santa Monica 72 (2008) – Live Album Release** : This live album started as a live radio broadcast, subsequently morphed into a bootleg, then received a semi-official release, before finally achieving full legal release in 2008. The record's complex journey caused considerable difficulties for Bowie, his manager Tony DeFries, and others involved.

## curiosities
**Management by Tony DeFries (1970-1975)** : Tony DeFries began managing David Bowie in 1970, after being recommended to the struggling young singer. Within 18 months, DeFries secured Bowie a new deal with RCA Records and successfully transformed him into an international star, guiding his entire early 1970s career, including the iconic Ziggy Stardust era.
**One-Sided Contract with Main Man (1972)** : David Bowie signed a new contract with Tony DeFries' management company, Main Man, at the end of September 1972. This agreement later proved to be very one-sided, with Bowie realizing by late 1974 that despite selling millions of albums, most of the profits were not reaching him, prompting his desire to exit the contract.
**Split from Tony DeFries (1975)** : On January 29, 1975, Bowie informed his label, RCA, of his intention to split from Tony DeFries, triggering a dispute over the ownership of Bowie's next album, which neither the label nor DeFries fully controlled. A marathon 48-hour negotiation in April 1975 finally led to a deal for Bowie's release.
**Harsh Separation Terms (1975)** : The terms of David Bowie's separation from Tony DeFries in April 1975 were reportedly so severe that Bowie broke down and cried at the conference table, remaining in shock for a week. The agreement stipulated that Main Man would receive half of Bowie's earnings from all albums, retroactively from *Hunky Dory* (1971) up to an upcoming live album, and DeFries was guaranteed 16% of Bowie's gross earnings for seven subsequent years, through to 1982. Bowie signed these terms due to his intense desire to leave the contract.

# artist - Sex Pistols

## members
Johnny Rotten

## labels
EMI
A&M Records

## curiosities
**Formation and Naming by Malcolm McLaren** : Malcolm McLaren created the Sex Pistols as a "living, breathing advertisement" for his Kings Road shop (then renamed Sex) and an outlet for his anti-establishment political beliefs. The group's name, "Sex Pistols," was derived from the shop's name and the perceived danger of "pistols."
**Early Label Troubles** : The Sex Pistols' initial management, Glitter Best (Malcolm McLaren's company), secured a deal with EMI, but the band was quickly dropped due to their controversial nature and paid to leave. A subsequent contract with A&M Records lasted only five days before they were again paid to depart. Their third deal with Virgin Records ultimately "stuck."
**Conflict with Malcolm McLaren** : Despite becoming a sensation and challenging the British establishment, the band, particularly singer Johnny Rotten, grew distrustful of Malcolm McLaren. McLaren was accused of widespread mismanagement, and band members frequently reported that funds were unavailable when they sought payment.
**Implosion and Litigation (1978-1987)** : The band's relationship with Malcolm McLaren rapidly deteriorated, culminating in their implosion at the end of a "disastrously executed American tour" in 1978. This was followed by years of litigation between band members and Glitter Best, with the band finally regaining control of their music in 1987.
**Bernie Rhodes' Involvement** : Bernie Rhodes, a figure in the early punk rock scene, initially worked with Malcolm McLaren on the Sex Pistols. However, his offer to co-manage the band was rebuffed, which greatly annoyed him. Rhodes later claimed to have "invented punk rock" by recruiting Johnny Rotten for the band.

## songs
**The Magnificent Seven (Dance Mix)** : The creation of this 12-inch dance remix was a direct result of manager Bernie Rhodes convincing the band of the commercial viability and artistic merit of dance remixes.
**Rock the Casbah (1982)** : This song contains a line sung by Joe Strummer, "now the king told his boogeyman, you have to let that ragga drop." **Sean Flynn (1982)** : An eight-minute song that was originally slated for the *Combat Rock* album. However, the record company insisted on its removal, and the band's manager, Bernie Rhodes, agreed to drop it from the album. This decision is referenced in the song "Rock the Casbah."

## curiosities
**Management by Bernie Rhodes (Initial & Return)** : Bernie Rhodes, a figure from the punk rock era, formed The Clash, viewing them as his own version of the Sex Pistols after his offer to co-manage the latter was rejected by Malcolm McLaren. Rhodes initially mentored the group, encouraging them to write about social and political issues. However, he was ousted by 1978 after negotiating a "very poor record deal" for the band. He returned to manage The Clash in 1981, alongside other bands, and during this second tenure, he notably convinced the band of the value of 12-inch dance remixes.
**Interim Management (1978-1981)** : After Bernie Rhodes' initial departure in 1978, The Clash were managed by Caroline Coon, a painter and rock journalist, but she "didn't work out." The management company that succeeded her also proved unsuccessful, leading to Rhodes' return in 1981.

# artist - U2

## members
Adam Clayton

## venues
Project Arts Centre

## curiosities
**Early Days as The Hype (1978)** : In March 1978, the band, then known as The Hype, was a four-piece group from Dublin known for their "terrible Ramones covers" but also for having good original songs. They regularly visited the offices of CBS Ireland, seeking guidance from marketing manager Jackie Hayden, who had been a judge at a battle of the bands where they won prize money for their first demos.
**Initial Self-Management and Paul McGuinness's Recruitment** : Bassist Adam Clayton initially served as the band's manager. He persistently pursued Paul McGuinness, a film technician turned aspiring manager, convincing him to attend a show at the Project Arts Centre where the band, now renamed U2, was opening for The Gamblers.
**Paul McGuinness as Manager (1978-2014)** : Impressed by their performance, Paul McGuinness agreed to manage U2 in March 1978. He promised them little beyond the future possibility of affording their own band. His commission was set at 20%, an unusually high rate, with all profits to be shared equally among the five individuals involved (the four band members and McGuinness). McGuinness managed U2 for nearly four decades through his company, Principal Management, before stepping down in 2014 and selling the company to Live Nation.

# artist - New Order

## labels
Factory Records

## venues
The Haçienda

## curiosities
**Rebirth from Joy Division** : Following the suicide of singer Ian Curtis in May 1980, the remaining members of Joy Division reformed as New Order, retaining Rob Gretton as their manager.
**Rob Gretton's Managerial Role** : As manager of New Order, Rob Gretton was deeply involved in the band's creative process, doing everything from helping to sort out song titles to even singing backup on some recordings.
**Influence of Dance Music** : Rob Gretton's strong enthusiasm for American dance music in the early 1980s profoundly influenced New Order's sound, as he directed the band's attention towards this emerging genre.
**Founding of The Haçienda (1982)** : Rob Gretton was one of the key figures behind New Order's decision to open their own nightclub in Manchester. The result was The Haçienda, a venue built in a former yacht showroom that became a focal point for the Manchester music scene and a significant part of 1980s DJ culture.
**Financial Drain of The Haçienda** : Despite its cultural importance, The Haçienda, which opened in 1982, was a constant financial drain. It proved to be a "complete and utter financial disaster" for New Order, costing the band millions of pounds throughout its operation.
**Rob Gretton's Long-Term Management** : Rob Gretton managed New Order for approximately 20 years, continuing his relationship with the band through the 1990s following their reformation from Joy Division.
**Rob Gretton's £1,000 Bet (1988)** : In 1988, Rob Gretton famously wagered £1,000 that a particular New Order song would reach the Top 10 in the UK charts. However, the song ultimately peaked at number 11, resulting in Gretton losing the bet.

## curiosities
**Management by Susan Silver (1985-1998)** : Susan Silver began managing Soundgarden in 1985, formally taking over in 1986. She continued to manage them until their breakup in 1997, formally retiring from working with the band and shutting down her company, Silver Management, in 1998.
**Chris Cornell and Susan Silver's Relationship** : Susan Silver began dating Soundgarden's singer, Chris Cornell, approximately a year after she became the band's manager in 1986. They married in 1990. The couple initially kept their personal and professional lives separate, with many fans unaware that the singer and manager were married.
**Lawsuit Against Susan Silver (2003)** : Following their divorce in 2003, Chris Cornell initiated a lawsuit against Susan Silver for $1 million in damages. He alleged that as Soundgarden's manager, she had diverted funds owed to him to other band members. The suit also claimed that Silver refused to return Cornell's guitars, demos, journals, notes, and his two Grammy Awards statues. It further sought to bar her from ever representing Soundgarden again due to "irreconcilable conflicts of interest." This lawsuit contributed to the delay in a Soundgarden reunion.

# curiosity

**Role of Music Manager** : Managers handle all business aspects for artists, including booking gigs, marketing, promotions, publicity, road crews, finances (collecting money, paying bills), and overseeing infrastructure (lawyers, accountants). Beyond business, they can act as advisors, fixers, parent figures, bail bondsmen, bouncers, psychologists, and even amateur physicians/pharmacists. The job requires staying abreast of trends, having connections, understanding audiences, and navigating record companies and contracts, making it a 24/7 commitment. Managers are incentivized by commission, typically around 15% of an artist's earnings, and can either "make or break a career."
**Svengali Term Origin** : The term "Svengali," used to describe a manipulative and controlling individual, originates from the 1894 book *Trilby*. The book features an evil hypnotist character named Svengali who manipulates and dominates a young female performer, turning her into his pawn.
**Colonel Tom Parker - Elvis Presley's Manager** : Colonel Tom Parker was the manager of Elvis Presley and gained a reputation for exploiting Elvis for his worth.
**Brian Epstein - The Beatles' Manager** : Brian Epstein is credited with guiding The Beatles from a local bar band to the biggest group in the world before his death in 1967.
**Alan Klein - The Beatles & The Rolling Stones' Manager** : Alan Klein succeeded Brian Epstein as manager of The Beatles and also managed The Rolling Stones. He played a role in the eventual breakup of The Beatles.
**Peter Grant - Led Zeppelin's Manager** : Peter Grant was the manager of Led Zeppelin, known for being "fearsome scary" and protecting the band with "fear and constant threats of violence."
**Don Arden - Black Sabbath & Air Supply's Manager** : Don Arden, whose clients included Black Sabbath and Air Supply, was known as "the English Godfather" and "the Al Capone of Pop" for his strong-arm tactics.
**Tony DeFries - Manager Profile** : DeFries was a solicitor (legal professional) from London in the 1960s. By the end of the decade, he was involved in setting up record labels, publishing, copyright, and personal management. He pioneered the use of digital controls for live performance elements like lighting and special effects. In 2004, he founded a private company holding patents for inventions in broadcast and digital wireless. Despite his innovations, his legacy is complicated by disputes; in 2011, he was sued by Capitol Records over Bowie material copyright infringement, losing and ordered to pay $9 million.
**Main Man - Tony DeFries' Company** : Main Man was Tony DeFries' management company, which signed David Bowie, Iggy Pop, Lou Reed, Mick Ronson, Mott the Hoople, Luther Vandross, and John Mellencamp. The deals clients signed with Main Man were often very one-sided. DeFries later monetized the indie labels he created by selling them to major labels.
**Malcolm McLaren - Manager and Cultural Provocateur** : Born in Yemen and raised in London by his grandmother, McLaren attended art schools before pursuing a career in fashion. With Vivienne Westwood, he opened influential shops at 430 Kings Road: Paradise Garage (1971), Too Fast to Live, Too Young to Die (1973), and Sex. He later became a manager for bands like the New York Dolls (disastrously), the Sex Pistols (whom he created to be a living advertisement for his shop and an outlet for his political beliefs), Adam and the Ants, Adam Ant, and Bow Wow Wow. He was accused of mismanagement by the Sex Pistols, leading to litigation. In the early 1980s, he became an evangelist for rap and hip-hop, releasing the influential album *Duck Rock* (1983) which featured scratching by the World's Famous Supreme Team. He also explored electronic and club music. Beyond music, McLaren worked on TV ads (e.g., British Airways' "The Flower Duet" with Yanni), film (oversaw *Heavy Metal Surfing Nazis*), and TV projects as a director, producer, and performer. He even campaigned for London mayor in 1999, promising alcohol in public libraries, but didn't make it to Election Day. He was diagnosed with mesothelioma in October 2009 and died on April 8, 2010, in Switzerland, buried in a graffiti-sprayed coffin and with Sid Vicious' "My Way" playing. His headstone reads, "Better a Spectacular Failure than a Benign Success."
**Vivienne Westwood - Fashion Designer & Collaborator with Malcolm McLaren** : The now-famous designer was Malcolm McLaren's girlfriend and collaborated with him on several influential clothing shops at 430 Kings Road in London, including Paradise Garage (1971), Too Fast to Live, Too Young to Die (1973), and Sex. Her new fashions were showcased by the group Bow Wow Wow, which McLaren managed. Her daughter with McLaren later founded the lingerie chain Agent Provocateur.
**Glitter Best - Malcolm McLaren's Management Company** : Glitter Best was Malcolm McLaren's management company, known for managing the Sex Pistols. Following the band's implosion, years of litigation ensued between the band members and Glitter Best, culminating in the band regaining control of their music in 1987.
**Bernie Rhodes - Punk Rock Manager & Provocateur** : The "second most famous manager of the original punk rock era" in Britain, Bernie Rhodes also had a background in fashion and won an award for designing a new type of plastic toy for children. He initially worked with Malcolm McLaren on the Sex Pistols but was greatly annoyed when his offer to co-manage the band was rejected. Rhodes later claimed to have "invented punk rock" by recruiting Johnny Rotten for the Sex Pistols. He went on to manage The Clash (initially mentoring them to write political songs, then replaced, then returned in 1981, convincing them on 12-inch dance remixes) and The Specials (who gave him a shout-out in their debut single "Gangsters"). Rhodes is referenced in The Clash's "Rock the Casbah" concerning his agreement to drop the song "Sean Flynn" from the *Combat Rock* album. After being diagnosed with cancer in 2016, he set up a website called CancerClash.com. At last word, he was living in the U.S., occasionally dabbling in music and fashion.
**Caroline Coon - The Clash's Interim Manager (1978)** : A painter and rock journalist, Caroline Coon replaced Bernie Rhodes as manager of The Clash in 1978, though her tenure "didn't work out."
**Linda Stein - Manager and Real Estate Agent to the Stars** : Linda Stein initially abandoned a teaching career to co-manage the Ramones with Danny Fields. She married Seymour Stein, founder of Sire Records, who signed the Ramones, Talking Heads, The Pretenders, and Madonna; they divorced amicably in the late 1970s. After about a dozen years in band management, Linda transitioned into real estate, becoming highly successful and dubbed "real estate agent to the stars" by New York papers. Her clients included Steven Spielberg, Sting, Billy Joel, Elton John, Angelina Jolie, and Bruce Willis, earning her millions in commission. She was the model for the real estate agent character in the movie *Wall Street* and appeared as a record executive with Mike Myers in the film *54*. Linda died on October 30, 2007, at age 62, from blunt force trauma in her Manhattan apartment, ruled a homicide. Her personal assistant, Natavia Lowry, was arrested, confessed (citing Linda's yelling and also stealing $30,000), and is serving 27 years to life, though Lowry later recanted her confession.
**Seymour Stein - Sire Records Founder** : Seymour Stein founded Sire Records and signed numerous iconic bands and artists, including the Ramones, Talking Heads, The Pretenders, and Madonna. He was married to Linda Stein, co-manager of the Ramones, and they divorced amicably in the late 1970s. Seymour later remarried and publicly came out as gay in 2017 at the age of 75.
**Natavia Lowry - Murderer of Linda Stein** : Natavia Lowry, a 28-year-old former personal assistant who had worked for Linda Stein for only four months, was arrested a week after Stein's murder on October 30, 2007. Lowry confessed to the murder, stating that Stein "kept yelling at her," and also made off with $30,000 in cash. Lowry is serving a sentence of 27 years to life, though she later recanted her confession and has proclaimed her innocence.
**Paul McGuinness - U2's Manager** : McGuinness began as a film technician in Ireland and the UK, even having credits in the 1974 sci-fi film *Zardoz*. Inspired by managers like Brian Epstein, Andrew Loog Oldham, and Albert Grossman, he pursued band management, seeing it as a creative role in building culturally impactful entities. After managing Sean Davy and Spud, and learning from a friend who managed Horslips, he discovered U2 (then The Hype) in March 1978. He managed U2 for four decades via his company, Principal Management, before stepping down in 2014 and selling the company to Live Nation, then moving into TV work. He stated his commission deal with U2 was 20% for all five people involved, with profits shared equally, an "unusually high" rate justified by his hard work.
**Rob Gretton - Manchester Music Scene Figure** : In 1977, Gretton was part of the burgeoning punk rock scene in Manchester, simultaneously working as a baggage handler at Manchester Airport and as a DJ at the Rafters club, where he played soul and reggae. He was involved with pre-punk bands Slaughter & The Dogs and The Panik before discovering and managing Joy Division (formerly Warsaw). He secured Joy Division's deal with Factory Records and later brought Happy Mondays and OMD to the label. Gretton managed Joy Division through their two albums and Ian Curtis's suicide, then continued as manager for New Order, profoundly influencing their sound by championing American dance music. He was instrumental in the idea for New Order to open The Haçienda nightclub in Manchester, despite its eventual financial failure. Gretton worked with New Order for 20 years, until his death from a heart attack on May 15, 1999, at age 46. He was known for being kind and generous.
**Susan Silver - Grunge Era's Influential Woman** : Silver began managing bands in Seattle in 1983, concurrently running the door and booking bands at The Metropolis club while majoring in Chinese at the University of Washington. She managed pre-grunge band The U-Men, Screaming Trees, and Soundgarden (starting 1985/86). She also managed a Seattle shoe store known for supplying Doc Martens to grunge bands. Silver became involved with Alice in Chains, taking over day-to-day management from Kelly Curtis when he was distracted by Mother Love Bone, and she secured their record deal. She became their sole manager when Curtis moved on to Pearl Jam. Silver was close with many Seattle acts, even advising Nirvana on a record contract, though she declined to manage them. She married Soundgarden singer Chris Cornell in 1990 (having dated him since 1986). After Soundgarden's 1997 breakup, she retired from formal management in 1998, closing Silver Management. Following her 2003 divorce from Cornell, he sued her for $1 million, alleging diverted funds, withholding guitars, demos, journals, notes, and two Grammy Awards, and seeking to bar her from future Soundgarden representation, which contributed to reunion delays. She is believed to co-own The Crocodile club in Seattle and remains involved with Alice in Chains, viewing them "like brothers."
**Kelly Curtis - Manager of Grunge Bands** : Kelly Curtis co-managed Alice in Chains. His increasing focus on his other band, Mother Love Bone, led to Susan Silver taking over much of Alice in Chains' day-to-day management. Later, his deep involvement in managing Pearl Jam meant he had no time left for Alice in Chains, resulting in Susan Silver becoming their sole manager.
**John Silva - Low-Key, Client-Focused Manager** : John Silva runs Silver Artist Management, a company that cares for bands like the Foo Fighters, Queens of the Stone Age, Beck, St. Vincent, Mike D., The War on Drugs, Jimmy Eat World, Norah Jones, The Lonely Island, The Chicks, and Spoon. He also handles the interests of deceased artists such as Beastie Boys, Sonic Youth, and Taylor Hawkins, and Trent Reznor was a past client. Silva was hired by Danny Goldberg of Gold Mountain Entertainment to meet Nirvana in 1990, securing the band's management after they were impressed by his integrity and music knowledge. He has managed Dave Grohl and the Foo Fighters since 1995. His company, based in a bunker-like building off Melrose Avenue in Los Angeles, employs about 15 people. Silva and his staff are known for being extremely low-key, private, and solely focused on their clients' needs, with very little public information available about him.
**Silver Artist Management - John Silva's Company** : Founded and run by John Silva, this company manages a diverse roster including Foo Fighters, Queens of the Stone Age, Beck, St. Vincent, Mike D., The War on Drugs, Jimmy Eat World, Norah Jones, The Lonely Island, The Chicks, and Spoon. It also represents the interests of artists who are no longer active, such as Beastie Boys, Sonic Youth, and Taylor Hawkins, with Trent Reznor being a former client. Headquartered in a bunker-like building off Melrose Avenue in Los Angeles, the company employs about 15 people and is known for its low-key approach and client focus.
**Danny Goldberg - Head of Gold Mountain Entertainment** : Danny Goldberg led Gold Mountain Entertainment, a company that actively sought to manage Nirvana as they transitioned from an indie band to a major label. He hired John Silva and sent him to meet with the band, ultimately leading to Silva becoming Nirvana's manager.
**Cliff Burnstein - QPrime Co-Founder** : Burnstein started in A&R at Mercury Records in the 1970s, signing Rush. In 1982, he co-founded QPrime with Peter Mensch. Together, they have managed artists who have sold hundreds of millions of records. He is known for being private, avoiding interviews and headlines.
**Peter Mensch - QPrime Co-Founder** : A former college radio DJ, Mensch was fired from a previous management agency where he managed Aerosmith. In 1982, he co-founded QPrime with Cliff Burnstein. Together, they have managed artists who have sold hundreds of millions of records. He is known for being private, avoiding interviews and headlines.
**QPrime - Management Powerhouse** : Founded in 1982 by Cliff Burnstein and Peter Mensch, QPrime initially had Def Leppard as its sole client, but quickly expanded due to the band's popularity. The company specializes in heavy rock, managing a vast current roster including Metallica (their biggest client, joined in 1984), Red Hot Chili Peppers, Muse, Snow Patrol, Foals, Silversun Pickups, The Black Keys, The Last Dinner Party, Disturbed, Dogstar (Keanu Reeves' band), Three Days Grace, and Cage the Elephant. Past clients include AC/DC, Scorpions, Screaming Trees, Queensrÿche, The Smashing Pumpkins, Veruca Salt, Hole, The Rolling Stones (for their 1989 Steel Wheels Tour), Led Zeppelin (for their 2007 one-off reunion show), Jimmy Page, Shania Twain, and even opera singer Renée Fleming (for her 2010 covers album). Burnstein and Mensch are known for their privacy, avoiding interviews, photos, and headlines, while collectively their artists have sold hundreds of millions of records.
**Sharon Osbourne - Manager & Daughter of Don Arden** : Sharon Osbourne, wife of Ozzy Osbourne, is the daughter of Don Arden, a notorious "old school" manager. Her client list has included bands like Motörhead and The Smashing Pumpkins, though her time managing the latter was reportedly not successful.
**John Reid - Manager of Rock Legends** : John Reid managed both Elton John and Queen, a task described as "a handful."
**Andrew Loog Oldham - Early Rolling Stones Manager** : Andrew Loog Oldham was The Rolling Stones' first manager and was instrumental in shaping their early sound.
**Irving Azoff - The Eagles' Manager** : Irving Azoff was considered essential to the career of The Eagles.
**Kit Lambert & Chris Stamp - The Who's Architects** : Kit Lambert and Chris Stamp were instrumental in building The Who into the iconic band they became.
