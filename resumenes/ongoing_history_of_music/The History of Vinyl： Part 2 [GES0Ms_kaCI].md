**1973 Arab Oil Embargo – Impact on Vinyl** : The 1973 Arab Oil Embargo, a response to the Israeli-Egyptian War, caused oil prices to jump from $3 to $12 a barrel, which drastically increased the cost of petrochemical products, including the raw materials for vinyl records. This forced record labels to cut costs by using thinner, recycled vinyl. "The things that killed vinyl was the 1973 Arab Oil embargo and disco."
**Israeli Egyptian War of 1973** : The 1973 Israeli-Egyptian War was the geopolitical context for the Arab oil states' decision to limit oil output, leading to the Arab Oil Embargo. "In response to the Israeli Egyptian War of 1973, Arab oil states announced that they were limiting their output."
**Petrochemical Products and Vinyl Costs** : The Arab Oil Embargo's impact on oil prices directly raised the cost of all petrochemical products, including the raw material used to manufacture vinyl records. "This not only affected the price of gas, but also the price of all petrochemical products, including the raw material used for vinyl records."
**Recycled and Thinner Vinyl (Post-1973)** : To reduce costs after the 1973 oil shock, record labels began producing records that were thinner and made from recycled vinyl, leading to poorer sound quality, more skipping, and increased surface noise. "To cut costs, record labels began issuing records that were thinner and made of recycled vinyl."
**Shallower Grooves (Post-1973)** : Thinner records meant shallower grooves, which stored less information (especially bass), resulting in inferior sound quality and a less secure seating for the stylus, causing more skips and scratches. "Thinner records meant grooves that weren't as deep. Shalor grooves stored less information, especially base, so these records didn't sound as good."
**Stylus Issues (Post-1973)** : Shallower grooves on thinner vinyl records meant the stylus did not seat as securely, leading to more frequent skips and scratches. "Shalor grooves also meant that the stylist didn't seat us securely, and that meant that records skipped and scratched more often."
**1979 Iranian Revolution – Second Oil Shock** : The Iranian Revolution in 1979 triggered a second oil shock, further increasing costs and leading to even lower quality vinyl production. "Then there was a second oil shock in 1979 after the Iranian Revolution. Higher costs led to even lower quality vinyl."
**Late 1970s / Early 1980s Recession** : A brutal recession hit in the late 1970s and early 1980s, characterized by 12% inflation rates and 22% mortgage rates, causing people to stop buying non-essentials, including music. "Record sales went into a free fall and a brutal, brutal recession kicked in. Imagine inflation rates of 12 percent and mortgage rates of 22 percent."
**Record Labels' Initial CD Hesitation** : Record labels initially resisted the compact disc concept due to the expense of building new factories, warehousing and shipping challenges, anticipated resistance from retailers who would need to redesign stores, and fears over lack of copy protection allowing easy home copying. "Believe it or not, though, the labels wanted no part of the CD at first. They hated the concept."
**CD Industry Push (Late 1981)** : By late 1981, record labels, in dire financial straits due to the recession, were "brow beaten" into accepting the compact disc as vinyl's successor, leading to extensive consumer marketing campaigns. "But by late 1981, the labels were in such bad shape financially that they had all been brow beaten into accepting the compact disc as the successor to vinyl."
**CD Marketing: "Perfect Digital Sound Forever"** : Consumers were bombarded with marketing campaigns that promised "perfect digital sound forever" for CDs, contrasting with the poor quality of late 70s/early 80s vinyl. "And this is when we, the consumers, began to get bombarded with campaigns that promised perfect digital sound forever."
**Consumer Reaction to Early CDs** : The initial experience of hearing CDs caused people to "freak out" and "loved it" because they were tired of the poor quality, static, skips, and pops of contemporary vinyl. "the first time the people heard CDs, they freaked out. They loved it. We were tired of bad quality vinyl. We couldn't abandon it fast enough."
**Vinyl Sales Decline (Post-1985)** : After 1985, vinyl sales began to slide rapidly, seemingly an "extinction level event" due to the introduction of the CD. "after 1985, vinyl sales began to slide faster and faster and faster. It seemed that the introduction of the CD was an extinction level event."
**1980 Vinyl Album Sales Share** : In 1980, before the CD's widespread introduction, vinyl albums accounted for 60% of music sales. "In 1980, with the CD still about two years away, vinyl albums accounted for 60% of music sales."
**1990 Vinyl Album Sales Share** : By 1990, vinyl album sales had plummeted to 1% of the music market. "Just 10 years later, that share had dropped to 1%."
**1990 7-inch Single Sales Share** : In 1990, 7-inch singles' market share had fallen from about 8% to less than 1%. "Seven inch singles fell from about 8% to a fraction of 1%."
**2000 Vinyl Market Share** : By 2000, all vinyl formats (albums, EPs, 12-inch, and 7-inch singles) accounted for a barely measurable 0.3% of the market, indicating its statistical disappearance. "By 2000, all vinyl albums, EPs, 12 inch singles, and 17 inch singles, accounted for, well, it was barely measurable. Somewhere around 3 tenths of 1%."
**Club Scene and Vinyl Survival** : The club scene was a significant reason for vinyl's survival, as DJs and turntableists preferred its tactile nature for mixing, scratching, and sampling. "The club scene is a big reason why it survived."
**Record Collectors and Vintage Vinyl** : Record collectors, dedicated to hunting for rare and interesting vinyl, formed a culture around collecting vintage vinyl, which became highly valuable and collectible. "Then we have record collectors, consumers who got into the hunt for rare and interesting vinyl. Certain vintage vinyl became highly collectible and very valuable."
**Record Collector Magazine** : A magazine specifically for record collectors, highlighting the established culture of vinyl collecting. "magazines like record collector."
**Online Vinyl Marketplaces (eBay, Discogs)** : Websites like eBay and Discogs facilitated the buying and selling of vintage vinyl, making it much easier for collectors. "And then there's eBay and Discogs and whole variety of other websites."
**Audiofiles and Vinyl Preference** : Audiofiles never believed that CDs sounded better than good vinyl on a good turntable, rejecting MP3s as awful and hating over-compressed music on CDs, thus supporting vinyl. "Consumers who never really believe the hype about CD sounding better than a good vinyl record on a good turntable."
**Labels Retaining Vinyl (Novelty/Promo)** : Many record labels never entirely abandoned vinyl, issuing albums or singles on the format as a novelty or for promotional purposes, which helped keep pressing plants in business. "And many record labels never entirely abandoned the format. Issuing albums or singles on vinyl became something of an novelty."
**MP3s, Piracy, and Digital Downloads Impact** : The introduction of MP3s, piracy, and legal digital music downloads further contributed to the decline in overall vinyl sales. "The introduction of MP3s, piracy and legal digital music downloads didn't help either."
**21st Century Digital Music Backlash** : In the latter half of the first decade of the 21st century, a small but powerful backlash against digital music began, contributing to vinyl's resurgence. "There was a small but powerful backlash against digital music."
**1997 Global Vinyl Sales** : Global vinyl sales in 1997 were less than $150 million. "In 1997, all vinyl accounted for a tiny fraction of the overall music market. Global sales were less than $150 million."
**2000 Global Vinyl Sales** : By 2000, global vinyl sales had dropped to less than $100 million. "Three years later, the number had fallen to less than $100 million."
**2006 Global Vinyl Sales** : In 2006, worldwide vinyl sales reached a low of approximately $30 million. "And by 2006, sales accounted for maybe $30 million."
**2013 Global Vinyl Sales** : By 2013, the global market for vinyl records rebounded significantly, reaching nearly $220 million, a level not seen since the 1990s. "By 2013, the global market for vinyl records was worth nearly $220 million."
**Vinyl as "Rock Snobbery" (21st Century)** : Sourcing vinyl from "cool bands" became a "badge of honor" and a form of "rock snubbery," demonstrating devotion to a band/genre or a deep care for music, even sacrificing money and convenience. "In other words, it became a form of rock snubbery."
**Tactile Experience and Analog Sound Preference** : A segment of the population desires the "old school tactile experience" of playing vinyl, appreciating the artwork, liner notes, and the "warm analog sound" in contrast to impersonal digital tracks. "A certain segment of the population wants that old school tactile experience of playing vinyl, ruminating over the artwork and liner notes, and basking in the warm analog sound."
**Virgin Vinyl (21st Century)** : Modern vinyl production uses "virgin vinyl" instead of recycled chemicals, resulting in higher quality records. "No more recycled chemicals. Instead, what we have is called virgin vinyl, pressed into heavyweight records."
**180 Gram Vinyl** : A common specification for modern heavyweight vinyl records, where a square meter of the material weighs 180 grams, resulting in thicker, flatter records with deeper grooves and potential for higher audio quality. "The most common is known as 180 gram vinyl, which means a square meter of the stuff weighs 180 grams."
**Tonearm Tracking Improvement** : Thicker, flatter records with deeper grooves, such as 180 gram virgin vinyl, lead to more accurate tonearm tracking. "Tonarm tracking is more true, and the grooves can hold more information, meaning that there's the potential for higher quality audio."
**Vinyl as High Margin Product (21st Century)** : In the resurgence, vinyl records sell for two to five times the price of compact discs, making them high-margin products for record labels once again. "Vinyl records now sell for two or three or four or five times the price of a compact disc. They are once again high margin products for record labels."
**Quality vs. Convenience** : The enjoyment of music is presented as not needing to be a rational experience, suggesting that society often sacrifices quality for convenience, a trend some believe should stop. "In terms of convenience, there's no rational reason to choose vinyl, compared to MP3's and digital music players, the whole thing's a pain in the ass. But since when does the enjoyment of music have to be a rational experience?"
**Photography Analogy (Film vs. Digital)** : A comparison is drawn to photography, where digital cameras offer excellent resolution, but some photographers still appreciate the quality of old-fashioned fine-grain film. "Some of my photographer friends feel the same way about film."
**Vinyl Market Share (Current)** : Despite its renaissance, vinyl still constitutes a "very, very, very, very, very, very, very, very tiny portion of the market," but it is growing. "vinyl is still a very, very, very, very, very, very, very, very tiny portion of the market. But it is growing."
