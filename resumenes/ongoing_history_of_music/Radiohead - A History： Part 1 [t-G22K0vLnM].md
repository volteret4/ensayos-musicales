## albums
**A Moon Shaped Pool (2016)** : This album, released in 2016, is part of Radiohead's independent projects, a model they adopted for all their work since 2008. They operate entirely on their own terms, having no use for the major label system anymore. "A Moon Shape Pool, which, like everything they've done since 2008, has been an independent project."
**Drill (1992)** : Radiohead's debut EP, released on May 5, 1992, with only 3,000 copies produced. It contained four tracks, all of which were previously "On a Friday" demos. The EP did not perform well, charting at number 101, and stores struggled to sell copies, partly due to its sound. Their managers produced the EP, attempting to emulate Nirvana's "Smells Like Teen Spirit," which resulted in a "confused mess." "The Drill EP did not do well. It charted at number 101 and record stores really couldn't give it away after a while."
**Pablo Honey (1993)** : Radiohead's debut album, released on February 22, 1993. The title was inspired by a segment from a Jerky Boys crank call tape, which an English group called Chapter House had given them. The band members, considering themselves "mama's boys," found the title "somehow an appropriate title." "Creep" was the lead single, "Anyone Can Play Guitar" was the second, and "Stop Whispering" was the third. The album received "OK press" but nothing "terribly spectacular." "Pablo Honey was, like I said, really on February 22nd, 1993."
**The Bends (1995)** : Radiohead's second album, with work beginning in mid-January 1994. The band rehearsed material in an old Apple storage shed located on a fruit farm, which they had purchased and refurbished using money earned from "Creep." They named this rehearsal space "Canned Applause." By February 28, they moved to a proper studio in London to work with producer Steve Lucky, known for The Stone Roses' debut album. The recording sessions were difficult due to a "real lack of communication" and disagreements over which songs should be singles, leading to constant rearrangement. The album's title reflects the intense pressure the band felt, akin to a deep-sea diver coming up too fast. "The pressure was so intense that the band felt that they were suffering from the bands, like some deep sea diver who came up to the surface too fast."

## songs
**Mushroom Cloud (c. 1978)** : This was Tom York's very first song, a "nuclear holocaust thing" written around age 10. He was inspired to write it after becoming "freaked out" by Cold War documentaries shown on the BBC, leading him to create a song about everything being "blown to bits." "Tom's first song, a nuclear holocaust thing called Mushroom Cloud."
**Stop Whispering (1991)** : An early version of this song was recorded in April 1991 as part of a three-song demo cassette when the band was still known as "On a Friday." It later became the third single released from their debut album, "Pablo Honey." "That's Radiohead, still known as on a Friday, with an early version of Stop Whispering that was recorded in April 1991."
**Prove Yourself (1992)** : This song served as the lead-off track on Radiohead's debut EP, "Drill," which was their first commercially available recording. "It's the lead off track. Prove yourself."
**Creep (1992)** : Written by Tom York during a drinking binge at university, reflecting his unrequited love for a girl he often saw in Oxford cafes and bars over eight months. Tom initially hid the song, considering it "too weak and wimpy," but when presented to the band, Johnny Greenwood vehemently disliked it, attempting to sabotage the studio recording by inserting a loud, grinding scratch with his guitar just before the chorus. Unbeknownst to the band, the producer secretly recorded this raw take, which became the version released to the public. The original version contained an "F-bomb" which was later reluctantly changed to "very" for release to gain airplay. The song was later subject to a copyright infringement lawsuit by The Hollies, who claimed it was too similar to their 1972 song "The Air That I Breathe." As a result, Albert Hammond Sr. and Mike Hazelwood now each receive 25% of the writing credit. Released as a single on September 21, 1992, with 6,000 copies pressed, it initially reached number 78 on the charts and was played only twice by the BBC before being pulled for being "too depressing." However, it gained massive popularity in Israel after an EMI employee, Uzi Price, gave it to DJ Yoav Kutner of Israeli Armed Forces Radio. Its success in Israel prompted EMI to interrupt Radiohead's UK tour for three shows in Tel Aviv in March/April 1993, further boosting its hit status. An Israeli import later led to play on a San Francisco radio station, and subsequently across North America, becoming one of the biggest alternative singles globally and a top 40 hit in some countries. Tom York used the song's earnings to buy a house, which he named "the house that Creep built." It is now considered one of the three great slacker anthems of the early 90s, alongside Nirvana's "Smells Like Teen Spirit" and Beck's "Loser." "The Creepe Pee was released on September 21st, 1992."
**Anyone Can Play Guitar (1991/1993)** : This song appeared on the "Manic Hedgehog tape" in October 1991. It was re-recorded and released as a single in February 1993, following "Creep." Radiohead frequently used it to open their live sets, where it was well-received. It became Radiohead's first top 40 hit in the UK, peaking at number 32 on the British charts. "It was Anyone Can Play Guitar, which reached number 32 on the British charts."
**Fake Plastic Trees (1995)** : Recorded during the challenging sessions for "The Bends" album. After a particularly bad day of yelling, producer Steve Lucky sent the band home, asking only Tom York to stay for a "guide vocal" for this track. Tom sang the song once, then burst into tears from the emotional strain, but his one-take performance was deemed "brilliant" and became the final album version. Legend suggests Tom's vocal was inspired by Jeff Buckley, whom the band had seen perform at a local bar the night before. "Tom sat down and sang the song once. And then he burst into tears because the strain had finally got to him."
**My Iron Lung (1993/1995)** : This song was written in 1993 while Radiohead was on tour with James. It was later released as the lead track of an EP, which, depending on the version, might have included up to eight new songs. The band road-tested the song live in Toronto on June 6, 1995. The title refers to a massive medical machine used to assist breathing, and Tom York reportedly owns a real one at home. "It's called My Iron One."

## curiosities
**Independent Operations (Since 2008)** : Radiohead has chosen to operate as an independent entity since 2008, managing all their projects without the involvement of major record labels. They conduct all their business "on their own and on their own terms," indicating a deliberate move away from the traditional music industry system. "Radiohead has no use for the major label system anymore, and does everything on their own and on their own terms."
**Band Reputation** : Radiohead is characterized as legendary and mythic, with every detail of their work being meticulously documented. They are highly praised by critics and have a particularly devoted fanbase, often referred to as "disciples." The band is considered "one of the most important bands of our lifetimes." "There's really no other way to put it, but to say that Radiohead is one of the most important bands of our lifetimes."
**Band Formation (1982)** : The core members, Tom York, Colin Greenwood, and Ed O'Brien, first met and started playing together in their final year at Abingdon, a private school in Oxford, around 1982. At this time, they were approximately 14 or 15 years old. Their initial setup included "three guitarists and a bad drum machine named Dr. Rhythm," and their very first gig was at a friend's birthday party. "Their first gig was at a friends birthday party. This would be sometime in 1982, which means everybody was about 14, 15 years old at the time."
**Name "On a Friday"** : The band originally adopted the name "On a Friday" because Friday was the sole day of the week when all the members could gather for rehearsals. "As it turns out, the only day the guys could rehearse was Friday. So the band became known as on a Friday."
**Parental Disapproval** : The band members initially concealed their musical activities from their parents. Their parents "really disapproved of this dabbling and rocking rule stuff," leading the young musicians to keep their rock endeavors a secret. "Tom, Phil, Colin, and Ed couldn't tell their parents what they were doing because they really disapproved of this dabbling and rocking rule stuff."
**Early Lineup and Instrumentation** : In its formative years as "On a Friday," the band's instrumentation was quite fluid and experimental. Johnny Greenwood, who joined around age 13, often played harmonica, recorder, and occasionally keyboards. At different points, the group featured "an all-female horn section" and even three saxophone players, despite acknowledging that the sax players "weren't very good, but at least they were having a good time." "Now at first, on a Friday was all over the place. Johnny played harmonica, sometimes the recorder, complete keyboards, every once in a while."
**First Recordings (1986)** : The earliest existing recording attributed to the band is a five-track cassette from 1986, which they self-issued at Abingdon. At this point, Johnny Greenwood was not yet a member, but a sax player named Razz Peterson was part of the lineup. The tape prominently featured 80s influences from bands like U2, Waterboys, Psychedelic Furs, and The Smiths, with Tom York handling lead vocals at 17 years old. "The earliest recording that seems to exist is a 1986 cassette that was recorded at Elbington."
**Hiatus for College (1991)** : In 1991, "On a Friday" entered an extended hiatus as all five members pursued higher education at different universities. Ed O'Brien went to Manchester University, Colin Greenwood studied English literature at Cambridge, Phil Selway also studied English literature at Liverpool University, and Johnny Greenwood enrolled in both music and psychology programs at Oxford. Tom York decided to take a year off before attending university. "In 1997, a 1991 on a Friday went on an extended sort of hiatus."
**Continued Rehearsals (Early 1990s)** : Despite their individual college pursuits, the members of "On a Friday" maintained their musical connection by meeting once a month to rehearse on Fridays. During this period, they occasionally recorded their material on a cheap tape recorder, amassing approximately 15 original songs by the close of 1990. "Meanwhile, once a month, all the on-off Friday guys would still get together and rehearse on Friday, of course, and applaud what they might do together once this school thing was out of the way."
**First Proper Demo (Early 1990s)** : Around the time all five members moved into a semi-detached house near the center of Oxford, "On a Friday" recorded their first professional demo. This three-song cassette cost 300 pounds to produce and was released without a specific title or catalog number. "They recorded their first proper demo. Three songs, 300 pounds."
**Post-Graduation Concerts (1991)** : The band's first concert after their graduation period took place on July 22, 1991, at the Hollywood in Oxford. Despite having played together "off and on for years" and estimated "at least 100 times" before, this gig was described as relatively modest: "five friends, two strobe lights and a smoke machine." A subsequent gig in October at the Jericho Tavern, known for hosting other Oxford bands like Supergrass, Ride, and Slowdive, proved to be a turning point. "The big day that everybody remembers is July 22, 1991. Just when they played a show at the Hollywood in Oxford, their first post-graduation concert."
**Manic Hedgehog Tape (1991)** : This was a five-song cassette that "On a Friday" sold for three pounds at the local indie record store, Manic Hedgehog, where Colin Greenwood worked. Colin strategically "slipped a copy of this cassette" to any record label representatives who visited the store. This grassroots promotion led to an unprecedented 25 record company reps attending their pivotal gig at the Jericho Tavern. "That cassette ended up being referred to as appropriately the Manic Hedgehog tape and it featured songs like this. It's called Philip a Chicken."
**Record Deal with EMI (1991)** : By the end of 1991, "On a Friday" was actively pursued by various record companies, culminating in a six-album deal with EMI, signed on December 21, 1991. This deal marked a significant milestone, although it came with a stipulation for the band to change its name. "On December 21, 1991, they signed an album deal with EMI."
**Name Change to Radiohead (1991)** : After signing with EMI, the label expressed dissatisfaction with the name "On a Friday" and instructed the band to find a new one. Various suggestions were considered, including "Jude the Obscure" (deemed "too obscure" from the Thomas Hardy novel), "Music" (liked by Tom but rejected), and "Gravitates" (which "didn't have the right ring to it"). The definitive name, "Radiohead," was discovered when someone retrieved a 1986 Talking Heads album, "True Stories," from Tom York's DJ collection, which featured a song of the same name. The name change officially occurred at the end of 1991. "And so it came to pass that at the end of 1991, on a Friday, became known as Radiohead."
**Early Touring and Struggles (1993)** : Following the release of "Pablo Honey," Radiohead embarked on an extensive tour, performing "350 live shows." During this period, they faced "an uphill battle to be taken seriously" by the music industry and audiences. "The band started out a string of 350 live shows facing an uphill battle to be taken seriously."
**Recording Second Album Pressure (1994)** : Under significant pressure from their record label, Radiohead was expected to complete their second album, "The Bends," by the summer of 1994. Consequently, they pre-booked a world tour. However, when the album remained unfinished by summer, the band was forced to interrupt their recording sessions "in midstream" to fulfill their concert commitments. This situation caused considerable anxiety among record companies on both sides of the Atlantic, who were eager for "another monster like Creep." "Everyone thought that the album would be finished by the summer of 1994, so they, under pressure from the record company, went ahead and booked a world tour."
**Iron Lung Machine** : Tom York is rumored to possess a real iron lung machine at his home. "Tom apparently has a real one at home someplace."

## curiosities
**Birth and Origin** : Born on October 7, 1968, in the small English town of Wellingboro. "He's from a tiny English town called Wellingboro. His birthday was October 7, 1968."
**Physical Characteristics and Health** : Described as a "delicate little guy," standing approximately 5'5". He was often "sickly and skinny," which was a source of worry for his father, a chemical engineer and championship boxer. Tom was born with a partially paralyzed left eye, with the eyelid fixed in a downward position. After several surgeries intended to correct it, the fifth operation resulted in his distinctive "droopy eye" and reduced vision of about 50% on that side. He wore an eye patch during his first couple of years in school. "Tom is a delicate little guy, standing about 5'5"."
**Childhood Nickname** : Due to his pale and thin appearance at school, Tom was given the nickname "Salamander." This led to him getting into "a lot of fights," which even boxing lessons from his father couldn't significantly help. "he picked up the nickname Salamander."
**Early Musical Frustration** : Tom received his first guitar at the age of eight. However, he quickly became so frustrated with the instrument that he "smashed the thing to pieces in a big temper tantrum." "He got his first guitar at age eight, but he soon got so frustrated with it that he smashed the thing to pieces in a big temper tantrum."
**First Band** : After overcoming his initial frustration with the guitar, Tom formed his first band at age 10. "he eventually got his act together, and a few years later he formed his first band. This was age 10."
**First Guitar Hero** : Tom York's earliest guitar hero was Brian May of Queen, whose guitar solo in "Bohemian Rhapsody" he still admires. "And his first guitar hero, it was Brian May of Queen."
**College Plans and Early Jobs** : Before attending university, Tom decided to take a year off. During this time, he worked selling men's suits, a job he "hated." He then found a "better job" working in a mental hospital before eventually enrolling at Oxford. "Tom decided to take a year off before university. He spent some time selling men's suits, something that he hated. So he got a better job. It gave him a mental hospital before he went back and enrolled in Oxford."
**DJ Gig at Campus Pub** : To supplement his income, Tom worked as a DJ at a campus pub. He was quite skilled and his regular Friday night gigs often attracted "a thousand people or more" to hear him spin. "Tom supplemented his income working as a DJ at a campus pub. And he was pretty good too."
**Meeting Stanley Donwood** : While performing with The Headless Chickens in Exeter, Tom York met Stanley Donwood. Donwood went on to become, and remains, Radiohead's primary artist responsible for all their artwork and promotional materials. "it was while at Exeter, and playing in this band, that Tom met Stanley Donwood. He would go on to become a radio head's key guy when it came to artwork and promotional material."

**Radiohead's First Post-Graduation Gig (July 22, 1991)** : On July 22, 1991, "On a Friday" performed their first post-graduation concert at the Hollywood in Oxford. Despite having played together "at least 100 times before," this gig was characterized by its modest setup: "five friends, two strobe lights and a smoke machine." "Just when they played a show at the Hollywood in Oxford, their first post-graduation concert."

# concert - Jericho Tavern (Oxford)

## curiosities
**Crucial Gig for "On a Friday" (October 1991)** : "On a Friday" played a pivotal gig at the Jericho Tavern in October 1991. This local venue was "semi-famous at the time for hosting other big-ish Oxford bands" like Supergrass, Ride, and Slowdive. Thanks to Colin Greenwood's efforts distributing their demo, no fewer than 25 record company representatives attended this particular show. "It didn't come until October when they played a local place called the Jericho Tavern, which by the way was semi-famous at the time for hosting other big-ish Oxford bands like Super Grass and Ride and Slow Dive."

# curiosity

**Music Industry Evolution** : In contrast to contemporary trends of short-lived success, "in the old days," musical groups were afforded the time to develop and evolve across "a series of albums, three, four, five, six records even." This slow, patient process, often involving "a lot of fighting to keep things together," frequently "paid off," as evidenced by bands like U2, REM, Blur, Depeche Mode, and Nine Inch Nails, who were allowed to build careers and "make mistakes along the way." "In the old days, a group developed and evolved over a series of albums, three, four, five, six records even. It was slow, took a lot of patience, a lot of fighting to keep things together, but more often than not, it paid off."
**"Guide Vocal" Definition** : A guide vocal is a temporary vocal track recorded to assist the rest of the band in laying down their instrumental parts. It is "not designed to be permanent" and is typically erased once the other components are recorded, allowing for the "real work on the vocals" to begin. "A guide vocal is not designed to be permanent. Its purpose is to guide the rest of the band as they were recording all the other parts and once all that has been accomplished, the guide vocal is erased and the real work on the vocals can begin."
**The Jerky Boys** : The Jerky Boys are a group famous for their prank phone calls. One of their tapes was given to Radiohead by the English group Chapter House, and a particular line from one of these calls inspired the title for Radiohead's debut album, "Pablo Honey." "Well, remember the jerky boys and their crankphone calls?"
**Stephen Hawking's Pizza Order** : While Ed O'Brien worked at a pizza parlor, physicist Stephen Hawking was a regular customer. Hawking would place his pizza orders himself, using his computerized voice device. "physicist Stephen Hawking was a regular customer, which was really kind of weird because Professor Hawking ordered pizza himself using his computerized voice device."
**Manic Hedgehog Record Store** : This local indie record store was where Colin Greenwood worked and also served as a selling point for "On a Friday's" five-song cassette, known as the "Manic Hedgehog tape." Colin leveraged his position to distribute copies to record label representatives. "Colin, between shifts at a local record store called Manic Hedgehog, was also the school's entertainment director"
**Our Price Record Store** : Colin Greenwood also worked at this record store, where he continued his efforts to promote "On a Friday" by handing out their demos to visiting record company representatives. His persistence here ultimately led to the cassette landing in the hands of Keith Wolzencroft and securing their record deal with EMI. "Calling Greenway had worked at another record store called Our Price where he kept handing out Arnafriade demos to any record company reps that might come in"
**Steve Lucky - Producer** : Steve Lucky served as the producer for Radiohead's second album, "The Bends." He was notable for having produced The Stone Roses' debut album, and his resume also included work with Simple Minds and XTC. Furthermore, he had worked as an engineer at Abbey Road Studios, where he collaborated with Pink Floyd. His sessions with Radiohead were characterized by a "real lack of communication" and internal conflict. "By the 28th of February, they were in a proper studio in London working on tracks with producers Steve Lucky, the guy famous for producing the Stone Rose's debut album."
**Uzi Price - EMI Israel** : Uzi Price was an EMI employee responsible for the label's operations in Israel. He played a critical role in "Creep's" international success by sending "excited reports" to EMI's Home Office regarding the song's massive popularity in Israel, which led to Radiohead's first international concerts in Tel Aviv. "The Home Office of EMI, Radiohead's record label, began receiving excited reports from an employee named Uzi Price, who looked after EMI affairs in Israel of all places."
**Yoav Kutner - Israeli DJ** : Yoav Kutner was a radio DJ working for Israeli Armed Forces Radio. He received a copy of "Creep" from EMI employee Uzi Price and decided to play it a couple of times, which sparked an "nuts" and "huge" response from listeners, initiating the song's global rise. "He had given a copy to a radio DJ named Yoav Kutner, who worked, believe it or not, for Israeli Armed Forces Radio."
**Mod Lang - Berkeley Record Store** : An Israeli import of "Creep" found its way to a clerk at Mod Lang, a record store in Berkeley, California. This clerk also happened to be an intern at a San Francisco radio station, and he brought the song to work, where the station "loved it so much that they began to play it," helping to break "Creep" in North America. "Then an Israeli import of Creepe wound up in the hands of a clerk in a record store in Berklee, California called Mod Lang."
**Stanley Donwood - Artist** : Stanley Donwood met Tom York in Exeter while Tom was performing with The Headless Chickens. Donwood subsequently became Radiohead's principal artist, responsible for all their artwork and promotional materials, a role he continues to hold. "Tom met Stanley Donwood. He would go on to become a radio head's key guy when it came to artwork and promotional material. And he still is."
