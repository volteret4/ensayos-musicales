**Blitzkrieg Bop (1974)** : This was one of 20 songs played in 17 minutes during The Ramones' debut gig at CBGB on August 16, 1974. It quickly became one of the first songs to establish the "myth and cult" of The Ramones, embodying their sound of bubblegum pop mixed with 60s garage rock played at high speed. "They played 20 songs in 17 minutes, including this one. Blitzkrieg Bob, one of the first songs to figure into the myth and cult of the Ramones."
## curiosities
**Formation (1974)** : The Ramones formed in 1974 when John Cummings (Johnny Ramone) and Douglas Colvin (Dee Dee Ramone) decided to start a band after getting drunk together. They spent $250 on a guitar, bass, and amp, holding their first rehearsal four days later. "They went from beers in a strip club, they got drunk, and they decided that they should form a band."
**Original Lineup and Roles** : The initial lineup included Jeff Hyman (Joey Ramone) on drums, a "6'6-inch skinny guy" and "OCD-suffering refugee from a failed band called Sniper." Their first gig was in March. By July, Jeff Hyman moved to lead vocals because he was a better singer than drummer, and their manager, Tommy (Tommy Ramone), a recording engineer who had worked with Jimi Hendrix, took over on drums. "Their drummer was a 6'6-inch skinny guy named Jeff Heimann, an OCD-suffering refugee from a failed band called Sniper."
**Stage Names and Image** : To symbolize their unity and brotherhood, all band members adopted the surname "Ramone." Jeff Hyman became Joey Ramone, Douglas Colvin became Dee Dee Ramone, John Cummings became Johnny Ramone, and Tommy kept his first name. They also adopted a uniform "tough leather jacket and jeans look." "Everybody adopted a tough leather jacket and jeans look, and everybody had a stage name. Jeff became Joey. Douglas was DD, John was Johnny, and Tommy stayed Tommy. And as a symbol of their brotherhood, they all adopted the same last name, Ramone."
**Name Origin (Paul McCartney Connection)** : The band's chosen surname, "Ramone," was inspired by Dee Dee Ramone's admiration for Paul McCartney. During his time with The Silver Beatles, McCartney would use the alias "Paul Ramone" to check into hotel rooms. Dee Dee, already calling himself Dee Dee Ramone, suggested "The Ramones" for the band to signify unity and family. "DD was a big fan of Paul McCartney and the Beatles, and the days of the Silver Beatles, Paul McCartney used to check on two hotel rooms using the alias Paul Ramone."
**CBGB Debut (August 16, 1974)** : On August 16, 1974, The Ramones played their first gig at CBGB, opening for Television or possibly two other bands. The audience was minimal, with only six people (plus Hilly and his dog) present. They played 20 songs in 17 minutes, characterized by a primitive, ragged, and goofy appearance. "On August 16th, 1974, the Ramones opened for television, or they might have been opening for two other bands. No one really remembers because there were just six people in the bar."
**Early Performance Quality and Reception** : Their debut performance at CBGB was largely unimpressive; they fought between songs, broke strings, and started/stopped at wrong times, being described as "awful." Despite their serious intentions, they looked funny to the audience. The entire set was over in just 17 minutes. "They fought between songs, strings broke, songs started and stopped in the wrong places. They were awful."
**Disdain for CBGB** : The Ramones themselves didn't think highly of CBGB initially, finding the stage too small, Hilly Crystal unhygienic, and the club plagued by rats and mice. They also found the clientele unpleasant, noting patrons would relieve themselves in corners due to the awful bathrooms. "Now to be fair, the Ramones didn't think too much of CBGB either. This stage was too small. They thought Hilly needed to bathe more and they hated how his dog, all the rats and all the mice, cracked all over the place."
**Frequent CBGB Performances (1974-1975)** : Despite their initial dislike, The Ramones played CBGB 22 more times before the end of 1974, and by early 1975, they began attracting attention and drawing crowds, contributing to CBGB becoming the center of the new music scene. "But with no place else to play, the Ramones returned to CBGB 22 more times before the end of 1974."
**Performance Length Controversy** : In their early days, The Ramones faced arguments backstage because audience members would complain they didn't play long enough, often only 45 minutes instead of the contracted hour. The band's defense was that they delivered an hour's worth of material in 45 minutes due to their rapid-fire style and lack of stage patter between songs. "There were always arguments backstage in the early days where the Ramones, when people would complain that the Ramones didn't play long enough. They would say, well, they only played 45 minutes in the contract, said they were supposed to play for an hour."
**Featured in Punk Magazine** : The fanzine "Punk," which appeared at the end of 1975, was dedicated to groups like The Ramones, who appeared at CBGB, helping to define the "punk artist" label for bands in the burgeoning scene. "This fanzine was dedicated to singers like Lou Reed and groups like the Ramones, who appeared at CBGB."

This growing interest led Hilly Crystal to institute a rock-only policy within a few months of Television's debut, signifying the club's transformation into a hub for the new scene. "That Sunday night residency became one of the best nights of the week for CBGB, the crowd that had been displaced by the collapse of the Mercer Arts Center reconvened there."

## songs
**Hey Joe (1974)** : Released on June 5, 1974, as the A-side of a seven-inch single on Robert Mapplethorpe's independent label, this song was written as a tribute to Patty Hearst, a kidnap victim who had recently gained newspaper notoriety. It was recorded with her musician friends vamped along in the background. "Side A was called Hay Joe, and written as a tribute to kidnaps newspaper-era's Patty Hurst."
**Piss Factory (1974)** : This poem, recited by Patti Smith with her musician friends vamping in the background, was recorded as the B-side to "Hey Joe" in June 1974, due to leftover time and money. It was written about a dead-end job Smith once held and unexpectedly captured the attention of the New York avant-garde underground, with its 1000 copies quickly selling out. It is often considered a contender for the "first true punk record." "Because they had some time and money left after recording Hay Joe, someone suggested that Patty read a poem that she had written about a dead-end job that she once held. Her musician friends just vamped along in the background as she read this poem."
## curiosities
**Avant-garde Poetry Readings (Since 1971)** : Since 1971, Patti Smith, a "thin, intense poet from New Jersey," had been performing hip avant-garde poetry readings on St. Mark's Place in The Village, laying the groundwork for her musical career. "She had been giving these hip avant-garde poetry readings on St. Mark's Place in the Village since 1971."
**Collaboration with Lenny Kaye** : Patti Smith met record store clerk Lenny Kaye, who would play guitar improvisations while she recited her poems, leading to their musical collaboration. "Then she met Lenny Kay, a record store clerk, and he would noodle on his guitar as Patty recited her poems."
**Suggestion to Record (Robert Mapplethorpe)** : Artist Robert Mapplethorpe, a friend of Patti Smith, suggested that she record her poetry readings to sell in "cooler bookshops" in The Village, which led to the recording of her first single. "This led to a suggestion by her friend, an artist named Robert Mapplethorpe, that she makes some recordings of these poetry readings that she could maybe sell in some of the cooler bookshops in the village."
**Debut Single Release (June 5, 1974)** : On June 5, 1974, Patti Smith independently released a thousand copies of a seven-inch single on Robert Mapplethorpe's small label. The B-side, "Piss Factory," gained significant attention from the New York avant-garde. "So on June 5th, 1974, Patty released a thousand copies of a seven-inch single on Mapplethorpe's tiny independent label."
**Called a "Punk" (Mid-1970s)** : In the mid-1970s, as the term "punk rock" was being used liberally and carelessly, Patti Smith was among the artists, including Aerosmith, Bruce Springsteen, and The Rolling Stones, who were called a "punk." "By the middle of the 70s, the term was being thrown around very carelessly, very liberally. Patty Smith was called a punk."
**Embraced by New York Avant-garde** : Patti Smith was recognized as an "artsy soulmate" for other experimental New York artists like Television, and her music was embraced when bands like Pere Ubu brought their Cleveland sound to New York. "When Ubu brought this music to New York, home of artsy soulmates like television and Patsy Smith, they were embraced."

**Early Semantic Mystery** : The term "punk" historically described a young male gangster or hood hundreds of years ago. Its first appearance in print in a music context as "punk rock" was in the Chicago Tribune on March 22, 1970, with Ed Sanders of The Fugs describing his solo album as "punk rock redneck sentimentality." "Now there's that word again. Punk? How did it end up being used to describe a certain type of rock music?"
**Early Uses in Music Criticism (1970-1971)** : The term "punk" began appearing in music criticism in the early 1970s; Lester Bangs called Iggy Pop a "stooge punk" in Cream Magazine (December 1970), and Dave Marsh referred to Question Mark and the Mysterians as "purveyors of punk rock" in Cream (May 1971). Alan Vega's band Suicide also adopted the term for their "punk masses" gigs. "In December 1970, the Detroit-based music magazine called Cream ran an article from a critic named Lester Banks who referred to Iggy Pop as that stooge punk."
**Ambiguous Usage (Mid-1970s)** : By the mid-1970s, "punk rock" was used carelessly and liberally, with artists like Patti Smith, Aerosmith, Bruce Springsteen, The Rolling Stones, and even The Bay City Rollers being called "punks," indicating a lack of clear definition. "The problem was that no one really knew what anybody meant by punk rock. By the middle of the 70s, the term was being thrown around very carelessly, very liberally."
**Term Definition by "Punk" Magazine (1975)** : The fanzine "Punk," launched at the end of 1975, was crucial in defining the term. It covered artists like Lou Reed, The Ramones, Iggy Pop, The Stooges, The New York Dolls, and 60s garage bands. Its name, inspired by The Dictators' 1975 album "Go Girl Crazy," solidified "punk" as the descriptor for the new, stripped-down rock and roll sound and the artists associated with the CBGB scene. "And so, the authors of this fanzine decided to call their publication, Punk. And as the CBGB scene grew bigger, circulation increased, and soon anyone who was featured in the fanzine was known as a punk artist."
**Global Germination (1975)** : By 1975, punk rock had started "germinating" in New York, with significant contributions from the Cleveland music scene, indicating its early spread and development beyond a single geographical origin. "By 1975, punk rock had started germinating in New York, thanks to help from Cleveland."

**Early 1970s Socio-Political Climate** : The early 1970s were characterized as a "bad hangover from the 60s," marked by the failure of peace and love ideals, economic struggles (first oil crisis, inflation, recession, unemployment), the Cold War, violent events like the Kent State shootings, and a corrupt American presidency. This somber, conservative mood was a backdrop for the emergence of radical new music. "The early 70s were like a bad hangover from the 60s."
**1960s Legacy and Disillusionment** : While the hippie generation achieved victories in civil rights, women's rights, birth control, and ending the draft and Vietnam War, there was a growing sense that the "peace and love" approach to social change had "played itself out." The adult 60s generation faced new realities, and rock music, once a force for change, felt "tired." "But there was also a sense that the whole peace and love approach to social change had pretty much played itself out."
**1950s Nostalgia (Mid-1970s)** : The movie "American Graffiti" and the TV show "Happy Days" triggered a revival of 1950s nostalgia, leading people to romanticize the decade as "the good old days," despite the underlying fear of atomic war that characterized the era. This cultural trend brought 1950s music back to AM radio and reintroduced terms like "punk" into common use. "Meanwhile, the movie American graffiti in the debut of the show Happy Days kicked off the revival of the 1950s."
**AM Radio in the Early 1970s** : AM radio in the early 1970s was criticized for playing "bad music," including artists like Freddie Fender, Billy Swan, and Paper Lace, and was seen as having "devolved" into playing bland material. **CBGB - Club History and Transformation** : CBGB, originally named "CBGB-UMPFUG" (for Country, Bluegrass, Blues, and Other Music for Uplifting Gourmandizers) by owner Hilly Crystal, opened in the winter of 1973. Despite its location in the notoriously bad Bowery area of New York City, surrounded by flop houses, drug dens, and ex-convicts, it transformed from a slow Sunday night venue to the epicenter of a new rock scene after Television's debut on March 31, 1974. On a Friday in August 1974, the building tragically collapsed into the street, killing four people and injuring 19, leaving the burgeoning music scene without a central gathering place and driving artists to venues like CBGB. "On Friday in August of 1974, the building creaked and cracked and then collapsed into the street. Four people died and 19 people were hurt. And that was the end of the Mercer Art Center."
