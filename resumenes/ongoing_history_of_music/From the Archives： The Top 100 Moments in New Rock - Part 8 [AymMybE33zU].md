## curiosities
**Chaotic New York Concert (May 15, 1980)** : Public Image Limited (P.I.L.), led by Johnny Lydon (formerly Johnny Rotten), agreed to play two nights at the Ritz in New York for a $12,000 fee, despite internal squabbles, drug abuse, and money problems. The band was in no shape to play, lacking a drummer (the sixth one quit) and a bass player (who had just walked out). They quickly hired a 65-year-old jazz drummer from a Broadway music shop. Instead of performing live, P.I.L. pantomimed along to their records, hidden from the audience by a large screen with their silhouettes projected by bright white lights. The 1,500 people in the audience were kept waiting in the pouring rain for over an hour, then discovered the band wasn't playing, and Johnny Lydon began taunting and insulting the crowd. This led to a rain of garbage, a storming of the stage, and the destruction of all equipment, with the gig ending after just 25 minutes. "The gig was killed after just 25 minutes."

## albums
**Green (1988) - Major Label Debut** : "Green" was R.E.M.'s first album released on Warner Brothers, a major record label, after their significant move from indie label IRS Records. The album was symbolically important because it demonstrated that a college rock indie band could transition to a major label without compromising their music, art, or integrity. Its title was theorized to be a humorous nod to their major label signing. "The Green album was hugely important in a symbolic sense. It showed that it was possible for a college rock indie band to work for a major without selling their music, their art, and their integrity."

## curiosities
**Indie Roots and Mainstream Growth (1982-1985)** : R.E.M. started as a no-name indie band from the Deep South, releasing their first records on Hib-Tone before moving to IRS Records, an indie label run by Miles Copeland. Between 1982 and 1985, they released five albums on IRS, earning respect as pioneers of the college rock scene in North America, while also gaining a mainstream following due to their accessible music. "R.E.M. released five albums on IRS between 1982 and 1985, and during that time they grew to be one of the most respected alternative bands in North America."
**Transition to Warner Brothers (April 8, 1988)** : By 1987, R.E.M. had outgrown IRS Records, which could no longer provide the necessary distribution and support for their expanding fanbase. Warner Brothers, a major label, offered a $6 million check and promises of complete creative control, leading R.E.M. to sign on April 8, 1988. This move was considered risky for their image with their fanbase, but was deemed necessary for worldwide record distribution and to get off the constant recording and touring treadmill. "R.E.M. became the biggest of the so-called college rock indie bands to make the jump to a multi-million dollar major record label."
**Balancing Art and Commerce** : Peter Buck articulated the band's struggle to balance artistic integrity with commercial interests, expressing their fear of diluting the band's essence while acknowledging their role within the music industry "machine." Michael Stipe further explained the move to Warner Brothers by stating that IRS's distribution capabilities had reached their limit, necessitating a partnership with a label capable of global reach. "In our own case, R.E.M. is always trying to figure out where the line is between commerce and what we do, because it means something to us."

Following a heart attack scare in the fall of 1976, both Bowie and Iggy Pop began to earnestly clean up their lifestyles, leading to a period of intense creativity. # instrument - Compact Disk

## curiosities
**Conceptualization and Prototype (1969-1970)** : In 1969, Dutch physicist Klaus Compaan conceived the idea of breaking sound into binary bits (zeros and ones) that could be read and converted back into sound. By 1970, working for Phillips with Peter Kramer, he developed a prototype of the compact disk, initially made of glass, and determined that a laser was the optimal method for reading the encoded information. "In 1969, a Dutch physicist by the name of Klaus Kompon had a thought, what if you could break sound down into tiny binary bits, you know, zeros and ones?"
**Standardization and Global Rollout (1979-1982)** : Phillips and its subsidiaries experimented with the CD for years, with Sony joining development in 1979, leading to the establishment of a worldwide standard. The final specifications included a polycarbonate disk (replacing glass), a diameter of 120 millimeters, laser reading from the inside spiraling outwards (opposite of vinyl), and a maximum length of 74 minutes, reportedly to accommodate the Sony chairman's favorite Beethoven symphony. In 1982, major electronics manufacturers were on board, and the technology rolled out in Europe and Japan that fall, followed by North America the next spring. "A worldwide standard is set. The disk will be made from polycarbonate instead of glass. They will be exactly 120 millimeters in diameter."
**Impact on Music Industry (1980s)** : Upon its introduction, the compact disk rapidly transformed the music industry. Vinyl records and pre-recorded cassettes, which collectively sold nearly a billion copies annually, largely disappeared by the end of the 1980s. Furthermore, the advent of the CD pulled the entire music industry out of a severe recession that had followed the end of the disco era. "And not only that, the introduction to the CD brought the entire music industry out of a terrible recession following the end of disco."
