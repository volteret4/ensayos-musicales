# curiosity

**Streaming History – Rhapsody Launched December 3, 2001; Now Trades as Napster** : The modern all-you-can-hear streaming subscription model traces back to Rhapsody, which first went online on December 3, 2001. Over the following year, Universal, EMI, BMG, Warner, and Sony all joined. Rhapsody still exists but rebranded as Napster after purchasing that trademark in 2011, making it the legitimate successor to the original file-sharing service. "This is the legitimate form of Napster."

**Streaming Platforms – Approximately Three Dozen Active Services Worldwide** : Most streaming conversations focus on Spotify, Apple Music, Amazon Music, Tidal, YouTube Music, and Deezer, but there are approximately three dozen active services globally at any given time. These include Anghami (Abu Dhabi, the Arab world's first streaming platform), Gaana and JioSaavn (India's two largest), Patari (Lahore, Pakistan), KKBox (Taiwan), and several large Chinese services including QQ Music. Defunct services include Songs, Mog, Xbox Music, and Slacker Radio. "There are about, and it changes from day to day, three dozen active companies operating around the world."

**Streaming Pricing – Same Service, Wildly Different Prices by Country** : Spotify is available in approximately 170 countries and charges different rates adjusted for local currencies and economic conditions. In Canada, the price is $9.99 CAD; in the US, $9.99 USD (about $12 CAD); in Egypt, roughly $4 CAD; in Vietnam, $3 CAD; and in India, $1.97 CAD. These represent different tiers of Spotify Premium, which grants full functionality and no commercials. "If you're in Japan, you're paying $980 per month."

**Streaming Library Size – 90 Million Songs, 60,000 New Tracks Uploaded Daily** : As of early 2021, the available streaming universe contained approximately 90 million songs, all of which are accessible on any major platform. Spotify reported that 60,000 new tracks were being uploaded every single day — 2.2 million per year. Between 20 and 25% of all Spotify songs have never been streamed even once, meaning 30 to 35 million songs have been heard by no one. A site called Forgotify lets users stream only songs with zero plays using a Spotify login. "There's even an app for that."

**Streaming and the Environment – Streaming 27 Times Uses More Energy Than Manufacturing a CD** : A 2017 Norwegian study found that, despite involving only digital zeros and ones, streaming music is actually worse for the environment than manufacturing physical media. Downloading one gigabyte of music data uses roughly the energy required to run a light bulb for an hour. Data centers consume approximately 2% of the world's energy to run and to keep cool. Streaming an album 27 times uses more energy than manufacturing a single CD or vinyl record. The amount of greenhouse gases generated by music streaming reaches up to 350 million kilograms per year. "Streaming an album over the internet just 27 times will use more energy than it takes to produce and manufacture a single CD or vinyl record."

**Music Piracy – Dropped Dramatically After Streaming Arrived** : Before streaming took off in the late 2000s, millions of people were stealing music through file sharing, torrents, and bootlegs. Streaming reduced piracy dramatically by offering high-quality music cheaply with no virus risk, incomplete files, or obligation to seed. A practice called stream ripping — recording songs from streams — persists, but piracy is nowhere near what it was. CD sales continue to decline but more slowly than before. "Why bother going through the hassle of stealing something when you could simply be assured of high quality music from streams."

**Average Music Spending – Jumped from $45 to $120 Per Year with Streaming** : During the file-sharing era, the average annual spend of a North American music fan was approximately $45. With hundreds of millions now paying for streaming at roughly $9.99 per month, the average spend has risen to at least $120 per year. "There's money coming in."

**Streaming Payouts – Platform by Platform Comparison** : Streaming payout rates vary significantly between services. For 100 listens, Spotify pays approximately 40 US cents; Apple Music and Amazon Music each pay about 50 cents; Deezer (France) pays 47 cents; YouTube pays only 18 cents; SoundCloud pays 13 cents; and Tidal may pay as much as $1.20. These figures are estimates because the system is opaque even for artists. A single Spotify stream pays roughly 6 tenths of a cent on the subscription tier, but the freemium (ad-supported) tier pays less — hence the blended average of approximately 40 cents per 100 streams. "The whole system is very, very opaque, even for the artist."

**Streaming Revenue Flow – Labels Take Their Share Before Artists See a Cent** : Before any payout reaches an artist, it passes through the record label, which deducts its share first. At the high end, Spotify might pay out $4 for 100 streams, but the label absorbs approximately $3.60 before the artist sees anything. Whatever the artist does receive is almost always less than the 10% they received from physical CD sales. The 10% figure on CDs assumed a $10 wholesale price, so $1 per album; streaming makes even that look generous. "Think about that the next time you don't pay for a streaming music service and go with a freemium tier."

**Pro Rata Payment Model – Drake Gets Paid from Jazz Fans' Subscriptions** : Spotify does not pay artists based on who specifically listened to them. Instead, artists are paid based on their percentage of overall streams in a given month. If Drake accounts for X percent of all streams, he receives X percent of all subscription revenue — regardless of who subscribed or why. This means a jazz fan's subscription money does not go to Miles Davis; it goes into a collective pool that flows primarily to whoever dominates the stream count. Small-audience artists with loyal but numerically limited fan bases are effectively squeezed out. "Musicians that used to have a small but loyal fan base are now making next to nothing."

**Labels and Spotify – Major Labels Hold Equity Positions; 85% of Revenue from Streaming** : After being dragged into streaming kicking and screaming, major record labels now love it: 85% of all label revenue comes from streaming. Labels also hold equity positions in Spotify, meaning they profit both from royalties and from the platform's market valuation of approximately $250 billion. Labels benefit from streaming's elimination of manufacturing, warehousing, shipping, and physical retail costs. "Labels would be thrilled if CDs disappeared tomorrow."

**Spotify's Business Model – Podcasts as a Way to Reduce Royalty Obligations** : Spotify's strategy for achieving profitability involves growing its podcast business. If 15 to 25% of users spend their listening time on podcasts, that portion of usage generates no music royalty costs. This is why the company paid enormous sums for exclusive rights to programs like the Joe Rogan Experience. As of Q1 2021, Spotify reported an operating profit of 15 million euros. They also earn from sponsored recommendation campaigns, where artists and labels pay to have music promoted — which some compare to payola and others compare to record store promotional placement. "This explains why the company is willing to pay huge dollars for exclusive rights to programs like the Joe Rogan experience."
