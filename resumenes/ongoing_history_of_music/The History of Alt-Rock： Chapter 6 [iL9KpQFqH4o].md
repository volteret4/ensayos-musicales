# genre - New Wave
## curiosities
**Origin of the Term (1976)** : The earliest known references to the phrase "New Wave" appeared in the British fanzine *Sniffing Glue* in 1976. The writers intended to draw a connection between the lofty artistic ambitions of British punks and the "renegade French filmmakers of the 50s and 60s" who pioneered the French Nouvelle Vague movement. "The earliest references we can find to that phrase are in a British fanzine called Sniffing Glue in 1976."
**Malcolm McLaren's Influence (late 1976)** : In late 1976, Sex Pistols manager Malcolm McLaren was quoted in *Melody Maker* magazine using the term "new wave." He applied it to bands that, though related to punk and sharing its essence, did not necessarily sound strictly punk, helping the term gain traction within a year to describe underground music emerging from punk. "Later that year, late 76, a story in Melody Maker magazine quoted Sex Pistols Manager Malcolm McLaren, very artsy guy. And he was using the term and applying it to bands that were related to punk, part of the same seamst punk, but not necessarily sounding that punk at all."
**Interchangeable with Punk (UK)** : For a period in the UK, the terms "punk" and "new wave" were largely interchangeable, reflecting the initial fluidity and lack of distinct categorization within the burgeoning music scene. "And actually for a while, in the UK, anyway, punk and new wave were pretty much interchangeable."
**American Marketing Strategy** : In New York City, Sire Records faced a marketing challenge as mainstream radio stations refused to play their punk signings like The Ramones. During a marketing meeting, the term "new wave" was proposed as a substitute for "punk" to describe new artists. This strategic re-branding successfully "wiped away" the stigmas associated with punk, facilitating radio play and broader acceptance. "At a marketing meeting at Sire Records, someone suggested that they use the term new wave in place of punk to describe their new signings. And it worked, just like that."
**Evolution of Definition** : Initially, there was considerable confusion surrounding the precise definition of New Wave, with diverse bands such as Talking Heads, Blondie, Elvis Costello, and Tom Petty and the Heartbreakers all being labeled under this umbrella. Over time, the term evolved to describe a branch of new rock music that retained punk's attitude, aggression, energy, tension, and twitchiness, but integrated a greater emphasis on experimentation, electronics, and artsiness. "But just like we saw with punk, there was some confusion at first at what was meant by new wave."
**"Punk Only Intelligent"** : New Wave was frequently characterized as "like punk only intelligent," a description that highlighted its experimental and artistic dimensions while retaining the raw energy and attitude of its punk predecessor. This broad definition allowed for significant "wiggle room" and fostered diverse musical approaches. "People described new wave as like punk only intelligent."
**North American Phenomenon (1978-1984)** : The New Wave era predominantly manifested as a North American phenomenon, spanning from approximately 1978 to 1984. This period was characterized by two distinct phases, with the first featuring hundreds of guitar-driven pop bands inspired by 1960s British and American rock, and liberated from conventional thinking by the influence of punk. "Stepping back just a little, we can see that the new wave era became mainly a North American phenomenon that stretched from 1978 through to about 1984."
**Geographic Spread** : Unlike punk, which was largely concentrated in major urban centers like New York or London, New Wave bands emerged from "everywhere," including regions not traditionally associated with punk. This wider geographic distribution signified a more decentralized and expansive musical movement. "These new bands were coming from everywhere."
**Vocal Style Development** : As New Wave gained popularity, new vocal styles began to emerge, with singers often sounding "geeky" and unashamed. This reflected a musical ethos that was catchy, fun, and perceived as more authentic than mainstream offerings, providing artists a license to be silly and goofy in a fashionable and artistic manner. "Singer sounded, uh, kind of geeky, orn't ashamed of it, because they didn't care."
**Rebellion Against Disco** : New Wave developed, in part, as a rebellion against what was perceived as the "hideousness of disco." It offered an alternative musical space for fans seeking sounds different from the polarizing offspring of R&B. "Freed from the rigid dogma of punk and anxious to rebellion to the hideousness of disco, the subgenres just kept on coming."

# genre - Disco
## curiosities
**Polarizing Force (1978)** : In 1978, disco was an intensely polarizing force in music, compelling fans to gravitate away from mainstream sounds and towards the expanding fringes of popular music. It was profoundly hated by rock fans, who condemned it as "evil, vacuous, boring, mindless, empty and obnoxious." "That repulsive force, that dark matter was known as disco."
**Impact on Live Music Venues** : The rise of disco led to clubs replacing live music stages with DJ booths, as spinning records proved significantly cheaper than compensating live musicians. This shift caused widespread resentment among musicians and rock music enthusiasts. "Clubs that used to have live music replaced their stages with DJ booths, spinning records was far cheaper than paying musicians."
**Bee Gees as "Poster Demons"** : The Bee Gees, prominently associated with the *Saturday Night Fever* soundtrack, were demonized by rock fans. They were cast as the "people who destroyed music" due to their immense popularity and influence within the disco movement. "The Bee Gees, the people behind the Saturday Night Fever soundtrack, were poster demons as the people who destroyed music."
**Call for Destruction** : Rock fans expressed an intense desire for disco to be "despised, shunned, mocked, sabotage and ultimately destroyed," highlighting the profound animosity and cultural divide that the genre created. "disco was evil, vacuous, boring, mindless, empty and obnoxious and needed to be despised, shunned, mocked, sabotage and ultimately destroyed."
**Contemporary Perception** : Today, disco is often merely regarded as "cheesy music from the past," a stark contrast to the fervent hatred it generated among rock fans in 1978. "Now today disco is merely cheesy music from the past, but if you were alive in 1978 and into rock there was nothing and I mean nothing you hated more than disco."

# genre - Electronic Music
## curiosities
**Post-War German Scene** : In post-World War II Germany, following a period of denazification, young German musicians explored modernism and the avant-garde. By the 1950s, this evolution led to the creation of an experimental electronic music scene, primarily centered around universities and institutes that possessed the means to afford the then-exotic and expensive equipment. "By the 1950s, this had resulted in the creation of an experimental electronic music scene centered around the universities and institutes that could afford such exotic and expensive gear."
**Fusion with Psychedelia (1968)** : By 1968, some enlightened young German musicians, grappling with their national identity, began to fuse these experimental electronic traditions with the psychedelic music prevalent at the time. Their objective was to forge a novel form of music that was independent of American or UK influences. "By 1968, some young enlightened musicians, those who were lost when it came to their identity as Germans, were involved in fusing these experimental electronic traditions with the psychedelic stuff of the day."
**Abandonment of Traditional Instrumentation** : For some German musicians, the path to creating a new and distinctive sound involved entirely abandoning the traditional instrumentation of rock and roll. Instead, they embraced the modern electronic music of their Fatherland as a means to establish a unique national musical identity. "To some, the only solution was to abandon the traditional instrumentation of Rock and Roll entirely. They had to embrace the modern electronic music of the Fatherland."
**Cold, Paranoid Appeal (late 1970s/early 1980s)** : Amidst the raw intensity of punk rock, a severe economic recession in the late 1970s and early 1980s, and the escalating Cold War, the concept of "cold, paranoid, hollow, dehumanized music" became particularly appealing to some audiences and artists. This aesthetic was strongly associated with the emerging electronic sound. "Under the all too real intensity of punk rock, the brutal recession of the late 70s and early 80s and the escalating cold war between America and the USSR, the idea of cold, paranoid, hollow, dehumanized music was actually pretty appealing for some people."
**Robot/Machine Creation** : The radical idea of delegating the creation of music to robots and machines was perceived as the ultimate "punk" act. This innovative approach significantly contributed to the development and distinct character of the electronic music sound. "And what could be more punk, more radical than turning the creation of music over to robots and machines?"
**Shift from Guitars to Keyboards (1980s)** : As the 1980s dawned, a widespread desire emerged to "ditch their guitars and get into the 21st century a little bit early by buying cheapos synthesizers and making the music of the future." Keyboards and other electronic instruments consequently became the primary focus of this new musical direction, although some artists still advocated for the guitar's continued relevance. "As the 1980s beckoned, it seemed that everybody wanted to ditch their guitars and get into the 21st century a little bit early by buying cheapos synthesizers and making the music of the future."

**Nouvelle Vague (French Film Movement)** : In the late 1950s and early 1960s, a group of young French filmmakers launched the Nouvelle Vague movement, advocating for increased artistic control and reduced interference from studios. They asserted that this "freeform attitude" was essential to advance the art of film, and this movement is now considered one of the most pivotal developments in the history of motion pictures. "In the late 1950s and early 1960s, a group of young French filmmakers decided to mess things up. They insisted on more artistic control and less meddling by the studios."
**Film to Music Analogy (1978)** : The concept of Nouvelle Vague from French cinema was directly paralleled by the emergence of New Wave music in 1978. Just as the French filmmakers embraced a "freeform attitude" to advance film, musicians adopted a similar approach to evolve rock and roll, essentially a "New Val Vague all over again" but using the English translation. "It was New Val Vague all over again. But only this time, they used the English translation. They called it New Wave."
**1978 as a Critical Year for Rock and Roll** : The year 1978 marked a critical juncture in the history of rock and roll, initiating a "crazy series of leaps" in its evolution. Prior to this, popular music categories like pop, rock, country, and R&B were relatively simple, but by 1978, they began to unravel, segment, and stratify into numerous distinct subgenres. "1978 is a critical year in the history of rock and roll. This is where its evolution took a crazy series of leaps because up until then it was fairly easy to categorize modern popular music."
**Music Genre Stratification (1978)** : By 1978, the four basic popular music categories—pop, rock, country, and R&B—began to fragment and stratify into many distinct subgenres. Rock diversified into punk, metal, prog rock, soft rock, and folk rock; country split into traditionalists and modernizers; and R&B gave rise to funk, rap, hip hop, and disco. "But by 1978, each of these basic categories had begun to unravel and get complicated. They began to separate segment and stratify into many different and distinct subgenres."

**British Genre Confusion (late 1970s)** : While New Wave was gaining traction as a North American phenomenon, a similar musical shift was occurring in Britain without a definitive name. Terms like "New Wave" had been appropriated by Americans, and alternatives such as "Avant Gard," "Post Modernism," and "Post Rock" were deemed either too generic, pretentious, or meaningless, leading to confusion over how to categorize emerging subgenres like Technopop, Goth, and Ska. "While New Wave was happening in North America, a similar thing with a different name was going on in Britain. And no one knew what to call it at first."
