## albums
**The Peppermint-eaky (Debut EP)** : Sloan's debut release, an EP that featured the original version of "Underwhelmed" and prominently displayed the face of Jason Larson, the band's namesake, on its cover. This EP was released on Murder Records in early 1992, established by the band to avoid major label delays and enable immediate touring. "The original version of Underwhelmed from Sloan's debut release, which is called The Peppermint-eaky."
**Smeared (1992) - Geffen Debut** : The band's first record on Geffen/DGC, initially recorded in a house in Halifax for approximately $1,200 with modest aspirations of pressing 500-1000 CDs. After signing, it was remixed by Geffen and its sound was heavily influenced by Nirvana and noisy pop bands from England like My Bloody Valentine and Ride, characterized by loud guitars. In 1996, Sloan regained the rights to this album in Canada, bringing it under their Murder Records label. "I think a lot of the records that really influenced us for smeared was, you know, the best parts of Nirvana but also the best parts of what was going on in England at the time, like my bloody Valentine and ride and all those sort of noisy pop bands that were on creation records and everything."
**Twice Removed (1994) - Quieter Departure** : Sloan's second album, released in 1994, which marked a deliberate stylistic shift away from the fuzzy, loud guitar sound of "Smeared" towards a more poppy and quieter aesthetic. Influenced by Fleetwood Mac, The Velvet Underground's third album, and The Beatles, this move was seen by the band as "career suicide" and initially met with skepticism from Geffen Records, who suggested redoing it. Recorded in Lenny Kravitz's studio using his analog board and Beatles gear, it cost $120,000, a significant increase from their debut. Despite early commercial struggles, it became one of their most beloved albums, eventually selling as many copies as "Smeared," and was named the number one Canadian album of all time by Chart Magazine in 1996. Sloan later regained the rights to this album in Canada. "Sloan from their second record, twice removed. Which sounded substantially less fuzzy and more poppy than the first couple of records."
**One Chord to Another (1996) - Independent Spirit** : Released in 1996, this album followed the band's near-breakup and contractual struggles with Geffen, almost becoming a Geffen release before they moved to The Enclave, a short-lived boutique label, and was released on Murder Records in Canada. Uniquely, the album was recorded with members in different locations, as Chris, Jay, and Patrick laid down instrumental parts in Toronto, then sent tapes to Andrew in Halifax for drum tracks. It earned a respectable three and a half out of five stars from Rolling Stone Magazine, won a Juno Award, and helped solidify their fanbase in the US, despite its American label collapsing. "Sloan from their 1996 album One Cord to Another, everything you've done wrong."
**Navy Blues (1998)** : Released in 1998, this album was self-released in the United States and continued to be released on Murder Records in Canada after the collapse of The Enclave. Jay Ferguson considers it, along with "Between The Bridges," as almost a double album because they were recorded close together at the same location. "Now it's 1997. And we've recorded Navy Blues. So we just put it out ourselves."
**Between The Bridges (1999)** : This album is viewed by Jay Ferguson as part of an unofficial double album alongside "Navy Blues," due to both being recorded closely together at the same location. Ferguson notably wrote only one new song for this record during that period, reflecting a continuous creative flow between the two albums. "I consider Navy Blues and between the bridges almost a double album because they were done close together at the same place and I only wrote one new song for this new record."
**Never Hear The End of It (2006) - Thirty-Song Opus** : Described as a "30 song, Opus," this album was recorded by the band in their own practice space, showcasing their self-sufficiency in production. Learning all 30 songs for subsequent touring proved to be a significant challenge for the band members, highlighting the extensive nature of the work. "Never hear the end of it. The 30 song, Opus."
**Parallel Play (2008) - Shortest Album** : Sloan's ninth studio record, released in 2008, notable for being their shortest at just over 37 minutes and featuring 13 songs. The title refers to a child psychology term where children play beside each other without interacting, humorously reflecting the band's collaborative yet often individualistic songwriting and recording process, particularly evident on this album which was made quickly in their rehearsal studio. "Sloan with Believe in Me, the first single from their 2008 album, Parallel Play."
**Four Nights at the Palais Royale (Live Album)** : This live album is noted as a "second real peak" for Sloan since their formation in 1991, signifying a high point in their career trajectory following an earlier peak. "The live record, four nights at the Palais Royale, was a peak for Sloan."
**Greatest Hits Collection** : Sloan has released a compilation of their most popular songs, which is part of their extensive discography that also includes studio albums, live records, videos, and DVDs. "Nine studio albums, the live record, the greatest hits collection of videos, the DVDs, the rarities, the bonus tracks, the tours."
**Rarities/Bonus Tracks Collections** : The band has released collections featuring rarities and bonus tracks, further expanding their comprehensive body of work. "Nine studio albums, the live record, the greatest hits collection of videos, the DVDs, the rarities, the bonus tracks, the tours."

## curiosities
**Formation and Longevity (1991)** : Sloan was formed in 1991, emerging from the Halifax area music scene after previous bands like Carney Lake Road. They are noted for their exceptional longevity, having existed since their formation. "Sloan. They were formed in 1991."
**Founding Members' Diverse Backgrounds** : The band's members brought diverse backgrounds to its formation: Jay Ferguson was a record store clerk from Halifax; Chris Murphy was a child model for Winnie the Pooh sleeping bags and Snyder's Bacon commercials; Andrew Scott was a provincial high school high jump champion and track and field record holder, later voted one of Canada's 20 sexiest musicians; and Patrick Pentland was born in Northern Ireland, lived in Jamaica, and settled in Sackville, Nova Scotia. "And this is a show on how a record store clerk, a saxophone player born in Northern Ireland, a former provincial high school high jump champion, and a model for Winnie the Pooh sleeping bags in the Eaton's catalog came together to become one of this country's most enduring rock bands."
**Chris Murphy - Child Modeling Career** : As a child, Chris Murphy modeled Winnie the Pooh sleeping bags for the Eaton's catalog and appeared in Snyder's Bacon commercials, where he played a child stealing bacon. This early experience is humorously compared to Eddie Vedder's own child modeling. "It was Chris. I did as a kid. I modeled Winnie the Pooh sleeping bag. Well, I was in the, like, the Eaton's in the series catalog. I know a couple of commercials. It's Snyder's Bacon."
**Jay Ferguson - Record Store Clerk** : Before Sloan, Jay Ferguson worked as a record store clerk at a secondhand shop in Halifax. He had been playing music with Chris Murphy since the age of seven. "Jay Ferguson was the record store clerk. It was a secondhand shop at Halifax."
**Patrick Pentland - International Upbringing and Role Change** : Patrick Pentland was born in Northern Ireland, lived in Jamaica, and later settled in Sackville, Nova Scotia. He joined Sloan as a guitar player, leading to Chris Murphy's "demotion" to bass within a year because Pentland was considered a better guitarist. "Patrick Pentland was the guy who was born in Northern Ireland. He also lived in Jamaica before his family finally settled in Sacville, Nova Scotia."
**Andrew Scott - Athletic and Charismatic** : Andrew Scott was a provincial track and field record holder in Nova Scotia before joining Sloan as the drummer. He was also voted one of Canada's 20 sexiest musicians. "And drummer Andrew Scott was the former holder of several provincial track and field records in Nova Scotia. He was also voted one of Canada's 20 sexiest musicians."
**Band Name Origin** : The band's name, "Sloan," was derived from the nickname of Jason Larson, the bass player of the New Brunswick/Bedford band The Straight Jackets. Larson's French-speaking boss in a Montreal warehouse mispronounced "Slow One" as "Sloan," and the band adopted this nickname, featuring Larson's face on their debut EP, "The Peppermint-eaky." "Their bass player, his nickname, he worked... And his French boss used to call... The bass player, Jason Larson used to call him Slow One. And with the French accent, it just turned into Sloan."
**Formation of Murder Records (Early 1992)** : Sloan established their own record label, Murder Records, in early 1992, to release their debut EP "The Peppermint-eaky" and bypass the extended timelines of major labels like Geffen/DGC, which projected a much later release date for their first album. The label allowed them to quickly put out music, facilitate touring, and later expanded to release records for other Halifax bands, fostering a communal scene. "So that's why we started murder records was just to put out that EP. And then there was other bands that we knew of that we were friends with."
**Signing to Geffen/DGC Records** : Sloan, an indie band from Nova Scotia, signed to Geffen/DGC, Nirvana's record label, after a rapid series of events. A demo tape sent to Cam Carpenter (A&R at MCA Canada) led to a recommendation to Rick Arboit (Network Records), who then passed it to Todd Sullivan (Geffen). Sullivan, impressed by "Underwhelmed," signed them, a rare and exciting occurrence for a Halifax band. "He set it to a friend of his Todd Sullivanic Geffen who loved the song underwhelmed and then came to see his play and then he was like, do you guys want to sign to Geffen?"
**Halifax Music Community Support** : After gaining major label attention, Sloan made a conscious effort to support the Halifax music scene, where they felt they "knew everybody who was into cool music." They expanded Murder Records to release albums by local bands like Eric's Trip, Hardship Post, and Thrush Hermit, and brought these bands on tour to share opportunities. "But we really tried to be gracious about it and really try part of the expanding murder records and putting out other records was an effort to be a part of the community where we were all of a sudden our aspirations changed to being national or international."
**Post-Twice Removed Breakup Rumors (1995)** : In late 1994 and early 1995, rumors of Sloan disbanding spread, culminating in an April 1st, 1995 newswire story. Reasons cited by manager Chip Sutherland included Geffen's perceived inadequate US promotion, Andrew Scott's move to Toronto, and the band's reluctance to continue under a major label contract. Chris Murphy even announced a Halifax show as their last. "After twice removed, things began to, well, they began to fall apart. This was when the breakup rumors started. On April 1st, 1995, a story was carried on the newswire. Sloan disbands Halifax, Grunge Pop's sensation may be calling it quits."
**Contractual Standoff with Geffen** : Facing a six-album contract with Geffen after their informal breakup, Sloan initially withheld the news from the label to avoid disadvantageous "broken up band" contractual clauses. Geffen eventually released them from their contract in an artist-friendly move, allowing Sloan to pursue other options. "As a band that was broken up, we were on Geffen and we were signed something like six records or something like that, which is not really in our favor."
**The Enclave Record Deal (1996)** : After departing Geffen, Sloan signed with The Enclave in 1996, a short-lived "boutique label with tons of money" founded by Tom Zutat, mentor to Todd Sullivan. Zutat aimed to make Sloan a major hit, but the label went out of business within six months, leaving Sloan without a US label by 1997. "And we signed to this other label called the OnClave, which was short lived, which just set up to be really good at head, like balance of bastion, like the water boys."
**Regaining Music Rights** : Following the collapse of The Enclave and their departure from Geffen, Sloan successfully fought to remain independent and regained the rights to their music. They eventually acquired the Canadian rights to "Smeared" and "Twice Removed," consolidating their entire catalog under their own Murder Records label, a point of pride for the band. "The good news is that Sloan successfully fought to stay independent. They also got the rights to their music back, which, as you heard, is something the band is pretty proud of."
**Collaborative and Individualistic Songwriting** : Sloan operates with an egalitarian approach where all four members sing, write, and contribute creatively, splitting money equally. Andrew Scott's move to Toronto in 1993 fostered a more individualistic recording process, with members often playing all instruments on their own songs. This "parallel play" style was particularly evident on their album of the same name. "So we've all kind of evolved into this band that writes sort of individually."
**Self-Sufficiency in Recording** : After expensive major label experiences (e.g., $120,000 for "Twice Removed"), Sloan embraced self-sufficiency, recording albums like "Never Hear The End of It" and "Parallel Play" in their own rehearsal studio. This allowed them to produce music on a budget, making sales successes easier to achieve. "Why wouldn't we just record it on our practice base?"
**Juno Award (1996)** : Sloan won a Juno Award in 1996, recognizing their contributions to Canadian music, around the time of their album "One Chord to Another." "They wanted Juneau, Chart Magazine named twice removed as the number one Canadian album of all time."
**Chart Magazine Accolades (1996)** : In 1996, Chart Magazine declared "Twice Removed" the number one Canadian album of all time and "Underwhelmed" the number two Canadian song of all time, signifying significant critical acclaim within Canada. "Chart Magazine named twice removed as the number one Canadian album of all time. And underwhelmed was picked as the number two Canadian song of all time."
**Aspiration for International Recognition and Box Sets** : Despite feeling they hadn't "gotten any bigger" in 1999, Sloan continues to record with the long-term goal of accumulating an "enormous body of work." They hope to eventually gain recognition from international publications like Mojo Magazine or Record Collector Magazine, which could lead to a retrospective box set and coffee table book, although they acknowledge the audience might currently be too small without a "huge hit." "Just to have an enormous body of work so that Mojo Magazine finally goes, we are sorry, we never wrote about you guys and you guys are unbelievable and you have all this stuff and nobody knows."

# label - Geffen

## curiosities
**Sloan's Major Label Deal** : Geffen Records (also referred to as DGC) signed Sloan after A&R representative Todd Sullivan was captivated by their song "Underwhelmed." The signing was a rapid and exciting event for the Halifax band. "He set it to a friend of his Todd Sullivanic Geffen who loved the song underwhelmed and then came to see his play and then he was like, do you guys want to sign to Geffen?"
**Skepticism Towards "Twice Removed"** : Geffen Records expressed significant concern about Sloan's album "Twice Removed" due to its stylistic departure from "Smeared," viewing it as a completely different band and suggesting they "redo the whole thing" because it would be difficult to market. Despite this, Sloan stood their ground, a move that later became part of their "sticking it to the man" narrative. "It is true that Geffen, when they heard it, they were like, what are you serious? Like, this is a different band. It's going to be hard for us to market you. Please, you know, consider redoing the whole thing."
**Artist-Friendly Contract Release** : Despite a six-album contract, Geffen Records ultimately chose to release Sloan from their contractual obligations, which the band found to be an "artist friendly and cool" decision, especially as Geffen was financially secure and "didn't really need us." This allowed Sloan to pursue other labels and eventually regain their music rights. "But in the end, Geffen was just cool. Like, first of all, they don't need, they made tons of money so they didn't really need us. But they were really artist friendly and cool. And they said, just go, we're not going to hold you to contract."

# label - Murder Records

## curiosities
**Band-Founded Independent Label (Early 1992)** : Sloan founded Murder Records in early 1992 as a means to release their debut EP, "The Peppermint-eaky," and bypass the slow pace of major labels like Geffen/DGC, which projected a much later release date for their first album. The label quickly became a vehicle for releasing their own music and supporting other Halifax bands like Eric's Trip and Hardship Post, fostering a communal scene. "So that's why we started murder records was just to put out that EP."
**Canadian Catalog Home** : After their major label experiences, Sloan consolidated their entire Canadian catalog, including "Smeared" and "Twice Removed" (whose rights they regained), under Murder Records. The label served as their primary Canadian outlet after their initial two Geffen records and became the label for "One Chord to Another" in Canada. "In Canada, we're on murder records the whole time in Canada after the two Geffen records. One court to another, sorry, is on murder records in Canada. And then we get the rights back in Canada to smeared in twice from him. So we have the whole catalog on murder records."

# label - The Enclave

## curiosities
**Short-Lived Boutique Label (1996)** : The Enclave was a short-lived boutique record label founded by Tom Zutat, the mentor of Todd Sullivan (who signed Sloan to Geffen). Sloan signed with The Enclave in 1996 after Geffen released them from their contract, with Zutat having a mandate to make them a big hit. However, the label went out of business within approximately six months, leaving Sloan without a US label by 1997. "And we signed to this other label called the OnClave, which was short lived, which just set up to be really good at head, like balance of bastion, like the water boys."

**Don Letts' Band Lifespan Theory** : Don Letts, who chronicled The Clash, theorizes that the average useful lifespan of a band is about seven or eight years. This cycle involves an initial rise fueled by passion, a peak around year four or five, and then a subsequent decline due to internal conflicts, artistic stagnation, financial woes, and personal problems, leading to an inevitable breakup. "He says that the average useful lifespan of a band is about seven or eight years."
