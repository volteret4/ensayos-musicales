## songs
**Numb (1993)** : A single from the 1993 album *Zooropa*, "Numb" is notable for featuring The Edge on lead vocals, employing a deadpan rap style, rather than Bono. Bono provided falsetto background vocals. Drummer Larry Mullen Jr. contributed a melodic hook to the track and can also be heard vocally, possibly for the only time in a U2 song. The song's origins trace back to "Down All The Days," a rejected track from the *Achtung Baby* sessions. The Edge, who was going through a divorce at the time, developed the song's industrial feel, incorporating various sound effects including a video arcade, a rewinding Walkman, and a Hitler Youth kid playing a bass drum from the 1935 Nazi propaganda film *Triumph of the Will*. "Numb a single from you to his 1993 album Zuropa with the edge on vocals Bono does back up and so does Larry Moan"

## curiosities
**The Edge's Divorce and Creative Process for "Numb"** : While working on *Zooropa*, The Edge was enduring a "pretty painful divorce," leading him to spend considerable time alone in the studio experimenting. This solitary work led to his fixation on a rejected *Achtung Baby* track, "Down All The Days," which became the foundation for "Numb." "At the time the edge was going through a pretty painful divorce and spent a lot of time alone in the studio tinkering with stuff"
**Bono's Rejected Poem for "Numb"** : The Edge initially considered having Bono read a poem he had written, titled "In Cold Blood," as the vocal for "Numb." This poem addressed the desensitizing effect of excessive violence in the news. However, the idea "didn't work," leading The Edge to perform the deadpan rap vocals himself. "His first thought was to have Bono read a poem that he had written called in cold blood which dealt with the Decentitizing effect of too much violence in the news. I didn't work."

**Thom Yorke's Writer's Block and Computer Vocals** : During the creation of *OK Computer*, Thom Yorke experienced a severe case of writer's block. To overcome this, he turned to his Mac (likely a PowerBook, given it was 1997) and used a program called SimpleText. He wrote various slogans and then employed the computer's speech synthesis function to vocalize them, resulting in the unique, unsettling, and powerful delivery heard on "Fitter Happier." "Tom had run into a bad case of writer's block It was looking for some way to break through so he turned to his Mac This is 1997 so it must have been some kind of power book and a program called simple text Tom just wrote a bunch of slogans into the program and let the computer speak"
