# genre - Low Fidelity (Lo-Fi)

## curiosities
**Origins in Post-CD Backlash (1980s)** : The concept of low fidelity emerged in the 1980s as a reaction and artistic regression against the perceived perfection, shininess, and unreality of music produced with the introduction of the compact disc. Proponents of lo-fi felt that too-perfect recordings lacked the human imperfections and mistakes that made music authentic and from the heart. "For some, music was now too perfect, too shiny, too unreal. They felt it contained none of the imperfections that made it human."
**Definition and Early Perception (1977-Mid-1980s)** : The term "Low Fy" was coined in 1977 by Canadian music educator R. Murray Schafer in his book "The Tuning of the World," where he defined it as an "unfavorable signal to noise ratio," describing recordings that were noisier than desired in an era valuing high fidelity. Initially, low-fidelity music was often dismissed as simply indicating an inability to afford better recording equipment. However, by the mid-1980s, an appreciation grew among critics and fans for the warmth, realness, and unpolished authenticity of these recordings. "It was music of low quality, at least in terms of sonics. Meanwhile, punk had happened."
**Evolution from Technique to Genre (Late 1980s)** : Initially viewed as an insult, the term "low-fi" began circulating around 1986 and 1987 as an appreciation for this style of recording and performance started to grow. By the end of the 1980s, lo-fi had evolved significantly, transforming from a mere recording technique into a full-fledged, standalone genre of rock music, moving from categorization to a term of praise. "Around 1986 and 1987, that turn began to make the rounds. And by the end of the 1980s, low-fi had evolved from an insult to a categorization, to a genre, to praise."
**Appeal and Scene Growth (Late 1980s)** : The late 1980s marked a significant period for lo-fi rock, as it blossomed into its own distinct scene. Its appeal was driven by several factors: the increasing availability of good quality home recording equipment, a broader reaction against the overly slick and shiny production of mainstream rock, and a specific pushback against the polished aesthetic of hair metal. "The late 1980s were a great time for low-fi rock. It had become its own scene. The availability of good quality home recording equipment, the reaction against shiny, over-produced mainstream rock and the pushback against hair metal, which was very, very slick and shiny, made this approach very appealing to some people."
**Crystallization and Subgenre Expansion (1995-Present)** : By 1995, the low-fidelity approach had fully crystallized into a legitimate and exalted method within rock music, offering a distinct path for artists. This established mindset led to the proliferation of numerous offshoot subgenres, including hip-negogic pop, chillwave, and glow-fi. The lo-fi aesthetic also penetrated hip-hop with the down-tempo style known as chill hop, and found its own niche within electronic dance music, demonstrating its broad and lasting influence across diverse musical forms. "Low five became such an established mindset and process that it began to spawn offshoot subgenres, such as hip-negogic pop and chill wave and glow five."
**Modern Digital Emulation (Present)** : Today, the appeal of lo-fi continues, with performers using digital technology to deliberately emulate the fuzzy, buzzy analog sounds that characterized early low-fidelity recordings. Artists now intentionally add hiss and background noise to their tracks to achieve the "old school effect" that, in the past, often occurred naturally but was frequently unwanted. "Today, people use digital technology to try to emulate the fuzzy, buzzy analog sounds of the original low-fire practitioners."
**"Adult Contemporary of the Alternative World" (Present)** : Currently, low-fi is sometimes referred to as "the adult contemporary music of the alternative world," a description that acknowledges its unique position and appeal. While some lo-fi can be high-energy and confrontational, much of it is characterized by a laid-back quality, making it ideal for relaxing or "slacking off." "Some people call it the adult contemporary music of the alternative world, and they're not wrong."

# instrument - Portastudio

## curiosities
**Introduction of Tascam 144 (1979)** : In 1979, Tascam introduced the T-AC-144 Portastudio, a revolutionary device approximately the size of a couple of modern-day laptops. This unit allowed musicians to make multi-track recordings anywhere using compact cassettes, eliminating the need for bulky reel-to-reel machines and their associated tapes. "In 1979, a company called Taskham introduced the T-AC-144 Porto Studio. It was the size of, I guess, a couple of modern-day laptops."
**Tascam 244 Upgrade and Capabilities (1982)** : An upgraded model, the T-AC-244, was released in 1982, transforming a standard cassette (with its 1/8-inch wide tape running at 1 7/8 inches per second) into a proper four-track recorder. This allowed musicians to layer instruments, for example, recording vocals on track 1, guitars on track 2, bass on track 3, and drums on track 4. The Portastudio also offered advanced features previously exclusive to professional studios. "In 1982, there was an upgrade, the T-AC-244. The Porto Studio turned a cassette with its eighth of an inch wide tape, running at 1 and 7 eighths of an inch per second, into a proper four-track recorder."
**Bouncing Tracks for More Layers (1982)** : The Portastudio, particularly the T-AC-244 model, allowed for "bouncing" tracks to overcome its four-track limitation. Musicians could mix completed parts, bounce them down to another tape, and then record that mixed track back onto one of the original tape's tracks (e.g., track 1), thereby freeing up the remaining three tracks for additional overdubs, instrumentation, or vocals. "If you needed more than four tracks, it was possible to mix completed parts and bounce them down to another tape. That mixed track could be recorded back on to, say, track 1 of the original tape, leaving three more open for things like overdubs and additional instrumentation or vocals."
**Audio Quality and Portability (1982)** : The Portastudio units offered "quite good" audio quality, especially considering they brought professional studio features to home recording. However, audio quality would deteriorate when creating multiple generations of a track. Their significant advantage was portability, enabling musicians to make recordings virtually anywhere. "The only time things got kinda dicey was when you made multiple generations of a track. Whatever you do that, the audio quality begins to deteriorate. But because they were so portable, musicians were able to make recordings virtually anywhere."
**Generic Term and Industry Impact (1982 Onwards)** : The term "Portastudio" became a generic descriptor for similar multi-track cassette units produced by various companies beyond Tascam, including Akai, Yamaha, Fostex, Sansui, and Marantz. In 1982, the Portastudio was considered a "revelation," offering capabilities now common with digital audio programs and laptops like Garageband, but groundbreaking at the time for democratizing multi-track recording. "Other companies got on board too. And the word Porto Studio became a generic description of units not only made by Tascam, but Akai, Yamaha, Fostex, Sonsui, Moranson, a bunch of others."

**Early Recorded Music Quality (Pre-Late 1940s)** : For the first 60 years of the recorded music industry, audio quality was "awful" or "terrible" compared to live music, primarily due to technological limitations. Early 78 RPM records played on gramophones simply couldn't capture audio in a way that sounded real. "For the first 60 years of the recorded music industry, things sounded awful. Well, relatively speaking."
**Advancements in Recording Technology (Late 1940s-Early 1950s)** : Significant technological shifts in the late 1940s and early 1950s led to substantial improvements in recorded audio quality. The introduction of vinyl records (33 1/3 RPM albums and 7-inch 45 RPM singles) in the late 1940s, followed by the adoption of magnetic recording tape in the early 1950s, marked key turning points. These innovations, combined with new microphones, better tape machines, and a deeper understanding of acoustics in studio design, progressively made recorded sound more realistic. "We just didn't have the technology to capture audio so that when we listen back, it sounded real. That began to change in the late 1940s with the introduction of vinyl records, the 33 and a third RPM vinyl album, and the 7 inch 45 RPM single."
**Rise of High Fidelity (Mid-Late 1950s)** : In the mid-1950s, the marketing term "high fidelity" emerged, created by the audio industry to describe equipment capable of reproducing music with proper faithfulness. With the advent of stereo recordings in the late 1950s, consumer enthusiasm for high-fi gear soared, leading people to purchase equipment for their homes, cars, and mobile use in an "endless pursuit for perfect sound" that was loud, clean, clear, and accurate. "In the middle 1950s, people started to hear about something called high fidelity. It was a marketing term invented by the audio industry to describe equipment capable of producing music properly with proper fidelity."
**Studio Demands and Retrofitting (1970s)** : During the 1970s, recording studios were in a constant state of retrofitting and refurbishment to meet the escalating demands of artists who sought the best possible sound for their music. This era was characterized by a push for high-quality production and engineering, ensuring recordings met professional standards. "Meanwhile, recording studios were constantly in a state of retrofitting and refurbishment because artists demanded the best for their music."
**Early Rock as Unintentional Lo-Fi (1950s)** : In its nascent stages, all rock music could be considered low-fidelity, not by artistic choice, but due to the inherent limitations of 1950s recording studios. These studios did their best with rudimentary equipment such as brand new tape machines, acetate cutters, and basic audio consoles, always striving for the most realistic sound possible. "At first, like I mentioned in the beginning, all music and all rock music was Lofi. The recording studios of the 1950s did the best they could with their brand new tape machines, their acetate cutters and rudimentary audio consoles."
**Cost of High-Quality Recordings (1950s)** : Achieving high-quality recordings in the 1950s was an expensive endeavor, requiring significant financial investment. Costs included studio rental, hiring a producer and an engineer, purchasing recording tape, and dedicating extensive time to meticulous technical details, such as selecting the right microphones and setting tape deck speeds. "But high quality recordings cost money. You needed to rent a studio, hire a producer, hire an engineer by recording tape and spend time ironing out an infinite number of technical details, ranging from the number and type of microphones you needed to the speed at which you ran your tape deck."

Murray Schafer coined the term "Low Fy" in the glossary of his book, "The Tuning of the World." He defined it as an "unfavorable signal to noise ratio," describing recordings that were noisier with hiss and distortion than they should be, particularly in an era that treasured high fidelity. This definition was descriptive of sonic quality rather than an artistic judgment. This approach, unfancy and reactionary, was seen as primal and stripped back, sometimes a little distorted and wobbly, but was considered "cool" and lent a specific context to the music, appealing to a large audience. "Some independent labels, companies operating outside the major label system and therefore offering an alternative really embraced this loose rough raw back-to-basics quasi-permative, cheap and cheerful approach."

"It was deliberately made to sound sonically inferior to the mainstream rock records of the day. Again, on purpose, what did you call that approach?"
