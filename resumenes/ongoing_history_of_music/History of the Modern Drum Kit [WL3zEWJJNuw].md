## curiosities
**1977 Breakthrough Sound** : When The Police emerged in 1977, Stuart Copeland's drumming immediately distinguished itself from other New Wave drummers of the era. His snare drum was tuned very tightly, producing a sharp, high-pitched crack, and his playing incorporated a distinct reggae and dub feel, often expressed with rim shots. There was also a subtle Middle Eastern infusion blended with jazz, evident in his use of small, high-pitched splash cymbals, making him one of the first modern rock drummers to adopt them. He was also notable for his syncopated beats led from the hi-hat. "When they came along in 1977, he immediately stood out from all the other new wave drummers of the era."
**Left-Handed on Right-Handed Kit** : Stuart Copeland is left-handed but plays a drum kit set up for right-handers, which made his complex rhythms even more distinctive. This playing style has been compared to Ringo Starr, who, despite playing right-handed, frequently led with his left hand for fills and flourishes. As a three-piece band, The Police's structure provided ample space for Stuart Copeland's elaborate drumming. "His rhythms were made all the more complicated because Stuart is left-handed."

## curiosities
**First Drum Superstar** : Gene Krupa is widely considered to be the very first drum superstar, gaining significant attention and respect during the mid-1930s swing era when drummers began to receive more prominence due to the emphasis on rhythm for dancing. "The very first drum superstar was probably Jean Crouppa."
**Orchestra Leader and Stage Presence** : Krupa led his own orchestra, and his drum kit was distinctively set up on a large riser, positioning him high above all the other musicians. He played with immense energy and flamboyance, captivating audiences who found his performance primal and powerful. "He had his own orchestra, and his kit was set up on a big riser high above all the other musicians, and he played with a lot of energy and flamboyance."
**Legendary Drum Battles with Buddy Rich** : One of Gene Krupa's most popular attractions was the "drum battle," where he would challenge another drummer, most frequently his friend Buddy Rich, to a competition to determine who was superior. These battles were held on stage, televised, and recorded, and played a significant role in making drumming a "really really cool thing." "His most frequent competitor was his friend Buddy Rich."
**Drum Kit Standardization** : Alongside Buddy Rich, Gene Krupa made substantial contributions to standardizing the construction and setup of drum kits. By 1948, Krupa's personal drum kit had evolved to a configuration that would be immediately recognizable and playable by any modern drummer. "Also, Crouppa and Rich did a lot to standardize how drum kits were constructed and set up."
**1948 Slingerland Kit Configuration** : Gene Krupa's 1948 drum kit, manufactured by Slingerland (a company that started making drums in 1913 and was his favorite at the time), featured a specific setup. This included a 24-inch bass drum played with a Ludwig-style pedal, a 14-inch snare, a 13-inch tom-tom mounted on a stand, a 16-inch tom-tom with legs for floor placement, 13-inch hi-hats on a hi-hat stand, and two additional cymbals mounted on their own stands. "Crouppa's 1948 kit featured a 24-inch bass drum, played with a Ludwig style pedal, a 14-inch snare, a 13-inch tom tom mounted on a stand, and a 16-inch tom with legs that stood on the floor."

**Biggest Impact on Rock and Roll Drums** : Ringo Starr is credited with having the biggest single impact on drumming in rock and roll history. When The Beatles burst into the mainstream in 1964, Ringo's distinctive persona, described as "fun and slightly odd-looking," drew many young people to the drums, making playing look enjoyable and accessible. "The biggest thing that ever happened to drums in rock and roll was Ringo Star."
**Signature Ludwig Kit** : Ringo Starr played a standard four-piece Ludwig drum kit, notable for its oyster gray finish. This particular setup, consisting of a snare, bass drum, mounted tom, and floor tom, became an influential template for many drummers. "He played a standard four-piece Ludwig kit with an oyster gray finish."
**Undeserved "Average Drummer" Reputation** : Ringo Starr has often been unfairly maligned with a "bad rap" as an average drummer and a below-average musician. However, drummers who have meticulously studied The Beatles' music recognize him as an innovative and tasteful player, refuting these accusations as both unfair and incorrect. "Ringo got this bad rap of not only being an average drummer, but also being a below-average musician."
**Pioneer of the Matched Grip** : Ringo Starr significantly influenced new drummers by popularizing the "matched grip" for holding drum sticks. Traditionally, drummers used the "jazz grip," with one stick held like a club and the other from underneath with the palm facing upward. Ringo favored holding both sticks like clubs, allowing drummers to "attack their instrument" with more energy and a primal feel. His appearances on Ed Sullivan and in movies during the mid-1960s led almost every subsequent rock drummer to adopt the matched grip. "Ringo favorite the matched grip, where both sticks were held like clubs."
**Influential Kit Setup** : The Ludwig drum kit that Ringo Starr played on his Ed Sullivan debut in 1964 became a benchmark. Even today, a vast number of players continue to use a 4 or 5-piece kit that has changed very little from that iconic setup. "But in between that is a vast number of players who use a 4 or 5 piece kit that's barely changed from the Ludwig set that Ringo played on Ed Sullivan in 1964."

Drums fundamentally consist of a membrane (skin) stretched over an open-ended cylinder (shell), which is then struck with a hand or stick. "Technically, drums are known as membrane of phones and are probably humankind's oldest instrument."
**Historical Consolidation from Multiple Players** : In the "modern era" prior to the late 19th century, various drum-related percussion instruments were played by different individuals; for example, one person played the bass drum, another the cymbals, and a separate player handled the snare drum. This multi-person approach is still visible in today's marching bands. The desire to consolidate these roles into a single player drove early innovation in drum kit design. "For the modern era, the various drum-related percussion instruments were played by different people."
**Post-Mid-60s Incremental Evolution** : Since the mid-1960s, the evolution of the modern drum kit has been primarily incremental. Changes have largely focused on cosmetic alterations, the introduction of new materials, and different arrangements of standard components, rather than fundamental design shifts. "Everything that's happened since the mid 60s has been incremental in the way drums are made and played."

# instrument - Snare drum

## curiosities
**Origins and Military Utility** : The snare drum first appeared in the 14th century, initially serving as a time-keeping device for flute players. It features two heads—a top and a bottom—with a series of metal wires running along the bottom head, which produce its characteristic staccato crack when struck. This distinct sound made the snare drum highly effective for military use, and it subsequently became the de facto rhythm-keeping machine. "There's a special drum called the snare, which first appeared sometime in the 14th century as a way to keep time for flute players."
**Standard Dimensions** : Today's snare drums typically measure 14 inches in diameter. Their depth can vary significantly, ranging from 4 inches to more than 8 inches, with deeper snares commonly used in marching bands. "Today's snares are 14 inches in diameter, but can range in depth from 4 inches to more than 8."

# instrument - Hi-hat

## curiosities
**Post-1927 Development and Function** : The hi-hat emerged as a new development after 1927, designed as two cymbals positioned facing each other and sitting one on top of the other. Mounted on a hi-hat stand, a foot pedal allows these cymbals to be moved apart or clanged together. Drummers can also strike hi-hats with sticks, creating various sounds depending on whether they are open, closed, or a combination. "A new development was the hi-hat."
**Barney Walberg, Inventor in 1926** : The invention of the modern hi-hat is attributed to Barney Walberg in 1926, while he was working for a small company specializing in drum accessories. These new time-keeping devices quickly became popular and were widely adopted by jazz players. "The guy who invented the modern hi-hat seems to be a dude named Barney Walberg, this was 1926, when he was working with a small company that made drum accessories."
**Standardization of Diameter** : Hi-hat cymbals were soon standardized to a diameter of 14 inches. While various sizes are available today, 14 inches remains the most common and widely used size. "Hi-hat symbols were soon standardized at 14 inches in diameter."

# instrument - Drum sticks

**Growth of Market with Rock and Roll** : As rock and roll gained popularity, a significant market for drum sticks emerged, driven by the increased demand from new drummers. "A big market grew for drum sticks."

This innovation fundamentally changed how drum sticks were made and how drummers selected them, making stick choice as personal as selecting golf clubs or hockey sticks. "One of the biggest companies was Vic Firth, which was founded in Boston in 1963."

# instrument - Matched grip

## curiosities
**Ringo Starr's Popularization and Impact** : Ringo Starr favored the "matched grip" for holding drum sticks, where both sticks are held like clubs. This technique allowed drummers to "attack their instrument" with greater energy and a more primal feel. Following Ringo's prominent appearances on television (like The Ed Sullivan Show) and in movies during the mid-1960s, almost every subsequent rock drummer adopted the matched grip, making it the dominant stick-holding technique in rock music. "Ringo favorite the matched grip, where both sticks were held like clubs."

# instrument - Electronic drums

## curiosities
**Distinction from Drum Machines** : Electronic drums are distinct from drum machines; they are electronic pads that drummers hit with sticks, designed to be played physically rather than programmed. "These aren't drum machines, they're drums, electronic things, pads that you hit with a stick."
**First Commercial Release: Pollard's "the syndrome" (1976)** : The first commercially marketed electronic drum was released by a company called Pollard in 1976 and was named "the syndrome." Two years later, Simmons released their own models, which were quickly embraced by new wave and synth pop bands for their futuristic "lightsaber" sounds. "The first commercially marketed electronic drum was released by a company called Pollard in 1976."
**Early Limitations and Decline in Style** : Early electronic drums, such as those by Simmons, had limitations; they couldn't store sounds and required constant programming and reprogramming. Although initially fun and futuristic, this distinctive early electronic drum sound quickly fell out of style, much like other 1980s aesthetics such as "big hair, shoulder pads, and white sport coats," becoming a hallmark that instantly dates music from that era. "But they couldn't store sounds and needed to be programmed and reprogrammed."
**Late 1990s Resurgence with Realism Focus** : Despite their earlier decline in popularity, electronic drums did not die out. By the end of the 1990s, companies like Roland and Yamaha began producing electronic kits with a strong emphasis on achieving a feel and sound indistinguishable from acoustic kits. Electronic hi-hats and cymbals also evolved to sound remarkably real. These modern electronic kits became excellent practice sets, especially for musicians living in condos and apartments. "By the end of the 90s companies like Roland and Yamaha were making electronic kits with emphasis on making them feel and sound indistinguishable from acoustic kits."
**Modern Adoption and Versatility** : Today's advanced electronic drums have been embraced by numerous prominent drummers, including Danny Carey, Neil Peart, Will Champion of Coldplay, and Rob Borden of Lincoln Park. These drummers utilize electronic kits to trigger a wide array of sounds, tones, patterns, and samples, demonstrating their versatility and integration into modern performances. "Today's electronic drums have been embraced by players such as Danny Carey, Neil Peart, Will Champion of Coldplay, Rob Borden of Lincoln Park, and many, many others."
