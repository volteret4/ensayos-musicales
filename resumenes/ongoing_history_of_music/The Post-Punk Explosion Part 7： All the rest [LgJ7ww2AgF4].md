# artist - Joy Division

## genres
Indie Rock
Post-Punk
Dark Wave (potential label)
## labels
Factory Records
## curiosities
**Uncategorizable Sound and Style** : Joy Division, an archetypical British indie band from Manchester, developed a unique "strange spooky paranoid sound" in the late 70s. Their music was minimalist with Ian Curtis's distinct vocals, and live performances featured his "weird, strange, jerky" dance moves, influencing fans to adopt a dark 3-quarter length raincoat as an unofficial uniform. They defied easy categorization, being too dour for New Wave and not thrashing enough for punk, highlighting their distinctiveness within the post-punk movement. "By the late 70s, Joy Division had developed a strange spooky paranoid sound."
**Ian Curtis's Suicide and Legacy** : Ian Curtis's death by suicide cut short Joy Division's potential, but it ultimately cemented their legend. His untimely passing contributed to the band's status as one of the most influential British indie bands of the 20th century and beyond. "His death, though, spawned a legend, and Joy Division has become one of the great influential British indie bands of the 20th century."

# genre - Punk Rock
## curiosities
**The 1970s Explosion** : The original punk rock explosion of the 1970s served as a "major reset for rock and roll," described as a "great musical decluttering" where music was stripped back to basics. It embodied a reactionary spirit against the perceived lack of revolution in the 70s, emphasizing a DIY attitude that led to what would become alternative music. "The original punk rock explosion of the 1970s was two things. First, it was a major reset for rock and roll."
**Evolution into Hardcore** : Far from dying out, punk rock evolved into Hardcore, primarily an American invention from the West Coast. This new form intensified the original punk formula, making it faster, more aggressive, harsher, angrier, and more violent, while also maintaining a strong anti-establishment stance. "And the most prevalent form was Hardcore, an American invention, mostly from the West Coast, that took the original punk rock formula, made it faster, more aggressive, more harsh, more angry, and more violent."

# genre - Alternative Music
## curiosities
**"Big Bang" Origin** : Alternative music emerged as a "big bang" from the two core principles of 1970s punk rock: a musical reset and a DIY attitude. This led to chain reactions of new ideas, sounds, attitudes, fashion, belief systems, and general approaches, breaking rules and exploring new concepts. "Taken together, these two principles resulted in what can be described as the big bang for what would later be called alternative music."
**Rise of the Term** : The term "alternative music" arose as a categorization for artists who sounded different and recorded for smaller independent labels, providing an "alternative to whatever the mainstream was offering at the time." This followed the evolution from "college rock" to "indie groups." "hence the rise of the term alternative music."
**British "Indie" vs. American "Alternative"** : In Britain, the term "alternative" did not gain widespread traction; instead, "indie" served as the preferred word for this type of music, reflecting a different linguistic and cultural adoption of similar musical phenomena. "In Britain, the A word alternative never really cut out. Instead the word indie was their word for alternative."

# genre - College Rock
## curiosities
**Late 70s/Early 80s Phenomenon** : College Rock was a distinct music category in the late 1970s and early 1980s, primarily played on campus radio stations across the US and Canada. It featured unsigned acts or those on independent labels, characterized by an "underground vibe," experimentalism, and a departure from mainstream radio sounds. "This was the material played on campus radio stations throughout the US and Canada in the late 70s and early 80s."
**Evolution to Indie/Alternative** : As the 1980s progressed, the term "college rock" became outdated. The artists previously labeled as such began to be referred to as "indie groups," and their distinct sound and independent label affiliations led to their categorization as an "alternative" to mainstream offerings, thus giving rise to the term "alternative music." "Eventually, the term college rock didn't really cut it anymore. People began referring to these artists as indie groups."

# genre - Dreampop
## curiosities
**Characteristics and Influence** : Dreampop emerged by blending ideas from psychedelic music with intricate textures and atmospheric sounds, often achieved through electronics or studio techniques. The genre aimed for "total immersion in music," with acts like the Cocteau Twins pioneering a "dreamy, slightly stone sort of sound" and unique vocal approaches. "These groups copped ideas from psychedelic music and blended them with interesting textures and atmosphere, often using electronics or studio tricks."

# genre - Shoegaze
## curiosities
**Origin of the Term** : Shoegaze was a genre characterized by its very loud, droning, distorted guitars, extensive use of effects pedals, languid rhythms, and barely audible vocals. The name "shoegaze" originated from British music journalists, particularly from *Sounds* magazine after a review of a Moose show, who observed bands staring at their shoes (and pedals) instead of interacting with the audience. "This sound became known as shoegas, because the British music press noted that these bands didn't really connect with the audience much well performing."
**Musical Style** : The sound of shoegaze built upon Dreampop, taking its focus on layered sound to an extreme. It featured dreamy, loud, droning, distorted guitars, extensive use of effects pedals, languid rhythms, and vocals mixed very low, almost "barely there." "It was sort of dreamy, but also very, very, very, very loud, droning, distorted guitars, running through all kinds of effects pedals with languid rhythms and almost barely their vocals low down in the mix."

# genre - Disco punk
## curiosities
**Hybrid Post-Punk Genre** : Disco punk was one of several names for a danceable post-punk genre that emerged around 1979-1980. It fused elements of New Wave, Kraut Rock, artsy punk from CBGB, funk, rap, Jamaican dub, and disco, creating grooves that encouraged movement. "This post-punk genre was known by a number of names. Disco punk, techno punk, dance punk, and punk punk."

# genre - Techno punk

"This post-punk genre was known by a number of names. Disco punk, techno punk, dance punk, and punk punk."

# genre - Dance punk
## curiosities
**Hybrid Post-Punk Genre** : Dance punk was a term for a post-punk genre, interchangeable with others like disco punk and punk funk around 1979-1980. It integrated elements from New Wave, Kraut Rock, funk, rap, Jamaican dub, and disco, often employing disco's "four on the floor" rhythm with slithering bass lines and punk/New Wave instrumentation. "This post-punk genre was known by a number of names. Disco punk, techno punk, dance punk, and punk punk."

# genre - No Wave
## curiosities
**Avant-Garde New York Movement** : No Wave was a short-lived, highly avant-garde post-punk style that appeared in New York City in the late 1970s and early 1980s. Its practitioners explicitly rebelled against all musical conventions, prioritizing noise, confrontation, and dissonance to create "groundbreaking art" and avoid rock clichés. "No Wave practitioners rebelled against all musical conventions. The worst thing, they believed, was to recycle the same old rock clichés over and over and over again."

# genre - Hardcore
## curiosities
**American Invention and Characteristics** : Hardcore punk was an American invention, predominantly from the West Coast, that intensified the original punk rock formula. It became faster, more aggressive, harsher, angrier, and more violent, characterized by a high "testosterone" level. It vehemently opposed the status quo, mainstream rock, and corporate culture, positioning itself as intensely anti-rock star. "Hardcore is against anything that the status quo had to offer. It had to be as far from mainstream rock as possible."
**Reagan Era Rebellion and Community** : American Hardcore flourished during the Reagan years, serving as a broad rebellion against everything, including the perceived rebellion of the original punk rockers. Despite its rebellious nature, it fostered a strong community through fanzines, dedicated promoters, and collaborative projects, such as the formation of independent record labels specifically for hardcore acts. "American Hardcore rose during the Reagan years, and was all about rebelling against, well, everything, including the rebellion of the original punk rockers."

**Post-Punk Explosion of the Late 70s and Early 80s** : This era followed the original punk rock explosion, serving as a "great musical decluttering" and attitude shift towards DIY. It resulted in a "big bang" for alternative music, unleashing chain reactions of new ideas, sounds, attitudes, fashion, and belief systems. This period laid "so many of the basic foundations of the music that we hear today," giving rise to genres like New Wave, Techno Pop, Industrial, Goth, and Ska. "We now look back on this as the great post punk explosion of the late 70s and early 80s, an era that created so many of the basic foundations of the music that we hear today."
**Definition of Indie Music** : Indie music refers to artists who undertake all aspects of their work independently, without assistance from major established record labels. This often includes self-producing albums or even launching and running their own record labels to release their music. "indie, as far as we're concerned, means artists who have done everything on their own with no help, that is independent from one of the big established record labels."
**College Rock Network and Charts** : As the 1980s progressed, a network developed among campus radio stations in the US and Canada that played college rock. Airplay was tracked, leading to the creation of charts that provided valuable data. This data was then utilized by bands and their labels for strategic decisions, such as planning tours. "Eventually, a network developed between these campus radio stations. Airplay was tracked, which led to charts, and these charts provided data used by the bands and their labels to determine things like tours."
**Hip-Hop Culture & Disco's Influence on Dance Music** : By the 1980s, hip-hop culture was gaining significant establishment. While disco had previously made dancing "uncool" for a certain demographic, this perception eventually shifted, leading people to embrace dancing again. This context facilitated the emergence of new danceable sounds in post-punk, incorporating elements from various genres. "As the new decade began, which is to say the 1980s, hip-hop culture was becoming more and more established, and while disco had made dancing very uncool for a certain segment of the population, it was only a matter of time before that changed and people started moving again to the music."
