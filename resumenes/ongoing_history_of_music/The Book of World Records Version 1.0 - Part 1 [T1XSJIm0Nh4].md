# artist - Chris Butler

## member of
The Waitresses

## albums
**The Devil Glitch (1996) – Longest Recorded Rock Song** : Released in 1996 as a solo project, this album featured only its title track, which stretched to 68 minutes and 53 seconds. The song contained 500 non-repeating verses and no instrumental breaks. Chris Butler spent three months writing it, then recorded his part (singing and acoustic guitar) in one continuous take. He subsequently divided the song into two to five-minute segments, which were then given to musician friends to add their instrumentation and backup vocals, before Butler reassembled everything into the final single track. It was nominated for a Grammy Award in 1998 and certified by the Guinness Book of World Records as the world's longest recorded rock song. "It stretched out to 68 minutes and 53 seconds, 500 verses, none of which repeated, and there were no instrumental breaks."

**Maximum Vinyl Song Length** : In the era of vinyl albums, the maximum length of a song was constrained by the physical capacity of an album's side, typically allowing for tracks of approximately 22 to 25 minutes. "Back in the day, the maximum length of a song was determined by the medium on which it was stored. In the case of a vinyl album, a song could be no longer than the maximum length of an album's side."
**Maximum Cassette Song Length** : A C120 cassette offered the technical possibility of songs up to 60 minutes per side. However, the tape used for C120 cassettes was very thin and prone to stretching, which could cause distortion, leading to this capacity rarely being utilized for extended single tracks. "The cassette offered possibilities of even longer songs. A C120 cassette offered a maximum of 60 minutes per side."
**Maximum CD Song Length** : When the Compact Disc (CD) was introduced in 1982, its maximum running time was established to be between 74 and 80 minutes. While a popular legend attributes this capacity to the chairman of Sony wishing to fit his favorite Beethoven symphony entirely, this story is not true. "But when the CD was introduced in 1982, that was a little bit different. According to legend, the chairman of Sony, one of the developers of the compact disc, decreed that the CD must be able to hold his favorite Beethoven symphony in its entirety. 74 minutes of uninterrupted Ludwig Van. The story isn't true, but it is true that the maximum running time of a CD is between 74 and 80 minutes."
**Shortest Seven-Inch Single** : A split seven-inch single featuring Napalm Death's "You Suffer" on one side and Electro Hippies' "Mega Armageddon Death Part III" on the other, holds the record for the shortest seven-inch single in the history of recorded music, with a combined running time of just under three seconds. "Together, this split seven inch single from Electro Hippies and Napom Death runs just shy of three seconds. This is the shortest seven inch single in the history of recorded music."
