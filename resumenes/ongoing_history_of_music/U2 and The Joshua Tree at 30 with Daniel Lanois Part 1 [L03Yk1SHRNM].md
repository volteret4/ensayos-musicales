## albums
**War (1983)** : This album, released in 1983, marked a significant advancement for the band, representing a "leap ahead" in their musical evolution after their initial two albums. "there was a leap ahead with war in 1983"
**The Unforgettable Fire (1984)** : Released in 1984, this album represented another significant leap forward for U2, making their sound more sophisticated, stronger, bigger, and better. Produced by Brian Eno and Daniel Landwom, it introduced new textures and sonics that were markedly different from previous U2 work, including songs like "Bad" and "The Name of Love." Initial recording took place at Slane Castle. "That represented another leap forward. Things seemed more sophisticated, stronger, bigger, better."
**The Joshua Tree (1987)** : Released on March 9, 1987, this album is U2's fifth and has sold over 25 million copies, establishing it as one of the best-selling albums of all time. It reached number one in two dozen countries, and five of its eleven songs were released as singles, with several selling over a million copies. The supporting tour expanded from arenas to stadiums, leading to the live record "Live from Paris" and the documentary film "Rattle and Hum." The album earned U2 two Grammys: Album of the Year and Group of the Year, solidifying their status as one of the biggest bands globally. In 2014, it was added to the US Library of Congress for its cultural, historical, and aesthetic significance. "The Joshua Tree set up the band as one of the biggest in the world, and over the coming decade, they would become the biggest band in the world."
**Live from Paris (Year not specified)** : A live record that was a direct result of the extensive tour supporting U2's "The Joshua Tree" album. "It resulted in a live record called Live from Paris"
**Rattle and Hum (Year not specified)** : A documentary film that was produced following the tour in support of U2's "The Joshua Tree" album. "and a documentary film called Radle and Hum"

## songs
**Bad (Year not specified)** : A song from "The Unforgettable Fire" album, it was noted for its new textures and spiritual, gospel-like overtones, distinguishing it sonically from U2's earlier work. "You get to the unforgettable fire and there are new textures. There are songs like Bad..."
**Forty (Year not specified)** : This song is mentioned as one of U2's tracks featuring spiritual and gospel overtones in its musical composition. "We think about songs like Bad and Forty"
**Where the Streets Have No Name (1987)** : The opening track of "The Joshua Tree," known for its cinematic quality, beginning with a synthesizer pulse and evolving into an expansive sound described as having "a bit of sunrise." Originally titled "White City," Bono envisioned it as a utopian place free from judgment, racism, and poverty. The Edge initially struggled with a complex time signature change (from 6/8 to 4/4) in the intro, which Daniel Landwom helped resolve using a blackboard. Brian Eno, due to impatience, instructed a tape op to erase the track, but the op refused. "Well it has a bit of sunrise. And it does not, you know, as if the day is opening and it suggests that there's a doorway to a certain kind of dimension."
**I Still Haven't Found What I'm Looking For (1987)** : This song features strong American gospel influences, which Bono consciously embraced, aiming to find a new twist on the genre. The background vocals were exclusively performed by the band members and Daniel Landwom, adhering to their rule of not using external singers. The song's core structure (spine) developed from a distinctive "Drumbie" part by Larry Mullen, originating from a jam session with a working title like "Under the Weather" or "Under the Weather Girls." "Yes it does have a gospel feeling to it, especially when all the background vocals come in, it was, we had a rule, we all did our own background singing, we didn't employ background singers, so it was always the edge and you know and myself."
**With or Without You (1987)** : The third track from "The Joshua Tree" and a surprising choice for a single, given its downbeat nature compared to U2's more energetic earlier hits. Despite this, it became a favorite among the band's office staff and others during its creation. The track features The Edge playing the "infinite sustain guitar," an instrument invented by Michael Brook in Toronto and delivered to Dublin, which The Edge recorded in just two takes. "It just started becoming everybody's favorite song and some of the folks from the U2 office and people hang around always gravitated to it and they said that's something very special about that."
**Bullet the Blue Sky (1987)** : Track four on "The Joshua Tree," recognized for its angry lyrical content, which is believed to address America's bombing of El Salvador during that period. The drums were initially recorded in a dry, small room, then processed for a harder-hitting sound by piping them into the band's PA system located in an empty warehouse next to Windmill Lane, and re-mic'd to create a "chamber of density" effect for added punch. "Well it's not hard to figure out the lyrical content. I think it has something to do with America bombing El Salvador at the time."
**Running to Stand Still (1987)** : This song closes side one of the vinyl version of "The Joshua Tree." Daniel Landwom contributed as the rhythm guitar player. The track is characterized by significant improvisation and features Larry Mullen playing primarily a floor tom and bass drum, which created a "lovely thundering part." The collaborative playing between the band members and guest musicians, including Landwom and Eno, fostered a special "magic." "Yes I play guitar. I'm the rhythm guitar player. And there's a lot of improvising in that particular track."
**Red Hill Mining Town (1987)** : Track six on "The Joshua Tree," notable for having never been played live by U2 for a long time. The difficulty in performing it live stemmed from its plodding, half-time groove, which made it challenging to sustain audience attention. Adam Clayton later confirmed that the band redesigned the groove to make it more suitable for live performance, and the brass sections might be performed using synthesizers in a live setting. "I think the groove works on record but it's a hard one to pull off live because it plods along."

## curiosities
**Band Formation Origin**: U2 formed in a Dublin kitchen, over a decade prior to the March 9, 1987 release of "The Joshua Tree." "a little more than 10 years after a bunch of kids met up in a Dublin kitchen"
**Early Albums' Musical Style**: The band's first three albums are retrospectively described as "fairly straight ahead rock-oriented," representing their foundational sound before their later experiments with new textures. "Because there was the first three albums, which are fairly looking back at retrospect, fairly straight ahead rock-oriented."
**Collaboration with Brian Eno and Daniel Landwom**: The production team of Brian Eno and Daniel Landwom was instrumental in developing U2's sound, starting with "The Unforgettable Fire," bringing new and more sophisticated elements to their music. "And much of the credit has to go to the new production team of Brian, Eno, and Daniel Lennon, guys who found new ways to bring new things out of the band."
**Daniel Landwom's Continued Involvement**: Daniel Landwom was invited to co-produce "The Joshua Tree" after "The Unforgettable Fire" because he expressed to The Edge that there was "something left to say" sonically and that their experiments had only just begun. "And on the strength of that tiny comment, there was an invitation to carry on into the next record, which became the Joshua Tree."
**Recording Environment Preference**: For "The Joshua Tree," U2 sought to move beyond conventional studio environments, preferring more expansive spaces for recording. This desire aligned with Daniel Landwom's experimental approach to utilizing acoustically distinct rooms for recording. "So when the invitation came to work with you too, they also wanted to break the habit of the usual studio and they wanted to record an environment that might be more expansive."
**Competitive Mixing Sessions**: During the mixing of "The Joshua Tree" at Windmill Lane, U2 sometimes used two mixing rooms simultaneously with separate teams. A scorecard system was employed to foster a friendly competition, where a team would "put up a score card which would insinuate that you'd be ahead of the other rooms" upon achieving a good mix. "And whenever you got a mix, you put up a score card which would insinuate that you'd be ahead of the other rooms."
**Pre-Release Anticipation for The Joshua Tree**: In the years 1985-1986, there was a palpable sense of anticipation that U2 was poised to achieve something monumental. The world was perceived as being ready for a "fresh, vibrant, rock and roll with a new spirit" that the album ultimately delivered. "85, 86, there was this anticipation that you too was on the verge of doing something absolutely super extort there."
**The Joshua Tree Planetarium Press Playback**: A unique press listening event for "The Joshua Tree" was held at the Old MacLachlan Planetarium. As the album's opening track, "Where the Streets Have No Name," began with its synthesizer pulse, stars appeared and rotated on the dome, creating a "cinematic" and immersive experience for the attendees. "And we were all ushered in and the lights went down and then the synthesizer pulse from where the streets have no name, slowly came up."
**Verbatim Initial Album Mention (Transcription Anomaly)** : The transcript's opening sentence, referring to the band's fifth album, contains a transcription error using "YouTube" instead of "U2." "YouTube released their fifth album."
**U2's Embrace of America**: A major subtext of "The Joshua Tree" was U2's fascination with the United States, driven in part by the band's exposure to American blues and Motown, which shaped their sonic preferences and respect for American innovation. "Well they embraced America in a huge way and that became the subtext, not a very subtle subtext, but definitely the subtext for the entire record."
**Self-Performed Background Vocals**: U2 adhered to a strict policy of performing all their own background vocals, explicitly choosing not to employ external background singers for their recordings, as demonstrated on "I Still Haven't Found What I'm Looking For." "we had a rule, we all did our own background singing, we didn't employ background singers, so it was always the edge and you know and myself."
**Larry Mullen's Drumming Style Development**: Larry Mullen's unique drumming style originated from his early experience as a marching band symbol player in school. He hit the symbols so forcefully that they inverted, leading him to transition to the snare drum, where he then developed his distinctive approach. "He was part of the marching band in school and he was a symbol player originally. He didn't even play the snare drum."
**Forthcoming Joshua Tree Full Album Performance**: U2 had plans for an upcoming tour where they would perform "The Joshua Tree" album in its entirety, playing all tracks front-to-back. "Now we have the two are coming up where they're going to play the album front to back"
**Unreleased Songs from Joshua Tree Sessions**: During the recording sessions for "The Joshua Tree," more songs were created than those that ultimately made it onto the final album. "Plus, there were all the songs that did not make the record."

# artist - Brian Eno

## curiosities
**Production Partnership with Daniel Landwom**: Collaborated with Daniel Landwom from late 1979 to early 1980s, producing "half a dozen ambient records in Hamilton" and developing a unique "textural sound" before working with U2. "I had been working with Brian late 1979 to, let’s say, late 82, 83. We made a half a dozen ambient records in Hamilton and we had refined this textural sound that Brian was very excited about"
**Impatience in the Studio**: Known for his lack of patience during recording sessions, particularly if a take was prolonged. His impatience led him to instruct a tape op to erase the "Where the Streets Have No Name" track for U2 when the band struggled with its time signature changes. "Eno doesn’t have a lot of patience. So if something takes too long, he probably thinks there’s something wrong with that and we should just move on to something else"

## curiosities
**Production Partnership with Brian Eno**: Collaborated with Brian Eno from late 1979 to early 1980s, producing "half a dozen ambient records in Hamilton" and developing a "textural sound" that they later brought to U2. "I had been working with Brian late 1979 to, let’s say, late 82, 83. We made a half a dozen ambient records in Hamilton and we had refined this textural sound that Brian was very excited about"
**Early Recording Experiments in Hamilton**: Prior to working with U2, he conducted experiments in an old, vacated Hamilton library building (constructed in the 1920s or 30s) which offered unique acoustics with its "endless corridors and labyrinths and beautiful foyers and mezzanines." The city provided him with keys to this building, allowing him to record rock and roll records in spaces that were more reverberant or dense than his own padded studio, seeking expansive recording environments. "I had already experimented in the old Hamilton library because the library had relocated and the building they vacated was a beautiful building. I think built in the 20s or 30s and it had endless corridors and labyrinths and beautiful foyes and mezzanines"
**Respect for the Rhythm Section**: Larry Mullen of U2 specifically wanted to work with Daniel Landwom on "The Joshua Tree" due to Landwom's reputation for having a strong respect for the rhythm section. "Larry Melon once and he said one of the reasons he wanted to work with you on the Joshua Tree was because you had a real respect for the rhythm section."
**"Science Teacher" Role for "Where the Streets Have No Name"**: During the recording of U2's "Where the Streets Have No Name," he used a large blackboard and a pointer, acting as a "science teacher," to visually guide the band members, particularly the rhythm section, through the complex time signature changes (from 6/8 to 4/4) in the song's intro. "I was in, I was the science teacher so I had the pointer and I pointed at the chord changes as they came around."
**Ownership of Infinite Sustain Guitar**: Daniel Landwom possesses one of the three "infinite sustain guitars" invented and built by Michael Brook. "He built one for himself, for Edge and then I have the third one."
**Canadian Heritage**: Identifies as Canadian and humorously claims responsibility for originating the "toque thing." "Listen man, I’m Canadian, I started the toop thing."

## curiosities
**Influence on U2**: The sounds of American blues and Motown from Detroit significantly influenced U2, as they, like many Canadians, grew up hearing these genres. This exposure fostered a high regard for American innovation in music and contributed to the sonic palette U2 explored, particularly evident in albums like "The Joshua Tree." "we all, even here in Canada, we all grew up with American blues ringing in our ears, the sounds of Detroit from Motown and there’s always been a very high regard for American innovation."

# curiosity

**Hamilton Ambient Music Scene**: From late 1979 to early 1980s, Brian Eno and Daniel Landwom collaborated in Hamilton, producing approximately "half a dozen ambient records." During this period, they refined a distinctive "textural sound" characterized by building sounds with different octaves, which later influenced their work with U2. "We made a half a dozen ambient records in Hamilton and we had refined this textural sound that Brian was very excited about and we found through various experiments, we found a way of building sounds with different octaves."
**Old Hamilton Library as Recording Studio**: Daniel Landwom utilized a vacant, historical Hamilton library building, dating back to the 1920s or 30s, as an experimental recording space. The city provided him with the key to this architecturally rich building, which featured "endless corridors and labyrinths and beautiful foyers and mezzanines," offering unique reverberant and dense acoustics for recording, particularly for drums, contrasting with his own conventional, padded studio. "I had already experimented in the old Hamilton library because the library had relocated and the building they vacated was a beautiful building. I think built in the 20s or 30s and it had endless corridors and labyrinths and beautiful foyes and mezzanines"
**Drum Recording Technique ("Chamber of Density")**: A specialized drum recording technique involves capturing drums in a dry, small room, then routing the sound through a band's PA system set up in an adjacent, empty warehouse. The PA is then re-mic'd to achieve a more powerful, "harder hitting" sound, creating a unique "chamber of density" effect without conventional reverb. This method was notably used by U2 for "Bullet the Blue Sky" and later by Rage Against the Machine for "Killing in the Name" in 1992. "For you technical people out there there’s kind of a variation on the reverb chamber but without reverb, a chamber of density."
