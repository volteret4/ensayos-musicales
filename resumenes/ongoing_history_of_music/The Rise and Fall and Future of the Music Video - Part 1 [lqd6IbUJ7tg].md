# curiosity

**Early Music Visualization (Pre-1970s)** : For centuries, people have sought to visualize music through forms like opera, ballet, and musicals. With the advent of motion pictures in the late 1800s, musical scores were specifically composed to complement on-screen action, predating modern music videos. "For centuries, people have been finding new ways of visualizing music."
**Oscar Fischinger - Music Video Pioneer (1921)** : Oscar Fischinger is recognized as the true inventor of the music video, having been fascinated by combining music and visuals as early as 1921. His experimental film "Study Number Five" featured abstract images moving to music, a primitive but "magic" concept in the 1920s and 30s. "But the guy who really invented the music video was a dude named Oscar Fishinger."
**Spoony Melodies (1930s)** : Warner Bros. debuted "Spoony Melodies" in the 1930s, which were short films shown before main features in music theaters. These were an early development in mating music to film, preceding the more sophisticated animated works by Walt Disney. "Warner Bros. debuted something called Spoony Melodies in the 1930s."
**Fantasia (1940) - Animated Music Film** : Walt Disney became particularly adept at combining music and film, producing "amazing animated works" such as "Fantasia" in 1940. This feature-length animated film was a significant advancement in visualizing classical music. "Walt Disney got especially good at this, producing amazing animated works like Fantasia in 1940."
**Panorama Soundi (Late 1940s) - Video Jukebox Precursor** : The Panorama Soundi machine debuted later in the 1940s, manufactured by a Chicago company led by President Franklin Roosevelt's son. For two nickels (10 cents), it projected a three-minute, black-and-white film on a 27-inch screen, with about 2,000 such films made exclusively for the Panorama. These machines, costing about $1,000 ($23,000 today), loaded with a continuous loop of about eight songs per reel, were common ways to see filmed music performances before television. "Later in the decade, the Panorama Soundi made its debut."
**Scopatone (1958) - European Video Jukebox** : The Scopatone was a video jukebox patented by a French manufacturer called Kamikap in 1958, resurrecting the Panorama Soundi idea in the early 1960s. Weighing 1,500 pounds, it could hold three dozen 16-millimeter films, mostly in color and shot exclusively for the machine. Despite a price of $3,500 ($35,000 today), about 800 were installed in the US, primarily in bars, lounges, and truck stops, intentionally avoiding teenage hangouts to protect the mob-controlled jukebox business. "The most successful was a French manufacturer called Kamikap, who padded the technology for a machine called the Scopatone in 1958."
**Scopatone Decline (Mid-1960s)** : The popularity of Scopatones began to decline around 1967, primarily due to poor machine build quality, the refusal of British Invasion stars like The Beatles and The Rolling Stones to participate, and the Sicilian mob's seizure of control over the business. Television programs featuring live musical performances also contributed to its demise, although films for the machines continued to be made until 1978. "Popularity of Scopatones began to taper off in about 1967, but they did hang on."
**Early TV Music Shows (Pre-MTV)** : Programs such as "American Bandstand," "The Ed Sullivan Show," and "The Tonight Show" in the US, along with "Top of the Pops" and "The Old Grey Whistle Test" in the UK, had been regularly featuring live musical performances for many years before the advent of dedicated music video channels. These shows provided an existing platform for music on television. "Programs like American Bandstand, Ed Sullivan, and the Tonight Show had been featuring live musical performances for years."
**Record Labels and Early Promotional Clips** : Record labels embraced the concept of promotional film clips because they allowed more of their acts to appear on television without the added expense and logistical challenges of travel and live performances. This cost-effective promotion enabled artists to reach wider audiences. "Record labels loved all these TV options because it allowed so many more of their acts to perform on television with all the added expense and travel."
**Internal Use of Music Clips (Early 1970s)** : In the early 1970s, many record labels produced music clips primarily for internal use. Directors would shoot films for up to 60 performers, compiling them onto 90-minute reels to be screened at sales conferences globally. This allowed label representatives to see and hear what they would be promoting, and when artists were unavailable, directors would create conceptual pieces that laid the groundwork for modern music videos. "Most of them were produced for internal use only."
**Music Industry Recession (1980)** : In 1980, the recording industry faced significant trouble, with sales down a drastic 10% year over year, marking the first such decline since the 1930s. This brutal recession, characterized by high interest rates, led to decreased discretionary spending and a desperate need for something to revitalize the recorded music market, setting the stage for MTV. "Because in 1980, the recording industry was in big trouble."
**MTV Launch (August 1, 1981)** : MTV officially launched at one minute after midnight on August 1st, 1981, with John Lack reading the introduction. Despite initial skepticism from many cable systems, approximately three million households had signed up, marking a pivotal moment in music and television history. "By August 1st, 1981, it was all ready."
**MTV's Initial Library (1981)** : At its launch, MTV had a library of about 250 videos, with a significant 30 of them from Rod Stewart. The channel relied heavily on its parent company, Warner Bros., which owned numerous record labels, to ensure a steady supply of new content. "MTV launched with a library of about 250 videos, and 30 of them were from Rod Stewart."
**Impact on Record Sales (Early 1980s)** : The effectiveness of music videos in boosting sales quickly became evident, with a direct correlation between a video being shown on MTV and a dramatic increase in record sales. This was crucial for an industry in a deep recession, prompting other labels to quickly incorporate music video budgeting into their marketing strategies. "Everyone was surprised by the effectiveness of music videos. There was a direct connection between showing a video on MTV and a dramatic increase in sales for that record."
**MTV's Influence on Artist Image** : Music videos profoundly changed the perception of rock stars, shifting them from "distant, unknowable, and mysterious" figures to visually accessible performers. This led to an emphasis on looks, glitz, and style, often at the expense of musical substance, fundamentally altering how artists were presented and perceived. "MTV began to quickly affect the course of music because it began to really matter what you looked like."
**British Bands and Early MTV Exposure** : MTV's desperate need for videos led to significant exposure for previously unknown British bands in the United States. Many British acts had already been producing promotional films for years to supply UK shows like "Top of the Pops," giving them a ready supply of content for the new American channel. "British acts had been making promotional films for years to satiate top of the pops and all those other UK shows. And that's why all these unknown British bands suddenly began to get exposure in the United States."
**Artist Reach Through Videos** : Music videos rapidly became an incredibly efficient tool for artists to reach a vast audience. An artist could connect with more people through a single video than they could achieve during an entire year of touring on the road. "An artist could suddenly reach more people with one video than they could with one year on the road."
**"I Want My MTV" Campaign (1980s)** : MTV, in collaboration with labels and artists, launched the "I Want My MTV" campaign to encourage viewers to contact their local cable companies and demand the channel. Featuring prominent artists like The Police, Pat Benatar, Pete Townshend, Cyndi Lauper, Billy Idol, and David Bowie, this campaign was "off the charts out of control" in its effectiveness, leading to a massive expansion of MTV's reach across the US. "MTV got together with labels and artists for a campaign called, I want my MTV, which encouraged people to phone their local cable companies and demand that they carry this new channel."
**MTV's Profitability** : MTV made history by becoming the first cable TV channel to achieve profitability. This financial success served as a powerful incentive for the proliferation of other video channels and cemented the music video as a lucrative marketing tool. "MTV was the first cable TV channel to be profitable."
**Proliferation of Music Video Channels (1980s-1990s)** : Following MTV's success, a wave of other music video channels emerged, including MuchMusic (Canada, 1983), VH1 (MTV sister channel, 1985), Black Entertainment Television (BET), The Box, MTV Europe (1987), M2 (1996), MCM (France), Viva (Germany), and ZDTV (France), alongside numerous satellite channels. This expansion solidified the music video's role as the most powerful marketing tool for labels and artists. "No wonder other video channels started popping up."
**Video's Influence on Careers and Trends** : Music videos became a powerful force that could both build and destroy careers. They were instrumental in establishing new trends not only in music but also in fashion and language, fundamentally affecting how music was bought and perceived. "The video's build careers, and if you did wrong, they could destroy careers."
**Music's Visual Interpretation** : Music videos changed how music was perceived, as performers and directors began to dictate the specific images and narratives that songs were "supposed to invoke." This shifted music from solely inhabiting the listener's imagination to a more directed visual experience, leading to the "style over substance" phenomenon. "Performers and directors began to tell us what images the music was supposed to invoke."
**Technological Challenges and Visual Over-the-Top Aesthetic** : Early music video creators faced the challenge of making a maximum visual impact on small, low-fidelity TV screens (e.g., 20-inch TVs with mono sound). They overcame this by employing "over the top" visuals, including bright colors, fast edits, crazy juxtapositions of images, and "dollops of sex," with the aim of selling a carefully curated youth culture. "So how did they surmount those technological challenges? By going over the top when it came to visuals, bright colors, fast edits, crazy juxtaposition of images, and of course, dollops of sex."
**MTV Aesthetic Influence (80s-90s)** : The production aesthetics developed for music videos, characterized by bright colors, fast edits, and dynamic imagery, gradually permeated other areas of media, including film and regular television. Everything began to adopt that distinctive "MTV feel," showcasing the widespread cultural impact of the format. "These production aesthetics slowly seeped into other areas like film and regular television. Everything had to have that MTV feel."
**Escalating Video Costs and ROI (80s-90s)** : Throughout the 1980s and 1990s, music videos became progressively more sophisticated and expensive to produce. However, the return on investment was considered worthwhile, as a hit video consistently translated into increased radio airplay and significantly higher album sales, particularly of high-margin CDs. "Through the 80s and 90s, music videos became more and more sophisticated and more and more expensive."
**Canadian Content Rules and MTV Exclusion** : For its initial years, MTV was illegal to broadcast in Canada due to its non-adherence to Canadian content (CanCon) rules. This forced Canadians to resort to illegal satellite dishes and reprogrammed boxes to access the channel, highlighting a significant regulatory barrier to its cross-border expansion. "Because MTV did not adhere to Canadian content rules, it was illegal for them to broadcast in Canada."
**CRTC License for Canadian Music Channel (1984)** : The CRTC (Canadian Radio-television and Telecommunications Commission) opened bidding for a license to create a Canadian music video channel, believing the market could only sustain one. Three companies applied: Rogers Radio Broadcasting, CMTV (Canadian Music Television), and Chum Limited. Chum Limited was awarded the license on April 2, 1984, due to its prior experience with music programs like "The New Music" and "City Limits." "The CRTC opened bidding for a license."
**MuchMusic Launch (August 31, 1984)** : MuchMusic, Canada's first 24-hour music channel, officially launched on August 31, 1984. Its inaugural programming included an old 1920s film featuring jazz and ragtime star UB Blake's "Snappy's On," followed by the "world video premiere" of Rush's "The Enemy Within" and The Spoons' "Tell No Lies," along with Canadian video premieres for several international acts. "By August 31, 1984, it was ready."
**MuchMusic Early Programming Schedule** : In its early days, MuchMusic did not broadcast live 24/7. Instead, an eight-hour programming segment was repeated twice daily, with specialty shows interspersed on certain days. "It wasn't live 24-7. An eight-hour segment was repeated twice daily, punctuated by specialty shows in certain days."
**Music Video as Recoupable Expense** : Historically, all music video productions were treated as a recoupable expense by record labels. This meant that artists effectively paid for their own music videos, with the costs deducted from their future royalties. However, if a video was sufficiently impactful, the resulting increase in sales and income could easily offset these costs. "All video productions were considered a recoupable expense by the record label. That meant that artists paid for their music videos because they were promotional tools."
**Music Industry Profitability in the 90s** : The 1990s saw the recorded music industry achieve unprecedented levels of profitability, largely driven by the high sales of high-margin CDs. Music videos were a significant contributor to this success, with hot videos directly translating into increased album sales. "Throughout the 90s, the recorded music industry was making more money than anyone thought possible and music videos were a very big part of that."
**Global Export of Western Culture** : Music videos, and the channels that played them, were highly influential in exporting "soft power" from the West, particularly the United States, across the globe. This cultural export spread American culture worldwide and influenced fashion, makeup, sexuality, and even introduced people to diverse music genres like grunge, alternative, hip hop, and rap. "Western culture, especially from America, was exported to the rest of the globe through videos and the channels that played them."
**Compilation CDs and Revenue Generation** : Music video channels collaborated with record labels to release compilation CDs. These compilations sold by the millions, generating substantial revenue for both the channels and the labels, adding another lucrative stream of income to the music industry. "Meanwhile, the channels released compilation CDs and cooperation with record labels. They sold by the millions."
