## albums
**Neon Bible (2007) – Second Album** : This album was a finalist for the 2007 Polaris Music Prize and received a nomination for Best Alternative Album at the Grammys, where it lost to "Icky Thump" by The White Stripes. It did, however, win a Juno in the same Best Alternative Album category. This marked the second consecutive album from the independent Canadian band to achieve significant global recognition. It is estimated to have sold around one million copies worldwide. "Neon Bible may have sold a million copies worldwide."
**The Suburbs (2010) – Third Album** : Released on August 2nd, 2010, this album is a semi-autobiographical and semi-apocalyptic exploration of the gated, planned community north of Houston, Woodlands, Texas, where Win and William Butler grew up. Recording took place in three locations: Win and Regine Butler's house in Montreal, a church they had purchased in Farnham, Quebec, and in New York. The album debuted at number one in Canada, the US, the UK, Ireland, Norway, Portugal, and Belgium, and reached the top 10 in Australia, Denmark, Germany, Mexico, Spain, Sweden, Switzerland, the Netherlands, Finland, and New Zealand. It achieved platinum status in Canada and the UK, and gold in the US. This album made history by winning Album of the Year at the Grammy Awards, Juno Awards, Brit Awards (Best International Album), and the Polaris Music Prize, making it the first album to achieve such a comprehensive sweep of major awards. "The Suburbs probably sold a little more than that."
**Reflektor (2013) – Fourth Album** : Released on October 28th, 2013, this album was profoundly influenced by the band's extensive experiences in Haiti, where they absorbed Rara music, and by their studies of the Brazilian movie "Black Orpheus" and essays by Danish philosopher Soren Kierkegaard. James Murphy of LCD Soundsystem co-produced the album alongside Marcus Draves, known for his work with Coldplay. Recording sessions were held in Louisiana and Jamaica, with the Jamaican sessions notably taking place in an abandoned castle built in 1979. The album's cover artwork features Rodin's famous sculpture of Orpheus and Eurydice, tying into its thematic undercurrents. "Reflector Arcade Fire's fourth album released on October 28th, 2013."
**Funeral (2004)** : This album contains the song "Haiti," which is accompanied by a video that was shot entirely on location in Haiti. Regine Chassagne maintains a blog on the band's website detailing the group's experiences related to this country. "Haiti, from the funeral album, if you're interested in going deeper, Regina blogs about the band's experiences at the group's website."

## curiosities
**Unconventional and Rule-Breaking Career** : Arcade Fire has consistently pursued an unconventional and rule-breaking career, characterized by rarely granting interviews, refusing to license their music, and preferring to collaborate with challenging individuals. Despite this, they have garnered critical acclaim, including Junos, Grammys, Brits, and the Polaris Music Prize, and are capable of selling out arenas. "Just about everything they've done in their career has not only been unconventional but against the rules."
**Philanthropic Efforts in Haiti** : The band maintains a deep and growing relationship with Haiti, stemming from Regine Chassagne's family fleeing dictator Papa Doc Duvalier's forces for Montreal in the early 1960s. They have donated at least one million dollars to Haitian relief, working with the NGO Partners in Health. Following the devastating 2010 earthquake, band members made regular, unassuming trips to Haiti to provide on-the-ground assistance. "A big chunk of the money they've made, a million dollar chunk was donated to a Haitian relief organization."
**Recording Process for "The Suburbs" Album** : To infuse their digital releases with the distinctive sound of vinyl for "The Suburbs," Arcade Fire recorded each song onto old-school 24-track magnetic tape through a 1940s console, which relied on vacuum tubes rather than transistors or computer chips. After mixing, each song was pressed onto a 12-inch lacquer or acetate, and then this physical recording was digitized. This technique, while aiming for an "archive of a physical thing," sometimes led to a loss of low-end frequencies, resulting in a slightly shrill or mid-rangey sound. "Win says the digital is the archive of this physical thing that exists in the world."
**Reflektor Guerrilla Marketing Campaign** : In the summer of 2013, a guerrilla marketing campaign for the "Reflektor" album involved enigmatic symbols appearing on sidewalks and walls in major cities worldwide and disseminating via social media. The symbol, a circle containing a square divided into nine smaller squares with letters and sometimes "999," was inspired by Veeve drawings used in Haitian voodoo. However, the campaign faced controversy as some logos were considered graffiti and caused surface damage, prompting a public apology from the band. "It was a guerrilla marketing campaign for the New Arcade Fire album."
**"The Reflectors" Secret Gig and NBC Special** : On September 9th, 2013, the same day the "Reflektor" 12-inch single was released under the alias "The Reflectors," the band performed a secret show at a Montreal Salsa Club for a $9 entry fee, attracting celebrities like Bono, Ben Stiller, and Michael Cera, and the entire event was filmed. Immediately following Arcade Fire's appearance as the special musical guest on the September 29th season opener of Saturday Night Live, a 30-minute special compiled from this Salsa Club gig was aired on NBC, an unprecedented feat for a musical act. "And immediately after Arcade Fire appeared as a special musical guest on the season opener of Saturday Night Live on September the 29th, a 30-minute special called from that Salsa Club gig was aired on NBC."
**Major Label Distribution for Indie Band** : Although Arcade Fire is signed to the independent label Merge Records, their manager, Scott Roger, negotiated a "Back in Services" deal with Capitol Records (part of the Universal Music Empire) for the "Reflektor" album. This strategic partnership provided the band with major label distribution, promotion, and marketing capabilities, essential for executing ambitious projects like the SNL special and wider reach. "Although the band is signed to merge records, which like I've said is an indie, they still need the clout that a major label has when it comes to marketing and distribution."
**Album Cover House and Car (The Suburbs)** : The house depicted on the cover of "The Suburbs" album is a real place, though its exact location remains a secret. The car featured in the artwork belongs to Tyler, who is one of the band's guitar technicians. "It's a real place, but it's location is secret. I can tell you, however, that the car belongs to Tyler, one of the band's guitar techs."

# genre - Rara music

## curiosities
**Haitian Parade and Procession Music** : Rara music is a vibrant form of Haitian music primarily associated with parades and processions. Its distinctive sound comes from instruments often improvised from everyday objects like pipes, empty coffee cans, and homemade drums, complemented by other percussion instruments such as maracas, and sometimes trumpets and saxophones when available. This genre served as a significant influence on Arcade Fire's "Reflektor" album. "Here's used instruments made out of whatever they can find, pipes, empty coffee cans, homemade drums, along with various other percussion instruments like maracas, if there are trumpets and saxophones available, great."

# curiosity

**Double A-Side Single Concept** : A "double A-side single" is a historical vinyl record format where a band released two songs on a 7-inch single, both of which were considered equally strong and expected to be hits, without one being designated as a secondary or "B-side" track. Arcade Fire utilized this traditional release strategy for their "The Suburbs" single, a practice famously employed by bands like The Beatles and The Rolling Stones. "That's a reference back to the old days of vinyl when a group would release two great songs on a 17-single, both were regarded equally as good and both were expected to be hits."
**Interactive Video for "We Used to Wait"** : Chris Milk developed a fascinating interactive video for Arcade Fire's song "We Used to Wait," employing HTML5, which was a relatively new programming language for web applications at the time. This video integrated Google Maps and Google Street View, and was created with the assistance of coders responsible for the Chrome browser, offering a personalized experience when users entered their street address at thewildernessdowntown.com. "Using HTML5, which at the time was a relatively new programming language for web applications, a guy named Chris Milk designed a fascinating interactive video that incorporated Google Maps and Google Street View and developed with the help of some of the coders responsible for the Chrome browser."
**Haitian Earthquake of January 12, 2010** : On January 12, 2010, Haiti was struck by a severe 7.0 magnitude earthquake, a catastrophic event that led to over 200,000 fatalities and rendered more than 2 million people homeless. Following this disaster, members of Arcade Fire made regular, unpublicized trips to Haiti to assist directly on the ground. "After the 7.0 earthquake of January 12, 2010, which killed over 200,000 and left upwards of 2 million homeless, arcade fires have been making regular trips to Haiti to help out on the ground."
**Kierkegaard's Existential Influence on "Reflektor"** : The Danish philosopher Soren Kierkegaard, known for his essays exploring existential concepts, particularly the act of reflecting on life and one's current state, provided significant intellectual inspiration for the thematic content of Arcade Fire's "Reflektor" album. "And then there was some reading of essays by Danish philosopher Soren Kirchegard, who liked to write about existential things like the notion of reflecting on life and one's present condition."
**"Black Orpheus" Film's Impact on "Reflektor"** : The Brazilian movie "Black Orpheus," an adaptation of an old Greek story, served as a key influence for Arcade Fire's "Reflektor" album. The band delved into both the rhythmic qualities of the film's music and its overarching themes of isolation and death during the album's creation. "The band also spent some time with a Brazilian movie called Black Orpheus, which is based on an old Greek story."
**Rodin's "Orpheus and Eurydice" on Album Cover** : The cover artwork for Arcade Fire's "Reflektor" album prominently features a photograph of Rodin's renowned sculpture of Orpheus and Eurydice. This artistic choice directly ties into the album's thematic underpinnings, particularly its connection to the "Black Orpheus" film and themes of myth and reflection. "When reflector finally came out, the cover artwork featured a picture of Rodin's famous sculpture of Orpheus and Eurides."
**Manager Scott Roger's Strategy** : Scott Roger, Arcade Fire's manager, is acknowledged for enabling the band to operate "way above their pay grade." He achieved this by strategically enlisting the right partners to ensure their music reached a wide audience, notably securing a major label distribution deal and arranging an unprecedented SNL broadcast event for the "Reflektor" album. "Their manager is a dude named Scott Roger, and he freely admits that Arcade Fire has a band that is operating way above their pay grade, and that by enlisting the right partners, they can get their music heard."
