# artist - Alanis Morissette

## albums

Its success contributed to her selling close to 40 million records. The album marked a complete reinvention of her image and sound, moving away from fluffy pop towards an authentic and direct expression of personal views. "Close to 40 million records sold."

## songs
**You Oughta Know (1995)** : This song, the first single from *Jagged Little Pill*, was released in the summer of 1995. It started on alternative radio and quickly became a generational anthem, articulating the feelings many people had about certain relationships. Speculation surrounded the identity of the song's subject, with names like comedian Dave Coulier, Mike Peluso of the New Jersey Devils, Matt LeBlanc from *Friends*, or Canadian producer Leslie Howe (who produced her first two albums) being suggested. "You want to know, started out on alternative radio and then spread and spread and spread until it became this generational anthem."

## curiosities
**Reinvention and Success** : After previous attempts at being a pop star, Alanis Morissette completely remade herself, moving to Los Angeles, finding new collaborators, and embracing a confident, forthright, and angry persona. This pivot into expressing her personal views honestly and directly led to her breakthrough success and status as a trailblazer. "She had completely remade herself. Instead of another fluffy pop song, Alinas had pivoted into being herself and expressing her personal views honestly and directly."
**Early Career and Producers** : Her first two albums were produced by Leslie Howe, a Canadian producer, before her significant reinvention for *Jagged Little Pill*. "it could have been Matt LaBlanc from France, or it's Leslie Howe, the Canadian producer, who was behind her first two albums."

# artist - Sinéad O'Connor

## curiosities
**Uncompromising Public Persona** : From early 1988, Sinéad O'Connor projected an aura of power and strength, characterized by her shaved head and combat-style dress. She was known for her insistence on speaking her mind, often creating her own controversies rather than just facing them down. "The shaved head, the style of dress, she was all in combat uniform. And just to look at her eye, you immediately understood that she was not someone you would mess with."
**American National Anthem Protest** : She faced media attacks, including criticism from Frank Sinatra, when she refused to allow the American national anthem to be played before one of her shows. "When she refused to let the American national anthem be played before one of her shows, she was attacked in the media, including by Frank Sinatra."
**Grammy Awards Protest** : Sinéad O'Connor withdrew herself from consideration for multiple Grammy Award nominations for her second album. This act was a protest against how the recorded music industry, particularly, treated artists and women. "When her second album was nominated for a bunch of Grammy Awards, she withdrew herself from consideration as a way of protesting how the recorded music industry treats artists, especially women."
**Pope John Paul II Protest** : She was banned from *Saturday Night Live* after tearing up a picture of Pope John Paul II on air. This was a protest against the sexual abuse of children by the Catholic clergy, a move that generated intense backlash but was seen by others as a powerful example of protest and free speech. "She was banned by Saturday Night Live after tearing up a picture of Pope John Paul II as a way of protesting the sexual abuse of children by the Catholic clergy."
**Self-Identified Protest Singer** : Ten years after her *Saturday Night Live* protest, Sinéad O'Connor famously stated her true artistic identity. She asserted that despite public expectations for her to be a pop star, she was primarily a protest singer driven by a need to express her convictions, without a desire for fame. "Ten years later, she had this to say, everyone wants a pop star, see? But I am a protest singer. I just had stuff to get off my chest. I had no desire for fame."
**Ongoing Activism and Personal Life** : Sinéad O'Connor continued to speak out about various issues, including the Catholic Church, organized religion, the AIDS crisis, war, child abuse, women's rights, and the treatment of women in the music industry. Her personal life was complicated, including becoming an ordained priest in a Catholic offshoot, converting to Islam in 2018, and facing mental health crises and difficult personal issues. "In the years since, Shanae continues to say the quiet things out loud about the Catholic Church and various aspects about organized religion. She talks about the AIDS crisis, war, child abuse, women's rights, and how women are treated by the music industry."

# genre - Rock and Roll

## curiosities
**Historical Gender Inequality** : For decades, women in rock and roll faced significant limitations, often confined to roles of looking pretty, singing, or playing simple instruments like the tambourine. Despite some achieving success, they were constrained by the industry's "old boys club" nature. "For years, for decades, women got a completely raw deal in the world of rock and roll. Their job was to look pretty, sing, maybe shake a tambourine."
**1990s Seismic Shift** : The 1990s witnessed a seismic shift in rock and roll, driven by Generation X's demand for music that reflected their concerns. This led to the displacement of legacy artists and hair metal bands by new sounds and attitudes, including increased attention to gender equality. "Then came the 1990s when we saw a seismic shift in music. Generation X, the sons and daughters of the baby boomers, demanded that music reflect their dreams, hopes, wishes, fears, anger, and concerns."
**Challenging the "Women Can't Rock" Myth** : Before the 1990s, it was a widely held belief that women could not "rock," and it simply wasn't done. The increasing number of women in rock throughout the late 70s and 80s, and particularly in the 90s, definitively debunked this misconception. "Remember, before then, it was common knowledge, common sense that women could not rock. It just wasn't done."
**Transformation of Rock** : By the end of the 1990s, rock music had been completely remade, largely due to the contributions of women who found their voice and audience during that decade, creating new avenues and opportunities for future artists. "By the time the 1990s were over, rock had been completely remade."

# genre - Punk Rock

## curiosities
**Foundational Values** : The original punk rock explosion in the 1970s was centered on the idea of tearing things up and starting anew. It championed the belief that anyone should be able to express themselves, regardless of appearance, background, or gender, a novel concept at the time. "Now punk was all about ripping it up and starting again. If you had something to say, you should be able to say it regardless of who you were, what you looked like, what your background was, or your gender."
**Influence on Riot Grrrl** : Female pioneers of punk and post-punk, such as Patti Smith, Siouxsie Sioux, and Kim Gordon, provided direct inspiration for the Riot Grrrl movement in the early 1990s. "Taking inspiration from the female pioneers of punk and post-punk, it would be Patty Smith and Susie Sue and Kim Gordon of Sonic Youth, Chrissy Hine, Oni DeFranco, and so on."

# genre - Hardcore

## curiosities

# curiosity

**Women in Rock Evolution** : Historically, women in rock and roll faced significant limitations, often confined to pretty, singing, or tambourine-shaking roles within an "old boys club." This slowly changed, but the 1990s brought a "seismic shift," with Generation X demanding music reflecting their realities and an increased focus on gender equality, enabling many women to find their voice and audience. "But over the years, that changed. It was painfully slow, but progress was made. Then came the 1990s when we saw a seismic shift in music."
**Generation X's Impact on Music** : Generation X, the children of baby boomers who fought for civil rights and women's rights, demanded that music reflect their own dreams, hopes, fears, anger, and concerns. They reshaped popular music in their image, quickly replacing older artists and genres like hair metal with new sounds and attitudes, emphasizing gender and sexual equality. "Generation X, the sons and daughters of the baby boomers, demanded that music reflect their dreams, hopes, wishes, fears, anger, and concerns. They remade popular music in their own image."
**The Riot Grrrl Phenomenon** : The Riot Grrrl movement of the early 1990s emerged as a direct response to the male-centric nature of hardcore and grunge. These women in the Pacific Northwest created their own space, making music about and for them, addressing their issues, and rocking just as hard as their male counterparts, promoting empowerment and strength. "Being driven sometimes involves identifying a problem and then finding a solution to that problem. And that's one way we can describe the riot girl phenomenon of the early 1990s."
**Riot Grrrl DIY Ethos** : Women involved in the Riot Grrrl movement were entirely self-sufficient, handling every aspect from playing music and promoting gigs to publishing zines and working with indie record labels. This DIY approach was inspired by female pioneers of punk and post-punk. "These women did everything themselves, from playing the music to promoting the gigs, to publishing zines, to working with indie record labels."
**Influence of 90s Female Performers** : The strong female performers of the 1990s played a crucial role in shifting the entire paradigm of rock music, creating new avenues and opportunities for subsequent generations of artists. Their collective impact made the decade special for female singers. "They help shift the entire paradigm of rock music into a completely new realm, creating new avenues and opportunities for the artists of today."
