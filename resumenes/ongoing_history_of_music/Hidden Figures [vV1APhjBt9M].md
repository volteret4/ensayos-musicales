# artist - Manny Smith

## curiosities
**First African-American Woman to Record Blues** : On August 10, 1920, an African-American woman from Cincinnati named Manny Smith traveled to a recording studio in New York, becoming one of the first of a long line of black female singers who went beyond the traditional bounds for female vocalists. Her work, alongside other women, laid foundations for blues, R&B, and ultimately rock music. "Manny Smith was one of the first of a long line of black female singers who went beyond the bounds of what female singers were supposed to do and supposed to sound like."

## songs
**Crazy Blues (1920) - Hidden Meaning and Impact** : Recorded in a New York studio on August 10, 1920, this song by Manny Smith openly addressed the mistreatment of black people. While it appeared to be about domestic violence, substituting "my man" for "the white man or white people" revealed its intended, hidden meaning, making it a powerful social commentary. The song immediately became a sensation, selling 75,000 copies in its first month and over two million copies overall, and was pivotal in establishing the blues as a popular recorded art form, laying foundational elements for future blues, R&B, and rock music. "It sounds like she was singing about domestic violence, but if you substitute my man for the white man or white people, the song takes on its intended hidden meaning."

"In 1942, she became a junior studio engineer at the BPC."

"This led to her experimenting with audio recording gear."
**First All-Electronic Score (1957)** : In 1957, Daphne Oram achieved a historical milestone by creating the soundtrack for a play titled "Amphatryon 38," which stands as the first-ever all-electronic score for any production. This groundbreaking work showcased her innovative approach to sound composition. "In 1957, she created the soundtrack for a play called Amphatryon 38, which was the first ever all electronic score for anything."
**Founding BBC Radiophonic Workshop and Own Studio** : Daphne Oram was instrumental in convincing the BBC to establish an electronic workshop, which became known as the BBC Radiophonic Workshop, where further experimentation in electronic music ensued. After leaving the BBC in 1959, she founded her own studio, dedicating it to the creation of new and experimental sounds. "Once she convinced the BBC to establish an electronic workshop, they call it the BBC Radio Fonic Workshop, more experimentation followed."
**James Bond Electronic Sounds** : Daphne Oram was responsible for creating the distinctive electronic sounds featured in three iconic James Bond movies: "Dr. No," "From Russia with Love," and "Goldfinger." Her unique audio contributions helped define the sonic atmosphere of these early spy thrillers. "For example, the electronic sounds you hear in three James Bond movies, Dr. No from Russia with Love and Goldfinger, were all created by Daphne Oram."
