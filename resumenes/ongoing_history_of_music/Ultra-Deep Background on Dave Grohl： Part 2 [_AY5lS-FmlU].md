## albums
**Pocketwatch (1992)** : A solo album recorded under the alias "Late." Dave Grohl recorded four songs in December 1990, just a few months after joining Nirvana, and the remaining six songs were completed over two days in July 1991, about two months before Nirvana's *Nevermind* was released. It appeared on the "cheapo label" Simple Machines, run out of a house in Arlington, Virginia. The album, which credits "Dave G" on all instruments, didn't get much attention due to Dave's insistence; he didn't want to be seen as "crashing the party" with his own material as Nirvana was taking off. The label, Simple Machines, begged him to release it on CD, but it remained a cassette-only release. "If you look at the liner notes of Pocketwatch, you'll see that all performances on all instruments are credited to one Dave G."

## songs
**Orbitting Persons in Space (1990)** : A demo track from Dave Grohl's solo project under the alias "Harling Talks AD," recorded in the summer of 1990 with the help of Barrett Jones and Toss Newyn Huizen. The project was an experiment Dave didn't expect to go anywhere, featuring "weird song titles" like "Recycle children never to be grown" and "Treason, daddy, brother and crime." The sound of this track was described as more akin to Megadeth than Foo Fighters. "This is called Orbitting Persons in Space."
**Color Pictures of a Mera Gold (1990/1991)** : A song from Dave Grohl's solo album *Pocketwatch*, released under the alias "Late." This track later reappeared as "Mera Gold" on the CD single version of Nirvana's "Heart-Shaped Box," making it the only song in the entire Nirvana canon not written or sung by Kurt Cobain. "That track would later turn up as a track called Mera Gold on the CD single version of Nirvana's Heart Shape Box."
**Friend of a Friend (1991)** : Described as the "best song" on Dave Grohl's solo album *Pocketwatch*, recorded under the alias "Late." Dave wrote this song in Olympia, Washington, while sitting on Kurt Cobain's couch, and it is explicitly about the friendship he observed between Kurt Cobain and Chris Novoselic. A re-recorded version of this song was later featured on the acoustic portion of the Foo Fighters' *In Your Honor* album in 2005. "It's about the friendship he saw Kurt had with Chris."
**Alone and Easy Target (1994)** : A track from Dave Grohl's Pre-Foo Fighters demo sessions in October 1994, where he played all instruments himself. These demos were recorded quickly, with songs typically finished within 40 to 45 minutes each. This particular track, along with 14 others, was distributed on ultra-rare cassettes with rough mixes to friends and record company confidants, and was immediately bootlegged. "The demo version of a loan and easy target recorded it again, about 40 minutes in October 1994."

## curiosities
**Quit School at 17** : Dave Grohl made the decision to quit school at the age of 17 to join the Washington D.C.-based band Scream. This early career move marked his full commitment to music. "Dave quit school at age 17 to join a DC band called Scream."
**Harling Talks AD Alias (1990)** : In the summer of 1990, while still with Scream, Dave Grohl recorded new demos on his own, creating the alias "Harling Talks Angel Divine," or "Harling Talks AD" for short. With assistance from Barrett Jones and Toss Newyn Huizen, he recorded experimental material that he never expected to go anywhere, and there was never a "Harling Talks show." "Dave created a new alias, he called Harling Talks Angel Divine or Harling Talks AD for short."
**Joining Nirvana (1990)** : A few months after recording his Harling Talks AD demos, Dave Grohl received a call to audition for Nirvana. Scream had broken up over "girlfriend troubles" while on tour, leaving Dave stranded in Los Angeles. Buzz Osborne of The Melvins, who was idolized by Kurt Cobain, recommended Dave for the open drummer position. Dave flew to Seattle, arriving on Friday, September 21, 1990, and after an informal audition, became a member of the band without any explicit invitation, simply because "they just didn't ask him to leave." "Dave packed his kit into a large cardboard box and boarded a flight for Seattle, arriving on Friday, September 21, 1990."
**Post-Scream Legal Issue (1990)** : After joining Nirvana and the band signed to DGC, Dave Grohl was sued for $40,000 by someone associated with Scream. This legal action alleged that Dave was still under contract to Scream due to an agreement he signed at some point during his time with them, even though Scream had already broken up. Fortunately, Dave had lawyers who were able to deal with the situation. "Dave was immediately sued by someone associated with Scream for $40,000 saying that he was still under contract to them, even though Scream had long broken up."
**Late Project & Jenny Toomey Crush** : Dave Grohl recorded his solo album *Pocketwatch* under the alias "Late" for Simple Machines, a "cheapo label" run out of a house in Arlington, Virginia. One of the people running the label was Jenny Toomey, and Dave had a "huge crush on here," which "might be the only reason he agreed to let her label put out that cassette." "Dave had a huge crush on here, which might be the only reason he agreed to let her label put out that cassette."
**Burying the Late Project** : Despite Simple Machines begging him to release *Pocketwatch* on CD, Dave Grohl insisted that the "Late" project be largely buried and forgotten. This was because, as Nirvana began to achieve widespread success, he did not want to be perceived as "crashing the party" with his own material, deeming it "rude." This insistence contributed to the album's lack of attention upon its release. "See, when Nirvana started to take off, he didn't want to be seen as crashing the party with his own stuff."
**Self-Doubt in Nirvana** : During his time in Nirvana, Dave Grohl had significant self-doubt about his musical abilities, particularly his singing voice and songwriting skills. He believed his own material was "substandard," leading him to record most of his personal music in secret and only share it with a select few. This self-perception, combined with the understanding that Nirvana was primarily "Kurt's band," limited his contributions to the band's songwriting, despite his innate musical talent. "He didn't like a singing voice, and he didn't think he was any good as a songwriter."
**Deep Depression Post-Nirvana (1994)** : Following Kurt Cobain's death in April 1994, Dave Grohl entered a period of deep depression and was unsure of his future in music. He seriously considered quitting music entirely or joining another band, having received invitations from Danzig and Tom Petty. However, he realized he didn't want to be "just a drummer," as playing drums would constantly remind him of his time in Nirvana, a painful memory at that point. "Give it up music actually was an option for a while."
**Encouragement from Seven Year Bitch Postcard (1994)** : The deciding factor in Dave Grohl's decision to continue making music after Kurt Cobain's suicide was a postcard he received from the Seattle band Seven Year Bitch. The band had endured significant tragedy themselves, losing a member to an overdose in 1992 and another to kidnapping, rape, and murder in 1993, yet they continued to make music. Their postcard read, "We know what you're going through. The desire to play music is gone for now, but it will return. Don't worry." This message convinced Dave that he was "put on this planet to make music" and that stopping would be wrong. "This convinced Dave that he needed to keep going."
**Pre-Foo Fighters Demos (1994)** : In October 1994, Dave Grohl booked six days of studio time in Seattle with Barrett Jones to record demos, playing all instruments himself. His sole purpose for these sessions was to "prove to himself that he could do it, that he could still make music," without any intention of pursuing a record contract. He would record his drum parts, then move to the control room for guitar, and then other instruments, completing each song in approximately 40 to 45 minutes. "His only purpose, his sole purpose was to prove to himself that he could do it, that he could still make music."
**Limited Demo Distribution and Bootlegging (1994)** : Dave Grohl initially pressed up only 100 vinyl copies of his Pre-Foo Fighters demo sessions, distributing them solely among friends, including Nirvana's tour manager Cristobus Selch and record company confidants. There were also "ultra-ware cassettes" featuring rough mixes of 15 songs. These limited-edition recordings were immediately bootlegged, leading to a surge of record label offers for Dave. "The copies of these sessions were pressed up on vinyl, just a hundred copies, and distributed amongst friends."
**Retaining Music Ownership via Roswell Records (1994)** : After his Pre-Foo Fighters demos were bootlegged and he was "bestieged with offers," Dave Grohl decided to protect his interests by setting up his own record company, Roswell Records. This was a direct result of his experience with the royalty issues in Nirvana, and his determination to "retain ownership of his music." He informed potential business partners that they would "have to go through" his label. "Dave was determined to retain ownership of his music."
**"Foo Fighters" Name Origin and Dislike** : Dave Grohl chose the name "Foo Fighters" for his project after a term used by World War II pilots to describe mysterious "balls of fire over Allied lines" (UFOs). The French word "feu" (fire) influenced the term. He preferred a cloaked, anonymous name, wanting people to think it was a band even though there wasn't one at the time. Despite the name's origins, Dave Grohl "thinks the name Foo Fighters is dumb" and feels "stuck with the name" because the project unexpectedly succeeded. "Dave, by the way, Dave thinks the name Foo Fighters is dumb."
**UFO and Science Fiction Fascination** : Dave Grohl has had a lifelong fascination with UFOs and science fiction, dating back to his childhood. He would lie in his front yard waiting for UFOs, had "crazy UFO dreams," and loved science fiction toys and action figures. This interest influenced his choice of the name "Foo Fighters" for his band and "Roswell Records" for his label, both referencing UFO phenomena. "Well, for a long time, since I was a kid, I'd always been really fascinated with UFOs."

## curiosities
**Name Origin (WWII Pilots)** : The name "Foo Fighters" was chosen by Dave Grohl, derived from a term used by World War II pilots. These pilots described "mysterious balls of fire over Allied lines" as "Foo Fighters," a slang term for UFOs. The French word for fire, "feu" (F-E-U), was noted as a potential influence. "He called his project Foo Fighters after a term used by World War II pilots to describe mysterious balls of fire over Allied lines."
**Initial Anonymity** : When Dave Grohl first started the "Foo Fighters" project, he intentionally chose the name to cloak everything "as if it were an unknown band from Seattle." At the time of the initial demos, there was "actually no band," only Dave playing all the instruments, and he wanted people to perceive it as a group. "Dave could have called the project after himself, but he preferred cloaking everything as if it were an unknown band from Seattle, even though there was actually no band at the time."

## concerts
**North Shore Surf Club (October 11, 1990)** : This venue in Olympia, Washington, hosted Dave Grohl's first official gig with Nirvana. The 300-person venue was sold out, a fact that excited Dave so much he immediately called his mom. During the performance, Dave played so hard that he broke his snare drum, and the band played so loudly that they "blew up the power three times." ## albums
**Nevermind (1991)** : This album became "the biggest thing in the world" after its release. In the spring of 1992, following its massive success, Kurt Cobain sought to rewrite the band's royalty arrangements, demanding 90% of songwriting royalties, backdated to the album's September 1991 release. After heated arguments, Kurt was eventually "bargained down to 75%." "In the spring of 1992, after the Never Mind album had become the biggest thing in the world, Kurt Cobain decided he wanted to rewrite the band's royalty arrangements."
**In Utero (1993)** : During the sessions for this album, around February 1992, Dave Grohl recorded a new version of his song "Mera Gold" while Kurt Cobain and Chris Novoselic were out of the studio, singing and playing drums and guitar, with Chris later adding the bass line. However, the song did not make it onto *In Utero* because "Kurt would have never allowed that." "The song did not make in Yudero, Kurt would have never allowed that, but it did show up as the last song in the CD single of Heart Shape Box, in other words, it was buried."
**With the Lights Out (Box Set)** : This box set later included "Mera Gold," a song written and sung by Dave Grohl. Its inclusion provided wider access to a track that had originally been "buried" as the last song on the "Heart-Shaped Box" CD single. "It was later issued as part of the box that called with the lights out."

**Smells Like Teen Spirit** : This song, along with "Lithium," "Come As You Are," "Breed," "Polly," "Stay Away," "On A Plain," and "Something in the Way," are the only two songs (out of the eleven on Nevermind) for which Kurt Cobain shared songwriting credit with the other band members. **Heart-Shaped Box (CD Single)** : The CD single version of "Heart-Shaped Box" included "Mera Gold" as its final track. This was significant as "Mera Gold" is the only song in the entire Nirvana canon not written or sung by Kurt Cobain, having originated from Dave Grohl's solo work and being recorded during the *In Utero* sessions. Its placement on the single meant it was "buried" but still released. "It is the only song in the whole Nirvana canon, not written or sung by Kurt Cobain."
**Mera Gold (1992)** : This song, written and sung by Dave Grohl, originated as "Color Pictures of a Mera Gold" on his solo album *Pocketwatch*. A new version was recorded by Dave during the *In Utero* sessions in February 1992, with Chris Novoselic adding the bass line. Although Kurt Cobain would not allow it on *In Utero*, it was released as the last track on the "Heart-Shaped Box" CD single and later included in the *With the Lights Out* box set. The Foo Fighters have also performed it live, as documented on their *Skin and Bones* album. "It's the only Dave Roll song that ever made it with Nirvana."

## curiosities
**Informal Audition and Membership (1990)** : When Dave Grohl arrived in Seattle on September 21, 1990, to audition for Nirvana, Kurt Cobain and Chris Novoselic picked him up from the airport and drove to Chris's place where Dave was to stay. After hanging out and auditioning, Dave "just kind of became a member of the band" without a formal invitation or declaration. "No one said anything like, congratulations, you're in the group. They just didn't ask him to leave. So he stayed."
**Royalty Dispute Post-Nevermind (1992)** : In the spring of 1992, after *Nevermind* became a global success, Kurt Cobain decided to unilaterally rewrite the band's royalty arrangements. He demanded 90% of the songwriting royalties, backdated to *Nevermind*'s release in September 1991, and called Dave Grohl and Chris Novoselic "greedy" for wanting a larger cut. After "a bunch of fights," Kurt was eventually "bargained down to 75%" of all material on *Nevermind*. "Things got really heated, especially when Kurt called Dave and Chris Greedy for wanting a bigger cut."
**Kurt's Band Dynamic** : Nirvana was widely known as "Kurt's band," with Kurt Cobain serving as the leader, singer, and primary songwriter. This dynamic was acknowledged by Dave Grohl, who understood he had "stumbled into a lottery win" and was hesitant to disrupt the established structure. This power dynamic contributed to Dave's self-doubt and limited his opportunities to contribute his own songs to the band's official releases. "First of all, Nirvana was Kurt's band. He was the leader, singer, songwriter, Dave knew that he stumbled into a lottery win and wasn't about to mess with the situation."

# curiosity

**Early Fame and Documentation** : When individuals achieve a certain level of fame, "everything you do will be recorded somewhere" and becomes "out of your control." This perpetual documentation by fans, management, social media teams, and record labels means a famous person's life "becomes one ever-expanding Wikipedia page." "Fans are going to do it, your management team will do it, your social media people will make sure it gets done, probably somebody at your label, they'll just keep doing it and doing it and doing it."
**Bootlegging of Unreleased Material** : Unreleased or demo material from famous musicians, even if "never supposed to be released," often finds its way to the public through bootlegs. For example, Dave Grohl's 16 demo songs from 1994, despite being discarded, appeared on an "unofficial CD" (a bootleg) released in Japan in 1995. Similarly, his Pre-Foo Fighters demos were "immediately bootlegged" after limited distribution to friends. "But, like I said, nothing that Dave has ever done seems to disappear."
**Collectibility of Rare Cassettes** : Certain rare cassette releases, particularly those from influential artists or early solo projects, can become highly collectible. For instance, a copy of Dave Grohl's solo album *Pocketwatch*, released as "Late" on a cassette-only label, can trade for "$700 or more" in good condition due to its rarity and historical significance. "A copy of an album called Pocketwatch and Good Condition trades for $700 or more."

Dave Grohl's lifelong interest in UFOs, sparked by reading books like "Above Top Secret," directly led to him naming his record label "Roswell Records" and his band "Foo Fighters." "Well, for a long time, since I was a kid, I'd always been really fascinated with UFOs."
