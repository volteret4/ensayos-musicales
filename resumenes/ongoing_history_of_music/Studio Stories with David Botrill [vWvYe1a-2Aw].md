## curiosities
**Early Childhood & Hometown** : Born in Dundas, Ontario, a small town west of Hamilton, Botrell grew up in a suburban environment. Dundas was described as a tougher town during his youth, but later became a "pretty tweed town" with retirees and young families. "I was born in Dundas, Ontario, which is kind of a small town tacked onto the very west end of Hamilton."
**Unfulfilled Dream of Being a Record Producer** : In high school, David Botrell aspired to be a record producer, driven by his love for music. However, he faced rejection from schools due to his lack of a portfolio at 18 and insufficient musical adeptness beyond being a "pretty good drummer" and having "seven years of accordion lessons." "I had the notion that I was going to be a record producer."
**Entry into Recording at Grant Avenue Studios** : Botrell's career began when his girlfriend's uncle, Bob Lannwaw, owner of Grant Avenue Studios, offered him a chance to hang out after he expressed interest in a job. Botrell learned about recording at night, assisting on sessions, cleaning, and making tea for nine months before securing an assistant position in 1983 or 1984. "I walked in the door of that studio and I looked to the left and there was that MCI console and all those lights. And music was going on in the other room and I was like, man, I want something to do with this."
**Formative Influence of Ambient Music** : His early experiences as an assistant in 1983-84 at Grant Avenue Studios were heavily influenced by ambient sessions, which provided an unusual, creative environment without traditional pop structure rules. This experience shaped his initial approach to making music, emphasizing feeling over rigid song forms. "my beginnings in recording and making music stemmed from those early ambient sessions."
**Move to England with Peter Gabriel** : David Botrell followed Bob Lannwaw to England, where Lannwaw was working with Peter Gabriel, initially assisting in setup and technical roles before becoming an indispensable part of Peter Gabriel's touring and studio operations. He stayed in the UK for 20 years, making his way from Bath to London. "when Bob went over to work at Peter Gabriel, he said I might need an assistant over there. Do you want to come over? I said yes, of course."
**Indispensable Role in Peter Gabriel's Organization** : Botrell made himself indispensable by handling various tasks from setting up keyboards, organizing tape storage, to preparing multi-track tapes for musicians during rehearsals for Peter Gabriel's tours. This led to him becoming a crucial part of the touring crew, requiring expedited visas to join the US leg. "I essentially did my best to make myself indispensable and if you do that then people like to have you around."
**Musical Skill Assessment** : David Botrell openly states that while he plays instruments, he does not play them "very well." He considers his primary talent to be helping "great musicians play better or write better," rather than his own instrumental prowess. "My talent is helping great musicians play better or write better."
**UK Studio Work (1986-1997)** : For the first 11 years in the UK, David Botrell worked primarily at Real World Studios, helping to build the facility in a mill building in Box (Barks). After moving to London, he also worked at Eden, Ten House, and Trident Studios. "I did most of my work at, for the first 11 years at Real World Studios."
**Move to New York City** : Around 1997, while living in Bethnal Green, London, Botrell moved to New York City to work on projects with Universal Republic. He lived in the West Village and worked at Soundtrack Studios near New Square, fulfilling a long-held fascination with the city. "I was always fascinated with New York City. And I'd always wanted to live there."
**Return to Toronto** : After living in New York City for about a year and a half, Botrell met his future ex-wife, a Toronto native, which led to him commuting between the two cities before making Toronto his permanent home in 2006. "I met my now ex-wife who is from Toronto. And so I sort of commuted back and forth from New York to Toronto and then ended up just moving back to Toronto."
**Production Philosophy - Collaborative Approach** : Botrell defines a record producer's responsibility as getting the "best out of the artist and musician and the songs," preferring a collaborative approach over autocratic styles like Todd Rundgren or Bob Ezrin. He encourages artists to try all ideas without argument, quickly discerning what works. "I'm a collaborative producer. So I like to work with the artist."
**Pre-Production Importance** : He emphasizes the importance of pre-production, where much collaborative writing and idea-testing takes place in less expensive environments like rehearsal rooms or home studios, before moving to a proper recording studio for final tracking. "I like to get involved in pre-production first."
**Hands-on vs. Delegating Engineering** : While proficient with Pro Tools and mic placement, Botrell prefers to delegate engineering tasks to skilled engineers like Tom Tapley, allowing him to focus on coaching and optimizing musical performances. He finds this approach, also favored by Steve Lilywhite, more efficient for band tracks, though he may handle vocals personally. "I'm often to the point of like you operate that and I'm going to work with the musician and I'm going to coach him."
**Involvement in Songwriting and Arrangement** : Botrell actively contributes to songwriting and arrangement, offering suggestions for chords and transitions to enhance the emotional flow of songs, especially in moving between distinct verse, bridge, and chorus blocks. He focuses on creating "transition moments" for greater impact. "I work really well in transitions."
**Producer's Perspective on Listening to Past Work** : Like many producers, Botrell rarely listens to his past work, citing the tendency to focus on perceived flaws or changes he would make, similar to Steve Lilywhite's reluctance to revisit his U2 albums. "Once I'm done with most of them, it's not that often."
**Genre-Agnostic Production Philosophy** : Botrell asserts that genre is "not that important" to him; he values "integrity in the writing and the conviction of the message" above musical style. His diverse catalog includes heavy music, world music, softer bands, Scottish country, and atmospheric singers like LCL, challenging his North American reputation as "the Tool guy." "I don't really care what the style of the music is. I look for the integrity in the writing and the conviction of the message that they're writing."
**Three Grammys Awarded** : David Botrell has received three Grammy Awards for his work as a producer. "He's got three Grammys."

# artist - Tool

## members
Adam Jones
Maynard (James Keenan)

## albums
**Lateralus (2001)** : Produced by David Botrell, this album saw significant internal conflict during its recording, particularly concerning Maynard's lyrics which were challenging for Adam Jones to hear. The band experienced legal problems between *Anima* and *Lateralus*, firing their manager and issues with the record company, which strained their relationship and nearly led to the band's end during the recording of *Lateralus*. "It was almost the end of the tool at that point."

## songs
**46 & 2** : From the *Lateralus* album, this song contains a specific bass part at the end which, due to the way distortion was hitting it, falls in the "wrong place in a couple spots." This detail consistently bothers producer David Botrell when he revisits the track. "there are moments in one of the two songs, 46 and two at the end of that song where the bass, like it's got this offbeat bass, but because of the way the distortion was hitting it, it kind of hit in the wrong place in a couple spots and it always drives me crazy."

## curiosities
**Meticulous and Slow Working Style** : Tool is known for its meticulous and slow working process, which producer David Botrell became accustomed to, having previously worked with Peter Gabriel, who also works at a deliberate pace. The band's patience is highly respected, as they refuse to be rushed until the music "feels right" to them. "How is it working with a band that works so meticulously and so slow?"
**Adam Jones's Guitar Tone and Precision** : Adam Jones is considered one of his generation's greatest guitarists, not for shredding, but for his extreme attention to guitar tone and precision. He is known to meticulously test up to ten volume pots to find one that feels "just the right way" for him. He utilizes the guitar's volume knob instead of the amp to achieve specific "slightly hollow-ish" tones. His playing involves precise control over how he hits the string, the angle, and position. "Adam, I think, is one of our generations greatest guitarists."
**Three-Piece Writing Process** : Tool's songwriting process involves the three instrumental members working as a trio; Maynard, the vocalist, typically does not sit in with them due to frustration with their iterative pace. Adam Jones often takes a practiced song home, re-edits its structure, and brings it back to test new arrangements repeatedly until the feel is perfected for each section. "And when they write, they write as a three-piece group. Mayer doesn't sit in with them because it's too frustrating for him to keep going."
**Near Breakup During Lateralus Recording** : While recording *Lateralus*, a song's lyrics written by Maynard, which Adam hadn't heard properly, caused significant conflict. This, combined with existing legal issues (firing their manager, record company problems) that had soured their relationship, led to a day and a half of intense discussion between Adam and Maynard, almost causing the band to break up. Maynard eventually changed some lyrics, and the band resolved their problems. "It was almost the end of the tool at that point."

# artist - Silverchair

## members

## albums
**Frogstomp** : This early Silverchair album, while "pretty amazing" for 14-year-old kids, is described by David Botrell as a "fairly derivative record" of "Grand John Rathin" (presumably grunge/thrash). Many fans desired the band to continue making similar records, but Daniel Johns was intent on exploring new musical directions. "The ones that just want frog stomp over and over again, they probably laughed."
**Diorama (2002)** : Produced by David Botrell, who was brought in to replace another producer, as the band sought a new musical direction. Daniel Johns's desire for diversity on this album alienated some fans who preferred their earlier sound. The album's tour was impacted by Daniel's reactive arthritis, leading to a "soft release." "This is the Diorama album in 2002."

## curiosities
**Fan Devotion and Band Evolution** : Silverchair's undying fan devotion is attributed in part to their young start, allowing fans to grow with them. Daniel Johns consistently sought to offer something new with each project, a diversity that some fans embraced while others, preferring their early "Frogstomp" sound, "probably laughed" and left. "Daniel's always given his fans something new on every project that he's ever done."
**Daniel Johns's Struggles with Fame and Health** : Daniel Johns experienced immense psychological pressure from early fame, starting at 14, leading to anorexia as a coping mechanism in hopes of being left alone. After the *Diorama* record, a diet consisting solely of fruit contributed to him developing reactive arthritis, which hindered the album's immediate touring plans. "He said that his whole eating sort of thing. He just started to stop eating. Maybe people just leave him alone."

**Fox Lake Cree Nation - Northern Manitoba** : This indigenous community is one of the locations where Make Music Matter establishes small recording studios to use music as a therapeutic tool for healing trauma. "or Northern Manitoba into the indigenous communities up there in Fox Lake Cree Nation."
**Conflict as Creative Fuel** : David Botrell believes that conflict and "rub" during songwriting and recording sessions can be essential for stirring emotion and capturing "great records." He draws a parallel to the "Lennon McCartney battle," suggesting that "records that are easy often aren't that good." "Yes, you need that rub, that conflict, the classic, the Lenin McCartney battle between whatever the battle is between to have that tension."
**Future of Professional Recording Studios** : David Botrell believes professional recording studios must "diversify a little bit," potentially using large spaces as event venues, due to shrinking label budgets and other challenges. He expresses frustration that major labels, despite doing well from streaming, often squeeze studios financially, leading to a "tragedy" of fewer great studios. "I think professional recording studios will probably have to diversify a little bit and maybe their spaces, if they're large, will be possibly used as event spaces as well."
**Make Music Matter Organization** : Make Music Matter is an organization that uses music writing and composition as a therapeutic tool to heal trauma in marginalized communities, such as war-torn areas like the Democratic Republic of Congo and indigenous communities in Northern Manitoba. They set up small recording studios where a music producer and therapist facilitate group sessions for songwriting, subtly integrating therapy. "Make Music Matter is an organization that uses the writing and composition of music as a therapeutic tool to heal trauma in marginalized communities and individuals."
**Therapeutic Model of Make Music Matter** : The organization's model involves participants writing songs about their experiences in group therapy sessions within small recording studios. The therapy is "sneaked in under the radar," as participants primarily think they are attending a recording and songwriting session. Studies show this approach effectively heals trauma, empowering individuals like the women in the DRC to become advocates. "we sort of sneak the therapy in under the radar. They think they're coming to a recording session and a songwriting session."
