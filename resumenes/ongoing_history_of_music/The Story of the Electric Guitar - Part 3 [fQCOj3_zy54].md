"The instruments gained a really bad rep with serious players shunning Fender's made after 1965."

# label - Vox

## curiosities
**Company Founding (1957)** : Vox, a British amplifier company, was established in 1957. It quickly became known for its distinctively designed amplifiers. "The first is Vox, an amplifier company founded in 1957 with the distinctive diamond-shaped grill pattern."
**Introduction of the AC30 (1959)** : The 30-watt Vox AC30, introduced in 1959, became the company's most crucial model. Before its advent, most electric guitarists relied on amplifiers made by Fender and other smaller American manufacturers, leading to a more uniform guitar tone. "The most important model was the 30-watt Vox AC30, first introduced in 1959."
**Unique Circuitry and Sound** : The Vox AC30 revolutionized guitar amplification with its use of vacuum tubes and innovative circuitry, including a "second treble stage" known as the "top boost circuit." This design produced a brighter, more "jangly" sound that differed significantly from existing amplifiers. "First it used vacuum tubes. Secondly, its amplification circuitry was different, employing a second treble stage, something called the top boost circuit."
**Defining British Invasion Sound** : The distinctive "Vox sound" became the definitive sonic characteristic for nearly all British Invasion bands from the mid-1960s onward. Artists like The Rolling Stones, The Kinks, and The Yardbirds, among dozens of others, prominently featured Vox products in their performances. "And the Vox sound became the sound for pretty much all British invasion bands of the mid-1960s and beyond."
**Enduring Impact and Notable Users** : Vox amplifiers have maintained a profound impact on guitar rock across several decades. Beyond the British Invasion, later prominent groups and guitarists such as Queen, Dire Straits, Echo and the Bunnymen, R.E.M., Tom Petty, Blink 1-2, Oasis, Muse, U2, Foo Fighters, Kurt Cobain, Slash, Arctic Monkeys, and Radiohead have all utilized Vox units. "You could not understate the impact Vox had on guitar rock through the decades."

# label - Marshall

## curiosities
**Founding and Early Business (1960)** : Jim Marshall, a British drummer and tap dancer, opened a shop in London in 1960, initially selling only drums. He later expanded his inventory to include other musical instruments, laying the groundwork for his entry into amplifier manufacturing. "Starting with a shop in London that sold just drums in 1960, Jim added more musical instruments to his inventory."
**Pete Townshend's Influence** : Pete Townshend, whose father was a professional dance band musician and friend of Jim Marshall, frequently purchased guitars from Marshall's store. Townshend, supported by other customers, strongly urged Jim Marshall to provide the burgeoning London rock scene with more powerful and suitable amplification. "Because he was friends with Pete Townsend's father, who had been a professional dance band musician, Pete bought a lot of his guitars from Marshall's store."
**First Amplifier (1962)** : Despite his initial lack of knowledge about amplifiers, Jim Marshall ventured into manufacturing. His first amplifier, a 35-watt model largely based on a Fender design, went on sale in 1962. "The first was a 35 watt amp based largely on a fender design that went on sale in 1962, and it was okay."
**Innovation of the Marshall Stack** : Jim Marshall and his partners revolutionized amplifier design by separating the amplifier electronics (the "head") from the speakers (the "cabinet"). This innovation resulted in a head capable of pumping out up to 100 watts—significantly more power than other available amps—and a cabinet typically containing four 12-inch speakers. "What if we separated the amplifier from the speakers? How's them in two separate units? The result was an enclosure featuring all the electronics called the head and a second enclosure with four 12 inch speakers."
**Designed for Distortion** : Unlike contemporary amplifiers that aimed for clean sound, Marshall amplifiers were explicitly engineered for distortion, a crucial characteristic for the evolving rock sound. Their unique circuitry and use of vacuum tubes produced a signature type of distortion. "And unlike all the other amps on the market that promised clear distortion for e-sound, Marshall's were designed specifically for distortion. Its circuitry and use of vacuum tubes were unique, resulting in a signature sort of sound."
**Iconic Super Lead Model 1959** : The Marshall stack, officially known as the Marshall Super Lead Model 1959, became the foundational design for the "walls of Marshall amps and cabinets" seen today. Available in half-stack (one cabinet) or full-stack (two cabinets driven by a head) configurations, it is considered the most iconic amp setup for guitarists seeking volume, power, and distortion. "This was the Marshall stack, officially known as the Marshall Super Lead Model 1959. It is the basis for all the walls of Marshall amps and cabinets that we see today, either with a half-stack design with one speaker cabinet or a full stack with a head unit driving two cabinets."
**Profound Contribution to Rock and Roll** : The impact of Marshall amps and cabinets on rock and roll is considered monumental, second only to the electric guitar itself. They became indispensable for achieving the genre's characteristic volume, power, and distorted sound. "It's not overstretching it to say that next to the electric guitar itself, Marshall amps and cabinets are the thing that contributed to rock and roll the most."

# label - Hiwatt

## curiosities

"This one, founded by an electronic engineer named Dave Reeves. **Focus on Volume** : Reeves initially sold his amplifiers directly to musicians, with a key selling point being their exceptional volume. He named his amps "Hiwatt" to reflect their high wattage output. "At first he sold directly to musicians, promising them plenty of volume. He called his amps high watt because, well, they had a lot of watts."
**High Wattage Output** : Hiwatt amplifiers quickly surpassed Marshall's 100-watt output, with models rated at 200 watts and later even 400 watts. This significant power attracted guitarists seeking maximum volume. "And while Marshall pumped out 100 watts, high watts were rated at 200, then 400."
**Adoption by Major Bands** : Hiwatt's powerful amplifiers attracted a roster of influential bands, with Pete Townshend famously defecting from Marshall to Hiwatt for its higher wattage. Other notable users included Pink Floyd, The Moody Blues, Fleetwood Mac, Genesis, Heart, and Jethro Tull. "P-tounds and defected from Marshall to high watt for this very reason. Pink Floyd, the Moody Blues, Fleetwood Mac, Genesis, Hart, and Jethro Tull were just some of the bands to follow."
**Contribution to Modern Guitar Amps** : Hiwatt, along with Marshall and Vox, played a crucial role in the development and popularization of the modern guitar amplifier, shaping the powerful and beloved sounds characteristic of guitar rock. "The upshot is that between high watt, Marshall, and Vox, we have the modern guitar amp, and that lovely, powerful sound."

"The lacquers used for finishing the guitars changed, making them look shabby. Cheaper woods were used for both the body and the neck. More plastic was used as well as thinner metal in place of prestige."

# instrument - Vox AC30

## curiosities
**30-Watt Output and 1959 Introduction** : The Vox AC30 is a 30-watt amplifier model first introduced in 1959. Its release marked a significant departure from the predominantly American-made amplifiers used by most electric guitarists at the time, which had resulted in a more uniform guitar tone across the industry. "The most important model was the 30-watt Vox AC30, first introduced in 1959. Until Vox came along, the majority of electric guitars were using amps built by Fender and a few other small American manufacturers."
**Vacuum Tubes and Top Boost Circuit** : The AC30's distinctive bright, jangly sound was achieved through its use of vacuum tubes and a unique amplification circuitry, which included a "second treble stage" specifically known as the "top boost circuit." This innovative design set it apart from other amplifiers on the market. "First it used vacuum tubes. Secondly, its amplification circuitry was different, employing a second treble stage, something called the top boost circuit. And the result was a brighter, jangly sort of sound."
**Defining Sound of British Invasion** : The Vox AC30 became synonymous with the sound of the British Invasion bands of the mid-1960s and beyond, influencing a generation of musicians. Its characteristic tone was sought after by numerous groups, cementing its legacy in rock history. "And the Vox sound became the sound for pretty much all British invasion bands of the mid-1960s and beyond."

# instrument - Marshall Stack

## curiosities
**Jim Marshall's Innovation** : The Marshall stack, officially known as the Marshall Super Lead Model 1959, was an innovation by Jim Marshall and his partners, who decided to separate the amplifier electronics (the "head") from the speakers (the "cabinet"). This allowed for a powerful head that could pump up to 100 watts, paired with a cabinet featuring four 12-inch speakers. "What if we separated the amplifier from the speakers? How's them in two separate units? The result was an enclosure featuring all the electronics called the head and a second enclosure with four 12 inch speakers."
**Designed for Distortion** : Unlike other amplifiers of its time that prioritized clear, distortion-free sound, Marshall amps were specifically engineered for distortion. Their unique circuitry and use of vacuum tubes were crafted to produce a signature, powerful distorted tone. "Its circuitry and use of vacuum tubes were unique, resulting in a signature sort of sound."
**Iconic Amplifier Setup** : The Marshall stack quickly became the most iconic of all amplifier setups for guitarists demanding high volume, power, and distortion. It laid the foundation for the "walls of Marshall amps and cabinets" commonly seen today, available in half-stack (one cabinet) or full-stack (two cabinets) configurations. "This is by far the most iconic of all amp setups for guitars who want volume and power and distortion."
**Crucial Contribution to Rock and Roll** : Beyond the electric guitar itself, Marshall amps and cabinets are considered to be the most significant contributors to the development and sound of rock and roll. Their ability to deliver immense volume and signature distortion was instrumental in shaping the genre. "It's not overstretching it to say that next to the electric guitar itself, Marshall amps and cabinets are the thing that contributed to rock and roll the most."

"The upshot is that between high watt, Marshall, and Vox, we have the modern guitar amp, and that lovely, powerful sound."

# curiosity

**Rise of the Guitar Hero** : The period following The Beatles' arrival in America in February 1964 marked the "beginning of the age of the guitar hero." During this time, the influence and prominence of electric guitarists like Eric Clapton, Pete Townshend, Jimmy Page, and Jimi Hendrix grew significantly, transforming the role of the lead guitarist in bands. "It was the beginning of the age of the guitar hero. And soon we'd be hearing about Eric Clapton, Pete Townsend, Jimmy Page, Jimmy Hendrix and more."
**Mid-1960s Guitar Market Boom and Bust** : The mid-1960s presented a paradoxical scenario for musicians and guitar manufacturers: demand for electric guitars soared "through the roof," with Fender, Gibson, Rickenbacker, and Gretsch struggling to keep pace. Simultaneously, market challenges arose, particularly concerning quality control issues at major manufacturers and a flood of cheap imports. "The mid-60s were both a boon and a bust for musicians. In the middle 1960s, demand for electric guitars went through the roof, with Fender, Gibson, Rick and Bachar and Gretch scrambling to make as many guitars as they could."
**Birth of the Vintage Guitar Market** : Quality issues stemming from the 1969 sale of Gibson and the 1965 sale of Fender to CBS directly led to the creation of the vintage guitar market. Musicians began actively seeking out superior-quality instruments made before these corporate changes, specifically 1958-1960 Les Pauls and pre-1965 Strats and Telecasters. "These changes at Gibson and Fender gave birth to the vintage guitar market. These musicians wanted their 58-60 less balls and strats and tellies from before 1965."
**Flood of Cheap Imported Guitars (Mid-1960s)** : In the mid-1960s, the market was inundated with inexpensive electric guitars imported from countries like Germany, Sweden, Italy, and especially Japan, which at one point produced nearly a million cheap guitars annually, exporting 80% of them. These "starter instruments" were primarily sold through department stores rather than specialized retailers, under brand names such as Del Rey, Kent, and Hagstrom, as well as American budget brands like Harmony, National, K, and Airline. "But this flood of cheap guitars turned out to be a very, very good thing. These instruments became the bedrock of a million garage bands that formed throughout the rest of the decade and into the 1970s."
**Bedrock of Garage Bands** : While not matching the quality of major manufacturers, the abundance of cheap imported and budget-brand guitars proved beneficial by forming the "bedrock of a million garage bands" throughout the late 1960s and into the 1970s. These accessible instruments allowed countless beginners to start playing, with some eventually upgrading while others found charm in their affordable guitars, such as Jack White with his red Airline. "But this flood of cheap guitars turned out to be a very, very good thing. These instruments became the bedrock of a million garage bands that formed throughout the rest of the decade and into the 1970s."
**Japanese Guitar Quality Improvement (Mid-1970s)** : By the mid-1970s, Japanese manufacturers, including Yamaha, ESP, and Ibanez, significantly improved the quality of their guitars. They moved past earlier issues of "goofy pickups," "warp necks," and "poorly finished fretboards," establishing themselves as producers of fine, high-quality instruments. "A lot of those cheaps came from Japan, but by the middle 1970s, the Japanese were making some pretty good stuff. Yamaha, ESP, and Ibanez began creating quality guitars."
**Music Videos and Wild Guitar Designs (1980s)** : The advent of MTV on August 1st, 1981, and the subsequent rise of music videos, dramatically influenced electric guitar design. As artists' instruments became highly visible, there was a drive for guitars that stood out, leading to an era of "wild and crazy" designs exemplified by brands like BC Rich. "After MTV started broadcasting in August 1st, 1981, everyone could see what instruments their heroes were playing. Not only that, but guitars wanted to stand out, and this led to an era of wild and crazy guitar design."
**Lack of New Guitar Heroes** : A contributing factor to the decline in electric guitar sales in the early 21st century in the United States may have been the perceived absence of new, inspiring young guitar heroes. This lack of role models might have failed to motivate younger generations to take up the instrument. "Part of the issue might have been a lack of any new young guitar heroes who will inspire other young people to head to the music store."
**Shifting Music Trends and Guitar's Role** : The role of the electric guitar has fluctuated with popular music trends: it dominated the 1960s and 70s, gave way to synthesizers and electronics in the 1980s, re-emerged strongly with Grunge and alternative in the 1990s, and powered the guitar-based indie revival in the 21st century. "Look, trends come and go in popular music. The electric guitar was the thing in the 1960s and 70s before synthesizers and other electronics came to the fore in the 1980s."
