## albums
**The Clash (1977) - Debut Album** : This self-titled album was recorded over three weekends and became a big hit in Britain. Despite having a worldwide deal with CBS Records, the label initially refused to release it in North America, deeming it "too raw, abrasive, crude, and political for American tastes" during the disco era. North American fans were forced to buy higher-priced British import versions, which sold 100,000 copies, making it the biggest-selling import at the time. CBS finally released a "somewhat watered down version with a different track listing" in 1979. The album's cover only features Joe Strummer, Mick Jones, and Paul Simonon because at the time the photo was shot, the band didn't have a full-time drummer after Terry Chimes split.
"It's a big hit in Britain."

**Give 'Em Enough Rope (1978) - Second Studio Album** : Released on November 10, 1978, this album was surprisingly produced by a person known for working with bands like Blue Öyster Cult, which alienated some hardcore fans. The record sounded different from their debut, featuring a more polished and cleaner sound, losing some of the amateurish energy. Despite the changes, it still conveyed "righteous anger" and introduced new musical options for punk, with Joe Strummer's lyrics covering topics from civil war and the drug trade to Palestinian terrorism.
"The amateur-ish energy that was all over the first album was gone."

**London Calling (1979) - Landmark Double Album** : This double album, released on December 14, 1979, featured 19 songs and marked the band's creative peak, becoming one of the most influential records ever and assuring The Clash's reputation. It expanded their sound to include Ska, Reggae, Calypso, Disco, Garage Rock, Rockabilly, and Rap, with some songs inspired by a North American tour and others by the music in the streets of Pimlico, London. Recorded over just a few days in August 1979, including 12 songs bashed out in three days, the album, originally titled "The New Testament," was produced by Guy Stevens, who prioritized feel over technical perfection. The band demanded its release as a double album, but to make it affordable for their fans, they agreed to package it like a single, lower the price, and accept a lower royalty rate.
"Not only would it become the record that would assure the clash's reputation for all time, but it would also become one of the most influential records ever."

**Sandinista! (1980) - Triple Album Endeavor** : Released on December 12, 1980, just 363 days after *London Calling*, this was a sprawling and sometimes messy triple album. Its title reflects the band's solidarity with the Sandinista Communist Rebels who overthrew the government in Nicaragua in 1979. To get CBS to agree to release a triple album, The Clash made significant financial concessions, foregoing any royalties on the first 200,000 copies, a risky move as *London Calling* had only sold 180,000 copies at that point. The album incorporated an even wider array of influences, including gospel music and children's choirs, in an attempt to display vision, though some felt it became self-indulgent and that not all 36 songs sounded finished.
"It was a triple album."

**Combat Rock (1982) - Final Critically Acclaimed Album** : Released on May 14, 1982, this album was issued while Joe Strummer was in an eight-week disappearance from the band. It featured the song "Should I Stay or Should I Go," inspired by Joe's then-girlfriend Ellen Foley. The album was released during a period of increasing internal strife within the band, leading up to Joe Strummer's controversial decision to fire Mick Jones in September 1983.
"The date may 14th, 1982."

**Cut the Crap (1985) - Final Disappointing Album** : This album was released in 1985 by a new version of The Clash that Joe Strummer attempted to carry on with after firing Mick Jones. It was met with dismal reviews and much ridicule, ultimately leading to the quiet disbandment of The Clash without any formal announcement or farewell concert.
"after one dismal and much ridicule album, something called Cut the Crap in 1985, the clash folded."

## songs
**White Riot (1977)** : Released as The Clash's first single on March 18, 1977, this song was inspired by a real-life riot that occurred in the Notting Hill area of London the previous September. It addresses the institutional racism prevalent in Britain at the time and a controversial police action at the annual carnival held by West London's Caribbean community.
"Here is the first ever clash single, which was released on March 18th, 1977."

**Complete Control (1977)** : Issued on September 23, 1977, this song was featured on their debut album and served as their "first of several pokes at their record company and themselves." The lyrics are about the "evils of signing a major record label contract," reflecting the band's critical stance on the music industry despite having signed with CBS Records for 129,000 pounds.
"It featured a single all about the evils of signing a major record label contract."

**Police and Thieves (1977)** : Included on their first album, this is a cover of a Junior Marvin and Lee "Scratch" Perry song. The original was written about police and crime in Jamaica, and The Clash's version provided a "hint of the dub and reggae" that would become more prominent on their later records. This choice highlighted their early versatility and willingness to incorporate non-punk sounds.
"It's a cover of a junior Marvin and Lee Scratch Perry song written about police and crime in Jamaica."

**Brand New Cadillac (1979)** : This song was recorded for the *London Calling* album, serving as a warm-up track on the first day in the studio with producer Guy Stevens. Stevens, prioritizing feel over technical perfection, rolled the tape machines as the band warmed up. Despite The Clash's confusion and belief that the take was "all wrong" due to tempo changes, Stevens insisted on using it, famously stating, "all rock and roll speeds up." The recording captures a live-off-the-floor energy with no overdubs or second chances.
"But as the clash started playing, Guy rolled the tape machines."

**London Calling (1979)** : The title track of their seminal double album, this song was inspired by "some football chance in Soho." It serves as a powerful anthem for the album, capturing the band's heightened artistic ambition and their expanded musical palette which began to incorporate diverse influences like Ska, Reggae, Calypso, Disco, and Rap.
"We've already heard the title track, which by the way was inspired by some football chance in Soho."

**Guns of Brixton (1979)** : Featured on the brilliant *London Calling* album, this song was written by bass player Paul Simonon. Its sound reflects the influence of "dub music" – "slow, rumbly, bass heavy sounds with a really deep groove" – which The Clash picked up from the Jamaican community living in the Pimlico area of London, their new headquarters.
"That was written by bass player Paul Simmonon by the way."

**Train in Vain (1979)** : This is an unlisted 19th song that appears at the end of side four of *London Calling*. It was not included on the original sleeve or labels because by the time it was recorded, the cover and labels had already been printed. The Clash managed to get it onto the acetate just before the vinyl was printed. The band had originally intended to release it for free as a flexi-disc with the *New Musical Express*, but the publisher refused, leading them to stick it onto the end of *London Calling*. It unexpectedly became an "honest to god top 40 hit" for the band.
"Training van was never on the only sleeve ever because by the time we recorded it the cover and the labels had been already printed."

**The Magnificent Seven (1980)** : This track from the *Sandinista!* album features Joe Strummer's "credible attempt at rapping." Released in 1980, it showcases how "in touch" Joe and The Clash were with nascent street music trends, at a time when rap was so new that "hardly anyone knew about it" outside of a few releases by artists like the Sugarhill Gang and Kurtis Blow.
"And notice Joe Sturmurs credible attempt at wrapping?"

**Should I Stay or Should I Go (1982)** : Featured on The Clash's 1982 album *Combat Rock*, this song was inspired by Joe Strummer's then-girlfriend, Ellen Foley, who was also known for her role in Meat Loaf's song "Paradise by the Dashboard Light." The song's theme of indecision resonated with the internal struggles and tensions that were increasingly plaguing The Clash during this period.
"It was inspired by Joe's then-girlfriend, Ellen Foley."

**Love Kills (1986)** : This was the first post-Clash release from Joe Strummer, contributed to the soundtrack of the 1986 movie *Sid and Nancy*, which chronicled the lives of Sex Pistols bassist Sid Vicious and Nancy Spungen. Although not explicitly credited, Mick Jones had a role in the two songs Joe contributed to the soundtrack, signifying a continuation of their songwriting partnership despite past animosity.
"The first post-clash release from Joe Strummer."

## curiosities
**Early Political and Social Awareness** : The Clash were distinguished from their punk contemporaries like the Sex Pistols and Ramones by their militant, passionate, and serious political and social awareness. They believed their agenda could change the world, aiming to change the system and rock and roll from the ground up, even if their expectations sometimes clashed with reality.
"The clash were not just aware, but militant in their positions, as contrary and as contradictory as some of those stances turn out to be."

**Joe Strummer's Death (December 22, 2002)** : Joe Strummer passed away from a heart attack on December 22, 2002. His death received some newspaper coverage in the last weeks of 2002, but the narrative suggests this coverage "didn't even come close to doing the man justice."
"Joe's gone now."

**The Clash as a Voice for a Generation** : For millions who came of age musically in the late 1970s and early 1980s, The Clash were considered "their Beatles," representing more than just punk aesthetics. They had a message and a mission rooted in integrity and honesty, serving as "the voice of a generation" that could articulate rage and alienation, and they were "their own worst critics."
"For millions of people who came of age musically in the late 70s and early 80s, the clash were their beetles."

**Musical Expansion of Punk Rock** : The Clash were instrumental in enlarging the musical scope of punk rock, insisting that diverse genres like rockabilly and reggae could also be considered punk. Joe Strummer, in particular, was key in introducing the band to non-punk sounds such as Ronnie Hawkins, blues, R&B, Bo Diddley, Chuck Berry, and his favorite, reggae artists like Bob Marley, Peter Tosh, Toots and the Maytals, Desmond Dekker, and Burning Spear.
"They were the ones who insisted that things like rockabilly and reggae could be punk as well."

**Early Arrests and Fines** : The Clash gained a reputation as "criminals" due to frequent incidents. On June 10, 1977, Joe Strummer and Topper Headon were arrested for spray-painting "the clash" on a wall. They were later fined 100 pounds for stealing a pillowcase from a Holiday Inn, and 800 pounds for shooting at expensive racing pigeons. Mick Jones also faced trouble for cocaine possession.
"On June 10th, 1977, Joe and Topper were arrested for spray-painting the clash on a wall."

**Activism and Affordability for Fans** : The Clash consistently prioritized their fans and their anti-capitalist ideals. They insisted that the price of their albums and concert tickets be substantially lower than prevailing rates. The band members also maintained a salary of only $200 a week per man, even after achieving world fame with *London Calling*, to reflect their understanding of scraping by. They would often open back doors at concerts to let in fans who couldn't afford tickets, meet audiences for beers after shows, and once donated a limousine sent by their record company to a committee for striking Welsh miners.
"They insisted that the price of their albums and their concert tickets be substantially lower than the prevailing rate, and they sang about the problems of the day."

**"No Beatles, Elvis, or Rolling Stones" Slogan** : Since 1977, The Clash had adopted the slogan "No Beatles, Elvis, or Rolling Stones" to assert their unique identity and reject the rock establishment. This stance created significant controversy and confusion among their fans when they decided to go on a big stadium and arena tour with The Who in September 1982.
"No Beatles, Elvis, or Rolling Stones that had been their slogan since 1977, and now that we're going on tour with the who, I mean, as long with that picture."

**Joe Strummer's Disappearance (March-May 1982)** : In March 1982, Joe Strummer suddenly disappeared for eight weeks after a phone interview with a journalist, leading to a tour cancellation and uncertainty about his well-being. This event occurred just before the release of *Combat Rock*. It was later rumored to be a publicity stunt orchestrated by the band's manager, with Joe reportedly spending his time in France to help a girlfriend bail her mother out of jail.
"In March 1982, Joe did a quick phone interview with a journalist, walked out the door, and wasn't seen for eight weeks."

**Mick Jones's Firing (September 1983)** : In September 1983, Joe Strummer orchestrated what was described as a "coup" by issuing an "infamous clash communique," essentially firing Mick Jones, who had co-founded The Clash in 1976. Joe expressed frustration with Mick's perceived lack of commitment, stating he had to "beg him to play the guitar" and comparing the situation to "dragging a dead dog around on a bit of string." This decision was widely seen as detrimental, and The Clash "was never the same after Mick left."
"He essentially orchestrated a coup and fired Mick Jones."

**Post-Clash Reunions Attempts** : Joe Strummer made several attempts to reform The Clash with Mick Jones over the years. The first documented attempt was in November 1985 when Joe, realizing his mistake in firing Mick, followed him on holiday to Spain to try and reconcile. However, Mick "basically laughed off the idea" because he was engaged with his own band, Big Audio Dynamite, at the time.
"The first time Joe Strummer asked Mick Jones to help him reform the clash was in November 1985."

**East German Stasi Opposition** : The Clash desired to bring their music to audiences in communist East Germany, believing their working-class, socialist, and anti-capitalist message would be well-received. However, the East German secret police, the Stasi, had been monitoring The Clash and studying Joe Strummer's lyrics. They concluded that the band was "just a little too left wing and a little too incendiary" to be allowed into the country, denying them visas.
"Well, it turns out that the East German secret police, the Stasi, had been monitoring the clash and studying Joe Sturmurs lyrics."

**Joe Strummer's Acting and Composing Career** : Following his work on the *Sid and Nancy* soundtrack, Joe Strummer found an unexpected calling in film. He developed a good relationship with director Alex Cox, leading to acting roles in Cox's next two movies. In the 1987 film *Walker*, set in 19th-century Nicaragua, Joe played a character named Fossett and composed all the music for the soundtrack. The same year, he appeared in another Alex Cox movie, *Straight to Hell*, a surreal western, playing a character named Sims. He continued doing movie work, both acting and composing, for the rest of the 1980s, including the soundtracks for *Permanent Record* (1988) and *Mystery Train* (1989).
"Joe found that movies were, apparently, in his blood."

**Joe Strummer's Work with Big Audio Dynamite** : Despite the acrimony of their split, Joe Strummer and Mick Jones maintained their songwriting partnership. In 1986, two weeks before the movie *Sid and Nancy* was released, Mick Jones's band, Big Audio Dynamite, released their second album, *Number 10 Upping Street*. Eight of the 12 songs on this album featured contributions from Joe Strummer.
"Eight of the 12 songs in the album featured contributions from Joe."

## curiosities
**Birth and Early Life** : Born John Miller on August 21, 1952, in Ankara, Turkey, Joe Strummer began life as a "well-bred, privately educated member of the British Empire." His father was a foreign service diplomat, leading the family to live in Cyprus, Cairo, and Mexico City by the time John was 10. He and his older brother, David, attended a private boarding school in London before he moved on to art school.
"He was born John Miller, August 21st, 1952, and Ankara, Turkey."

**Brief Cartooning Ambition** : While at art school, Joe Strummer briefly flirted with the idea of becoming a professional cartoonist. However, this ambition was cut short when he was expelled from art school for taking LSD. During this "cartoonist phase," he adopted the name Woody Miller, inspired by the American folk singer Woody Guthrie.
"But then he was expelled for taking LSD."

**Musical Influences** : Joe Strummer's early musical heroes included The Beatles and The Beach Boys. He also developed a strong interest in American folk singer Woody Guthrie, to the extent that he adopted the name Woody Miller during his cartoonist phase. His later, more significant influences, which he described as "Black men," encompassed Canadian Ronnie Hawkins, blues, R&B, Bo Diddley, Chuck Berry, and various reggae artists like Bob Marley, Peter Tosh, Toots and the Maytals, Desmond Dekker, and Burning Spear.
"Musical heroes were the Beatles and the Beach Boys."

**Learning Guitar and Unique Strumming Style** : John Miller didn't seriously pick up a guitar until 1972, at the age of 20. He met busker Timon Dogg at the Green Park Tube Stop in London, who taught him a few elementary chords. Joe later adopted the name Joe Strummer in early 1975 because of his unique guitar strumming method. He was "too lazy" to learn chords "the right way" and found shortcuts, which, while "technically ugly," resulted in a distinctive sound and style.
"Timon Dogg had taught him those rudimentary chords, but Joe was too lazy to learn how to play them the right way."

**Loss of Posh Accent** : Legend has it that Joe Strummer's ability to enunciate clearly and his "posh boarding school accent" changed forever after he had his teeth kicked in during a pub brawl. This incident is associated with his transition to a more working-class punk image.
"Legend has it that his ability to enunciate clearly change forever when he had his teeth kicked in during a pub brawl."

**Singing for The Pogues** : Joe Strummer sang for The Pogues at one point after the breakup of The Clash. This engagement highlights his continued involvement in the music scene and his collaborations with other influential bands.
"the time Joe sang for the pogues."

**Director Alex Cox Collaborations** : Joe Strummer formed a strong working relationship with director Alex Cox, leading to several film collaborations. After contributing to the soundtrack of *Sid and Nancy*, Joe was given acting roles in Cox's next two movies, *Walker* (1987) and *Straight to Hell* (1987), for which he also composed the entire score for *Walker*.
"He hit it off so well with director Alex Cox that Joe was given roles in his next two movies."

## curiosities
**Formation and Name Origin** : Sometime in 1974, after busking in London, Joe Strummer (then John Miller) rounded up friends to form a group called The 101ers. The band's name comes from "the number of the house in West London where all the band members lived."
"So he rounded up a bunch of friends in form of a group called the 101ers."

**Pub Circuit Success and Horn Section** : The 101ers achieved considerable success on the London pub circuit, playing covers of American R&B songs. At one point, the band even featured a full horn section composed of "guys in the run from the evil regime in Chile," adding a unique element to their sound.
"The band did quite well in the pub circuit, playing covers of American R&B songs."

**Disbandment After Sex Pistols Show (April 3, 1976)** : Joe Strummer realized The 101ers were "going nowhere" after attending a Sex Pistols concert at The Nashville Rooms in Kensington, London, on April 3, 1976. He recognized that the Sex Pistols' raw energy represented "the future," prompting him to seek a new musical direction which led to the formation of The Clash.
"After watching the tear through their set, Joe realized that The 101ers were going nowhere."

# genre - Punk

## curiosities
**Evolution and Expansion** : While early punk bands like the Ramones focused on "three chords really fast and screaming about how life sucked," The Clash significantly expanded the genre's musical scope by incorporating Ska, Reggae, Rockabilly, R&B, and even Rap into their sound. They also deepened punk's lyrical content to address alienation, working-class politics, racism, unemployment, and police brutality. This evolution ensured that punk did not "die out with a whimper by the beginning of the 1980s" but instead evolved into "a hundred different flavors," leading modern-day punk bands to owe a lot to The Clash.
"In other words, while they were around, the clash changed the rules of punk, expanding its musical scope and its subject matter."

**Death of Punk Sentiment (January 25, 1977)** : The day The Clash signed a worldwide contract with CBS Records for 129,000 pounds on January 25, 1977, was considered by some of "Punk's hardcore adherence" as "the day Punk died." This sentiment arose despite the band's position that they intended to "subvert the record industry from within," as signing with a major label was seen as a betrayal of punk's anti-establishment ethos.
"So to them, January 25th, 1977, was actually the day Punk died."

# label - CBS Records

## curiosities
**Controversial Contract with The Clash (January 25, 1977)** : CBS Records signed The Clash to a worldwide contract for 129,000 pounds on January 25, 1977. This deal was highly controversial among punk's hardcore fans, who viewed it as a betrayal of punk's anti-establishment ethos and declared it "the day Punk died," despite The Clash's stated intention to "subvert the record industry from within."
"They got a worldwide contract with CBS Records, a major for 129,000 pounds."

**Refusal to Release The Clash's Debut in North America** : CBS Records initially refused to release The Clash's self-titled debut album in North America for two years after its British release. Executives believed the record was "too raw, and abrasive, and crude, and political for American tastes" during the disco era. This decision forced North American fans to buy higher-priced British import versions, which astonishingly sold 100,000 copies, making it the biggest-selling import ever at the time, before CBS finally relented in 1979 with a "watered down version."
"CBS decided not to release this record in North America."

**Reluctance Over Double and Triple Albums** : CBS Records expressed reluctance to release The Clash's *London Calling* as a double album and *Sandinista!* as a triple album, due to concerns about manufacturing hassle and market price. The band had to make significant financial concessions, such as accepting lower royalty rates for *London Calling* and foregoing royalties on the first 200,000 copies of *Sandinista!*, to convince the label to release these ambitious projects.
"CBS, the label, wanted nothing to do with the hassle of manufacturing a triple album."

# curiosity

**British Social and Economic Turmoil (Mid-Late 1970s)** : The mid-to-late 1970s were a challenging period for young people in Britain, characterized by severe austerity measures imposed by the Labour government due to the country's financial troubles. These measures deeply affected the working classes and public sector workers, leading to wage cuts, reduced benefits, and deteriorating working conditions. This created widespread social turmoil, including strikes, lockouts, high unemployment, falling living standards, and a general sense of despair and alienation, particularly among the youth.
"The middle and late 70s were a tough time for young people in Britain."

**Institutional Racism in Britain (Mid-Late 1970s)** : During the mid-to-late 1970s in Britain, institutional racism was a significant problem. Authorities were "quite enthusiastic" in using an "anti-vagrancy law from 1824 to harass young blacks." Additionally, fascist elements like the National Front were gaining traction, seeking scapegoats for the problems faced by young working-class people, with immigrants as their primary targets.
"There was that institutional racism that we talked about earlier."

**Winter of Discontent (1978-1979)** : From late 1978 through mid-1979, Britain experienced a "famous winter of discontents," during which the country was "paralyzed by a series of massive strikes and labor actions." The situation became so severe that the army had to be called out to provide "scab labor." This period of widespread industrial unrest significantly fueled the anger expressed in The Clash's third album, *London Calling*.
"From late 1978 through to mid 1979, the country was paralyzed by a series of massive strikes and labor actions."

**Rolling Stone's Best Albums of the Decade (1980s)** : As the 1980s concluded, *Rolling Stone* magazine ranked *London Calling* by The Clash as the number one album of the decade. This prestigious recognition placed it above other formidable albums such as Paul Simon's *Graceland*, Talking Heads' *Remain in Light*, Prince's *Purple Rain*, and U2's *The Joshua Tree*.
"Anyone remember what finished at number one? London Calling by the clash."

**Reunion attempt with Mick Jones (1990)** : Around 1990, Mick Jones suggested reforming The Clash, but Joe Strummer's stubbornness prevented it. Joe insisted on the original late 70s/early 80s lineup (Joe, Mick, Paul Simonon, Topper Headon if sober, or Terry Chimes) and the return of former manager Bernie Rhodes, whom the other members refused to work with due to his past lawsuit attempt. "Joe was stubborn. He believed that if the clash were to rise again, it would have to be just as it was back in the late 70s and early 80s."
**Record Label Dispute with Sony (1990s)** : Joe Strummer was in a nearly decade-long dispute with his record label (later identified as Sony). The label was reluctant to fund another album, fearing the returns wouldn't cover expenses or Joe's advance, and viewing him as "over." This "Mexican standoff" lasted eight years, ending with a compromise where Sony released him from his contract on the condition that if The Clash ever reunited, they would record for Sony. "This explains why there were no Strummer solo records for nearly a decade."
**Joining The Pogues (1991)** : In 1991, after The Pogues fired their singer Shane McGowan for alcoholism, Joe Strummer was asked to fill in as lead singer, having previously done studio work with the band. "Joe Strummer during his stints as the lead singer with the Pogs in the early 1990s."

"There was an aborted attempt with a group called Machine and then Joe tried to get something going with Bez, X of the Happy Mondays, but that didn't work out."
**Formation of The Mescaleros** : Following unsuccessful attempts to form other groups, Joe Strummer successfully assembled a new band he named The Mescaleros, who were noted for being "pretty good," even when performing old Clash songs. "But in the end, he pulled together a band he called the Mescaleros."
**Final Gig with Mick Jones (November 16, 2002)** : On November 16, 2002, Joe Strummer and The Mescaleros played a benefit concert for a London fire brigade. Mick Jones, who was in the audience, joined them on stage to perform a couple of songs, marking their first joint performance since 1983. Both agreed the experience "felt very, very, very good," though it was unknowingly Joe's final live performance. "That marked the first time in 1983 that Joe and Mick had performed together."
**Death and Legacy (December 22, 2002)** : Joe Strummer died suddenly on December 22, 2002, at the age of 50, from a heart attack, likely due to an unknown hereditary condition, at his home in Somerset, England, shortly after walking his dogs. His death shocked the world and led to reflections on his importance, with some comparing him to Kurt Cobain or Joey Ramone for an entire generation. "Joe was just 50. The world was shocked."
**Funeral Procession and Service (December 2002)** : Joe Strummer's funeral procession traveled through London, intentionally passing by locations where The Clash used to perform. The simple coffin was adorned with stickers saying "vinyl rules" and "question authority asked me anything." A private service was held at the West London Crematorium, attended by the remaining members of The Clash, "the cream of British Rock and Roll," and Courtney Love. Two dozen uniformed firemen, beneficiaries of Joe's last gig, stood at attention, and a fire truck led the procession. "The funeral procession wound its way through London, deliberately passing some of the places where the clash used to play."
**Charitable Requests in Memory (December 2002)** : Joe Strummer's wife, Lucinda, requested that instead of sending flowers, people donate money in Joe's name to an organization addressing the AIDS crisis in Africa. Fans were also encouraged to plant a tree in his name for the Future Forest's Environmental Charity, a gesture intended to offset pollution from CD manufacturing and disposal. "Instead, she asked that people donate money and Joe's name to an organization dedicated to the AIDS crisis in Africa."
**Meeting Courtney Love (1987)** : Joe Strummer befriended Courtney Love in 1987 while filming "Straight to Hell." After production wrapped, Joe purchased a plane ticket for Courtney to return to the States when she couldn't afford to go home. "She enjoyed being friends since 1987 when they made on the set of Straight to Hell."
**Starbucks Street Performance (Toronto)** : Joe Strummer once arrived late, hung over, and grumpy to a live radio broadcast in Toronto. Frustrated by the noise preventing him from tuning his battered guitar, he abruptly left the studio and performed solo for passersby outside a crowded Starbucks store on Yonge Street. "Up there on Young Street, Interrano was one of the greatest living punks playing solo for people as they walked by."
**Ethos and Contradictions** : Joe Strummer and The Clash were characterized as idealistic, conflicted, and confused, embodying both reactionary and revolutionary tendencies. They championed the working class and the poor, and "sneered at consumerism and the privileged," yet controversially sold their songs for TV commercials, advertising products like Levi's jeans and Jaguar cars. "They were both a reactionary and revolutionary."
