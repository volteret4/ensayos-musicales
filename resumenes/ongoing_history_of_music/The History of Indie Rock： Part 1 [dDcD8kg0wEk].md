# genre - Indie Music

## curiosities
**Definition of Independent (Indie)** : "Indie" is short for independent, referring to music from artists not directly signed to one of the major multinational record labels (Universal, Sony, Warner). Artists recording for any other label, or operating independently of the three majors, are considered indie. The term describes a type of record company, its signed artists, and the music they create, having nothing to do with a band's sound or record sales. "Indy is, of course, short for independent. This means we're dealing with music from artists who are not signed directly to one of the major multinational record labels."
**DIY Ethic** : A crucial aspect of being indie is the "do it yourself" (DIY) ethic. Without the extensive infrastructure of a massive label, artists and those committed to distributing their music often have to handle much of the business and marketing themselves, acting as independent business people. "Another aspect of being Indy is the do it yourself, Ethic."
**Evolution of Labels (Historical Context)** : Initially, all record labels were considered indie, with thousands existing in America alone in the early 1960s (at least 3,000). Over time, larger labels acquired smaller ones, merged, or went out of business, consolidating the industry. By the 1970s, there were six or seven major labels, which has since reduced to just three (Universal, Sony, Warner) today. "Now once upon a time, all record labels were Indy. They were some that were bigger than others like Deca and London and RCA and EMI, but there were literally thousands of small labels that did their own thing."
**Major Label Subsidiaries** : Many subsidiary labels of the major corporations often adopt the appearance and feel of indie labels, frequently by design, but they are still considered part of the major label machine. This allows for a perceived independent image while still being part of a larger corporate structure. "Many of those subsidiaries have the look and feel of an Indy label, and often that's very much by design, but they are still considered to be part of the major label machine."

# genre - Punk Rock

## curiosities
**Early Major Label Rejection** : When punk arrived in the 1970s, its radical nature meant most of the music was too extreme for larger labels, whose primary concern was maximizing record sales. Much punk and subsequent music was not mainstream enough for these major labels to invest in. "When punk arrived in the 1970s, most of the music was too radical for the bigger labels. Their main concern was to sell as many records as possible."
**Rise of Independent Labels** : People who appreciated the radical new punk music started independent labels to provide an outlet for it, as mainstream labels showed no interest. These independent labels became known as "alternative labels," and the music they promoted was "alternative music." "But there were people who appreciated this radical new music and started new independent labels because they loved it so much and wanted to give it an outlet."
**UK Terminology** : In the UK, the term "alternative" did not gain traction, with "Indie" becoming the prevalent term due to the music predominantly originating from independent labels. Despite this, there is significant overlap between "Indie" and "alternative." "Meanwhile, in the UK, the word alternative never really caught on. Since most of this new music was coming from independent labels, the word Indy stuck."
**First Modern Rock Scene on Indie Labels** : Punk of the 1970s marked the first major music scene in the modern rock era built entirely on independent record companies. In Britain, punk rock's existence depended on independently-minded musicians and entrepreneurs because major, "respectable" companies and radio stations completely rejected it as "programming and ratings poison." "The punk of the 1970s was the first major music scene of the modern rock era to be built entirely on independent record companies."
**Documentation and Spread (Pre-Video Channels)** : With no video channels available at the time, punk music was documented and disseminated through cool record stores, small publications (fanzines), and word of mouth, all originating from independent record labels and the artists they attracted. "So the only way the music got documented and spread was through cool record stores, small publications, and word of mouth."

# genre - New Wave

## curiosities
**Origin of the Term (Late 1970s)** : The phrase "New Wave" had been in use for a few years, with some critics using it to describe music that showed punk's influence without being strictly punk. Traces of the term appeared in the UK zine "Sniff and Glue," the NME, and Melody Maker, and was used by Malcolm McLaren. "The phrase New Wave had been bandied about for a few years. Some critics used it to describe music that wasn't punk, but by listening to it, you could tell that punk had happened."
**Sire Records Marketing Campaign (Early 1977)** : Sire Records, led by Seymour Stein, launched a marketing campaign in early 1977 called "Don't Call it Punk" to re-brand British punk rock that was evolving into something more melodic, less violent, and less political. They aimed to "blunt the negative perceptions" associated with punk by heavily promoting "New Wave" as a replacement term for "punk" and "underground." "And so in early 1977, Sire launched a marketing campaign called Don't Call it Punk and began leaning heavily on the term New Wave."
**Impact on British Bands in North America** : The "New Wave" marketing campaign was successful, establishing the term as a new umbrella for post-punk music, predominantly from Britain. This strategic linguistic shift by Sire Records opened up new opportunities and "vistas" for British bands in the North American market. "By simply altering the language of music marketing, Sire opened up whole new vistas for British bands in North America."

"In 1976, Jeff Travis took a trip across the US buying up all kinds of records and when he got home he didn't have anywhere to put them so he decided to start up a store with his record collection acting as inventory."
**Community Hub** : The Rough Trade store served as a community gathering place where people bought records, promoted fanzines, exchanged gossip, and advertised for musicians. The original shop remains open near Portobello Road in Notting Hill. "It also became a hangout for people into non-mainstream music. They bought records, they pushed fanzines, they swapped gossip and advertised for musicians."
**Transition to Record Label (1978)** : In 1978, Jeff Travis expanded Rough Trade into an independent record label. One of its earliest releases was "Alternative Ulster" by the Belfast band Stiff Little Fingers. "In 1978, Travis decided to jump into the indie record label game and one of the labels very earliest releases was by a bell fast band called Stiff Little Fingers."

**Consolidation of Record Labels** : Over time, the music industry saw a significant consolidation of record labels. In the early 1960s, there were at least 3,000 independent labels in America alone. As the industry matured, bigger labels either acquired smaller ones, merged, or went out of business, reducing the number of major labels to six or seven by the 1970s, and eventually down to just three (Universal, Sony, Warner) in the present day. "In the early 1960s, there were at least 3,000 such labels in existence in America alone. But as the music industry grew, bigger labels either swallowed up smaller ones merged with each other or just plain went out of business."
**Indie Labels Driving Music Forward** : Despite major labels receiving most of the attention and money, independent labels were frequently responsible for pushing music forward. While outsiders might view them as picking up "the drags" (artists rejected by majors), indie labels were, in fact, "investing in the future" by taking chances on music that no one else would touch, thereby providing an outlet for experimental and underground sounds. "And while the major labels and major label artists got most of the attention and the money, the underclass of indie labels were often responsible for driving music forward."
**Evolution of Underground Music** : Independent labels fostered the "experimental, low-fi, developed underground" music scene. This underground gave rise to proto-power pop groups like Jonathan Richman and the Modern Lovers and punk bands such as the Ramones. These bands, in turn, influenced acts like the Sex Pistols, leading to the formation of hundreds, if not thousands, of new bands in the UK. Simultaneously, Island Records, through Bob Marley, exported reggae globally. "The walked out experimental low five developed underground, which begat the proto-power pop of groups like Jonathan Richmond and the modern lovers and punks like the Ramones, both of which found favor with the sex pistols who then spurred the formation of hundreds of not thousands of bands in the UK."
**1970s British Indie Culture** : By the end of the 1970s, labels like Rough Trade, Stiff, and Virgin were central to indie culture in Britain. These companies were small, nimble, and privately owned, allowing them to quickly record and react to new music. Despite often being underfunded, financial constraints sometimes forced them to be more creative with revenue generation. "Rough Trade, Stiff, Virgin and dozens of others were at the center of indie culture and Britain at the end of the 1970s."
