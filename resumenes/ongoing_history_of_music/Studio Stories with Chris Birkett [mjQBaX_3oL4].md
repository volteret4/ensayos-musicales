## curiosities
**Early Career Mentorship with Tony Visconti** : Chris Berkett signed a deal for a couple of albums with Tony Visconti and his band Omaha Sheriff. While in the studio, Berkett constantly asked Visconti questions due to his electronics degree, learning how to make records by observing him. "I learned how to make records by watching Tony."
**Designing and Building Tapestry Studios** : After leaving Omaha Sheriff and working as a milkman, Chris Berkett was asked by John Kongos to design and build his new studio in London, Tapestry Studios, leveraging his electronics degree. "Would you like to come and design and build my new studio?"
**First Recording Experience with Tape Handling Error** : As an engineer at Tapestry Studios, Chris Berkett's first recording session involved a big horn section (possibly for "Geno" by Dexys Midnight Runners). He mistakenly put the 24-track, two-inch tape on backward, resulting in a "wooly" sound, learning from the error without prior instruction. "I realized I put the tape on back to front. No, nobody told me."
**Pioneering African Samples with "Afro-Dizzy Act"** : Chris Berkett wrote "Afro-Dizzy Act," which became a huge club hit in England and was notable as the first song to use African samples, well before others adopted the technique. "that's the first song to use, African samples. It's way before anybody else did it."
**Daughter's Singing Opportunity with Bob Geldof** : Chris Berkett's then 16-year-old daughter had the opportunity to sing on one of Bob Geldof's records. "My 16-year-old daughter at the time sang on one of his records, which is a great opportunity for her."
**Producer's Role as Liaison** : Chris Berkett defines a music producer's role as a liaison between the artist and the record company, bridging creative expression with financial needs and ensuring the project is radio-friendly and sellable. "I see a producer's role as liaison between the artist and the record company."
**Multi-Instrumentalist Producer** : Chris Berkett became a multi-instrumentalist (piano, bass, drums, guitar) to effectively communicate with musicians in the studio, often by demonstrating desired grooves or parts directly on the instrument rather than solely relying on written scores or verbal explanations. "I had to learn piano, bass, drums, and play, and then to play everything so that I could communicate with the musicians that are making the records in the right way."
**Patience in the Studio** : As a producer, Chris Berkett emphasizes the need for enormous patience, citing an experience with the Buena Vista Social Club trumpet player where 250 takes were recorded for a solo, requiring extensive editing. "You have to sit there sometimes for hours."
**Capturing Performance Over Technical Perfection** : Chris Berkett prioritizes capturing a spontaneous, authentic performance over meticulous technical adjustments. He uses a single, trusted microphone (AKG 414 B-ULS) and avoids excessive tweaking during recording sessions to ensure artists aren't exhausted and crucial moments aren't missed. "I want to capture performance, like, like nothing compares to you, and she probably knows she needs song, that was a one take vocal."
**Engineer's Role as Unsung Hero** : Chris Berkett regards engineers as "unsung heroes" responsible for the technical aspects of recording, from capturing source sounds to mixing and balancing. He was a "creative musical engineer" and highly sought after in London during the 1980s. "An engineer. They're the kind of... The unsung heroes of music, in my opinion."
**Accidental "Perfect" Take** : While engineering a session for Trevor Horn with session pianist Nick Piotis on a Steinway Grand, Chris Berkett was tweaking the EQ during the take, not intending to record the final version. Trevor Horn declared it "the take," forcing Berkett to meticulously reverse his real-time EQ changes to save the performance, which taught him not to interfere with the performance itself. "I said, well, we can't use that. I was changing all the EQ. I was filling with the sound while it was... And he goes, well, that's your problem. We're going out for dinner. You fix it."
**Preference for Melodyne for Vocal Tuning** : Chris Berkett prefers Melodyne over Auto-Tune for vocal tuning due to its transparency. He uses it sparingly to correct only specific, irritating notes in an otherwise good performance, leaving the majority of the vocal natural, as he did with Buffy St. Marie. "I use a program called Melodyne, which is the most transparent tuning program I've ever discovered."

# artist - Def Leppard

## albums
**Pyromania (1983)** : This album, produced by Mutt Lange, was incredibly expensive to make, costing around $3 million. Chris Berkett was responsible for programming all the drums on a Fairlight CMI, a process that took six weeks of meticulous typing in musical code for every single event. "not wonder that album cost something like $3 million to make."

## instruments
Fairlight (for drum programming on Pyromania)

## curiosities
**Drum Programming Challenges on "Pyromania"** : For the album "Pyromania," producer Mutt Lange insisted on the latest technology for drums. Chris Berkett spent six weeks programming all the drums on a Fairlight CMI. This pre-MIDI process required converting the drummer's demo tapes into musical score and then typing every single event into the monophonic Fairlight using a mathematical music composition language. "And I had to write all that stuff out in score. Because it had to be converted to mathematical music language."
**Meticulous Drum Production on "Pyromania"** : The drum programming for "Pyromania" was an incredibly meticulous process. Mutt Lange would spend half a day listening to a single hi-hat sound against a click track, checking if it was "a bit ahead, a bit behind," before approving it. "We would listen literally to a hi hat against the click for half a day."
**Repeated Drum Recording Due to Tape Wear on "Pyromania"** : The drums for "Pyromania" had to be re-recorded three separate times from the original Fairlight floppy disks. This was because the analog 24-track master tapes, on which guitars and vocals were overdubbed, wore out through repeated transfers to new tapes, forcing the producer to request "first generation drums" again. "We've worn out three tapes doing all the guitars and the vocals. And they transferred... It wasn't digital in those days. It was analog. They transferred 24 track onto another new one because it was getting thin."

# instrument - Fairlight CMI

## curiosities
**First Sampling Machine** : The Fairlight was an Australian machine, recognized as the first sampling machine. "It was the first sampling machine."
**Limited Memory and Monophonic** : The Fairlight was monophonic, meaning it could only handle one sound at a time, and had very limited memory, possibly around 20K. Samples were stored on large floppy disks. "It was monophonic, sampler. And I think it had, what, 20K of memory?"
**Music Composition Language (MCL)** : Before MIDI, the Fairlight used Music Composition Language, requiring users to write complex code to define sounds, their duration, and placement in a musical sequence. For drum programming, specific numbers in brackets would dictate where a kick drum event, for example, would occur within a bar divided into 48 segments. "in the early days, it was called music composition language. So you had to... There's a mono machine. So you can only get one sound in it at a time. You had to write a whole code for... To identify the sound and how long sound would play and where it played."
**High Cost** : The Fairlight CMI was extremely expensive; the one at Tapestry Studios cost 60,000 pounds, translating to almost twice that in dollars. "The one that we had at Tapestry Studio. It was cost 60,000 pounds."

# instrument - 24-track, two-inch tape

## curiosities
**Analog Recording Medium** : This was a common multi-track recording system in the past, allowing for 24 separate audio tracks to be recorded on a two-inch wide magnetic tape. "when I was doing this stuff, it was a two-inch tape. You only had 24 tracks."
**Limited Usable Tracks** : In practice, only 23 tracks were usable for audio, as one track was often reserved for a code to drive or sync another machine. "And one of them had a code on it to drive another machine. So you could only use 23 tracks."
**Tape Wear Issue** : Analog tape, specifically 24-track, two-inch tape, could wear out with repeated playbacks and transfers, leading to degradation of the recorded sound. This necessitated transferring the content to new tapes, as seen during the production of Def Leppard's "Pyromania." "They transferred 24 track onto another new one because it was getting thin. The tape was wearing out."

# curiosity

**Recording Studio's Importance** : The recording studio is likened to "the kitchen" of music, where the magic of music creation and recording happens, prompting questions about roles like producers and engineers, and the evolution of technology. "Like a lot of music fans, I am fascinated by what goes on in the kitchen. That is to say, the recording studio."
**Artists as "Vehicles" for Songwriting** : Chris Berkett believes that many well-known artists, including Sting and Peter Gabriel, perceive their songs as "coming through them" rather than being consciously written, suggesting artists are "vehicles" for a larger creative energy. "every one of them has admitted that they don't write their songs, they come through them."
**Analogue vs. Digital Sound Quality Debate** : Chris Berkett acknowledges a sonic difference between analog and high-resolution digital recordings, citing a personal experience where vinyl (analog) filled a room more robustly than a CD (digital) of the same mix, which sounded "smaller." "The vinyl recording just filled the room. The CD, when you play this, it just seemed much smaller."
**Technical Explanation of Analog Superiority** : Technically, magnetic tape is believed to precisely capture the "energy flow" of a sound wave by altering tiny magnets on the tape. Digital technology, however, chops the wave into segments (e.g., 44,100 steps per second), resulting in a "step" rather than a continuous sine wave, which Berkett suggests humans can "feel" as a difference even if not consciously "heard." "magnetic tape captures precisely the energy flow that you're putting into it."
**Practical Drawbacks of Analog Recording** : Despite potential sonic advantages, Chris Berkett avoids analog recording for general studio work because it is "really expensive" and "murder to edit." He would only consider it for live recordings or "off the floor" sessions where minimal editing is anticipated. "I always steer away from it. I get really expensive. And be it's murder to edit."
**John Bonham's Minimalist Drum Miking Technique** : Led Zeppelin drummer John Bonham preferred a minimalist approach to recording his drums, disliking microphones placed directly on his kit. Producers would typically use a single microphone placed in a "nice room" (like a slate or stone room, or a stairwell) to capture the entire kit's natural sound, compressed and EQ'd, which defined his signature drum sound. "So the producer has to put one mic in a room. And they use a nice room, like a slate room or stone room. Or stairwell."
**Impact of Digital Perfection on Music Emotion** : Chris Berkett expresses concern that modern records, made "too precise and too perfect" by technologies like Auto-Tune and quantization, lack emotional impact. He argues that slight imperfections, such as a drummer slightly dragging behind the beat, create excitement and "vibe." "a lot of records now, they're too precise and too perfect. And when something is too perfect, it's because of auto tune, because of quantization, because of all the things that you can do to fix the slightest imperfection."
**Sound Design vs. Emotional Connection in Modern Music** : Berkett contrasts modern "sound design," which he finds intellectually impressive, with music that affects emotions. He feels that while sound design can be technically amazing, it often fails to connect deeply with listeners on an emotional level. "For me, a lot of music today is about sound design. Now, sound design is very impressive on an intellectual level... But it doesn't actually affect the emotions."
**Philosophical View on Perfection in Art** : Chris Berkett suggests that perfection, especially in music, can be "dead" because it leaves "nowhere to go." He believes that the "reaching for perfection" and the evolutionary process involved in striving for the best is what truly helps art, and music, to evolve, as long as imperfections aren't completely ironed out. "And on a philosophical level, if something is perfect, then it's actually dead. It's nowhere to go, right?"
**Analogue Tape as Energy Flow** : Magnetic tape captures the precise energy flow of music, reproducing the original sine wave by altering tiny magnets on the tape. "magnetic tape captures precisely the energy flow that you're putting into it. All music's made a sign wave, right?"
**Digital Audio as Chopped Wave Segments** : Digital technology converts sound waves into discrete segments, typically 44,100 steps per second, which means it represents a "step" rather than a continuous sine wave. "But digital technology, the wave has to be chopped up into segments. So you're not really getting a sign wave, you're getting like a step."

# genre - Indian Music
## curiosities
**32-Note Scale System** : Indian music employs a 32-note scale system, which contrasts significantly with the 12-note scale used in Western music. While this unique tuning may sound "out of tune" to unaccustomed Western ears, it is considered exciting and deeply resonant within its cultural context. "Indian music, they use 32 notes. And to us, it sounds out of tune, but to them it feeds them. It gives them excitement."

# instrument - Voice
## curiosities
**Natural Vocal Pitch Nuance** : For a human voice to sound natural and not artificial, it should not consistently hit perfect pitch. Instead, slight deviations, often described as being "a bit below the perfect pitch," are preferred, as perfect pitch can make a voice sound like a machine or a musical instrument rather than a human voice. "you don't want to be hammering it out into the perfect pitch, because then it's going to sound like a machine, right? Like a musical instrument, not a voice anymore."
