# curiosity

**Stereo Technology's Impact on Recorded Music Quality** : For over 60 years, advancements in stereo technology have enabled recorded music to achieve a level of audio quality that is often superior to live performances. These principles and techniques have transformed how music is experienced by listeners, providing a highly immersive sound. "For the last 60 plus years, technology has relied on a set of principles and techniques that allow recorded music to sound exponentially better than 99% of live performances, at least in terms of audio quality."
**Immersive Experience of Stereo** : Listening to music in stereo offers a deeply immersive experience, allowing listeners to visualize the placement of performers and instruments as if present at a live performance. This sense of space and direction greatly enhances engagement with recorded sound. "Listening to music this way is a totally immersive experience."
**Etymology of "Stereo"** : The word "stereo" originates from the Greek word meaning "solid," reflecting the intention to create a more three-dimensional and realistic sound experience. "The word stereo comes from the Greek word for solid."
**Definition of Stereophonic Sound** : Stereophonic sound is characterized by the use of two or more independent audio channels for reproducing sound. This multi-channel approach allows audio to emanate from more than one source, enhancing the spatial quality of the music. "Stereophonic sound means the use of two or more channels when it comes to reproducing sound."
**Multi-source Audio in Stereo** : In stereophonic sound, audio is reproduced from multiple sources, typically left and right channels, each carrying slightly different information. This differentiation is crucial for creating a perception of space and direction for the listener. "The audio comes from more than one source, and this is the way most of us enjoy music."
**Channel Differentiation in Stereo** : A fundamental aspect of stereo is that the audio signals delivered to the left and right channels are subtly different. This slight variation in sound information reaching each ear is what allows the brain to perceive a spatial quality and enhance the overall listening experience. "The audio coming from the left channel is slightly different than what we hear from the right channel."
**Spatial Quality of Stereo** : Stereo sound enhances the listening experience by imparting a spatial quality to the music. This allows listeners to perceive the location and movement of sounds within a soundstage, making the audio experience more dynamic and realistic. "This gives the music a spatial quality and enhances the overall experience of listening to music."
**Early Recorded Music Format (Mono)** : When Thomas Edison introduced the phonograph, his "talking machine," in 1877, all recorded music was monophonic, or "mono." This meant only one channel of audio was captured and reproduced, severely limiting the spatial information available to listeners. "In the beginning, which is to say starting with the introduction of Thomas Edison's talking machine, the phonograph, and 1877, all recorded music was in mono or monophonic."
**Mono Sound Characteristics** : Monophonic sound is characterized by having only a single audio channel, meaning there is no perceptible difference between the primary and secondary sounds reaching a listener's ears. This lack of differentiation results in a flat, non-spatial listening experience. "There's only one channel. There's no difference between the primary and secondary sounds entering our ears."
**Limitations of Mono** : Due to its single-channel nature, mono sound fails to provide sufficient psychoacoustic information for the human ears and brain to create a natural and realistic listening experience. This deficiency prevents the perception of depth, movement, or clear separation of individual sound elements. "As a result, our ears and our brains do not receive enough psychoacoustic information to create a natural sounding and realistic listening experience."
**Advantages of Stereo over Mono** : Stereo offers significant advantages over mono, including enhanced clarity, the ability to perceive and feel reverb, and the sensation of musicians or music moving across a sound stage. These elements contribute to a much more immersive and realistic listening experience that mono cannot provide. "With stereo, there's more clarity, an ability to feel and hear reverb, and we can sense if the music or the musicians are moving across the sound stage, not so with mono."
**Clement Adder's Stereophonic Studies** : Clement Adder, a French engineer, was the first individual to extensively research and implement the concept of stereophonic sound. His pioneering work in 1881 laid the groundwork for future developments in multi-channel audio transmission. "The first person to really study the idea of stereophonic sound was Clement Adder, an engineer working in Paris."
**Clement Adder's Tiatrophone Project (1881)** : In 1881, Clement Adder developed a special telephone transmission system known as the Tiatrophone. This innovative project aimed to transmit live opera and theater performances directly to subscribers' homes via telephone lines. "He was working on a special sort of telephone transmission in 1881."
**Purpose of the Tiatrophone** : The Tiatrophone's primary purpose was to deliver live opera and theater performances from public venues directly to private residences. This system essentially created an early form of remote, immersive entertainment for its subscribers. "The idea was to run telephone lines from an opera house or a theater to somebody's home."
**Tiatrophone Technical Setup** : Clement Adder's Tiatrophone system utilized 80 telephone transmitters, acting as microphones, positioned across the front of an opera stage. These transmitters were then grouped into two channels, 40 for each, to create a stereophonic effect for remote listeners. "Adder spaced 80 telephone transmitters, the microphones from telephones, across the front of the stage, and then tied them all together into two channels, 40 for one and 40 for the other."
**Tiatrophone User Experience** : Subscribers to the Tiatrophone service could connect to the direct lines from their homes and use primitive earphones to hear a reasonably accurate sonic representation of the live performance. This provided an early, albeit basic, form of stereo listening. "People in their homes using these direct lines could be then connected and using primitive earphones here are reasonably accurate sonic representation of what the audience was hearing."
**Tiatrophone as Wired Radio and its Target Audience** : The Tiatrophone functioned as a wired radio service, complete with a subscription fee. Its high cost made it exclusively a luxury item, primarily enjoyed by the upper classes, indicating its initial status as a premium entertainment option. "It was essentially cable or wired radio, complete with a subscription fee, and it wasn't cheap, so this was mainly a plaything for the upper classes."
**Tiatrophone's Success** : Despite its high cost and niche market, the Tiatrophone proved to be highly successful among the upper classes. Its innovative approach to remote entertainment was well-received by those who could afford it. "But still, among those folks, it was wildly successful."
**Tiatrophone's Geographic Expansion** : Following its success in Paris, the Tiatrophone technology rapidly spread to other European countries, including Belgium, Germany, Hungary, and the UK. This expansion showcased the early demand for remote, high-fidelity audio experiences. "The technology spread to Belgium, Germany, Hungary, and the UK."
**Tiatrophone Experiments in the US** : Experiments with the wired radio system, similar to the Tiatrophone, also took place in the United States. These trials involved broadcasting events from prominent venues such as Madison Square Garden, indicating an early interest in widespread audio transmission. "There were even experiments with this wired radio in the United States involving broadcasts from Madison Square Garden."
**Tiatrophone's Longevity** : The wired Tiatrophone system enjoyed a surprisingly long period of operation. It persisted in use for several decades, even continuing to function well after the advent of wireless radio broadcasting. "And the wired Tiatrophone did well for longer than you might think."
**Tiatrophone's Disappearance (1932)** : The companies behind the Tiatrophone system finally ceased operations in 1932, a full twelve years after wireless radio broadcasting had begun to gain widespread popularity. This marks the end of a significant early chapter in remote audio entertainment. "It wasn't until 1932, a dozen years after radio started taking off, that the companies that were pushing this thing finally disappeared."
**Origin of "Wireless" Term for Radio** : The existence and eventual obsolescence of wired radio systems like the Tiatrophone contributed to the coining of the term "wireless" to describe conventional radio broadcasting. This distinction highlighted radio's key advantage of not requiring physical cables to transmit signals. "And now, you know, one of the reasons why people used to call the radio the wireless."
**Tiatrophone's Unique Stereo Offering** : For a considerable period, the Tiatrophone system offered one of the only available forms of stereo listening. Its innovative two-channel setup provided a listening experience that was largely unmatched by other recording or transmission technologies of the era. "The Tiatrophone may have offered a version of stereo listening, but that was about the only version out there."
**Limitations of Early Recording Formats** : Early recording technologies, such as Thomas Edison's cylinders and Emil Berliner's rotating discs, had severe limitations. They could only capture audio directly funneled into recording horns, resulting in the loss of all spatial attributes of a performance and generally terrible audio quality. "Thomas Edison's cylinders and Emil Berliner's rotating discs could only capture and record the audio that came at the recording horns."
**Mechanics of Early Recording** : The process of early sound recording involved sound waves being funneled through horns to a diaphragm. A stylus, connected to the diaphragm, would vibrate in response to the sound, and these vibrations were then mechanically transferred to a continuous groove cut into either a cylinder or a rotating disc. "These horns funneled sound down to a diaphragm, connected to the diaphragm was a stylus that vibrated with the sound."
**Poor Audio Quality of Early Recordings** : Early recording methods, which involved funnelling sound into horns and then mechanically cutting grooves, resulted in the loss of all spatial information and generally produced audio of very low quality. This made the listening experience far from lifelike. "All the spatial attributes of the performance were lost, and the audio quality was terrible."
**78-RPM Disc as Standard (improved audio)** : Audio quality saw significant improvement after the 10-inch, 78-RPM rotating disc became the standard recording format. **Cook Binaural Clip Conversion** : The Cook Binaural Clip Conversion was one of the first post-WWII attempts to bring stereo to records. This system required two stylized cartridges and two tone arms, with each needle tracking a separate groove for the left and right channels. "The first attempt was the Cook Binarol Clip Conversion, which involved two stylized, in two cartridges and two tone arms."
**Cook Binaural Clip Conversion Mechanism** : The Cook Binaural Clip Conversion system featured two separate needles; one was designed to fit into a groove dedicated to the left channel, and the other into a groove for the right channel. Each channel was then routed to its own amplifier and speaker, ensuring channel separation. "One needle fit into a groove for the left channel, and the other dropped into the groove for the right channel."
**Cook Binaural Clip Conversion Playback** : In the Cook Binaural Clip Conversion system, each of the two distinct audio channels was independently sent to its own amplifier. These amplifiers then powered individual speakers, resulting in a clear separation of the left and right audio streams. "Each channel was sent to its own amplifier, which in turn drove its own speaker."
**Benefits of Cook Binaural Clip Conversion** : The Cook Binaural Clip Conversion system successfully achieved left and right channel separation, leading to a larger, louder sound image. It also offered improved fidelity, better frequency response, and a greater sense of realism compared to previous mono recordings. "The result was left and right channel separation, creating a sound image that was bigger, louder, with more fidelity, better frequency response, and more realism."

**Negative Effects of Duplex Misalignment** : If the styluses in a duplex recording system were not perfectly aligned, one channel would play slightly behind or ahead of the other. This misalignment resulted in a distorted and unpleasant listening experience, undermining the intended stereo effect. "If you got it wrong, one channel would be slightly behind or slightly ahead of the other, and that didn't sound good."
**Limited Capacity of Duplex Recordings** : A major drawback of duplex recordings was their limited music capacity. "The bad news is that you didn't get much music on each side of a record, because, well, you needed twice as many grooves, and that took up twice as much space."

**RCA's Stereo Showcase Component (1958)** : As part of its major stereo product launch in June 1958, RCA displayed seven different types of high-end speakers. These speakers were integral to delivering the enhanced sound quality and spatial imaging that stereo technology promised. "RCA showed off 55 records along with 15 new phonographs and seven different types of high end speakers."

# instrument - Westrex cutter

## curiosities
**Key to Stereo LPs (1957)** : In 1957, Westrex developed a revolutionary cutter that could carve a unique 45-degree angled groove, shaped like a valley, into vinyl. This innovation was the crucial element that enabled the creation of widely compatible and effective stereo LPs. "They built a cutter that carved a 45 degree groove, a little valley, in the vinyl."

# instrument - 7-inch single (stereo)

## curiosities
**Introduction (May 1959)** : Stereo 7-inch singles first emerged in May 1959, bringing the new two-channel audio format to the popular single record market. This development contributed significantly to the rapid obsolescence of the older 78 RPM singles. "The next development came with the Stereo 7-inch single, they first appeared in May 1959."

# instrument - balance controls

## curiosities
**Listener Adjustment in Early Stereo** : In the early experimental phase of stereo mixing, engineers sometimes employed extreme panning, expecting listeners to use their stereo system's balance controls. The idea was that users would adjust these controls to find their preferred or most pleasing stereo mix. "The thinking was that listeners would use their balance controls to find the most pleasing stereo mix."
