# genre - Can Rock

## curiosities
**Definition and Origin** : "Can Rock" is a term that emerged with the book *Have Not Been The Same: The Can Rock Renaissance* (1985-1995) to describe Canada's significant contribution to Alt Rock. It represents a "huge burst of new acts" in the 1990s that never ceased, encompassing a broad range of Canadian music from The Tragically Hip to Sarah McLachlan and Sloan to the Cowboy Junkies. "Can Rock is a term that came into use with a book called Have Not Been The Same, subtitled The Can Rock Renaissance, which detailed for the first time what happened to music in this country between 1985 and 1995."
**Revolution of the 1990s** : The "Can Rock Revolution" describes the transformation of Canadian music in the 1990s, where a robust industry, funding, media support, and a supportive Generation X audience led to a boom in high-quality Canadian artists achieving domestic and international success. "the Can Rock Revolution of the 90s."
**Enduring Momentum** : The momentum generated by the Can Rock Revolution in the 1990s "never faded," leading to an "insanely healthy" Canadian music scene where artists continue to make great music, export globally, and receive strong domestic support. "But the momentum created by the Can Rock Revolution never faded."

# concert - Edgefest

## curiosities
**Pioneering Canadian Rock Festival** : The first Edgefest show in 1987 pioneered the concept of a mostly Canadian summer rock music festival. By the early 1990s, it became an annual event, expanding to four festivals in 1995, and then national (1997-1999) playing eight cities with predominantly Canadian lineups (Our Lady Peace, I Mother Earth, Finger Eleven, Gob, Sloan, Matt Good, The Tea Party), while also featuring international acts like Green Day and Foo Fighters. "The idea of an all-Canadian, or at least mostly Canadian, summer-all-rock music festival, and we traced back to the first Edgefest show in 1987."

# curiosity

**Canada's Music Market Size** : Canada is the sixth largest music market globally, despite having the smallest population (36 million) among the top six nations (US, Japan, UK, Germany, France). Its domestic music industry isn't protected by language barriers like Japan, Germany, or France, as English-language music is consumed worldwide. Canada exports significantly more music than its size would suggest, and has a strong domestic market where Canadians support homegrown talent. "Canada is the sixth largest music market in the world."
**Canadian Music Export Success** : Canada consistently increases its music export numbers annually, thanks to contemporary stars like Drake, The Weeknd, Justin Bieber, and Alessia Cara, as well as earlier artists such as Alanis Morissette, Sarah McLachlan, Celine Dion, Shania Twain, Rush, and The Guess Who. "Canada exports far more music to the rest of the world than we should."
**Canadian Pride in Homegrown Talent** : Canadians strongly support and take pride in their domestic music, as evidenced by the "national outpouring of affection for the Tragically Hip in the summer of 2016." "Canadians listen to and support Canadian music, and the country tends to be very proud of its homegrown talent."
**Past Perception of Canadian Music** : Before the 90s, Canadian music was often seen as "substandard, or not very good, or bad," with Canadians avoiding it unless it was validated by US music fans. This attitude is now "pretty much extinct." "There was a time when Canadian music was a synonym for substandard, or not very good, or bad."
**Can Rock Revolution Origins** : The roots of Canada's current musical nationalism and strong domestic support can be traced back to the Alt Rock 90s and what is termed the "Can Rock Revolution." "And the roots of our current musical nationalism, in many ways, can be traced back to the days of the Alt Rock 90s."
**Canadian Cultural Identity Challenge** : Canada faces a challenge in maintaining a distinct cultural identity due to its proximity to the United States, which has ten times its population and is a major economic engine, sharing similar values, experiences, and language. The solution involved cultural protection measures. "How could Canadians maintain a separate, distinct, and strong cultural identity when our neighbor to the south has ten times the population and is the biggest economic engine on the planet?"
**Cancon (Canadian Content) System** : Cancon is a quota system enacted in Canada to protect its culture from foreign influence, specifically requiring radio and TV broadcasters to allocate a percentage of their content to material "at least partly created by Canadians, written, produced, presented, or contributed to by Canadians." While controversial, it's also been very helpful. "The answer was to create a series of laws and measures that protect Canadian culture from being overrun by outside forces. And some of those laws and measures involve music."
**International Content Quota Systems** : Canada is not unique in having content quota systems; similar rules exist in Australia, New Zealand, Mexico, South Africa, Nigeria, France, Ireland, Jamaica, Venezuela, Israel, and the Philippines. The United States and the UK do not have such laws, the US due to its size and the UK due to its unique English-speaking isolation. "Many countries have these quota systems."
**Pre-1970 Canadian Music Industry** : Before 1970, Canada lacked a significant music industry infrastructure, with most record labels being foreign branches and few recording studios, promoters, or managers. This led promising Canadian artists like Neil Young, Joni Mitchell, Paul Anka, and Leonard Cohen to seek opportunities in the United States. "Before 1970, there really wasn't any sort of music industry in Canada, at least compared to many other countries."
**RPM (Radio and Records Industry Publication)** : RPM was a radio and records industry publication launched in 1964 by Walt Gralas to keep the Canadian music industry informed from coast to coast during the pre-internet era. "RPM was started in 1964 as a way to inform the radio and music industry about what was going on from coast to coast."
**Gold Leaf Awards** : Established by Walt Gralas and Stan Klees through RPM magazine to recognize Canadian musical achievement. The first awards were announced on December 28, 1964, with Terry Black named Top Male Vocalist and Shirley Matthews as Top Female. The initial event was a modest wine and cheese reception with 39 attendees, and notably, no rock category. "RPM also established something called the Gold Leaf Awards."
**Pre-Cancon Radio Landscape** : Before Cancon, Canadian radio stations predominantly played international hits, and Canadian records rarely charted well unless they were already hits abroad. This lack of popularity meant little money for Canadian music, and record labels largely ignored homegrown talent. "There was no money to be made in Canadian music."
**Pierre Juneau** : Pierre Juneau, influenced by Prime Minister Pierre Trudeau, served as the head of the CRTC, a government body responsible for broadcasting and telecommunications. He proposed and oversaw the implementation of Canada's first content rules for radio. "Then along came Pierre Zuno, a product of a Jesuit school who had the same sort of social activist tendencies as his friend Prime Minister Pierre Trudeau."
**CRTC (Canadian Radio-television and Telecommunications Commission)** : The CRTC is a Canadian government body responsible for broadcasting and telecommunications issues. Under its head, Pierre Juneau, it implemented the first Canadian content rules for radio. "He was the head of a new organization called the CRTC, a government body entrusted with looking after the broadcasting and telecommunications issues of the country."
**First Canadian Content Rules (1971)** : Implemented on January 1, 1971, these rules mandated that AM radio stations devote at least 30% of their music to Canadian artists. This was a dual strategy for cultural promotion and industrial development. Initially resisted by radio stations, some created "beaver hours" to meet quotas by playing short edits of Canadian songs late at night, a loophole later closed. "This gave birth to the first Canadian content rules for radio, which went into effect on January 1, 1971."
**Impact of Cancon Rules** : Despite initial resistance and concerns about quality in the early-to-mid 1970s, Cancon rules were a necessary step that led to increased radio exposure, popularity, and sales for Canadian acts. This, in turn, spurred the growth of record labels, recording studios, tours, managers, and agents, fostering a domestic music industry infrastructure. "Greater radio exposure turned into increased popularity of Canadian acts across the country."
**Juno Awards** : The Gold Leaf Awards were officially replaced by the Juno Awards, named after Pierre Juneau, the CRTC head who initiated the Cancon rules. The first Juno Awards event took place on February 23, 1970. "They were officially replaced by a new national Canadian awards show. And it was named The Junos after Pierre Junot, the CRTC head who came up with the Cancon rules."
**Cancon's Role in Artist Success (1970s)** : Although some early 1970s Canadian music played due to Cancon was considered subpar, the rules significantly boosted the careers of numerous Canadian rock bands, including Rush, April Wine, The Guess Who, Triumph, Crowbar, Teenage Head, Max Webster, Chilliwack, FM, Saga, and A Foot In Coldwater, many of whom achieved national and even international success. "the Cancon rules also gave a boost to an astounding array of homegrown talent."
**1980s Canadian Music Growth** : By the early 1980s, Canadian music consumption and sales increased significantly. Canadian musicians improved their sound, talent, and production quality, leading to the development of local scenes and opportunities for national and international success. By mid-80s, bands like Rush, April Wine, BTO, Loverboy, Triumph, and Bryan Adams achieved domestic and international multi-platinum success. "By the early 1980s, Canadians were listening to and buying much more Canadian music than they ever had."
**Canadian Music Industry Infrastructure (Mid-80s)** : By the mid-1980s, Canada had state-of-the-art recording studios, major record labels investing in local talent, and new organizations supporting artists. "There were state-of-the-art recording studios, domestic offices of major record labels were now investing in nurturing and promoting local Canadian talent."
**FACTOR (Foundation to Assist Canadian Talent on Records)** : Founded in 1982, FACTOR is an organization that provides millions of dollars in annual grant assistance to Canadian musicians, funded by broadcasters. "For example, there's Factor, the foundation to assist Canadian talent on records, founded in 1982."
**Starmaker** : Starmaker is an organization similar to FACTOR, established to foster new Canadian talent through financial assistance. "There's another similar organization called Starmaker."
**VideoFACT** : VideoFACT is an assistance organization created in 1984 as a condition of MuchMusic's broadcast license. It established a fund to help Canadian artists produce music videos, providing content for MuchMusic to play. "Then there's Video Fact, another assistance organization that was created when Chumlimated established much music in 1984."
**MuchMusic** : MuchMusic was established in 1984 by CHUM Limited. Its broadcast license required it to set up VideoFACT to fund Canadian music video production, which then provided content for the channel, giving Canadian artists significant exposure. "Chumlimated established much music in 1984."
**Canadian Content Development Funds (CCD Funds)** : Formerly called Canadian Talent Development Funds, these are legally mandated funds that radio stations and other broadcasters must contribute annually to the music community. They can range from tens of thousands to millions of dollars and are administered by independent third parties, funding initiatives such as music festivals (e.g., North by Northeast), contests, and local awards shows. "When a radio station is granted a license, they have to provide something called Canadian Talent Development Funds."
**1990s Canadian Music Landscape** : By the 1990s, the Canadian music industry was robust: artists received funding for records and videos, MuchMusic provided extensive video play, radio offered strong support, and the overall quality of music had significantly improved. "by the time we got to the 90s, all the pieces of the puzzle were finally settling into place."
**Generation X and Canadian Music** : Generation X, the "sons and daughters of the baby boom," came of age in the 1990s as the first generation with a mature Canadian music industry, having grown up with Cancon rules for almost 20 years and MuchMusic since 1983. They sought music reflecting their experiences during the first Gulf War and a recession, and found it in Canadian Alt Rock, fostering a new appreciation for homegrown talent. "This was the first generation in the country's history to enter their coming of age years with a mature Canadian music industry at their disposal."
**Factors in Can Rock Success (1990s)** : The rise of Can Rock in the 1990s was attributed to a combination of radio exposure, music video play (especially on MuchMusic), record company support, and a growing realization among fans that Canadian musicians were highly talented, a significant shift from earlier perceptions. "Radio exposure was video play, it was record company support. And most of all, a realization by fans that Canadian musicians were pretty damn good."
**Early 1990s Domestic and International Success** : Many Canadian bands in the early 1990s achieved success, with most carving out "nice livings as domestic artists." Some, like Crash Test Dummies, Alanis Morissette, and Our Lady Peace, also achieved significant international breakthroughs. "The early 90s were pretty amazing with a long list of Canadian bands getting a shot at prime time."
**Limited Music Access in 1990s** : In the pre-internet, pre-iTunes, pre-MP3 era of the 1990s, music access was "extremely limited." CD burners and blank CDs were expensive, hard drives were small, and creating mixtapes was time-consuming. Radio and MuchMusic were primary sources. "Access to music was extremely limited."
**MuchMusic's Monopoly (1990s)** : MuchMusic held a "virtual monopoly on the video market" in Canada because MTV was "banned in Canada, literally illegal." This, combined with content quotas, made Canada a "closed system" for music media, ensuring Canadian listeners a minimum of 30% Canadian content. "Much music had a virtual monopoly on the video market. MTV was banned in Canada, literally illegal."
**Virtuous Cycle of Cancon** : The once-controversial Cancon quotas created a "virtuous circle": guaranteed exposure to Canadian music led to increased demand, which in turn inspired more musicians, led to growth in recording studios, indie labels, managers, agents, promoters, and venues, ultimately generating more money within the Canadian music industry. "The once-hated quotas had created a virtuous circle."
**1990s Record Industry Boom** : The 1990s were the "golden age of the compact disc," characterized by high sales, crowded stores, and substantial profit margins for the record industry in Canada, fueled in part by the popularity of CD compilations. "This was the golden age of the compact disc. And stores were jammed, margins were high, sales were good, and this applied to Canada too."
**CD Compilations (1990s)** : CD compilations became "insanely popular" in the 1990s due to limited access to music (pre-internet, expensive CD burners). Radio stations collaborated with record labels to release these collections, which offered a mix of hits, mid-range, upcoming, and obscure acts. "The most successful of all the CD comps was the big shiny tune series from much music."
**Big Shiny Tunes Series (from MuchMusic)** : A highly successful series of rock CD compilations launched annually by MuchMusic starting in 1996, in partnership with major labels. Curators aimed for a balance of popular, mid-range, emerging, and obscure artists, selling hundreds of thousands of copies each in Canada. "The most successful of all the CD comps was the big shiny tune series from much music."
**Big Shiny Tunes 3 (1997) - Highest Selling Volume** : Released in December 1997, this was the highest-selling volume in the *Big Shiny Tunes* series and, for a period, the third highest-selling album in Canadian history since SoundScan began tracking sales in 1995. "The highest selling volume was the third, released in December of 1997. And for a while, it was the third highest selling album in Canadian history since soundscans started measuring sales in 1995."
**SoundScan** : SoundScan began measuring music sales in 1995, providing official sales data that confirmed *Big Shiny Tunes 3* as the third highest-selling album in Canadian history for a time. "since soundscans started measuring sales in 1995."
**Enduring Legacy of Can Rock Revolution** : The 1990s were a "very, very good period for Canadian music," and the momentum of the Can Rock Revolution continued, leading to an "insanely healthy" Canadian music scene. Many acts like The Tragically Hip, Sloan, Our Lady Peace, Sarah McLachlan, Tea Party, and Matt Good survived, while others like I Mother Earth, Rheostatics, and Lowest of the Low reformed due to public demand. "But the momentum created by the Can Rock Revolution never faded."
