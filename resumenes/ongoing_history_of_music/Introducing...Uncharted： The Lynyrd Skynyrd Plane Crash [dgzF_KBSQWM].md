## curiosities
**Formation and Early Names (1964)** : The band that would become Leonard Skinnerd was formed in Jacksonville, Florida, in 1964. For the remainder of the decade, they worked the North Florida circuit, playing schools and bars under various names, including My Backyard, Conquer the Worm, The Noble Five, and The One Percent. "The band that would become Leonard Skinnerd was formed in Jacksonville, Florida in 1964."
**Naming the Band (Late 1969)** : The name "Leonard Skinnerd" emerged sometime in late 1969. It was a riff on the name of a high school gym teacher who famously disliked men with long hair. "Leonard Skinnerd, a riff on the name of a high school gym teacher who hated long hair on men, appeared sometime in late 1969."
**Al Cooper's Involvement (1973)** : Al Cooper of Blood Sweat and Tears was greatly impressed by the band. He signed them and facilitated their entry into a recording studio, leading to the release of their debut album, *Pronounced 'Lěh-ńérd Śkin-ńérd*, in 1973. "Al Cooper of Blood Sweat and Tears was really impressed. He signed the band and put them in the studio."
**Substance Abuse and Car Accidents (September 1976)** : In September 1976, during Labor Day weekend, founding member Gary Rossington was involved in a severe car accident while driving his new Ford Torino on Mandarin Road in Jacksonville, passing out behind the wheel due to excessive alcohol and cocaine use. He knocked down a telephone pole, careened off an oak tree, and smashed into a house, causing thousands of dollars in damage. The same weekend, bandmate Allen Collins crashed into a Volkswagen after his own drug and alcohol binge. Both were fined $5,000 by Ronnie Van Zant, who subsequently wrote a song about the incidents. "Gary Rosington, one of the founding members, was in a bad car accident over Labor Day weekend in September 1976. Too much alcohol and cocaine."
**Ronnie Van Zant's Premonition** : Drummer Artemis Pyle recalled Ronnie Van Zant expressing a persistent "creepy feeling" about their fate. Van Zant told Pyle he believed he would "never live to see 30 and would go out with his boots on," signifying a death while on the road. "Drummer Artemis Pyle recalls Ronnie telling him that he would never live to see 30 and would go out with his boots on."
**Street Survivors Tour Launch (April 1977)** : In April 1977, the group began working on their fifth album, *Street Survivors*. As was their custom, immediately after finishing the album, they went on the road to promote the still-unreleased record, playing 35 dates throughout the spring and summer before a six-week break. "In April 1977, the group started to work on their fifth album, Street Survivors."
**Last Concert and Tour Plans (October 1977)** : The next leg of their tour commenced on October 13 in Statesboro, Georgia. The band had ambitious plans to play Madison Square Garden several weeks later, a long-held dream. Their final performance before the crash was on October 19, 1977, at the Greenville Memorial Auditorium in Greenville, South Carolina. "The next leg of the tour began on October 13th in statesboro, Georgia, and would eventually see the band realize their dreams by playing Madison Square Garden several weeks down the road."
**Chartered Plane - Convair 240** : For their tour, Leonard Skinnerd chartered a Convair 240, a twin-engine prop plane built in 1947 with registration number N55VM. The aircraft was owned by the LNJ company of Addison, Texas, and was a former commercial airliner with 29,000 flight hours, indicating its extensive prior use and age. "Along the way, Skinner chartered the use of a Converter 240, a twin engine prop plane built in 1947, registration number N55VM."
**Plane's Prior Rejection by Aerosmith** : The Convair 240 offered to Leonard Skinnerd had previously been offered to Aerosmith earlier that same year. Aerosmith's manager, Peter Rudge (who also managed Skinnerd), was aware that Aerosmith had rejected the plane due to its poor condition and freaked out by its state. "The Conver, which by the way had been offered to Erosmith earlier that year. But they were so freaked out by the plane's poor condition, they said, thanks, but no thanks."
**Band's Reputation and Charter Difficulties** : Leonard Skinnerd had a history of misbehavior on chartered aircraft, including an incident where someone reportedly attempted to throw a roadie out of the cabin at 13,000 feet. This reputation made it difficult for their manager, Peter Rudge, to secure suitable aircraft, leaving the Convair 240 as the best available option. "Skinner had a reputation of behaving badly on chartered aircraft. There's a story that someone once tried to toss a roadie out of the cabin at 13,000 feet."
**Band's Dissatisfaction with the Plane** : The band members were deeply unhappy with the Convair 240, recognizing it as "junk" and believing that a band of their stature should have a proper private jet, comparable to what Led Zeppelin used. They were also displeased with their manager, Peter Rudge, for arranging the flight "on the cheap" at $5,000 a month for three months, including crew. "The band was not happy. They knew the plane was junk."
**"Survivors Tour" Luggage Tags** : Foreshadowing the tragic events, Leonard Skinnerd had luggage tags made for their suitcases that all read "Survivors Tour." "Skinner had some luggage tags made up for their suitcases. And they all read Survivors Tour."
**Crew Impairment Allegations** : An Aerosmith road manager reported seeing Captain Walter McCurray and First Officer William Gray smoking and passing a bottle of Jack Daniels in the cockpit of the Convair 240. However, subsequent toxicology reports nullified accusations that the pilots were drinking or doing drugs during the flight or the night before the crash. "One of Erosmith's road managers saw Captain Walter McCurray and first officer William Gray smoking up and passing around a bottle of Jack Daniels in the cockpit."
**In-flight Engine Incident (October 18, 1977)** : On October 18, 1977, during a flight from Lakeland, Florida, to Greenville, the right engine of the Convair 240 shot out ten-foot flames at an altitude of 12,000 feet for several minutes. This alarming event led to the band's decision to ditch the Convair for a brand new Learjet upon reaching Baton Rouge for their October 20 gig at Louisiana State University. "On October 18th, the band flew into Greenville from Lakeland, Florida. At one point, ten-foot flames shot out of the right engine at an altitude of 12,000 feet for several minutes."
**Pilots' Assurances and Mechanic Decision** : Despite the engine incident, the flight crew maintained that the final leg to Baton Rouge would be safe. The plane's owners also assured safety but agreed to send a mechanic from their Dallas headquarters to Baton Rouge to inspect everything after the flight. A mechanic was not sent to Greenville, with stories circulating that the company simply couldn't afford it. "The flight crew maintained that it would be completely safe. The plane's owners said things would be fine, but agreed to send a mechanic from their headquarters in Dallas to Baton Rouge to inspect everything."
**Pre-flight Fears Among Band Members** : Several individuals associated with Leonard Skinnerd harbored significant fears about taking the fateful flight. Keyboardist Billy Powell's wife begged him not to board the plane, while backup singer Cassie Gaines, Steve Gaines' sister, initially wanted to travel with the equipment truck but was persuaded to fly. Guitarist Gary Rossington was "scared out of his mind," and backup singer JoJo Billingsley had a dream the night before that the plane crashed. "Keyboardist Billy Powell remembers his wife begging him not to get on the plane. Cassie Gaines, sister of Steve, wanted to ride with the band's equipment truck but was persuaded to fly."
**Ronnie Van Zant's Calm Demeanor** : Despite the widespread anxiety, the ultimate decision to take the flight rested with Ronnie Van Zant, who remained completely calm. When Gary Rossington expressed his worries, Van Zant philosophically replied, "hey, if the Lord wants you to die in this plane, when it's your time, it's your time. Let's go, man. **Final Flight Details (October 20, 1977)** : The Convair took off from Greenville at 5:02 PM Eastern Time on October 20, 1977, with 22 passengers, Captain McCurray, and First Officer Gray. They climbed to a cruising altitude of 12,000 feet for the approximately three-hour flight to Baton Rouge, initially carrying 5.5 hours' worth of fuel. "The Convair took off from Greenville at 502 Eastern Time, with 22 passengers and Captain McCurry and first officer Gray at the controls."
**In-flight Activities and Ronnie Van Zant's Sleep** : During the flight, some passengers played poker, while others drank and partied, celebrating that it would be their last flight on the old Convair. Ronnie Van Zant, exhausted from the previous night, was curled up with a pillow on the floor near the front of the cabin, trying to sleep after taking sleeping pills to block out the noise. "Ronnie was curled up with a pillow on the floor towards the front of the cabin, trying to get some sleep."
**Engine Failures and Emergency Communication (October 20, 1977)** : Around 6:40 PM, roughly halfway through the trip, the right engine, which had sounded problematic from the start, sputtered and died. At 6:42 PM, Captain McCurray contacted Houston Air Route Traffic Control, requesting the closest airport, and was directed to McCone Pike County Airport in McCone, Mississippi, about 17 kilometers behind them. Before the plane could turn, the left engine also failed, rendering hydraulics and steering inoperative. "Around halfway through the trip, at 6.40pm, the right engine, which didn't sound good to begin with, spotted and then died."
**Pilots' Announcement and Crash Preparation** : With both engines dead, the plane rapidly descended from 4,500 feet, leaving only the sound of wind over the fuselage. Captain McCurray informed the passengers, "We're out of gas," instructing them to adopt the crash position by putting their heads between their legs and buckling up tightly. "McCurry turned to his passengers. We're out of gas, he said. Put your head between your legs, in the crash position, and buckle up tight."
**Jean Odom's Outburst** : Amidst the unfolding disaster, security chief Jean Odom was remembered by some as yelling, "I hope you two sons of bitches lived through this so I can kill you both," directed at the pilots, reflecting the sheer terror and anger among the passengers. "Some people remember security chief Jean Odom yelling, I hope you two sons of bitches lived through this so I can kill you both."
**Ronnie Van Zant's Final Moments** : During the ten minutes the plane glided silently without power, Ronnie Van Zant, possibly disoriented by heavy-duty sleeping pills, got up from his spot, found another pillow, walked to the front of the cabin, and laid down without a seatbelt. Drummer Artemis Pyle recalled shaking his hand, smiling at each other, and Van Zant moving forward to sit down. "Ronnie was still unfazed. He got out from his spot in the aisle, found himself another pillow, walked back to the front of the cabin and laid down. No seatbelt."
**Plane Crash (October 20, 1977)** : At 6:52 PM, the Convair 240 carrying Leonard Skinnerd and a significant portion of their crew crashed into a forest near the Mississippi-Louisiana border, 60 miles from Baton Rouge, which should have been only ten minutes away. The plane, traveling at approximately 200 miles an hour, cut a 500-foot swathe through pine trees before splitting into three pieces—the cockpit and tail ripping away—and coming to a stop at 6:53 PM in a snake-infested mangrove swamp. "At 6.52pm, the Conveyor 240 carrying Leonard Skinnerd in a big chunk of their crew descended into a forest close to the Mississippi Louisiana border, 60 miles from Baton Rouge."
**Impact and Fatalities** : The crash was horrific; every seat was ripped from the floor and thrown forward. Ronnie Van Zant, at the front of the cabin, died from a single blunt force trauma to the head, crushed by the seats and debris from the back. Steve Gaines and assistant road manager Dean Kilpatrick were killed on impact. Backup singer Cassie Gaines bled out before help could arrive. Both pilots, McCurray and Gray, were found dead, still strapped in their seats and hanging upside down from a tree. "Ronnie Van Zantz, who had been at the front of the cabin, died of a single blunt force trauma to the head."
**Severe Injuries of Survivors** : Many survivors suffered catastrophic injuries. Guitarist Allen Collins broke two vertebrae in his neck and had a right arm so severely cut it nearly required amputation. Gary Rossington sustained two broken arms, legs, wrists, ankles, a punctured stomach and liver, and pelvis injuries. Bass player Leon Wilkeson endured a double fracture of his left leg, a compound fracture to his left arm, six broken ribs, a punctured lung, severe facial damage, and lost 15 teeth, with his heart stopping twice that night. Keyboardist Billy Powell crashed headfirst through a table, nearly ripping off his nose, and suffered a broken right knee and shattered ribs. "Those who weren't killed were badly injured."
**Leon Wilkeson's Dream** : After the crash, severely injured Leon Wilkeson recounted a dream where he spent time on a cloud-shaped log conversing with Ronnie Van Zant and Duane Allman of The Allman Brothers, who had died in a motorcycle crash six years earlier. In the dream, Ronnie told him, "boy, get yourself out of here. It's not your time yet." "He later recounted that he dreamed spending time on a cloud-shaped log talking with Ronnie Van Zant and Duane Alman of the Alman Brothers, who had died in a motorcycle crash six years earlier."
**Billy Powell's Rescue Efforts** : Despite shattered ribs, some of which were sticking out of his body, keyboardist Billy Powell, a former Marine, was determined to seek help. He waded through a creek, crawled under a barbed wire fence, and stumbled through a herd of cattle before encountering 22-year-old dairy farmer Johnny Moat. "But as a former Marine, he was determined to get help. He waited through a creek, crawled under a barbed wire fence, and stumbled through a herd of cattle, before encountering 22-year-old dairy farmer Johnny Moat."
**Discovery by Johnny Moat** : Dairy farmer Johnny Moat, who initially mistook the crash sound for a vehicle on a gravel road, later saw a helicopter circling and thought police were searching for escaped convicts. He found Billy Powell, sound engineer Ken Pieden, and roadie Mark Frank, and, believing them to be convicts, fired his shotgun into the air. The three men hit the dirt before explaining the actual plane crash. "Moat found Powell, along with sound engineer Ken Pieden and Rody Mark Frank, firing his shotgun into the air to warn these convicts to stay away that he meant business."
**Difficult Rescue Operation** : Rescue vehicles faced significant challenges reaching the crash site due to the thick forest and a creek, with the wreckage located at least a quarter-mile from the nearest road. Two bulldozers were deployed to create a path, and it took hours for police, the Coast Guard, and local EMS personnel to rescue the injured and remove the deceased. "Rescue vehicles tried to make their way to the crash site, but they had problems with thick forest and that creek."
**Identification Challenges** : Identifying the victims was complicated because many passengers had been playing poker before the crash, and their wallets, which were kept nearby, were scattered everywhere into the swamp. "Identifying everyone was difficult because remember, at the time of the crash, many of the passengers were playing poker. They'd had their wallets up and they'd been scattered everywhere into the swamp."
**Looting at the Crash Site** : Approximately 3,000 people descended upon the crash site, with a few attempting to help but the majority being looters. They stole anything they could, including suitcases, airplane seats, metal chunks, pillows, seatbelts, wallets, purses, and cash. Security chief Jean Odom, suffering from a broken neck and burns that blinded him in one eye, reported that looters took his watch, wallet, ring, and money. "The rest were looters. They grabbed whatever they could from the crash site."
**Hospital Overwhelm** : Most of the 20-plus survivors were transported to the Southwest Regional Medical Center in McComb, Mississippi, where over 30 doctors were called in. The hospital was so overwhelmed that its lobby had to be used to treat the injured, and the local high school gym was converted into a morgue. Others were taken to a hospital in Jackson, Mississippi. "The hospital was so overwhelmed that they had to use the lobby to treat the injured. The high school gym became the morgue."
**NTSB Investigation Findings (June 1978)** : Eight months after the crash, in June 1978, the National Transportation Safety Board (NTSB) ruled the probable cause of the accident as "fuel exhaustion and a total loss of power from both engines due to crew inattention to fuel supply." The report stated that the crew was "either negligent or ignorant of the increased fuel consumption because they failed to monitor adequately the engine instruments for fuel flow and fuel quantity." "The National Transportation Safety Board looked into things. And eight months later, in June 1978, it ruled that the probable cause of the accident was fuel exhaustion and a total loss of power from both engines due to crew inattention to fuel supply."
**Contributing Technical Factors to Fuel Exhaustion** : The problematic right engine was running in an "auto-rich" mode, consuming an excessive amount of fuel due to a "bagged magneto" that required more fuel than normal to start. The Convair's fuel mixture couldn't be adjusted independently for each engine, meaning both engines had to run rich. Although pilots believed they had over five hours of fuel, this rich mixture dwindled it to less than three hours, a factor they failed to account for. "This had been exacerbated by the troublesome right engine, the one that was seen shooting out 12-foot flames. It was running on auto-rich mode."
**Pilot Decision Errors** : There was discussion of landing in McComb, Mississippi, to allow the troublesome right engine to cool, but a fatal decision was made to continue the flight, as Baton Rouge was perceived to be only ten minutes away, whereas a refueling stop would take at least 45 minutes. One pilot reportedly attempted to transfer fuel to the dead right engine but may have accidentally dumped all remaining gas from the left engine instead. "There was talk of landing in McComb's Mississippi to let the engine cool, but a fatal decision was made to continue with the flight."
**Broken Fuel Gauge and Lack of Manual Check** : A significant factor in the fuel exhaustion was a non-functioning fuel gauge in the cockpit. The only way to ascertain the fuel load was to manually check the wing tanks with a dipstick. Apparently, no one thought to perform this crucial check before the plane departed from Greenville, despite a considerable 45 minutes spent trying to correct the fuel mixture in the heat. "The fuel gauge in the cockpit wasn't working, and the only way to check on fuel load was to go out to the wing tanks with a dipstick."
**Band's Shared Responsibility (Artemis Pyle's View)** : Drummer Artemis Pyle believed the band itself bore some responsibility for the crash. He stated that "a lot of people on the plane that knew something was wrong," but "we all kind of followed each other and that's where we made our mistake." "Artemis Piles, as the band itself, was responsible. There were a lot of people on the plane that knew something was wrong, he said. **Funerals and Burials** : Funerals for the victims began a week after the crash. Steve and Cassie Gaines were buried in Orange Park, Florida, though their graves were later vandalized, necessitating their relocation to a more secure site 23 years later, leaving their original tombs in Jacksonville Memory Gardens as empty memorials. Assistant road manager Dean Kilpatrick's grave is in Jacksonville, Florida. Ronnie Van Zant was laid to rest in Jacksonville with his favorite black hat and fishing pole, with about 150 people attending his funeral, including many Southern rockers. "The funerals began a week later. Steve and Cassie Gaines were buried in Orange Park, Florida."
**Charlie Daniels' Poem for Ronnie Van Zant** : At Ronnie Van Zant's funeral, Southern rocker Charlie Daniels read a poem he had written in tribute.
