# artist - Monty Norman

## songs
**James Bond Theme (1962) – Original Composition** : Monty Norman composed this theme, drawing inspiration from his earlier song, "Good Sign, Bad Sign," which featured Indian characters on the island of Trinidad in a musical based on the book "A House for Mr. Bizwaz" (also released around 1962). Norman adapted the melody by moving it to a 20-guitar. Although the film's producers initially disliked his version, composer John Barry later arranged it for orchestra, creating the classic rendition used in all Bond movies. Norman has continued to collect royalties for its use throughout his life, reportedly still cashing checks at 94 years old. "His inspiration was a song called Good Sign, Bad Sign, which is sung by Indian characters on the island of Trinidad in a musical based on a book called A House for Mr. Bizwaz."

## songs
**James Bond Theme (1962) – Orchestral Arrangement** : After Monty Norman's initial composition was rejected by producers, John Barry was brought in to arrange the melody for an orchestra. His arrangement transformed the theme into the iconic classic that has been featured in every James Bond film. This version set the standard for the franchise's musical identity. "So they gave it to composer John Barry, who arranged it for orchestra and boom, there's your classic."
**From Russia with Love (1963) – Instrumental Title Theme** : John Barry created the instrumental title theme for the 1963 James Bond film "From Russia with Love." While an instrumental, it served as the opening music for the movie. Barry contributed significantly to the film's score. "From Russia with Love in 1963 had an instrumental as a title theme done by Barry."
**Goldfinger (1964) – Co-writer** : John Barry had a hand in writing the theme song for the 1964 film "Goldfinger," collaborating with Anthony Newley. Sung by Shirley Bassey, this song became a top 40 hit, despite initial strong disapproval from producer Harry Saltzman. "John Barry had a hand in this as did Anthony Newley, a British song and danceman who was admired greatly by a young David Bowie."
**Thunderball (1965) – Composer** : John Barry composed the title theme for the 1965 James Bond film "Thunderball." He specifically went to Wales to recruit Tom Jones to sing the song, marking another collaboration that continued the tradition of grand orchestral Bond themes. "Barry went to Wales again for 1965's Thunderball getting Tom Jones to sing the title theme."
**You Only Live Twice (1967) – Composer** : John Barry was the composer for the title theme of the 1967 James Bond movie "You Only Live Twice," which was sung by Nancy Sinatra. This version was heard by moviegoers, although a different version became a hit single. "Nancy Sinatra, daughter of Frank got the gig. A different version ended up being something of a hit single but this is what movie goers heard."
**On Her Majesty's Secret Service (1969) – Instrumental Opening** : John Barry wrote a wordless composition for the opening sequence of the 1969 James Bond film "On Her Majesty's Secret Service." This instrumental piece set the tone for the film starring George Lazenby, who only played Bond for this one movie. "John Barry wrote something wordless for the opening sequence."
**A View to a Kill (1985) – Orchestral Arrangement** : John Barry took the bones of the song created by Duran Duran for "A View to a Kill" and arranged it, using a 60-piece orchestra. His contribution helped shape the track into a classic Bond theme and one of Duran Duran's biggest hits, reaching number one on the American singles charts. "Durand Durand came up with the bones of the song and John Berry took it from there using a 60 piece orchestra."
**The Living Daylights (1987) – Co-writer** : John Barry collaborated with A-Ha guitarist Paul Waaktaar-Savoy on the theme song for "The Living Daylights" in 1987. This partnership helped create a sizable hit, especially in Norway, aligning with the producers' decision to embrace the new wave sound for Timothy Dalton's first Bond film. "I guess guitarist Paul Wackertar and John Berry worked on it together."

## curiosities
**Departure from Bond Films** : John Barry had moved on from scoring Bond films by 1989, making "Licensed to Kill" the first title song not to involve him. His extensive contributions to the franchise had defined much of its musical character for decades. "He had moved on from scoring Bond films."

# curiosity

**James Bond Film Franchise – Overview (1962-Present)** : The James Bond film franchise began with "Dr. No" in 1962 and has since produced 27 films, grossing around $14 billion US dollars at the box office. The process for selecting a theme song involves competitive tenders each time a new chapter is announced, making the commission one of the most prestigious in film music. "There have been 27 Bond films, as I write this, starting with Dr. No in 1962. Box office grosses are now around $14 billion US dollars."
**Harry Saltzman's Dislike of "Goldfinger" Theme (1964)** : Harry Saltzman, one of the producers of the James Bond film "Goldfinger," vehemently hated Shirley Bassey's title theme. He sent a note back stating, "That's the worst blanking song I've ever heard in my blanking life," and desired another theme song, but time constraints forced its inclusion in the film, where it became a top 40 hit. "Harry Salisman, one of the producers of the film absolutely hated it."
**Shift to Rock for Bond Themes (1973)** : The 1973 film "Live and Let Die," starring Roger Moore as the third actor to play Bond, prompted a "rethink" in the approach to title theme music. Producers considered it time for a rock song to open the film, leading to the selection of Paul McCartney's track, a significant departure from earlier, more conservative musical choices. "Maybe it was finally time for a rock song to open the film."
**Bond Theme Musical Commonalities (1962-Present)** : Over the decades, many James Bond themes share common musical characteristics: most are in a minor key, possess a forbidding and serious tone, and always feature a significant orchestral arrangement. These elements constitute "the major bit of continuity" that producers believe defines a proper Bond theme, distinguishing it from other action movie themes. "Most are in a minor key. There's almost always a forbidding serious tone and like I said a few minutes ago there has to be some kind of big orchestral arrangement."
**The "Minor Ninth" in Bond Themes (All Eras)** : After extensive analysis of past James Bond themes, co-writer Paul Epworth discovered a recurring musical theory element: all successful Bond themes contain a variation of something called a "minor ninth." This harmonic commonality proved to be a critical insight for creating Adele's "Skyfall," which blended modern sound with traditional Bond elements. "It turns out that if you dig into music theory, they all contained a variation of something called a minor ninth."
**"No Time to Die" Film Release Delays (2019-2021)** : The James Bond film "No Time to Die" experienced multiple release delays. Originally slated for November 2019, it was pushed to February 2020, then April 2020 for a spring release, and again to November 12, 2020, due to COVID-19. It was further held back for nearly another year, with the UK release on September 30, 2021, and North America on October 8, 2021. "It was supposed to have been in theaters in November 2019, but then for some reason, it was pushed back to February 2020, some marketing thing."
**"Never Say Never Again" – Non-Canonical Bond Film (1983)** : In 1983, Sean Connery returned to play James Bond in "Never Say Never Again," a film explicitly stated to be "outside the normal Bond universe." This movie was a remake of 1965's "Thunderball" but produced by a different party who had battled for decades to make it. While reviews and box office were good, its music did not reach traditional Bond standards. "Sean Connery came back to play Bond in Never Say Never Again, which was outside the normal Bond universe."
