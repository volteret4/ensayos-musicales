## albums
**Mellow Gold (1994)** : This album featured Beck's breakthrough song "Loser." "It's from the mellow gold album of 1994."
## songs
**Loser (1993)** : This breakthrough song was developed with Carl Stevenson, whom Beck's indie label, Bungload, connected him with. Stevenson initially found Beck "a little weird" but became interested after hearing Beck's "doodlings with a guitar and a sampler and a drum machine." Stevenson encouraged Beck to rap like Public Enemy's Chuck D, but when this failed, Beck became self-deprecating, singing "I'm a Loser baby. So why don't you kill me?" This led to the song's iconic chorus. It was issued as a single in late 1993. "Anyway, the song was finished and issued as a single in late 93 and resulted in some pretty good things for Bex."
**Loser (1994) – Raw Demo Version** : A very raw and unlisted demo of "Loser" was discovered buried at the end of the "Beer Can" CD single released in 1994. This track provides insight into the creative process between Beck and Carl Stevenson as they "trying to sort things out," offering a unique glimpse into the development of one of the 90s' most enduring songs. "A very raw, raw demo found buried and unlisted at the end of his Beer Can CD single of 1994."
**Beer Can (1994) – CD Single** : Released as a single from the *Mellow Gold* album, the "Beer Can" CD single officially featured four previously unreleased tracks. However, it also contained a fifth, unlisted track buried at the very end, which was a raw demo version of "Loser." "One of the other singles from that record was Beer Can. And it was released as a CD single featuring four other previously unreleased tracks."

## albums
**With the Lights Out (2004) – 4-CD Box Set** : Released on November 23rd, 2004, more than 10 years after Kurt Cobain's death, this highly anticipated 4-CD box set compiled "what was left of the Nirvana legacy." It contained "some pretty raw stuff" from the band's history, dating back to 1987, and was released just in time for Christmas. "One of the most anticipated box sets in the history of music was with the lights out, a 4 CD set featuring what was left of the Nirvana legacy."
**Nevermind (1991)** : This album features the polished, official version of "Smells Like Teen Spirit," which, in its earliest demo stages, was "almost a minute longer" and had different words and arrangements. "But that's how the song sounded during its earliest days. The words are different. The arrangement is different, and it's almost a minute longer than what we got on, never mind."
## songs
**You Know You're Right** : Following Kurt Cobain's death, this was identified as "the only truly finished thing" that turned up from his remaining work. "The only truly finished thing that turned up was the song You Know Your Right."
**Smells Like Teen Spirit (1990/1991) – Rehearsal Demo** : An early, raw rehearsal demo of this song from late 1990 or early 1991 was included in the *With the Lights Out* box set. This demo, which Kurt Cobain called "Smells Like Teen Spirit," featured their new drummer Dave Grohl and differed significantly from the final *Nevermind* version, being "almost a minute longer" with different words and arrangements. "It's a rehearsal demo of a new song that Kurt called Smells Like Teen Spirit. And it's one of the first things that featured their new drummer Dave Grohl."
## curiosities
**Post-Kurt Cobain Legacy** : After Kurt Cobain's death, fans wondered what material he had left behind. The *With the Lights Out* box set, released over 10 years later, addressed this by compiling remaining material, but it was noted that "the real answer was not much" in terms of truly finished works. "And Kurt died, fans wondered what he had left behind. Had to be something, right? Well, the real answer was not much."

# artist - Pearl Jam
## members
Eddie Vedder
Stone Gossard
Jeff Ament
Jack Irons
## member of
Mother Love Bone

## albums
**Ten (1991)** : This album featured the "full and proper version" of "Alive," following a long evolutionary process from initial cassette demos to studio recordings under the band's various names. "Finally it was done and the 10 album was released with the full and proper version of Alive that we know today."
## songs
**Dollar Short (Gossman Tape) – Cassette Demo** : This was an early cassette demo with "no words, just loose ideas" that was created by Stone Gossard and Jeff Ament after Mother Love Bone disbanded. It became known as "the Gossman tape" in Pearl Jam mythology and was a foundational recording that later evolved into "Alive." "This tape became known in Pearl Jam mythology as the Gossmen tape, and it featured a recording that had the working title Dollar Short."
**Dollar Short (Mamason Tape) – Vedder's Vocal Demo** : This version originated when the "Gossman tape" of "Dollar Short" was passed to Jack Irons, who then gave it to Eddie Vedder in San Diego. Vedder listened to the instrumental demo "over and over again," wrote lyrics, and "dubbed his singing over that original cassette and sent it back up to Seattle." This specific recording is referred to by fans as "the Mamason tape." "Fans call this the Mamason tape."
**Alive (Mookie Blaylock Demo)** : After Eddie Vedder was invited to Seattle, the new band, then called Mookie Blaylock, went into the studio. By this point, "Dollar Short" had been renamed "Alive," and this demo was a "proper demo" that led to Mookie Blaylock securing a record deal. "And when Mookie Blaylock made a proper demo, it sounded like this."
**Alive (Pearl Jam Demo)** : Following the Mookie Blaylock demo and securing a record deal, the band changed its name to Pearl Jam. This demo represents the next stage in the song's evolution before its final release on the *Ten* album. "By the next round of demos, they had changed their name to Pearl Jam and Alive sounded like this."
## curiosities
**Fan Taping Policy** : Pearl Jam is known for being very open with their creative output, even allowing fans to tape their live shows. "Fans are allowed to tape their shows."
**Encouragement of Fan Websites** : Pearl Jam does not object to fans setting up special websites dedicated to their music, even those that "freely distribute Pearl Jam material that's never ever been officially released." The band's "silence is encouragement" in these cases, fostering a strong fan community. "Pearl Jam doesn't seem to mind fans that take some extra initiative and set up special websites dedicated to them and their music."

# label - KTS
## curiosities
**Bootleg Label Characteristics** : KTS was a renowned bootleg label known for two main things: super high-quality live recordings, which they obtained from unknown sources, and a wide selection of studio recordings that were never intended for official release. The label operated in offshore locations like Italy, Singapore, and Indonesia, where copyright laws were less stringent. "They were renowned for two things. Super high quality live recordings that they got from somewhere. And a wide selection of studio recordings that were never ever supposed to be released."

# curiosity
**Early Bootleg Records** : In the past, access to everything a band did was tightly controlled by distribution systems, making it difficult for fans to acquire unreleased material. The best fans could hope for were rare, elusive, and often illegal bootleg records, which were unauthorized recordings issued by shadowy labels without the artist's permission. These typically featured live performances, as they were the easiest to make, but some also contained vault material never designed for public hearing. "About the best you could hope for was for one of those rare, elusive and highly legal bootleg records. Unauthorized recordings issued by some shadowy label without the permission of the artist."
**Cat and Mouse Game with Bootleggers** : For many years, a "cat and mouse game" existed between record labels, artists, and bootleggers, with hardcore fans caught in the middle, hoping to get their hands on unreleased material. Bootleggers often moved their operations offshore to countries like Italy, Singapore, and Indonesia, where copyright laws were perceived as "a little looser." "For years, we had this cat and mouse game between the labels and the artists and the bootleggers."
**Internet's Impact on Bootleg CD Industry** : The advent of the internet led to the sudden collapse of the bootleg CD industry almost overnight. What previously required manufacturing in places like "a backstreet factory in China" could now simply be put online, making physical bootleg production largely obsolete. "Then along came the internet. And the bootleg CD industry suddenly dried up pretty much overnight."
**Artists Embracing Demos and Unreleased Material** : A significant shift occurred where performers and managers, instead of being "freaked out" by unfinished or unapproved material being released by others, began releasing it themselves. This strategy aimed to "forge a deeper relationship with their best customers, their biggest fans," leading to an "explosion of interesting material" from major bands. "Instead of being all freaked out about this unfinished or unapproved stuff getting released into the wild by someone else, they started doing it themselves."
**Seven-Inch Singles as Source for Demos** : Limited-run seven-inch vinyl records, particularly popular as collectibles in England, are a key source for demo versions and alternate takes. Their scarcity, due to limited editions, and the exclusivity of B-side tracks, which often feature unique material, make them highly sought after by fans. "So there's one place you can find demo versions and alternate takes on the B sides of limited run seven inch vinyl records."
**CD Singles as Source for Demos** : Although less prominent in North America, CD singles were historically used to entice fans to purchase them by including bonus tracks not available elsewhere. This strategy was particularly mastered by British bands like Oasis and Blur, providing another avenue for fans to acquire unique recordings. "But one of the many ways fans could be persuaded to buy a CD single version of a song was by tempting them with some bonus tracks that aren't available anywhere else."
**Reissues of Older Albums as Source for Demos** : Reissues of older albums, often presented as "new and improved versions" or "Deluxe Editions" with extra bonus material, serve as a common method to entice long-term fans to repurchase records they already own. This strategy allows artists to release previously unavailable material. "One way to get fans to buy another copy of a record that they already own is to put out a new and improved version."
**Box Sets as Source for Demos** : Box sets have become "big business" during an era of declining CD sales, offering a high-profit margin for artists willing to undertake the extensive and legally complex process of sorting, cleaning, and authenticating old tapes and recording sessions. Fans are willing to pay a "hefty premium" for the comprehensive package, including liner notes, books, booklets, and extra artwork, and the limited nature of many box sets can also make them an investment. "Box sets have become big business in the era of declining CD sales."
**Challenges of Creating Box Sets** : Compiling a box set is a "very long time" consuming and difficult process. It involves sorting through old material, which is often lost, misplaced, mislabeled, or unlabeled. Establishing the provenance of each song, cleaning up recordings, and navigating extensive legal hurdles to prevent lawsuits are all necessary steps. "Often the tapes or discs are lost or misplaced or mislabeled or not labeled at all."
**Official Band Websites as Source for Demos** : Official band websites have become a key source for demo material, with bands like Weezer using them to "feed fans with cool material that's not on any of their CDs." This approach leverages a well-developed fan club to build a deeper relationship by sharing unique, often raw, recordings. "This brings us to the next source of demo material, official band websites."
**Fan Websites as Source for Demos** : Some bands, like Pearl Jam, are "open about everything they create" and actively encourage fans to take initiative by setting up dedicated fan websites. These sites often freely distribute "material that's never ever been officially released," and the bands' "silence is encouragement," making them a valuable source for finding unique demos and recordings. "Pearl Jam doesn't seem to mind fans that take some extra initiative and set up special websites dedicated to them and their music."
**Indie Releases as Source for Demos** : Indie albums or cassettes released before a band's major label debut often feature early versions of songs that are later refined, re-recorded, and become significant for the band. These early indie releases can be extremely rare, with very limited production runs, making the originals difficult to acquire. "For account list cases of indie albums or indie cassettes that feature early versions of a song that would later be refined, re-recorded and released to become something big for the band."
