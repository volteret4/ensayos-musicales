## albums
**In Rainbows (2007) – Pay-What-You-Want Experiment** : Released digitally on October 10, 2007, this seventh album by Radiohead was initially offered on a radical "pay what you want" model directly to fans, bypassing traditional record label distribution. Despite 62% of downloaders paying nothing, those who did paid an average of $6, resulting in an overall average of $2.26 per download, and earning Radiohead more from digital sales than all their previous six albums combined. "On October 1st, 2007, the group announced that their new album in Rainbows, their 7th, would be available on October 10th, 2007, and it would be offered for free."
**In Rainbows (2007) – Limited Physical Editions** : In addition to the digital release, Radiohead offered limited physical editions of "In Rainbows," including a CD and various box sets. Approximately 100,000 copies of these physical products were sold, generating an estimated £3 million directly into Radiohead's bank account, showcasing the success of their independent distribution strategy. "Limited physical editions sold about 100,000 copies, resulting in about 3 million pounds right there, right into Radiohead's bank account."
**In Rainbows (2007) – Conventional Release Sales** : Following its initial direct-to-fan digital release, "In Rainbows" was conventionally released through XL Recordings. The album achieved total sales of over 3 million copies, including nearly 2 million physical sales, demonstrating its broad commercial success beyond the experimental digital release. "More people bought the album when it was released conventionally through Excel recordings. Total sales seemed to be somewhere north of 3 million copies, including nearly 2 million physical sales."

## songs
**Body Snatchers (2007)** : This track from Radiohead's "In Rainbows" album was recorded directly off the studio floor. This recording approach means the song is essentially a live performance captured in the studio, giving it a raw and immediate quality. "That recording was made right off the floor in the studio, so it's pretty much a live recording."

## curiosities
**Discontent with Major Label (Pre-2007)** : For the first half of their career, Radiohead was signed to EMI, but they expressed significant dissatisfaction with their treatment, the lack of control over their music, and the compensation they received. This unhappiness eventually led them to seek freedom from their contract. "For the first half of their career, they were a major label band and in their case, they were signed to EMI. But like a lot of groups, they were unhappy with the way they were treated, the amount of control that they had over their music and how much they were getting paid."
**Contractual Freedom (2007)** : By 2007, Radiohead had fulfilled their obligations with EMI and were free from their contract, granting them complete autonomy over their music and future releases. This newfound independence enabled them to undertake innovative distribution strategies like the "In Rainbows" experiment. "By 2007, they were free of their contract, and able to do whatever they wanted."
**Music Industry Chaos (2007)** : In 2007, the music industry was in turmoil due to the internet's disruptive impact, rampant music piracy, and plummeting CD sales, leading to widespread uncertainty about how musicians could generate income from recorded music. This unstable environment provided the context for Radiohead's bold experiments in distribution. "The whole industry was in chaos thanks to the disruption by the internet. Music piracy was rampant, CD sales were falling, no one had any idea of how anyone was going to make money from recorded music."

# artist - The Beatles

## curiosities
**Delayed Adoption of CD Format (1983-1987)** : When compact discs emerged in early 1983, The Beatles' organization was highly cautious, delaying their entry into the new format until early 1987 to observe its market development and stability. "When the compact disc started showing up in stores in early 1983, the Beatles would have none of this new format."
**Delayed Release of Box Sets (Pre-Mid-1990s)** : Despite the growing popularity and profitability of box sets, The Beatles did not release any until the mid-1990s, even though rare and unreleased tracks had been widely bootlegged for over 25 years, indicating their conservative approach to new release formats. "Even though box sets were becoming a big and profitable thing, the Beatles didn't bother releasing anything that way until the mid 1990s, even though rare and unreleased tracks have been widely bootleg for more than 25 years."
**Boycott of Digital Music Stores (2003-2010)** : The Beatles, having been significant victims of music piracy, initially boycotted digital music distribution. Their songs and albums only became legally available on the iTunes Music Store (launched 2003) and other digital platforms on November 16, 2010, seven years after iTunes' debut. "The iTunes music store went online April 28, 2003, but the Beatles, victims of music piracy more than most, boxed."
**Boycott of Streaming Services (Pre-2015)** : The Beatles extended their cautious approach to streaming platforms, withholding their music from all legal streaming services until Christmas Eve 2015, long after other artists had embraced the format. "No platform could legally stream any Beatles song until Christmas Eve 2015."
**Symbolic Endorsement of Digital Distribution (2010, 2015)** : The Beatles' eventual decision to make their music available on iTunes (2010) and then streaming services (2015) was viewed by many in the music industry as a crucial legitimization of these digital trends, given their status as "the biggest band the world has ever seen." "Since among many people inside and outside the music industry, was that none of these trends or technologies were fully operational and legitimate, unless the Beatles, the biggest band the world has ever seen, embraced them."

**Innovative "Claw" Stages (2009-2011)** : The 360 tour featured three massive, four-legged "claw" stages, built by companies in Belgium and the United States. Each stage stood over 50 meters tall, cost up to $30 million, and could support 200 tons of equipment, allowing for a 360-degree audience view and increasing venue capacity by 10-20%. "Three massive, four-legged claw stages were constructed by companies in Belgium and the United States. Each was over 50 meters tall and cost up to $30 million each."
**Complex Logistics of 360 Tour (2009-2011)** : The tour's intricate logistics required 120 trucks to transport each "claw" stage, along with 137 permanent roadies and 120 locally hired crew members. "And each stage required 120 trucks to transport it. Plus, 137 permanent roadies and 120 locally hired crew."
**Venue Adaptations for 360 Tour (2009-2011)** : The immense size of the "claw" stages demanded costly modifications at various venues. U2 paid $2 million to hoist an HD screen out of the way at Cowboy Stadium in Dallas and $3 million to expand the Hipodrome in Montreal to accommodate the stage. "U2 had to pay $2 million to hoist the HD screen out of the way at Cowboy Stadium and Dallas. "There was a long postponement in 2010 when Bono badly hurt his back."
**Tour Duration and Attendance (June 30, 2009 – July 30, 2011)** : The U2 360 tour ran for over two years, from June 30, 2009, to July 30, 2011, encompassing 111 shows. **Former Highest-Grossing Tour Record (Pre-2019)** : For several years, the U2 360 tour held the record as the highest-grossing concert tour of all time, with an official box office take of $736 million US dollars. **One Claw Stage Preserved (Post-Tour)** : One of the three distinctive "claw" stages from the U2 360 tour has been repurposed and is now located at the Loveland Living Planet Aquarium in Salt Lake City, where it functions as a versatile space for concerts, movies, community gatherings, and a farmer's market.
