# artist - Ben Dorsi

## curiosities
**Pioneer Roadie** : Ben Dorsi is widely considered by those who study the history of road crews to be the very first roadie of the modern era. He was known for his big heart and his habit of calling people he liked "Lovie." "Those who study the history of roadcrues tend to point to Ben Dorsi, who also went by the name Lovie because he had a really big heart and tended to call people he liked Lovie."
**Early Life and Career** : Hailing from San Antonio, Texas, Ben Dorsi began his career in show business by working for the Ice Capades. After a stint in the Navy, he moved to California, where he held various jobs including a gardener and John Wayne's chauffeur. "Dorsi was from San Antonio, Texas, and he began his career in show business by working for the ice capades."
**Start with Hank Thompson** : On Halloween night in 1950, Ben Dorsi began a new job traveling with honky-tonk western performer Hank Thompson to help set up his gear. This role marked the start of his dedicated career as a roadie, a gig he would continue for the next six decades. "On Halloween night, 1950, he started a new job with Hank Thompson, a honky-tonk western performer, traveling with him to help him set up his gear."
**Extensive Work History** : Throughout his six-decade career, Ben Dorsi worked for numerous prominent artists, starting with Hank Thompson, then a young Elvis Presley, followed by Johnny Cash, Patsy Cline, Kris Kristofferson, Merle Haggard, and Willie Nelson. "After working with Hank Thompson, Dorsi worked for a young Elvis Presley. Then he moved on to Johnny Cash, Patsy Klein, Chris Christopherson, Murrell Haggerd, and Willie Nelson."
**"World's First Roadie" Title** : Willie Nelson bestowed upon Ben Dorsi the unofficial title of "world's first roadie," acknowledging his pioneering and long-standing role in the music industry. "Willie was the guy who gave him the title of world's first roadie."
**Musical Tributes and Literary Appearances** : Ben Dorsi was honored in song by Waylon Jennings with "Ode to Ben Dorsi," and Red Sovine released a song titled "Ben Dorsi III." He also appeared as a character in Kinky Friedman's novel "Roadkill," which is set aboard Willie Nelson's tour bus. "Well, in Jennings, we're a song about him entitled, Ode to Ben Dorsi. Red Sovine has a song called Ben Dorsi III. Kinky Friedman wrote a novel called Roadkill, which is set aboard Willie Nelson's tour bus."
**Inspiration for "Roadie" Movie and "King of the Roadies" Documentary** : In 1980, a movie titled "Roadie" starring Meat Loaf was released, with the lead role explicitly inspired by Ben Dorsi's life and career. Dorsi was also the subject of his own documentary called "King of the Roadies." "There was a 1980 movie called Roadie starring Meatloaf in a role inspired by Dorsi. And Dorsi was the subject of a documentary called King of the Rodies."
**Annual Commemoration Event** : Every February 22nd, an event is held at the John T. Floore Country Store outside of San Antonio. This event commemorates the day Ben Dorsi first met both Hank Thompson and Willie Nelson, two significant figures in his long career. "Plus, there's an event every February 22nd at the John T. Floor Country Store outside of San Antonio in commemoration of the day Ben met both Hank Thompson and Willie Nelson for the first time."
**First Inductee to Roadie Hall of Fame** : In 2009, Ben Dorsi achieved another historical first by becoming the inaugural inductee into the Roadie Hall of Fame, located in Nashville. "And in 2009, Ben Dorsi was the first person to be inducted into the Rody Hall of Fame in Nashville."
**Death and Legacy** : Ben Dorsi passed away on September 17th, 2017, at the age of 92. At the time of his death, he held the distinction of not only being considered the world's first roadie but also the oldest working roadie in the industry. "Ben Dorsi died on September 17th, 2017 at the age of 92. Not only was he the first Rody, but he was also the oldest working one."

# curiosity

**Roadies as Essential "Ghosts"** : Roadies are described as paradoxically both visible and invisible; they perform essential, often unnoticed tasks to ensure live music events run smoothly. If they do their jobs well, they are "ghosts," but if they fail, things can go disastrously wrong, or not at all. "If they do their jobs well, they're ghosts, but if they don't, things don't go so well. In fact, they may not go at all."
**Demanding Nature of Roadie Work** : The life of a roadie involves incredibly long hours, often up to 20 hours a day, without time for partying after shows. The job is physically and emotionally tough, demanding extensive travel, poor food, less than ideal sleeping arrangements, and can be detrimental to personal relationships due to constant time away from home. "If they're lucky, their days will run only 20 hours, partying backstage after the show, no way. There's no time, gotta tear it down, load up and move out."
**Diverse Roles Within Road Crew** : The term "roadie" is an umbrella term encompassing a wide array of specialized roles within a touring support team. These include generalists, instrument technicians (guitar, drum, bass, keyboard techs), designers and operators for sound systems, lighting rigs, special effects, and pyro, as well as tour, production, and stage managers, bodyguards, security, merch personnel, various drivers, medical staff, and caterers. "Well, that is an umbrella term for support personnel. Some roadies are generalists who can do everything. Others are specialists who have expertise in a certain area."
**Local Hires vs. Permanent Employees** : Roadies are hired in various capacities; some travel with an act, others are hired locally on an as-needed basis as gig workers, and a select few are permanent, long-term employees of specific acts or management companies. "Some roadies travel with an act. Are there's are hired locally on an as needed basis? Some are gig workers taking whatever jobs come their way. A few are permanent long term employees of a specific actor management company."
**Lack of Credit for Roadies** : Despite their essential contributions, roadies rarely receive the credit they deserve from the public, even as they facilitate the live music experience. "And do they get the credit they deserve? Never. Well, almost never."
**Roadie Hall of Fame** : There exists a Roadie Hall of Fame in Nashville, established to honor the contributions of individuals in this vital but often overlooked profession, with Ben Dorsi being its first inductee in 2009. "In 2009, Ben Dorsi was the first person to be inducted into the Rody Hall of Fame in Nashville. Yes, there is a Rody Hall of Fame."
**Evolution of Roadie Roles** : As the live and recorded music era progressed, the demand for behind-the-scenes personnel increased. PA equipment and lighting became more complex, requiring specialized support staff for security, wardrobe, catering, and driving, creating numerous opportunities in the roadie vocation. "As the live and recorded music era progressed, more behind the scenes people were required. PA equipment became bigger and more complex. Lighting for shows got more complicated. Support staff was needed for security and wardrobe and catering."
**Pathways to Becoming a Roadie** : Individuals often become roadies through various informal routes, such as being helpful at a local bar, working in student unions that book bands, assisting musician customers from music stores, or simply through recommendations from friends. "Maybe somebody who worked at a bar was extra helpful to axeloting in and loading out. That could have resulted in a Rody gig."
**Close-Knit Seattle Scene (Pre-Grunge)** : In the pre-grunge era in Seattle, the music community, including future stars like Kurt Cobain and Krist Novoselic, was described as a "real close family," with many working as roadies for bands like The Melvins before finding their own success. "And was a real close family in the pre-grunge era in Seattle."
**Roadie Attire Evolution** : While traditional roadie attire often includes cargo shorts, old band t-shirts, and a ring of keys on the belt, this image is not universal. Some artists, like Peter Gabriel and Jack White, have opted for coordinated roadie uniforms, such as orange jumpsuits, lab coats, or black suits and fedoras, to integrate the crew into the visual spectacle. "While we still see crew members in cargo shorts and old band t-shirts with a ring of keys on their belt, it's not always that way anymore."
**Roadie Demographics - US Survey (2020s)** : A survey of 1,350 live music professionals in the US revealed significant demographic disparities: 67% were male and 85% were white, with only 2% black and just over 3% Hispanic, indicating a lack of diversity in the profession. "I saw some stats out of the US that said out of 1,350 live music professionals surveyed 67% were male and 85% were white. Only 2% were black and a little more than 3% were Hispanic."
**Women's Roles in Live Music** : Women in the live music industry are disproportionately involved in specific areas, such as the road production office, catering, and wardrobe, highlighting a gendered division of labor within touring crews. "Women are disproportionately involved in areas like the road production office, catering and wardrobe."
**Roadies of Color United (2009)** : Founded in 2009 by Casey Jackson and others, Roadies of Color United is an organization dedicated to fostering diversity by opening up opportunities and providing support for people of color interested in careers within the live touring business. "Rodeys of Color United, an organization formed in 2009 to help people of color interested in the live touring business."
**Diversify The Stage** : Co-founded by Noelle Scaggs of Fitz and The Tantrums, Diversify The Stage is a group committed to creating new opportunities for underrepresented groups in the music industry, including women, people of color, LGBTQ+ individuals, and female-identifying and gender non-conforming individuals. "Noile Skakes, the co-leader of Fits and the Tantrums, founded a group called Diversified the Stage, which is dedicated to opening up new opportunities for women, people of color, LGBTQ people, and female identifying and gender non-conforming individuals."
**Need for New Industry Professionals** : The touring business is expanding, and a significant portion of the "old guard" roadies are retiring, creating a pressing need for new, skilled professionals to enter the industry. "Touring has never been a bigger business and a lot of the old guard is retiring."
**Formal Roadie Training Programs** : Education in live music production is now available, with institutions like the Academy of Contemporary Music in London, England, and Metalworks Institute in Mississauga, Ontario, offering diploma courses in live music, concert, and sound production to train modern roadies. "You could actually go to school to learn to be a Rodey. For example, the Academy of Contemporary Music in London, England will teach you everything you need to know."
**Modern Roadie Skills Requirements** : Contemporary roadies are no longer just "humpers" (unskilled labor); they are specialized technicians who require a deep understanding of math, physics, electronics, and acoustics to handle today's complex gear. The industry has fewer and fewer jobs for unskilled labor, most of which are hired locally. "With today's gear, you have to know math, physics, electronics, acoustics and more."
**"Roadie" as a Derogatory Term** : Some modern road crew professionals consider the term "roadie" to be derogatory, preferring "techs" or "technicians" instead, due to the highly specialized and demanding nature of their work beyond simple gear handling. "It's to the point now where some road crew professions consider the term Rodey to be derogatory because they do so much more."
**Job Uncertainty for Roadies** : Despite being gainfully employed for a tour, roadies often face significant job uncertainty, constantly worrying about securing their next gig after a tour concludes. "Even when you're gainfully employed for one tour, you have to worry about where your next job is going to come from."
**COVID-19 Impact on Live Music Industry** : The COVID-19 pandemic severely impacted the live music industry, leading to a complete cessation of touring and, consequently, a lack of jobs for roadies and technicians for two years. This resulted in hundreds, possibly thousands, leaving the business permanently. "And when COVID hit, bam, that was a problem. With no live touring, there were no jobs."
**Typical Show Day Schedule** : A standard show day for a road crew is highly demanding, beginning around 10 AM with rigging and setup, followed by sound and lighting adjustments, instrument placement, soundchecks by 4 PM, catering at 5 PM, and opening acts and headliners performing in the evening. As the audience leaves by 10:30 PM, the crew immediately begins tearing down, packing everything by 11:30 PM, and moving on to the next city. "After driving all night from the last gig, the first people at the next venue arrive in around 10 to sus things out. Within 20 minutes, rigors and texts are working on the stage."
