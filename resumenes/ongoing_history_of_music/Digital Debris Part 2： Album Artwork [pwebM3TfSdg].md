# artist - The Clash

## members
Paul Simonon
Joe Strummer

## albums
**London Calling (1979)** : Released just before Christmas 1979, the album features iconic artwork. The cover photo, an out-of-focus shot taken by Penny Smith on September 21, 1979, at the Palladium in New York, depicts bassist Paul Simonon trashing his instrument. Simonon was in a bad mood that night because bouncers prevented the audience from standing up and engaging with the show, leading him to destroy his bass near the end of the set. The exact time, 10:43 pm, is known because Paul broke his wristwatch while smashing his bass. The photo was accidentally taken by Smith, who was startled and didn't have time to change her Pentax camera lens. Initially embarrassed by the shot's quality, Penny was convinced by Joe Strummer the next day that he loved it and it should be the cover. The artwork's layout and typography were partly inspired by Elvis Presley's self-titled 1956 album. It has since been named the best rock and roll photo of all time and became an official British postage stamp. "At first, Penny was really embarrassed at the quality of the shot. But the next day, Joe Strummer said he loved it and that it should be the cover of the next Clash album."

## curiosities
**Paul Simonon's Bass Trashing Incident (1979)** : During a show at the Palladium in New York on September 21, 1979, bassist Paul Simonon smashed his instrument on stage. His frustration stemmed from the crowd's perceived lack of engagement, which he attributed to bouncers preventing the audience from standing up. The incident occurred around 10:43 pm, as evidenced by his broken wristwatch. This moment was captured in an iconic, out-of-focus photograph by Penny Smith, which later became the cover of "London Calling." "Simmon was in a very bad mood that night at the palladium in New York. He was upset that the crowd just wasn't into the gig, largely because the bouncers had refused to let the audience stand up and get into the show."

# label - Columbia Records

## curiosities
**Pioneering Album Artwork (1938)** : Columbia Records hired 22-year-old artist Alex Steinweiss in 1938 as their art director, initially for store displays and newspaper ads. Steinweiss, recognizing the durability of new cardboard record jackets and the need to protect LP records from acidic paper sleeves, convinced the label to print artwork directly on the jackets. In 1939, for a new Rogers and Hammerstein record, he photographed a marquee he temporarily altered at the Imperial Theatre. Despite initial reluctance from management over the additional $2,500 cost, the record was released with his artwork and saw sales 900% above expectations, marking the first-ever example of album cover artwork. "In 1938, Columbia Records had an idea. They hired a 22-year-old guy named Alex Steinweiss, an artist who was very good at poster art."
**The Record Jacket Invention** : Columbia Records worked on 12-inch long-playing vinyl album technology, which highlighted the problem of acidic paper sleeves damaging records. Alex Steinweiss designed a new cardboard sleeve, known as the "record jacket," which the Imperial Paper Box Company patented. This new jacket became a "palette for a new art form" suitable for printing artwork. "He designed a new sleeve made from cardboard and got a box company to develop some special equipment. The result was known as the record jacket."

# curiosity

**Early Recorded Music Packaging - Edison Cylinders** : In the nascent days of the recorded music industry, Edison cylinders featured Thomas Edison's name most prominently on the packaging, printed larger than either the song title or the artist's name. No artwork was present. "If you bought one of the super old school Edison cylinders, Thomas Edison's name was the most prominent thing that you saw in the packaging."
**Early Recorded Music Packaging - Flat Discs** : Flat rotating discs were sold in plain sleeves, with the only visual element being circular cutouts that allowed the record's center label to be seen. Special records might have decorated sleeves, but this was rare. Packaging was typically stark, practical, and cheap, made of brown paper or cardboard, and offered little protection for the fragile shellac records. "When the flat rotating disc came along, they were sold in plain sleeves. Their only nod to the visual was the part of the sleeve with the circular cutouts that allowed you to see the centered label of the record."
**The Great Depression's Impact on Music Industry (1930)** : In 1930, the recorded music industry experienced a spectacular collapse due to the Great Depression, leading to a 90% to 95% drop in record sales. "Then in 1930, the recorded music industry collapsed spectacularly. The great depression set in and record sales dropped somewhere between 90 and 95%."
**Record Jacket as an Art Form** : The introduction of the cardboard record jacket, patented by the Imperial Paper Box Company, transformed packaging into a new art form. Unlike fragile paper sleeves, these tougher jackets could be printed with designs without damage, opening up opportunities for visual creativity. "The Imperial Paper Box Company held the patent, and the jacket soon became the palette for a new art form."
**Conceptual Art Trend (1940s-1950s)** : After the success of early album artwork, the trend shifted from simply featuring performers to creating conceptual art that aimed to capture the mood or energy of the music within the album. Examples include a blue sleeve with a piano under a New York street light for Gershwin's Rhapsody in Blue, and the emergence of surrealistic designs, new graphic languages, and diverse illustration techniques. "The original trend was creating conceptual art designed to capture the mood or the energy of the music within the album."
**Introduction of the 33 1/3 Long Playing Vinyl Album (1948)** : The 33 and a third long-playing vinyl album, introduced in 1948, provided art directors with two 12x12 inch canvases (front and back) for artwork. This format was predominantly used for classical recordings, show tunes, and jazz records, with jazz records often featuring the most creative and abstract visual interpretations due to the challenge of visualizing the genre. **Rise of Photography in Album Art (1956)** : In 1956, photography gained vogue in album artwork, with stylish pictures of artists, particularly jazz performers and early rock and roll figures like Elvis Presley, replacing conceptual illustrations. This marked a shift in visual trends, leading to a mix of illustrations and photographs in subsequent album art. "Photography came into vogue. Photographers were hired to take stylish pictures of artists, starting with jazz performers. But then there was Elvis Presley too."
**Rock Albums in the Mid-1960s** : Prior to 1965, rock albums were scarce as they were considered too expensive for teenagers, who preferred 45 RPM singles. The trend began to change with artists like The Beatles, Bob Dylan, The Rolling Stones, and The Beach Boys, who started releasing full albums with multiple songs, necessitating artwork that appealed to rock fans. "1965 seems to be a dividing line between the era of the seven-inch single and the era of album rock. If a rock band was going to issue albums, then they needed to come up with artwork that appealed to rock fans."
**The Golden Age of Album Art (Late 1960s - Early 1980s)** : This period is recognized for the amazing caliber, quality, and artistic achievements in album design. Design studios like Hipgnosis, along with individual designers, created highly conceptual, surreal, and often puzzling artwork that encouraged deep viewer engagement and often influenced purchase decisions. "The golden age of album art was from the late 60s until the early 1980s. The caliber and quality and overall artistic achievements were amazing."
**Hipgnosis Design Studio** : Based out of London, Hipgnosis was a prominent design studio known for its work for Pink Floyd, Led Zeppelin, ACDC, and Peter Gabriel. The company was founded by Strom Thorgerson, Aubrey Powell, and later included Peter Christopherson. Hipgnosis had a successful run until its dissolution in 1983, with Thorgerson continuing album art creation until his death in 2013. Their designs were characterized by their enigmatic and surreal qualities, prompting viewers to interpret deeper messages. "Three guys were behind the company, Strom Thorgerson, Aubrey Powell, and eventually Peter Christofferson. They had a great run that ended with the dissolution of the company in 1983, and Thorgerson continued to create album art until he died in 2013."
**Explosion in Band Logo Design** : Album art led to a significant increase in the design of band logos, which became powerful marketing tools. Examples include The Rolling Stones' "Lips and Tongue" and Motorhead's "Devilhead." "Album art led to an explosion in the design of band logos. A logo is a very powerful marketing tool."
**Impact of the Compact Disc on Artwork** : The advent of the compact disc reduced the available canvas for album art from 12x12 inches to approximately 5x5 inches. Designers adapted by creating CD booklets with multiple pages, allowing for more detailed content, although the smaller front image made it harder to appreciate subtleties. "Then along came the compact disc, reducing the palette art is set to work with from 12 inches by 12 inches to the size of a CD case, which is about 5 by 5 inches."
**Disappearing Album Artwork in Digital Age** : In the era of downloads and streaming, the physical space for album artwork has diminished from 12x12 or 5x5 inches to mere pixels, often as small as 225x225 pixels on platforms like Wikipedia. This reduction makes detailed artistic expression difficult and largely overlooked by consumers. "Instead of 12 by 12 or 5 by 5 inches, we have images measured in pixels."
**Music Industry's Digital Adaptation and Artwork Costs** : The music industry, recovering from a near-death experience due to its initial struggle with the internet, clawed back profitability by cutting costs, including selling fewer CDs. While vinyl has seen a resurgence with high prices and margins, the vast majority of revenue now comes from streaming, which eliminates physical products entirely. "Meanwhile, the music industry, which had a near-death experience thanks to its initial inability to adapt to music on the internet, has clawed its way back to profitability, partly by cutting costs."
**Embedded Artwork in Digital Formats** : Digital music formats like MP3s, Apple's M4A, and ACC have the capability to contain artwork directly within the files, referred to as CoverArt or CoverImages. However, consumers often don't notice these, and when they do, the images are frequently low-resolution JPEGs. "MP3s, Apple's M4A and ACC all have the ability to contain artwork within the files. But when you're happy people take the time to notice it, and if you do bother to look at this art, it's often a low-resolution JPEG image."
**iTunes LP (2009)** : Apple introduced iTunes LP in 2009 as a form of interactive artwork, aiming to preserve and evolve the art form in the digital realm. This format allowed for interactive content and images connected to individual tracks, exemplified by Nine Inch Nails' "The Slip." "In 2009, Apple introduced something called the iTunes LP, which is a form of interactive artwork."
