# genre - Heavy Metal

## curiosities
**Etymological Research (2013)** : The origin of the term "heavy metal" when applied to music was extensively researched by Dina Weinstein, a professor of sociology at DePaul University in Chicago. In 2013, she published a paper titled "Just So Stories, How Heavy Metal Got Its Name, A Cautionary Tale," attempting to solve this etymological puzzle by examining various sources. "She wrote a paper entitled Just So Stories, How Heavy Metal Got Its Name, A Cautionary Tale."
**Scientific Origin** : The term "heavy metals" originates from chemistry and metallurgy, referring to actual metals with high densities such as iron, copper, tin, silver, gold, platinum, gallium, thallium, and hafnium. This scientific usage predates its musical application. "Heavy metals are actual metals with high densities."
**William S. Burrows' Literary Usage (1961-1965)** : Writer and poet William S. Burrows used the character "Uranian Willie, the Heavy Metal Kid" in his 1961 novel "The Soft Machine" and a 1965 spoken word performance. While the term appeared, Burrows was discussing aliens and addictive drugs, not music, and thus his work is not considered the origin of the musical genre's name. "Burrows isn't talking about music at all. He's actually talking about aliens and addictive drugs."
**Early Print Musical Reference (1970)** : The earliest verified print reference to "heavy metal" in a musical context appeared on February 7, 1970, in Cream Magazine. Music critic Lester Bangs described The Guess Who as "heavy metal robots," though this was intended as an insult rather than a definitive genre label. "They're quite refreshing in the wake of all the heavy metal robots of the year past."
**Emergence as Genre Descriptor (Mid-1970s)** : The term "heavy metal" entered the music vernacular in the mid-1970s, gaining widespread use to describe a new wave of "loud, heavy guitar rock." This sound was pioneered by bands like Led Zeppelin, Black Sabbath, and Deep Purple, solidifying "heavy metal" as one of rock's most significant subgenres. "By the mid-1970s, everybody was using it."

The slide is then moved up and down the fretboard to create a continuous, gliding effect called a glissando. "The slide is then moved up and down the fretboard to create a cool effect known as a glissando."
**African Origins (Didley Bow)** : The technique of using a slide on strings has ancient roots, with a form of it required for playing the Didley bow, a one-stringed instrument found in Africa. "In Africa, there was a one stringed instrument known as the Didley bow, which required a form of a slide."
**Hawaiian Adaptation (19th Century)** : When European sailors introduced the guitar to Hawaii in the 19th century, local musicians adapted it. They would lay the guitar flat on their lap and use a piece of metal, known as a "steel," on the fretboard, which evolved into the steel guitar. "When the guitar was introduced to Hawaii in the 19th century by sailors from Europe, the locals adapted it to their own way of playing, which was laying it flat on the lap and using a piece of metal on the fretboard."
**Mississippi Delta Blues Innovations (Early 20th Century)** : Early 20th-century blues players in the Mississippi Delta improvised slide instruments, using objects like knives or beer bottle necks when proper steels were unavailable. This raw approach significantly influenced the development of slide guitar in blues and rock music. "They used a knife on their strings and when a knife wasn't available, the neck of a beer bottle would do an pinch."
**Evolution with Electric Guitar** : The introduction of the electric guitar further evolved slide playing, with prominent blues artists such as Muddy Waters and Elmore James adopting and popularizing the technique. "With the blues evolved and the electric guitar was introduced, playing slide evolved."
**Influence on Rock and Country** : Slide guitar, particularly as exemplified by Blind Willie Johnson, influenced countless country players and rock musicians including Rye Cooder, Brian Jones, Keith Richards, and Duane Allman. # instrument - Vinyl

## curiosities
**Technical Drawbacks and Limitations** : Vinyl records have inherent limitations in data storage. Deep bass and loud sounds require wider grooves, which physically restrict the amount of music that can fit on a record side. If bass or loudness is too high, the stylus can struggle to track grooves accurately, leading to missing music or jumping. "The louder the music and the more deep bass it contains, the less of it will fit onto the side of a vinyl record."
**High-Frequency Tracking Issues (Sibilance)** : Vinyl can suffer from "sibilance," a smearing of higher frequencies, especially "S" sounds. High frequencies create very fine groove details that can be too small for the stylus to track accurately, causing it to "plow through" and resulting in distortion and missing music. "Vinyl can suffer from something known as the Siblins issue, where higher frequencies are smeared, especially with S sounds."
**Mastering Distortion from Overheating Cutters** : Distortion on vinyl can also originate during the creation of the master disc. The cutter, which has to move faster to track high frequencies, is prone to overheating. This overheating creates distortion that is then transferred to the master plate and subsequently to all vinyl records pressed from it. "Overheating while cutting these grooves means distortion."
**RIAA Curve for Equalization (1940s)** : In the 1940s, American engineers developed the Recording Industry Association of America (RIAA) Curve, a standard equalization method to mitigate vinyl's physical limitations. This curve de-emphasizes bass and enhances high frequencies before pressing, allowing for about 22 minutes of music per side (versus ~5 minutes without it) and reducing surface noise. "Base is de-emphasized, and high frequencies are enhanced."
**Need for Turntable Preamps** : All turntables require a special preamp (either built-in or external) to function correctly. This preamp not only amplifies the tiny voltage generated by the stylus and cartridge but also applies the reverse of the RIAA Curve, restoring the recording's natural sound. "This also explains why all turntables need a special preamp, either built in or an external one."
**Perceived "Warmth" and Psychological Preference** : While technically inferior to CD in many aspects, vinyl is often perceived to sound "warmer" and more pleasant. This perception is largely psychological, tied to nostalgia and positive emotional associations, rather than an objective sonic advantage, though mastering differences contribute to a real sonic distinction. "Well, it's mostly psychological. These people love the nostalgia of vinyl and have positive feelings about it, feelings that cover what they think they're hearing."
**Immunity to "Loudness Wars" Compression** : Vinyl is inherently immune to the extreme compression used in "loudness wars" mastering, which often distorts CD audio by squeezing out dynamic range. Its physical limitations prevent the application of such excessive compression, meaning a properly mastered vinyl record can sound significantly better than an overly compressed CD version of the same album. "But the same compression cannot be applied to vinyl because of all those physical limitations that I mentioned earlier. It properly mastered for vinyl makes vinyl immune to the distortions that we hear caused on CDs by the loudness wars."
**45 RPM Albums for Enhanced Fidelity** : Some artists release albums on multiple 12-inch 45 RPM records instead of a single 33 1/3 RPM record. This format allows for fewer songs, more physical space for grooves, and a higher playback speed, which accommodates more bass and less abrupt high-frequency tracking, resulting in less distortion and improved sound quality. "Fewer songs taking up more space and running at a higher speed allows the grooves to swing out as much as they need to in order to accommodate more bass."

# instrument - Compact Disc

## curiosities
**Technical Superiority over Vinyl** : From a technical standpoint, compact discs offer superior audio quality compared to vinyl. They boast a better signal-to-noise ratio, eliminating rumble, pops, and clicks common to records. "The compact disc offers a better signal to noise ratio."
**Digital Sampling Rate (44,100 segments/second)** : CDs process a musical signal by sampling it 44,100 times per second. This rate is scientifically determined to be indistinguishable by human ears and brains from a smooth analog source, ensuring high fidelity. "most human ears cannot sense the fact that the digital signal of a CD is broken down into 44,100 segments per second."
**Anti-Aliasing for Smoothness** : CDs employ anti-aliasing technology to smooth out the tiny jaggedness of the digital signal, theoretically ensuring a continuous stream of audio. "And CDs use something called anti-aliasing, which is supposed to smooth out that tiny jaggedness and theoretically non-continuous stream of the digital signal."
**Absence of Bass and High-Frequency Limitations** : Unlike vinyl, which has physical limitations for deep bass and high frequencies, compact discs, being digital and read with a laser, do not suffer from such issues. This means CDs can consistently deliver deeper, louder bass without distortion or missing audio. "You will always, always, always get deeper, louder bass from a CD than you will from a vinyl record."
**Vulnerability to "Loudness Wars" Compression** : CDs are susceptible to distortion caused by the "loudness wars," a post-recording practice of using excessive compression to make a CD sound louder. This process often removes dynamic nuances, resulting in a "pristine digital signal" that sounds "awful" and distorted. "CDs can sound awful because of compression, this post-recording practice of making a CD scene louder."

# curiosity

**Origin of On-Hold Music (1962)** : The concept of on-hold music originated in 1962 with Alfred Levy, who owned a factory in New York. A loose wire in his phone system touched a metal girder, turning his building into a giant radio antenna that picked up a signal from a nearby radio station. Callers placed on hold would hear this music, leading Levy to realize it kept them occupied and confirmed they were still connected. "This meant that every time someone was put on hold, this interference with the phone system resulted in music leaking through to the callers who were waiting."
**Alfred Levy's Patent (1966)** : In 1966, Alfred Levy filed a patent for a machine that automatically played music to callers on hold. This early patent serves as the foundation for virtually every on-hold device used today, whether it provides generic pre-taped music, a radio station, or another musical source. "In 1966, he filed a patent on a machine that automatically played music for callers anytime they were placed on hold."
**Impact of On-Hold Music on Customer Interactions** : On-hold music revolutionized customer interactions by assuring callers they remain connected and preventing hang-ups. Studies suggest that at least 70% of customers subjected to silence while on hold will disconnect within 60 seconds, highlighting that any music, even "bad music," is preferable to dead silence. "Playing the music, even bad music, is way better than the occasional beep or dead silence."
**"M-O-H" Input in Phone Systems** : Most commercial phone systems include an "M-O-H" input, which stands for "Music On Hold." This dedicated input facilitates the integration of various musical sources for callers awaiting assistance. "And pretty much every commercial phone system comes with an M-O-H input."
**Origin of Paid Public Concerts (1672)** : The modern concept of a paid public concert, where tickets are sold in advance for a specific performance in a private venue, is traced back to 1672. In London, John Bannister organized and performed a concert for which the general public paid one shilling for admission in his home, marking the earliest known evidence of such an event. "The general public was allowed into the gig after they bought a ticket, which sold for the price of one shilling. And this happened in 1672."
**Ancient Origins of Ticket Scalping** : Ticket scalping, the practice of reselling tickets at inflated prices, has roots stretching back to ancient times. It's speculated that in ancient Rome, individuals outside the Coliseum likely engaged in similar activities, attempting to sell tickets. "Back in ancient Rome, there were probably guys and togas and sandals outside the Coliseum yelling, Quizz and Tiniot Terpiss, which is, I think, Latin for who's got tickets."
**Emergence of Modern Ticket Scalping (Mid-1800s)** : The contemporary form of ticket scalping emerged in the mid-1800s. "Sidewalk men" would acquire tickets for events and resell them at a premium to people waiting outside theaters. "But ticket scalping, as we know it today, first appeared in the mid 1800s."
**Scalping Methods: "Diggers" and "ICE"** : Early ticket scalpers, often called "sidewalk men," used specific methods to obtain tickets. They would hire individuals, known as "diggers," to stand in line for them. Another tactic was "ICE," which involved bribing box office personnel for a share of the ticket proceeds. "Some would hire people to stand on the sidewalk in line for them, and these folks were called diggers."
**Charles Dickens' Book Tour and Scalping (1867)** : A situation similar to Jenny Lind's ticket scandal occurred in 1867 during Charles Dickens' book tour of America. Tickets for his readings immediately vanished from box offices but were readily available from "sidewalk men," illustrating the pervasive nature of ticket scalping even for non-musical events. "A similar situation arose in 1867 when Charles Dickens went on a book tour of America."
**Persistent Problem of Ticket Scalping** : Ticket scalping, involving resellers and speculators, remains a significant issue for live music fans. Despite nearly two centuries of legal attempts to eliminate it, the practice continues to thrive. "The law has been trying to stamp them out for almost two centuries. And guess what? No luck."
**Vinyl vs. CD Audio Quality Debate** : The debate over whether vinyl or compact discs offer superior audio quality is both scientific and emotional, influenced by subjective factors like emotional state and ear health. Technically, CDs are often superior, but perceived "warmth" and mastering differences create a complex listening experience. "To explain this is kind of difficult because the appreciation of audio quality is a very, very subjective thing that involves everything from your emotional state to the health of your ears."
**Sampling Rate Theory (1928, 1949)** : The theory that a musical signal sampled at 44,100 times per second is indistinguishable to the human ear and brain from a smooth analog source was first proposed in a paper in 1928 and experimentally proven in 1949. This underpins the fidelity of compact disc audio. "This sampling rate theory was first discussed in a paper printed in 1928 and then proven experimentally in 1949."
**"Loudness Wars" and CD Compression** : The "loudness wars" describe a post-recording trend where music, particularly for CDs, is heavily compressed to make it sound louder. This process, while aiming for impact, often introduces significant distortion and eliminates the dynamic nuances between loud and quiet passages, leading to a "pristine digital signal" that ironically sounds "awful." "We've come to know this as the loudness wars. This has introduced a ton of distortion into what should be a pristine digital signal."
