## albums
**Scream (2009) - Third Solo Album** : Chris Cornell collaborated with producer Timbaland for this album, shifting from his previous rock records to an R&B and hip-hop sound. The album was recorded in just six weeks, featuring drum machines, R&B grooves, and samples instead of heavy guitars and rock rhythms. The cover artwork depicted Cornell jumping, "apparently ready to smash a guitar." It was a "critical and commercial disaster." "It was if Chris Cornell was trying to be Justin Timberlake."

## curiosities

He pursued this by working with producer Timbaland on his third solo album. "Chris was convinced that drum machines and R&B grooves and samples was the way to go."
**Lack of Record Company Intervention** : During the production of the *Scream* album, there was no one from the record company to tell Chris Cornell that an R&B pop album was "a bad idea" or "off-brand," leading to the album's commercial and critical failure. "But there was no one to tell Chris no."

"And you have to wonder how the critical and commercial disaster of the scream album eventually played into the, it'll never happen in our lifetime, Sound Garden reunion the following year."

# artist - Nirvana

## members
Kurt Cobain
Chris Novoselic

## labels
DGC Records

## albums
**Nevermind (1991)** : This album became immensely popular, but Kurt Cobain felt it was "too slick and wasn't punk rock enough." The follow-up, *In Utero*, was intentionally made to contrast with its polished sound. "Kurt also didn't like the record company game very much. He thought that never mind was too slick and wasn't punk rock enough."
**In Utero (1993)** : Recorded in mid-February 1993, Kurt Cobain was determined to make this album "rawness and raggedness" in sound, explicitly as an "FU song for the record called Radio Friendly Unit Shifter" to the expectation of another *Nevermind*. Steve Albini was chosen to record it for his "ragged and raw" style. The record label, DGC Records, was "upset" with early samples, claiming poor songwriting and an "un-listenable" mix, fearing its release. The album's final mix was modified from Albini's original due to label pressure, though Kurt "hated what was eventually released officially." Initial first week sales were 180,000 copies, considered "very, very light" compared to *Nevermind*. Major retailers Walmart and Kmart refused to stock the album due to its content not conforming to "family values." "For a while, there were doubts as to whether the album would ever be released."

## songs
**Radio Friendly Unit Shifter (1993)** : A song written by Kurt Cobain for the *In Utero* album, intended as an "FU song" to the record company and expectations for another *Nevermind*. "He even wrote an FU song for the record called Radio Friendly Unit Shifter."
**Heart-Shaped Box (1993) - Original Mix** : The original, untouched mix of this song by Steve Albini was rejected, and Kurt Cobain "hated what was eventually released officially." "The original and rejected mix of heart-shaped box."

## curiosities
**Kurt Cobain's Discontent with Stardom** : After *Nevermind* achieved massive success, Kurt Cobain became unhappy with the "baggage" that came with being a famous rock star and the "face of a generation." He felt he lost freedom, privacy, and control over his public image and that his art became "property of the world." "He really wasn't happy with how things were turning out for Nirvana."
**Intentional Sabotage of Career** : Kurt Cobain deliberately sought to "return some rawness and raggedness to Nirvana's sound" with *In Utero* to alienate fans expecting another *Nevermind*, as part of a plan to "sabotage much of what he and Nirvana had achieved." "I'd rather die than do that. This is exactly the kind of record I would buy as a fan that I would enjoy owning."
**Record Label Conflict over In Utero** : DGC Records was alarmed by the raw sound of *In Utero*, calling the songwriting subpar and the mix "un-listenable." They considered not releasing the album. "They claimed that the songwriting was not up to par and that the overall mix of the album was un-listenable."
**Steve Albini's Master Tapes Hostage Situation** : Producer Steve Albini had a deal stipulating no changes after his final mix. When the label wanted modifications to *In Utero*, he refused to hand over the master tapes, "essentially holding them hostage," until bassist Chris Novoselic convinced him to comply. "He even refused to hand over the master tapes for a while, essentially holding them hostage."
**Low-Key Marketing of In Utero** : DGC Records took a "low-key approach to marketing" *In Utero*, avoiding hyping it as the big follow-up to *Nevermind*, hoping it might "catch fire on its own." "Maybe that's why they deliberately took a low-key approach to marketing the record."
**Retailer Blacklist of In Utero** : Walmart and Kmart, significant record retailers at the time, refused to stock *In Utero* because it "didn't conform with their family values." "Then again, Walmart and Kmart, two department stores, which were also important record retailers back then, refused to stock the album because it didn't conform with their family values."

**Napster Lawsuit (2000-2001)** : Metallica initiated a public $10 million lawsuit against Napster after discovering an unreleased demo, "I Disappear," and their entire catalog on the peer-to-peer file-sharing network. The lawsuit was for a minimum of $10 million, or $100,000 per infringed song, whichever was greater. "They fired off a $10 million lawsuit at Napster, very, very public."
**Public Backlash Against Napster Stance** : The media largely sided with music fans during the Napster controversy, portraying Metallica as "rich greedy rock stars." This led to a "hand-handed PR disaster" for the band. "The media, which almost always took the side of music fans back then, wasn't very sympathetic to Lars and Metallica on how the story was covered."
**Lars Ulrich's Napster Office Visit** : Lars Ulrich delivered a 60,000-page printout listing 335,435 Napster users who allegedly poached Metallica songs to the Napster offices, generating significant media attention. "Then, with great media fanfare, Lars Ulrich pulled up in front of the Napster offices, in Olimom, with a 60,000-page printout of 335,435 Napster users, who allegedly had poached Metallica songs."
**Lars Ulrich's Congressional Testimony** : Lars Ulrich testified in Washington, D.C., about the "evils of piracy, file sharing, and copyright infringement," expanding the debate beyond music to general information exchange. "Lars ended up testifying in Washington about the evils of piracy, file sharing, and copyright infringement."
**"Until It Sleeps" Web Cartoon** : A popular web cartoon satirized Lars Ulrich's stance against Napster users, depicting him as ungrateful and threatening to fans who downloaded their music. "But when the show went live, she held up a picture of Pope John Paul II, which she then proceeded to tear up as a way of protesting against child abuse within the Catholic Church."
**Post-SNL Career Impact** : After the *Saturday Night Live* incident, Sinéad O'Connor's career became "pretty rocky."
