# genre - Punk Rock
## curiosities
**Early Usage (1590s)** : The word "punk" dates back to Old England around 1590, where it may have been used to describe a certain brand of male prostitute. This historical usage predates its musical application by centuries. "Old England about 1590. It may have been used back then to describe a certain brand of male prostitute."
**Colonial American Usage** : In Colonial America, a "punk" referred to a specific type of stick used for lighting fires, fuses, or fireworks. This usage was apparently appropriated from the Algonquin Indians of Delaware, who spelled it "P-O-N-K" and used it to mean dust, powder, and ashes. "In colonial America, it was a different story. A punk was a type of stick used to light a fire or maybe light a fuse or set off some fireworks."
**Victorian Era Slang** : By the Victorian era, "punk" had evolved into a slang word with dual meanings: either a type of Chinese incense or a derogatory term for someone or something perceived as of poor quality or utterly worthless. "By the Victorian era, a punk was a slang word that described either a type of Chinese incense or someone or something who was of poor quality or downright worthless."
**Early 20th Century Criminal Connotation** : In the early 20th century, Victorians began using "punk" to refer to a young criminal. Within the first three decades of the century, the connotation expanded to include the idea of being young and inexperienced, adding nuance to its definition. "By the early 20th century, Victorians were using punk to refer to a young criminal."
**"The Wild One" (1953) Influence** : The 1953 movie "The Wild One," starring Marlon Brando as the leather-jacketed motorcycle rider Johnny Strombo, was a pivotal film in exploring American motorcycle gangs. This movie, and the attention it brought to the subculture, resurrected the older Victorian definition of a "punk" as a young criminal in the public consciousness. "The next step in the evolution of the word came with the release of a 1953 movie called The Wild One."
**Association with Early Rock and Roll (1950s)** : The release of "The Wild One" in 1953 coincided with the birth of rock and roll. The establishment soon conflated these two emerging forms of youthful rebellion, labeling young rock and rollers as "punks," meaning troublemakers, though this was still distinct from its later musical meaning. "So it wasn't long before the establishment managed to intertwine these two new forms of youthful rebellion. Young rock and rollers were also punks, but not in the modern sense."
**Mid-60s Garage Rock Visuals** : Throughout the 1960s, a series of rough, ragged, and raw-sounding garage bands from North America (including The Seeds, Count Five, The Barbarians, The Standells, and The Troggs) adopted a tough image, often wearing leather jackets. This look, reminiscent of Marlon Brando or James Dean, led some observers to describe these musicians as appearing like "punks." "And so it was inevitable that some people said the jackets made these guys look like punks."
**Lester Banks' Early Print Usage (April 6, 1969)** : Detroit rock writer Lester Banks made a significant early use of the word "punks" in a musical context in a review of an MC5 album. Published in Rolling Stone magazine on April 6, 1969, he described the group as "a bunch of 16-year-old punks on a meth power trip," helping to forge the connection between the term and rebellious rock music. "In a review of an album by a Detroit group called the MC5, published in Rolling Stone magazine on April the 6th of 1969, he managed to use this phrase. A bunch of 16-year-old punks on a meth power trip."
**Dave Marsh Coins "Punk Rock" in Print (May 1971)** : Writer Dave Marsh is credited with the first printed use of the phrase "punk rock" in May 1971. He used it in an article about the reformation of the 60s garage band Question Mark in the Mysterians, describing their reunion as "a landmark explosion of punk rock," a crucial moment in the term's evolution. "And the guy who seems to have done it first in print anyway was a writer named Dave Marsh."
**Early "Punk Rock" as an Aesthetic (1971)** : In 1971, "punk rock" was understood more as an aesthetic or attitude than a defined sound, primarily used by writers and critics rather than musicians themselves. Early artists like Alice Cooper and Kiss were even considered "punk rock" by some, indicating the term's initial broad and somewhat unfocused application. "In 1971 punk rock was more of a aesthetic than a sound. It was a phrase used by writers and critics not musicians."
**Legs McNeill's "Punk" Fanzine (Mid-1970s)** : Legs McNeill, a fan of Manhattan's underground scene, founded a fanzine he eventually renamed "Punk." This decision was inspired by the cover art of The Dictators' "Go Girl Crazy" album, which depicted the band in leather jackets, leading McNeill to conclude they looked like "punks." The fanzine's growing circulation helped popularize the term for New York artists around CBGBs. "So that became the name of his fan scene. Punk."
**British Adoption of "Punk" (Summer 1976)** : British writers like Nick Kent and aspiring managers such as Malcolm McLaren discovered the nascent New York scene and eagerly adopted the term "punk." They imported it into the UK, where it coincided with the rise of bands like the Sex Pistols and The Clash, leading to the "British punk rock explosion" that began in the summer of 1976 and officially christened their movement "punk rock." "It wasn't long before British writers and scene stars happened upon what was going on in New York. And they really liked the word punk."

# genre - Post-Punk
## curiosities
**Emergence from Punk Burnout (Late 1978)** : By late 1978, the initial punk rock scene and its distinct sound began to wane. A new wave of underground music emerged that, while recognizing punk's influence, deviated significantly in sound, arrangements, instrumentation, fashion, and politics from bands like The Ramones and Sex Pistols, necessitating new descriptive terminology. "But by late 1978 the original punk rock scene in sound was beginning to burn out."
**Early Terminology Attempts (1978-1979)** : As the original punk rock scene declined, writers sought new ways to categorize the evolving music. John Savage proposed "new music" (with a K) in late 1978, while Simon Frith tried to popularize "after punk" (one word) in spring 1979, but neither term successfully captured public imagination. "As the original punk rock scene ran down a number of people offered up new words. There was new music spelled with a K on the end."
**First Print Usage (February 1978)** : The phrase "post punk" first appeared in print in an article in the UK's NME magazine in February 1978, authored by Charles Shaar Murray. Paul Morley later adopted the term, integrating it into his reviews starting in 1980, which contributed to its broader acceptance. "The first time anybody can remember the phrase post punk being used in print was an article in the UK's NME magazine written by Charles Schar Murray in February of 1978."
**Tony Wilson's "Postmodern" and its Link** : Tony Wilson, founder of Factory Records, introduced the term "postmodern" to describe the new music his label championed, such as Joy Division, aiming to connect it with the aesthetic of actual postmodernism in art. Other writers, including Charles Shaar Murray and Paul Morley, interpreted "postmodern" as an abbreviation for "postmodern punk," which eventually shortened to simply "post punk." "This phrase was postmodern and he applied it to groups like Joy Division."
**Defining Theme: "I'm F'd"** : A succinct way some defined the core thematic difference of post-punk, in contrast to punk's confrontational "FU" stance, was "I'm F'd." This encapsulates the genre's often darker, more complex, and introspective exploration of existential angst. "Some people put it this way. If punk was all about FU, post punk's main theme was I'm F'd."
**Effectiveness and Criticism of the Term** : The term "post punk" proved effective for distinguishing groups like Joy Division and Gang of Four from conventional punk, particularly among UK bands. However, some found the term "a little too elitist or vague," prompting the search for alternative descriptors. "It worked quite well, especially with groups like Joy Division and Gang of Four and several others out of the UK."

# genre - New Wave
## curiosities
**Distinction from Punk** : Music categorized as New Wave, much like post-punk, did not directly adhere to the sound of punk. Nevertheless, its emergence clearly bore the imprint of punk, exhibiting new arrangements, instrumentation, fashion, and political sensibilities that set it apart from established punk conventions. "Again songs, albums and performers who came to be classified as New Wave weren't punk at all. But by listening to them, you could tell that punk had happened."
**Origin in French Film Industry** : The term "New Wave" found its roots in the art school aesthetic prevalent in the UK, but its original incarnation was a film school term. It was derived from "New Valve Egg," referring to a period of creative renewal within the French film industry during the 1960s. "In its original incarnation, New Wave was a film school term, referring to a creative renewal in the French film industry in the 1960s."
**Malcolm McLaren's Early Advocacy (Late 1979)** : Starting in late 1979, art-oriented members of the British punk community, notably former Sex Pistols manager Malcolm McLaren, attempted to popularize the phrase "New Wave." McLaren sought to use this term to describe the musical landscape in the months following the Sex Pistols' breakup. "The art C1s amongst the British punk community, including ex-sextal manager Malcolm McLaren, tried to get people to accept this word, this phrase beginning in late 1979, as Malcolm sought to describe his world in the months after the sex pistols broke out."
**Seymour Stein's Marketing Strategy** : Seymour Stein, the founder of Sire Records, strategically adopted "New Wave" as a marketing term to sell his artists' records in America. This was in response to radio stations' apprehension about playing "punk" records, which they perceived as too weird, violent, political, or merely a fad. Stein used "New Wave" to "disguise things and soften the blow," making the music more palatable for mainstream radio. "That's when Seymour started using the phrase New Wave to market his stuff. It was a way to disguise things and soften the blow."
**Major Label Adoption (By 1980)** : Seymour Stein's "New Wave" marketing term was swiftly embraced by major labels, including Warner Brothers Records and Polygram Records. By 1980, this led to "New Wave" becoming a widely used catch-all term for a broad spectrum of experimental, artsy, and more poppy music that emerged in the wake of punk. "Seymour's marketing term was then picked up by Warner Brothers Records, and then by Polygram Records, and then other major labels."
**Characteristics and Commercial Potential** : In contrast to post-punk, New Wave was generally characterized by a less dark or gloomy sensibility and possessed significantly greater commercial potential. It served as an expansive umbrella term for a variety of subgenres, including Power Pop (e.g., Elvis Costello, Tom Petty), the 2 Tone scene (e.g., The Specials, English Beat), and Technopop (bands favoring synthesizers over guitars). "Unlike post-punk, it wasn't quite as dark or as gloomy, and it certainly had a lot more commercial potential."
**Peak Popularity and MTV Influence (1982-1983)** : New Wave experienced its peak popularity in 1982 and 1983, a period significantly bolstered by the debut of MTV in 1981 and MuchMusic in 1983. These music video channels provided a new visual platform for music, turning "telegenic New Wave bands" like Flock of Seagulls, Eurythmics, and Duran Duran into major stars. "New Wave was at its biggest through 1982 and 1983. The music video certainly helped too."

# genre - Alternative
## curiosities
**Non-Mainstream Counterpart to Major Labels** : For many years, rock music was bifurcated into the mainstream, populated by famous bands on major labels (e.g., Led Zeppelin, The Stones, The Beatles, ACDC, Van Halen), and a category of "all the music that didn't get mainstream attention." This latter category comprised music deemed too weird, different, or innovative for general media and the public, though it cultivated dedicated fan bases often experienced in smaller club settings rather than stadiums. "On the other side of this equation, there was all the music that didn't get mainstream attention. This was the stuff that was just too weird or too different or too innovative for the general media and the general public."
**Origin on Independent Labels (Late 1970s/Early 1980s)** : The "alternative" designation originated with small, independent, niche record companies such as Epitaph, SST, Beserkley, Rounder, and Sire, which emerged in the late 1970s and early 1980s. These "alternative labels" were unaffiliated with major multinational corporations and provided a vital platform for groups influenced by the punk movement, often signing bands that major labels overlooked due to perceived lack of mass commercial appeal. "The only way these acts could get their music out there was to create alliances with small, independent, niche record companies who weren't affiliated with a major label."
**Relationship with "Indie"** : The term "alternative" eventually became synonymous with "Indie," the British equivalent for independent labels and the music they produced (e.g., Mute, Factory, Rough Trade, Creation). Over time, "Indie" gained broader acceptance and ultimately superseded "alternative" as the preferred descriptor across the Atlantic, being perceived as "a better word." "The British had their alternative labels too, but they didn't call them that. They refer to their version as Indy as an independent."
**"College Rock" as Predecessor** : For a period during the 1980s and 1990s, non-mainstream rock was sometimes referred to as "college rock," largely because it was the type of music frequently broadcast on campus radio stations. However, this term was considered somewhat limiting, especially for non-college audiences, leading to the eventual adoption of more inclusive terms like "alternative" and "Indie." "For a while, some called it college rock because it seemed to be the kind of music that you heard on campus radio stations."
**Catch-All Umbrella Term** : Similar to New Wave, "alternative" functioned as a broad umbrella term encompassing all music that emerged following punk and consciously rejected mainstream commercialization. It covered a vast stylistic spectrum, from low-fi fuzzy guitar rock (Sonic Youth) to electronic pop (Depeche Mode), heavy industrial music (Skinny Puppy, Ministry), and even delicate music from major-label artists (Kate Bush, Tori Amos). "Like New Wave, this was a catch-all umbrella term that described all the music that came in the wake of punk."
**Fragmentation and Loss of Meaning (1980s-1990s)** : As the 1980s transitioned into the 1990s, alternative music fragmented into numerous subgenres, including various forms of punk, hardcore punk, synthesizer-based music, goth, and industrial. This proliferation made the term increasingly confusing, especially after the mainstream success of Nirvana and grunge effectively made the "alternative world mainstream," causing the word to lose its original intent of signifying an *alternative* to established norms. "As the 80s turned into the 90s, alternative music began to fragment, into dozens and dozens of different streams."
**Modern Usage and Diminished Relevance** : While some "artsy types" favored "post-modern" (which was sometimes simplified to "modern rock"), the most common phrase for contemporary rock music is now simply "New Rock." Consequently, the word "alternative" has largely lost its original meaning and intended purpose, as what was once considered alternative frequently now occupies the mainstream. "So as a result, the word alternative has lost its meaning, or at least the intent of its original meaning."

It continues to signify independence, specifically referring to groups that record for labels not associated with the majors in terms of artist signings and the music they create.
