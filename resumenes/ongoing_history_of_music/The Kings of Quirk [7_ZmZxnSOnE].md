# artist - Lou Reed

## member of
The Velvet Underground

## curiosities
**Singing Style - Monotone Draw** : Starting his recording career in the late 1950s, Lou Reed's singing style deviated significantly from conventional norms. He was characterized by a limited vocal range, often speaking rather than carrying a tune, and exhibiting a monotone draw without much melody or rhythm. His timing with the music was frequently odd, a style notably different from contemporaries like Bob Dylan. "He really speaks more than he actually carries any kind of attune."
**Influence on Alt Rock** : Lou Reed's unique approach to music, particularly with The Velvet Underground, had a profound impact on the evolution of alternative rock. His unconventional vocal style and the band's subject matter — which often explored themes of sex and drugs — were considered too "weird" for mainstream audiences at the time but influenced generations of musicians. "Yet Reed's music found its way and influenced generations of musicians who'd likely idea of being weird, even if it was just for the sake of being weird."

# artist - The Velvet Underground

## members
Lou Reed

## curiosities
**New York City Origins and Controversial Subject Matter** : Emerging from the streets of New York City in 1966, The Velvet Underground, featuring Lou Reed, was deemed too unconventional for mainstream appeal. Their music, characterized by its "weirdness," openly addressed taboo subjects like sex and drugs, which were largely unaccepted in rock and roll at the time. This thematic content, more so than Lou Reed's vocal style, was a primary reason for their non-mainstream status. "Lou and the Velvet were from the streets of New York City and were just too weird for mainstream consumption."

## albums
**Trout Mask Replica (1969) - Magnum Opus** : This double album, consisting of 28 tracks, is widely regarded by critics as Captain Beefheart's best work and a highly important rock album. Released in 1969, it garnered favor with specific types of music fans, including The Beatles, who appreciated its experimental nature, and others who enjoyed its deliberately "disagreeable" sound. Influential figures like Johnny Lydon of the Sex Pistols, Matt Groening, and film director David Lynch were noted fans. **Frownland (1969)** : This song is featured on Captain Beefheart's double album *Trout Mask Replica* from 1969. It is notable for its intentionally designed unconventional sound, reflecting Beefheart's experimental artistic approach. ## curiosities
**Artistic Background and Dyslexia** : Born Don Van Vliet, Captain Beefheart was primarily a sculptor and painter before his music career, showing enthusiasm for various forms of artistic expression. He was a close friend of Frank Zappa. He was also very dyslexic and claimed to have never attended public school, a statement later proven untrue but contributing to his enigmatic persona. "Beefheart was mainly a sculptor and a painter, but he was enthusiastic for all sorts of artistic expression which inevitably led to music."
**A&M Records Signing and Antics** : Captain Beefheart and His Magic Band signed with A&M Records in 1966, an indie label co-founded by Herb Alpert. The label was notably indulgent of Beefheart over the years, despite his unpredictable behavior. One anecdote describes him showing up at the label's offices with a loaded crossbow, angered by criticism of his work. "Beefheart and his magic band were eventually signed to An M in 1966 and over the years the label indulged him a lot, even though he would do things like show up with a loaded crossbow at the offices because he was angry that somebody dared critique his work."

"according to legend, he deliberately tortured his vocal chords with lots of whiskey and cigarettes to make him sound older and more world-weary."

The arrival of punk further influenced Smith, with bands like the Sex Pistols, Ramones, and Elvis Costello helping him discover his artistic niche. Smith does not view himself as a traditional singer, stating that in the early days, he focused more on achieving the necessary emotional state for a song, often resulting in out-of-tune performances. He consciously started singing in different voices to avoid predictability, finding it challenging to sing in a voice far from his natural speaking tone. "In the early days, I never cared about giving a performance. I just worked myself up into the state of mind needed for the song, and that would be it."

**Pauline Kael's Description** : Legendary film critic Pauline Kael of The New York Times described David Byrne as a "parodist" who commanded the stage with a "hollow wide, frosty verb." Kael noted that Byrne's voice, lacking resonance, was more akin to a shouter or chanter, with a "faintly metallic whale," yet it never cracked or weakened despite his constant jiggling, aerobic walking, jumping, and dancing on stage. "Burn himself is the parodist, and he commands the stage by his hollow wide, frosty verb."
**Asperger's Syndrome Diagnosis** : David Byrne has been diagnosed with Asperger's syndrome, a condition on the mild end of the autism spectrum. While two other members of Talking Heads reportedly attributed the band's breakup to Byrne's autism, Byrne acknowledges his diagnosis, stating he has learned to manage it through music, treating it more as a personality trait than a disorder. "Burn has been diagnosed as having Asperger's syndrome, which is on the autism spectrum, mild end, but still."
**Influence on Yelping Vocal Style** : David Byrne was highly influential in popularizing a distinct "yelping" vocal style that became prominent among some New Wave artists in the early 1980s. This style was also adopted by bands such as Devo and Oingo Boingo, solidifying Byrne's impact on the vocal trends of the era. "Burn was very influential in introducing that yelping sort of singing voice that became very popular with some new waivers in the early 80s."

**Vocal Technique and Range** : Justin Hawkins is known for his distinctive vocal style, often mistaken by journalists for falsetto. However, Hawkins clarifies that he primarily uses "head voice" for softer high singing and "high belt" for most of his powerful high notes, feeling the tone resonate in his head rather than throat or chest. He possesses an impressive vocal range of over four octaves, capable of reaching a low A (A2) and as high as an E6. "Justin says he doesn't use a falsetto. However, Hawkins maintained that his approach was, and remains, entirely serious. His use of high-pitched vocals was noteworthy because it had been a long time since such a style was prevalent in Alt Rock. "This guy's making fun of Freddie Mercury or Queen. No, Justin was and remains dead serious."

The label's creation was spurred by Geffen's client, Jackson Browne, who struggled to secure a record deal. Geffen envisioned Asylum as a haven for singer-songwriters, embracing a slightly "crazy" ethos reflected in its name. "Asylum was founded in 1971 by a couple of former show business agents at the William Morris Agency."
**Key Artists on Roster** : Asylum Records quickly attracted a roster of prominent singer-songwriters and bands, establishing itself as a significant force in the early 1970s. Artists such as Joni Mitchell, Linda Ronstadt, J.D. Souther, and The Eagles were among those who joined the label. The founders, being fans of Bob Dylan's unique voice, were also open to signing and experimenting with artists like Tom Waits, whom they saw as a potential "Dylan." "They got Johnny Mitchell, Linda Ronstadt, JD Suther, the Eagles? They all became members of the roster."

# curiosity

**Subjectivity of Musical Beauty and Imperfection** : The perception of music, particularly singing voices, is highly subjective. What one person finds pleasant, another might consider mere noise, highlighting the need for patience with art forms whose beauty isn't immediately obvious. Often, imperfections in technical execution can make music more beautiful or interesting, challenging the notion that perfection is required for artistic merit. "Sometimes imperfection makes something more beautiful, or at least more interesting."
**Singing as a Personal Expression** : Singing is an intensely personal form of musical expression, where the human body itself serves as the instrument. The resulting sound is influenced not only by physical characteristics but also by deep-seated emotions and experiences—things beyond conscious control, sometimes painful or fantastical—which give music its life and supersede other technical considerations. "Singing is especially personal because this is the body as a musical instrument."
**Historical Gender Disparity in Vocal Acceptance** : Historically, male singers have enjoyed greater freedom to deviate from conventional vocal styles compared to women. For many years, female singers were often confined to a specific vocal aesthetic, whereas men faced fewer restrictions. Examples like Bob Dylan, Neil Young, Bon Scott, Brian Johnson, Robert Plant, and Rod Stewart illustrate this broader acceptance of varied male voices. "Dudes have always had it much easier than women when it comes to being different."
**Conservative Approach of Talent Shows** : Popular television talent competitions like American Idol, The Voice, X Factor, and even musical-drama series like Glee are noted for promoting a very conservative and technically polished approach to singing. This emphasis on conventional vocal perfection might make it difficult for artists with truly unusual or experimental voices to succeed in such platforms today. "Idol and the voice and X Factor and Glee all push a very conservative approach to singing."
**Record Label Environment in Late 60s/Early 70s** : The late 1960s and early 1970s were characterized by an "everything goes" attitude within many record labels, a period before the industry became highly corporatized and formal. As rock music was still a young and rapidly evolving art form, labels and their personnel were more inclined to take chances on new and experimental sounds, without clear expectations of where musical trends were heading. "in the late 60s and early 70s, there was a certain everything goes attitude at a lot of record labels."
**Rise of Indie Labels for Discovery** : In the contemporary music industry, the discovery and nurturing of artists with unusual or experimental styles are increasingly confined to indie labels. While such discoveries can still occur, major labels are less likely to invest in unconventional artists compared to the more open-minded approach historically taken by smaller, independent imprints. "these kinds of discoveries are almost exclusively the domain of indie labels."
