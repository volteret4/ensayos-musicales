## albums
**Chuck (2004) - Third Album** : Released in the fall of 2004, it was the band's third studio album. It was followed by "Underclass Hero." "Some 41 from the third album Chuck released in the fall of 2004."
**Does This Look Infected (2002) - Original Album for Over My Head** : This album is mentioned as the original home for the song "Over My Head." "There's a live version of Over My Head, originally from the Does This Look Infected Record, recorded in London, Ontario in April of 2005."
**Underclass Hero (2007) - Fourth Album & Semi-Concept Record** : This album was the follow-up to "Chuck" and took nearly a year to write due to Derek Whibley's "paranoid writer's block" and lost confidence. Derek ended up producing it himself as they couldn't find anyone else. It featured a different sound, with Derek adding piano and keyboards, and was described as a semi-concept record encompassing Derek's outlook on life. Several songs are admitted by Derek to be about his relationship with Avril Lavigne, to whom he was married. "What some fans ended up with was a semi-concept record that can be loosely described as encompassing Derek's outlook on life, including several that he admits being about his relationship with Avril Lavigne, whom he was married."
**Screaming Bloody Murder (2011) - Fifth Album & Last with Founding Drummer** : Released on March 29th, 2011, this album was produced by Derek Whibley himself after Gil Norton (known for work with Pixies and Foo Fighters) was unavailable. It marked the last record with founding member and longtime drummer Steve Jocz, and the first with guitarist Tom Thacker from the band Gav. The title track was the band's first single in three years. The album cycle tour for this record lasted three years. "Album number five for some was Screaming Bloody Murder. It was released on March 29th, 2011."
**13 Voices (2016) - Sixth Album & Comeback Record** : Released five years after "Screaming Bloody Murder," this album was branded as a comeback. It saw the return of lead guitarist Dave Baksh, who had left in 2006, rejoining in 2015. Tom Thacker, Baksh's replacement, remained with the band. It also introduced new drummer Frank Zummo. The record was notable for being funded through crowdsourcing and led to a tour aptly named "Don't Call It A Some Back Tour." "The 13 Voices record was inevitably branded a comeback album."
**Order in Decline (2019) - Seventh Album & Trump Era Context** : This album, released in 2019, is the band's seventh studio album and their first in the "age of Trump." Derek Whibley describes the themes as open to interpretation, stating he wrote about his personal feelings and observations rather than explicit political statements, even for songs like "45." "The 7th Sum 41 album was 2019's Order in Decline."

## songs
**Walking Disaster (2007) - Childhood & Lennon Inspired Single** : One of three official singles from the album "Underclass Hero," this track draws inspiration from Derek Whibley's childhood, specifically addressing the father he never met. Its title is a direct nod to John Lennon's 1970 song "Working Class Hero." "Then there's this track which draws from Derek's childhood, including the father he never met. It's called Walking Disaster."
**Underclass Hero (2007) - Lennon-Influenced Title Track Single** : This is the title track from the album "Underclass Hero" and was also released as a single. Like "Walking Disaster," it reflects John Lennon's significant songwriting influence on Derek Whibley. "John remains a big, big songwriting inspiration for Derek which means that this might be a good time to play the title track because it too was a single."
**Screaming Bloody Murder (2011) - Three-Year Single Hiatus Breaker** : The title track from the album "Screaming Bloody Murder," this song marked the first Sum 41 single released in three years. "The title track was the first some 41 single in three years."
**Blood in My Eyes (2011) - Grammy Nominated Track** : Featured on the "Screaming Bloody Murder" album, this song was nominated for a Best Hard Rock Metal Performance Grammy. It ultimately lost the award to the Foo Fighters. "a song called Blood in My Eyes from the Screaming Bloody Murder album was nominated for a best hard rock metal performance Grammy, but they lost to the Who Fighters."
**Fake My Own Death (2016) - Post-Recovery Composition** : This song, from the album "13 Voices," was written during Derek Whibley's personal recovery period after his severe health crisis in 2014, marking a return to songwriting. "One of the songs that came out of this period was Fake My Own Death."
**War (2016) - 13 Voices Track** : A track featured on the 2016 album "13 Voices." "Some 41 with war, another track from the 13 Voices album from 2016."
**God Save Us All (Death to Pop) (2016) - 13 Voices Track** : A song from the 2016 album "13 Voices." "That's God save us all, death to pop."
**45 (2019) - Politically Interpreted Song** : This song from "Order in Decline" is sometimes perceived by listeners as being about Donald Trump due to its title. However, Derek Whibley clarifies it's about his personal feelings, anger, and frustrations, describing it as "you're just not my kind of guy" rather than a protest song calling out specific policies, unlike "Rage Against the Machine" style tracks. "we have a song called 45, which is about him in a way, but not."
**With Me (Undated, from post-2004/2007 album)** : This ballad was initially not highly regarded by the band and was never released as an official single, though a video was made. About a year after its album release, it unexpectedly entered the iTunes Top 10 for weeks. Ten years later, while touring in Europe, the band played it for fun expecting no recognition, but the entire festival crowd sang along, making it the biggest song of their set. It also amassed huge numbers on YouTube, comparable to "Fat Lip." The band considers it an unexpected hit that gained popularity without traditional promotion. "I was always surprised we had this song called With Me and it was a ballad and we never really thought much of it."
**Over My Head (2002) - Live Recording from London, Ontario** : This song, originally from the "Does This Look Infected" album, had a live version recorded in London, Ontario, in April 2005. "There's a live version of Over My Head, originally from the Does This Look Infected Record, recorded in London, Ontario in April of 2005."
**Fat Lip (Undated) - Benchmark for YouTube Popularity** : This song is referenced as a benchmark for high YouTube view numbers, indicating its significant popularity and status as a known hit for the band. "kind of like the same as fat lip numbers and I just couldn't ever understand it."

## curiosities
**Band Formation & Early Success** : Sum 41 formed during a summer vacation in the 1990s, with their initial period from 1996 through 2005 characterized by intense activity and rapid success, ultimately selling over 10 million records. "The band burned very hot and very bright from 1996 through to 2005."
**Derek Whibley's Constant Role** : Derek Whibley has been the only constant member of Sum 41 throughout its history, acting as the frontman, guitarist, chief songwriter, and producer, effectively embodying "the band." "He's been the only constant in the group."
**Derek Whibley's Chronic Back Pain & Alcohol Use** : Derek experienced chronic back pain from extensive touring, which he attempted to manage by drinking alcohol. This continued through a three-year album cycle tour, and worsened significantly during a year-long break from touring in 2013, as he had "no responsibility, no shows, nothing to do and nothing to curb any kind of bad behavior." "My drinking didn't get any better. With a year of that, having no responsibility, no shows, nothing to do and nothing to curb any kind of bad behavior, it just got worse in that year."
**Dave Baksh's Departure & Return** : Lead guitarist Dave Baksh quit the band in 2006 due to an argument over musical direction, as he preferred a metal style while Derek Whibley sought a more artistic pop-punk approach. Baksh left to focus on his own group, Brown Brigade, reducing Sum 41 to a trio. He later rejoined the band in 2015 for the "13 Voices" album, and his replacement, Tom Thacker, remained. "Lead guitarist Dave Brown sound quit. I came down to an argument of musical direction."
**Derek Whibley's Writer's Block** : Prior to writing "Underclass Hero," Derek Whibley experienced a "paranoid writer's block" and lost confidence in his abilities, leading to him taking nearly a year to write all the songs for the album. "Meanwhile Derek had run into some kind of paranoid writers block. He lost confidence in his abilities which is why it took him nearly a year to write all the songs to the follow up to Chuck."
**Derek Whibley's Self-Production** : Derek frequently produced Sum 41's albums, including "Underclass Hero" and "Screaming Bloody Murder," often by necessity when preferred producers like Gil Norton were unavailable, working extensively alone on the music. "Derek didn't want to do it but they couldn't find anybody else so in the end the work fell to him."
**Derek Whibley's Health Catastrophe (May 2014)** : In May 2014, Derek Whibley was hospitalized for approximately a month with "Leverie Kidney failure" (likely liver and kidney failure), followed by two to three months of outpatient care, largely supported by his mother, a nurse. During this time, he required hospital check-ups every other day. The illness caused severe physical and cognitive impairments, including muscle atrophy that prevented him from walking, and an inability to play guitar, make chords, form complete sentences, remember, or spell. He described it as "starting from zero for everything." "I was in hospital for about a month and then I was released but I was still like an outpatient. So luckily my mom's a nurse so she was with me the whole time for about another two or three months after that because I still needed to be in some kind of care."
**Derek Whibley's Recovery & Return to Music (2014)** : After his severe health crisis in May 2014, Derek Whibley embarked on a rigorous recovery, including daily physical therapy to regain his ability to walk and play guitar. By November 2014, he began writing music again and felt physically better. To reintegrate into the music world, he started attending concerts, including shows by Aerosmith and Fleetwood Mac, as he was unable to perform himself at the time. "I would say by November of 2014, I was starting to write music again and felt like I could kind of walk around, okay, and it was getting better."
**Crowdsourcing for 13 Voices Album** : The album "13 Voices" was uniquely funded by Sum 41 through crowdsourcing, indicating a shift in their financing model. "some 41 funded this record through crowdsourcing."
**"Don't Call It A Some Back Tour"** : The tour accompanying the "13 Voices" album was deliberately named "Don't Call It A Some Back Tour," humorously addressing the media's branding of the album as a comeback after a five-year gap and lineup changes. "So no wonder the subsequent tour was called Don't Call It A Some Back Tour."
**Reasons for Lineup Changes** : Derek Whibley attributes the band's lineup changes over the years to members wanting to pursue other interests, the grueling nature of extensive touring, and decisions potentially made during periods of burnout. He states he cannot definitively speak for others' reasons for leaving but offers these observations. "I think the thing with lineup changes over the years comes from probably a few different things."
**Derek Whibley's Songwriting Process** : Derek's songwriting begins by playing guitar (through a loud, "stun" amp) or piano, focusing on creating a riff that dictates the song's style. He aims for riffs that make him feel "like I want to jump around or move and be like what I'm like on stage." He then creates extensive demos using a drum machine and all possible parts, usually with placeholder "gibberish" melodies, before focusing on actual lyrics, which he often procrastinates on until recording. He collects lyrical fragments but finds full lines emerge with a melody. "Songwriting process for me is kind of how it's always been where I just pick up a guitar or I sit at a piano and I just start playing."
**Observation on Contemporary Rock Music** : Derek Whibley observes a trend where many rock bands have shifted towards a lighter, more pop, and mainstream sound, seeking commercial success. While he understands this path for others, he notes it doesn't align with Sum 41's approach, and he doesn't perceive a general trend of bands becoming "darker or heavier." "I feel what I find, if I notice anything, I feel like a lot of bands that were rock bands have gone another way, have gone a lot lighter, a lot more pop, trying to be a little bit more mainstream, which is totally fine."
**Total Record Sales** : Sum 41 has sold "somewhere north of 10 million records" over their career, originating as "suburban brats" who formed a band during summer vacation in the 1990s. "This man has been responsible for tons of hits somewhere north of 10 million records, not bad for a bunch of suburban brats who decided to form a band during summer vacation back in the 1990s."
**Adapting to Music Industry Changes** : Derek Whibley acknowledges the drastic changes in the music industry from CD sales to streaming but admits he doesn't fully understand the business models. He instead gauges the band's success by the increasing size of their live audiences, seeing bigger crowds every year and with every album. "I don't know if we're doing good. Well, or, you know, we just go out when we tour and we see people at the shows. And we know our shows are getting bigger everywhere we go."
**Artistic Philosophy of Constant Building** : Derek Whibley believes that for a band like Sum 41, there's a constant need to build and prove themselves with each new record. He feels that every album is "like your first record" and that they should always be pushing their sound and capabilities further. "I feel like a record is always kind of like your first record. You've always got something to prove."
**Unexpected Hit - With Me** : The song "With Me," a ballad, became an unexpected hit many years after its initial album release, without being officially promoted as a single or serviced to radio. Its popularity grew organically, entering iTunes Top 10 and being sung by entire festival crowds in Europe, surprising the band. Its success is attributed to the "digital environment," possibly playlists or movie soundtracks, and it garnered YouTube numbers comparable to "Fat Lip." "I was always surprised we had this song called With Me and it was a ballad and we never really thought much of it."

# curiosity

**The Phenomenon of Unexpected Digital Hits** : In the contemporary digital music environment, songs can achieve hit status and widespread popularity through non-traditional means, such as influential playlists or movie soundtracks, even without being released as official singles, receiving radio promotion, or being actively pushed by the band. This can lead to surprising scenarios where a band discovers a song's immense popularity years after its release, as observed by Sum 41 with their song "With Me," which gained huge YouTube numbers and was sung by entire festival crowds despite minimal band promotion. "And to have one of those now of our own, I see how it can happen."
