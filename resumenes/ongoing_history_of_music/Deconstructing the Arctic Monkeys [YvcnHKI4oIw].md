## albums
**AM (2013)** : AM is the band's 2013 album, from which the song "Why'd You Only Call Me When You're High?" was one of six singles.
"The Arctic Monkeys with one of the six singles from their 2013 album AM."
**Favorite Worst Nightmare (Second Studio Record)** : This was the band's second studio record, featuring the international hit "Fluorescent Adolescent."
"The Arctic Monkeys with one of their first big international hits, Florescent Adolescent from their second studio record, Favorite Worst Nightmare."
**Whatever People Say I Am, That's What I'm Not (2006) – Debut Album** : Released on January 23rd, 2006, this album became the fastest-selling record in British history, moving 360,000 copies in its first seven days. It has since sold around 2 million copies in the UK and several million more worldwide. The album garnered significant critical acclaim, with two songs reaching number one on the UK charts, five music magazines naming it the year's Best Album, and winning the Mercury Prize for Best Album of 2006, Best International Album of the Year in Ireland, and two Brit Awards for Best Album and Best Group. Many of its tracks were re-recorded demos that had previously been shared online.
"It became the fastest-selling record in the history of Britain."

## curiosities
**Early Lyrical Prowess** : The Arctic Monkeys' songs are constructed in a very distinctive fashion, and their lyrics are considered "above and beyond so many bands." The band achieved fame while still teenagers.
"And lyrically, they're above and beyond so many bands."
**Unusual Path to Fame** : The way the Arctic Monkeys became famous was completely antithetical to the conventional methods of the music business. Their approach to gaining an audience made them unusual but earned admiration.
"And the way they became famous was completely antithetical to the way you're supposed to do things in the music business."
**Sheffield Origins** : The Arctic Monkeys hail from Sheffield, England, a city with a rich musical history, particularly noted for synth pop in the 1980s and the subsequent era of Acid House. Growing up in Sheffield in the 90s meant being aware of its diverse music scene.
"In the case of the Arctic Monkeys, they're from Sheffield."
**High Green Isolation** : All four members of the band originated from High Green, an isolated northern suburb of Sheffield. During their formative years, the lack of a direct transit connection to downtown meant young residents often stayed within their neighborhood, fostering local activities like forming bands. This close proximity, with everyone living within a ten-minute walk and attending the same school, facilitated the band's formation.
"All four guys are from an isolated northern suburb called High Green."
**Alex Turner's Lyrical Talent** : Alex Turner is recognized as one of the best lyricists of his generation in non-hip hop music, possibly one of the best alt-rock lyricists since Morrissey. He developed a habit of writing song lyrics in the back of his school books when bored in high school.
"Alex Turner is one of the best lyricists we've seen in our lifetimes, at least of the non-hip hop variety."
**Matt Helders' Drummer Origin** : Matt Helders became the band's drummer by necessity, as all other instruments were already claimed, making him the "Ringo" of the group. His background included being "very much into beats" and listening to Motown due to his parents' influence. By his teens, he was deeply immersed in hip hop, with Wu Tang, Outcast, and Eminem as favorites, and he harbored dreams of becoming a DJ by age 14.
"He didn't want to be the Arctic Monkeys drummer, but since all the other instruments were taken, he had no choice."
**Matt Helders' Hip Hop and Dance Scene Disillusionment** : By the late 1990s, Matt Helders' interest in the dance scene, encompassing genres like garage and jungle, waned as it became more commercial ("big money territory"). This shift was a "huge turnoff" for those who preferred the underground nature of these scenes.
"But by the end of the 90s, the dance scene that Matt was interested in had begun to morph into big money territory."
**Embracing File Sharing for Fame** : The Arctic Monkeys are sometimes credited as the first band to effectively use the internet to break through to a mass audience, achieving this when they were in their late teens and early twenties. In 2003, they began recording demos at Sheffield's Two Fly studio, accumulating 22 tracks by the following year. These demos were burned onto CDs and given away at gigs for two years, leading fans to rip them to MP3s and share them online.
"The Arctic Monkeys are sometimes credited as being the first band to use the internet to break through to a mass audience."
**The Sheriff's Crucial Role** : A fan named Mark Bull, known as "The Sheriff" in Arctic Monkeys lore, was instrumental in their online breakthrough. He is believed to be the first person to post the band's demo tracks online for public access. As the band distributed more songs and played more shows, The Sheriff continued to upload their material, collecting it under the title "Beneath the Boardwalk," a reference to one of the band's regular venues. His website eventually hosted 18 Arctic Monkeys songs.
"Of the fans, the most important was a guy named Mark Bull, a dude known in an Arctic Monkeys lore as The Sheriff."
**Band's Initial Unawareness of Online Sharing** : Initially, the Arctic Monkeys were unaware that their music was being posted online. They only began to notice the impact when crowds at their gigs grew larger and a significant number of fans were singing along to songs the band hadn't officially released.
"At first, the band paid absolutely no attention to this."
**Virtuous Circle of Internet Growth** : The online sharing of demos, facilitated by The Sheriff, created a "virtuous circle" of increasing popularity. Fans downloaded, shared, burned, and created MySpace sites dedicated to the songs. This led to widespread word-of-mouth, more downloads, and larger attendance at gigs, establishing a self-generating community that grew rapidly.
"And this created a virtuous circle."
**Defying Music Industry Fears** : At a time when the music industry was "freaking out" over file sharing, the Arctic Monkeys leveraged it to become famous. Despite industry warnings that giving away music for free would lead to financial ruin, the band continued to embrace online sharing. This approach proved that sharing music online could broaden and deepen a band's appeal, setting a new standard for rapidly building a passionate fanbase.
"At a time when the music industry was absolutely freaking out over file sharing and downloading, the Arctic Monkeys were using that to become famous."
**Media Frenzy for Internet Breakthrough** : When the media recognized that the Arctic Monkeys were poised to be the first band to achieve mainstream success through the internet, a "frenzy" ensued, intensifying their rapid rise to fame.
"And when the media figured out that the Arctic Monkeys seemed to be headed towards becoming the first band to break out of the internet, the frenzy got even crazier."

## curiosities
**Long History and Mid-90s Success** : Pulp had been active since the late 1970s but did not achieve significant success until the mid-1990s, coinciding with when the Arctic Monkeys were in junior high. This timing meant Pulp was an unavoidable presence for young, aspiring musicians in Sheffield.
"They've been around since the late 1970s, but didn't have any kind of success until the middle 90s, right about when the Arctic Monkeys were in junior high."
**Artsy Style and Observational Lyrics** : Pulp was noted for being "more artsy than most," with lead singer Jarvis Cocker's lyrics frequently characterized as "observational." This lyrical approach was a key influence on the Arctic Monkeys.
"They were more artsy than most, with singer-jarvis Cochars lyrics often being… well of the observational kind."
**Unavoidable Influence in Sheffield** : For anyone growing up in Sheffield, England, during the mid-90s, Pulp was an inescapable part of the musical landscape, making them a strong influence on local bands like the Arctic Monkeys.
"And if you were growing up in Sheffield England at the time, there was no way that you could have avoided them."

# curiosity

**Sheffield's Musical Legacy** : Sheffield, England, despite receiving less attention than London, Manchester, or Liverpool for its music scenes, boasts a significant musical history. Anyone growing up there in the 1990s would have been exposed to its rich array of musicians, bands, DJs, clubs, concert venues, and record labels.
"While London, Manchester and Liverpool get most of the attention when it comes to English music scenes, you cannot ignore Sheffield."
**Poetry: Economical Use of Words** : Poetry can be defined as the economical use of words, aiming for maximum information, emotion, entertainment, and enlightenment in the fewest possible words, regardless of the subject's complexity. This concept of concise yet impactful language is reflected in Alex Turner's lyrical style.
"Poetry can be defined as economical use of words."
**Two Fly Studio (Sheffield)** : In 2003, the Arctic Monkeys began recording their initial demos at Two Fly, a local studio in Sheffield. The band recorded a total of 22 tracks there over two years.
"In 2003, when everyone was just in their late teens, the band started recording demos at a local Sheffield studio called Two Fly."
**Beneath the Boardwalk Demos** : "Beneath the Boardwalk" was the title given to a collection of Arctic Monkeys demo tracks by a fan named Mark Bull, also known as The Sheriff. This title referenced a regular venue where the band performed. The collection, which eventually contained 18 songs, was instrumental in the band's online breakthrough as fans downloaded and shared the music.
"He collected everything under the title Beneath the Boardwalk, something he came up with himself referencing a regular Monkeys venue."
