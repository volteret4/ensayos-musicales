# artist - Suzanne Vega

## songs
**Tom's Diner** : This song was used by the Fraunhofer Institute in Germany as a crucial test case during the perfection of the Moving Pictures Expert Group Layer 3 (MP3) compression algorithm. Researchers selected it because it was a very pure acapella recording, which allowed them to easily detect distortions and glitches in the compression if the algorithm was not absolutely perfect. They meticulously listened to tens of thousands of versions of the song to fine-tune the mathematical processes for optimal audio quality in compressed music files. "When the Fronthoffer Institute in Germany was working on perfecting moving pictures expert group Layer 3, they used a song called Tom's Diner by Suzanne Vega as a test case. The reason they picked this song was that it was a very pure accapella recording, and unless the algorithm was absolutely perfect, you could hear distortions and glitches in the compression."

# label - Billboard magazine

## curiosities
**Origin of Name and Founding (1894)** : Billboard magazine was established in 1894, years before the modern music industry, originally named "Billboard Advertising." Its initial purpose was to cover outdoor advertising, including the posting of bills, flyers, posters, and billboards, which were the primary means of advertising before radio and television. "Its original name was Billboard Advertising, and its first job was covering advertising and the posting of bills."
**Evolution into Entertainment and Music (Early 1900s)** : The magazine's coverage expanded from general advertising to county fairs, circuses, vaudeville, and the gossip generated by these entertainment industries, thereby entering the entertainment sector. In the early 1900s, with the rise of sales of photographs and pre-recorded discs, "Billboard Advertising" began tracking which records were selling most, covering the jukebox industry, and later, radio airplay. "From there, the magazine moved on to covering county fairs, circuses, vaudeville, and Superlux show, and the kind of advertising those things did and the gossip those industries generated. This got the magazine into the entertainment industry. But then in the early 1900s, when sales of photographs and pre-recorded discs and records began to take off, Billboard Advertising started taking an interest in that."
**First Music Chart and Industry Leadership (1936-1950s)** : By the time of the Great Depression, Billboard was predominantly focused on music. It published its first music chart on January 4, 1936, followed by additional charts ranking America's best-selling records. By the 1950s, Billboard had become the definitive industry leader in tracking music sales. "And then on January 4th, 1936, the magazine published its first ever music chart. That was followed by more charts which ranked the best selling records in America. And by the 1950s, Billboard was the industry leader when it came to tracking the sales of music."
**Continued Relevance and Name Retention** : Billboard magazine remains one of the most crucial and influential sources for music and industry information globally today. It retained its original name throughout its evolution, reflecting its historical roots in covering "billboards" as a primary advertising medium. "Billboard still exists today and is considered to be one of the most important and influential sources for music and industry information in the world. And through it all, it kept its original name because back in the day, Billboard was really all about Billboard."

# instrument - Synthesizers

## curiosities
**Early Limitations and Polyphony (Pre-FM Synthesis)** : In their early development, keyboard synthesizers were monophonic, capable of playing only one note at a time, which restricted them from playing chords. The subsequent introduction of polyphonic synthesizers allowed for multiple notes, but the sounds produced were often artificial and robotic, thereby limiting their practical applications. "In the early days of keyboard synthesizers, you could only play one note at a time. You couldn't play chords. Then came polyphonic synthesizers, which allowed you to play, you know, two, three, four, five, six, whatever, as many notes as you want. Press the keys, you get sounds. But since sounded very artificial and very robotic, which limited their use."
**Frequency Modulation (FM) Synthesis Breakthrough** : John Chowning at Stanford University pioneered Frequency Modulation (FM) Synthesis, a technology that revolutionized the synthesizer industry. By manipulating electronically generated sine waves, Chowning discovered a method to make synthesizers produce sounds resembling real instruments, significantly enhancing their utility and appeal. "The big breakthrough came from a guy named John Chaoning at Stanford University. He was fiddling with some electronically generated sine waves, and he figured out a way to make them behave so that they began to sound like real instruments. Long story short, he came up with a technology known as Frequency Modulation Synthesis, or FM Synthesis for short."

# instrument - Yamaha DX7 synthesizer

## curiosities
**Ubiquitous Sound of the 1980s** : The Yamaha DX7 was the first instrument to fully leverage FM Synthesis technology, earning it the distinction of being the "most important, most popular, and most ubiquitous electronic instrument of the 1980s." **Evolution of 1980s Recording Techniques** : The 1980s saw a significant departure from traditional close-miking techniques for acoustic snare drums, which previously aimed for a tight, sharp crack by placing microphones very close to the drumhead. "The sound of 80s snares was a break in tradition. And the studio producers and recording engineers began using microphones differently. This meant that you put the pickup of the microphone very close to the drum head with an inches or even less. The goal back then, the aesthetic back then, was to get a pure sound of the stick striking the drum. The result was a tight sharp crack from the snare."

**MP3 Compression Algorithm Development** : In the mid-1980s, research institutes began developing "codecs" – technologies based on psychoacoustics – to efficiently transmit digital audio signals over copper wires. The goal was to reduce file sizes by stripping out audio material inaudible to the human ear. The Moving Pictures Expert Group (MPEG) standardized three audio versions: MPIG Layer 1 (MP1), MPIG Layer 2 (MP2), and MPIG Layer 3 (MP3). "In the middle 1980s, a number of research institutes embarked on developing a way of sending digital signals down copper wires. These are called codecs, and we're all based on the principles of psychoacoustics. The goal of a codec was to strip out all the material from an audio file that the human ear couldn't hear anyway. That made the file smaller and therefore easier to transmit down the limited capacity of a copper wire."
**MP1 Technology Overview** : MPIG Layer 1 (MP1) was an early audio compression standard that, while functional, offered suboptimal audio quality. It was limited to compressing audio for only two channels, was incompatible with higher bit rates, and lacked backwards compatibility for higher quality recordings. MP1 ultimately became the standard audio format for VHS tapes, reflecting its limitations. "MPIG Layer 1 or MP1 for short worked, but didn't offer the best audio quality. It could only compress audio for two channels, something that wasn't very helpful for things like home theater. It didn't work at higher bid rates, and it wasn't backwards compatible for higher quality recordings. MP1 became the audio standard for VHS tapes, which should tell you something."
**MP2 Technology Overview** : MPIG Layer 2 (MP2) represented a significant improvement over MP1 and was adopted as the standard for DVDs, set-top TV boxes, and certain camcorders. Although MP2 streams were not compatible with MP1, this was generally acceptable given MP1's inferior quality. MP2 was also widely used for broadcasting radio stations, which often converted their music from WAV files to MP2 for hard drive playback systems. "MPIG Layer 2 or MP2 was much better and became the standard used for DVDs, set top TV boxes and certain camcorders.
