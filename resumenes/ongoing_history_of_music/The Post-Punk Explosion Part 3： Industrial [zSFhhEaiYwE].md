# artist - Dr. Robert Moog
## instruments
Moog synthesizer

## curiosities
**Invention of Moog Synthesizer (1965)** : Dr. Robert Moog invented the Moog synthesizer in 1965, a crucial development because previous synthesizers were unwieldy, huge (the size of railway cars), heat-generating, and programmable only with hundreds of patch cords. While still ungainly, Moog's invention was more manageable in size, allowing more musicians to experiment. "That's when Dr. Robert Mogue invented the Mogue synthesizer. This is very important because up until then, synthesizers were honestly the size of railway cars."
**Invention of Mini Moog (1971)** : In 1971, Dr. Moog introduced the Mini Moog, an invention credited with making much of today's music possible. This synthesizer could perform almost everything its larger predecessors could, but was significantly smaller (a little bigger than a suitcase) and, crucially, much cheaper, making the technology accessible to a new generation of musicians, many of whom came of age during the punk era. "The big break came in 1971 when Dr. Mogue showed up again, this time with a new synthesizer design he called the Mini Mogue. This one invention made much of today's music possible."

**Formation and Early Style** : Formed in Hull, northeast of England, a bleak economic area in the late 1970s, Throbbing Gristle began as a hybrid of punk and performance art. They actively gave sound to this statement, reflecting the industrial image in their music, which often sounded like it was recorded in a factory, and through their record label, Industrial Records. "Oh, and another thing. Throbbing Gristle was probably the first group to use the term industrial to describe their music."

## curiosities
**Non-Traditional Instrumentation** : In their early days, Einstürzende Neubauten deliberately eschewed conventional instruments, melodies, or chord changes. Instead, they created music using objects like anvils, sledgehammers, broken glass, banging lead pipes, sheet metal, and power tools, aiming to sound like a "15-car pile-up on the floor of a steel plant." "In the beginning, NoiButton didn't even bother with instruments. These guys weren't into chord changes or melodies or anything like that. They made their music with anivals, sledgehammers, by breaking glass and banging-led pipes and sheet metal, and of course, power tools."
**Extreme Sound Experimentation** : The group was known for extreme sound experimentation. For example, their singer, Blixa, once had his chest wired with microphones while someone gave him a bearhug until his ribs cracked, and this sound was incorporated into one of their compositions. These extremes became a hallmark of their classroom music. "For example, Blixer, the singer, once wired his chest with microphones and had someone give him a barehug until his ribs cracked, and NoiButton used the sound in one of their compositions."
**Shift to Chord Changes by Mid-1980s** : By the mid-1980s, Einstürzende Neubauten had evolved their approach to music, notably beginning to incorporate chord changes into their compositions, marking a departure from their earlier, purely noise-based methodology. "Noe Bowden had discovered to use chord changes, and a new synthesis of styles was developing."
**Influence on Martin Gore (Depeche Mode)** : Martin Gore, the songwriter for Depeche Mode, listed Einstürzende Neubauten among the industrial bands he listened to in his spare time, indicating their significant influence on his musical development and later work with Depeche Mode. "And of course, Einstein's Andy Neubaden. This aggressive approach to music really appealed to Martin."

# genre - Industrial music

**Evolution from 1988 to Present** : The period from 1988 onwards has been a significant time for industrial music fans, with the genre evolving from its primitive clanging and banging into a sophisticated amalgam of metal, distortion, percussion, samplers, and synthesizers. **Mid-1970s Rock Rules** : By the mid-1970s, mainstream rock music had become highly organized and rule-bound, prescribing specific ways for things to be done. "By the time we got to the mid-1970s, most of Rock had organized itself so that there were rules. You did things this way and not that way."
**Post-Punk Fragmentation and New Wave** : After punk began to fade, the environment it created led to New Wave, which redefined the sound of rock. New Wave itself then fragmented due to technological advancements. "Once Punk began to cool, the environment it created coalesced into what became known as New Wave, an approach that redefined what Rock could sound like. Then New Wave itself began to fragment, thanks to technology."
**Synthesizer Accessibility** : Cheaper, portable, and more powerful synthesizers like the Mini Moog became a "godsend" at the end of the 1970s. These instruments were easy to operate, allowing musicians to experiment with cool sounds and organize them into songs without extensive musical ability, prioritizing attitude over skill. "The new cheaper, portable and more powerful synthesizer was a godsend. You really didn't have to know much about music to operate one."
**Electronic Music Revolution (Post-1928)** : Following the introduction of the first electronic instruments in 1928, a revolution in musical instrument design occurred. Over the next two decades, various electronic keyboards emerged, some producing musical sounds and others non-musical. "This was the start of a revolution in musical instrument design because over the next 20 years, a bunch of other people came up with new types of keyboards that used electricity to make sound."
**Synthesizer Size Before Moog** : Prior to Dr. Robert Moog's innovations, synthesizers were enormous, described as the "size of railway cars," humming, generating much heat, and requiring hundreds of patch cords for programming. "Up until then, synthesizers were honestly the size of railway cars. These giant humming things that gave off a lot of heat and could only be programmed by making connections with hundreds of patch cords."
**Post-Punk Era Defined** : The period immediately following the appearance of Punk in the 1970s is referred to as the post-punk era, characterized by an explosion of new music and approaches. "We call this the post-punk era."
**Industrial Music Evolution (Post-1982)** : Before 1982, industrial music was dominated by bands like Throbbing Gristle and Einstürzende Neubauten, known for cold, clanging, and banging sounds. By the mid-1980s, a new synthesis emerged, blending factory sounds with aggressive Technopop elements, becoming loud, harsh, angry, dark, distorted, but also danceable. "Before about 1982, industrial music was the domain of bands like Throbbing Crystal and I was ending Noe Bowden with their cold, clanging and banging. But by the mid-1980s, there had been a change."
**Art Context Podcast Premise** : Art Context, co-hosted by Director X and Taj Krushlo, focuses on interviewing storytellers from the music video world to give "amazing creative artists" the recognition they deserve. The podcast explores their come-ups, journeys, processes, and philosophies behind creating impactful music videos, films, and content. "Our show is pretty much about, it's in the world of music. It's pretty much us sitting down with storytellers that come from music videos. I feel like we live in the world where we don't give these amazing creative artists, the flowers they deserve."

"But you don't see what went into to make that product. And that piece of art, I feel, is the storyboards and the art direction. And sitting down with your head departments and the collaboration. So it's pretty much we're giving them that kind of close set behind experience where you get to see the process of how we get to the finish line."
