# artist - Les Paul

## genres
Jazz
Pop

## instruments
Electric Guitars (experimental)
Multi-track Tape Machine Technology

## curiosities
**Experimentation with Music Technology** : Les Paul was a jazz and pop artist known for his pioneering experiments in creating his own electric guitars and his work with early multi-track tape machine technology. His innovations were fundamental to the development of modern recording. "He was a jazz and pop artist who experimented with making his own electric guitars, as well as messing around with this new multi-track tape machine technology."
**Consultant for Gibson** : Paul had been actively "pestering" Gibson since the mid-1940s about developing a new type of electric guitar. The company eventually brought him in-house to work as a consultant alongside Gibson designer Tim McCarty. "Paul had been pestering Gibson about such things since the mid-1940s. The company finally said, OK, all right, fine. Let's see what you can do."
**Full Endorsement of His Signature Guitar** : After working with Gibson on various shapes, designs, and materials for approximately a year, Les Paul was so pleased with the final product that he gave it his full, enthusiastic endorsement. In recognition, Gibson named the iconic guitar after him. "Les Paul loved the final product so much that he gave it a full-throated endorsement. And Gibson returned the favor by naming the guitar after him."
**Unveiling of the Gibson Les Paul Guitar** : The first Gibson Les Paul guitar was officially unveiled on June 24, 1952, during a special event hosted for musicians at the Waldorf Astoria Hotel in New York. This landmark guitar was crafted from mahogany and featured a distinctive bright gold finish. "The first Gibson Les Paul was unveiled on June 24, 1952, at a special event for musicians at the Waldorf Astoria Hotel in New York."

# artist - Jim Marshall

## instruments
JTM45 amplifier (inventor)
Marshall Stack (inventor)

## curiosities
**Drummer and Music Shop Owner** : In 1962, Jim Marshall was a drummer based in London who also gave lessons at his music shop. His shop became a hub for musicians, including many future British stars, who frequently requested more powerful amplifiers. "In 1962, Jim Marshall was a drummer playing around London and giving lessons at his music shop."
**Challenge to Design Louder Amplifiers** : Jim Marshall was consistently approached by musicians like Pete Townshend and Ritchie Blackmore, who expressed a strong desire for bigger, more powerful, and louder amplifiers. They challenged him to design an amp with specific sound and flexibility requirements. "And the one thing that they always talked about, the one thing they always pushed Jim for was a bigger, more powerful, and louder amplifier."
**Inspiration from Fender Bassman** : Before designing his own amplifiers, Jim Marshall imported units, including many Fender Bassman amps, despite their high cost. He used the Bassman as a starting point, taking one apart to understand its design and mechanics. "Before he started building his own amps, Jim Marshall imported units from elsewhere. And that included a ton of Fender Basements, even though they were very expensive."
**Creation of the JTM45** : After six attempts, Jim Marshall and three employees, working out of a series of garden sheds, successfully created the JTM45. This was the first high-power guitar amplifier specifically engineered to endure and excel in the demanding environment of rock and roll. "Using that design, and after six attempts, Jim and three employees working in a series of garden sheds created the JT-M45, the first high-power guitar amp designed to survive and thrive in a super-intense rock-and-roll environment."
**Meaning of "JTM45"** : The "J" in JTM45 stands for Jim (Marshall), the "T" for his son Terry, and the "M" for Marshall. The "45" originally denoted its output of 45 Watts, though later models pushed significantly more power. "Why JT-M? Well, the J is for Jim, the T is for his son Terry, and the M is for Marshall, of course. And the 45? Well, that was its original output, 45 Watts."
**Rapid Success of Marshall Amplifiers** : The JTM45's first live performance occurred at a pub gig in September 1963. Within just 18 months, Marshall amplifiers had become the most popular amps throughout the United Kingdom, even surpassing the previously dominant Vox amplifiers used by The Beatles. "Within 18 months, Marshall's were the most popular amps in all of the UK, even blowing away Vox amplifiers, which were used by the Beatles."

**Marshall Stack's Origin from Bass Amplification** : The development of the Marshall stack, the most iconic guitar amp setup, is directly linked to the Fender Bassman amplifier. Jim Marshall used the Bassman as a starting point for his designs, making the bass player's need for a dedicated amp an unexpected catalyst for a guitar amplification revolution. "And that's because without the Fender Basement, there would be no Marshall stack, which is the most iconic of all the guitar amp setups."
