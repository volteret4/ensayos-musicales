# artist - Les Paul

## instruments
Tape Machine

## curiosities
**Pioneering Sound-on-Sound Recording** : Les Paul, a guitarist, began experimenting with his tape machine to create innovative recordings. He developed a technique to make "sound on sound" recordings by playing a song, rewinding the tape, and then recording another part over what was already laid down. He repeated this process multiple times to create the illusion of a large ensemble of guitarists, even though he was the only performer. "He figured out a way to make sound on sound recordings."
**Purist Backlash Against Overdubbing** : Les Paul's sound-on-sound recordings, an early form of overdubbing, drew extreme criticism from purists who viewed it as "cheating." They argued that this music deceived the listener because it was impossible for one man to perform it live, deeming it "wrong, wrong, wrong." This fierce opposition contributed to the American Federation of Musicians issuing an edict against overdubbing. "The purists were apoplectic. That's even worse cheating. This music deceives the listener. It's impossible for one man to play this live, so it is wrong, wrong, wrong."

"The best example is Louis Louis by the Kingsman, a song recorded for $50 in Portland, Oregon, on April 6, 1963."
**Louie Louie (1963) – Lyrics Controversy & FBI Investigation** : Following its release, rumors spread that The Kingsmen intentionally garbled the lyrics to conceal sexually explicit content about a Jamaican sailor. Though no one knew for sure, the alleged "secret lyrics" circulated among teenagers, prompting one to contact the governor of Indiana, who subsequently banned the song. This controversy paradoxically boosted the song's mystique and national chart performance. The publishing company offered $1,000 to anyone proving the lyrics were obscene, and the FBI launched a two-year investigation, analyzing the recording forwards and backwards, concluding that the lyrics were "unintelligible at any speed." "Rumors began to spread that the Kingsman had intentionally garbled the lyrics to cover up what was actually being sung."
**Louie Louie (1963) – Influential Cover** : The Kingsmen's 1963 recording is a cover of Richard Berry's 1957 original. Despite its controversial recording and subsequent FBI investigation into its unintelligible lyrics, it became highly influential. The song has since been covered more than 1600 times by a wide range of artists across various genres. "The track is a cover of a 1957 released by Richard Berry and tells the story of a sailor returning to Jamaica to see his true love."

Singers in this era had to project their voices forcefully (belt out songs) to be heard over the band, as all sound was acoustically funneled into a large horn. "There were no microphones back then."
**Introduction with Electrical Recording (1925)** : Microphones, referred to as "newfangled listening sticks," were introduced with electrical recording technology in 1925, alongside amplifiers. This innovation marked a significant technological shift from the acoustic recording methods that preceded it. "This involved the use of these newfangled listening sticks called microphones and also things called amplifiers."
**Impact on Singing Style (Crooning)** : The invention of the microphone revolutionized singing, allowing for new vocal techniques like crooning. Previously, singers had to belt out songs to be heard, but the microphone enabled the human voice to explore a wider range of expression, which purists often criticized as a "degenerate form of singing." "The microphone became a new tool for the human voice, allowing it to go in all kinds of new directions."
**Blamed for "Perverting" Music** : The microphone was blamed by some purists as a "dastardly, dirty, perverting thing" because of its role in facilitating new vocal expressions and changing traditional singing styles. "And this was all the result of the invention of that dastardly, dirty, perverting thing called the microphone."

"One microphone hung in the center of the room with all the musicians gathered around it, very much like an old recording done about 1920."

# instrument - Overdubbing

## curiosities
**Early Criticism as "Cheating"** : Overdubbing, pioneered by Les Paul's "sound-on-sound" technique, was met with fierce opposition from purists who considered it "cheating." They argued that music created this way "deceives the listener" and was "wrong, wrong, wrong" because one person could not replicate it live. This led to an edict from the American Federation of Musicians. "The purists were apoplectic. That's even worse cheating. This music deceives the listener. It's impossible for one man to play this live, so it is wrong, wrong, wrong."
**American Federation of Musicians Ban** : The reaction against overdubbing was so intense that the American Federation of Musicians issued an edict forbidding its members from participating in any recording session that used it. A major concern was that musicians called in to record instrumental backgrounds for a later-added singer would be sent home, losing income for the time the singer spent perfecting their part. "The reaction against overdubbing was so fierce that the American Federation of musicians actually put out an edict forbidding its members to participate in any recording session that used overdubbing."
**Benefits for Singers** : While controversial for musicians regarding compensation, overdubbing significantly benefited singers. It allowed them to repeatedly work on their vocal performance until it was perfect, without the pressure or cost of having all the instrumentalists waiting in the studio. "Now this was great for the singer because it meant that they could work on their performance over and over again until it was perfect without having all these musicians sitting around."
**Drawbacks for Musicians** : Overdubbing created a significant economic disadvantage for musicians. If they were called in to lay down an instrumental track and then sent home while a singer worked on their part, they were unable to charge for the singer's extended time, leading to lost income opportunities. "It was awful for the musicians because if they were sent home, they were unable to charge for the time the singer spent working on their part."
**Modern Ubiquity** : Today, multi-tracking and overdubbing are standard practices in music recording, used in virtually all contemporary music production. This reflects a complete shift from the early purist opposition to these techniques. "Now today, multi-tracking and overdubbing is how all music is recorded, and it's not just two or four tracks."

"This cycle of life has been going on since the invention of music, and it only accelerated with the birth of the recording industry in the late 1800s."

**Acoustic Recording Method (Pre-1925)** : Before 1925, recording sessions were conducted acoustically: musicians gathered in a room around a large acoustic horn, with louder instruments positioned further back for balance. Sound was funneled into the horn, vibrating a diaphragm that jiggled a stylus to cut grooves into a rotating disc, creating a master recording without editing, mixing, or overdubbing. "Musicians were gathered in a big room around a large acoustic horn."
**"Pure Recording" Philosophy** : The pre-1925 acoustic recording method was viewed by purists as "pure recording," seen as a faithful capture of live music. This philosophy held that recording should accurately reflect a single, unedited performance, and any deviation or manipulation was considered illegitimate. "This was seen as pure recording, a faithful capturing of live music. And it was the only way things ever should be done."
