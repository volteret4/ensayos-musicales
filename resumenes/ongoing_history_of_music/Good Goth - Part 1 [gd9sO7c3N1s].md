# genre - Goth music

## curiosities
**Subculture Characteristics** : Goth is a comprehensive subculture that carefully controls its membership, encompassing music, art, fashion, lifestyle, and sometimes religion and politics, with varying levels of adherence from casual dabblers to devoted hardcore members. "Goth is a subculture. And it really is a full blown subculture. It carefully guards its membership."
**Misconceptions and Stigma** : Despite common misconceptions, being Goth is not synonymous with being a psychopath. The subculture faces stigma due to the actions of a "tiny, tiny, tiny few" extremists who commit violent acts like the Columbine or Dawson College shootings. While Goth fashion and music can be dark, adherents are typically highly opinionated and not inherently evil. "To be Goth or to be Intugoth is not to be a psychopath."
**Global Reach as Rock Subculture** : Goth has the potential to be the largest rock-related subculture globally, influencing music, comic books, cartoons, fashion, television, and movies, to the extent that it's suggested director Tim Burton's work wouldn't exist without it. "Goth could very well be the biggest rock-related subculture in the world."
**British Origins** : Goth music emerged as a significant byproduct of the British punk rock explosion of the 1970s, making it primarily a British creation, despite some early American involvement. "Goth music was one of the many different byproducts of the British end of the punk rock explosion of the 1970s."
**Early Meaning of "Gothic"** : Initially, describing music as "Gothic" referred to its doomy, gloomy atmosphere, drawing parallels to themes found in 19th-century Gothic literature, such as Mary Shelley's "Frankenstein," and even older Gothic architecture or the Visigoths. "At first, describing music as Gothic meant that it had a doomy gloomy atmosphere."
**Roots in Punk Refugee Movement** : A considerable number of punk refugees gravitated towards dark, gloomy music during a period of high unemployment, strikes, and recession in England, which was particularly difficult for young people. This collective turn towards darkness helped form the Goth scene. "Whatever the case, a significant number of punk refugees began to turn towards dark gloomy music because it was... Well, it was a dark gloomy time in England."
**Initial Musical Characteristics** : Early Goth music was less aggressive and more introspective and artsy than punk. It included an intellectual component, incorporating elements of traditional Gothic horror, existentialist philosophy, romanticism, and nihilism, alongside inspirations from monsters, The Addams Family, B-horror movies from the 1950s and 60s, and The Rocky Horror Picture Show. "Early Goth music was less aggressive than punk. It was also introspective and artsy."
**Strength and Conviction over Whining** : Despite its dark and introspective nature, Goth was characterized as possessing strength and conviction rather than being "whiny," a quality that was celebrated. "Now, despite this darkness, Goth was never whiny. Introspective, maybe, but there was a strength there, a strength and a conviction that needed to be celebrated."
**Reclamation of "Goth" Term** : Initially, "Goth" was used as an insult, but by the summer of 1982, the nascent scene reclaimed the word as its official name, demonstrating self-identification and community. "Now, at first, to be called a Goth was an insult. But by the summer of 1982, the scene reclaimed that word as its name."
**The Batcave as Turning Point (1982-1983)** : The period between the summer of 1982 and the end of 1983, particularly with the opening of The Batcave club, marked a significant turning point, providing a central hub for the Goth subculture to take root and flourish. "The big turning point for Goth came between the summer of 1982 and the end of 1983..."
**Mainstream Break in Early 1983** : Goth found its way into the mainstream in early 1983, largely due to the British music media and public becoming bored with "bouncy, lightweight technopop," leading to Goths becoming "new darlings" on the indie charts. "Goth's big break into the mainstream came in early 1983."
**First Generation (1979-1985)** : The first distinct wave of Goth bands was active from 1979 until approximately the end of 1985, with Britain leading this initial development. "The first generation of Goth bands existed from 1979 through to about the end of 1985."
**Distinct Musical Sound** : The Goth sound was identifiable by "fairly tribal" drums, bass players often playing high on the fretboard and carrying much of the melody, sharp and thin guitars, and singing that could be "almost operatic," often creating a hypnotic and mesmerizing effect. "For a while there was a Goth sound. The drums were fairly tribal. Bass players played high on the fretboard and sometimes carried much of the melody instead of just providing the rhythmic foundation."
**End of First Wave (Late 1985)** : By the end of 1985, the first wave of Goth and "Goth Mania" began to recede, yet the music and subculture demonstrated a lasting resilience, proving "just as undead as some of their subjects of fascination." "By the end of 1985 the first wave of Goth and Goth Mania was coming to an end."
**Quiet Continued Growth** : After the initial mainstream peak, Goth continued to grow and expand, but in a more "quiet" manner. "So what happened next? Well, Goth got bigger. But quietly."

# concert - The Batcave

## curiosities
**Founding as Goth Headquarters (July 1982)** : Opened in July 1982 in London's Soho district by the theatrical group Speseman, The Batcave became the central "headquarters" and "Ground Zero for the Goth explosion." "That all changed in July of 1982 when a new club opened in the Soho district of London called the Batcave."
**Horror-Inspired Decor** : The club's interior was decorated with black, leather, and lace, incorporating design elements from 1930s horror movies. "They decorated the place in black and leather and lace with other design cues taken from horror movies from the 1930s."
**Strict Music Policy** : The Batcave enforced a strict music policy, explicitly stating "absolutely no funk." Its musical aim was to fuse "glam era David Bowie" with Dr. Frankenforder from The Rocky Horror Picture Show, promoting an environment of diverse sexuality. "And there was a definite music policy in this place, absolutely no funk."
**Expansion and Influence** : The Batcave's popularity quickly grew beyond its original London location, leading to "Batcave nights" in other UK clubs, franchised versions, and even a showcase at the Danceterian club in New York, significantly contributing to the spread of Goth culture. "The Batcave took off. In fact, it couldn't be confined to just that one location."
