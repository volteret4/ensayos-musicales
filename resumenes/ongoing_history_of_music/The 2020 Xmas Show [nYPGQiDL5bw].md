**Challenges of 2020** : The year was extensively described as a "raging dumpster fire" due to numerous global and personal crises, including the COVID-19 pandemic, economic ruin, political turmoil (Trump administration), widespread wildfires (California, Australia, impacting koalas), the death of democracy in Hong Kong, Ebola resurgence, a record-breaking hurricane season (exhausting naming conventions), the Beirut explosion, the deaths of George Floyd, Kobe Bryant, Neil Peart, and Eddie Van Halen, the postponement of the Tokyo Olympics, the emergence of murder hornets, locust swarms in Africa, the mass shooting in Nova Scotia, the rise of QAnon freaks, and toilet paper shortages.

**Live Music Industry Losses (2020)** : The touring industry suffered an estimated $30 billion USD in losses during 2020. This resulted in widespread unemployment for musicians (some driving for Uber), roadies, and significant financial distress for sound and light companies, with numerous venues shutting down or closing entirely and festivals being cancelled.
**Return of Live Events (2021)** : There is hope for the return of live events in 2021, with Live Nation and Ticketmaster developing protocols for safe event execution, and major festivals like Glastonbury and Acheaga planning their returns.
**Radio Stations Switching to All-Christmas Format** : This trend, which began approximately 20 years prior to 2020, involves radio stations switching to an all-Christmas music format for November and December. In 2020, the first station to do so was in Ohio, starting on September 28th at 12:25 PM. While often presented as spreading "extra Christmas cheer," this strategy is primarily a commercial move to "troll for ratings," as stations adopting this format consistently achieve huge audience totals and significantly higher ratings than competitors.
**Christmas Songs as Evergreen Revenue Streams** : The substantial demand for Christmas songs from radio stations creates a lucrative opportunity for artists and record labels. A widely adopted Christmas track can become an "evergreen revenue stream," generating consistent royalties for many decades.
**Most Covered Christmas Songs** :
    *   **Silent Night**: The most covered Christmas song, with no fewer than 137,315 recorded versions. Written by Franz Xaver Gruber in 1818, it is in the public domain.
    *   **White Christmas**: The second most covered Christmas song, written by Irving Berlin in 1942. It has sold at least 100 million singles across hundreds of versions.
    *   **Jingle Bells**: The third most covered Christmas song, published by James Lord Pierpont in 1857 under its original title "One Horse Open Sleigh." It is in the public domain.
    *   **The Christmas Song (Chestnuts Roasting on an Open Fire)**: Written by Robert Wells and Mel Tormé in 1945, it has sold in the tens of millions and continues to generate substantial royalties.
    *   **Winter Wonderland**: Originating in 1934, it has been covered by more than 200 artists and continues to generate revenue.
    *   "Frosty the Snowman" was also noted for its numerous versions, with one all-Christmas radio station playing 22 different artists' renditions over a 24-hour period.
**"Little Drummer Boy" History** : Originally composed in 1941 by classical composer Catherine Davis as "Carol of the Drum," reportedly based loosely on an unverified Czech song. Through recordings by various artists, the song's title evolved to "Little Drummer Boy" by the end of the 1950s. Currently, there are over 220 recorded versions in nearly a dozen languages.
**Christmas Song for Dogs ("Raise the Woof") (2020)** : A unique Christmas song created in the UK in 2020 by a veterinarian and a dog behaviorist. It incorporates sounds and noises known to appeal to dogs, such as squeaky toys, bells, and phrases like "go for a walk," and is set to a reggae beat due to reggae's proven calming effect on dogs. The song was recorded at Abbey Road, and its sales benefit an animal charity. The Christmas theme is further elaborated in its accompanying video.
