**Bittersweet Symphony (1997)** : Written by Richard Ashcroft, this song was inspired by a five-note progression from Andrew Loog Oldham Orchestra's 1965 cover of The Rolling Stones' "The Last Time." The Verve initially licensed the snippet from Abkco Music (controlled by Allen Klein) for 50% of the song's earnings. However, Abkco later sued, claiming more of the sample was used than agreed, and Andrew Loog Oldham also sued for $1.7 million in royalties. Under duress, The Verve surrendered 100% of the publishing to Abkco and Oldham, leading to the songwriting credits being changed to "Ashcroft Jagger Richards." Abkco subsequently licensed the recording to Nike for a TV commercial, which was seen as "selling out" and severely damaged the band's artistic integrity with their indie fanbase, despite it not being their decision. The song was nominated for a Grammy, with the songwriters listed as "Jagger Richards," and the original five-note bit was actually written by orchestral arranger David Whitaker. "Richard Ashcroft of the Verve came up with a song called Bittersweet Symphony."

## curiosities
**Copyright Dispute and Loss of Publishing Rights** : The Verve faced a significant legal battle over their song "Bittersweet Symphony," stemming from a licensed sample. Despite an initial agreement for 50% of earnings, Abkco Music (controlled by Allen Klein) and Andrew Loog Oldham sued, leading to The Verve being forced to surrender 100% of the song's publishing rights, resulting in Mick Jagger and Keith Richards being credited as co-writers and receiving all royalties. "Alright, give us 100% of the money made from this song or we will force you to recall every single copy of the song an album from every store and radio station and whatever worldwide."

# curiosity

**Major Record Labels** : Major labels are very large multinational corporations that maintain offices globally. Currently, there are three primary major labels: Universal, Sony, and Warner. These majors also own and operate numerous sub-labels or imprints, which often specialize in particular genres or niches within recorded music. "Major labels and there were just three of them are the very large multinational corporations that have offices all over the world."
**Independent Record Labels (Indies)** : Independent labels operate separately from the major record companies. They are standalone businesses not controlled by the three big majors, and there are hundreds, possibly thousands, of such labels worldwide. "Indie labels operate independently from the major labels. They are standalone companies that are not under the control of the big three."
**Vanity Label Definition** : A vanity label is an imprint specifically created for a particular artist. While it gives the impression that the artist has their own record company, in reality, it functions as an arm of a larger major label. "You might even come across on the called Av vanity label, which is an imprints set up for a specific artist."
**Artist Manager Role** : An artist manager, who can be an individual or an organization, is primarily responsible for handling all business-related tasks, allowing the artist to concentrate solely on their music. Managers aim to develop the artist's career, assist with business decisions, manage media coverage, and promote demos to labels, venues, and agents. "Basically, the manager takes care of all the business stuff so the artist can concentrate on the music."
**Artist Manager Compensation Structure** : Managers are compensated through a commission model, typically receiving between 15% and 20% of the artist's total income stream. This means that the more financially successful the artist becomes, the greater the manager's earnings. "They get anywhere from 15 to 20% of the artist's income stream."
**Agent Role in the Music Industry** : In the context of music, an agent is specifically the person or organization tasked with securing performance opportunities, or "gigs," for an artist. This role is distinct from that of an agent in the film and television industries, where agents often have broader managerial responsibilities. "In the world of music, an agent is the person or organization responsible for getting the artist gigs."
**Road Manager / Tour Manager Responsibilities** : Also known as a tour manager, this individual travels with the artist to ensure that all tour logistics are meticulously organized and executed according to plan. Their duties include ensuring the band's transportation, timely arrival at gigs, and that venues adhere to their contractual commitments. "This is the person who goes on the road with the artist to make sure that everything is organized and goes according to plan."
**Creative Plateau in Artist Careers** : A common challenge artists face is reaching a "creative plateau," where they either exhaust their creative ideas and begin to repeat themselves, or a group dynamic shifts significantly, leading to a situation where the band effectively becomes a solo project, as seen with The Police or The Pretenders. "Most artists run out of steam somewhere along the way. They either reach a creative plateau and just start repeating themselves or a group might become a solo singer..."
**Copyright Recapture for Artists** : Many new artists initially sign deals that assign the copyrights to their material, both songs and master recordings, to others. However, through persistent renegotiations over time, it is possible for artists to regain ownership of these crucial intellectual properties. "And we were able to recapture all the copyrights of the songs, which had been, you know, we did the usual sort of new artist deals at the beginning."
**Recording Process: Mixing Stage** : After songs are recorded, the mixing stage involves balancing all the individual components, such as vocals, guitars, and drums, to achieve the most aesthetically pleasing sound. These numerous tracks are then combined and reduced to a final two-stereo-track mix. "songs are recorded, then the songs are mixed, which means all the different parts like the vocals and the guitars and the drums and so on are balanced in the most pleasing ways and then mixed down from all those extra tracks to just two stereo tracks."
**Recording Process: Mastering Stage** : Mastering is the final phase of preparing a two-track recording. This involves precisely balancing the overall volume without altering the existing mix and polishing the ultimate sound of the songs using tools like equalization and other specialized studio equipment, performed by a mastering engineer. "This is the final preparation of that two track recording, balancing up the volume, without messing with the mix, polishing the final, final, final sound of the songs with things like equalization and other studio tools."
**Remastering Process** : Remastering involves retrieving older two-track recordings from archives and enhancing them using modern studio tools to clean up and improve their sound quality. These "new and improved" versions are then re-released into the marketplace. "This is when the old recordings are brought out of the vault, the old two track recordings, and are cleaned up and otherwise enhanced with modern studio tools."
**Metadata in Digital Music** : Metadata refers to "data within data," which is invisibly embedded within digital music files. This includes essential attributes such as the song title, artist name, album title, music genre, track number, release year, associated artwork, lyrics, liner notes, composer, record label, publisher, file creation date, and the unique ISRC number. "metadata is data within data."
**Critical Importance of Metadata for Streaming** : Accurate and comprehensive metadata is paramount in the era of digital streaming. The more thoroughly a song is tagged with metadata, the easier it becomes to precisely identify that specific recording, ensuring it is correctly credited and that appropriate royalty payments are disbursed. "The better you tag your song, the more metadata you include in that file, the easier it will be to identify this exact recording, so that when it's streamed, it'll be lard correctly and you'll be paid."
**Performing Rights Organization (PRO)** : A PRO is an organization, found in most countries, responsible for collecting royalties on behalf of artists and copyright holders for the public performance of songs. They grant licenses to any entity that plays music in public—such as clubs, venues, stores, and radio stations—and then distribute the collected money to the music's owners. "These are companies that collect money on behalf of artists and copyright holders for public performances of songs."
**Canadian Performing Rights Organizations** : Canada has SOCAN (Society of Composers, Authors, and Music Publishers of Canada) which grants licenses for public music performance and distributes fees to songwriters, composers, and music publishers. Additionally, Resound collects "neighboring rights" fees for musicians on a recording and the record label, and the Canadian Musical Reproduction Rights Agency (CMRRA) collects fees for making copies of songs. "The main one in Canada is called So Can, that stands for the Society of Composers, Authors, and Music Publishers of Canada."
**US Radio Royalty System Limitations** : In the United States, when songs are played on commercial radio, stations only pay fees to the songwriters and the publisher. No royalties are paid to the performers or the holder of the song's copyright, based on the belief by US radio stations that their airtime provides promotional value. "But when songs are played on American radio, the only fee stations pay are to the songwriters and the publisher."
**Music Producer's Comprehensive Role** : In the music industry, a producer supervises the creation of a recorded song or album, with the objective of transforming the artist's material into the best possible final product. This role encompasses helping with songwriting, arranging, technical studio setups, budget management, and mixing the music, sometimes including hiring session musicians. "In the music business, the producer is the person who oversees the making of a recording of a song or an album."
**Producers as Coaches and Mediators** : Beyond technical expertise, the most effective producers also act as coaches, psychologists, "lion tamers," and peacemakers. These interpersonal skills are essential for managing the often-tricky dynamics and personalities of musicians during the creative and recording process. "The best producers are also coaches and psychologists and lion tamers and peacemakers."
**Producer Payment Models** : Producers are compensated in one of two ways: either through a flat fee for their time, or by taking a percentage of the sales and other revenue generated by the music they produce. "Some take a flat fee. Just pay me for my time and we're done. Others take a piece of the action when it comes to the sales and other revenue generated by that music."
**Notable Music Producers** : Prominent figures in music production include George Martin, Daniel Lanois, Steve Lillywhite, Quincy Jones, Rick Rubin, Dr. Dre, Butch Vig, Danger Mouse, Jimmy Iovine, Mutt Lange, John Leckie, Nigel Godrich, Brian Eno, Kim Gordon, Linda Perry, and Sylvia Massey. "Here are some names that you may have encountered. George Martin, who is famous for his work with the Beatles."
**Modern Music Publishing** : In contemporary terms, music publishing involves a company signing a deal with a songwriter who assigns the copyright (the right to copy) to their song. The publisher then works to place the songwriter's music in various avenues, such as with other artists, in movies, or TV commercials (known as sync or synchronization rights), protects the music from infringement, collects royalties, and subsequently pays the songwriter. "A company signed to deal with a songwriter who assigns the copyright, literally the right to copy, to that song."
**Publishing Deals and Artist Advances** : New bands signing publishing deals often receive an advance payment against the future earnings of their songs. This advance helps them get established financially. Once the advance is recouped, the publisher continues to receive a commission for their ongoing work on behalf of the songwriter. "When you hear of a new band signing a publishing deal, it's very possible that they get an advance against future earnings of their songs."
**Critical Importance of Publishing Ownership** : Owning one's publishing rights is an extremely significant aspect of intellectual property in the music industry. Artists are frequently deceived into signing away these rights to record labels, managers, or producers, which can lead to major disputes and financial exploitation. "An ownership of one's publishing is a very, very, very big deal."
**General Definition of Royalties** : A royalty represents the income that the owner of a song receives from its various uses. This can include "airplay royalties" collected by performing rights agencies for public performances, and "mechanical royalties" earned from a song's inclusion on physical formats like CDs and their subsequent sales. "A royalty is the income the owner of a song gets from the use of the song."
**Mechanical Royalty Definition** : A mechanical royalty is the income an artist earns when their song is included on a physical product, such as a compact disc, and from the subsequent sales generated by that CD. "That's what an artist makes for having a song included on a CD and whatever sales that CD achieves."
**"Selling Out" in 1997 Context** : In 1997, the act of licensing a "cool song" for use in a television commercial, such as Nike's use of The Verve's "Bittersweet Symphony," was widely regarded as the "height of selling out." Such actions were considered uncool and could severely damage a band's artistic integrity in the eyes of their independent fanbase. "Now remember back in 1997, this sort of thing was seen as the height of selling out."
