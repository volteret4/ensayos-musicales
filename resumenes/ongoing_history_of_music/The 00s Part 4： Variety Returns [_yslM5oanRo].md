**Alternative Music Attitude (Late 70s/80s)** : In the late 1970s and 1980s, alternative music was not defined by a specific sound but by an "anything goes" attitude. This meant any music that was left of center, experimental, or disagreeable to mainstream rock was categorized as alternative, establishing a distinct and tribal separation from mainstream rock. "It was an attitude. And that attitude was, for lack of a better description, anything goes."
**Rapid Burnout of 90s Alt Rock** : The focus on primarily guitar-based alt rock in the 1990s, driven by the massive popularity of grunge, led to the music industry and fans chasing those specific sounds excessively. This resulted in a "rapid burn down," with music becoming increasingly diluted and derivative, putting alt rock and rock in general "in big trouble" by the end of the decade due to a limited artistic sandbox. "But in the end, this resulted in a rapid burn down. There was only so many good artists who could play in this particular sandbox. Things got more and more diluted and derivative and by the end of the decade, alt rock and rock in general wasn't big trouble."
**Emo Kid Stereotype** : Over the 1980s and 1990s, the underground gestation of emotional hardcore culture led to the creation of a stereotypical "emo kid," often depicted with black hair, long bangs, black clothes, and glasses with thick rims. This image also carried stigmas such as depression and self-harm. "Emotional culture gestated underground through the 80s and 90s, resulting in the creation of the stereotypical emo kid with his or her black hair, the long bangs, the black clothes, the glasses with the thick rims."
**Post-9/11 Emo Explosion** : The traumatic events of September 11th marked a turning point for emo music, which had previously remained an underground phenomenon. In the years immediately following 9/11, emo bands experienced an explosion in popularity, becoming ubiquitous in the early 2000s. "Emo stayed in its own corner of the alternative universe until after the traumatic events of 9-11. For the following several years, emo bands exploded in popularity."
**Generational Shifts in Music Agenda (Millennials/Gen Z)** : The mid-2000s saw a demographic shift where Generation Y (Millennials), who were "children of the 80s and 90s," began setting the music agenda, creating music for other Millennials. As the decade ended, Generation Z (born mid-1990s onward), a large cohort, started influencing the music marketplace, contributing to shifts in popular genres. "The artists setting the agenda for the middle odds and forward were children of the 80s and 90s. Millennials making music for other millennials."
**Music Cycle and Innovation in Decline** : Music operates in cycles, with genres rising and falling in dominance. When a genre like rock enters a "down part of a cycle," it isn't necessarily negative; rather, uncertainty can lead to "a lot of innovation" and a greater willingness to experiment with new and untested sounds. "And this isn't necessarily a bad thing because being in the down part of a cycle can lead to a lot of innovation. Because when there's uncertainty, there's more willingness to try something, anything new."
**Alt Rock Reunions in the 21st Century** : By the first decade of the 21st century, alt rock had been established long enough for many significant acts to have broken up and subsequently been "enticed to reunite." These reunions, including bands like the Pixies, Smashing Pumpkins, and Rage Against The Machine, temporarily injected new life into the scene and drew back long-time fans who might have drifted away. "By the first decade of the 21st century, alt-rock had been around long enough that big axe had broken up and been enticed to reunite."
