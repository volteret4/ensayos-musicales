# artist - DA

## members

## labels
Rascal Records

## albums
**Ready and Steady (1979)** : This song was thought to have never been officially released by a record label, but some old singles charts indicated it came out on Rascal Records. Billboard, the biggest chart compiler in North America, somehow listed this song, which no one knew about, at number 102 on the Billboard singles charts on June 23, 1979. It then disappeared forever, appearing to be the only song without an official release to appear on any Billboard chart. "But somehow this song that no one knew anything about made it all the way to number 102 on the Billboard singles charts on June 23, 1979."

## curiosities
**Unreleased Billboard Chart Entry** : The song "Ready and Steady" by DA was the only song without an official release to ever appear on any Billboard chart in history, reaching number 102 on June 23, 1979. No physical copy was ever found, leading some to question its very existence before the mystery was resolved. "It appears that Ready and Steady by DA was the only song without an official release to appear on any Billboard chart in the history of Billboard charts."
**Mystery Identity Solved** : The mystery of DA's identity took decades to solve, finally being cracked when a search through the US Copyright Office revealed a track entitled "Ready and Steady" credited to composer Dennis Armand Lucci and Jim Franks, registered in 1986. Further investigation revealed Dennis was a mortgage broker in California who fronted a part-time local band called DA and the Dukes, and he had died in 2005. "It took decades, but the mystery was finally solved after someone thought to search through the US copyright office."
**Co-Writer Confirmation** : In 2016, Joel Franks, identified as the co-writer of "Ready and Steady," was located. He possessed another recording of the song, which helped to confirm the identity and partially resolve the long-standing mystery. "In 2016, the co-writer of Ready and Steady, Joel Franks, was found."
**Unexplained Chart Placement** : The track, despite being unreleased and without any sales or airplay, managed to nearly crack the Billboard Hot 100. It is believed an unknown record promoter with connections to a major label somehow manipulated its placement on the charts, though the exact method remains unknown. "It appears that an unknown record promoter who had connections with a major label somehow got it on the charts without any sales and without any airplay."

# artist - Paula Toledo

## genres
Pop (implied by the sound and subsequent streaming release)

## labels
Bandcamp
Spotify
Apple Music

## songs
**How Long Will It Take (2003)** : This song was recorded in a studio in Langley, BC in 2003 and subsequently licensed to one TV show and one made-for-TV movie, after which it was largely forgotten by the artist. It later surfaced as a looped section in the menu of a Russian bootleg DVD around 2007, intriguing a Ukrainian music fan who posted a snippet online, initiating a 16-year search. "Paula Toledo did indeed record this song in a studio in Langley, BC in 2003."

## curiosities
**Discovery on Russian Bootleg DVD** : The song "How Long Will It Take" (dubbed "How Long Will It Take?" or "How Long?") turned up on a Russian bootleg DVD around 2007, not as a full track but as a looped section in the DVD's menu. Shazam and SoundHound couldn't identify it, despite it being a "nice song" that many people wanted to know more about. "It wasn't even a full version of the song, just a section that was looped in the menu of the DVD that listed other illegal DVDs."
**Online Search and Identification** : The song's elusive nature bugged many people, leading to a widespread online search, with some fans creating tribute videos, remixes, and covers. The mystery was finally solved on December 8, 2023, when Reddit user L. Aerebaba found it in the Sokan database in Canada after searching performing rights societies, leading back to a podcast containing the full song. "Then, 16 years after the first initial post from Ukraine, a user named the Aerebara cracked it."
**Unexpected Re-emergence** : Upon identification, Paula Toledo, then a 52-year-old woman in Vancouver, experienced her phone and email "blow up" with inquiries and even money being sent to her website. Her son confirmed that people had been searching for her song for 16 years, much to her surprise. "Mom! Someone is asking about one of your songs. People have been looking for you for 16 years!"
**Streaming Fraud Controversy** : After its rediscovery and release on streaming platforms, an unofficial duplicate of "How Long Will It Take" appeared, siphoning royalties to an unauthorized entity. This confusion led to Paula's official version being temporarily removed by streaming services in a "Kafka-esque streaming fraud case." "That caused so much confusion that Paula's version was taken down by the streaming music services as some kind of Kafka-esque streaming fraud case."
**Charitable Proceeds** : Once "How Long Will It Take" was officially re-released on platforms like Bandcamp, Spotify, and Apple Music by May 2024, Paula Toledo directed all proceeds from the song to the Music Heels Charitable Foundation, which specializes in music therapy. "But now that the song is back, she's posted on Bandcamp and Spotify and Apple Music, directing all proceeds to the Music Heels Charitable Foundation, which works in the area of music therapy."

When offered an opportunity by a friend who made porn films, he started writing music for them due to the good money, carefully registering the copyrights with Sokan, the Canadian Performing Arts Organization. "Then, a friend who made porn said, I need music for one of my new films. Can you do it? Sure they can."
**Lost Song Discovered in Porn Film** : The song "Alter Your Motives" (known as "Everyone Knows That" during its search) was found within an obscure 1986 porn film titled "Angels of Passion" by online music detectives. The "carnal noises" from the film were removed by fans, not the original creators, to isolate the music. "The searchers then watched every porn film featuring Chris Booth's music until they found the song in an obscure movie from 1986 called Angels of Passion."
**Bafflement at Rediscovery** : When Chris Booth learned about the extensive online search for his song, he was completely baffled by the attention. He considered officially releasing it but faced challenges, as he no longer possessed all the constituent parts of the song (like guitar and synth pieces) and the master recording was with a deceased friend. "When news of the search and mystery reached Chris, he was completely baffled."
**Post-Porn Film Career** : After 1986, Christopher St. Booth and his twin brother Philip Booth shifted their focus from porn film music to specializing in movies about ghosts and exorcisms. "Well they went on to specialize in movies about ghosts and exorcisms."

## albums

It was found in a British charity shop, but despite having the title, artist, and track listing, it became a Lost Wave song due to the music community's obsession with finding more about the band. ## curiosities
**Discovery in British Charity Shop** : The "Death Metal" EP was discovered in a British charity shop, ripped to a CDR. The disc itself was in poor condition, suffering from "Discrot," a phenomenon where CDR layers separate, resulting in distortion and skips. "He'd found in a British charity shop. It had the title of Death Metal, and was apparently released in 2000 by a group called Penchico."
**Limited Initial Information** : The original CD offered very little information about the band members, identifying them only by their first names: Owen, Andy, Sean, and John. This scarcity of detail fueled the online community's determination to learn more. "Again, we look at this CD and there were four guys identified only by their first names, Owen, Andy, Sean and John."
**Barcode Leads to Identification** : The search for Pinchico intensified after the "Death Metal" EP was uploaded to YouTube in 2017, garnering approximately 200,000 views and extensive discussions. Someone eventually noticed the barcode on the CD's cover, which helped trace the origin back to the charity shop in Sherwood, Noneham. "Someone noticed the barcode on the CD's cover. Finally, this turned the search back to the charity shop which was in Sherwood in Noneham."
**Tracking Down Owen Davis** : Owen Davis, one of the band members who used the unusual spelling "O-W-A-I-N," was tracked down via Facebook in January 2020. He was reportedly shocked to learn that Pinchico had been the subject of a massive online search. "Boom. Owen Davis was tracked down via Facebook in January 2020. He was shocked that Pinchico had been the target of this search."
**Band Reunion and New Releases** : Following Owen Davis's discovery, he contacted two other band members, leading to a reunion (minus the original drummer, John, who remains missing). Although they didn't have the original master tapes, a friend possessed a copy of the EP in good condition. Four tracks were remastered and officially released, and the band has since played many gigs, undertaken international tours, and released a proper debut album. "No one had the original master tapes, but a friend had a copy of the EP in good condition. Four tracks from that CD were remastered and released."

# genre - Lost Wave

## curiosities
**Defining Characteristics** : Lost Wave refers to a category of songs that exist but are nameless, unclaimed, and missing from the musical record, often dubbed the "Jane and John Doe's of the music world." It is not considered a true genre because the songs don't need to share any sonic commonalities; their defining trait is simply being lost. "It's not really a genre because all these songs don't need to have anything sonically in common."
**The Challenge of Identification** : Identifying Lost Wave songs has become a serious game for music fans, with significant prestige awarded to anyone who successfully cracks these "cold cases." This challenge is often referred to as "Challenge accepted." "However, much glory in a claim will go to anyone who correctly identifies any of them."
**Canadian Roots** : A notable curiosity of Lost Wave songs is that a significant number of them appear to have roots in Canada, a phenomenon that has yet to be explained. "Now, you've probably noticed by now that for some reason a lot of Lost Wave songs have roots in Canada."

# curiosity

**Billboard Singles Charts Methodology** : Billboard is the primary compiler of charts in North America, employing a complex methodology to determine which songs will chart. Despite this, "Ready and Steady" by DA managed to appear on the charts without known official release, sales, or airplay. "Billboard is the biggest compiler of charts in North America and follows a complex methodology when it comes to determining which songs will chart."
**Napster Metadata Issues** : During the early days of Napster, users frequently downloaded music online, but these downloads often came with incorrect and incomplete metadata. This problem led to many songs lacking proper titles or artist information, contributing to the "Lost Wave" phenomenon. "The problem was that many of these downloads contained incorrect and incomplete metadata."
**I Don't Want To Live In Your World / Plastic Pearls (c. 1998)** : This song originated from a Napster-era download, with incorrect or incomplete metadata. It was theorized to be a New Order demo, a claim which was debunked. The lyrics suggest possible titles like "I don't want to live in your world" or "plastic pearls," with a recording date estimated around 1998. "The lyrics may indicate that the track is called, I don't want to live in your world, or it may be called plastic pearls."
**Thought So At The Time (c. 2013)** : This mystery song has absolutely no known information surrounding it, beyond the placeholder title "Thought So At The Time" which was given to it by searchers. The online search for its identity began in 2013. "There is zero information about it. The placeholder title given to it is, Thought So At The Time, the search began in 2013."
**Cast Away From Here (c. 2005)** : This song is a low-quality recording believed to have been made from a radio broadcast in Seville, Spain, in 2005. It has been given the placeholder title "Cast Away From Here," but its artist and full details remain unknown. "It's a recording made from the radio. We think in Seville, Spain in 2005."
**Curly Toes (unknown date)** : An unknown artist, given the title "Curly Toes," was featured on the 2000 compilation album "Songs in the Key of Z," which focused on outsider music. The song was reportedly found on a tape by someone digging in a dumpster, and despite being on a released album, no leads regarding the artist's identity emerged for a decade after the album's release. "It's claimed that this was found on a tape by someone who is digging in a dumpster."
**Unknown Mystery 60s Group (1960s)** : A reel-to-reel tape found in a flea market in Philadelphia contained a series of songs by unknown musicians, seemingly recorded in the 1960s, with song titles listed and two photos inside the box. Distortion Records later released these songs under the name "Unknown Mystery 60s Group," issuing two other volumes, though questions persist about whether the group was real or a modern fabrication. "It contained a series of songs by unknown musicians that appeared to have been recorded in the 1960s."
**Bird Houses (1960s)** : This is an example song by the "Unknown Mystery 60s Group," whose origin from a reel-to-reel tape found in a Philadelphia flea market remains shrouded in mystery, with ongoing debate about the group's authenticity. "It's called Bird Houses."
**Like the Wind / Blind the Wind / The Sun Will Never Shine (c. 1984)** : Dubbed "the most mysterious song on the internet," this new wave track was recorded by teenager Darius X from the West German radio station Noit Deutier Rudd Funk in the 1980s, possibly 1984, alongside artists like Depeche Mode and The Cure. Despite widespread circulation, analysis, and numerous theories—including a debunked lead involving Christian Brandy and Ronnie Irrune, and an unhelpful Shazam result "Antwon Zero One"—its true identity remains unsolved. "No one on planet Earth or in this section of the Sagittarius arm of the Milky Way has been able to identify that song so far."
**Lost Media Archive** : Established in 2012, this online resource is dedicated to searching for Lost Wave songs and other lost media. "Start with the Lost Media Archive, which has been looking for Lost Songs since 2012."
**Lost Waves-Finist.Fandom.com** : This website provides a comprehensive list of Lost Wave songs from around the world that people are actively trying to identify. "There's also the Lost Waves-Finist.Fandom.com, which is a long list of songs from all over the world that people are trying to identify."
**Whatzatsong.com** : A prominent online platform that serves as a valuable resource for discussion and research related to the identification of unknown songs. "You can also try whatzatsong.com, which is a great place for discussion and research."
**Sokan Database** : Sokan is Canada's performing rights organization, which maintains a database where composers register their songs to receive royalties. This database proved instrumental in identifying the artists behind several Lost Wave tracks, including Paula Toledo's "How Long Will It Take" and Christopher St. Booth's "Alter Your Motives." "L. Aerebaba found it in Canada, in the Sokan database, and this led back to Reddit, and that led to a podcast that contained the full song."
**Discrot (CD Deterioration)** : This term describes a physical degradation process that affects CDRs, where the layers of the disc begin to separate. This condition can lead to the disc being in bad shape, suffering from distortion, and skips, as observed with Pinchico's "Death Metal" EP. "It suffered from something called Discrot, which happens when the layers of a CDR begin to separate."
