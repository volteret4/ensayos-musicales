## curiosities
**Glenn Matlock's Departure (February 1977)** : Matlock's departure was officially reported as a firing in February 1977 because he "liked The Beatles too much," specifically Paul McCartney, and allegedly washed his feet too often. This narrative was fabricated and fed to the press by manager Malcolm McLaren. In truth, Matlock quit due to intense friction with Johnny Rotten, McLaren's manipulative "head games," constant media attacks, canceled gigs, venue bans, lack of pay, and the physical assaults suffered by Johnny and Paul. "Glenn Matlock was fired in February 1977... Matlock wasn't fired. He quit."
**Manager Malcolm McLaren's Manipulation** : Malcolm McLaren actively fostered animosity between Glenn Matlock and Johnny Rotten, setting them up to play "head games" with each other. He also created the false story of Matlock being fired for liking The Beatles, disseminating it to the press immediately after Matlock left. "Malcolm McLaren set it up so the two were always playing head games with each other."
**External Pressures on the Band (Pre-1977)** : Before Matlock's departure, the Sex Pistols faced severe external pressures, including constant negative press, numerous canceled gigs, bans from most venues across the UK, and financial instability where no one was getting paid. Johnny Rotten and drummer Paul Cook had already been physically assaulted, contributing to Matlock's decision to leave. "The pistols were under constant attack by the press. Giggs were getting canceled. The group was banned for most venues across the UK. Nobody was getting paid."
**McLaren's Attempted Re-hiring of Matlock** : Two weeks after Matlock's departure, Malcolm McLaren called him, acknowledging that his replacement, Sid Vicious, was not working out and asked Matlock to rejoin the band. Matlock refused, stating it was "too late" and he wanted "none of you locked anymore." "What if Madlock had returned to the Sex Pistols? How would their future and their legend and myth and legacy turned out?"

## curiosities
**Bernie Rhodes' Initial Firing (After Second Album)** : Bernie Rhodes, credited with mentoring The Clash through their early years, was fired after their second album. While cited as "creative differences," the actual reason was his poor management, including negotiating a highly unfavorable 13-album deal with CBS and causing the band to fall deeply into debt. "The first person to get the axe was manager Bernie Rhodes... A big part of the reason Bernie was axed was because under his supervision, the band fell deeply into debt."
**Bernie Rhodes' Re-hiring and Internal Strife (1981)** : Rhodes was re-hired in 1981 after the band was unsuccessfully managed by Joe Strummer's girlfriend. Upon his return, he actively worked to pit Joe Strummer and Mick Jones against each other, possibly with the goal of becoming the band's musical director. "But then he was re-hired in 1981 after the group was unsuccessfully run by Joe Stromer's girlfriend. And this is where things went wrong again."
**Topper Headon's Dismissal (May 10, 1982)** : Drummer Topper Headon was let go on May 10, 1982, due to his "monstrous heroin habit." "First it was Jermore Topper Heaton, who had a monstrous heroin habit. He was let go on May 10th, 1982."
**Mick Jones' Dismissal (September 1, 1983)** : Guitarist Mick Jones was fired on September 1, 1983, a decision announced via a "clash communique" that stated he had "drifted apart from the original idea of the clash." This followed increasing difficulties, including being late for rehearsals and recording, refusing tours, and his relationship with Joe Strummer deteriorating due to Bernie Rhodes's machinations. Joe Strummer expressed frustration about having to "beg him to play the guitar," comparing the situation to "dragging a dead dog around." "It was September 1st, 1983, and his firing was announced in what was called a clash communique."
**Band's Demise from Internal Politics** : The Clash ultimately imploded due to internal politics, with their true demise occurring after Joe Strummer fired Mick Jones, a co-founder of the band. "The clash, a band that was eventually torn apart by internal politics, but their real demise came after Joe Strummer fired Mick Jones."
