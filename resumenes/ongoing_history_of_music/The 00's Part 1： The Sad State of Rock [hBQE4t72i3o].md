"So on April 13, 2000, the band filed a lawsuit against Napster for aiding and abetting in the theft of their music."

The evidence presented amounted to a printout spanning over 60,000 pages, detailing 1.4 million individual copyright infringements. "335,435 Napster users were on a printout, each of whom downloaded one or more of 95 Metallica songs. This printout spread over 60,000 pages and detailed 1.4 million copyright infringements."
**Early MP3 Sharing Experience (1996)** : Metallica had an early encounter with unauthorized online music sharing when an unreleased song, "Until It Sleeps," was shared as the "very first shareable MP3" on August 12, 1996, by "Compressed to Audio," leading them to pay close attention to such activities. # curiosity

**1950s - Birth of Rock and Roll** : The 1950s are historically recognized as the decade where rock and roll music originated, establishing a new and influential genre. "The 1950s marked the birth of rock and roll."
**1960s - The Rise of the Album and Megarock Festivals** : The 1960s were characterized by significant musical developments, including the emergence of The Beatles, the increasing prominence of the album as a format, and the advent of large-scale rock festivals. "The 1960s brought us the Beatles, the Rise of the Album, the Megarock Festival."
**1970s - Punk, Metal, Disco, and Rap** : The 1970s saw the rise of diverse and influential music genres such as punk, metal, disco, and rap, shaping the decade's unique soundscapes. "In the 70s we got punk and metal, disco and rap."
**1980s - Techno Pop, Hair Metal, and Classic Rock** : The 1980s were defined by genres like techno pop and hair metal, alongside the solidification of classic rock as an era, reflecting varied musical trends. "The 80s, well, techno pop, hair metal, the era of classic rock."
**1990s - Grunge, Low-Slung-Loose Generation, and Britpop** : The 1990s were significantly influenced by grunge music, the cultural impact of the "low-slung-loose generation," and the Britpop movement, marking a transformative period in music. "The 90s of course were all about grunge and the low-lop-lose generation in Britpop."
**First Decade of 21st Century (2000-2009) - "The OTS"** : The initial decade of the 21st century, from 2000 to 2009, is referred to as "the OTS," signifying a period of significant change and transformation in music and society. "This is a history of that first decade of the 21st century, which we are going to call the OTS."
**Y2K Bug (December 31st, 1999)** : As 1999 drew to a close, widespread uncertainty prevailed globally due to fears surrounding the Y2K bug, with concerns about potential computer crashes, power grid failures, and ballistic missile launches. "As the clock ticked down towards midnight on December 31st, 1999, there was plenty of uncertainty across the planet. Would the world's computers crash or freak out because of the so-called Y2K bug?"
**Panama Canal Handover (1999)** : In 1999, the United States transferred control of the Panama Canal back to Panama, an event that raised questions about its potential impact on world shipping and the global economy. "The US handed the Panama Canal back to Panama. What was that going to mean for world shipping and the global economy?"
**Boris Yeltsin's Resignation and Vladimir Putin's Rise (1999)** : As 1999 concluded, Boris Yeltsin resigned as president of Russia, handing power to the then-unknown Vladimir Putin. This event, initially overlooked, proved to be of major historical significance. "we should have been paying more attention to Boris Yeltsin's resignation as the president of Russia. As 1999 came to an end, he handed power over to some unknown guy named Vladimir Putin."
**Generation X Transition (Late 1990s)** : Generation X, who had been instrumental in propelling grunge, punk rock revival, and Britpop into the mainstream during the 1990s, had matured and moved on by the end of the decade, leaving Generation Y to shape the evolving musical landscape. "The gen-exiers that propelled grunge in the punk rock revival and Britpop and all the other alternative genres that went mainstream during the 90s had grown up and moved on, leaving generation why to deal with things."
**Falling Guitar Sales (1999)** : Around 1999, guitar sales experienced a decline, contrasting with the spike in sales of turntables, CD decks, and mixers, reflecting a shift in musical trends and consumer interests towards electronic and DJ culture. "Sales of turntables and CD decks and mixers spiked guitar sales fell."
**Hip Hop and Rap Growth (Late 1990s)** : Hip hop and rap maintained a consistent march forward at the close of the 1990s, continuously attracting more and more fans each week, solidifying their growing influence in popular music. "Meanwhile, hip hop and rap marched on, attracting more and more fans every week."
**Internet in 1999 - Novelty and Dial-up** : In 1999, the internet was still considered a novel technology, with many users relying on slow dial-up modems for connections, making downloads and uploads ponderously slow. "The internet was still a novel thing in 1999. A lot of people were still using dial-up modems, so downloads and uploads were ponderously slow."
**Browser Competition - Netscape vs. Internet Explorer (1999)** : In 1999, Netscape was a popular internet browser, but its dominance was soon challenged by the release of Internet Explorer version 5 in March 1999, which was integrated with the new Windows 98 operating system. "Netscape was the favorite browser of millions, although version 5 of Internet Explorer, which was released in March 1999 and was a big part of the new operating system called Windows 98, would soon spell it in."
**MP3 File Format - Transportable and Copyable** : MP3 was an easily transportable, infinitely copyable, and completely unprotected file format used by students with high-speed university internet connections to trade music, despite the clumsy and frustrating process requiring a certain level of technical know-how. "Students started using the Internet through their schools to trade music that had been encoded into an easily transportable, infinitely copyable, and totally unprotected file format called MP3."
**Napster Launch (June 1st, 1999)** : On June 1st, 1999, Sean Fanning, a student at Northeastern University in Boston, introduced Napster, a new program he developed that made trading MP3s significantly easier and triggered the era of peer-to-peer file sharing. "But then on June 1st, 1999, a student at Northeastern University in Boston named Sean Fanning introduced some friends to a new program he had written called Napster."
**Rapid Napster Adoption (1999)** : Within days of its introduction, Napster rapidly expanded from a small group of 30 people to over 15,000 users. By the fall of 1999, it boasted 150,000 registered users, who were actively trading 3.5 million music files. "In just a few days, Napster spread from 30 people to more than 15,000, and by the fall of 1999, there were 150,000 registered users trading 3.5 million music files."
**Music Industry's Initial Indifference to File Sharing** : Initially, the music industry paid little attention to the widespread practice of people ripping CDs to their hard drives and sharing them globally via the internet. "At first, the music industry paid a little attention to the fact that people were ripping CDs to their hard drives and then trading them with other people all over the world."
**Compressed to Audio (August 12, 1996)** : "Compressed to Audio" was an online entity that, on August 12, 1996, shared what is believed to be the very first shareable MP3 file, an unreleased Metallica song. "On August 12, 1996, an online outfit calling itself Compressed to Audio shared what we think was the very first Sherable MP3."
**Napster's Extinction and Proliferation of File Sharing** : Napster was eventually sued into extinction, but its demise was too late to halt the phenomenon of file sharing, as new programs multiplied rapidly. "Napster was eventually sued into extinction, but it was too late. New file sharing programs were multiplying like rabbits."
**Consumer Motivation for File Sharing - Anti-Industry Sentiment** : Music fans embraced file sharing as a means of retaliation against the music industry, which they felt had overcharged them for CDs for decades, providing an affordable alternative to expensive physical albums. "Music fans felt that they were hitting back at the man for overcharging them, for CDs, for decades. Why spend 20 bucks for a piece of plastic when you could get whatever you wanted for free?"
**Impact of Napster on Digital Music** : Napster's influence on popularizing the concept of digital music files cannot be overstated, unleashing the disruptive power of the internet and irreversibly changing the music landscape. "The extent to which Napster turned people on to the idea of digital music files cannot be understated. The disruptive power of the internet had been unleashed, and there was no going back."
**Compact Disc Pricing Controversy (1982-1990s)** : From the announcement of the compact disc in late 1982, consumers were assured that CD prices would eventually decrease. However, prices remained high, leading to resentment among music fans who felt they were being overcharged. "Here since the compact disk was announced in late 1982, consumers were told, yeah, don't worry, prices for CDs will eventually come down. Problem is they didn't, at least not enough in the eyes of music fans."
**Music Industry's Incentive for High CD Prices (1990s)** : The music industry had no incentive to lower CD prices in the 1990s because sales were consistently increasing each year, resulting in very high-profit margins. "The music industry had no incentive to drop prices. People were buying more and more and more CDs every year. Profit Markins were really fat."
**CD Sales Peak (2000)** : CD sales reached their peak in 2000, with 943 million CDs sold in the US alone, a figure partly attributed to the industry's decision to phase out the concept of singles in many territories. "Sales peaked in 2000, and the US alone, 943 million CDs were sold."
**Phasing Out of Singles (Late 1990s)** : The music industry in many territories phased out the concept of selling individual singles, forcing consumers to purchase entire albums for $15-$20, even if they were only interested in one specific song. "part of the reason that number was so high was due to the fact that the industry had phased out the concept of the single in many territories. What's that you want just that one song from the band? Too bad, buy the whole album for 15 or 20 bucks or whatever."
**Perception of Rock's Decline (Late 1990s)** : At the end of the 1990s, there was a growing perception among rock fans that many rock acts produced only one or two good songs per album, leading to frustration, especially with the rise of pop and hip hop. "This was especially going for all rock fans, because there seemed to be a growing number of acts with just one good song."
**Music Industry's Diminished Interest in Rock (Circa 2000)** : Around 2000, with the spectacular rise of pop and hip hop, the music industry was perceived to have lost much of its interest in rock, appearing to merely go through the motions and release a lot of uninspired material. "It was like with their spectacular rise of pop and hip hop, the music industry had lost much of its interest in rock, and we're kind of just going through the motions and just tossing a lot of stuff against the wall."
**Punk Revival's Demise (Late 1990s)** : The punk revival that flourished in the mid-1990s had run aground by the end of the decade, with prominent bands like Green Day and The Offspring experiencing struggles. "The punk revival of the middle 90s had run aground and bands like Green Day and the offspring were having struggles."
**July 10, 2000 (UK Album Release)** : An album released in the UK on July 10th, 2000, with little initial fanfare, gradually gained traction. This album, later released in North America on November 7th, 2000, eventually sold over 100 million copies worldwide and was instrumental in revitalizing alt-rock in the 2000s. "Take for example, an album that was released on July 10th 2000 in the UK. It was offered up with little fanfare, but seemed to find some fans."
**New Generation of Artists for Rock Revival (2000s)** : The resurrection of rock and alt-rock would be spearheaded by a new generation of artists in the 2000s, bringing fresh sounds and attitudes. These artists emerged perfectly timed to align with new demographic changes. "Rock and alt rock would rise again. And this resurrection would start with a new generation of artists, with new sounds, new attitudes. And they arrived just in time to ride the wave of a new demographic change."
**Cycles in Music Driven by Demographics** : Cycles and shifts in music trends are fundamentally driven by demographics, indicating that changes in audience composition and preferences are key factors in musical evolution. "When it comes to cycles and music, it is always about demographics."
