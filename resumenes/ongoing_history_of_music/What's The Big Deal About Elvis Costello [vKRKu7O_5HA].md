## albums
**My Aim Is True (1977)** : Released on September 22, 1977, on Stiff Records, this album was recorded on weekends, vacation days, and sick days from Costello's computer programming job at Elizabeth Arden. His backing band for this record was The Sham Rocks, whose real name was Clover, an American group living in London, minus their lead singer Huey Lewis. Despite his folk singer and rhythm and blues background, Costello was lumped into the burgeoning British punk scene due to his sneer, anger, and geekiness, offering a unique angry singer-songwriter perspective in a landscape dominated by groups and "lightweights" like James Taylor and Cat Stevens. "Declan's first album was recorded during weekends, vacation days, and sick days from the computer room at Elizabeth Arden."
**When I Was Cruel (2002)** : This album features the song "Tear Off Your Own Head," showcasing Costello's continued passion in his later career. "Ter off your own head from his 2002 album, When I Was Cruel."
**Armed Forces (1979)** : This was Costello's third album, released as part of a highly prolific period where he released a new album every year between 1977 and 1986. His rapid output, while critically acclaimed, often outpaced his record label's marketing efforts and the music press's ability to digest the material. "Armed Forces from 1979."
**Get Happy (1980)** : As his fourth album, "Get Happy" continued Costello's prolific streak in the late 1970s and early 1980s, showcasing his evolving musical style which was moving at "warp speed" through different genres. This album contributed to the "glut of material" that diluted his market presence despite positive reviews. "get happy from 1980."
**Almost Blue (1981)** : This fifth album marked a significant stylistic shift for Costello, as he ventured into country music, even performing at the Grand Ole Opry in Nashville. This genre exploration was part of his rapid musical evolution during the 1980s, moving beyond his initial punk identity. "almost blue from 1981."
**Imperial Bedroom (1982)** : This sixth album represented Costello's transition from an angry young punk to a more mature pop songwriter. His continuous stylistic changes in the 1980s made it challenging for fans and critics to keep up with his artistic direction. "Imperial Bedroom from 1982."
**Punch the Clock (1983)** : Costello's seventh album, "Punch the Clock," yielded his only significant North American hit, the Top 40 single "Every Day I Write the Book." By this time, he was often working as a solo artist in the studio, reserving The Attractions primarily for live performances. "Punch the Clock from 1983."
**Blood and Chocolate (1986)** : This album marked a reunion with his band, The Attractions, and was seen by fans as the closest return to his "old school Elvis" sound after years of musical experimentation. It also features "I Want You," which is the longest song in his catalog. "The album was called Blood and Chocolate. And this was the closest thing to old school Elvis fans had heard in a long, long time."
**Spike (1989)** : Released after a three-year break following "Blood and Chocolate," this album solidified Costello's reputation as an influential "artist's artist" among fellow singer-songwriters. It famously includes a collaboration with Paul McCartney, signifying his high regard within the music community. "From 1989 and a record called Spike, there's just on that features Paul McCartney."
**Painted From Memory (1998)** : This entire album was a collaboration with the renowned songwriter Burt Bacharach, resulting in a "fabulous pop standards album." This project exemplified Costello's commitment to stretching his musical limits and experimenting beyond rock, acknowledging his vocal limitations and desire to grow as an artist. "It was a whole album entitled Painted from Memory from 1998."

## songs
**Tear Off Your Own Head (2002)** : Featured on his 2002 album "When I Was Cruel," this song demonstrates Costello's enduring passion for performance, even decades into his career. "Ter off your own head from his 2002 album, When I Was Cruel."
**Less Than Zero (1977)** : This song was Costello's British hit single and was the specific track NBC initially requested him to perform during his appearance on Saturday Night Live on December 17, 1977. However, he ultimately defied their request by playing a different song. "Elvis could have the gig on the condition that he performed his British hit single, which was called Less Than Zero."
**Radio, Radio (1977)** : This track, critical of radio, was forbidden by NBC executives for Costello's Saturday Night Live performance on December 17, 1977, due to the network's ownership of many radio stations. Despite promising to comply, Costello famously played "Radio, Radio" live, an act of defiance that inspired many. "That other song was a track very critical of radio and since NBC owned a lot of radio stations, no one wanted to risk making the executives with the network angry."
**Pump It Up** : This song is mentioned as an example of how Costello primarily earns money through publishing rights, as opposed to album sales, if it is played publicly. "For example, if pump it up gets played somewhere in public, Elvis gets a piece of the action."
**Every Day I Write the Book (1983)** : Featured on the album "Punch the Clock," this song became Elvis Costello's only significant Top 40 single in North America during the 1980s. Its success highlighted a period when he was often working solo in the studio, while still using The Attractions for live performances. "From punch the clock, a top 40 North American single called Every Day Are Breakable."
**I Want You (1986)** : This song, from the album "Blood and Chocolate," holds the distinction of being the longest track in Elvis Costello's extensive catalog. The album itself was a reunion with The Attractions and was lauded by fans for its return to a more traditional Costello sound. "This album also contained the longest song in the Elvis catalog. It's called I Want You."

## curiosities
**Early "Dorky" Appearance** : In his early career, even when young, Costello presented a distinctive, somewhat awkward image characterized by "bad glasses and poor posture in that geeky stare," which contrasted with the prevailing punk aesthetic of Mohawks and safety pins. He also notably insisted on wearing a sport coat during the punk era. "Even when he was young, he looked dorky, the bad glasses and poor posture in that geeky stare."
**Computer Programmer Background** : Before his music career took off, Declan McManus worked as a computer operator for a cosmetics company, Elizabeth Arden, wearing a white lab coat and feeding punch cards into an IBM 360 mainframe. This seemingly unglamorous background stands in stark contrast to his eventual rock star persona. "If this is a guy that was a computer programmer for a cosmetics company and in the age of punk, whenever one had Mohawks and had safety pins stuck through their clothes and their cheeks, this guy insisted on wearing a sport coat for God's sake."
**Origin of Glasses** : Costello began wearing his signature glasses due to severe headaches, which he believed were probably brought on by the bright fluorescent lights in the computer room where he worked for Elizabeth Arden. This practical necessity became a defining part of his visual identity. "He also developed severe headaches, probably brought on by the bright fluorescent lights in the computer room. That's when he started wearing those glasses."
**Early Musical Influences** : While initially struggling to find music he liked despite a musical household, by age 15, Declan McManus became a fan of The Grateful Dead and The Band, a Canadian group known for backing Ronnie Hawkins and Bob Dylan. These influences shaped his early musical interests before he gravitated towards punk and new wave. "By the time he was 15, though, he had become something of a fan of the grateful dead. He also was a big fan of the band, that Canadian group that backed up both Ronnie Hawkins and Bob Dylan."
**Stiff Records Signing and Name Change** : After a period as a folk singer and a member of Flip City, Declan McManus (then calling himself DP Costello) sent out numerous demo tapes and received many rejections before becoming the very first artist signed to the newly formed Stiff Records. It was Stiff Records' boss, Jake River Yara, who famously dubbed him "Elvis Costello." "He sent out a bunch of demo tapes, a lot of demo tapes actually, and got rejection letter after rejection letter, until he managed to be the very first person signed to the new Stiff Records."
**Huey Lewis and the News as Backing Band (Clover)** : For his debut album, "My Aim Is True," Elvis Costello, then a solo singer, was backed by a band called The Sham Rocks. The Sham Rocks were actually Clover, a group of Americans living in London, with the notable exception of their singer, who simply observed the sessions. After recording, Clover returned to the United States and later gained fame as Huey Lewis and the News, meaning Costello's first backing band was essentially Huey Lewis and the News minus Huey. "The real name of the Sham Rocks was actually Clover, and they were a bunch of Americans living in London who had some extra time."
**Saturday Night Live Act of Defiance (December 17, 1977)** : Elvis Costello's North American breakthrough came when he was a last-minute replacement musical guest on Saturday Night Live on December 17, 1977, after the Sex Pistols faced visa issues. NBC insisted he play his British hit "Less Than Zero" and avoid "Radio, Radio," which was critical of radio (a sensitive topic given NBC's radio station ownership). Costello feigned agreement but defiantly launched into "Radio, Radio" live on air, an "act of corporate nose thumming" that inspired thousands. "That act of defiance, that act of corporate nose thumming inspired thousands."
**Pete Thomas's "Thanks, Malcolm" T-shirt on SNL** : During Elvis Costello's famous Saturday Night Live performance on December 17, 1977, drummer Pete Thomas wore a t-shirt emblazoned with "Thanks, Malcolm." This was a direct jab at Malcolm McLaren, the manager of the Sex Pistols, whose visa screw-up inadvertently opened the door for Costello's iconic appearance. "By the way, if you ever get to see that performance from December 17th, 1977, take a look at the t-shirt worn by drummer Pete Thomas. It says, thanks, Malcolm."
**Prolific Output and Market Dilution (1977-1986)** : Costello released a new album every year between 1977 and 1986, a rate considered "too prolific." This rapid creation of new music meant his labels often lacked sufficient time to promote and market his albums effectively, and the music press struggled to keep up with reviews. The "glut of material" ultimately diluted his market worth and equity, even among diehard fans. "Elvis released a new album every year between 1977 and 1986."
**Rapid Musical Evolution** : Elvis Costello's musical style evolved at an astonishing pace, transforming him from the "angry young punk" of 1977-78 to a more "mature pop songwriter" by 1982. As the 1980s progressed, he branched into even more styles and attitudes, including country, which made it difficult for some fans to follow his artistic journey. "Elvis's musical style was evolving at warp speed."
**Financial Earnings Primarily from Publishing** : Despite releasing numerous albums, Elvis Costello claims he made "pretty much zero on all of them" from record sales. His primary source of income is from publishing rights; for example, if a song like "Pump It Up" is played publicly, he receives royalties. "Elvis says he made pretty much zero on all of them. The only place where he gets money is from publishing."
**An Artist's Artist** : By the time of his 1989 album "Spike," Elvis Costello had established himself as one of the world's most influential singer-songwriters, especially among his peers. He became known as an "artist's artist," a figure other performers looked to for guidance and inspiration, demonstrating how to develop one's craft and grow artistically. "He had become an artist's artist. Someone other performers looked to for guidance and inspiration."
**Collaboration with Paul McCartney** : His high stature in the music world was underscored by his collaboration with Paul McCartney on the 1989 album "Spike." The willingness of a "Beatle" to work with him on an album was seen as a significant validation of Costello's unique talent and influence. "If a beetle agrees to work with you on an album, then you gotta be something special."
**Acknowledged Vocal Limitations** : Elvis Costello has always been aware of his "serious vocal limitations," which motivated him to constantly stretch his abilities as a musician and experiment with diverse projects if he wanted to sustain his career. This self-awareness drove his extensive collaborations and genre explorations. "Elvis knows that he has serious vocal limitations, he's always known that he's need to stretch his limits as a musician if he was going to keep doing this for a living."
**Diverse Projects Beyond Rock** : Driven by a desire to evolve and experiment, Costello ventured into various non-rock projects. These included scoring British television shows like a production of the Dickens classic "Oliver Twist," contributing to movie soundtracks, and recording a "song sequence for strings and voice" with the classical Brodsky Quartet. "This is why he chose to work with Bert Bakerak on a decidedly non-rock project."
**Respected Musicologist** : Beyond his musical performances and compositions, Elvis Costello has also earned respect as a musicologist, contributing articles to major magazines such as Vanity Fair. This showcases his intellectual depth and broad engagement with music beyond performance. "That's why he became a respected musicologist, writing articles for magazines as big as Vanity Fair."
**Credit for Ska Revival** : Elvis Costello is credited with significantly contributing to the ska revival that began in the late 1970s. He produced the first album for The Specials on the Two-Tone label, successfully capturing the raw energy of the Coventry band on tape, an album that "kick[ed] the whole SCAR revival into gear in 1979." "He also deserves credit for the whole SCAR revival that began the late 1970s and continues to today."
**Producer for The Pogues** : In addition to his work with The Specials, Costello also lent his production talents to The Pogues, helping to produce some of their early material. This further solidified his reputation as a supportive and influential figure within the music community, particularly for bands emerging from the punk and new wave scenes. "Just ask any fan of the poges how Elvis helped produce some of their early material."
**Long-Term Relevance and Longevity** : Elvis Costello is celebrated as one of the rare survivors from the 1970s punk scene who remains relevant, continues to make records, receives major mainstream media coverage, is revered by top songwriters, and can still headline large venues like the Molson Amphitheatre in Toronto. His career is considered a testament to artistic growth and perseverance. "How many survivors from the 1970s punk scene are A, not only making records, but B, still considered relevant, C, still accorded major coverage in the mainstream media, D, admired, if not revered by some of the best songwriters in the world, and E, still be able to be booked into large theaters like the Moulson Amphitheater in Toronto."
**Biographical Resources** : For fans seeking more information, a recommended biography is "Elvis Costello, a biography" by Tony Clayton Lee. Additionally, LuckyGoon.com, known as the "TrainSpotters guide to Elvis Costello," offers extensive online resources. "The best biography out there is called appropriately. Elvis Costello, a biography by Tony Clayton Lee."
**Passport vs. Driver's License Names** : Although officially Declan McManus on his passport for international travel, Elvis Costello uses "Elvis Costello" on his driver's license. This detail highlights the duality of his public and private identities. "He's still officially declinic manus for international travel. However, if you look at his driver's license, it reads Elvis Costello."

# curiosity

**Music Industry Operations and Career Longevity** : The music industry often operates in a way that suggests a career like Elvis Costello's, characterized by his unconventional appearance and stylistic choices (like wearing a sport coat during the punk era), should have ended long ago. Yet, he persists, making music and earning the respect of multiple generations of fans. "With the way the music industry operates, this guy's career should have been dead and buried long ago."
**Comparison to Lightweights in 1977** : In 1977, the male singer-songwriter genre was largely dominated by artists considered "lightweights" like James Taylor and Cat Stevens. Elvis Costello, with his aggressive and untimid approach, provided a stark contrast, carving out his own niche as an angry singer-songwriter. "You got to remember that in 1977, the male singer songwriter thing was dominated by such lightwages James Taylor and Kat Stevens."
**Developing Craft and Artistic Growth** : Elvis Costello is seen as a leading example for other songwriters, demonstrating how it is possible to continuously develop one's craft and grow as an artist. His experimental approach and evolution across genres serve as inspiration within the songwriting community. "Leading by example, Elvis showed how it was possible for songwriters to develop their craft and how to grow as artists."
**Bert Bacharach's Craftsmanship** : Burt Bacharach, known for writing "some of the biggest easy-listing pop hits in the 1970s," is acknowledged as a master craftsman in songwriting, despite some of his material being considered "schmaltzy" by contemporary standards. His collaboration with Elvis Costello further solidified Bacharach's reputation. "Sure, some of Bakerak's stuff is considered schmaltzy by today's standards, but as a songwriter, this guy's a craftsman."
**Evolution or Parody** : Elvis Costello recognized early on that the "angry young punk thing" had a limited "shelf life," understanding that an artist must either grow and evolve or face artistic stagnation, or worse, become a "parody of yourself." This insight fueled his continuous experimentation and genre diversification. "It was either grow and evolve or die. Or worse, you end up becoming a parody of yourself."
**Importance of Experimentation in Art** : Costello's career is presented as a shining example of the value of experimentation in art. While some experiments may result in "failures and missteps," the critical takeaway is the learning process and continuous forward movement, which ultimately define an artist's journey. "Some things work, others don't. What matters is that you learn and keep moving forward."
