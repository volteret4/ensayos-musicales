## albums
**Up To Here (Year Not Given) – Studio Album** : This album was recorded away from home, in Denon Memphis. Its guitar sound on this record is specifically contrasted with the sound achieved on *Fully Completely*. "After recording the last two albums away from home, up to here was Denon Memphis and Road Apples required to move to New Orleans, a trip to London and seemed like a good idea, a chance to get away from all the distractions back home in Kingston, Ontario."
**Road Apples (1991) – Studio Album** : Recorded away from home in New Orleans, this album was the direct predecessor to *Fully Completely*. The guitar sound on *Road Apples* is highlighted for comparison with the distinct guitar sound developed for *Fully Completely*. "After recording the last two albums away from home, up to here was Denon Memphis and Road Apples required to move to New Orleans, a trip to London and seemed like a good idea, a chance to get away from all the distractions back home in Kingston, Ontario."
**Fully Completely (1992) – Third Studio Album** : Released on October 6, 1992, this album was recorded at Battery Studios in Willesden Green, North London, in July 1992, produced by Chris Sangerides. It marked a departure from the band's usual live-recording style, with Sangerides building tracks from the ground up, starting with bass and drums for the first three weeks. This method contributed to a "bigger sound," more instrument isolation, and enhanced guitar sounds. The album immediately sold 140,000 copies in Canada upon release, reaching 200,000 within three months, and by January 2007, it was certified for over 1 million domestic sales (a diamond award), placing it in company with albums like U2's *Joshua Tree* and Nirvana's *Nevermind*. It was the only Hip album to be released in Japan, on April 23, 1993. A 2014 Deluxe Reissue included a remastered album, two bonus tracks, and a complete live recording from an invitation-only record release show at the Horseshoe Tavern on September 13, 1992. "they had no idea that they had created something extraordinary."
**Day for Night (Year Not Given) – Later Album** : While on his honeymoon, Rob Baker was preoccupied with ideas for album covers and promotional photos, making sketches and developing concepts. These initial ideas, though conceived around the time of *Fully Completely*'s creation, ultimately "turned into Day for Night," suggesting a later application of these creative concepts for a subsequent album. "But it ended up that all that stuff turned into day for night."

## songs
**Courage (Year Not Given) – For Hugh MacLennan** : This track was one of several new songs written during a four-month delay in recording *Fully Completely*. Its full title, "Courage (for Hugh MacLennan)," is a dedication to the renowned Canadian author and McGill English professor, Hugh MacLennan, who had passed away approximately two years prior to the song's creation. MacLennan is recognized for coining the influential phrase "Two Solitudes" to describe the relationship between Quebec and the rest of Canada. "One of those new tracks was Courage, a full title of which is Courage and then parenthetically for Hugh MacLennan, the famous Canadian author and English professor at McGill who had died about two years earlier."
**Locked in the Trunk of a Car (Year Not Given) – First Single from Fully Completely** : Released as the first single from *Fully Completely*, this song had been frequently performed live under the working title "Dump in the Body" right up until about a week before the band went to England to record the album. Producer Chris Sangerides specifically encouraged guitarist Paul Langlois to contribute more background vocals to this track, recognizing his "unusual" voice as a unique asset that subtly but significantly enhanced the arrangement. "Locked in the Trunk of a Car, the first single from fully completely."
**At the Hundredth Meridian (Year Not Given) – Fourth Single from Fully Completely** : This song, the fourth single from the album, is deeply rooted in Canadian geography and carries metaphorical themes. It refers to the 100th Meridian West Longitude, a significant geographical line that approximately marks the divide between the Canadian Shield and the Canadian Prairies, and in the U.S., the start of the Great Plains. The lyrics evolved during live performances and explore themes of political divisions between the liberal East and conservative West, as well as the negative aspects of the "Wild West mentality." "So the song, At the Hundred Meridian, evolved during live performances. Its themes are metaphorical."
**50 Mission Cap (Year Not Given) – Second Single from Fully Completely** : This song, the second single from the album, delves into a specific piece of Canadian hockey history. It recounts the story of Bill Barilko, a Toronto Maple Leafs defense player who won four Stanley Cups before disappearing in a float plane crash on August 26, 1951, whose wreckage was found on June 6, 1962. The title refers to a World War II US Army Air Force cloth officer's cap, often reshaped by pilots inserting a stiff object, like a hockey card, after being crushed by headphones. Despite initial concerns about a hockey-themed song's universal appeal, its strong lyrics made it work globally, with Gord Downie reportedly improvising the lyrics while playing a "caveman riff" and holding a hockey card. "But we all knew exactly what he was singing about. We'd been on that trip to the Smithsonian in Washington and seen the 50-mission cap and the whole concept."
**Looking for a Place to Happen (Year Not Given) – Single from Fully Completely** : This song explores Canadian history through its lyrics, featuring a lyrical reference to Jacques Cartier, the French explorer credited with claiming Canada for France in 1534. Gord Downie's lyrical delivery shifts perspective, embodying a colonial explorer in the initial two verses, and then adopting the viewpoint of a First Nations person in the third verse, lamenting the arrival and encroachment of Europeans. "Gord Downey seems to assume the role of a colonial explorer in the first two verses. But then he reverts to the viewpoint of a First Nations person in verse 3 in which he laments the arrival and the encroachment of Europeans."
**We Kings (Year Not Given) – Deep Track from Fully Completely** : Identified as a deep track on *Fully Completely*, this song is noted for being "a really easy song to play." This characteristic makes it particularly popular for casual gatherings, described as a "great campfire" song and a "great summer thing for Canadians to do" due to the brevity of Canadian summers. "I think the song We Kings has touched a nerve for a lot of people. And I think maybe in part it's because it's a really easy song to play."
**Radio Show (Year Not Given) – Bonus Track on Fully Completely Reissue** : This song was originally written and intended for *Fully Completely* but ultimately did not make the final cut for the album's 1992 release. It was later included as a "brand new hip song" bonus track on the 2014 Deluxe Reissue of the album. "This is a song that did not make the cut for the original version of fully completely. For all intents and purposes, this is a brand new hip song."

## curiosities
**Recording Delay for Fully Completely** : The band's initial plans to record *Fully Completely* in April 1992 were delayed by four months. This postponement was due to the complexities involved in transitioning to a new producer, Chris Sangerides, and a different studio in a new country (Battery Studios in the UK). Despite the initial complications, this delay proved beneficial, allowing the band crucial time to refine songs written in late 1991, undertake a quick tour of Australia to road-test new material before tough audiences, and compose additional new songs. "The hip had planned to record it in April 1992 but something went wrong with the recording dates. They were moving to a different producer and to a different studio in a different country, so arrangements were a little more complicated and the recording dates had to be pushed back four months."
**Chris Sangerides as Producer for Fully Completely** : Chris Sangerides, a Greek Cypriot, was chosen to produce *Fully Completely*. He was known for his work with heavy metal acts like Judas Priest, Black Sabbath, Ozzy, and Thin Lizzy, but The Tragically Hip were primarily drawn to the sound of records he produced for Concrete Blonde. The band felt disarmed by his lovely demeanor, and he showed his enthusiasm by traveling to Toronto to see them play at the Horseshoe Tavern before recording. Sangerides implemented a traditional 1970s recording technique, which was "completely alien" to the band's usual live-style approach, focusing on building tracks from the bass and drums upwards. "In my recollection, it was our idea to work with Chris, but in retrospect, I think Bruce Dickinson was very good at putting an idea out there and making you think that it was your own."
**Recording Process for Fully Completely** : The recording sessions for *Fully Completely* commenced at Battery Studios in North London on July 29, 1992. The initial four weeks were dedicated exclusively to recording bass and drums, with scratch guitars and vocals used only as guides for placement. The subsequent instrumental and vocal layers were recorded over two weeks, followed by another two weeks for mixing. This meticulous, layered approach, distinct from their previous live-style recordings, aimed to achieve a "bigger sound" with greater isolation for individual instruments, allowing for more specific treatment and resulting in more impactful guitar sounds. "When the sessions began on July 29th, 1992, the first four weeks were nothing but drums and bass."
**Cost of Fully Completely Recording** : The total production cost for *Fully Completely* amounted to just under 61,000 pounds, which was approximately 130,000 Canadian dollars at the time. This budget included 1,800 pounds for pre-production, 3,000 pounds for recording tape, 10,000 pounds for equipment rentals, 3,000 pounds for accommodation, 3,200 pounds for food, and approximately 1,000 pounds for cab rides. "Total costs including 1,800 pounds in pre-production, 3,000 pounds for recording tape, 10,000 pounds for various equipment rentals, 3,000 pounds for a place to stay, 3,200 pounds in food, and about 1,000 quid for cab rides was just under 61,000 pounds, about 130,000 Canadian dollars at the time."
**Guitar Sound Evolution on Fully Completely** : A notable characteristic of *Fully Completely* is the distinct guitar sound, which is explicitly contrasted with the sound on their preceding albums, *Up To Here* and *Road Apples*. This evolution was a direct result of producer Chris Sangerides's "bottom-up" recording technique, which allowed for enhanced isolation and individual treatment of instruments, ultimately leading to "bigger guitar sounds." "First, the guitars. Compare how they sound, unfully completely, with the ones on up to here and road apples."
**Background Vocals on Fully Completely** : Producer Chris Sangerides made a specific request for guitarist Paul Langlois to contribute more background vocals on *Fully Completely*. This decision was based on Langlois's "unusual" and distinctive voice. Although subtle, these vocal contributions are noted to significantly enhance the album's overall arrangements. "Producer Chris Sangerides called upon guitar player Paul Langwad to contribute more, mainly because his voice is a little unusual."
**"Imperial Phase" of The Tragically Hip's Career** : The release of *Fully Completely* marked a pivotal point, initiating what is described as The Tragically Hip's "imperial phase." This period in their career was characterized by exceptional success, where "almost everything went right," indicating a peak of critical and commercial achievement. "What they didn't know was that they were about to enter the imperial phase of their career, a time when almost everything went right."
**Sales Success of Fully Completely Canadian Tour** : The Canadian tour in support of *Fully Completely* achieved remarkable success, with tickets selling out within minutes in numerous cities. The demand was so high that in some locations, The Tragically Hip's tickets sold out faster than those for established artists like Rod Stewart and John Mellencamp. "The supporting Canadian tour sold out in minutes and in some cities the hip sold out faster than Rod Stewart and John Mellon Camp."
**Artwork for Fully Completely by Leav Prinz** : Rob Baker, the band's art student, was tasked with overseeing album art. While on his honeymoon, he developed conceptual ideas for the cover, including themes of a "Dutch life cycle" encompassing decay, decadence, birth, and death, as well as a "back and alien feast." Discovering artist Leav Prinz in *Zoom* magazine, Baker was impressed by her technique of pressing objects like bodies, coins, fish, and fruit onto a laser copier to create images with perceived depth. He contacted Prinz, who then brought a sketch to London. The final album cover was created in Battery Studios over a weekend (Friday night through Sunday) using a laser copier, guided by Prinz's distinct artistic vision and unique technique. The artwork was considered a crucial step in defining the band's visual identity. "And she created this beautiful life-size piece of art. And I think at that point that I realized there was something really special going on."

# studio - Battery Studios

## curiosities
**Historic Recording Venue for Fully Completely** : Battery Studios, located in the "grimy northwest London neighborhood of Willesden Green," was the recording facility for The Tragically Hip's *Fully Completely* in July 1992. The studio was protected by a large metal gate and possessed a significant historical legacy, having been the site where numerous classic albums were made by artists such as Pink Floyd, Paul McCartney, Black Sabbath, Rod Stewart, The Cure, and The Who. "The tragically hip were in the UK, recording what would be their third full studio album at Battery Studios, a facility protected from the rest of the surrounding grimy northwest London neighborhood of Williston, Green, by a big metal gate."
**Recording Environment for Fully Completely** : While the building itself was rich with history, the exterior of Battery Studios and its surrounding neighborhood were described as "dreary" and "wet" during the July 1992 recording sessions for *Fully Completely*. The band had initially envisioned recording in the English countryside, expecting "rolling hills and flocks of gentle sheep," a stark contrast to the urban reality. "It may have been dreary on the outside, but the building itself was full of history, pink Floyd, Palmer Cartney, Black Sabbath, Rod Stewart, the Cure, the Who, and dozens more had all made classic albums there."

**Corduroy Road Explanation** : A "corduroy road" is a historical type of road construction where planks of wood, pieces of timber, or logs are laid across ground that would otherwise be too muddy or boggy to support a proper road. This term is explained in the context of understanding specific lyrics within The Tragically Hip's song "At the Hundredth Meridian." "Well, these were roads made by laying planks of wood or pieces of tinder or logs across ground that would be otherwise too muddy or boggy to construct a real road."
**100th Meridian West Longitude** : The 100th Meridian West Longitude is a geographical line extending from the North Pole, passing through Nunavut, Manitoba, North Dakota, South Dakota, Nebraska, Kansas, Oklahoma, Texas, and Mexico, before reaching the Pacific Ocean. In Canada, this meridian roughly marks the end of the Canadian Shield and the beginning of the Canadian Prairies, while in the U.S., it signifies the start of the Great Plains. This geographical feature forms the basis for The Tragically Hip's song "At the Hundredth Meridian." "A little further west, and you cross 100 degrees west Longitude, the 100 Meridian."
**Bill Barilko's Disappearance and the Toronto Maple Leafs** : Bill Barilko, a defenseman for the Toronto Maple Leafs who won four Stanley Cups in five years, scored an overtime goal on April 21, 1951, against Jerry McNeil of the Montreal Canadiens in the fifth game of the Stanley Cup finals, securing the cup for the Leafs in a series where all five games went to overtime. On August 26, 1951, he disappeared during a fishing trip in Quebec in a small float plane piloted by his dentist friend, Henry Hudson. The crash, likely due to pilot error, bad weather, and an overloaded aircraft, resulted in both men's deaths. The wreckage was not discovered until June 6, 1962, approximately 100 kilometers north of Cochran, Ontario, indicating the plane was at least 35 miles off course. During the entire period of Barilko's disappearance, the Toronto Maple Leafs did not win another Stanley Cup, giving rise to the legend. "During the whole time Borrillco was missing, the Leafs did not win another Stanley Cup, so there's the truth behind the legend."
**50-Mission Cap Origin** : A "50-mission cap" refers to a cloth officer's cap worn in the US Army Air Force during World War II. Pilots often wore these caps under bulky headphones, which would crush them. To restore the cap's shape, pilots would insert a stiff item, such as a piece of cardboard or a hockey card, inside it. This historical detail provides the background for the title of The Tragically Hip's song "50 Mission Cap." "Well, a 50-mission cap refers to a cloth officers cap worn during World War II in the US Army Coron Air Force."
