## curiosities
**Canadian Rock Band of 21st Century** : Billy Talent is described as one of the biggest Canadian rock bands to emerge in the 21st century. "And the result is an unvarnished history of one of the biggest Canadian rock bands to emerge in the 21st century."
**No Lineup Changes** : Since its formation in 1993, the core lineup of Ben Kowalewicz, Ian D'Sa, John Gallant, and Aaron Solowaniak has remained constant, aside from some temporary situations. "Never. No. Never. That's insane. Yeah. There's been some temporary things here and there, but this has been the band."
**High School Formation (1993)** : The band members first came together while they were all attending high school in Streetsville in 1993. "So we started in 1993. You guys are in high school? Yeah. We're all in high school together."
**Members Ages at Formation** : At the time of their first jam session in July 1993, the band members were either 17 or 18 years old. "18. 17. 17. 18."
**DIY Stages - Milk Crates and Plywood** : In their early days, when there were no official venues, the band would organize their own shows, constructing stages by "stealing" milk crates from behind grocery stores and drywall/plywood from local housing units under construction in Mississauga. "And then when we would have the show, we would put the milk cartons together and tape them up and steal drywall from local, like housing units and make our own stage."
**Ian's Parents' Basement - Milk Crate Storage** : Ian D'Sa's parents' basement served as a storage location for the milk crates and plywood used for their DIY stages. At one point, there were approximately 500 milk crates stored there, causing the basement to reek of spoiled milk. "I remember one time there was like 500 milk crates in Ian's basement and his mom would come down and say, Ian, what are you doing with all these milk crates? Don't worry, mom, it's all good. Well, my parents' basement wreaked like, like, like, broad milk."
**Aaron's Dad - Chrysler Truck Access** : Aaron Solowaniak's father, who worked for Chrysler, allowed the band to borrow large trucks to transport their stolen milk crates and plywood for their self-organized shows. "Like, Aaron's dad worked for Chrysler too. So we bore these large trucks and, and basically not steel, but borough."
**First Jam Session - Unproductive to Productive** : The band's initial jam session in July 1993 was unproductive, characterized by "noises and sucking." However, their second jam, which involved covering Neil Young's "Keep On Rockin' in the Free World," allowed them to complete a full song, giving them confidence. "I remember the first jam basically nothing productive happened. We just made noises and we sucked. And I remember us three being like, I wonder if this is going to work out."
**Pez - Rage Against The Machine Comparison** : When Ben Kowalewicz left his radio job to pursue the band, his then-boss, Phil Evans, described the band as being "kind of like Rage Against The Machine." "Yeah, he's got this band that, you know, they're kind of like rage against the machine."
**1999-2003 - Life Distractions from Band** : The period between 1999 and 2003 was challenging for the band, as members were in their early 20s, dealing with college, university, jobs, rent, and relationships, which pulled them away from the band. "It was like, there was that from 1999 to 2003, Ian and I went to a college in university, Ben went to college for a year. Aaron was, everything was just, it was just life happening in your early 20s."
**Band Rebirth - Name Change, New Songs, New Rehearsal Space** : Following a period of struggles and individual pursuits, the band experienced a "rebirth." This was catalyzed by Ian D'Sa bringing in new songs like "Try Honesty" and "Cut the Curtains," Ben Kowalewicz suggesting a band name change (from Pezz to Billy Talent), and acquiring a new rehearsal space. "And then Ben suggested we change the band name and everything just kind of came together all at once and it did feel like a rebirth and we got a new rehearsal space down the front and sure burn over here and we felt like a real band again and was up."
**Canadian Music Week - Critical Acclaim** : The band received positive "report cards" at Canadian Music Week, scoring 93% in 1999 (making them a Top 10 act) and 94% in 2000. These strong reviews garnered attention from industry professionals. "So like we, so in 1999, we had a 93% report card. So we made it into their like top 10 thing. And then 2000, we made it again. We had a 94. And all of a sudden, all these industry people started paying attention to us."
**Jen Hurst - Discovery and Support** : Jen Hurst, who initially worked at a defunct label before becoming Junior A&R at Warner Music Canada, discovered Pezz at the 360 Club. She saw potential in the band and continued to support them, eventually making them a top priority for signing at Warner. "And Jen Hurst was working at this one label, which is now to funk, which I can't remember. And I remember talking to her and her and I got along really well. And I said, I'm in a band."
**Warner Music Deal - Jen Hurst's Role** : Jen Hurst's belief in the band, her move to Warner Music Canada as Junior A&R, and the positive reviews from Chartattack led to Pezz being at the top of her list of indie bands to sign, ultimately leading to their record deal. "That was the TSN turning point in a lot of ways. And then she got the job at Warner and the ASTAS, as her, who her top favorite indie bands in the city were that she would consider signing. And we were on the top of her list."

## curiosities
**Band Name - The Other One** : The band's initial name was "The Other One" for a handful of shows, as they struggled to decide on a permanent name. "Well, originally it was the other one. Yeah. Because we couldn't figure out a name. Yeah. So for a handful of shows who were the other one."
**Band Name - Zigzag** : After "The Other One," the band briefly used the name "Zigzag" for a single show. "And then Zigzag. That was for one show."
**Band Name - Pezz (Origin and Spelling)** : The band settled on the name "Pezz" because Ben Kowalewicz had many Pez dispensers in his room, which served as a band meeting spot. They added "X-RZ" to avoid potential legal issues with the Pez candy company. "And I think we settled on Pes because in my room I had a bunch of Pes dispensers all the time. We'd always have like band meetings up there. And it was just like, we couldn't think of a name. So we're like, why don't you just call it Pes and add an X-RZ so we don't get sued."
**Band Name Challenge - Pez vs. Pezz** : In 1996, Pezz received an email from a representative of another punk band called "Pes" on BIO Records in the United States. This band's records were available in Canadian stores, leading to a request for Pezz to change their name. They continued to use the name "Pezz" until 1999 as they didn't have a record deal at the time. "And technically, yeah, we ended up getting an email when email first came about in 1996. I got an email from a guy representing a band called Pes on BIO records in the United States. And he just basically said we have records in Canadian stores so you have to change your name."
**Early Toronto Music Scene Networking** : After gaining a foothold in the Toronto club scene through Elvis Mondays, Pezz met many other local bands like The Salads and A Flatulite, expanding their network and securing more gigs. "And from there, we met so many other bands like the salads, a flatulite, just a lot of Toronto bands and we got into different circles and started getting other gigs."
**Demoluca - Rarity and Copies** : Their first 4-song demo, "Demoluca," released in 1994, is very rare as only 100 copies were originally made. "Demoluca, yes. Does it exist anywhere? Yeah. It's hard to find. It's hard to find. Yeah, yeah. There was only a hundred copies made."
**Wattouche Recording - Tattoo Music Studio** : Their 1999 full-length album, "Wattouche," was recorded at Tattoo Music, a studio primarily used for jingles. The band was able to record there for free at night. "So it was a big deal for us and he was working in a place called Tattoo Music. And we would go and record at night when the studio, because it was like a jingle kind of studio, but we would go record there at night for free."
**Alanis Morissette - Wattouche Studio Encounter** : During the recording of "Wattouche" at Tattoo Music, Alanis Morissette was present in the kitchen, as she was friends with the studio owners, Tim and Tom Thorney, and was working with them. At the time, she was a superstar, having sold 40 million records. She listened to their music, offered advice, and even made them spaghetti. "When we were making Wattouche, we walk in and I remember I was going to go get a cup of tea and a lanness more set was standing in this kitchen. ... And then she ended up coming in and like listening to our tunes and hanging out and was so cool and gave us good advice and... Made us spaghetti."
**Wattouche Production and Promotion** : The band self-produced 500 copies of "Wattouche" using Aaron Solowaniak's credit card. They attempted to promote the album by participating in events like Canadian Music Week, and the self-released record garnered reviews despite having no label support. "So then when we finished this record, we, you know, we... Again, on Aaron's credit card, we produced I think 500 or something like that at the first run. ... And then, you know, we would try to get on to like Canadian music week and all those, that kind of circuit. ... It did get out a little bit. Yeah. So we were pretty... You had no management either. No management. Nothing. Yeah, it was a handful of like temporary managers at the time."
**Early Management Support** : In their early days, Pezz operated without official management, relying on temporary managers like Susan Jazzvak and Andrew Lownsbury, who volunteered their time to help the band. "No management. Nothing. Yeah, it was a handful of like temporary managers at the time. That helped us out. Like Susan Jazzvak, Andrew, Lownsbury. Yeah. Yeah, just people that would donate their time, literally, to help us."
**Pez - Genre Fluidity** : In the early Toronto music scene, Pezz did not strictly adhere to one genre. They played with diverse bands, including punk rock/ska bands like Flashlight Brown and various hard rock acts, without feeling the need to fit into a specific scene. "But back in the day, like we would play with, you know, flashlight brown and there were like a punk rock on a scob band. And then we'd play with like hard rock bands and we'd play... Like there was no... It didn't matter, necessarily what Jean-Roy, you were, it's just... You were part of this thing, you know? So every night you can go out and play with all these different kinds of bands."
**The Billy Talent EP - "Saved the Band"** : The 2001 "Billy Talent EP" (often referred to as the green album demo) is credited by the band members as having "saved the band" during a particularly difficult period that included Aaron Solowaniak's MS diagnosis and John Gallant's temporary departure. This EP represented a rebirth for the group. "That's actually a CD that saved the band. But that was a dark time, though, as well, because- Just prior to that, because that was a rebirth, like that CD, that green EP represented- Like the rebirth of our band."

## curiosities
**To Each His Own Drummer** : Ben Kowalewicz initially joined a band called To Each His Own as a drummer, having just moved to Meadowvale and meeting Trevor Bowman. "And long story short, I was the drummer in this band called To Each His Own for a minute."
**Drummer Booted** : Ben was "booted" from his drumming role in To Each His Own when Aaron Solowaniak, a more proficient drummer, was discovered practicing in a friend's basement. "And it was Aaron. And so I got the boot out of the band."
**First Singing Role** : After being removed as the drummer, Ben was called back to To Each His Own to sing backups, a role he accepted despite never having sung before. "And I got a call about a month or two later asking if I wanted to sing backups in this band. And I was like, what? So I just wanted to be in a band. So I said, sure, I never had sung before."
**Dropped Out, Multiple Jobs, Independent at 17** : Ben dropped out of school and held numerous part-time jobs, including at Second Cup, Deli's, music shops (Music World), and unloading airplanes. By age 17, he was living independently with his brothers, working three or four jobs to pay rent. "I dropped out. ... I was in the King of part-time jobs. Like I worked at second cups. I worked at Deli's. I worked at a music shop. I worked at music world. I unloaded airplanes for a long time. ... I was doing three, four jobs, you know. Because by the time I was 17, I was kind of living by myself and living with my brothers."
**Mod Robes Job, George Stroumboulopoulos, Ted's Wrecking Yard** : Ben worked at Mod Robes on Queen Street, selling pants. During this time, George Stroumboulopoulos would attend Pezz shows at Ted's Wrecking Yard, where Pezz became known as "the cancel band" due to frequently filling last-minute slots. "I was working at a mode robes on Queen Street selling pants. And I remember a guy named by the name of George Straumelopoulos would come and see us play because we were playing like Ted's wrecking our knowledge. We kind of became like the cancel band."
**Quit Mod Robes, Sought Radio Job** : After watching the movie "Office Space," Ben abruptly quit his job at Mod Robes. He then contacted George Stroumboulopoulos, seeking advice on how to get a job in radio. "And I remember watching the movie Office Space and then the next day I went into mod robes and I quit. And I called George and I said, hey, I want to get a job at radio. How do I do it? How, where would I start?"
**Hired at The Edge** : Following George Stroumboulopoulos's advice, Ben emailed Phil Evans at The Edge radio station for a job interview. Despite having no high school or radio/television education, he convinced Phil Evans to hire him by promising to work harder than anyone else. "And he said, you know, when did you graduate from radio television? I'm like, I didn't. He's like, well, when did you graduate from high school? Like, I didn't. And he said, well, why should I give you the job? And I was like, because I'll work harder than anybody else. I'll come here early and I'll stay late every day. I promise. ... And as I was walking home, he called me and said, you start Monday."

**Chrysler Job Description** : Aaron's job at Chrysler involved inserting six screws every 46 seconds, 500 times a day, at the bottom of a car door using a torque wrench and a sticker. He described it as a "craziest job" where one "leave[s] your brain at the door" and acts "basically a robot." "Oh, man, I would insert six screws every 46 seconds for 500 times a day at the bottom of a door that came up. It was just, it was the craziest job I've ever done in my life because you just leave your brain at the door and you don't really talk to anyone. Sometimes you make friends with people. But for the most part, you're basically a robot."
**Band Banker** : Aaron Solowaniak was considered the band's "banker" because he used his well-paying job at Chrysler to max out his credit card and fund the band's early demos and studio time when no one else had the means to do so. "We're in hell of a paycheck, though, because he paid for all of our demos. Yeah. Is that true? Yeah. I just, when we were making the demos, I would just max out the credit card to pay for the studio time. And then we'd eventually pay it back. But we just needed money. So, and I had a credit card because I had a job at Chrysler."
**MS Diagnosis and Career Shift** : Aaron was diagnosed with MS, which posed a significant challenge. At 24, he faced the difficult decision of leaving a stable, well-paying job with benefits at Chrysler to pursue his dream of being a drummer, a choice heavily influenced by his personal struggles at the time. This decision marked a "huge rebirth" for both him and the band. "Well, Aaron had been diagnosed with MS, which obviously threw us all in for a loop. ... And for me, at that time, in my life, I had to make the decision of staying at the good job that paid well and had benefits. ... But I was really just chasing this dream of being a drummer. And it just meant so much more to me because I was going through so much hard stuff in my life at that point. So, yeah, it was a huge rebirth for me personally and the band."

# concert - Elvis Mondays

## curiosities
**Old El Mocambo Debut** : Pezz received their first major break in downtown Toronto by performing at Elvis Mondays, a recurring event hosted by William New at the Old El Mocambo. They secured a last-minute slot when another band cancelled. "So our first big break downtown was we gave this tape to a guy named William New who had the Elvis Mondays at the Old Elmucongo Monday nights downstairs. And like months went by, he never called. And finally, we got a phone call on a Monday at like six o'clock. Last minute, he said, I had a band cancel at nine o'clock, why don't you guys come up and play."
**Start of Toronto Presence** : The band's performance at Elvis Mondays at the Old El Mocambo was considered the beginning of their presence in the Toronto music scene for the four members from Mississauga. "And that, I think that's kind of what started our whole Toronto thing for four guys from Susaga."
**Continued Bookings** : Following their successful debut, William New continued to book Pezz for multiple Elvis Mondays nights. "And William kept booking us and we ended up doing a bunch of those Elvis Mondays nights."

# concert - Canadian Music Week

## curiosities
**Wattouche Promotion** : After self-producing "Wattouche," Pezz attempted to promote the album by trying to get into circuits like Canadian Music Week. "And then, you know, we would try to get on to like Canadian music week and all those, that kind of circuit."
**Critical Acclaim** : Pezz achieved significant recognition at Canadian Music Week, receiving "amazing report cards" with scores of 93% in 1999 (placing them in the top 10) and 94% in 2000. These strong reviews led to increased attention from industry professionals. "So like we, so in 1999, we had a 93% report card. So we made it into their like top 10 thing. And then 2000, we made it again. We had a 94. And all of a sudden, all these industry people started paying attention to us."

# curiosity

**Mississauga Gigs - Self-Organized** : In their early days, when venues were scarce, the band primarily performed around Mississauga by organizing and throwing their own shows, often by pooling money for deposits to rent halls. "And that was basically how we gigged at kind of at the beginning all around Mrs. Saga, just throwing her own shows."
**Mississauga Houses from Stage Materials** : A unique legacy of Pezz's early DIY stage construction is that some houses in Mississauga were inadvertently built using plywood and milk crates that the band had "borrowed" (and then returned) from local construction sites. "And so there are houses in Mississauga that are made with former Billy Town stages. Yeah, yeah, yeah, yeah. And real crates that were used as stages at some point."
**Early Toronto Music Scene Genre Fluidity** : The early Toronto music scene was characterized by its lack of strict genre divisions. Bands like Pezz could play on bills with punk rock, ska, and hard rock bands, fostering a sense of community rather than isolated scenes. "but back in the day, like we would play with, you know, flashlight brown and there were like a punk rock on a scob band. And then we'd play with like hard rock bands and we'd play... Like there was no... It didn't matter, necessarily what Jean-Roy, you were, it's just... You were part of this thing, you know?"
**No Internet Era Information** : During the band's early years, there was no widespread internet access, meaning information about other bands or music generally had to be discovered through word-of-mouth or by physically visiting record stores. "And for the people listening, there was no real like internet at that time. No, there was no way of really knowing unless you had to do. Well, you'd have to go the record store and yeah, and more to mouth than just hearing things, right?"
**Office Space Movie Influence** : The movie "Office Space" directly influenced Ben Kowalewicz's decision to spontaneously quit his job at Mod Robes on Queen Street, leading him to pursue a career in radio and ultimately focus on the band. "And I remember watching the movie Office Space and then the next day I went into mod robes and I quit."
