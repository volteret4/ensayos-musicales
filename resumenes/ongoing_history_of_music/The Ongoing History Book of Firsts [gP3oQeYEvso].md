# artist - Jack Miller

## genres
Jazz

## instruments
Rickenbacker electro guitar
Amplifier

## curiosities
**First Live Performance with Electric Guitar and Amplifier** : In 1932, jazz player Jack Miller performed a gig at Grommand's Chinese Theatre in Los Angeles using a brand new Rickenbacker electro guitar, which was considered the first true electric guitar equipped with a pickup. He documented this pioneering experience in a series of articles for the jazz magazine Downbeat. This performance spurred other musicians to adopt amplified lead guitar within a year, allowing them to compete with the loud horns of Big Band Orchestras. "We know that Jack was the guy who first did a gig with an electric guitar in an amplifier because he's told us."

# label - Recording Industry Association of America (RIAA)

**Introduction of Multi-Platinum and Diamond Awards (1984, 1999)** : Due to the increasing volume of record sales, Multi-Platinum awards were introduced in 1984, followed by the Diamond Award in 1999 for sales of 10 million copies. In both instances, there were multiple simultaneous recipients, thus no single "first" awardee for these categories. "Multi-platinum awards were introduced in 1984. # instrument - Phono Autograph

## curiosities
**First Device for Recording Sound Visually (1860)** : Invented by French stenographer Edward-Léon Scott de Martinville in 1860, the Phono Autograph was designed to visualize sound by inscribing waveforms onto paper. It created the first audio recordings, though it lacked a playback mechanism until 2008 when laser scanning enabled their reproduction. "He came up with this device called the Phono Autograph which scribbled a waveform on a piece of paper when you spoke into it."

# instrument - Record (Flat Rotating Disc)

## curiosities
**Invention and First Creation (1889)** : Emil Berliner, the inventor behind the concept of a flat rotating disc, created the very first record of this type in 1889. He recorded himself reciting a German ballad, and although the physical record was later destroyed, its audio was recreated in 1890 by scanning a picture found in a magazine. "Emil Berliner, the inventor that came up with the idea of a flat rotating disc in the first place, was the guy that made the first record as well."
**Early Capacity Limitations** : In the nascent days of recorded music, flat discs could hold only about four minutes of audio per side. This limitation meant that longer performances, such as symphonies, had to be fragmented across multiple discs, which ultimately led to the development of the "record album" concept. "Back in the early days of recorded music, discs could only hold about 4 minutes per side and that was pushing it."

# instrument - 78 RPM Record

## curiosities
**Dominant Recording Format (until 1948)** : The 78 RPM record served as the primary recording format for music until 1948. It was then largely superseded by the introduction of the 12-inch, 33 1/3 RPM LP album, which offered significantly longer playback times. "The 78 RPM record was the main recording format until 1948"

# instrument - LP (12-inch, 33 1/3 RPM album)

## curiosities
**Introduction and First Release (1948)** : The 12-inch, 33 1/3 RPM album, known as the LP, was launched by Columbia Records in 1948, replacing the 78 RPM record as the industry's primary format. The first LP featured the Philharmonic Symphony Orchestra of New York performing Mendelssohn's Violin Concerto in E Minor. "when the 12 inch, 33 and a third album was released by Columbia Records."

# instrument - 45 RPM Single (7-inch)

## curiosities
**RCA's Response to the LP (1949)** : RCA Records introduced the 7-inch, 45 RPM single in March 1949 as a direct competitor to Columbia's newly launched LP format. "Texarkana Baby" by Eddie Arnold was among the first 45s to be released. "RCA Records responded to Columbia's LP with the 7 inch 45 RPM single."

# instrument - Pre-recorded Cassettes

## curiosities
**Simultaneous Market Entry (1966)** : Pre-recorded cassettes first appeared in 1966, with a large number of titles released on the same day. This simultaneous market entry means there was no single "first" pre-recorded cassette title. "The first pre-recorded cassettes, I can tell you about, they first appeared in 1966."

# instrument - 8-track

## curiosities
**Simultaneous Market Entry (1966)** : Eight-track tapes entered the market in 1966, with 175 titles all being released at once. Due to this mass release, there was no single "first" 8-track title, similar to pre-recorded cassettes. "And they hit the market in 1966, 175 titles all came out at once."

# instrument - Compact Disc (CD)

## curiosities
**First Demonstration (1981)** : The Bee Gees' album "Living Eyes" was the first album to be transferred to CD for demonstration purposes in 1981. It was used extensively on television and radio to introduce the world to the new Compact Disc technology. "In 1981 their album Living Eyes was used on TV and radio as a way of introducing the world to Compact Discs."
**First Commercial Production Date (August 17, 1982)** : The first Compact Discs intended for consumers rolled off the production line on August 17, 1982, from a factory located just outside of Hanover, in what was then West Germany. This date marks the beginning of mass commercial CD availability. "Whatever they were, we do know the date they were made. August 17th, 1982."
**Disputed First Commercial Release** : The identity of the very first commercially available CD is a matter of dispute. Leading contenders include a classical recording of Strauss's "Alpine Symphony" conducted by Herbert von Karajan, ABBA's "The Visitors," and Billy Joel's "52nd Street," which went on sale in Japan in October 1982. "Well, some say it was a classical recording of Strauss's and Alpine Symphony, a performance conducted by Herbert von Karien. Others insist it was The Visitors by Ava. Maybe it's Billy Joel's 52nd Street."
**Initial Global Manufacturing Hubs** : Initially, all Compact Discs were produced exclusively in either Germany or Japan, as these were the only two manufacturing plants in the entire world. This concentration meant that all early CDs originated from these two countries. "At first, all CDs came out of either Germany or Japan because there were only two manufacturing plants in the entire world."
**First North American Pressing (1984)** : Bruce Springsteen's "Born in the USA" was apparently the first Compact Disc to be pressed in North America, a milestone achieved in 1984. This occurred as new CD factories began coming online globally, expanding production beyond Germany and Japan. "And apparently the first CD pressed in North America was Bruce Springsteen's Born in the USA."
**First to Sell Over a Million Copies (1985)** : Dire Straits' "Brothers in Arms" was the first Compact Disc to sell more than one million copies, reaching this significant sales milestone in 1985. This marked a pivotal moment for the commercial success and widespread adoption of the CD format. "The first compact disc to sell more than a million copies was Brothers and Arms by Dyer Straits that happened in 1985."

# instrument - MP3

## curiosities
**First Songs Ripped for Piracy (1996)** : In 1996, a group known as Compressed to Audio (CDA) began ripping CDs and making them available for free online for illegal file sharing purposes. Metallica's "Until It Sleeps" is believed to be the first song ripped and uploaded, closely followed by Rage Against the Machine's "Bulls on Parade" and Soundgarden's "Black Hole Sun." "It was 1996 and a group of geeks calling themselves Compressed to Audio or CDA for short. Started ripping CDs and making them available for free online."

# curiosity

**The First Audio Recording (1860)** : On April 9, 1860, French inventor Edward-Léon Scott de Martinville created the Phono Autograph, a device that scribbled sound waveforms on paper. This produced the first ever audio recording, although it was not designed for playback and could only be heard after laser scanning technology was used in 2008. "The first ever audio recorded dates back to April 9th of 1860."
**The First Playable Audio Recording (1877)** : Thomas Edison made the first audio recording that could also be played back in 1877, using his new "talking machine" invention to recite "Mary Had a Little Lamb." Although the original recording was later destroyed during his experiments, it established the principle of sound reproduction. "The first audio ever recorded and played back was by Thomas Edison who recited Mary Haddle Little Lamb into his new talking machine invention in 1877."
**The First Commercially Available Recording (1888)** : In November 1888, the first commercially available recording was an Edison cylinder inserted into a talking doll that recited "Twinkle Twinkle Little Star." The doll retailed for $10, an "insane price" at the time. "It was a tiny cylinder, an Edison cylinder inserted into a talking doll that went on sale for the insane price of $10 in November of 1888 and she recites Twinkle Twinkle Little Star."
**The Oldest Playable Musical Recording (1888)** : An Edison cylinder recording from 1888 of 500 musicians and 4,000 singers performing Handel's "Israel and Egypt" at London's Crystal Palace is considered the oldest playable musical recording. This monumental recording captures a massive musical event of its era. "The oldest playable musical recording is thought to be an Edison cylinder of 500 musicians and 4,000 singers performing Handel's Israel and Egypt which was made at London's Crystal Palace."
**The First Flat Rotating Disc Record (1889)** : Emil Berliner, the inventor of the flat rotating disc, created the first record of this type in 1889, on which he recorded a German ballad. Though the original disc was later destroyed, a sound historian recreated its audio in 1890 by scanning a picture of the record found in a magazine. "Emil Berliner, the inventor that came up with the idea of a flat rotating disc in the first place, was the guy that made the first record as well."
**The First Commercial Recordings (1890s)** : Commercial recordings began to be released in the 1890s. However, due to the niche nature of the market at the time, it is not entirely clear who was the absolute first to issue a commercial record, though a few early hits are recognized. "The first commercial recordings were released in the 1890s. We're not entirely sure who was the first to issue a commercial record because this was such a niche thing to do at the time"
**The Origin of the Term "Record Album"** : In the early days of recorded music, long performances had to be split across multiple discs due to limited capacity. These discs were then gathered and packaged in books with sleeves, which resembled photo albums, thus giving rise to the term "record album." "This reminded people of a photo album, a book that contained a collection of pictures. Therefore it wasn't much of a stretch to call this collection of records, a record album."
**Sales Awards Based on Dollar Sales (1958-1976)** : In 1958, the RIAA initially defined a gold record as any single or album that achieved more than a million dollars in sales, specifically in the United States. These awards were based on dollar sales, not copies, and remained in effect until 1976. "In 1958, the Recording Industry Association of America decided to formalize everything. They announced that every single or album that achieved more than a million dollars in sales, not copies, sales, would be a gold record."
**Reformulated Sales Awards (1976)** : In 1976, the RIAA reformalized its sales awards criteria. A gold album in America was redefined as sales of 500,000 copies, and a new Platinum Award was introduced for sales of one million copies in the US. "Awards were based on dollar sales until 1976 when things were reformalized. A gold album in America meant that it had sales of 500,000 copies. And then there was a new thing called a Platinum Award, which meant it had sold a million copies in the US."
**Multi-Platinum and Diamond Awards Introduction (1984, 1999)** : Due to ever-increasing record sales, Multi-Platinum awards were introduced in 1984. Later, in 1999, the Diamond Award was established for sales of 10 million copies. In both cases, there were multiple simultaneous recipients, meaning no single "first" awardee. "Multi-platinum awards were introduced in 1984. And then in 1999, they came up with a diamond award, which meant sales of 10 million copies."
**Unintentional F-Bombs on Record (1964, 1968)** : Before any deliberate F-bombs, there were alleged unintentional instances on record. Dave Davies of The Kinks supposedly uttered one during the 1964 recording of "You Really Got Me" due to anger, and during The Beatles' "Hey Jude" (1968), John Lennon or Paul McCartney might have exclaimed "F-ing hell" after hitting a bum note. "When it comes to unintentional F-sharps, we can look at the King's song You Really Got Me. Someone, maybe John, maybe Paul, hits a bum note at about two minutes and 57 seconds into the song."
**Pioneering Record Cover Artwork (1938)** : Prior to 1938, record artwork was generally dull, featuring only text or generic designs. Alex Steinweiss, a 23-year-old designer, convinced Columbia Records to create original graphic designs for record sleeves, starting with Rodgers and Hart's "Smash Song Hits." This innovation significantly boosted sales and led other labels to adopt original artwork as a standard. "It wasn't until 1938 that original graphics were designed for the sleeve of a record."
**Early Instances of Gradual Musical Endings (1772, 1918)** : The concept of a gradual musical ending predates electronic fade-outs. Joseph Haydn's "Farewell Symphony" (1772) ended with musicians leaving the stage one by one. Gustav Holst's "The Planets" (1918) included instructions for a women's chorus to sing repeatedly from an unseen room as a door slowly closed, creating an auditory fade-out. "In 1772, Joseph Hayden wrote Symphony 45, which was Nick Dam the Farewell Symphony. The instructions for the performance are actually quite complicated. The last movement is called Neptune."
**Mechanical Fade-Outs in Early Recordings (1894)** : Early recordings achieved fade-outs mechanically, such as an 1894 Berliner Disc of a Fife and Drums Corps playing "Yankee Doodle Dandy" which faded as the band moved away from the recording equipment. At this time, there were no electronic volume controls; everything was mechanical. "There's an 1894 Berliner Disc of a Fife and Drums Corps playing Yankee Doodle Dandy that fades into nothing, and the only way that that could have been accomplished is to have the band move away from the recording equipment until they couldn't be heard."
**Origin of the Phrase "Stick a Sock in It"** : In the early days, if one wanted a phonograph to play music more quietly, a ball of fabric, referred to as a "sock," would be placed in the acoustic horn that served as a speaker. This practice is the origin of the common idiom "stick a sock in it." "That ball of fabric was called a sock, and this is the origin of the phrase, stick a sock in it."
**Disputed First Artificially Faded Song (1930s)** : The identity of the first song to be artificially faded out on record is difficult to ascertain, with "Beyond the Blue Horizon" by George Olsen (1930) and "Old Man Harlem" by the Dorsey Brothers (1933) both being put forth as possibilities. Electronic fading became simple with magnetic tape in the 1950s. "For songs that were the first to be artificially faded out, that's hard to say."
**Dominance and Rejection of the Fade-Out (Post-1950s)** : From the 1950s onward, with modern recording techniques and magnetic tape, the fade-out became a common way to end songs, with more than half of all rock songs before the alternative era using it. However, punk and New Wave movements largely shunned the fade-out, preferring definitive song endings, a legacy seen in bands like Pearl Jam and Nirvana. "Since the 1950s, performers, producers, and engineers have used the fader out as a way of bringing a song to a close."
**The First Band Picture on the Internet (1992)** : Les Horribles Cernettes, a singing group formed by CERN graphic designer Michelle DiGenerro and three co-workers in 1990, became the subject of the first picture ever posted on the internet. In 1992, Tim Berners-Lee scanned their image and published it on info.cern.ch, establishing them as the first band on the internet. "And that's how the Cernets not only became the first band on the internet, but their image was also the first picture ever posted on the internet."
**The Internet Underground Music Archive (1992)** : The Internet Underground Music Archive, launched in 1992 by computer enthusiasts at the University of California at Santa Cruz, is recognized as potentially the very first online repository of music. It featured early online bands like Death Specula. "This was launched by some computer nerds at the University of California at Santa Cruz in 1992. That may have been the very first online repository of music."
**Introduction of the Alternative Album Grammy (1991)** : The Grammy Awards introduced the "Alternative Album" category in 1991, years after the genre had been established. This marked the first official recognition of alternative music by the Recording Academy. "It wasn't until 1991 that the genre had its own category."
**MTV Debut and First Videos (1981)** : MTV launched in 1981, famously playing "Video Killed the Radio Star" by The Buggles as its inaugural music video. The second video ever shown on the channel was "You Better Run" by Pat Benatar. "Pretty much everybody knows what the first video on MTV was when they debuted in 1981. It was video killed, the radio star for the Buggles."
