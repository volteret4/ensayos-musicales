# instrument - Compact Disc (CD)

## curiosities
**Profit Margins and Consumer Cost (1990s)** : The compact disc became a major driver of record industry revenue in the 1990s, offering significantly higher profit margins for record labels compared to vinyl and cassettes. Despite initial promises that prices would drop with increased manufacturing, CDs retailed above $15 throughout the decade, with some, like Led Zeppelin's fourth album, selling for $24.99 ($41 in 2020s dollars), long after production costs were recouped. "As vinyl and pre-recorded cassettes fell out of favor, people began collecting CDs, which were much more expensive, and offered higher profit margins for record labels."

**Shift in Music Consumption and Industry Strategy** : As CDs gained popularity, people not only bought new releases on disc but also replaced their existing vinyl and cassette collections. This coincided with the industry phasing out singles, forcing consumers to purchase full $20 albums even if they only wanted one song, thereby boosting album sales and label profits. "And not only were people buying CDs, but they were buying discs to replace their old vinyl and cassettes."

**Integration with Personal Computers** : The requirement for CD-ROM drives in new personal computers, driven by software installations like Windows, inadvertently led to CDs becoming a format for digital music transfer. Users discovered they could play music CDs on their computers and, with free software, transfer digital files to their hard drives, paving the way for digital music consumption outside physical media. "Somewhere along the line, someone discovered that a computer's CD-ROM drive was just like the compact disk player they had on the shelf."

# instrument - Magnetic Recording Tape

## curiosities
**Traditional Recording Medium (Pre-1990s)** : Prior to the widespread adoption of digital recording, recording studios relied heavily on large reel-to-reel tape machines using spools of magnetic recording tape, often up to two inches wide. This was considered solid technology for capturing sound but had inherent limitations compared to emerging digital methods. "Recording studios had been using big, real-to-real tape machine with huge spools of magnetic recording tape that could be up to two inches wide."

**Digital Signal on Magnetic Tape (Early Digital)** : The earliest digital recordings, made in Japan in 1971, still utilized magnetic tape for storage. However, the crucial innovation was that the signal stored on this tape was digital, rather than analog, representing an initial step in the transition from purely analog recording. "They still used magnetic tape, but the signal stored on the tape was digital, not analog."

# instrument - Digital Audio Workstation (DAW)

## curiosities
**Revolutionary Recording Technology (1990s)** : The Digital Audio Workstation (DAW) emerged in recording studios around 1990 as a fully digital recording device. Unlike older systems using magnetic tape, DAWs stored audio information on hard drives, allowing waveforms to be visualized on a screen for unprecedented editing, modification, and manipulation. This capability was previously impossible with magnetic tape. "By 1990, a new beast was appearing in studios, the digital audio workstation."

**Empowering Musicians and Producers** : The combination of DAWs and new digital recording software, such as Cubase Audio, Stooder Diaxis, Deck, Logic, and Pro Tools, provided musicians, composers, producers, and engineers with unparalleled tools to explore and manipulate audio. This technology enabled the creation of sounds and compositions previously unimaginable, fundamentally changing the creative process. "All these programs and the computers on which they ran allowed musicians, composers, producers, and engineers to explore the universe of audio in unimaginable ways."

"All new computers had to have a CD-ROM drive."

# curiosity

**1990s as a Transformational Decade** : The 1990s marked a profound transformational decade, signifying "the end of the way music used to be and the beginning of what it would become in the next millennium." This era witnessed the rise of grunge, Britpop, raves, electronic music, Generation X, hip-hop, sampling, and the advent of cell phones, personal computers, the internet, MP3s, and music piracy. Many observers believe it was the "last great decade for music." "The 90s were a transformational decade in so many ways the end of the way music used to be and the beginning of what it would become in the next millennium."

**Generation X Demographics and Disillusionment** : As the 1990s began, Generation X faced a challenging economic and social landscape marked by a brutal recession, widespread overeducation, and underemployment. This demographic group, aged 15-25, felt disillusioned, jaded, angry, and anxious about their future, concerned they wouldn't achieve the living standards of their parents. Their discontent was exacerbated by events like the Gulf War. "Much of Gen X was overeducated and under-employed."

**Generation X's Musical Identity** : Generation X, musically coming of age (15-25), actively rejected the music of their parents and older siblings, including late 1980s classic rock (Van Halen, Aerosmith, Leonard Skinner, Pink Floyd, Elton John), 80s pop (New Kids on the Block, Tiffany, Debbie Gibson), and hair metal (excess, makeup, power ballads). They sought authentic, humble music made by "their own people" that resonated with their hopes, dreams, fears, and anger. "Gen X was not interested in what their parents or older brothers and sisters were into."

**Political Influence on Music (Republican Presidents)** : A historical observation suggests that during periods when a Republican president is in the White House, music tends to become heavier, angrier, and more "rocky." This trend correlated with Generation X's preference for making and listening to such music, aligning their disillusionment with a perceived political climate. "If you match up history with politics, you'll see that whenever there's a Republican in the White House, music tends to turn heavier, angrier and more rocky."

**Record Industry Revenues (1990s)** : The record industry experienced immense financial prosperity in the 1990s, with revenues flowing freely. In the US alone, revenues grew from $8 billion in 1990 to $14 billion by the end of the decade, with global revenues on track to reach $37 billion. This financial boom was largely driven by the high profit margins of compact discs and the strategy of selling full albums. "In the US alone, revenues were $8 billion in 1990, with a trajectory that pointed almost straight up."

**Long-Term Artist Development in the 1990s** : The substantial financial resources available to record labels in the 1990s allowed them to invest in long-term artist development. Labels could afford to let bands cultivate their sound over multiple albums (two, three, four, five or more) without immediate pressure for a breakout hit, fostering diverse sounds and backgrounds. "The positive side of all this was that there was money for long-term development of a band."

**Decline of Music Singles (1990s)** : Throughout the 1990s, the record industry intentionally phased out the single format, including seven-inch vinyl, CD singles, and casingles. This strategy aimed to compel consumers to purchase entire albums, often costing around $20, to obtain desired songs, thereby increasing album sales and profit margins for labels. "But by the end of the 1990s, the output of singles cratered."

**Growth of Mega-Stores and Retail Competition (1990s)** : The 1990s saw a significant expansion of record stores, including the rise of mega-stores like HMV, Tower Records, and Virgin Records, alongside chains such as Sam the Record Man, Sam Goodie, Camelot, and Sunrise, Music World. These retailers fiercely competed to sell CDs for record labels, signifying a boom in physical music retail. "More CDs were sold and more record stores opened. And not just stores, mega stores."

**HMV Retail Innovations (1990s)** : HMV introduced several customer-centric innovations in the 1990s that were revolutionary for music retail. These included providing shopping baskets and carts due to the large number of CDs people bought, a loyalty card program (buy 10 CDs, get the 11th free), and remarkably, a return policy for unwanted CDs, reflecting the lucrative nature of the market. "One of the biggest innovations from HMV in the 90s was to provide customers with shopping baskets and shopping carts."

**The Power of Music Video (1990s)** : The 1990s saw the music video industry expand significantly, building on MTV's launch in 1981. Initially skeptical, record labels soon recognized that music videos effectively "moved merchandise" and began spending vast sums on them, with budgets routinely exceeding $250,000 per video, charged back to the artists. Being featured on channels like MTV or MuchMusic could make a band "blow up." "The video channels controlled what we saw, and people sat in front of their TVs for hours, and hours and hours, waiting for their favorite video to come up."

**Pre-SoundScan Chart Manipulation (Before March 1991)** : Before March 1, 1991, the music industry lacked an accurate method for tracking record sales, relying on Billboard magazine's calls to a sample of stores since 1945. This "honor system" was prone to errors, manipulation, bad memory, bias, bribery, lies, and fraud, leading to "chart fixing" dating back to the 1930s. Consequently, chart positions often did not reflect actual sales or popularity, misleading radio stations, video channels, and fans. "With zero accountability, the system was wide open to errors, manipulation, bad memory, bias, bribery, lies, and fraud."

**SoundScan Introduction and Impact (1991)** : The SoundScan system, introduced in 1991, revolutionized music sales tracking by counting each sale individually when a barcode was scanned at the cash register, eliminating estimates and making manipulation difficult. The first Billboard chart using SoundScan, published May 25, 1991, revealed drastically different sales realities, showing that genres like country, metal, rap, hip-hop, and alternative music were selling far more than previously believed. "This all changed in 1991 when the sound scan system was introduced."

**Record Label Embrace of SoundScan** : Although initially resistant, record labels quickly adopted SoundScan technology because it provided invaluable hard data on who was buying what music and where. This accurate information enabled labels to identify genuinely popular genres, such as alternative rock, and allocate significant marketing and talent scouting resources accordingly, fueling an "Alt Rock Gold Rush." "Now that, thanks to Sound Scan, labels saw that this weirdo music was actually selling tons of units without much marketing power."

**Development of Digital Recording Technology** : Digital recording techniques originated with digital machines developed by NHK in Japan in 1967, with the first digital recordings made there in 1971. These early digital recordings still used magnetic tape but stored a digital signal. Experiments continued throughout the 1970s, paving the way for the full digital audio workstation in the 1990s. "Digital machines came first. They were developed by NHK, the National Broadcaster of Japan in 1967."

**Sampling as a Foundational Change** : Sampling, defined as surgically excising and reusing audio from one source as a building block for new compositions, was a critical foundational change enabling the rise of alt-rock in the 1990s. While rudimentary experimentation with deconstructive audio concepts dates back to the 1950s (Jamaican DJs in the 60s, hip-hop in the 70s), electronic, computer-controlled sampling became prevalent in the 1980s, allowing old sounds to be made fresh again. "Sampling is when you surgically excise a piece of audio from one source and use it as a building block for a brand new composition."

**Microsoft Windows Revolutionizes Personal Computing** : The personal computer's accessibility dramatically increased in the early 1990s, especially after Microsoft launched Windows 3.1 on March 1, 1992. This "killer operating system" made computers much easier for "normal people" to use, a trend that continued with Windows 95 (launched August 15, 1995), which was Microsoft's most user-friendly creation yet. "But with 3.1, Microsoft hit something of a sweet spot. Who is their killer operating system that made it far easier for normal people to use a computer?"

**The Internet's Commercialization and Impact (1990s)** : While the internet originated as a Defense Department project in the 1960s, its widespread civilian adoption began after Tim Berners-Lee introduced the World Wide Web in 1990. Mark Andreessen's 1993 browser, Mosaic (later Netscape), made it user-friendly. A pivotal moment occurred in 1995 when all restrictions on commercial use were dropped, opening the internet to the public and leading to a rapid transformation of music distribution through email, websites, file sharing, MP3s, torrents, iPods, and iTunes. "In 1995, all restrictions on commercial use of the Internet were dropped. All of the public was allowed in."

**Bill Gates and CD-ROMs as a Catalyst for Digital Music** : The theory posits that Bill Gates's demand for computers to come standard with CD-ROM drives (initially for Windows software installation) inadvertently sparked the digital music revolution. This led to the discovery that CD-ROM drives could play music CDs and transfer digital files to hard drives, facilitating the shift from physical plastic to digital file sharing at light speed. "And it all began when Bill Gates demanded that computers come standard with CD-ROM drives so they could install Windows."
