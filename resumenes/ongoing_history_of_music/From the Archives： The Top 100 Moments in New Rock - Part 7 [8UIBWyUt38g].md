**Bill Berry's Near-Fatal Tick Bite (May 1989)** : While R.E.M. was on tour in Germany in May 1989, supporting the "Green Album," drummer Bill Berry fell severely ill shortly after arriving in Munich. Initially suspected to be bronchitis or measles due to a rash, his condition worsened with hallucinations and fluctuating consciousness. Bill's doctor in Athens, Georgia, later diagnosed Rocky Mountain spotted fever, which Bill had contracted from a tick bite while gardening at home a week prior, and whose week-long incubation period had led to symptoms emerging during the tour. **Bill Berry's Brain Aneurysms (March 1995)** : On March 1, 1995, during a Monster Tour stop in Lausanne, Switzerland, ninety minutes into the set, Bill Berry was suddenly afflicted by a headache so intense he described it as if "somebody had dropped the bowling ball on a skull," and he had to be carried off stage. Initially thought to be a severe migraine caused by stress and the high altitude of the Swiss Alps, a subsequent CAT scan revealed two potentially fatal brain aneurysms. Bill underwent emergency brain surgery and endured an eight-week recovery period, during which there were serious concerns about potential brain damage affecting his ability to speak or remember. "Bill was rushed in for emergency brain surgery and he spent eight long weeks recovering."

**Troubled Residency at Cafe Bizarre (December 1965)** : In December 1965, this "new and very weird group" secured their first regular gig at Cafe Bizarre in Greenwich Village, New York City. Warhol was so profoundly impressed by their "avant-garde approach to rock" that he immediately signed them to be his official house band at his performance art headquarters, The Factory, located on East 47th Street. Warhol assumed the roles of their patron, producer, and promoter, a pivotal moment that launched the band's "long, strange road to becoming legends as the first alternative rock band in history." "Andy Warhol adopts the Velvet Underground, thereby giving the band the break they needed, to eventually be hailed as the founders of alternative rock"

**Drug Overdoses and Jonathan Melvoin's Death (1996)** : Within six weeks of the Smashing Pumpkins' 1996 World Tour beginning, drummer Jimmy Chamberlain and touring keyboardist Jonathan Melvoin overdosed on heroin in Thailand in February, and again in Spain in May, narrowly escaping death. Jonathan Melvoin was subsequently fired but remained with the band until the tour concluded, while Jimmy was placed on "double secret probation." On July 11, 1996, just before two sold-out shows at Madison Square Garden in New York, Jimmy and Jonathan went to the East Village to purchase "red rum," a highly potent form of heroin. At 4 AM the next morning, Jonathan, who was ironically trained as an emergency medical technician, died from an overdose. Jimmy awoke from his stupor and called the police, leading to official statements being given within hours. "Jonathan Melvoin O.D. on heroin in Thailand."
**Jimmy Chamberlain's Firing and Return (1996-1999)** : Five days after Jonathan Melvoin's death, Billy, James, and Darcy of The Smashing Pumpkins issued a statement announcing their decision to "sever our relationship with our friend and drummer Jimmy Chamberlain." They explained that for nine years they had "battled with Jimmy's struggles with the insidious disease of drug and alcohol addiction," which had "nearly destroyed everything we are and stand for," and confirmed they would "carry on without him." Jimmy was exiled from the band for three years, a period during which the Pumpkins' fortunes began to decline. In an effort to revitalize the band, Jimmy was eventually asked to rejoin, making his return on April 10, 1999, at St Andrews Hall in Detroit, where the band appeared in their familiar lineup for the first time since July 1996. "Today, we are very sorry to tell you that we've decided to sever our relationship with our friend and drummer Jimmy Chamberlain."

## songs
**Blue Monday (Single)** : This iconic 8-minute 12-inch single originated from New Order's reluctance to perform encores. They devised a plan to turn on Stephen Morris's drum machine and walk off stage to drink backstage. The concept evolved with a sequencer triggering Gillian Welch's keyboards and Barney Sunder suggesting sequencing Peter Hook's bass lines, thus forming the entirely machine-played foundation of a new song. Legend has it the band, reportedly under the influence of LSD, recorded the tracks, after which the studio engineer, seeking peace, sent them to a cafe on Croc's Street to finish mixing. Upon its release, it gained radio play but had its most profound impact in dance clubs, dramatically shifting the landscape of dance music, making dancing "cool again," and forging crucial links between new music, rock, and dance. "Blue Monday" sold over 3 million copies, establishing it as the best-selling 12-inch record in the history of Planet Earth. Ironically, the elaborate artwork and cover for the single were so expensive to produce that New Order lost one pence on each copy sold, resulting in a total loss of 30,000 pounds for issuing "the world's favorite dance record." "Blue Monday sold over 3 million copies and it's still the best selling 12-inch record in the history of Planet Earth."

**Noel Gallagher's "Coup d'Etat" (Spring 1993)** : In the spring of 1993, after being fired from his job as a guitar roadie with the Inspiral Carpets and left with only £2,000 in severance pay, Noel Gallagher was determined to forge a proper music career. Having hitched a ride with another Manchester group, 18 Wheeler, they threatened the venue's owner and bouncers with burning down the club if they weren't allowed to perform. Coincidentally, Alan McGee of Creation Records was present, initially to see other bands on the bill, but was so impressed by Oasis that he signed them "more or less on the spot," later lending them £1,000 for equipment. "Oasis literally bullied their way on stage for a set at King Tuts Wawa Hut in Glasgow, Scotland."

**Brit Pop Rivalry with Blur (1995)** : Oasis was deeply involved in an intense rivalry with Blur, described as a "war" between "Brit Pop's two biggest stars," which significantly hyped the release of Oasis's second album. The conflict began with a near-fight at a San Francisco radio station and escalated when Liam Gallagher and Damon Albarn almost brawled backstage at an award ceremony. Damon Albarn intentionally advanced Blur's single release date to coincide with Oasis's, culminating in the "Record War" of August 14, 1995, involving "Roll With It" versus "Country House," a battle that captivated all of Britain's music and daily papers, with even bookmakers taking bets. "This was much more than a rivalry. This was a war."

**Debut Performance at CBGB (August 14, 1974)** : On Wednesday, August 14, 1974, The Ramones played their first-ever show at the iconic New York club CBGB. The initial audience was sparse, consisting of fewer than a dozen people, including the club owner's dog. However, by the end of 1974, the band had performed at CBGB 74 times, with each gig progressively increasing their buzz. The club soon became packed with a diverse crowd, attracting Andy Warhol's circle, intellectuals, wealthy uptown youths seeking to "slum it," and music fans bored with existing sounds and looking for novelty. The Ramones' distinctive style — four "brothers" dressed in torn jeans and leather jackets, playing 1960s-style "bubble gum pop songs" at "a million miles an hour," without solos, stage interaction, or audience acknowledgment, pausing only when Johnny counted off the next song — marked the genesis of the New York punk rock revolution. "The Ramones played their first ever show at a New York club called CBGB."
**Music Philosophy and Band Name Meaning** : A journalist astutely characterized the Ramones' music as serving for "the head, the heart, and for the feet." The band's name, "Ramones," itself carries connotations of "unity or family, brother, gang, brotherhood, all that crap." "one journalist put it very well, he said he'd use music as for that head, the heart, and for the feet."
