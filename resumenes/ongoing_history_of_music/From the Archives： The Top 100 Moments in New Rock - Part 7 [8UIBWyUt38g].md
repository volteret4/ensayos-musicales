## curiosities
**Band Name Meaning** : The name "Ramones" signifies unity, family, brother, gang, and brotherhood.
**Debut Performance (1974)** : The band played their first ever show on Wednesday, August 14th, 1974, at the CBGB club in New York. There were fewer than a dozen people present, including the owner's dog.
**CBGB Residency (1974)** : By the end of 1974, The Ramones had played CBGB 74 times. With each performance, the buzz grew, and the club became crammed with diverse audiences, including Andy Warhol's entourage, intellectual types, wealthy uptown kids looking to "slum it," and music fans seeking something new and exciting.
**Distinctive Image and Sound** : In the fall of 1974, The Ramones were distinct from other bands. They dressed in torn jeans and leather jackets, playing what amounted to 1960s bubblegum pop songs but at an extremely fast pace ("a million miles an hour"). Their performances featured no solos, no specific stage patterns, no acknowledgment of the audience, and no "letting out." The only breaks occurred when Johnny counted off the next song. This marked the beginning of the New York punk rock revolution.

## curiosities
**Attacks by Monarchists (1977)** : In response to "God Save the Queen," members of the Sex Pistols and their associates were victims of violent attacks by monarchist supporters. On the evening of June 18th, 1977, Johnny Rotten, producer Chris Thomas, and engineer Bill Price were assaulted by a gang armed with razors in the parking lot of London's Pegasus Hotel. Johnny Rotten stated that his thick leather pants saved him from bleeding to death, though some tendons in his arm were severed, making it permanently difficult for him to play guitar. The following night, June 19th, drummer Paul Cook was attacked outside a pub by six individuals wielding pipes, resulting in him needing 15 stitches in his head.
**Critic's Harsh View of Band and Genre** : An unnamed journalist expressed an extremely negative opinion of punk rock, describing it as "nauseating, disgusting, degrading, ghastly, sleazy, pruriant, biogristic and generally nauseating." This critic believed that most punk groups "would be vastly improved by sudden death" and singled out the Sex Pistols as "unbelievably nauseating" and "the antithesis of humankind," wishing for their "total and other non-existence."

## curiosities
**Rocky Mountain Spotted Fever Scare (1989)** : In May 1989, while R.E.M. was on tour in Germany for the first time in four years, drummer Bill Berry became severely ill in Munich after a successful show in Dusseldorf on May 9th. He was rushed to a hospital where doctors initially misdiagnosed his condition as bronchitis, then measles (after a rash developed), and then observed hallucinations and intermittent consciousness. Doctors administered a "huge buffet of antibiotics," and one blindly prescribed tetracycline, which proved effective. Bill's doctor in Athens, Georgia, later correctly diagnosed the condition as Rocky Mountain spotted fever, a rare disease in Germany. It was discovered that Bill had been bitten by a tick while gardening at home a week prior, and the fever's week-long incubation period meant symptoms only appeared once in Germany. Had tetracycline not been prescribed, Bill Berry might have died.
**On-Stage Brain Aneurysm (1995)** : On March 1st, 1995, during the "Monster Tour" in Lausanne, Switzerland, 90 minutes into the set and just before the falsetto section of the song "Tongue," Bill Berry was struck by an excruciating headache, which he described as feeling like a bowling ball had dropped on his skull. The pain was so severe that he had to be carried off stage. Doctors initially suspected a severe migraine induced by stress and the high altitude of the Swiss Alps, but a CAT scan revealed two potentially fatal brain aneurysms. Bill underwent emergency brain surgery and spent eight weeks recovering. There was significant concern about potential brain damage, including uncertainty about his ability to speak or retain memories.
**Retirement Due to Health (1997)** : The two severe health incidents, the Rocky Mountain spotted fever scare in 1989 and the on-stage brain aneurysm in 1995, played a significant role in Bill Berry's decision to retire from music on October 30th, 1997.

# artist - The Offspring

## labels
Epitaph

## albums
**Smash (1994) – Biggest Selling Independent Album** : Released on April 15th, 1994, on the independent label Epitaph, this album initially received little fanfare. It first gained traction among skateboarders and snowboarders. Through a stroke of luck, its first single was picked up by an LA radio station, an uncommon occurrence for Epitaph bands, and then spread to other stations. The label invested $5,000 for a music video, which MTV subsequently began playing, causing the album's sales to skyrocket. "Smash," which was originally expected to sell 60,000 copies over its entire lifespan, began selling that many copies every couple of days. Ultimately, it sold over 12 million copies, making it the biggest selling independent record of all time.

# artist - The Velvet Underground

## members
Andy Warhol (patron, producer, promoter)

## venues
Cafe Bizarre (Greenwich Village, New York City)
The Factory (East 47th Street, New York City)

## curiosities
**Discovery by Andy Warhol (1965)** : In December 1965, The Velvet Underground secured their first regular gig at Cafe Bizarre in Greenwich Village, New York City, where they were contracted to play six sets a night, six nights a week, for five dollars per set. However, their unique and "weird" music frequently drove away patrons, leading to them being fired at least once. Just before a second dismissal, Andy Warhol and his entourage (including groupies and "weirdos") visited the club. Warhol was profoundly impressed by the band's "avant-garde approach to rock" and immediately signed them to be his official house band at his performance art headquarters, The Factory, on East 47th Street. Warhol assumed the roles of their patron, producer, and promoter, providing the band with a crucial break that allowed them to embark on their "long, strange road to becoming legends" and eventually be hailed as the first alternative rock band in history. Without Warhol's intervention, it is speculated their career trajectory would have been vastly different.

## curiosities
**Drug Overdoses and Jonathan Melvoin's Death (1996)** : During The Smashing Pumpkins' 1996 World Tour, significant drug problems emerged. In February, drummer Jimmy Chamberlain and touring keyboardist Jonathan Melvoin overdosed on heroin in Thailand. Chamberlain promised it was an isolated incident. However, in May, both overdosed again in Spain and "almost died." Melvoin was subsequently fired but remained with the band to complete the tour, while Chamberlain was put on "double secret probation." On July 11, 1996, prior to two sold-out shows at Madison Square Garden in New York, Jimmy and Jonathan went to a part of the East Village to purchase heroin, buying a potent type known as "red rum." At 4 AM the next morning, Jonathan Melvoin, who was a trained emergency medical technician, died from an overdose. Chamberlain, upon waking, called the police, and everyone involved gave statements at the precinct within hours.
**Jimmy Chamberlain's Firing (1996)** : Five days after Jonathan Melvoin's death, around July 17, 1996, Billy Corgan, James Iha, and D'arcy Wretzky issued a statement announcing their decision to "sever our relationship" with Jimmy Chamberlain. The statement expressed devastation and detailed a nine-year struggle with Chamberlain's "insidious disease of drug and alcohol addiction," which had "nearly destroyed everything we are and stand for." The band resolved to continue without him, wishing him well. Chamberlain was exiled from the band for three years, during which time The Smashing Pumpkins' fortunes began to decline.
**Jimmy Chamberlain's Return (1999)** : In an effort to revitalize the band's fortunes, Jimmy Chamberlain was approached to rejoin The Smashing Pumpkins. On April 10th, 1999, the band appeared in their "familiar form" for the first time since July 1996 at St Andrews Hall in Detroit, marking Chamberlain's return.

## songs
**Blue Monday (1983) – Accidental Origin and Monumental Impact** : The song originated from New Order's reluctance to perform encores. They devised a plan to simply activate Stephen Morris's drum machine and let it play while they exited the stage to start drinking. To add interest for the audience, they incorporated a sequencer to trigger parts on Gillian Gilbert's keyboards. Barney Sumner then suggested sequencing one of Peter Hook's bass lines, which ultimately laid the foundation for a new song played entirely by machines. Legend suggests the band, under the influence of LSD, recorded the tracks in the studio, leading the studio engineer to send them to a cafe to finish mixing the song in peace due to their inebriated state.
**Blue Monday (1983) – Commercial and Cultural Success** : "Blue Monday" was released solely as an 8-minute 12-inch single. While it received some radio play, its most significant impact was felt in dance clubs, where it stood out dramatically from contemporary tracks like Olivia Newton-John's "Physical" and Joan Jett's "I Love Rock 'n Roll." The song's influence was "staggering," establishing crucial connections between new music, rock, and dance music, and is credited by many for making dancing "cool again." It sold over 3 million copies and remains the best-selling 12-inch record in history. Despite its immense success, the elaborate artwork and cover production for the 12-inch single were so expensive that New Order lost one pence on each copy sold, resulting in a total loss of 30,000 pounds for the band and their record company from its release.

## songs
**Columbia (1993) – White Label Demo** : The first action taken by Alan McGee of Creation Records after signing Oasis was to release a simple 12-inch single with a plain white label. This demo of "Columbia" was strategically distributed to industry insiders to generate hype and contributed to Oasis becoming one of the biggest British bands of the 1990s.
**Roll With It (1995)** : This single was released on August 14th, 1995, in direct competition with Blur's "Country House" as part of a highly publicized "record war." The battle sparked national frenzy in Britain, with bookies taking bets on chart positions. "Roll With It" sold 220,000 copies in its first week but was ultimately outsold by Blur's single.

## curiosities
**Noel Gallagher's "Coup d'État" (1993)** : In spring 1993, Noel Gallagher, recently fired as a guitar roadie for Inspiral Carpets and with £2,000 in severance pay, was determined to forge a music career. Impressed but critical of his younger brother Liam's band, Noel offered to join on the condition that he would be "in charge of everything," a move described as a "coup d'etat."
**Aggressive Debut and Discovery (1993)** : On Sunday, May 31st, 1993, Oasis, not scheduled to perform, forcibly secured a slot at King Tut's Wah Wah Hut in Glasgow, Scotland. They had hitched a ride with another Manchester band, 18 Wheeler, and upon arrival, threatened the venue owner and bouncers with phrases like "Let us play or burn the club down or something to that effect." By coincidence, Alan McGee of Creation Records was in the audience that night to see other bands. He was so impressed by Oasis that he signed them "more or less on the spot."
**Liam Gallagher's Account of Meeting Alan McGee** : Liam Gallagher vividly recalled the encounter at King Tut's Wah Wah Hut, noting McGee's attire: "He was wearing a blue top and white jeans and black buffers and ginger out and sunglasses." McGee inquired about the band's name, their record deal status, and then offered them a deal. The next day, after confirming McGee's seriousness, the band traveled to London, where they "Managed to borrow £1,000 off him that day, blackening him that we needed some equipment went back and spent it all. And got a lawyer, and we got a deal, founder manager, Sander Deel, the rest is history."
**The Brit Pop "Record War" with Blur (1995)** : A significant rivalry, described as a "war" between two of Brit Pop's biggest stars, erupted between Oasis and Blur. Their first confrontation was almost a fight at a San Francisco radio station, followed by a near-brawl between Liam Gallagher and Damon Albarn backstage at an award ceremony. The rivalry escalated when Damon Albarn deliberately moved Blur's new single release date to coincide with Oasis's, setting up a direct chart battle on August 14th, 1995. Oasis released "Roll With It" against Blur's "Country House," sparking national frenzy in Britain. Music papers, magazines, daily newspapers, and even bookmakers took bets on which single would reach number one, with the BBC assigning reporters to the story. The two singles combined accounted for nearly 30% of all singles sold in the UK that week. Blur ultimately won the sales battle in the first week, selling 270,000 copies of "Country House" compared to Oasis's 220,000 for "Roll With It." Blur's victory was aided by their strategy of releasing two versions of "Country House," each with different bonus tracks on the CD, encouraging fans to purchase both copies.
