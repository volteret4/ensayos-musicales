**Teddyboys Subculture (Britain, 1950s)** : The Teddyboys were a British youth movement that emerged in the mid-1950s, catalyzed by the introduction of American rock and roll. They adopted a distinctive fashion style inspired by Edwardian England (from 40-50 years prior), characterized by tapered trousers, long jackets, waistcoats (sometimes with velvet-trimmed collars), narrow ties, loose-collared white shirts, "brothel creeper" shoes, and hair styled into a "duck's arse." Originating in East and North London, they were named by British newspapers ("Ted" being short for Edward). This movement is considered potentially the first instance of a British youth movement self-identifying as "teenagers," a concept that was not well-defined before World War II. Musically, they loved rock and roll artists like Elvis, Bill Haley, and Eddie Cochran, but also accepted jazz. They were known for being combative, with gangs roaming neighborhoods, and even members of The Beatles in Liverpool were reportedly terrified of them. "Ted's first started showing up in England in the middle 1950s in response to this new thing from America called rock and roll."
**Teddyboy Revival (c. 1974)** : A revival of the Teddyboy style, particularly in fashion, occurred around 1974. **Mods Subculture (Britain, from 1958)** : The Mods were a British subculture dating back to 1958, fundamentally defined by four pillars: fashion, motor scooters, drugs, and music. They exhibited an intense obsession with fashion, characterized by shortish (but not cropped) hair, tailor-made suits with narrow lapels (mohair preferred), white dress shirts with skinny ties, sharp blazers, slim-fit slacks or chinos with rolled cuffs, and shoes like loafers, desert boots, or bowling shoes. Outerwear often featured Union Jack or Royal Air Force logos. Notably, some male mods used makeup, mainly eye shadow and eyeliner. Scooters were favored over motorcycles for their Italian coolness and covered parts that prevented oil stains on clothes. Amphetamines ("speed") were widely used to sustain all-night dancing and fuel brawls with rival rockers. Musically, they progressed from jazz to ska, then to British bands embracing R&B (e.g., The Rolling Stones, The Yardbirds, The Kinks, The Who). Mod culture declined in the late 1960s with the rise of hippie culture, with some ex-mods joining skinhead or Swadehed movements or gravitating to Northern Soul. "Mods were very much about music and fashion and modern things. Four things define mods. Fashion, their motor scooters, their drugs, and their music."
