**Hunger Project and Germanel Roots** : The roots of the Cowboy Junkies can be traced back to a punky band called Hunger Project, formed in Toronto in the early 1980s, which also existed for a time in New York. After Hunger Project collapsed, the core members reassembled into an experimental group named Germanel, which garnered good reviews, especially in the UK, but ultimately did not succeed. "The roots of the cowboy junkies are in a punky sort of band called Hunger Project, which was formed in Toronto in the early 1980s and existed for a while in New York."
**Margot Timmins' Early Career** : Before joining Cowboy Junkies as a singer, Margot Timmins had a background in school plays and camp productions. She worked as part of the road crew for her brother Michael's previous band, Hunger Project, managing the door. While Michael was touring Germany with Hunger Project, she was in university studying to become a social worker. "When Hunger Project was a thing, she worked as part of the road crew, working the door. When Michael was off touring with Hunger Project in Germany, she was in university planning to be a social worker."
**Accidental Sound Development** : In 1985, Margot began singing with her brothers Michael and Peter in their garage. They accidentally developed their distinctive down-tempo, almost whispery sound after neighbors called the police on one of their rehearsals for making too much noise, forcing them to tone down their volume. "They soon developed an interesting down tempo, almost whispery sound. And then sort of came about by accident because the neighbors called the cops on one of the rehearsals for making too much noise so they just kind of toned it down."

## albums
**The Trinity Session (1987) – Single-Microphone Church Recording** : Recorded on November 27, 1987, by producer Peter Moore using a single microphone in the Church of the Holy Trinity in downtown Toronto, this album cost only $150, including the price of pizza for the night. "The album was called the Trinity Session, and it became an unexpected international hit, going platinum in both Canada and the United States."

**Frankenmuth Hometown and Musical Upbringing** : The Kiszka brothers grew up in Frankenmuth, central Michigan, a town of approximately 5,000 residents whose main attraction is the "world's largest Christmas store." Their parents exposed them to classic rock and blues music, including artists like Led Zeppelin, Jimi Hendrix, Joni Mitchell, and 30s/40s folk and blues legends like Muddy Waters and Robert Johnson. "They're really from the middle of nowhere, a central Michigan town called Frankenmuth. In about 5,000, the biggest attraction is the self-proclaimed world's largest Christmas store. This is where the Kiska family grew up."
**Vocal Style Development** : Formed in 2012, Josh Kiszka, the singer, initially lacked his own vocal amplifiers. This forced him to develop a powerful vocal style to be heard over the guitars and drums, which, according to Jake Kiszka, naturally evolved into his Robert Plant-like delivery, likely also influenced by their exposure to old Led Zeppelin records. "Josh, for example, didn't have his own vocal amps, so he had to develop a style where he could be heard over the guitars and drums. And according to Jake, which is what he told me, this is how John ended up with that Robert Plant-like delivery."
**Nurturing Musical Environment** : The Kiszka brothers were raised in a "musically nurturing environment" that extended beyond just music to include film and literature. They were surrounded by traditional music, including early American blues artists like Elmore James, Willie Dixon, Robert Johnson, Muddy Waters, and Howlin' Wolf, as well as folk artists like Arlo Guthrie, Bob Dylan, Joni Mitchell, and John Denver, primarily from their parents' vinyl collection. "we were kind of raised in a musically nurturing environment where it wasn't necessarily just music, but there was a lot of film and a lot of literature. And we had a lot of traditional music, you know, lying around."
**Later Exposure to Rock and Roll** : While traditional music was prevalent in their early lives, the brothers recall getting into what is commonly known as rock and roll, specifically the 60s British Invasion, around high school. "it wasn't until like, I think about high school that we all started getting into what is, you know, commonly known for his rock and roll, you know, a lot of the British invasion and that sort of stuff."
**Shared Experiences and Artistic Communication** : The fact that the three brothers, including twins, shared the same life experiences and picked up on musical influences at the same time contributed significantly to their sound. Their strong familial bond allows for easy communication, especially artistically, enabling them to instinctively understand each other's creative direction, even during live performances. "Yeah, same life experiences that we've shared, commonalities and things like that. And it's very easy to communicate, you know, especially, you know, just in general, but artistically, I think that we're on a, you know, we're on the same page a lot of the time where it's like, well, what creatively has to happen here when we come to a junk share where we're deciding, well, should, you know, what should the guitar be doing here? And I think we're all like, yeah, it should be doing this."

## curiosities
**Pioneers of Accidental Lo-Fi** : In 1969, long before lo-fi music became a deliberate genre, The Shaggs, a group of sisters from Fremont, New Hampshire, released what is considered by some to be the definitive lo-fi record of all time. "In 1969, long before people started making music that was deliberately low-fi, there was a group of sisters from Fremont, New Hampshire, who released what could be the definitive low-fi record of all time."
**Cult Status and Musician Endorsements** : Despite or because of their unconventional sound, The Shaggs are frequently name-checked by famous musicians who cite their recordings as favorite garage rock albums. They are paradoxically also described as "quite possibly the worst group to ever walk into a recording studio." "The Shags are often named checked by famous musicians, asked to name their favorite garage rock recordings of all time. They are also paradoxically quite possibly the worst group to ever walk into a recording studio."
**Father's Managerial Vision** : Their father, Austin Wiggins, served as their manager and producer. Despite being financially poor, he purchased instruments for his daughters and paid for their lessons. He drove them to Massachusetts to record, believing he needed to capture their sound "while they were still at their peak," despite what others might perceive as their lack of musical talent. "They were managed and produced by their father Austin Wiggins, and I'm putting air quotes around managed and produced. Despite being dirt poor, he bought his children instruments and paid for lessons. And then he drove everybody down to Massachusetts in order to get his girls on tape while they were still at their peak."
**Rapid Recording Process** : The Shaggs recorded an entire dozen songs in less than a single day. "They recorded a dozen songs and less than a day."
**Kurt Cobain's High Regard** : Despite the album's peculiar nature, Kurt Cobain notably ranked *Philosophy of the World* as number five on his personal list of the top 50 albums of all time. "Kurt Cobain ranked this album as number five on his top 50 albums of all time."

## albums
**Philosophy of the World (1969) – Legendary Cult Record** : Released in 1969, this album by The Shaggs is considered one of the great cult records of the last 50 years. It is described as "weird" and "hard to listen to," often guaranteed to clear a party. Despite its unusual qualities, Kurt Cobain ranked it as number five on his top 50 albums of all time. "The result was a record called Philosophy of the World... That is one of the great cult records from the last 50 years. It is weird, it is hard to listen to, and guaranteed to chase everybody out of a party at the end of the night."
