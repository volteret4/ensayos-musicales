# genre - Rock and Roll

## curiosities
**Origin of the Term** : The term "rock and roll" predates the music itself, with sailors using it in the 1600s to describe the motion of a ship. By the 1920s, it evolved into an African-American euphemism for sex, appearing in songs of that era. "The term rock and roll predates the music. In the 1600s, sailors used the term to describe the motion of a ship on the open sea."
**DJ Alan Freed's Subversive Catchphrase** : In the 1950s, DJ Alan Freed adopted "rock and roll" as a catchphrase for the new R&B-influenced music. He understood its euphemistic meaning for sex, and used it subversively, knowing his teenage audience would understand, while their parents likely would not. "Fast forward to the 1950s when DJ Alan Fried was looking for a new catchphrase."
**Characteristics of Early Rock and Roll** : For nearly two decades, rock and roll was primarily associated with music from the 1950s and early 60s, a direct descendant of 12-bar blues. It featured clean, clear guitar sounds, mostly clean lyrics (with some double entendres), and was considered "music for kids," played on 7-inch, 45 RPM records for teenagers. "Guitar sounded clean and clear. The lyrics were clean. Mostly, some double entendres did slip through. And most importantly, rock and roll was music for kids."
**Shift to "Rock"** : In the mid-1960s, the term "rock and roll" became specifically tied to the music of the 1950s and early 60s (like Elvis, Little Richard, Jerry Lee Lewis), while newer, more complex music began to be simply called "rock." "The rock and roll sound became associated and intertwined with the music of the 1950s and early 60s, Elvis, Little Richard, Jerry Lee Lewis and so on."

# genre - Rock

## curiosities
**Evolution from Rock and Roll** : Emerging in the mid-1960s, "rock" represented a heavier, harder, louder, and more complex evolution of "rock and roll." It embraced distortion and fuzz, moved beyond simple 12-bar blues structures, and was no longer solely for kids, becoming recognized as a serious art form and business. "Rock was this heavier, harder, louder and more complex version of rock and roll."
**Elevation to an Art Form** : After years of being dismissed as "kiddie music," rock gained respectability as an art form in the mid-1960s, suitable for study, analysis, and critique. The release of sophisticated albums by artists like The Beatles and Bob Dylan contributed significantly to this perception change. "After years of being criticized as kiddie music, it was now an art form to be respected, studied, analyzed and critiqued."
**Distinction from Rock and Roll** : "Rock" is often used interchangeably with "rock and roll" today, but historically, "rock and roll" referred to the 1950s/early 60s sound, while "rock" emerged in the mid-60s with more sophisticated and complex music like The Beatles' "Sergeant Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band." "Today, rock is the most prevalent term, but now it's often interchangeable with rock and roll, especially if you want to punctuate things with a cry of rock and roll."

# label - RCA

## curiosities
**Introduction of the 45 RPM Record** : RCA invented and introduced the 45 RPM record in 1949 with a larger, one-and-a-half-inch center hole. Their strategy was to promote a proprietary turntable system where users could stack 45s on a spindle for continuous play, requiring RCA-specific equipment. "This goes back to RCA's introduction of the 45 back in 1949. They were the ones to invent it."
**Proprietary Spindle System** : RCA's 45 RPM records, with their large center holes, were designed to be played on RCA record players equipped with a special spindle. This proprietary design aimed to ensure that consumers would purchase RCA's turntables to play their new format. "First of all, if you wanted to play these records, you had to buy an RCA record player with a special spindle."
**Development of the Spider Adapter** : RCA developed the "spider" adapter, a three-armed, usually yellow or red plastic insert, to allow 45s to be played on turntables with standard LP-sized spindles. These spiders had "drive dots" that interlocked when records were stacked, preventing slippage. "This was RCA's idea. Spiders looked like a three-armed galaxy, usually yellow or red, and each was made of plastic."

# label - Capitol Records

## curiosities
**"Optical Center" 45 RPM Adapter** : Capitol Records took inspiration from Fidelatone's adapter design to create their own solution called the "optical center." This adapter was a triangular piece of cardboard built into the big hole of a 45, covering most of the open space but leaving a central hole for an LP spindle. It could be punched out but not reinserted. "But then capital records took the Fidelatone idea and created an adapter that they called the optical center that was built into the big hole."

**The Earliest Instrument Smashing** : In 1956, Rockin' Rocky Rockwell performed on "The Lawrence Welk Show" and, after a "not-so-respectful" version of Elvis Presley's "Hound Dog," smashed his acoustic guitar, predating other famous instances of instrument destruction. "And when he finished, he smashed his acoustic guitar to bits."
**Pete Townsend's Accidental Destruction** : In 1964, Pete Townsend of The Who accidentally broke his new Rickenbacker guitar by hitting a low ceiling at the Railway Tavern in London. His frustration led him to intentionally smash the guitar, an act that was well-received by the audience and became a band trademark. "At some point, Pete Townsend raised his brand new Rikin' Backer guitar too high and smashed the headstock into the ceiling."
**Jimi Hendrix's Pyrotechnic Destruction** : In 1967, Jimi Hendrix concluded his set at the Monterey Pop Festival by dousing his guitar with lighter fluid, setting it on fire with a match, and then breaking it into pieces, creating one of rock's most iconic moments. "In 1967, Jimmy Hendrix set his guitar on fire at the end of his set at the Monterey Pop Festival, a little lighter fluid, a match, and boom!"
**Theatrical Destruction by Paul Stanley** : In the 1970s, Paul Stanley of KISS frequently and theatrically destroyed multiple guitars as part of the band's elaborate stage show. "Paul Stanley, theatrically, went through many, many guitars as part of his show."
**Kurt Cobain's Rampant Destruction** : In the early 1990s, at the height of Nirvana's fame, Kurt Cobain was known for destroying numerous guitars during performances, and also wrecking other band members' equipment. "Kurt Cobain went through many, many guitars. He also wrecked as much of everyone else's gear as possible."
**Phoebe Bridgers' Televised Act** : Phoebe Bridgers made headlines when she dramatically smashed her guitar at the end of her performance on "Saturday Night Live." "Phoebe Bridgers killed her guitar at the end of a Saturday Night Live performance."
**Matt Bellamy's World Record** : Matt Bellamy of Muse holds the Guinness Book of World Records for destroying the most guitars, having totaled 140 guitars during the band's 2004 tour in support of their "Absolution" album. "The all-time champion guitar smasher is... Oh, it's not Pete Townsend of the Hoot. It's Matt Bellamy of Muse."

# instrument - Amplifier

## curiosities
**Jeff Beck's Frustrated Destruction** : In a scene from the 1966 movie "Blowup," Jeff Beck, frustrated by his amp shorting out, ended up trying to destroy it with his guitar. "His amp started to short out and Jeff ended up trying to destroy it with his guitar."
**Pete Townsend's Intentional Destruction** : After accidentally breaking his guitar on a low ceiling, Pete Townsend of The Who amplified his frustration by hammering his guitar into his amplifier until both were splintered, setting a precedent for instrument destruction. "He was annoyed and decided to make a spectacle of his frustration and hammered the guitar into the stage in his amp until it was nothing but splinters."

# instrument - Recording Horn

## curiosities
**Early Fade-Out Simulation** : In an 1895 recording called "The Spirit of 76" featuring a marching band, a fade-out effect was achieved by having the band physically march away from the acoustic recording horn. As they moved further away, their sound naturally became quieter, simulating a fade. "As the song winds down, the band marches away from the acoustic recording horn. The further they get from that recording horn, the quieter they become and thus fade out."

# instrument - Magnetic Tape

## curiosities
**Revolutionizing Recording and Editing** : In the 1950s, recording studios transitioned from direct-to-disk methods to magnetic tape. This innovation allowed for mistakes to be corrected by rewinding and re-recording, eliminating the need to discard entire disks and paving the way for multi-track recording and electronic fade-outs. "But in the 1950s, recording studios started changing over from the direct to disk method to magnetic tape."
**Analog Recording Medium** : Magnetic tape is an analog recording medium, often represented by the "A" in Spars Codes (e.g., AAD, ADA, AAA). Music recorded on old-school magnetic tape is considered an analog recording. "If it was recorded on old-school magnetic tape, that's analog, so A."

# instrument - Hard Drive

## curiosities
**Digital Recording Medium** : A hard drive is a digital recording medium. When music is recorded directly to a hard drive, it is considered a digital recording, represented by the "D" in Spars Codes (e.g., DDD). "If the music went directly to a hard drive, then that's digital, hence D."

# instrument - Digital Audio Workstation (DAW)

## curiosities
**Modern Digital Recording Systems** : Digital Audio Workstations (DAWs) like Pro Tools, linked to hard drive systems, began replacing massive reel-to-reel machines in recording studios in the very late 1980s and early 90s, signifying a major shift towards digital production. "It wasn't until the very late 1980s and early 90s that they started switching away from massive real-to-real machines to hard drive systems linked to a digital audio workstation like Pro Tools."

# curiosity

**The "Say Cheese" Trick** : The practice of saying "cheese" for a photograph to encourage a smile is of uncertain origin, but the earliest reference dates to an October 1943 Texas newspaper report. Joseph E. Davies, a former ambassador to Moscow, revealed it as his secret for looking pleasant in photos, though he learned it from another politician. In early photography, smiling was considered undignified, with photographers often instructing people to say "prunes," which resulted in a "duck face." "The earliest reference to say cheese comes from a Texas newspaper report in October 1943."
**Origin of HTTP and WWW in URLs** : The "HTTP" in a website address stands for Hypertext Transfer Protocol, a set of rules for transferring files online. "WWW" stands for World Wide Web, where the URL resides. The "slash slash" (//) is a holdover from computer code written for the Apollo missions to the moon. Tim Berners-Lee, who first assembled this in spring 1992, has expressed regret for the complexity and wished he had designed something better. "The www is worldwide web, of course, which is where the URL lives. What's with that slash slash thing? That's a holdover from the computer code that was written for the Apollo missions to the moon."
**Countdown to Rocket Launches** : The tradition of a countdown to a rocket launch, seemingly obvious, was actually adopted by NASA from a 1929 German silent film titled "Fraumund" (Women in the Moon). This film, considered an early serious sci-fi movie, featured a countdown from six for its rocket launches. "NASA took the idea from a 1929 silent film called Fraumund, which translates from German as Women in the Moon."
**Explicit Language in Music** : Fifty years ago, swearing in songs was shocking. While seemingly innocent songs were misinterpreted and raunchy songs with suggestive titles existed (e.g., "New Rubin on that Darn Old Thing" by Oscars Chicago Swingers, "Hot Nuts" by Lil Johnson, "60 Minute Man" by The Dominoes), explicit swearing was not intentionally recorded until The Fugs' "C.I.A. Man" in 1965. "Cussing in songs goes back decades and decades."
**The "Rock Dot" Umlaut** : The umlaut, a diacritic form of punctuation, appears as two dots over letters in certain languages like German and Swedish to indicate pronunciation. In music, particularly rock and metal, these have become known as "rock dots." Bands like Blue Oyster Cult were the first to adopt them in 1970 purely for aesthetic reasons, as they looked "cool," "foreign," "Gothic," "exotic," "mean," "frightening," or "evil." "If you're unclear, the umlot is the two dots that appear over letters in certain languages like German and Swedish."
**Historical Precursors to Fade-Outs** : The concept of a musical piece gradually diminishing in volume has classical precedents. Joseph Haydn's "Farewell Symphony" (c. 1772) ended with musicians leaving the stage one by one, extinguishing candles. Gustav Holtz's "The Planets" (1914) concluded with an offstage female chorus whose volume decreased as a door was gradually closed. "We might go as far back as classical composer Joseph Heiden. In about 1772, he started performances of what would become known as the Farewell Symphony."
**Early Recording Fade-Outs** : An 1895 outdoor recording called "The Spirit of 76," featuring a marching band, achieved a fade-out by having the band march away from the acoustic recording horn, naturally diminishing the volume. A 1930 recording by George Olson's Big Band, "Beyond the Blue Horizon," ended with a train receding into the distance, simulating a fade. "The Spirit of 76 that featured a marching band. As the song winds down, the band marches away from the acoustic recording horn."
**Electronic Fade-Outs Emerge (c. 1950s)** : Song fade-outs, as commonly known, first appeared around 1950, coinciding with the shift from direct-to-disk recording to magnetic tape. Magnetic tape allowed for editing and multi-track recording, making electronic fade-outs possible by gradually turning down input volume from microphones. "Songfade-outs, as we know them, first appeared sometime around 1950."
**Reasons for Electronic Fade-Outs** : Fade-outs offered several benefits: artistically, they freed artists from composing definitive endings, allowing endless repetition of hooks; aesthetically, they reinforced the song's impact by making it feel like it continued indefinitely; technically, engineers used fades to prevent audio degradation towards the center of master disks; and for radio, fades were used to shorten songs over three minutes and provide a cue for DJs to talk over. "Artistically, it meant that the artist did not have to come up with a definite ending."
**Evolution of Fade-Out Styles** : Over the years, different types of fade-outs developed, including the quick fade for AM radio, the very fast Motown fade, and the long, slow FM fade, exemplified by The Eagles' "Hotel California." Fade-outs have cycled in and out of popularity, big in the 60s/70s, less so in the 80s/90s, and hit-or-miss today. "Over the years, different types of fades appeared. There was the quick fade for AM radio."
**The 7-Inch Single's Large Hole (45 RPM)** : Unlike the standard 9.30-second hole of 33 1/3 RPM LPs (standardized since 1948), 7-inch 45 RPM singles, introduced by RCA in 1949, feature a full one-and-a-half-inch hole. This design facilitated RCA's proprietary system for stacking multiple 45s on a special spindle for continuous playback. "Most seven inch singles have a hole that is much, much bigger than what we see on 33 and a third vinyl albums."
**Purpose of the Large 45 RPM Hole** : The large hole served multiple purposes for RCA: it required consumers to buy RCA's turntables with a special spindle; it made it easier for records to drop onto the platter in a stacking system; and it spread the turntable's torque over a greater circumference, reducing wear on the hole. "The bigger hole served several purposes. First of all, if you wanted to play these records, you had to buy an RCA record player with a special spindle."
**The 45 RPM Adapter (Spider)** : The large hole of 45 RPM records necessitated adapters for playback on standard multi-speed turntables designed for LPs. Early adapters were solid zinc (Webster Chicago Corporation) or similar (Fidelatone), and difficult to use. Capitol Records developed an "optical center" cardboard adapter. RCA later introduced the iconic "spider" – a three-armed plastic insert with "drive dots" to prevent slippage when records were stacked. "And so was born the 45 RPM adapter."
**SX2 Adapter for Club Mixing** : In the 1980s, a Boston nightclub DJ designed the SX2, a large frisbee-like adapter that fit over the entire turntable platter. It had a central space for the 7-inch single and ample room around the circumference, making it easier for DJs to mix 45s, scratch, and slipcue in clubs. "In the 1980s, we had something called the SX2, designed by a Boston nightclub DJ that made it easier to mix 45s while spinning in clubs."
**Instrument Smashing in Rock** : The practice of bands smashing their instruments at the end of a set dates back to Rockin' Rocky Rockwell on "The Lawrence Welk Show" in 1956, where he smashed his acoustic guitar. The Who, particularly Pete Townsend, popularized this in 1964 after an accidental break led to intentional destruction, making it their trademark. Other notable destroyers include Jeff Beck, Jimi Hendrix, KISS's Paul Stanley, Nirvana's Kurt Cobain, Nine Inch Nails' Trent Reznor, Phoebe Bridgers, and Guinness record holder Matt Bellamy of Muse. "When did it become fashionable for a band to smash their instruments at the end of a set?"
**The Spars Code for Digital Audio (AAD, ADA, ADD, DDD)** : Introduced in 1984, about a year after the CD, the Spars Code uses three letters (A for analog, D for digital) to indicate the type of audio recorder used for recording, mixing, and mastering. The first letter denotes recording method, the second for mixing, and the third for mastering. For CDs, the final letter is always D as it's a fully digital format. "This is something called the Spars Code. It appeared in 1984, about a year after the CD was introduced."
**Transition from Analog to Digital Studios** : Recording studios were predominantly analog for a long time, relying on massive reel-to-reel machines. The shift to hard drive systems linked to Digital Audio Workstations (DAWs) like Pro Tools began in the very late 1980s and early 90s, though experiments with full digital recordings date back to 1973. "Recording studios were very analog for a very long time. It wasn't until the very late 1980s and early 90s that they started switching away from massive real-to-real machines to hard drive systems linked to a digital audio workstation like Pro Tools."
**Early Digital Recording Limitations** : Despite early efforts like Roy Cooder's "Bop to You Drop" (1978) and The English Beat's "Special Beat Service" (1981) using digital recording, the absence of digital mixing boards at the time meant these albums still underwent analog mixing and mastering steps. Artists like Stevie Wonder, Christopher Cross, and Fleetwood Mac also made partially digital recordings. "However, there was no such thing as a digital mixing board at the time, so while it was recorded digitally, it still went through two analog steps for mixing and mastering."
**Vinyl vs. CD Sound Quality Debate** : Technically, CDs capture an audio signal with more accuracy due to factors like bit depth, sample rate, and Red Book standards. However, the perceived sound quality (warmth, smoothness) is highly dependent on how the music was mixed and mastered for each specific format. Mastering for vinyl involves subtle frequency tweaks to compensate for physical limitations (e.g., deep bass), while CDs can handle a full range of frequencies but require specific mastering to avoid harshness. "From a sheer technical point of view, the CD captures an audio signal with more accuracy."
**Vinyl's Unique Artifacts** : Vinyl records inherently come with noise, rumble, hiss, and clicks due to their mechanical playback nature. These artifacts can contribute to a "feeling of realness and warmth" for some listeners. "And vinyl does have problems with noise, rumble, hiss, clicks. Those are artifacts of the mechanical nature of vinyl playback and may contribute to its feeling of realness and warmth."
**CD's Potential for Harshness** : While properly constructed CD recordings avoid vinyl's noise artifacts, poorly mastered CDs can suffer from harshness or a high-frequency shrillness. This issue often arises when master tapes originally prepared for vinyl are directly transferred to CD without specific remastering. "However, CDs can have an issue with harshness, a high frequency shrillness that can be really off-putting."
**Impact of Mastering on Sound Quality** : The mastering process is crucial for both vinyl and CD, requiring specific adjustments to match the characteristics and abilities of each format. An album mastered for one format and "shoe-horned" into another (e.g., a vinyl master directly to CD, or vice versa) will result in suboptimal sound quality, irrespective of the format itself. "If you do not mix and master specifically to vinyl, and specifically for CD, you are going to get music that doesn't sound as good as it should, regardless of the format."
**Example of Poor CD Remastering** : Early CDs from the 1980s sometimes suffered from poor sound quality because labels simply transferred master tapes intended for vinyl directly to CD without remastering. This could lead to issues like background hiss, lost bass, and rolled-off trebles, as heard on some copies of Elvis Costello's "My Aim Is True." "I have some CDs from the 1980s where they didn't bother to remaster them for the new format. All they did was take the master tapes they used to press the album and transfer that to CD as is."
**Example of Poor Vinyl Remastering** : Some vinyl records issued since 2001 exhibit poor sound quality when the equalization of a CD master is simply transferred to vinyl without proper adjustment. This "shoe-horning" can lead to distortions and issues with low frequencies. "Then again, I have some vinyl records issued since 2001 where the label did not bother to change the equalization of the master to something that worked on vinyl."
