# artist - Jimi Hendrix

## studios
Olympic Studios
Electric Lady Studios

## curiosities
**Work at Olympic Studios** : Jimi Hendrix recorded at Olympic Studios, a prominent London recording facility. "Jimmy Hendrix also worked here."
**Purchase of Generation Nightclub** : In 1969, Jimi Hendrix purchased the Generation Nightclub, a venue he enjoyed for jamming, with the intention of converting it into his personal recording studio. "In 1969, Jimmy bought a nightclub, which is a place he loved to jam in. It's called the Generation Nightclub."
**Vision for Electric Lady Studios** : Hendrix initially expressed a desire to install a "little 28 tracks" in a corner of the nightclub, which his producer Eddie Kramer understood as the need for a comprehensive studio given Hendrix's substantial recording expenses at the time. "Hey, when you come down and we want you to see this place because Jimmy wants to put a little 28 tracks to the end of the back corner."
**"Surround Windows" Request** : The only specific design request Jimi Hendrix made for Electric Lady Studios was for "surround windows," which were incorporated into the control room doors using a plasma cutter. "He only said one thing to me. He said, hey man, I want surround windows."
**High Studio Spending** : In 1969, Jimi Hendrix was incurring "insane" studio costs of $200,000 annually, which served as a major impetus for him to build his own recording facility. "Do you know how much Jimmy was spending in 1969 dollars? 200,000 a year, which was an insane amount because he was always in the studio recording."

# artist - George Martin

## member of
Associated Independent Recording (Air)

## labels
EMI

## curiosities
**Producer for The Beatles** : George Martin produced The Beatles during the first half of their career while he was a salaried employee of EMI Recording Studios. "George was working with the Beatles during the first half of their career."
**Salary Dispute with EMI** : Despite The Beatles' immense success, George Martin, as an EMI employee, received only a "tiny weekly salary" and no royalties for his production work, which caused him considerable annoyance. "No matter how successful the Beatles became, no matter how many records they sold, George did not receive any royalties as their producer."
**Resignation from EMI (1965)** : In 1965, George Martin resigned from EMI after the label repeatedly refused to modify his pay structure to include royalties. "And when EMI turned them down again to have his pay structure modified, he quit. That was 1965."
**Contractor for The Beatles** : After leaving EMI, George Martin was hired back by The Beatles to continue his production work with them, but this time as an independent contractor, not an EMI employee. "The Beatles then hired him back and he continued to work with them as a contractor, not as an employee of EMI."
**Founding of Air Studios** : George Martin established his own company, Associated Independent Recording (Air), which subsequently operated a series of recording facilities under the name Air Studios. "His new company was called Associated Independent Recording or Air."
**Investment in Air Oxford Street** : George Martin invested a substantial sum, over six million dollars (a fortune at the time), into equipping the first Air Studios location on Oxford Street. "George sunken off a lot of money into that place over six million dollars, which was a fortune for that time."
**Custom 56-Track Console** : Air Studios on Oxford Street featured a specialized 56-track recording console that was custom-made specifically for George Martin. "There was a 56 track recording console that was custom made just for George."

# curiosity

**Recording Studio Atmosphere** : A recording studio is likened to "the musical equivalent of an operating theater," characterized by clean, dust-free air, constant air conditioning (to counter heat from electronics), a faint whiff of ozone, and extreme quiet due to extensive isolation and soundproofing, creating a serious and dedicated environment for music. "To me, a recording studio is the musical equivalent of an operating theater, a place that's dedicated to one thing and one thing only."
**Decline of Traditional Studios** : The number of traditional, fully equipped recording studios has drastically decreased, falling victim to the changing economics of the music industry and the advent of bedroom recording capabilities with laptops. "Many of the legendary studios are gone, a victim of the changing economics of the music industry."
**Trident Studios - Founding and Reputation** : Founded and built in 1967 in London's Soho district on St. Anne's Court, Trident Studios quickly earned a reputation for having the "loudest control room" in London, with monitors capable of producing sound so powerful it could "blow back your hair." "It was founded and built in 1967 as a full-service music recording facility. And it quickly gained your reputation as the studio with the loudest control room in all of London."
**Trident Studios - Technological Pioneer** : Trident Studios was a leader in recording technology, possibly the first UK studio to offer 8-track recording and definitively the first to provide 16-track recording, making it a state-of-the-art facility for its era. "It might have been the first UK studio to offer eight track recording. Now that's disputed, but it was certainly the first to offer 16 track recording."
**Trident Studios - Men's Bathroom Reverb** : For certain recordings, notably piano parts for David Bowie's "Life on Mars," microphones were sometimes strategically placed in the men's bathroom at Trident Studios, utilizing its natural reverb to achieve a distinctive and special sound. "It's also quite possible that the microphones were arranged in the nearby men's bathroom, because there was something special about the natural reverb in there that just made a recording sound so special."
**Trident Studios - Current Operation** : Trident Studios continues to operate in its original London location but no longer accepts clients for full album recordings, having transitioned to focusing on voiceovers and other production projects. "Triton still operates in the same location today, although it no longer takes on clients to record albums that's used just for voiceovers and other production projects."
**Wessex Studios - Conversion from Church Hall** : Wessex Studios in Islington, North London, was originally a large Gothic church hall (St. Augustine's church), built in 1881, which was converted into a recording studio in 1966. Its main room, measuring 50 by 30 feet with a 15-foot ceiling, maintained a "really live sound" suitable for orchestras. "This was a big gothic place built in 1881 and then converted into a studio in 1966."
**Wessex Studios - "Mono Deluxe" Technique** : Producer Chris Thomas, who experienced partial deafness in his left ear, devised a unique mixing method called "mono deluxe" at Wessex Studios. This involved layering duplicated guitar parts—one centered, one hard left, and a third hard right with a subtle pitch shift to introduce rawness—and duplicating the bass an octave lower, which became the signature studio sound for The Sex Pistols. "Producer Chris Thomas was also a little deaf in his left ear, so he had something of a hard time perceiving true stereo. So he came up with something he called mono deluxe."
**Wessex Studios - Post-Closure Fate** : Wessex Studios ceased to exist as a recording facility after being sold in 2003. The former church hall has since been redeveloped into nine luxury apartments and is now known as "The Recording Studio." "Westc studio is no longer exist by the way. It was sold in 2003 and that old church hall now consists of nine luxury apartments."
**Olympic Studios - Historical Building Use** : The building that eventually housed Olympic Studios in London had a diverse past, serving first as a movie theater for 50 years, then as a TV studio in the late 1950s, before its acquisition and transformation into a proper recording studio in 1965. "The building that housed Olympic studios spent 50 years as a movie theater before it was turned into a TV studio in the late 1950s and then in 1965 the facility was bought and turned into a proper recording studio."
**Olympic Studios - Studio Dimensions and Reverb** : Olympic Studios featured a massive Studio 1 (62x42 feet) with a low ceiling (<9 feet), a smaller Studio 2 (20x14 feet) with a higher ceiling (14 feet), and a Studio 3. Notably, stairwells, including one designated as an emergency exit, were ingeniously used to generate natural reverb. "Studio 1 was huge, 62 by 42 feet. But the ceiling was low, less than 9 feet... There were plenty of stairwells too and that was important because they were used as natural generators of reverb, including one that was supposed to be used as an emergency exit."
**Olympic Studios - Closure (2009)** : Olympic Studios closed its doors in 2009, a casualty of the 2008 financial crisis coupled with the shifting economics of the music industry, which made sustaining such an expensive facility unfeasible amidst drastically reduced recording budgets. "But then came the financial crisis of 2008, combined with the changing economics of the music industry. It just wasn't possible to maintain such an expensive facility when recording budgets had been slashed to the bone. So Olympic closed in 2009."
**Olympic Studios - Current Use** : The building that once housed Olympic Studios has returned to its original function from over a century ago, now operating as a two-screen cinema. "It is now a two-screen cinema. It's like it started out to be over 100 years ago."
**Air Oxford Street - Location and Capacity** : The first Air Studios, Air Oxford Street, opened in 1970 on the fourth floor of the Peter Robinson department store, located directly above the Tube Station at Oxford Circus. It operated until 1991, featuring four distinct studios, including one large enough to accommodate up to 50 musicians for orchestral film scoring. "The first was at the corner of Oxford and Regent Street, right on Oxford Circus as a matter of fact, right above the Tube Station... It featured four separate studios, including a big one that could have combined up to 50 musicians at a time, which was great for orchestras that were scoring movies."
**Air Oxford Street - Closure (1991)** : Air Oxford Street closed in 1991 because the costs associated with maintaining the Oxford Street address became prohibitive. "But after a while, it became just too expensive to maintain the Oxford Street address."
**Air Lyndhurst Hall - Church Conversion** : In 1991, Air Studios relocated to an old church, originally built in 1880, in North London, which was transformed into Air Lyndhurst Hall. This facility remains operational today, widely used for recording music, film scores, and video game sound effects. "So in 1991, Air moved to an old church that was built in 1880 in North London."
**Air Lyndhurst Hall - Studio One Capacity** : Studio One at Air Lyndhurst Hall is a spacious, hexagon-shaped room covering 300 square meters, designed to be large enough to host an entire orchestra and a full choir performing simultaneously. "Studio one. It's a big hexagon. It has 300 square meters of space to move around in. Now to give you an idea how big that is, you can have an entire orchestra and a full choir setup and performing at the same time."
**Air Montserrat - Caribbean Location and Destruction** : Air Montserrat, located in the Caribbean, was a previous Air Studios facility that was tragically destroyed by Hurricane Hugo in 1989 and has remained in ruins on the island ever since, never having been rebuilt. "In between was Air Montserrat in the Caribbean. We covered that studio in the last program. This was the one destroyed by Hurricane Hugo in 1989 and never rebuilt."
**Electric Lady Studios - Founding Context** : Electric Lady Studios, situated in Greenwich Village, New York, was conceived and built for Jimi Hendrix by Eddie Kramer in 1969, converting a nightclub Hendrix owned. It was pioneering as arguably the first recording studio specifically designed for an artist. "We designed Electric Lady for Jimmy. It was the first time this was a studio that was built for the artist so that it was Jimmy's place."
**Electric Lady Studios - Opening and Cost** : Electric Lady Studios had its unofficial opening in May 1970, with its construction spanning a year and the outfitting of its two studios costing approximately $1 million in 1969/early 1970 dollars. "We opened an unofficially May of 1970... It was a million dollars to outfit it. It was two studios."
**Electric Lady Studios - Technological Evolution** : Initially featuring 16-track recording, Electric Lady Studios was rapidly wired for 24-track technology shortly after Jimi Hendrix's passing, reflecting the quick advancements in recording from early mono tracks through various multi-track systems, including an ill-fated "bastard kind of formation" 12-track system. "It was the state of the art. In fact, we had it wide for 24 track, was within, you know, very, very shortly after Jimmy died, we were up to 24."
**Electric Lady Studios - Current Location** : Electric Lady Studios continues its operations at its historic address, 52 West 8th Street in New York City. "Which is still in operation at 52 West 8th Street in New York City."
**Fame Recording Studios - Southern R&B Hub** : Fame Recording Studios, located on Avalon Avenue in Sheffield, Alabama, opened around 1960 in a former tobacco warehouse. It quickly earned a reputation as a premier destination for soul, blues, and R&B musicians, notably offering a safe and undisturbed recording environment for black artists during a period of severe racial discrimination. "The first facility is Fame Recording. This is on Avalon Avenue. It was built at a former tobacco warehouse in about 1960. And a Gainer reputation is the place for soul, blues, and R&B musicians to make records."
**Muscle Shoals Sound Studio - Founding and Initial Operations** : Also located in Sheffield, Alabama, Muscle Shoals Sound Studio was founded in 1969 by a quartet of session musicians from Fame Recording, collectively known as The Muscle Shoals Rhythm Section. "Then came Muscle Shoal Sound Studio, a modest sort of place founded in 1969 by four session musicians from Fame who went by the name The Muscle Shoal's Rhythm Section."
**Muscle Shoals Sound Studio - Closure and Revival** : The studio closed in 1979 and was converted into an appliance store. Years later, it was successfully rescued and meticulously restored with vintage equipment, thanks in part to a $1 million donation from Beats headphones, becoming a protected historic site that now offers daily tours. "That place closed in 1979 and ended up as of all things an appliance store. All the gear was either moved to another facility or so low."
**Muscle Shoals Documentary** : A documentary titled "Muscle Shoals" exists, which comprehensively details the studio's rich history and significant musical contributions. "If you're into music documentaries, there's one called Muscle Shoals that details everything."
**The Bath House - Historical Building and Location** : The Bath House, situated west of Kingston, Ontario, on Bath Road near Amherst Island, is a historic building dating back to 1842. It originally functioned as a coach stop and later a bed and breakfast before being transformed into a recording facility. "On your left will be Amherst Island. Eventually you'll come to a lakeside parking lot. Across the road on the north side is a building that dates back to 1842. This is the Bath House, the live work recording facility owned by the tragically hip."
**The Bath House - Residential Studio Amenities** : As one of Canada's few residential studios, The Bath House allows artists to live on-site while recording. It boasts a control room equipped with vintage gear (including a console from The Tonight Show with Johnny Carson), instrument rooms, a massive piano, a full kitchen, bedrooms, a pool room, and outdoor amenities such as a basketball court, swimming pool, horseshoe pit, pitch-and-putt golf area, barbecues, bonfires, and a hockey pond in winter. "It is one of the few places in Canada where you can move in for as long as it takes to make your album."
**Rolling Stones Mobile Recording Studio - Construction and Features** : Built by Helios Electronics in 1968, this 10-meter-long truck was a highly successful innovation, designed to house a control room and snake necessary cables to remote playing areas, providing unprecedented flexibility for recording on location. "A company called Helios Electronics was hired to build the 10 meter long truck, and it was an amazing success."
**Rolling Stones Mobile Recording Studio - Immortalized in Song** : This mobile studio holds a unique place in rock history, being explicitly mentioned and immortalized in the lyrics of Deep Purple's song "Smoke on the Water." "It's even immortalized in the deep purple song Smoke on the Water."
**Rolling Stones Mobile Recording Studio - Decommissioning and Legacy** : Decommissioned in 1993, the truck was sold to a New York company, refurbished, and subsequently used to record live performances by many punk bands, starting with the Ramones. It was later discovered in New Jersey and acquired by the National Music Center in Calgary, where it continues to function as a recording facility today. "Well, it was decommissioned in 1993 and sold to a New York company which refurbished it and put it back to work. The truck was used to record live performances by a long line of punk bands from the Ramones on down."
**National Music Center in Calgary** : This center in Calgary serves as a custodian of significant musical heritage, housing both one of the famed modular consoles from Olympic Studios and the Rolling Stones Mobile Recording Studio, both of which are still operational as recording facilities. "If you ever find yourself at the National Music Center in Calgary, you should be able to find one of the famous modular consoles used at Olympic studios in the late 60s and early 70s."
