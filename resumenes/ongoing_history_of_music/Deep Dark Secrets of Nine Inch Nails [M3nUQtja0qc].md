## curiosities
**Early Life and Family History** : Trent Reznor was born on May 17, 1965, in Mercer, Pennsylvania. His family is descended from the founders of a heating company, "Resner heaters," established two generations prior. His parents, Nancy and Michael, married quickly when Nancy became pregnant during high school in 1964, making Trent a "high school dating accident." They split up after his sister was born in 1971, and Trent went to live with his maternal grandparents. "Trent Resner was a high school dating accident back in 1964."
**Musical Beginnings and Influences** : Trent began piano lessons at age five and later learned to play the tuba and saxophone in the school band. He was fascinated by bands like Supertramp and Pink Floyd, as well as anything related to the Antichrist. His primary inspiration for becoming a musician, surprisingly, came from synth-driven bands like The Human League, OMD, and Depeche Mode, which he found "really cool" compared to punk bands like the Sex Pistols, The Clash, and The Ramones. "The piano lesson started at age five. He learned to play the tuba and the saxophone in the school band."
**Early Career and Studio Work** : Trent initially considered a job making synthesizers but instead moved to Cleveland, working in a music store that sold many keyboards. This allowed him to experiment with the latest gear. Later, in late 1987 or early 1988, he found work at The Right Track recording studio in Cleveland, performing menial tasks like gathering cables and cleaning toilets. During the quiet hours, usually between 3 and 8 AM, he had the studio to himself to develop what would become the first Nine Inch Nails album. "By this time, late 87 early 1988, Trent had found work in Cleveland in a recording studio called The Right Track."
**Personal Song Inspiration - Sanctified** : The anguish and heartbreak in the song "Sanctified" from "Pretty Hate Machine" were genuinely inspired by a personal relationship. Just before Trent left for England to record the debut album, he reconnected with an ex-girlfriend named Patty, only to discover she had met someone else, gotten pregnant, and was getting married. Legend also suggests the song might have been inspired by a relationship with a cocaine pipe. "So a lot of anguish and heartbreak that we hear in that song is completely genuine."
**Frustration and Evolution with "Broken"** : While battling TVT Records, Trent felt immense frustration, amplified by the unexpected success of Nine Inch Nails, which made him uncomfortable. Facing the prospect of being unable to release new music due to the legal dispute, and dealing with personal crises, he channeled these feelings into the "Broken" EP. He aimed for a more aggressive sound, experimenting with real guitars and drums, having previously only used machine buttons. He also harbored a desire to "commercially destroy" Nine Inch Nails with this record. "I also was faced with the prospect of it all being over because I couldn't put in music anymore because of this f***ing record label that I was on, and I was on some personal crises at the same time, so it all got focused into that little EP of hate."
**"Eat Your Heart Out, Steve" Message** : Following the commercial and critical success of the "Broken" EP, which won a Grammy, Trent Reznor included a hidden message for TVT president Steve Gottlieb. At the 32-second mark of track 98, an unlisted track called "Physical," Trent can be heard whispering, "eat your heart out, Steve." This subtle act of defiance was his personal vindication against the label executive who had called his debut album an "abortion." "It's there, but you'll eat headphones to hear it properly."

## albums
**Pretty Hate Machine (1989) – Debut Album** : Released on November 29, 1989, this album was initially hated by the label, TVT, and received no interest from MTV, commercial radio, or even the nascent alternative radio. Despite this lack of support, it sold over 150,000 copies in 14 months, which was considered impressive given its grassroots success. The album marked a shift for Trent Reznor from his earlier techno-pop influences towards a harder, more sinister industrial sound, incorporating elements of rage and fury inspired by bands like Ministry and Skinny Puppy. It was recorded by Trent largely alone in The Right Track studio during off-hours, with the help of four different producers in England who guided him in toughening up the sound. "Pretty Hate Machine was released on November 29, 1989, and that's when things started to get very strange."
**Broken (1992) – Halo 5 EP** : This EP, released on September 16, 1992, debuted at number seven on the album charts and quickly went platinum. It earned a Grammy for Best Metal Performance, marking a significant vindication for Trent Reznor amidst his legal battles with TVT Records. Created out of intense frustration and personal crises, the EP was designed to be more aggressive than previous work, incorporating real guitars and drums for the first time, as Trent had gained experience touring with live drummers. "The Broken EP Halo 5 in the 9-inch nails catalog released September 16, 1992."
**The Downward Spiral (1994) – Breakthrough Album** : Released two years after the "Broken" EP, this album marked a turning point for Nine Inch Nails, propelling Trent Reznor into widespread success. After its release, Trent's career took off with extensive tours, remixing projects, producing gigs, and the growth of his Nothing Records label. The period from "The Downward Spiral" onwards is described as well-documented, contrasting with the more obscure early years. "Two years after Broken came out, the downward spiral. And from that point, Trent Resner has really never looked back."
**The Fragile (1999) – Double Album** : This long-awaited double album was released in 1999. It represents a later phase in Nine Inch Nails' career, following the success of "The Downward Spiral," and is noted as one of the significant releases in Trent Reznor's well-documented post-platinum period. "And of course, the long-awaited 1999 double album, The Fragile."

## songs
**Down In It (1988)** : This was the very first song Trent Reznor wrote in his new, harder, and more sinister style, moving beyond his techno-pop influences. The original version was about half the speed of the record version and was admitted by Trent to be "a total ripoff of Dig It, my Skinny Puppy." Lyrically, it stemmed from a train-of-thought process, reflecting Trent's coming-of-age realization that earlier illusions about life's certainties were false, leading to a feeling of things not working out as hoped. "That was the first song I'd ever written. When I sat down, I took a very experimental approach to it."
**Sanctified (1989)** : Featured on "Pretty Hate Machine," this song underwent a significant transformation during production. Initially described as sounding like "angry Loverboy" with "cheesy 80-style pitch blend flourishes on the keyboard and the YouTube type guitars," it was reshaped by producer John Friar into a much more sophisticated and sinister track. The lyrics are deeply personal, reflecting Trent's anguish over an ex-girlfriend, Patty, who became pregnant and engaged to someone else shortly before he left for England to record. "While comparing what you just heard with the finished version, thanks to producer John Friar, Sank Defied turned into something much more sophisticated and much more sinister."
**Twist / Ring Finger (1989)** : This track from "Pretty Hate Machine" was initially known as "Twist" before being renamed "Ring Finger." Its creation process began with a rhythm track, and Trent built upon it piece by piece, retaining elements that worked and discarding those that didn't, showcasing his experimental approach to composition during the album's development. "A track called Twist was eventually renamed Ring Finger, and in the beginning, this song was simply known as You Know Who You Are."
**Physical (1992)** : An unlisted track, designated as track 98, on the "Broken" EP. This track contains a hidden message from Trent Reznor for Steve Gottlieb, the president of TVT Records. At the 32-second mark, Trent can be heard whispering "eat your heart out, Steve," an act of defiance and vindication following the success of the EP amidst his legal battles with the label. "If you listen very closely to the 32nd mark of track 98 on this CD, a track called Physical, you'll hear Trent whisper in the background, eat your heart out, Steve."

## curiosities
**Origin of the Name "Nine Inch Nails"** : The name "Nine Inch Nails" does not carry any special meaning or symbolism, despite potential rumors. Trent Reznor simply came up with it while doodling names and liked how it sounded, so he decided to keep it. "Well, nowhere really. It doesn't mean anything special, despite any rumors that you may have heard."
**Early Label Deal and TVT Dispute** : Nine Inch Nails' first record deal was with TV Toons, a Chicago label previously specializing in TV theme song compilations, whose president, Steve Gottlieb, rebranded it as TVT. Gottlieb initially expected an "edgier Depeche Mode" but was deeply disappointed by "Pretty Hate Machine," calling it an "abortion" and predicting only 20,000 sales. This led to a "nightmare" legal battle lasting almost two years (1990-1992), during which Trent was contractually prohibited from releasing new music. "However, Trent was beginning to move beyond his favorite techno pop bands and into something a little more sinister and a lot harder."
**Sampling on Pretty Hate Machine** : The debut album "Pretty Hate Machine" extensively utilized samples from various artists, including Jane's Addiction, Public Enemy, and This Mortal Coil. These samples were often heavily modified and mutilated by computers, making them largely unrecognizable in the final tracks, a technique Chris Vrenna was particularly skilled at. "For example, if you go through that first album, you'll hear samples from Jane's Addiction, Public Enemy, This Mortal Coil, and many others."
**Financial Struggles During TVT Battle** : During the protracted legal dispute with TVT Records, Trent Reznor and Nine Inch Nails faced significant financial challenges due to their restrictive contract. The band's participation in the first Lollapalooza tour in the summer of 1991, where they were paid $12,000 per gig, was crucial. This money largely went towards paying for legal fees, as well as essential living expenses like food and rent, keeping the band afloat during a difficult period. "The $12,000 nine-inch nails was paid for each gig, but a long way towards paying for lawyers, not to mention for things like food and rent."
**Transition to Interscope Records** : The band was eventually rescued from its legal quagmire with TVT Records by Interscope. Trent Reznor initially viewed this move as "the lesser of several evils" at the time, initially wary due to his perception of Interscope's then-image (associated with Marky Mark). However, he later expressed happiness with the situation, appreciating Interscope's label president who was also a producer, understanding the creative process and potential difficulties artists face. "They rescued us from TBT, it was a lesser of several evils, and at the time my vision of the inner scope was Mark E. Mark, it was like, Christ, one TVT, and another."
**Songwriting Philosophy** : Trent Reznor's songwriting for "Pretty Hate Machine" and subsequent works was deeply rooted in honest, personal experience. Lacking a guitar-centric background, he utilized computers to simulate sounds, allowing him a broad sonic palette. He focused on relating his own feelings and dissatisfactions with relationships, religion, or government, disguising them to avoid preaching, aiming for relatability rather than political commentary. "So the only thing I could speak about with any authority was my own personal experience and tried to relate situations I'd been into or feelings I'd thought, or dissatisfactions with relationships or religion or the government of the country I live in, or whatever it is."

## curiosities
**Early Band Formation and Style** : In 1983, an 18-year-old Trent Reznor played keyboards and sang in Option 30, a band that identified primarily as New Wave but explored a variety of styles, including reggae. They aimed to avoid being overly "synth pop" or "polisish" in their material, indicating an early interest in diverse sounds while seeking their own direction. "Well, right now we're trying to see what direction we're going to write the material in. We don't want to bear too heavily towards synth pop."
**Performing in a Conservative Area** : Option 30 struggled to play original material in conservative areas like Edinburgh or Meadville, where audiences preferred familiar cover songs by artists such as Loverboy and Sammy Hagar. This commercial pressure meant they had to include many cover songs to attract a crowd, despite their aspiration to be an all-original band, even though they claimed they wouldn't play certain covers "even at gunpoint." "The problem with an area like Edinburgh or Medeville, which where we play a lot is that it's very conservative. And people just don't want to go out and hear 40 songs they've never heard before."
**Unique Cover of "Machine Gun"** : The band performed a distinctive, Appalachian-style rendition of The Commodores' song "Machine Gun." This version incorporated unusual instrumentation like a washboard and harmonica, showcasing their experimental approach to covers and a desire to put their own spin on familiar tunes. The recording of this particular version was done very spontaneously, with the band joking it was recorded "in the car on the way up." "It's kind of a new arrangement, but I think you'll recognize it. It's kind of like Appalachian music, right?"

**Evolving Understanding of Drums** : Initially, for Trent Reznor, a "drum was a button on a machine" for early Nine Inch Nails recordings. However, after touring for a couple of years with "real drummers," he developed a deeper understanding and appreciation for the sound of actual drums, leading him to incorporate them more in later works like the "Broken" EP. "To me, drum was a button on a machine, but after touring for a couple years with real drummers, I understood, wow, it doesn't sound like that button, and I wanted to incorporate some of the sounds."

**Acquired Skill and Integration** : Initially, Trent Reznor was not primarily a guitar player, using computers to simulate guitar sounds for early Nine Inch Nails material. However, through persistent effort and touring, he improved his guitar playing. This skill development, combined with an appreciation for "what guitar sounded like" and "real drums," led him to incorporate more actual guitar into aggressive releases like the "Broken" EP. "I got better at playing guitar just from doing it."

# curiosity

**MTV's Influence on Taste and Trends** : MTV, referred to as a "God" by some, held significant power to dictate musical tastes and fashion. If MTV decided a band was "the next big thing," it often became true, with people foolishly buying into the hype. However, bands lacking substance beyond this hype would falter once MTV moved on, highlighting the transient nature of media-driven fame. "They dictate to people what tastes is and they dictate what fashion is. And for the most part, people foolishly listen to them and believe it and buy it and then they are the big thing."
**The British Press's Role in Hype Cycles** : Similar to MTV, the British press was observed to pick a band, heavily hype them for approximately six months, and then discard them. If the hyped band lacked genuine talent or substance, they would struggle to build a sustainable career after being dropped by the press, indicating a cyclical pattern of promotion and abandonment. "They'll pick a band, they'll hype the shit out of them for six months. And then they discard them and if that band isn't really that great, they can't go anywhere from them."
**Corporate Influence on Music Industry** : Trent Reznor criticized the music industry for its corporate, pre-packaged, and lifeless approach to music, contrasting it with what he saw as the dead spirit of rock and roll. He expressed disdain for labels pushing for "radio-friendly" songs and making creative compromises for commercial gain, stating he would rather not be a band if it meant becoming a "folk commercial." "I think most of the music that's out right now is pretty more in-between. Safe and pretty prepackaged and corporate and lifeless."
**Integrity in Music Production** : Trent Reznor emphasized the importance of integrity in music creation, believing artists should make music they genuinely think is good rather than conforming to corporate trends or label demands. He specifically called out the "ridiculous" notion of labels considering what's "in fashion," like grunge, to dictate music production. "I wouldn't compromise the actual music to fit into some of the ideas of what they think of going on."
**Importance of Good Label Relationships** : Trent Reznor expressed a desire to work with record company people who genuinely love music and possess knowledge of the industry, rather than solely focusing on profit. He cited Daniel Miller and people at Sire as examples of "cool record company people" who are in it for the music, contrasting them with the experience of TVT Records. "I'm convinced there are good people, somewhere, you know, I would think Daniel Miller was probably a good, and there's people at Sire, you know, that are a cool record company people that seem to have some knowledge of what's going on, and seem to be in it because they like music, not because you know how much money I can make if I, you know, I mean, it's, they like music, you know, which is why I make music."
