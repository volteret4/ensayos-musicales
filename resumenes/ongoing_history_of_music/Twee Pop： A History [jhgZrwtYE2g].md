## curiosities
**Core Characteristics** : Tweetpop is fundamentally characterized by simplicity, innocence, intense emotion, romanticism, and a strong emphasis on melody. It frequently incorporates childlike qualities in both songwriting and performance, positioning itself as a gentle counterpoint to the alt-rock world, akin to how Bubblegum music contrasted with hard rock in the 1960s and early 1970s, but without the negative connotations. "Tweetpop is all about simplicity, innocence, intense emotion, and romanticism."
**Origins in Punk and Post-Punk** : Prototweet music emerged from the 1970s punk scene, which championed the idea that anyone should be able to express themselves regardless of background or musical skill. While much punk was loud and angry, the underlying punk spirit allowed for gentler forms of expression, leading to the rise of "cool and arty" post-punk bands in the late 70s and early 80s who recorded sad, romantic songs for indie labels, often written in their bedrooms. "Prototweet came out of the punk scene of the 1970s."
**"Girly" Aesthetic and Influences** : The term "Tweey" is suggested to be a phonetic interpretation of a baby trying to say "sweet," reflecting the genre's inherent gentleness. This style draws heavily from 1960s pop, particularly girl groups. Its fans tend to be shy and introverted, shying away from standing out, often favoring plain attire like parkas or "anoraxes" (in the UK), seeking music that celebrates simplicity and naiveté over glamour and fame. "This Tuy music lifted elements from 60s pop, especially the girl groups of the era."
**Scotland as an Epicenter** : Scotland, notably Glasgow, became a key hub for this nascent post-punk sound that would evolve into Tweetpop. Bands such as The Pastels, formed in Glasgow in 1981, exemplified the early elements of Tweetpop, characterized by sweet yet languid melodies, an intimate and casual vibe, and an absence of pretense, establishing the region's significance. "For some reason, Scotland became an epicenter for this new post punk sound."
**Survival and Integration in the 1990s Alt-Rock Era** : Contrary to expectations that it would be overshadowed by the aggressive rise of grunge and alt-rock in the 1990s, Tweetpop endured. This was largely because the broader indie scene, which included Tweetpop, aligned with the initial anti-corporate, anti-capitalist, and philosophically independent stance of 90s alt-rock. The "indie sheen" of the era made indie pop and indie rock cool, opening doors to wider popularity for Tweetpop as a gentle alternative for fans disinclined toward grunge, industrial, punk funk, or ska punk. "Now, you would think that the Tweet sound would have been washed away by the rise of the aggressiveness and grunge and alt-rock in the 1990s."
**Emphasis on Female Participation and Inclusivity** : Tweetpop and related indie pop bands were characterized as "nice, loving, inclusive, [and] gentle," offering a stark contrast to the aggressive nature of full-on alt-rock. A significant factor in its appeal was the substantial female participation, which provided a counterbalance to the male dominance in the rock world. This resulted in a non-macho musical selection that resonated with fans seeking gender equality and "girl power," but in a more pop-oriented, melodic, and harmonious way, less strident than the Riot Grrrl movement. "One thing that helped was the female participation in Tweet bands."

# label - NME (New Musical Express)
## curiosities
**C86 Compilation (May 1986)** : As one of the UK's prominent weekly music tabloids, the NME offered its readers a landmark compilation cassette titled *C86* in May 1986. This cassette, whose name referenced both tape length conventions and its release year, became a pivotal moment—a "big bang"—for the British indie scene by showcasing guitar-based indie songs and playing a crucial role in the discovery and promotion of new music. "In May 1986, the NME, the new musical express, one of the UK's weekly music tabloids, hadn't offered for their readers."
