**Encore Tradition Origins (18th Century)** : The practice of encores dates back to at least the 18th century, a time before recorded music when live performance was the only way to hear favorite pieces. Audiences, or wealthy patrons, would shout "Encore" (French for "again") or "Unotera" (Italian for "one other") to demand a repeat of popular songs or parts of pieces, even during a performance. "This is a tradition that goes back to at least the 18th century."
**European Encore Bans** : Encores were not always appreciated; at one point, many European opera houses, including those in Italy and Germany, banned them, deeming them too disruptive. Emperor Joseph II of Austria, a fan of Mozart, was among the first to make such a ruling. "At one point, many European opera houses banned Encore's, saying that they were too disruptive."
**Encores in Dance Halls and Vaudeville** : By 1900, the idea of an encore was largely considered "uncouth," but the tradition persisted in dance halls and vaudeville. Due to the lack of recorded music, audiences would yell "Encore" for popular songs, compelling performers to play them multiple times. "When we get to dance halls and vaudeville, the same thing happened. You couldn't hear songs any other way than to see them live."
**Encore Revival in Rock and Roll** : The encore tradition was revived in the 1950s and 60s with the advent of rock and roll. Artists would leave the stage after their main set only to return for a few more songs, possibly as a spontaneous response to an excited audience or influenced by Broadway productions where actors returned for extra bows. "When Rock came along in the 50s and 60s, the tradition was revived. At the end of a set, artists would leave the stage only to return to play a few more minutes."
**Stage Diving: Performer vs. Crowd Participation** : Stage diving encompasses two main types: when a performer leaps into the crowd, and when an audience member climbs onto the stage and then dives back into the crowd. While performer stage diving has been attributed to Jim Morrison and Iggy Pop, crowd participation stage diving was first documented at a Rolling Stones show in the Netherlands on August 8, 1964. "Okay, there are two types of stage diving. The first is when the performer leaps off the stage and into the crowd. The second is when a member of the audience climbers up on stage and then dives back into the crowd."
**Dangers of Stage Diving** : Stage diving, particularly when involving crowd participation, has led to serious injuries and even fatalities. An incident at an Everclear concert in 1997, where a professional football player dove into the crowd and crushed a fan, resulted in significant injuries and a $1.2 million lawsuit. "People have been seriously injured and have even died as a result of either diving or having someone fall on top of them."
**Moshpit Evolution from Pogo and Slam Dancing** : Moshpits are a descendant of the Pogo, a dance where British punks jumped up and down, likely originating from the need to see over crowds at early stage-less punk shows. As music intensified, Pogoing became more aggressive, evolving into "slam dancing," which was intensified further by American hardcore bands like Black Flag and Fear in the late 1970s. "They are the descendants of groups of British punks doing a dance called the Pogo. Basically it involved jumping up and down in place."
**Moshpit Variations and Dangers** : Modern moshing has diversified into various forms, including "circle pits," "walls of death" (which are explicitly dangerous), "hardcore dancing," and "crowd killing." These practices have frequently resulted in injuries and deaths, leading many bands to actively oppose moshing during their shows. "Since then, moshing has taken on a variety of forms. Circle pits, walls of death, which is as dangerous as it sounds, hardcore dancing and something even called crowd killing."
**Pre-Show Music Practices** : The music played before and between acts, often called PA music or pre-show music, is chosen by various parties: the venue, the PA operator (to tweak sound), the gig sound designer (to create a vibe), or the artist (to expose the audience to music they enjoy). A common technique is to slowly increase the volume as showtime approaches to build energy. "And there's no hard and fast rule about what this music should consist of.
