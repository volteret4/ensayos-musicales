# artist - David Bowie

## albums
**25 Albums Recorded Before 1990** : 287 songs from David Bowie's 25 albums recorded before 1990 were used to create "Bowie Bonds." "287 songs from 25 albums recorded before 1990 became asset-backed securities."

## curiosities
**Creation of Bowie Bonds (1997)** : In 1997, David Bowie, in collaboration with investment banker David Pullman, created a financial instrument called "Bowie Bonds." This involved securitizing 287 songs from 25 albums recorded before 1990, effectively making them asset-backed securities. In exchange for forfeiting 10 years of royalty income, Bowie received an immediate lump sum payment of $55 million. He utilized this money to repurchase the rights to songs he had lost to his former manager, Tony DeFrees. "David Bowie and an investment banker named David Pullman created a financial instrument called Bowie Bonds. To use Wall Street speak, 287 songs from 25 albums recorded before 1990 became asset-backed securities. In exchange for forfeiting royalty income for a period of 10 years, Bowie received an immediate payment of $55 million. He used that money to buy out the rights to songs he lost to his former manager, Tony DeFrees."
**Bowie's Foresight on Internet Disruption** : At the time of the Bowie Bonds' creation, many considered his decision to forfeit royalties as "crazy." However, Bowie was studying the emerging internet and correctly predicted its disruptive impact on the music business, anticipating a decline in traditional record royalties, which materialized with the rise of Napster and file-sharing. "At the time, though, people thought that Bowie was crazy giving up his songs like this. But Bowie knew what was coming. He was studying this new thing called the Internet, and he knew that it was going to completely disrupt the music business, and he knew that old school record royalties were going to drop, which is exactly what happened, especially when Napster and all the other file-trading programs popped up."
**Bowie Bonds Hit Junk Status (2007)** : By 2007, ten years after their inception, record sales had dropped so significantly, and royalties had dried up to such an extent, that the Bowie Bonds lost their value, hitting "junk status." "Bottom line was that after 10 years, record sales had dropped so much, and royalties on these sales had dried up so much that Bowie bonds hit junk status."
**Songs Revert to Bowie (2007)** : When the Bowie Bonds hit junk status in 2007, the songs and their income reverted back to David Bowie, making his initial $55 million gamble a win. "2007, Bowie swoops in, and the songs revert back to him along with their income. So Bowie made a $55 million bet, and he won."

**Publishing as Music Monetization** : "Publishing" is the primary method to monetize music, a practice dating back to the late 19th century when professional songwriters sold music as printed sheet music. **Evolution of Publishing Industry** : Publishing became more complex with the advent of recorded music (cylinders, rotating discs in early 20th century), then movies, radio, television, various formats (vinyl, tape, CDs), digital downloads, and now streaming. Publishers represent songwriters, lyricists, and composers to ensure compensation for commercial use. "Publishing became much more complicated when we started recording music on cylinders and rotating discs at the beginning of the 20th century. **Copyright - Legal Concept and History** : Copyright is a legal concept from the 15th-century printing press era, granting the right to copy an intellectual work. Unauthorized copying is piracy, making copyright a foundational concept of Western law. "Copyright is a legal concept that goes back centuries to the days of the first printing press in the 15th century. If you wanted to copy something, you had to secure permission the right to copy from the author. If you didn't, that's called piracy. Copyright is one of the foundational concepts of Western law."
**Two Types of Music Copyrights** : There are two main types of music copyrights: the composition (the song itself, written as notation) and the sound recording (the audio representation, the master). The composition copyright usually stays with the artist, while the sound recording copyright usually resides with the record label. "If we look at music, there are two types of music copyrights. The first is the composition, which is the song itself... Then there's the sound recording. This is the audio representation of the song... The master sound recording is your finished product"
**Publishing Income as Primary Artist Revenue** : Publishing income, generated whenever a song is played, performed, sold, or streamed, is typically an artist's greatest source of income from their music. "publishing is usually the artist's greatest source of income when it comes to their music." "And this is the publishing income that's the main focus of this new breed of companies."
