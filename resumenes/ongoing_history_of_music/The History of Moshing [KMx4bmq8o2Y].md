# curiosity
**Alan Cross - The Ongoing History of New Music Podcast** : Hosted by Alan Cross, this entire program is dedicated to the history of the mosh pit, including stage diving and crowd surfing.
**Pogo Dance Origin (1970s UK)** : The direct ancestor of moshing, which became popular during the original punk rock days in the UK in the 1970s. It involved standing in place and jumping up and down, often as a way to see bands when stages were on the floor level in venues.
**Slam Dancing Emergence (1977, Southern California)** : An aggressive form of dancing that emerged in the hardcore punk scene, involving deliberately crashing or slamming into other dancers. It was seen as a response to hippie culture and disco, often driven by a desire for aggression and "testosterone."
**Transition from Slam Dancing to Moshing (Early 1980s)** : Initially, punk fanzines in the early 1980s referred to the frenzied activity as a "mash" (as in mashed potatoes). The pronunciation shifted to "mosh" due in part to HR, the singer of Bad Brains, whose Jamaican accent made "mash" sound like "mush" when he urged audiences to "mash it up."
**"The Decline of Western Civilization" (1980 Film)** : A documentary by filmmaker Penelope Spheeris that captured the peculiar punk scenes in DC and Southern California, showcasing moshing to bands like the Circle Jerks and Black Flag. This film helped spread moshing to other parts of the world.
**Walls of Death** : A super aggressive manifestation within the mosh pit where the crowd breaks into two sides and then charges after each other.
**Hardcore Dancing** : A distinct and often violent form of dancing that emerged out of New York City, featuring specific moves such as "Windmilling" and "Axe Handling."
**Stage Diving History** : The act of audience members getting onto the stage and immediately diving back on top of the audience. Documented instances date back to The Rolling Stones in Germany (1964), Iggy Pop with The Stooges (1969), and Jim Morrison with The Doors (around 1969). Dee Snider of Twisted Sister also claims to have invented it in the 1970s.
**Moshing Goes Mainstream (Late 1980s - Early 1990s)** : Moshing became a significant part of the Grunge era, spreading from the Pacific Northwest, which had strong connections to the California punk scene. By about 1992, it was ubiquitous at major events like the Lollapalooza tour and became an expected "tribal behavior" at Alt Rock gigs.
**Backlash Against Moshing (Mid-1990s onwards)** : Due to escalating injuries, sexual assaults, and deaths in mosh pits, a major backlash began. This led to bands, promoters, and venues implementing anti-moshing policies, resulting in lawsuits, higher liability insurance premiums, increased security, and ultimately higher ticket prices.
**Venue Safety Measures** : Signs began to appear at venues recommending against crowd-surfing and moshing. Some concert tickets featured fine print stating that individuals caught crowd-surfing or stage-diving would be ejected from the gig.
**Woodstock '99 Violence** : The mosh pits at the 30th anniversary Woodstock show in 1999 became tremendously violent, leading to numerous injuries (including broken bones and concussions) and horrific sexual assaults and charges of rape.
**Warped Tour Anti-Moshing Policy (2014)** : The 2014 Warped Tour prominently displayed "No Moshing" signs which read, "You get hurt, we get sued, no more Warped Tour."
**Classical Music Crowd Surfing Incident (2014)** : In 2014, an audience member was ejected for crowd surfing during a classical music concert featuring Handel's Messiah, demonstrating the pervasive nature of concert-going behaviors.
**Coronavirus Impact on Moshing (2020s)** : The COVID-19 pandemic, with the idea of "slamming up against sweaty strangers and all their bodily fluids and spit," is suggested as a potential factor in the decline or end of moshing, at least until widespread vaccination and herd immunity are achieved.
