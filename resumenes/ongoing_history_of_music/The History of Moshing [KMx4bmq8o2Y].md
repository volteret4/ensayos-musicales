# genre - Punk Rock

**Origin of Pogo (1970s UK)** : The Pogo dance became prominent during the original punk rock era in the UK in the 1970s. In many venues lacking stages, bands played on the floor at audience level. To see the band, attendees would jump up, leading to the Pogo's development. "The Pogo became a thing during the original punk rock days in the UK back in the 1970s."

**Fracturing into Hardcore (Late 1970s)** : By the end of the 1970s, the original punk rock sound began to fracture and segment, with one of the most aggressive forms evolving into hardcore. One of those segments turned into hardcore."

# genre - Hardcore

## curiosities
**Aggressive Dance Forms (Late 1970s)** : Hardcore emerged from punk rock in the late 1970s as one of the most aggressive forms of music. This genre encouraged dancing with "enthusiasm," which involved deliberately crashing or slamming into other dancers, giving rise to "slam dancing." "This was among the most aggressive forms of music ever invented. The idea was to dance with, well, let's call it enthusiasm, deliberately crashing or slamming into other dancers, hence the term slam dancing."

**Influence on Thrash Metal** : Hardcore punk began to influence metal bands, pushing them to play faster and harder. This fusion ultimately led to the creation and development of the thrash metal genre. "Hardcore punk began to influence metal. Metal bands started playing faster and harder, resulting in thrash metal."

It is claimed that he would jump up and down on the spot while attempting to play the instrument, which may have contributed to the Pogo's development. "There's another story that says when Sid played the saxophone, yes, it's claimed that he played the saxophone, that he jumped up and down on the spot."

# curiosity

**The Pogo Dance** : The Pogo dance, a precursor to moshing, involves standing in place and jumping up and down. It emerged during the original punk rock era in the UK in the 1970s, partly as a necessity in venues without stages where jumping was the only way to see the band. Sid Vicious is often credited with its invention, either to mock non-punks or due to movement restrictions from his attire. "The most direct ancestor of mashing is the Pogo."

**Slam Dancing** : Slam dancing originated during the hardcore punk movement as an aggressive form of dancing, where participants deliberately crashed into or "slammed" into each other. It emerged as a response to the disco era and a desire for aggressive expression, particularly for those looking to distance themselves from "hippie" culture. The most frequent location for slam dancing was directly in front of the stage. "deliberately crashing or slamming into other dancers, hence the term slam dancing."

**David Wood's Role in Slamming (1977)** : David Wood, a roadie for the LA punk band The Weirdos, is a contender for originating slam dancing. In 1977, he began intentionally slamming into people at shows at the Roxy in Los Angeles, around the time The Weirdos released their single "Destroy All Music." "We might want a credit adude named David Wood. He was a roadie for an LA punk band called The Weirdos."

**Reasons for Slam Dancing's Emergence** : Several factors contributed to the rise of slam dancing: it was a rejection of "hippie" culture, it served as a defiant response to the prevalent disco and "Saturday Night Fever" culture, and it offered an outlet for aggressive expression, particularly among young men. Bands sometimes actively provoked audiences to increase the intensity. "But slam dancing is something hippies would not do."

**Circle Pits and Wall of Death** : As slam dancing evolved, larger gatherings at gigs saw the formation of "circles of slam dancers" at various points in the crowd. These intense formations became known as circle pits, and sometimes escalated into the "wall of death," where two sides of the pit would charge at each other. "These became known as circle pits and sometimes the wall of death."

**Shift from "Mash" to "Mosh" (Early 1980s)** : In the early 1980s, punk fanzines used the term "mash" (as in "mashed potatoes") to describe the frenzied activity in the pit. However, the pronunciation shifted to "Mosh," largely influenced by HR, the singer of Bad Brains, whose Jamaican accent made "mash" sound like "mosh" when he urged audiences to "mash it up." "But somehow the pronunciation shifted to Mosh."

**The Decline of Western Civilization (Film)** : This film, made by Penelope Spheeris, documented the peculiar punk scenes in D.C. and Southern California. It captured footage of predominantly male audiences moshing to bands like the Circle Jerks and Black Flag, and its global distribution played a significant role in spreading moshing behavior to other parts of the world. "A filmmaker named Penelope Sphereis decided she needed to document this peculiar form of punk scene in DC and Southern California."

**Hardcore Dancing (New York City)** : Hardcore dancing, which originated in New York City, introduced specific moves like "wind milling" and "axe handling." These movements likely involved aggressive arm and body motions, contributing to the physical intensity of the mosh pit. "And one of the moves was called wind milling and another was axe handling."

**Stage Diving History** : Stage diving, where audience members jump from the stage into the crowd, has a long and disputed history. Iggy Pop with The Stooges and Jim Morrison of The Doors were doing it in 1969. There's also documented evidence from a Rolling Stones show in Germany in 1964. Dee Snider of Twisted Sister claims he invented it in the 70s out of aggression. "Iggy Pop was doing this with the Stooges as far back as 1969."

**Increased Injuries and Liability Issues (1980s-1990s)** : As moshing became standard, injuries intensified, including groping and sexual assault, and instances of attendees bringing weapons like brass knuckles into the pit. This led to venues, bands, and promoters facing lawsuits, liability issues, and rising insurance premiums, compelling them to discourage moshing. "Venues did what they could to discourage mashing because people were getting hurts or worse. There were lawsuits and issues of liability."

**Backlash Against Moshing (21st Century)** : The 21st century saw a major backlash against moshing due to persistent injuries, lawsuits, and escalating insurance premiums for artists, promoters, and venues. This resulted in increased security staff, higher ticket prices, and explicit warnings or bans on behaviors like crowd-surfing and stage-diving at concerts. "The 21st century has seen a major backlash against Moshe."

**Coronavirus Impact on Moshing** : The coronavirus pandemic introduced a new, invisible deterrent to moshing. The idea of close physical contact with sweaty strangers and exposure to bodily fluids made the activity unappealing, potentially marking a temporary or permanent end to moshing until widespread immunity is achieved. "The idea of slamming up against sweaty strangers and all their bodily fluids and spit isn't the kind of thing that sounds very good anymore."
