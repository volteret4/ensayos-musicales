## albums
**Saskadelvia (2021)** : This album features unreleased material from the band's "glory days of the early 90s," primarily tracks recorded for, but not included on, the 1991 *Road Apples* album. The discovery of these songs, which even the band hadn't heard for decades, was accidental, leading to a "remarkable musical archaeology." The album evokes a sense of Gordon Downie being "in the room" again, as some between-song chatter has been preserved. The album title "Saskadelvia" was an inside joke that the US record company rejected as "too Canadian" for *Road Apples*. The physical packaging includes a front photo from the same session and photographer as *Road Apples*, and a back photo by another photographer involved in the *Road Apples* project, with guitarist Rob Baker credited as "Bobby Baker," reflecting his name in 1991. "enjoyable to hear songs that were so dear to us 30 years ago and 30 years go by and you haven't heard them."
**Road Apples (1991)** : This album was the Tragically Hip's first to reach number one in Canada. The band went to Kingsway Studios in New Orleans in September 1990 armed with about a dozen songs for the record, but continued writing and jamming, ending up with far more material than they could use. The producer, Don Smith, focused on microphone placement and non-stop recording, with an ethos to "just keep playing and playing and playing in the studio and never stop recording." Though a double album was discussed, the final decision was to pare down the selection to an even dozen tracks to avoid the "sophomore curse" following *Up To Here*. The original title concept was "Saskadelvia," but it was deemed "too Canadian" by the US record company, leading to the adoption of "Road Apples," which referred to both a game of road hockey with frozen horse manure and a way to "beat the critics to it" by calling their own album "horse shit." Actual road apples encased in Lucite were sent out for promotion. "We called road hockey road apples because in my parents' generation, they would go out and play road hockey with a piece of frozen horse. And those are road apples."
**Up To Here** : This album preceded *Road Apples*. The band's producer, Don Smith, wanted to ensure the follow-up album, *Road Apples*, was "as good or better" than *Up To Here*.
**Fully Completely** : For its 25th anniversary, liner notes were written, which involved playing in a portion of the band's archives. The master tapes for this album were stored at Richard Branson's Mastering House in California.

## songs
**Little Bones (1991)** : This was the first single released from the *Road Apples* album. It was written in New Orleans during the pre-production rehearsals for *Road Apples* and ultimately "supplanted" the song "Not Necessary" as the album's lead-off rocker. "it kind of supplanted necessary is like you're kind of lead off rocker."
**Outch (2021)** : This song is the first track on the *Saskadelvia* album and is described as "pretty much vintage hip." It was not included on the *Road Apples* album, not due to a lack of quality, but because the band wanted to create a diverse record and avoid being "pigeonholed." It was compared to "Twist My Arm" and "Cordelia," both of which made the album, suggesting it was cut because of its similar sound. "it's not a quality issue. We wanted to make a diverse record and we didn't want to get pigeonholed as one thing."
**Not Necessary (2021)** : A lost song from the *Road Apples* sessions unearthed for *Saskadelvia*, this track was played "tons live" and was considered a "sure thing" for the *Road Apples* album, intended as a lead-off rocker. However, it was supplanted by "Little Bones," which was written during the New Orleans pre-production. The song had its live debut as an encore at The Spoke, a student pub at Western University in London, where Gordon Sinclair taught the band the chords moments before going on stage. "it kind of supplanted necessary is like you're kind of lead off rocker."
**Montreal (2021)** : This song, featured on *Saskadelvia*, is not from the *Road Apples* sessions in New Orleans, but was written around 1991. Its lyrics concern the Polytechnique Massacre of 1989. The version on *Saskadelvia* is a live recording from 2000 at the Bell Center in Montreal, performed on an anniversary of the massacre. Guitarist Rob Baker remembered the chords, but Gordon Downie had forgotten the lyrics and needed them printed from a phone provided by a fan just before the encore performance. "I think I had suggested that we do it as an encore, and Gordon was like, yeah, I don't know the words."
**Crack My Spine Like A Whip (2021)** : This song, finally given an official release on *Saskadelvia*, had circulated as a bootleg for decades and was a popular "non-release track" among fans. The band frequently used it as an opening, closing, or encore song in their live sets in 1991 due to its "immediate energy" and "punky" feel. It didn't make the *Road Apples* record possibly because the band felt they had "a better collection of songs" and because producer Don Smith's method of playing songs "over and over and over until we get a killer version," with live guitar solos and vocals, might have fatigued such a high-energy track after multiple takes. "I think we just had a better collection of songs when it came down to count them up."
**Just As Well (2021)** : Track five on *Saskadelvia*, this song also circulated as a bootleg for decades and was played live extensively by the band. It has a "country twang" and embodies an "amalgam of blues and country and whatever that became rock and roll." Numerous versions were recorded, and the track "narrowly missed" being included on *Road Apples* when the band was deciding between A and B-side selections. Engineer Bruce Barris can be heard at the beginning of this particular version. "It doesn't really matter so much as it swells. And I guess that's just as well."
**Reform Baptist Blues (2021)** : The sixth and final track on *Saskadelvia*, this is the oldest song on the album, dating back to at least the late 80s. It was among the very first songs the band wrote, originating from a period when Gord Sinclair was the band's "sole songwriter" during their first year and a half as they tried to blend original material into cover band sets. Later, the band evolved beyond individual songwriting. Lyrically, it is seen by band members as "proof positive why we put Gord and charge the lyrics in the band." "Gord was basically our soul songwriter."
**Twist My Arm (1991)** : A song from the *Road Apples* album, it was considered alongside "Outch" for the record. "it came down to twist my arm or out."
**Cordelia (1991)** : A song from the *Road Apples* album, it was mentioned in comparison to "Outch."
**Three Pistols** : A version of this song is known to exist on tape, but some tapes containing other versions are currently missing from the archives.

## curiosities
**Gordon Downie's Passing** : Gordon Downie, the lead singer, passed away on October 17th, 2017, marking one of the "saddest days in the history of Canadian music." His death followed a final tragically hip concert in Kingston on August 20th, 2016, which millions of Canadians watched knowing it would be the band's last live performance together due to his illness. The sentiment was widely captured by a tweet stating, "Canada closed, death in the family." "Canada closed, death in the family."
**Unreleased Material Discovery** : A trove of unreleased material was found by accident, surprising even the band members who hadn't heard these songs for decades. This "remarkable musical archaeology" led to the release of the *Saskadelvia* album. The process took almost two years, with Johnny Fay making numerous trips to Universal Music's storage facilities. Manager Jake Gold and Johnny Fay were described as "relentless" in their pursuit of the tapes. "it's been a really crazy process, almost two years in the making trunks, trying to track this record down."
**Road Apples Recording Sessions** : The *Road Apples* album was recorded at Kingsway Studios in New Orleans in September 1990. The band arrived with around a dozen songs but continued writing, ending up with "way more songs than they could use." Producer Don Smith's style involved extensive pre-production, meticulous microphone placement, and non-stop recording to "catch a vibe." This creative period, marked by the band ditching individual songwriting for collective effort, resulted in a surplus of tracks, some of which were left unreleased to pare the album down to a concise dozen songs and ensure diversity. "the start of a really creative period for us where we had kind of ditched the idea of individually writing and really starting to get our chops together, writing together, which is opening up huge."
**Master Tape Search and Recovery** : The search for the *Road Apples* session tapes was an "archeological dig" involving multiple trips by Johnny Fay to Universal Music's storage. Of the estimated 65 reels of two-inch tape recorded, only about 20 were found initially, some labeled, while 25 reels are still missing. Subsequent batches were identified by matching the handwriting of engineer Bruce Barris on the spine of unlabeled boxes. The tapes were not archived systematically, being "spread all over the place" and sometimes mixed with recordings by other artists. This haphazard storage contributed to the two-year search. "it's been a really crazy process, almost two years in the making trunks, trying to track this record down."
**Magnetic Tape Degradation and Baking Process** : Magnetic tapes degrade over time, a process often indicated by an "acidic" or "vinegary" smell. To recover audio, these tapes must undergo a "baking" process, typically in a small oven (likened to an easy-bake oven) at a low temperature, such as 325 degrees for 30 minutes. This process aims to remove humidity and re-adhere the magnetic particles to the tape, allowing for a single transfer. The transfer must occur within a "12 hour period" after the tapes have cooled, as the effect is temporary. After their discovery, the *Saskadelvia* tapes were baked and digitized at Revolution studio in Toronto, with the band acknowledging the poor preservation of the originals. "if you lift the lid on it and it smells kind of acidic like vinaigrette, then you really have got like one go out and you've got to bake it."
**Road Apples Album Title Origin** : The original title proposed for the 1991 album was "Saskadelvia," a band inside joke derived from frequently touring and not knowing what town they were in. However, the US record company rejected it as "too Canadian." The band then chose "Road Apples," a term with multiple Canadian meanings: it refers to horse manure (hence the joke about calling their own album "horse shit" to preempt critics) and is also slang for road hockey, where a piece of frozen horse manure might be used as a puck in their parents' generation. For promotion, actual road apples were encased in Lucite and sent to various people. "Someone made the joke. Oh, I think we're in Saskadelvia. And we thought it was a funny title. And the record company, US record company, said, yeah, too Canadian. We can't do that."
**CD vs. Vinyl Era Recording Mentality** : When recording *Road Apples*, their first CD, the band's approach was still influenced by vinyl. They aimed for "five songs on A, five songs on B" to create their best ten tracks, rather than filling the CD's 60-70 minute capacity. This contrasts with the later trend of artists extending album lengths to maximize CD space. "This was our first foray into, this is the first CD we ever made for road apples... We always thought in five songs on A, five songs on B. And you pick your best ten."
**Future Archive Releases** : Following the successful release of *Saskadelvia*, the band confirmed that "the quest will continue" for more unreleased material. They recall an afternoon during the *Road Apples* sessions when producer Don Smith encouraged them to "empty the cupboard" and record everything they had. While some songs were extensively worked on, others were simply recorded and moved past. There are still missing tapes, including versions of songs like "Three Pistols," but the band is now enthusiastic about exploring their archives further. "The quest will continue. I think everyone's into it. Everyone's enthusiastic and back and we're out of our fog. And so it's like, okay, well, let's see what we can find."
**Juno Awards 50th Anniversary Performance** : The surviving members of The Tragically Hip reunited to perform at the 50th anniversary of the Juno Awards on June 6th, 2021, with Feist filling in on vocals. There was initial "resistance amongst all of us to delve back in and open that door," but the decision was made due to the significance of the 50th anniversary, the honor being received, and the desire to support musicians and live venues during a period of hardship. Feist was suggested as a singer, and the band felt "everyone had that same response," believing Gordon Downie would approve. The performance was compared to Nirvana's Rock and Roll Hall of Fame induction performance with female vocalists like Lorde, St. Vincent, Kim Gordon, and Joan Jett. "if there was a time when we were going to entertain the idea that this is probably it."

**Sophomore Curse** : This is a common phenomenon in the music industry where a band's second album struggles to live up to the success or quality of their debut. The Tragically Hip's producer, Don Smith, was conscious of this challenge when working on *Road Apples*, their follow-up to *Up To Here*, wanting to ensure it was "as good or better." "everyone's aware of the sophomore curse. And we wanted to make sure the follow-up top to here was as good or better."
**Magnetic Tape Storage in "Mindshafts"** : For long-term preservation, master tapes, particularly two-inch tapes that "take up a lot of real estate," are sometimes stored in specialized facilities known as "Mindshafts." These are old mine shafts that have been encased in concrete, climate-controlled, and repurposed for archival storage. Examples include vaults used by Johnny Carson for his master tapes and an even larger one by Frank Zappa. "what they do is they take old Mindshafts and encase them in concrete and then climate control them and then they put those tapes down there."
**Producer Don Smith's Recording Philosophy** : Don Smith, who produced The Tragically Hip's *Up To Here* and *Road Apples*, had a distinct recording approach focused on constant capture and live performance. His "ethos was just to keep playing and playing and playing in the studio and never stop recording," aiming to "roll and do different versions and just try to catch a vibe." He prioritized "microphone placement" and getting musicians set up correctly, desiring "guitar solos live off the floor" and "vocals live off the floor." Smith also preferred using "fresh tape," a practice he observed with artists like Keith Richards, Tom Petty, and the Traveling Wilburys. "the ethos was just to keep playing and playing and playing in the studio and never stop recording."
**Unlabeled Master Tapes** : A significant challenge in retrieving The Tragically Hip's unreleased material was that many of the two-inch master tapes were "not labeled" as "the tragically hip or anything like that." They were often "spread all over the place" in storage, sometimes in miscellaneous boxes alongside recordings by other artists. This lack of proper archiving made the discovery process akin to an "archeological dig." "They were two-inch tapes. They were not labeled, and you sort of figured out what they were from the handwriting you saw in the boxes."
