# artist - Matthew Good

## member of
Matthew Good Band

## albums
**Avalanche (2003) - First Solo Album** : Released in March 2003, this album was written a month after the Matthew Good Band's breakup. It marked a new era where Matthew Good had complete creative control, allowing him to arrange instruments and command the overall sonic context without external conflicts. This freedom led to a "balanced" album where "everything wasn't competing against one another." "Finally got to do what I wanted to do."
**White Light Rock and Roll Review (2004) - Experimental Record** : This album was ready by mid-June 2004, just 15 months after `Avalanche`. Matthew described it as an "experiment," influenced by his extensive listening to Buddy Holly and repeatedly watching *Hail! Hail! Rock 'n' Roll*. The recording process was intentionally primitive, recorded "live off the floor" with all musicians playing simultaneously in one room, a technique Matthew admired for its organic quality. "That record for me was more of an experiment."
**In a Coma (Implied Post-2004) - Greatest Hits Collection** : A comprehensive collection comprising two CDs and a DVD. The DVD is notable for featuring Matthew Good's audio commentary, where he describes the videos as they play. While Matthew would have preferred a greatest hits album to feature more album cuts for fans, he acknowledged that labels typically prioritize singles for such releases, often timing them for Christmas sales as "flash cash." "That's actually a very solid collection. Two CDs and a DVD loaded with stuff."
**Hospital Music (2007) - Cathartic Album** : Released in 2007, this album featured the song `Born Losers`. Its creation was described by Matthew as a "very cathartic experience," following a period of intense personal crisis, including his divorce, a diagnosis of bipolarity, and a period of hospitalization. "obviously the creation of that record as being a very cathartic experience."
**Live at Massey Hall (2008) - Live Album** : A live record released in 2008, which includes a recording of the song `Born Losers`. "That recording was from a 2008 live record entitled Live at Massie Hall."
**Vancouver (2009) - City Observation Album** : Released in 2009, this album's title is not a tribute but rather an artistic reflection on the profound changes Matthew Good observed in Vancouver over 16 years of living downtown. He noted the dramatic diminishment of the city's arts community, particularly its grassroots live venues and overall musical "edge." "the reason that I did it was that I lived downtown for a better part of 16 years. And I saw one of what I think... one of the most dramatic changes that I've ever seen a city undergo."

## curiosities
**Juno Award Disdain** : Matthew Good won two Juno Awards, including one for Best Video, but consciously chose not to attend the ceremonies. He views award shows as "counterproductive," comparing them to judging the relative greatness of painters like Chagall, Rothko, and Kandinsky, and expressed discomfort with how they are marketed as the "Canadian experience." "I didn't go the year I won. I didn't go the year I won one for best video. And it's okay. My mom's got them."
**Sarcoidosis Diagnosis** : Matthew Good suffered from sarcoidosis, a disease that progressively affects different parts of the body, and for him, specifically his lungs. It causes lung tissue to turn into scar tissue over time, making it difficult to breathe, a condition also experienced by Bernie Mack. Matthew uses an inhaler but has avoided steroid treatments like prednisone due to their severe side effects. "Sarcoid osus. It's basically a disease that can affect different parts of your body. For me, it affects my lungs."
**Intense Anxiety and Illness During Recording** : During the recording of the `Audio of Being` album, Matthew Good experienced severe anxiety and was physically ill, throwing up daily for eight months. This period was marked by his sarcoidosis and the worsening internal dysfunction of the Matthew Good Band, making the entire recording process a "nightmare." "Well, I threw up that entire year, every day."
**Personal Crisis and Divorce** : Matthew Good experienced a "dark" period in his personal life following a sudden and traumatic divorce. This event was compounded by a sense of "betrayal," including the discovery that his ex-wife had hidden a suitcase of cocaine in their home, an action linked to a friend's legal troubles. "Well, I got divorced. And that was a sudden thing. Yeah, it was a very, very sudden thing. And there was a great deal of betrayal involved in it."
**Bipolarity Diagnosis and Dysphoric Mania** : While on antidepressants (a drug referred to as "Effexor" or "fixer" in the transcript) for anxiety, an increased dosage inadvertently amplified an undiagnosed bipolarity. He identified his condition as Type 1 bipolarity, eventually leading to dysphoric mania—a state of intense manic energy combined with a depressive state, which he described as "worse than a nuclear weapon" due to its overwhelming suffering and high associated suicide rate. "Now bipolarity is a genealogical disorder, which means you have it from birth."
**Hospitalization and Enlightenment** : During a manic episode while in Bristol, England, Matthew Good returned home and, in an accidental overdose of sedatives (referred to as "Ativan" or "out of Ann" in the transcript) combined with alcohol, collapsed. He woke up in the hospital, where he willfully committed himself for about a week. This period, facilitated by cooperative hospital staff and a familiar psychiatrist, led to a "mass enlightenment" about his condition, marking it as a "cathartic experience." "I woke up in the hospital and I had to willfully commit myself."
**Self-Reflection on Band Breakup** : Matthew Good expressed ongoing guilt and self-doubt regarding the Matthew Good Band's dissolution, questioning whether his undiagnosed illness, his perceived "difficult" personality, or media portrayals of him contributed to the split. He acknowledged these as possibilities and felt it would be ignorant to deny his potential role. "I think I probably look back more and think what the hell was I thinking."
**Comparison to Jeff's "I'll Break Your Heart"** : Matthew Good referenced the song "I'll Break Your Heart" by "Jeff" (presumably Jeff Buckley), where the artist throws up in a stall, to illustrate his own intense physical illness and vomiting before vocal takes during the recording of the `Audio of Being` album. "it's kind of like, you know, I'll break your heart. You know, where Jeff just goes into the stall and just throws up."

## albums
**Underdogs (Implied Pre-1999) - Predecessor Album** : This album is mentioned as the creative starting point that `Beautiful Midnight` built upon, suggesting it was an earlier successful release. "beautiful midnight certainly picks up where underdogs take off"
**Beautiful Midnight (1999) - Highly Successful Record** : Released on September 14, 1999, this album contained major hits like `Load Me Up`, `Hello Time Bomb`, and `Strange Days`. Despite its high success, its potential for even greater achievement was hampered by significant internal band dysfunction and Matthew Good's principled refusal to engage in what he perceived as "games" within the American music industry, which affected its U.S. release. "Beautiful midnight came out on September 14, 1999, and it was loaded with it."
**Audio of Being (Implied ~2001) - Challenging Recording Period** : The album following `Beautiful Midnight`, its creation was fraught with difficulty due to intensifying internal band politics, Matthew Good's severe sarcoidosis and anxiety, and a contentious recording process. Matthew worked 14 hours a day writing in Whistler, but rehearsals and recording were plagued by band members seeking publishing credits, leading to Matthew being physically ill during vocal takes. "we're into the audio of being, which was awkward. Well, I threw up that entire year, every day."

## curiosities
**Top Billing Misperception** : The band was publicly perceived as primarily "Matthew Good," giving him top billing despite his personal preference against it. This public misperception was a factor in the band's identity and internal dynamics. "The Matthew Good Band was a group that gave Matt top billing, something that he never wanted. This all came as the result of a public misperception."
**Internal Dysfunction and Political Dynamics** : The Matthew Good Band suffered from significant internal dysfunction, described as "very political and weird," particularly during the creation of `Audio of Being`. This included members vying for publishing rights by injecting ideas and a general lack of communication during tours, ultimately contributing to the band's dissolution. "It might have been even more successful had the Matthew Good band been functioning properly. There was a lot of internal dysfunction."
**Dave's Disregard for Band Work** : During the writing phase for `Audio of Being` in Whistler, Dave (likely Dave Genn) arrived late because he was busy working with "Hollywood" (presumably Holly McNarland) and in "Ireland." Matthew Good expressed frustration, believing Dave should have prioritized band commitments given their success (a near Triple Platinum album). "Dave came up for a little bit. But unfortunately, he was a little late because he was busy with Hollywood and Ireland working with Hollywood."
**Publishing Rights Conflicts** : During rehearsals for `Audio of Being`, band members deliberately tried to inject their ideas into songs, not purely for creative reasons, but "because they know that if they do, then they can make a case for publishing." This self-serving behavior significantly "hampered" the entire creative and recording process. "People are trying to inject ideas because they know that if they do, then they can make a case for publishing."
**Dysfunctional Tour and Matthew's 30th Birthday** : The tour supporting `Audio of Being` was marked by extreme dysfunction, with band members refusing to speak to one another. Matthew's 30th birthday occurred on this tour in "Gogory," where he avoided his bandmates by leaving a celebratory cake in his hotel room and going to the Corral Center. He also sustained a back injury in a car accident during the tour. "The whole tour was ridiculous."
**Breakup in 2001** : The Matthew Good Band dissolved around 2001, an outcome of pervasive internal dysfunction, Matthew's illness, and an unwillingness to tour the `Audio of Being` record. The band members had reached a point where communication broke down, and individuals asserted their indispensability, leading Matthew, who usually tried to "placate" others, to finally end the group. "And that was the end of the Matthew Good Band."

# curiosity

**American Music Industry "Games"** : Matthew Good maintained an "ethical and moral" stance against what he perceived as "games" required by the American music industry. His unwillingness to cooperate with these industry expectations reportedly hindered the commercial success of the Matthew Good Band's `Beautiful Midnight` album in the United States. "Matt wasn't interested in playing the games he felt he had to with the American music industry."
**Bernie Mack's Sarcoidosis** : Comedian Bernie Mack also suffered from sarcoidosis, the same lung disease as Matthew Good. While pneumonia was officially cited as his cause of death, it is suggested that complications from sarcoidosis likely played a significant role. "Bernie Mack had it. He's dead. And of course, the official thing was he'd died from pneumonia, but he definitely died probably from complications of sarcoid from the pneumonia."
**Hail! Hail! Rock 'n' Roll (Film)** : Matthew Good frequently watched *Hail! Hail! Rock 'n' Roll*, drawing inspiration from the film for his 2004 experimental album `White Light Rock and Roll Review`. The film influenced his decision to record the album in a primitive, "live off the floor" style. "I was watching Hail Hell Rock and Roll over and over again and stuff"
**Diminishment of Vancouver's Arts Community** : Matthew Good observed a "dramatic change" in Vancouver over 16 years, specifically the diminishment of its grassroots arts community. This decline is exemplified by the loss of staple live venues like The Town Pump, The Cruel Elephant, The Smiling Buddha, and Richard's on Richards, and the shift in venues like The Commodore Ballroom to cater primarily to touring acts. "The diminishment of its arts community."
**Vancouver's Lost Musical "Edge"** : Over approximately 15 years, Matthew Good believes Vancouver has lost its distinctive musical "edge," which previously fostered influential hardcore music in the 70s and 80s. This decline is linked to the loss of grassroots venues and a shift toward a singular sound dominating emerging bands, impacting musical ambition. "Over the last, I would say 15 years, the edge of Vancouver has been lost."
**Vancouver's Influence on Seattle Music Scene** : Vancouver bands significantly influenced the Seattle music scene in the early 1990s. When the Seattle scene gained widespread recognition, some musicians in Vancouver felt a sense of bitterness, believing their contributions and the city's "edge" were overlooked. "how many bands from Vancouver influenced specifically what happened in Seattle, you know, in the early 90s?"
