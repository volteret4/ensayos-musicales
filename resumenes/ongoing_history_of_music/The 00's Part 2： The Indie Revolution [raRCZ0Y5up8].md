# curiosity

**Pop Music Disruptions History** : Every ten years or so, significant events disrupt the pop music landscape. This pattern traces back to the 1950s with the birth of rock (initially dismissed as a fad), continued with The Beatles and the rise of the album in the 1960s, the emergence of Punk in the 1970s, the ascent of Hip Hop and Rap alongside music video channels in the 1980s, and the Alt Rock explosion of the 1990s. "every ten years or so, something happens to disrupt everything."
**Music as a Reflection of Society** : Music consistently serves as a reflection of broader social conditions and attitudes. This principle was exemplified after the 9/11 attacks, when the prevailing mood shifted, rendering previously dominant happy pop music inappropriate. "Music always reflects what's happening in the world at large."
**Generational Shifts and Music Preference** : Approximately every decade, demographic changes impact musical tastes. Generation X, which had fueled the alt rock of the 1990s, aged into adulthood. They were succeeded by Generation Y (Millennials), a larger cohort born between the early 1980s and late 1990s, who initially favored pop but later sought more meaningful, harder, raw, and authentic music, especially after the shock of 9/11. "This shift went hand in hand with one of those demographic time bombs that we see every 10 years or so."
**Napster and Peer-to-Peer File Sharing** : In its early days, Napster and peer-to-peer file sharing provided a novel way for people to access a vast, previously invisible universe of music. It enabled free sampling of new music and the discovery of off-the-radar indie bands, facilitating connections between users with similar tastes. This method was crucial for music discovery before the advent of widespread streaming, YouTube, or a mature iTunes. "Peer-to-peer file sharing, League of Bar-N-O-D, was a way for everyone to get access to a universe of music that had largely been out of sight, totally invisible."
**Breakdown of Traditional Cultural Gatekeepers** : In earlier eras, record labels, radio stations, and record stores acted as powerful cultural filters, influencing what music was considered good or widely available. The internet, particularly through file sharing, liberated music listeners from these traditional gatekeepers, allowing thousands of indie bands to be discovered and strengthening the position of indie artists and labels. "The internet had delivered them from the traditional cultural gatekeepers and it made it possible for thousands upon thousands upon thousands of indie bands to be discovered."
**Major Labels' Financial Crisis and Reputation Decline** : The rise of file sharing and the decline in CD sales led to a financial crisis for major record labels, causing their market share to fall and the industry to shrink. This decline was perceived by many fans as "karma" for years of overpricing CDs, offering one-sided record deals that disadvantaged artists, and exhibiting an overall contempt for both artists and fans. "While the majors were being battered by file sharing and falling market share, the Indies were just bobbin and weave and do whatever they had to do to survive."
**Indie Labels as "Saviors of Rock and Roll"** : Amidst the major labels' struggles, a new conventional wisdom emerged: "Major labels bad, indie labels good." Indie labels were seen as non-corporate entities run by individuals who genuinely cared about music, leading many fans to view them as "the saviors of rock and roll," despite this being a somewhat simplistic attitude. "The new conventional wisdom was that indie labels were doing God's work and they were the saviors of rock and roll."
**Millennials Fueling Piracy and Indie Discovery** : Millennials, being the most internet-savvy generation, were adept at finding music online. Many were too young to have credit cards or sufficient funds to purchase many CDs, which fueled the piracy boom. Through this, they uncovered and shared a vast amount of music not released by mainstream or major labels, contributing significantly to the indie revolution. "At the same time, many were too young to have credit cards or enough money to buy as many CD's as they wanted. That fueled the piracy boom, and in the process, they uncovered and started sharing a lot of music that wasn't in the mainstream or for major labels."
**Rising Fortunes of Indie Artists (2004-2006)** : As indie artists and labels gained popularity and influence, a wave of other independent acts saw their fortunes improve. This included bands like Bloc Party, Editors, Bat for Lashes, The Kooks, Babyshambles, and The Libertines, who all experienced increased success between 2004 and 2006. "Other artists saw success too. Block party, editors, bat for lashes, the cookes, baby shambles, libertines, editors. A lot of them saw their fortunes rise in 2004, 2005, and 2006."
