## songs
**Planet Claire (1979)** : As Side One Track One of their 1979 debut album, "Planet Claire" features mysterious Morse code. This code is identified as a call tape or ZKR tape, a Canadian Forces message from Milkov and Hubbard's, Nova Scotia, informing ships about optimal ship-to-shore communication frequencies. "It's what's known as a call tape or a ZKR tape. It's a recording of a message sent out by the Canadian forces-based Milkov and Hubbard's Nova Scotia."

## curiosities
**Formation and Early Ethos** : The B-52s formed in Athens, Georgia, after a cocktail gathering in a Chinese restaurant, with the intention of injecting silliness and fun into rock music, which they felt was too serious. "They were formed over too many cocktails in a Chinese restaurant in the unassuming college town of Athens, Georgia. Why did rock have to be so serious?"
**Distinctive Style and Rehearsal Space** : The band cultivated a unique visual style, incorporating 1950s thrift store clothing and bouffant hairdos. For their sound, their guitarist would replace the lower two strings with bass strings. They rehearsed in the blood-letting room of an abandoned mortuary, emphasizing their unconventional approach. "And just for fun, we'll rehearse in the blood-litting room of an abandoned mortuary."
**Initial "Novelty Band" Perception** : Due to their overtly goofy and silly presentation, The B-52s were initially dismissed by some as merely a novelty band. "They were so goofy that some wrote them off as a novelty band, at least at first."
**Inspiration for New Musicians** : Despite early skepticism, The B-52s, a mixed-gender and multi-sexual band, became a significant inspiration for a new generation of musicians, with Kurt Cobain notably transfixed by their performance on Saturday Night Live in 1980. "The truth is that this mixed gender and multi-sexual band became an inspiration for a new generation of musicians. Kurt Cobain saw the one Saturday Night Live in 1980. It was absolutely transfixed by them."

# genre - Punk

## curiosities
**1970s Special Time** : The 1970s punk movement created a strong sense among its followers that it was a truly special and exciting time for music and for being alive. "If you were tied in with punk in the 1970s, there was a sense among you and your friends that this was a really special time for music and a good time to be alive."

"Punk changed the way people looked at music, breaking down artistic, social, and demographic barriers."
**Limitations and Evolution** : While initially impactful, punk's limitations, such as its often angry themes and reliance on two or three chords, led to cross-pollination with pop music, eventually birthing New Wave. # genre - New Wave

## curiosities
**1978 Emergence and Characteristics** : New Wave began to appear in 1978 as punk was peaking and burning out. It resulted from a cross-pollination between punk's attitude and pop's melody, featuring twitchy, spiky, choppy, and nervous sounds with experimental structures, geeky vocals, artsy intellectualism, and a suburban vibe, contrasting with traditional rock's blues roots. "Pop and punk had a baby, and they called it New Wave."
**Early Application of the Term** : The term "New Wave" was initially used in the late 1950s/early 1960s for French filmmakers, then in the early 1970s by critics for artsy bands like the New York Dolls. In 1976, it appeared in the British fanzine *Sniff and Glue* and was used by Malcolm McLaren in *Melody Maker* to describe punk-related but not strictly punk-sounding bands. "The earliest references we can find to the term New Wave are in a British fanzine called Sniff and Glue in 1976."
**Sire Records Marketing Strategy** : Facing resistance to "punk rock" on mainstream radio, Sire Records, led by Seymour Stein, launched a "Don't Call It Punk, Call It New Wave" campaign around Christmas 1976. This marketing rebrand successfully removed the stigma, making the music more palatable for radio and the public. "So Sire launched a NAD campaign called Don't Call It Punk, urging people to call this new music New Wave."
**North American Acceptance (1977)** : By the end of 1977, the term "New Wave" gained traction in North America, with mainstream publications like *Time* and *Newsweek* covering the "punk slash New Wave movement," despite initial confusion over its exact definition. "By the end of 1977, the phrase had caught on in North America. Time and Newsweek, which is about as mainstream as you can get, both wrote stories on the punk slash New Wave movement."
**Broadening Definition in America** : While the UK developed terms like "New Pop" and "Postmodern," "New Wave" in America became a very broad term, initially encompassing artists like Tom Petty, and later defined as "like punk, but intelligent" or "like punk, except weirder," especially as acts started charting and appearing on TV. "But it became really, really broad."
**1978-1984 Era and Evolution** : The New Wave scene, roughly from 1978 to 1984, can be divided into two periods. The first saw a proliferation of guitar-driven alternative pop bands, liberated by punk to experiment and emerging from cities beyond New York and London, such as Toronto, Chicago, Los Angeles, and Akron, Ohio. "And as these new groups started to come to our attention, many of them came from cities other than New York and London, the two main centers of punk."
**Authenticity vs. Mainstream** : New Wave music from bands like The Police, The Pretenders, and Blondie was perceived as more authentic and innovative than the corporate arena rock, lightweight pop, and "vacuous" disco dominating the mainstream at the end of the 1970s, offering a danceable yet artsy and intellectual alternative. "Songs like that from the police, along with the pretenders and blondie and others, was not only fun and different, but it was also perceived as being more authentic than what was populating the mainstream at the time."
**Economic Boost and Label Shift** : The disco bubble burst and a worldwide recession in the late 1970s and early 1980s led to record labels finding it cheaper to produce and release New Wave records than those by mainstream acts. This economic reality prompted more labels to sign New Wave groups, with successes like The Knack demonstrating phenomenal returns on investment. "Record labels found that it was cheaper to produce and release records by this new generation of new wave bands than it was to do the same with bigger mainstream acts."
**American Radio Reluctance and MTV Breakthrough** : Despite commercial success for some acts, American FM Rock Radio remained hesitant to play New Wave, often banning the term "New Wave" on air, which hampered many bands. This resistance persisted until August 1, 1981, when MTV launched, breaking the genre wide open. "Thanks to the reluctance of American radio to play New Wave music, the whole thing seemed to be on its way out."
**MTV's Role in Popularity** : MTV, launched in 1981, significantly fueled the growth and popularity of New Wave, especially through its programming dominated by British bands, even mistakenly stating it did so "through most of the 1960s" (a likely error, as MTV started in '81). The channel extensively played New Wave groups, showcasing their visual styles. "MTV fueled the growth and popularity of New Wave through most of the 1960s."
**Soundtrack Licensing** : New Wave music was cost-effective to license for movie soundtracks, a factor exploited by director John Hughes for films like *Sixteen Candles*, *Pretty in Pink*, and *The Breakfast Club*, filling their albums with edgy, new bands, many from the UK. "This music was also cheap to license for movie soundtracks, something that director John Hughes used to his full advantage..."
