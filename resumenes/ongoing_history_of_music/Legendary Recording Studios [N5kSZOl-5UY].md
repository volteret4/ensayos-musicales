# studio - Le Studio
## curiosities
**Location and Construction (1974)** : Le Studio was a purpose-built recording facility constructed in 1974 in Morin Heights, Quebec. Designers Andre Perry and Yale Brandeis envisioned it as a country retreat where the natural surroundings would inspire artists. "It was a purpose-built facility, built in 1974."
**Unique Design with Glass Windows** : The studio was designed with large, floor-to-ceiling glass windows to maximize its connection to the natural environment. While acoustically challenging, this feature was integral to the studio's unique "vibe." "This is why the studio had big floor-deceiling glass windows."
**Success and Artist Retreat** : Le Studio achieved great success, allowing artists to "cocoon themselves" for extended periods to complete their projects. Its client list included renowned acts such as Rush, The Police, Sting, The Bee Gees, David Bowie, and Bryan Adams. "La Studio was fantastically successful."
**Sale and Decline (1988)** : Andre Perry and Yale Brandeis sold the facility in 1988, after which its condition and reputation deteriorated significantly. "Andre Perry and Yale Brandeis sold the facility in 1988, and after that it was, frankly, never the same."
**Abandonment and Deterioration** : Following its sale, Le Studio was abandoned for years, remaining closed, unheated, and unpowered. Attempts to sell the property in 2008 failed, leading to its decay into a "wrecked hulk." "La Studio sat closed, unheated, unpowered, and abandoned for years."
**Tax Sale and Restoration Attempt (2015)** : In 2015, the town of Morin Heights put the studio up for sale for unpaid property taxes, briefly listing it for $18,000. A Quebec drummer, Richard Baxter, launched a crowdfunding campaign to purchase and restore it, but the current owner ultimately paid the taxes. The studio remains stripped of its gear and is slowly being reclaimed by nature. "In 2015, it was put up for sale again, but this time by the town of Moran Heights for unpaid property taxes."
**The Tea Party Recording (1997)** : The Tea Party recorded their fourth album, *Transmission*, at Le Studio in 1997. "The Tea Party, clients of the former La Studio and Moran Heights Quebec."

# studio - The Power Station / Avatar Studios
## curiosities
**Founding and Location (1977)** : The Power Station, located in New York at approximately 53rd and 10th, was founded in 1977 by Tony Bongiovi, the second cousin of John Bon Jovi. The building previously served as a Consolidated Edison power plant and later a TV studio for "Let's Make a Deal." "In 1977, the building was bought and rebuilt as a recording studio by Tony Bongeovi, the second cousin of some New Jersey kid named John Bongeovi."
**Tony Bongiovi's Background and Vision** : Tony Bongiovi began his recording career at 17 at Motown Studios in Detroit, later moving to Media Sound in New York. Observing the rapid changes in recording technology and musical tastes, he conceived a studio design that challenged the conventional "dead" room acoustics of the mid-1970s. "Tony got into the recording game when he was just 17."
**Innovative Reverberation Time-Based Design** : Bongiovi designed The Power Station as a "reverberation time-based studio," incorporating a deliberate, slight reverb of about three-quarters of a second in the main recording room. This decay time matched that of the original Motown Studios in Detroit, resulting in records that sounded "live in full of energy." "Tony Bongeovi built the power station as a reverberation time-based studio."
**Distinctive Drum Sound** : A key characteristic of recordings from The Power Station was its unique drum sound, described as "big, powerful and boomed." This was achieved through the studio's special ambient acoustics, sometimes augmented with noise gates on kits like Tony Thompson's. "The biggest difference between the power station and just about everywhere else was how drums sounded."
**Influence on Rock Recording** : The Power Station's innovative acoustic approach gained immense popularity, profoundly changing rock recording techniques for years. It fostered a demand for bigger drum sounds, moving away from close-miking and instead leveraging natural room reverb found in large spaces. "The power station, the studio, their approach was so popular that it changed the way many rock recordings were made for years."
**Current Status (Avatar Studios)** : The Power Station continues to operate today under a new name, Avatar Studios. "The power station still exists, although it's not called that anymore."

# studio - Château d'Hérouville
## curiosities
**Historical Background** : Located in rural France, this was a French château built in 1740 on the remnants of a 16th-century structure. It featured two wings, 30 rooms, a swimming pool, a tennis court, outbuildings, and 42,000 acres of parkland. "It was an honest-to-god French château that was built in 1740 on the remains of an earlier building that dated back to sometime in the mid-16th century."
**Conversion to Residential Studio (1960s)** : In the 1960s, composer Michel Magne purchased the château and transformed it into a recording studio. He converted one wing into one of the world's first residential recording studios, where artists could live and work on their projects. "He turned one wing into one of the first residential recording studios in the world."
**Unique Atmosphere and Host** : Magne was known for being an "interesting host," and the studio fostered a decadent atmosphere with frequent parties, abundant alcohol, drugs, and "not infrequent sex parties." "Not only could you live and work at the studio, Manga was also a very interesting host."
**"France's Abbey Road"** : The château earned the nickname "France's Abbey Road" due to its popularity and the high caliber of artists it attracted. "They called it France's Abbey Road."
**Notable Clients and Recordings** : Many famous artists recorded and performed at the Château. The Grateful Dead recorded there and played a show on its grounds. Elton John named his 1972 album after the place (the first of three he recorded there). Pink Floyd, T. Rex, David Bowie, Fleetwood Mac, and Iggy Pop (who recorded his 1977 album *The Idiot* with Bowie) also utilized the studio. "The Grateful Dad not only recorded there, but played a show on its grounds."
**Decline, Owner's Suicide, and Abandonment (Mid-1980s)** : The Château encountered legal problems in the mid-1980s, culminating in owner Michel Magne's suicide. The buildings were subsequently abandoned and began to fall into disrepair. "Sadly, the place ran into legal problems in the mid-80s, and owner Michelle Magne committed suicide."
**Protected Status and Sale (2013)** : Despite its abandonment, the Château was a protected site, which prevented developers from acquiring the land. In 2013, it was listed for sale at approximately $2 million, with an estimated half-million dollars needed for repairs to make it livable. "Fortunately, though, it was a protected site."
**Rebirth as Arts Center** : The Château was eventually purchased by four music fans and is now in the process of being reborn as a center for the arts. "Happily, though, it has been purchased by four music fans and is being reborn as a center for the arts."
**Haunted Reputation** : During its operational years, the Château was said to be haunted, with guests reporting "weird apparitions and touches on the shoulder when no one was there." "Back in the day, though, the Château was said to be haunted."

# studio - AIR Studios Montserrat
## curiosities
**Founding by George Martin (Mid-1970s)** : After establishing Associated Independent Recording (AIR) in London, producer George Martin and his wife, who were fond of Montserrat, built a state-of-the-art residential recording facility on the Caribbean island in the mid-1970s. "So in the mid-70s, they did."
**Residential Studio Concept** : Following a contemporary trend, AIR Montserrat was designed as a residential studio, allowing musicians to live and work on their projects for as long as needed in an isolated, inspiring environment. "Instruct a state-of-the-art residential facility where musicians could work and play for as long as they liked."
**Accessibility** : Artists would fly into the neighboring island of Antigua and then take a smaller plane or boat to reach Montserrat and the studio. "Artists would fly into neighboring Antigua and then take a small plane or boat over to Montserrat."
**Popularity, Amenities, and Setting** : The studio became consistently booked starting around 1979. It was a "gorgeous place" with top-tier gear, a large swimming pool, and a deck offering views of the surrounding mountains and the Soufrière volcano. "It was a gorgeous place, with the best gear, a big swimming pool, and a deck featuring a view of the surrounding mountains and the sufrere volcano off in the distance."
**Isolation and Inspiration for Artists** : Artists highly valued the studio's isolated location, which allowed them to mingle with locals, immerse themselves in the atmosphere, and find unique inspiration unavailable elsewhere. "Artists loved it because it was so isolated."
**Notable Clients and Albums** : Over its decade of operation, AIR Montserrat hosted many major artists, including Bush, Elton John, Supertramp, The Rolling Stones, Pink Floyd, Duran Duran, Black Sabbath, Paul McCartney, and Dire Straits. Albums like *Brothers in Arms*, *Ghosts in the Machine*, and *Synchronicity* were recorded there. "Bush, Elton Johns, Super Tramp, The Rolling Stones, Pink Floyd, Durand Durand, Black Sabbath, Paul McCartney, Dire Straits, they all went to Air Studios Montserrat."
**Decline Due to Label Resistance** : One factor in the studio's decline was record labels' increasing dislike for artists working in such remote locations, making it difficult to monitor projects despite the labels' ability to recoup costs from future sales. "First record labels didn't like the fact that their artists were so far away working in places where they couldn't easily check up on them."
**Destruction by Hurricane Hugo (1989)** : AIR Studios Montserrat was ultimately destroyed by Hurricane Hugo in 1989. This Category Five hurricane devastated Montserrat, leaving 11,000 of the 12,000 residents homeless, causing six-meter storm surges, mudslides, and widespread utility outages. While the buildings remained standing, nothing inside was salvageable. "But the bigger disaster was Hurricane Hugo."
**Musical Legacy** : Despite the physical destruction of the studio, the music created within its walls endures as its lasting legacy. "The only thing that survives was the music that was made there."

# studio - Abbey Road Studios

**Extensive Microphone Collection** : Abbey Road Studios possesses an impressive collection of microphones, with some dating back to its opening in the 1930s. "Their microphone collection dates all the way back to when they opened in the 1930s."
**John Lennon DNA Anecdote** : An anecdote suggests that an ancient microphone in Studio Two could potentially reveal John Lennon's DNA if analyzed, highlighting its direct connection to legendary artists. "you know, if you analyze that one for DNA, you'd find a bunch of John Lennon in there."
**Educational Initiative (Abbey Road Institute)** : Abbey Road has established the Abbey Road Institute, an educational initiative headed by Luca Barassi. The institute aims to share the studio's over 80 years of expertise and knowledge through vocational training for aspiring music industry professionals. "This official name is Abbey Road Institute."

# genre - Music Industry
## curiosities
**Traditional Recording Studio Costs (Pre-Millennium)** : In earlier decades, making an album necessitated renting expensive recording studios, incurring hourly, daily, weekly, or monthly rates. Additional costs included producers, engineers, recording tape, and catering, making the process very expensive. "In addition to paying an hourly, daily, weekly, or monthly rate, you needed to pay for a producer, an engineer or two, all the recording tape you used, and any catering that was required."
**Label Funding Model (Pre-Millennium)** : Historically, the music industry was highly profitable, enabling record labels to readily advance funds for recording costs, which were then recouped from the profits generated by future album sales. "Your label would happily advance you the money to cover your recording costs because they were just going to take it out of the profits derived from the future sales of that album."
**Recording Studio Building Boom** : The significant profitability of the music industry in earlier eras spurred the construction of numerous large, expensive recording studios. These facilities were established in major centers like New York, Los Angeles, and London, as well as in more secluded locales such as countryside chateaus or exotic islands. "Because there was so much money to be made, a lot of big, expensive recording studios were built."
**Shift to Home Recording** : Contemporary technological advancements, including laptops and even smartphones, now enable musicians to produce high-quality, professional-sounding albums in home environments. "These days, though, it's possible to make a very good sounding album on a laptop in your bedroom."
**Ongoing Relevance of Professional Studios** : Despite the prevalence of home recording, large-scale professional recording studios maintain their relevance for specific sounds, particular needs, and recording environments that cannot be replicated at home. "There are some things, some sounds, and some needs that require a dedicated recording studio environment."
**Studio Closures Due to Industry Changes** : Many recording studios have been forced to close down due to profound shifts in the music industry and the high operational costs associated with maintaining a studio, particularly when booking rates decline. "But then there were those facilities that have been forced to shut down, killed by the massive changes to the music industry and the high cost of maintaining a studio when bookings are down."
**Canadian Content Policy (1971)** : In 1971, Canada lacked a developed music industry. A government policy mandating that 30% of radio broadcasts feature Canadian-origin music spurred the creation of a national music infrastructure, leading to the establishment of record labels and recording studios. "In the year 1971, there really wasn't any kind of music industry in Canada."
**Evolution of Studio Business Models (Education)** : To ensure financial viability, many recording studios have diversified their operations by establishing trade schools. These institutions teach subjects ranging from recording engineering to sound reinforcement and music industry law, with the educational component subsidizing their core recording business. "A facility like Metalworks can afford to stay in business because it's now more than just a recording studio."
**Crisis in Pro-Recording Studios (Post-Millennium)** : Since the beginning of the millennium, professional recording studios have faced a crisis due to high maintenance costs, the continuous need for technology upgrades, and significantly reduced recording budgets. Advances in home recording technology further contribute to this challenge. "Pro-Recording studios have been in crisis since the beginning of the millennium."

**Traditional Studio Acoustics (Pre-Mid-70s)** : Before the mid-1970s, the prevailing wisdom in recording studio design was to construct rooms that were as quiet and "dead" as possible. This approach aimed to provide the most precise control over recorded sound by eliminating all external influences. "Up until the middle 70s, most recording studios were constructed so that they were as quiet as possible."
**Impact of Dead Rooms on Performance** : Musicians often found "dead" recording rooms detrimental to their performances. The absence of sound returning to them from the room led to an unconscious tendency to overplay to compensate, frequently resulting in subpar performances. "What he found was that musicians hated the conventional wisdom of dead studios."
**Reverberation Time-Based Studio Design** : Tony Bongiovi pioneered a studio design philosophy focused on incorporating deliberate, slight reverb into recording rooms. His Power Station studio featured a reverb decay time of approximately three-quarters of a second, matching that of the original Motown Studios in Detroit. "Tony Bongeovi built the power station as a reverberation time-based studio."
**Evolution of Drum Sound in Rock Recording** : The innovative acoustic approach pioneered by studios like The Power Station, which emphasized natural room reverb and big drum sounds (sometimes augmented by noise gates), revolutionized rock recording for years. This shift led engineers and artists to demand larger drum sounds, moving away from tight, dry, close-miked techniques. "The power station, the studio, their approach was so popular that it changed the way many rock recordings were made for years."
**Natural vs. Electronic Reverb** : While electronic effects can digitally simulate reverb, they are unable to replicate the real-time interaction and enhanced performance a musician achieves when playing in a room with natural, live acoustics. "Now, you can simulate those effects electronically, but you can't simulate the kind of performance that you get out of the musician playing and reacting to those live acoustics in real time."
