# artist - The Guess Who

## members
Chad Allen (original name)

## genres
Garage Rock

## labels
Quality Records

## songs
**Shake It All Over (1965)** : This very solid garage rock single was originally released in 1965 by Chad Allen and the Expressions from Winnipeg. Initially, radio stations ignored it because it was Canadian. Quality Records cleverly sent out a 7-inch single with "Guess Who?" on the label instead of the band's name, fooling DJs into believing it was a new British invasion band. The song, a cover of Johnny Kid in the Pirates, hit number one in Canada, number 22 in the US, and number 27 in the UK, leading the band to officially change their name to The Guess Who. "The DJs did not get the joke. They told their listeners that they were listening to shake it all over by this cool new band called the Guess Who?"

## curiosities
**Name Change Due to Stunt** : The band, originally known as Chad Allen and the Expressions, was forced to change their name to The Guess Who after a publicity stunt by Quality Records. The label sent out a single with "Guess Who?" on it, leading DJs to mistakenly promote them as a hot new British invasion band when their cover of "Shake It All Over" became a hit. "the group had no choice but to change their name from Chad Allen and the expressions to the Guess Who."
**Increased FM Airplay** : Due to new FM radio rules in Canada that mandated playing more album tracks and Canadian content, The Guess Who's music, alongside others like Bachman-Turner Overdrive and Neil Young, saw significantly more airplay. This was in part because there weren't enough new Canadian bands of a certain caliber yet. "a lot more guest who, back then turn or overdrive the band and Neil Young got played."

# artist - The Tragically Hip

## albums
**Up To Here (1989)** : Released on September 5, 1989, this debut album became a landmark, selling over a million copies in Canada alone and establishing the group as one of the country's most beloved musical acts. A remarkable seven of its eleven tracks received regular rotation on radio across Canada, and the album was supported by four or five music videos. This success was a crucial indicator of Generation X's growing appetite for homegrown music, marking the beginning of the Can Rock explosion. "An EP did well, but then a full album was released on September 5th, 1989. It would go on to sell over a million copies just in Canada."
**Road Apples (1991)** : This album, released in February 1991, successfully maintained and built upon the initial success of "Up To Here." Its performance solidified The Tragically Hip's momentum and further demonstrated the Canadian Generation X's strong demand for domestic music. "when road apples appeared in February 1991, it maintained the momentum and built on the initial record success."

# curiosity

**Historical Canadian Bias Against Domestic Music** : For several decades, Canadians largely ignored or disliked Canadian music unless it received validation from outside the country, particularly the United States. This deeply rooted insecurity meant that ambitious Canadian musicians often sought fortune in America, and even those who stayed weren't fully accepted until achieving a US hit. "The majority opinion regarding music was, if it's Canadian, it can't be good. Unless, of course, another country says it is."
**The Can Rock Revolution of the 1990s** : This term describes a transformative period in the 1990s when Canadian attitudes towards homegrown talent completely shifted. Not only did Canadian music fans embrace domestic bands, but Canadian music became highly sought after worldwide, marking a significant change from previous decades of insecurity. "Some have called this the great Ken Rock Revolution of the 1990s. It changed everything."
**Early Canadian Music Industry State** : Prior to the 1970s, there was barely a Canadian music industry to speak of. It consisted mostly of small local labels and offices that were branch plants of foreign record companies, indicating a lack of robust domestic infrastructure to support Canadian artists. "Meanwhile, there was barely a Canadian music industry to speak of. There were a bunch of small labels. But the industry was essentially a bunch of offices that were branched plants of foreign record companies."
**Canadian Content (Cancon) Rules Adoption (1971)** : As of January 18, 1971, Canadian radio stations were legally required to devote 30% of their music programming to Canadian artists. This mandate, driven by rising national pride after Canada's centennial, was both cultural protectionism and an industrial strategy, creating demand that spurred the development of a domestic music infrastructure. "As of January 18, 1971, all radio stations were required by law to devote 30% of the music they played to records by Canadian artists."
**Impact of Cancon on Industry Development** : The legal quotas imposed by Cancon rules led to the creation of a full-fledged domestic music industry. This included access to recording studios, agents, managers, promoters, venues, and distribution networks, all essential elements that had been largely missing previously. "Because there was now a legal quota to fill, an industry was built to fill those quotas."
**"Beaver Hours" Loophole and CRTC Intervention** : Early attempts by some Top 40 radio stations to circumvent Cancon rules involved editing Canadian songs down to 60-90 seconds and jamming them between 11 PM and midnight to meet quotas. The CRTC discovered this practice and quickly shut down the loophole, declaring these "beaver hours" illegal and initiating modified rules. "When the CRTC found out about that, they shut that loophole pretty quick and initiated some modified rules, making these beaver hours. Seriously, that's what some people call them, illegal."
**Evolution of FM Radio Rules (1960s)** : Changes to Canadian FM radio rules in the 1960s prevented stations from simply re-broadcasting AM content. New, stringent rules limited song plays to 18 times a week and restricted hit songs (Top 40) to under 49% of the playlist, forcing programmers to explore albums for more diverse material. This also created time for longer album rock tracks due to limited commercial minutes, with 30% of these songs also needing to be Canadian. "One of the original rules was that it was illegal to play a song more than 18 times a week on any given station. It meant that stations had to play more songs to fill the broadcast day."
**Factor (Foundation to Assist Canadian Talent on Record) Establishment (1981)** : Established in 1981, Factor proved "fabulously successful" in funding emerging Canadian talent for recording and touring. It filled a gap in Canada, which lacked Britain's tuition-free art school tradition that had produced numerous musicians, including British invasion bands. "One solution came on in 1981, the establishment of Factor, the foundation to assist Canadian talent on record. Factor, which still exists today, was fabulously successful in funding emerging talent in this country when it came to things like recording and touring."
**VideoFACT Establishment (1984)** : In 1984, VideoFACT was established, funded entirely by MuchMusic. This program was a government agreement where MuchMusic would fund the nascent Canadian music video industry in exchange for keeping MTV out of the country. This ensured MuchMusic had Canadian videos to play, vital for artist exposure. "Yet much music signed on in 1984, another program called Video Fact was established. This was funded entirely by much music in exchange for the government keeping MTV out of the country."
**MuchMusic's Role in National Culture** : MuchMusic acted as a national pop and rock radio station, creating a shared musical experience for young Canadians across the country. By exposing kids from St. John's to Whitehorse to Canadian artist videos, it helped knit together a national music scene and foster a sense of shared identity. "For the first time, a kid in St. John's Newfoundland could have something in common musically with a kid in Toronto, a kid in Edmonton, and a kid in Whitehorse."
**Demographic Shift: Generation X's Impact** : By the late 1980s, a new generation of young music fans, Generation X, emerged without the cynicism towards domestic music prevalent in the 1960s and 70s. They embraced Canadian music on radio and MuchMusic, making it their own and fueling the rise of Canadian artists. "They simply just did not grow up with the cynicism regarding domestic music that was present in the 1960s and 70s."
**Halifax: "The New Seattle" (1991-1993)** : Between late 1991 and 1993, Halifax experienced a period where it was discovered by America and even suspected, at one point, to be the "new Seattle" due to its vibrant music scene. This perception was largely influenced by bands like Sloan signing with labels like Sub Pop. "There was a time between late 1991 and 1993 that America discovered Halifax. It was even, at one point, suspected to be the new Seattle, largely because of this band who ended up being signed to Subpop for their first album."
**Recorded Music Industry Prosperity in the 1990s** : The 1990s were a period of immense financial prosperity for the Canadian recorded music industry, driven by high record sales. This influx of money translated into significant investment in advertising for both Canadian and international releases, supported flourishing music magazines, and funded extensive touring and festivals. "In today's digital world, it's hard to cast your mind back and imagine exactly how much money was flowing through the system because people were buying so many records."
**Canadian Music Success Post-2000** : Since 2000, Canada has continued to "punch far above its weight" on the world stage, producing global superstars like Justin Bieber, The Weeknd, Drake, and Arcade Fire. Beyond these megastars, many Canadian artists have contributed to an "international coolness factor" that is hard to quantify by traditional record sales metrics. "since 2000 the country has continued to punch far, far above its weight in the world stage, with artists like Justin Bieber, the weekend, Drake and Arcade Fire."
**Radio Star Maker Program (2000)** : A new funding program, Radio Star Maker, was added in 2000 to supplement the money available from Factor. This cash proved instrumental in furthering the careers of artists such as Metric, Broken Social Scene, Alexisonfire, and City and Colour, often converting loans into grants and increasing federal government involvement. "In 2000, a new program called Radio Star Maker was added to the money available from Factor, and soon this cash was instrumental in helping further the careers of metric, broken social scene, Alexis on fire, city and color, and many more."
**Economic Return on Investment in Canadian Music** : A study by the Canadian Independent Music Association indicates that for every dollar invested into Canadian music, there is a return of $1.22 in economic benefits. This positive return underscores the financial justification for continued government and broadcaster investment in the Canadian music sector. "A study by the Canadian Independent Music Association says that for every dollar put into Canadian music, the return is a dollar 22 in economic benefits, which is pretty good."
**Contemporary Challenges for Canadian Musicians** : Despite past successes, Canadian musicians face significant challenges in the modern era, including setbacks from COVID-19, gentrification and rising real estate prices making it difficult to find places to live, work, rehearse, and perform, and the struggle to earn a living wage in the streaming era. Additionally, the absence of Cancon quotas on streaming platforms is theorized to make it harder for Canadians to discover Canadian music. "And yes, gentrification and rising real estate prices are making it tougher for musicians to find places to live and work and rehearse and perform. And yes, there were huge, huge problems when it comes to musicians earning a living wage in the streaming era."
