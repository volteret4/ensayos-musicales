## curiosities
**Band Naming Origin** : The name "Queens of the Stone Age" originated from producer Chris Goss, a member of Masters of Reality, who would jokingly refer to Kyuss as "Queens of the Stone Age" during studio sessions, suggesting they sounded like royal women of the Stone Age. Josh Homme found the phrase cool and funny, especially imagining it on a marquee or concert ticket, leading him to adopt it for his new project. "Whenever he was in the studio with the band, he would yell at Kias, calling them Queens of the Stone Age. As in you guys all sound like Queens of the Stone Age."
**Musical Direction Shift** : After Kyuss, Josh Homme aimed for a "clean slate" with Queens of the Stone Age, desiring a sound that was shorter, sharper, tighter, cleaner, and more focused, consciously moving away from the long stoner rock jams of his previous band. "Josh wanted a clean slate, so no more long-stone or rock jams. It said to be shorter, sharper, tighter, cleaner, and with more focus."
**First Official Gig** : The band's inaugural performance took place on November 20, 1997, at a Mexican restaurant in Palm Desert, California, to an intimate audience of approximately 25 people. "The first official Queens gig was on November 20th, 1997, and a Mexican restaurant in Palm Desert, California, in front of about 25 people."

## curiosities
**Early Life and Musical Beginnings** : Born into a well-to-do family in the high desert east of Palm Springs, California, where his father worked as a contractor in the Coachella Valley. Josh initially wanted to play drums, but his parents encouraged him to learn a "real instrument," leading him to acquire his first $50 acoustic guitar at age 10. "Josh Ami, who is the only continuous member of Queens of the Stone Age, was a kid from a well to do family. Dad was a contractor who built houses in the Coachella Valley. Josh first wanted to play drums, but Mum and Dad insisted that he learn a real instrument."
**Early Music Lessons** : His initial formal music training involved learning to play polkas on the guitar, a venture that lasted only about two weeks before he discovered the raw energy of hardcore punk. "The first thing Josh learned how to play were polkas. That lasted about two weeks before he discovered hardcore punk."
**Unique Guitar Strumming Style** : Homme's distinctive guitar playing style is attributed to a combination of the straight up and down strumming techniques found in hardcore punk bands like GBH and Discharge, integrated with the strumming required for playing polkas. "You combine that with the strumming required to play polkas, and you suddenly begin to understand why Josh plays guitar the way he does, and why Queens of the Stone Age sounds just a little bit different."
**Early Band Practice Locations** : During his time with Cats and Jammer, Josh Homme recalls practicing in garages in the high desert that were later converted into meth labs at night. "Josh remembers practicing in garages that were later converted into meth labs at night."
**Guitar Sound Secrecy** : Josh Homme is famously protective of the specifics of how he achieves his guitar sound, carefully guarding his techniques. "And by the way, do not ask Josh about how he gets his guitar sound. He guards it very, very carefully."
**Post-Kyuss Musical Influences** : Following the breakup of Kyuss, Homme's musical direction was significantly influenced by his discovery of albums like Iggy Pop's "Lust for Life" and "The Idiot," steering him towards a new sound distinct from Kyuss's. "It was around this time that Josh discovered albums like Lust for Life and the Idiot. That was the kind of music he wanted to make now, not whatever Kaya's was doing."
**Seattle Hiatus and Education** : After his Gamma Ray project, Homme sought a change of scenery from the desert, moving to Seattle in 1996 and 1997. During this period, he took a break from music to study business at the University of Washington. "He needed a change of scenery from the desert. So he moved to Seattle, a very wet place to do nothing. The idea was to take a break from music and go back to school. And he did that for 1996 and 1997. He studied business at the University of Washington."
**Screaming Trees Tour as Guitarist** : Josh Homme spent two years touring as the rhythm guitarist for the Seattle band Screaming Trees after meeting their singer Mark Lanegan, a Kyuss fan. He was described as a six-foot-five red-haired rhythm guitarist during their Lollapalooza 1996 set. "Mark was a fan of Kaya's and needed someone to tour with the trees and ask Josh if he would like to be his guitarist. So he did that for two years. If you were at Lollapalooza in 1996 and saw the tree set, that six foot five red-haired rhythm guitarist was Josh Hommie."
**Attempt to Revive Gamma Ray** : After his work with Screaming Trees and the first Desert Sessions, Josh Homme considered resurrecting his Gamma Ray project for a new long-term endeavor. However, a German power metal band already held the name and threatened legal action, forcing him to choose a different name. "His first thought was to resurrect gamma ray, but there was a problem. A German power metal band had dibs on that name and started making noises about legal action."
**Near-Death Experience** : It is mentioned that Josh Homme once nearly died, a significant event in his history. "Oh, and then there was that time Josh nearly died."

## curiosities
**Name Origin and Change** : Initially known as Sons of Kyuss, the name was derived from the Dungeons and Dragons game, where "Caes" (Kyuss) is an evil high priest with hideous offspring. The band shortened their name to just "Kyuss" in 1990 at the insistence of their record label, Dali. "Now that makes no sense unless you play Dungeons and Dragons. The game features an evil high priest called caes, and he had some hideous offspring, hence sons of caes."
**Distinctive Guitar Sound** : Kyuss developed a unique sound by tuning guitars down as low as possible, to the point where the strings were floppy. Josh Homme further enhanced this by running his guitar through both guitar and bass amps and cabinets, making the low end particularly prominent. "The guitars were tuned down as low as possible, right to the point where the strings were so floppy that they couldn't be played. The sounds that came out seemed to appeal to the stoners in the crowd, so they went with that."
**Anti-Radio Philosophy** : Kyuss intentionally avoided mainstream radio appeal, actively working to write songs that would "make radio cringe." They would even cut out parts that sounded too much like a catchy chorus, focusing instead on building a cult following. "In fact, they worked hard at writing songs that would make radio cringe, except interestingly, a station in Minneapolis who became a big supporter. If something sounded too much like a catchy chorus, they'd actually cut that from the song."
**Sales and Success Metrics** : For Kyuss, selling 40,000 copies of an album was considered a significant victory, reflecting their focus on a cult following rather than mass appeal. "And if an album sold 40,000 copies, that was fine. That was the big victory for them."
**Career Highlight** : A notable achievement for Kyuss was opening for Metallica during their tour in Australia. "Their career highlight was opening for Metallica in Australia."
**Lineup Instability and Label Issues** : The band experienced numerous lineup changes, including Nick Oliveri leaving and returning, and two different drummers and bass players over time. They also faced challenges when their record label went under and they were absorbed into Elektra, a major label part of the Warner Empire, which they disliked. "And the low light was probably all the lineup changes. Nick all of a very left and came back. The band went through two drummers and two bass players. And there were other problems. Their record label went under and they got absorbed into Elektra, part of the major label Warner Empire. That didn't sit well with them."
**Manager Firing** : Kyuss made the decision to fire their manager at some point in their career. "And oh, they also fired their manager."
**Breakup in 1995** : Kyuss broke up in October 1995, with the lineup consisting of Josh Homme, Scott Reeder on bass, Fredo Hernandez on drums, and John Garcia on vocals. The breakup appeared amicable, largely due to boredom and a desire by members like Josh Homme (who was 21 at the time, having started when he was 17) to explore other musical avenues. "And then suddenly, out of nowhere, in October of 1995, caes broke up. At the time, the group consisted of Josh, Scott Reader on bass, drummer Fredo Hernandez, and singer John Garcia. The breakup seemed to be fairly friendly. Everybody just seemed to be bored. It was time."
**Musical Influence and Genre Pioneering** : Kyuss was highly influential, considered "ground zero" for a new form of fuzzy guitar music. They were credited with pioneering genres known as Desert Rock, Dirt Rock, and particularly Stoner Rock, a term Josh Homme personally disliked. "They were ground zero of a new form of fuzzy guitar music. Some called it Desert Rock. You can see why. And others used the term Dirt Rock. But the one that really stuck was Stoner Rock."
