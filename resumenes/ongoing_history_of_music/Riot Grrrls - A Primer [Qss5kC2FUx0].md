## albums

## curiosities
**Godmother of Riot Grrrl** : Patti Smith is widely considered the godmother of all the Riot Grrrls. She was a punk poet, author, and musician who first surfaced in New York City even before the punk movement fully took hold, releasing four significant and influential albums between 1975 and 1979. "But if there's a godmother to all the Riot Girls, it's gotta be Patty Smith."
**Influential Albums (1975-1979)** : Between 1975 and 1979, Patti Smith released four very important and extremely influential albums. These works were bold, filled with strong statements and opinions on diverse topics such as religion, racism, feminist principles, and the state of society, profoundly changing the lives of many female punks and future Riot Girls, as well as many guys. "She released four very important and extremely influential albums between 1975 and 1979."

## curiosities
**Pioneers of Riot Grrrl** : Bikini Kill, active for about a year before their 1991 performance at the International Pop Underground Convention, were recognized as one of the pioneers of the entire Riot Grrrl scene. They combined radical feminist politics with hard music and championed safe spaces for women at their gigs. "Amongst the performers that night was a group called Bikini Kill, a group that had been around for about a year, and they sounded like this."
**Feminist Show Ethos** : Bikini Kill was known for favoring women at their shows, often with a "girls to the front, guys to the back" policy, and for demanding that their gigs be safe spaces for women. This ethos was a core tenet of the Riot Grrrl movement, establishing an empowering environment. "Favouring women at their shows. Check. Demanding that these gigs be safe spaces for women? Absolutely."
**Evergreen State College Roots** : Kathleen Hanna, a member of Bikini Kill, was a student at Evergreen State College in Olympia and was involved with an art gallery that occasionally hosted bands. This environment contributed to the nascent Riot Grrrl scene by providing a platform for emerging artists and ideas. "Kathleen was a student at Evergreen State College in Olympia, helping with an art gallery that sometimes hosted bands."
**Origin of Name "Bikini Kill"** : The name "Bikini Kill" came several iterations after Kathleen Hanna's first group, Amy Carter, and was named after a zine that she published with Tobi Vail. This reflects the DIY zine culture central to the Riot Grrrl movement and the band's feminist messaging. "Amy Kill came several iterations later and was named after a scene that she published with Toby."
**Spelling of "Riot Grrrl" Origin** : Kathleen Hanna and Tobi Vail published a zine in 1991 titled "Riot Girl" using the three 'R's spelling. The concept behind this specific spelling was to convey fierceness and reclaim the word "girl" by adding a "growl" sound. "But then we have another scene published in 1991 by Kathleen Hannah and Toby Vale. They called it Riot Girl with the three Rs."
**Longevity of Original Riot Grrrl Bands** : Bikini Kill was the only original Riot Grrrl band to continue performing beyond the mid-90s, lasting until 1997. Most other early bands like Bratmobile, Heavens to Betsy, and Huggy Bear had broken up by 1994 due to internal conflicts and issues surrounding media coverage. "The only original Riot Girl band to make it beyond all that was Bikini Kill. They lasted until 1997."
**Reunion and Continued Influence** : Bikini Kill has reunited, demonstrating the lasting impact of the band and the Riot Grrrl movement. Kathleen Hanna, a key member, has also pursued other projects, including documentary filmmaking and coaching at an all-girl music camp. "They have also reunited, but Kathleen has been very busy doing other things, including documentary filmmaking, acting as a coach at an all-girl music camp."

# genre - Punk Rock

## curiosities
**Mid-1970s Dogma** : The original punk rock of the mid-1970s was instrumental in challenging gender prejudices in music, promoting the idea that anyone with something to say should be able to express it, regardless of musical ability, social class, race, or sex. This ethos allowed many women to enter the scene and was a foundational inspiration for Riot Grrrl. "The original punk rock of the mid-1970s was a great help thanks to the movement's dogma that anyone with anything to say should be able to say it, regardless of musical ability, social class, race, or sex."

# genre - Riot Girl

## curiosities
**Emergence as a Female Response to Grunge** : Riot Grrrl bands emerged in the early 1990s, primarily as a female response to the male-dominated grunge scene. Women sought to create their own music about issues important to them, fighting back against "bro culture" and societal misogyny, and taking a DIY approach to all aspects of their music. "Riot Girl bands sprang up largely as a female response to Grunge."
**Peak in Early 1990s, Olympia Hub** : The Riot Grrrl movement and scene was at its peak in the early 1990s, with Olympia, Washington, located south of Seattle, identified as its primary root and home, analogous to Seattle's role for Grunge. This location became a critical incubator for the movement. "a scene and movement that was at its peak in the early 1990s. If Seattle was home to Grunge, you had to go south down Interstate 5 to Olympia, Washington to find where the Riot Girl thing took root."
**Manifesto from Puncture Zine (1989)** : In 1989, a zine called "Puncture" published an article titled "Women's Sex and Rock and Roll," which was widely distributed and considered a "manifesto for a burgeoning subculture" that deeply influenced the young women of the underground music scene in the Pacific Northwest. "For example, there was a Zine called Puncture, that in 1989 published an article entitled Women's Sex and Rock and Roll. It was distributed around the area until this was considered to be a manifesto for a burgeoning subculture."
**Coalescence of Ideas** : The Riot Grrrl attitude and sound coalesced from various influences, including "Sassy" magazine, local zines, the "Your Dream Girl" radio show on KAOS, and the existing music scene. It reflected a determination to move beyond being just "girlfriends of boys who go to punk shows" and instead actively create their own punk shows. "Taking all together, the magazine, the radio show, the music, the attitude coalesced into this."
**Core Principles at Shows** : A defining principle of Riot Grrrl shows was "girls to the front, guys to the back," and songs focused on issues pertinent to women. This created a distinct and empowering atmosphere, making gigs safe spaces for women. "When we play, it's girls to the front, guys to the back, and our songs are about things that matter to us."
**Definition of a Riot Grrrl Band** : Defining a Riot Grrrl band was "a tough question," as some identified with the term, others were associated due to playing Riot Grrrl-organized shows, and some were classified based on singing feminist themes over "ragged garage punk type music." Despite the ambiguity, contemporaries "knew it when [they] heard it." "So what made a band a Riot Girl band? That's a tough question."
**Early Scene Intensity and Decline (Mid-90s)** : The original Riot Grrrl scene was "very, very, very hot," characterized by its intensity, political nature, and emotional depth, which ultimately made it unsustainable for a long duration. By the mid-1990s, the scene "had blown up into a million different pieces." "The original Riot Girl scene burned very, very, very hot. Anything that intense, that political, that emotional just can't last for long."
**Causes of Decline: Media, Message, Co-option** : The decline of the original Riot Grrrl scene was attributed to several factors: differing attitudes towards media coverage, conflicting messages about its goals, and annoyance from some members regarding how their concept of "girl power" was "co-opted and watered down by mainstream acts like the Spice Girls." These internal and external pressures led to its fragmentation. "torn apart by different attitudes about media coverage or lack thereof. There were conflicting messages about what everybody was trying to accomplish and others were annoyed by how their idea of girl power had been co-opted and watered down by mainstream acts like the spice girls."
**Second Wave of Groups** : After the original scene fragmented, a "second wave of groups" emerged to carry on the mission of Riot Grrrl, including bands like Sleater-Kinney and Team Dresch. These groups continued the feminist and political messages of the movement, adapting them for a new era. "But there was also a second wave of groups that carried on with the mission."
**"Riot Grrrl Adjacent" Artists** : Poet Tracy Morse coined the term "Riot Girl Adjacent" to describe artists who had a kinship with the Riot Grrrl scene but were not direct members. These artists were often "a little more polished, not nearly as political and occasionally heavier and more riff happy," such as L7, Babes in Toyland, and Hole. "Before we wrap up, we should talk about what poet Tracy Morse calls the Riot Girl Adjacent."
**Lasting Impact** : Despite its relatively short active period, the original Riot Grrrl scene created a "new paradigm for fierce, uncompromising female-oriented rock." Its influence can still be felt throughout rock music today, with many disciples and ongoing impacts on female-driven music. "The original Riot Girl scene is long gone, its influence can still be felt throughout all rock."
**Longevity Comparison** : The Riot Grrrl movement, similar to original punk rock, had a concentrated impact in the "early to mid-90s" but created a lasting "new paradigm for fierce, uncompromising female-oriented rock," with its impacts still felt today. It proved that a scene doesn't need to last long to be profoundly influential. "And the same thing with what the Riot Girls did in the early to mid-90s."

# curiosity

**Prejudices Against Women in Rock** : Historically, it was a "common knowledge" prejudice that girls "just couldn't rock," lacked the "feel," were "built wrong," and it wasn't "in their DNA" like it was for guys. This prejudice took a long time to be defeated, highlighting the societal barriers women faced in music. "There was a time when it was common knowledge that girls just couldn't rock."
**Backsliding in the 80s** : After the initial inclusivity of 70s punk rock, the 1980s saw "backsliding" with genres like hardcore punk becoming "testosterone-driven bro rock," where women were "pushed to the back." This regression created a vacuum for a new female-led movement. "But then there were some backsliding in the 80s."
**Women Creating Their Own Space** : In response to male-dominated music scenes like grunge, a group of women became "determined to carve out their own space," creating music "about them and for them" that addressed issues they cared about and could rock as hard, or harder, than male-led bands. This self-determination was the genesis of the Riot Grrrl movement. "This time, though, a group of women were determined to carve out their own space."
**Initial Inspiration from Female Pioneers** : The Riot Grrrl movement drew initial inspiration from female pioneers of punk and post-punk, including The Slits, The Raincoats, Kim Gordon of Sonic Youth, Siouxsie Sioux ("Suzy Su"), Chrissie Hynde of The Pretenders, and Exene Cervenka of X, with Patti Smith considered the "godmother." These artists paved the way for future generations of women in rock. "Some initial inspiration came from female pioneers of punk and post-punk."
**Sassy Magazine's Influence** : The Riot Grrrl movement found some inspiration in "Sassy" magazine, a publication directed at teenage girls that, despite its target audience, "dealt with some pretty heavy subjects," contributing to the feminist discourse of the time. It provided a mainstream-accessible platform for critical thought. "One magazine was called Sassy, which, although directed at teenage girls, dealt with some pretty heavy subjects."
**Importance of Zines** : Zines, homemade magazines created on photocopiers and often distributed for free, were crucial to the do-it-yourself (DIY) scene and played a significant role in the American Pacific Northwest, where the zine culture was particularly "well organized and sophisticated," leading to the dissemination of proto-Riot Grrrl manifestos. This grassroots media was vital for communication and organization. "Even more important were the Zines. These were homemade magazines, created on things like photocopiers, and distributed to like-minded people often for free."
**Anita Hill Hearings (1991)** : The Anita Hill hearings, where she accused Supreme Court Justice Clarence Thomas of sexual harassment, served as a "tremendously polarizing" event across North America in 1991. This event poured "gasoline on things" for the burgeoning Riot Grrrl movement, galvanizing feminists to protest for Hill and against Thomas. "Women was the Anita Hill hearings when she accused Supreme Court Justice Clarence Thomas of sexual harassment, which became a tremendously polarizing thing across North America."
**Anti-Abortion Attacks and Pro-Choice Protests** : Several attacks on legal abortion rights in the early 1990s led to more pro-choice protests, which further galvanized and unified feminist activists, contributing to the context in which the Riot Grrrl movement flourished. These political struggles fueled the movement's urgency. "Then there were several attacks on legal abortion that meant more pro-choice protests. Again, galvanizing stuff."
**KAOS Radio Show "Your Dream Girl"** : In the Olympia area, a campus radio station called KAOS hosted a show named "Your Dream Girl," hosted by Lois Muffet (who later joined several bands). The program was specifically "aimed at angry young women," serving as a crucial outlet for the developing Riot Grrrl ethos and building community. "The show was called Your Dream Girl, and the host was Lois Muffet, who would later be in a bunch of bands. Her program was aimed at angry young women."
**Origin of the Term "Riot Girl"** : The precise origin of the term "Riot Girl" is uncertain. Possible inspirations include the 1991 Mount Pleasant disturbance (race riots in Washington, D.C.), Jen Smith of Bratmobile using "Girl Riot" in a letter, and Kathleen Hanna and Tobi Vail's 1991 zine titled "Riot Girl" with three 'R's, aiming to convey fierceness and reclaim the word "girl" with a "growl." These various influences converged to define the movement's name. "So why the name Riot Girl? We're not entirely sure."
**Spelling Variations of "Riot Girl"** : There were variations in the spelling of "Riot Girl," with some using two 'R's and no 'I' (Riot Grrl), while others, like Kathleen Hanna and Tobi Vail, insisted on three 'R's and no 'I' (Riot Grrrl), which was intended to add a "growl" to the word "girl" for fierceness. This deliberate spelling highlighted the movement's intention to redefine the word "girl." "Some spell Girl with two Rs and No I. Others insisted that it should be spelled with three Rs and No I."
**Lasting Impact of Brief Scenes** : The Riot Grrrl movement, much like the original punk rock explosion, demonstrates that a "scene and a sound doesn't have to be around for long, for it to have a lasting impact," creating a new paradigm for "fierce, uncompromising female-oriented rock" whose influence is still felt today. This idea challenges the notion that longevity is essential for profound cultural influence. "Sometimes, a scene and a sound doesn't have to be around for long, for it to have a lasting impact."
