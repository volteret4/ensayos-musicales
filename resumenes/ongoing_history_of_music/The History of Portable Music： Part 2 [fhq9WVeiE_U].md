# label - Fraunhofer Society

## curiosities
**MP3 Technology Development** : The Fraunhofer Society in Germany was responsible for developing the MP3 technology. Their initial goal was to find efficient ways to transmit large quantities of audio data over older copper telephone wires, which they achieved by creating an algorithm that compressed data by removing non-essential information. "This technology was developed by the frown-hofer society in Germany. Initially, they were looking for ways to send large amounts of audio data through old, telephone, copper wires. And the only way to do that was to compress the data using an algorithm that stripped out everything that was non-essential."

**MP3 Technology Licensing (1993)** : In 1993, the Fraunhofer Society licensed its MP3 compression technology broadly to various parties, contributing to its widespread adoption and impact on digital music. "Frown-hofer licensed this technology to everybody in 1993."

**Hockey Game Audio for MP3 Algorithm Testing** : To refine their MP3 compression algorithms, Fraunhofer engineers utilized raw audio recordings from hockey games as one of their two primary test sound sources. The complex and unique sounds produced in an arena, such as skates slashing ice and pucks hitting boards, presented a significant challenge for clean compression, prompting engineers to spend thousands of hours on this specific audio. "The first was, believe it or not, recording raw audio from hockey games. There was something about how hockey sounds in an arena with the skate slashing the ice and the puck booming off the boards that proved to be really tricky to compress cleanly. Frown-hofer engineers spent thousands of hours listening to the sounds of hockey and trying to compress them properly."

## curiosities
**Introduction of the MP3 Man (1997)** : In 1997, the South Korean company Sehan Information Systems launched the MP3 man, Model F10, which holds the distinction of being the first-ever portable MP3 player. It was available in 32MB and 64MB capacities and initially sold for prices ranging between $700 and $1000. "In 1997, a South Korean company called Sehan Information Systems introduced the first ever portable MP3 player, which they called the MP3 man, Model F10."

**MP3 Man Technology and Capacity** : The MP3 man featured flash memory, distinguishing it as the first portable music device since the transistor radio to contain no moving parts, thereby eliminating skips during playback. Music was transferred to the device by docking it to a computer via a parallel port. Its capacity varied with bitrate: the smaller unit could hold six uncompressed WAV files (or more at lower bitrates), while the larger unit could hold twelve. At the time, an hour's worth of music was considered sufficient for most users. "It used flash memory, meaning that unlike every other portable music device since the transistor radio, there were zero moving parts. It could not skip. You loaded it by docking the MP man in a docking port that you connected to your computer. RIP tracks could then be transferred over. Now many RIP tracks depended on the bit rate. If you transferred uncompressed wave files, the smaller unit could hold just six songs, and the bigger one, 12. But if you lowered the bit rate, the more music you could take out of your computer. ... With something like this device, an hour's worth of music was just about right for most people at that time."

**MP3 Man Power and Loading Speed** : The MP3 man operated on a single AA battery, and the process of loading music onto the device was notably slow due to its reliance on parallel port connections. "And the device ran on a single AA battery. So you can just imagine how long it took before you ran out of juice. It took a while to load because we were still using parallel port connections, which were awfully slow."

# label - Rhapsody

## curiosities
**Launch as First Streaming Platform (2001)** : Rhapsody debuted in December 2001 as the pioneering platform for music streaming. It offered subscribers unlimited access to its online music library for a monthly fee, a concept deemed radical by both record labels and music fans at a time when music ownership was the norm. "First, out of the gate was a platform called Rhapsody in December 2001. For a monthly fee, subscribers could stream as much of the company's online library as they wanted."

**Ownership vs. Access Challenge** : Rhapsody initially faced resistance from music fans and the industry due to its model of paying for access rather than ownership, meaning music access ceased upon subscription cancellation. This challenged the deeply ingrained "ownership mindset" that had characterized the music industry for its first century, where physical ownership was the only guarantee of on-demand listening. "This was way too radical for most record labels. And it was weird for music fans too. Wait, we're just accessing the music and we're not owning it? And when we stop paying our monthly fee, all the music goes away? Yeah, that's the way it was. Remember, though, that we were still locked into the ownership mindset back then. For the first hundred years of the music industry, the only way we could guarantee unlimited on-demand listening to music was to own it on a piece of plastic. Streaming was entirely virtual, and you didn't own it. You rent it. It took a long time for people to come around to this idea."

**Rebranding as Napster** : Rhapsody eventually acquired the intellectual property rights of the file-sharing service Napster and subsequently rebranded itself under the Napster name, becoming a legal streaming version of the formerly controversial platform. "Even Rhapsody, the original streaming music service, is still with us. Although after they bought the intellectual property rights of Napster, they rebranded themselves with that name. While both delivered high-quality sound, they were limited to playing pre-recorded discs, unlike cassettes which offered the flexibility of creating custom mix tapes. "In the 80s, portable CD players came in two flavors, the kind you installed in your car after you bought it and the fragile sort that you could walk around with. Both delivered great sound, but you were still stuck with pre-recorded disks."

**Cassettes vs. Portable CD Players** : Cassettes offered the distinct advantage of allowing users to create their own mix tapes, a capability not present in portable CD players, which relied exclusively on pre-recorded discs. This presented a trade-off between the creative freedom of mix tapes (despite requiring fast forwarding and rewinding) and the instant accessibility of digital tracks from CDs. "The big advantage of cassettes was that you could make your own mix tapes. **Mini Disk Format Details** : The Mini Disk format was described as an intermediary between a CD and an old 1.44 megabyte floppy disk. It consisted of a rotating magnetic disk encased in a plastic shell, played by insertion into a dedicated player. Depending on the version, a single Mini Disk could store 60, 74, or 80 minutes of digital CD-quality music, and notably, it featured recording capabilities. "The mini disk worked like this. A rotating magnetic disk was mounted inside a plastic case, which was played by inserting it into a slot or a bay of a dedicated player. Depending on the version, a single mini disk could hold 60, 74, or 80 minutes worth of data, digital CD quality music. And it essentially worked just like the CD except that you could record on it."

**Early MP3 Users and Hard Drive Limitations (Mid-90s)** : In the mid-1990s, the first users of MP3 technology were individuals who converted (ripped) CDs onto their computer hard drives. Given that a 100-megabyte hard drive was considered substantial at the time and a full CD contained 640 megabytes, MP3 compression was essential for saving space and enabling users to store a selective collection of music.
