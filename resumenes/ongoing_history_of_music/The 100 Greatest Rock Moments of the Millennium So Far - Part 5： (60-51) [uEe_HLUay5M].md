# artist - The White Stripes

## labels
Third Man Records

## albums
**Elephant (2003)** : This album featured the single "Seven Nation Army," released a year after its iconic riff was first conceived. The song's completion and inclusion on "Elephant" propelled it to become one of the band's most popular and critically acclaimed tracks. "The song was completed, added to the elephant album, and released as a single."

## songs
**Seven Nation Army (2003)** : The song's distinctive seven-note riff originated from Jack White during a soundcheck at the Corner Hotel in Melbourne, Australia, on January 29, 2002. Initially, it was met with skepticism by Ben Swank of Third Man Records, and Jack White briefly entertained the idea of it becoming a James Bond theme. A year later, it was developed further at Toe Rags, a primitive analog studio in England, beginning as a "little experiment" without a complete chorus or lyrics. The song was eventually completed, retaining its working title, and upon its release as a single from the "Elephant" album, it achieved massive radio success, critical acclaim, and a popular video. It won a Grammy for Best Rock Song and was nominated for Best Rock Performance, ultimately becoming the band's most popular song, a global sports anthem, and adopted by political movements in the UK and Israel. "It then won a Grammy for Best Rock song and was also nominated for Best Rock Performance."

## curiosities
**Riff Origin Anecdote** : Jack White spontaneously created the signature seven-note riff for "Seven Nation Army" during a soundcheck on January 29, 2002, at the Corner Hotel in Melbourne, Australia. He found the riff "cool" and played it repeatedly, even though Ben Swank from Third Man Records initially disliked it and suggested Jack "can do better." White even considered it for a James Bond theme before deciding to use it for a White Stripes song. "Jack White came up with a seven note riff, which he thought sounded cool, so he played it over and over and over again."

# concert - CBGB

## curiosities
**Club History and Punk Ground Zero** : Founded in 1969 by Hilly Crystal as "Hilly's on the Bowery" and re-opened on December 10, 1973, as CBGB and OMFUG at 315 Bowery in Lower Manhattan, the club initially aimed to feature Country, Bluegrass, and Blues. However, it swiftly transformed into the "ground zero" for punk music after the collapse of the Mercer Art Center in August 1973, which prompted avant-garde artists, including early punk bands like Television, The Ramones, Patti Smith, Suicide, Blondie, Talking Heads, and The Heartbreakers, to seek a new performance space. The venue subsequently gained global fame and became an enduring punk icon. "CBGB became ground zero for an emerging form of music called Punk."
**Closure Due to Rent Hikes (October 15, 2006)** : CBGB was forced to permanently close its doors on October 15, 2006, primarily due to an unsustainable increase in rent, which skyrocketed from $9,000 to $55,000 a month. Owner Hilly Crystal had briefly considered relocating the entire club, including its distinctive urinals, to Las Vegas, but these plans never materialized. # instrument - Backing Tracks

## curiosities
**Evolution of Live Performance Practice** : Backing tracks, which are pre-recorded audio elements used to supplement live performances, have evolved from an accepted practice for "filling in the sound" (as exemplified by Muse) to a widespread and often controversial phenomenon in pop, hip-hop, and even classic rock shows. Historically viewed as "cheating" and dishonest (e.g., Milli Vanilli, Ashlee Simpson), their modern acceptance is frequently attributed to audience expectations for "perfect studio quality" sound for their concert investment. Artists use them to reduce the need for large numbers of musicians, ensure consistency, synchronize with complex stage effects, and mask vocal deterioration or enable strenuous dancing. "But today, backing tracks and full blown lip syncing are everywhere and remarkably few people seem to be bothered by it."

# instrument - Auto-Tune

## curiosities
**Origin and Impact on Vocals** : Invented in 1997 by Andy Hildebrand of Antares Audio Technologies, who adapted his oil-searching technology to correct musical pitches, Auto-Tune was initially intended for subtle studio vocal corrections. Its use was radically transformed by Cher's "Believe" (1998), which maximized the effect to create a distinctive "robotic sound." This popularized Auto-Tune as an overt artistic tool, widely adopted by artists like Kanye, T-Pain, and Travis Scott for exaggerated effects. Its ubiquitous presence in modern recording has sparked debates about "fakery," the "humanity" being "squeezed out of music," and the value of intentional flaws, exemplified by Nirvana deliberately leaving a Kurt Cobain vocal error in a song. "Andy Hilderbrand, realized that his oil-searching tech could be used to correct musical pitches, including vocal performances that could be slightly off-key."
