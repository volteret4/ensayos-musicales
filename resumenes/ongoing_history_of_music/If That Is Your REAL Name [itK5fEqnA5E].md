## curiosities
**Uncredited Backing Band for Elvis Costello** : Clover was an American band that found itself broke while touring the UK. They were hired by Jake Riviera of Stiff Records to back Elvis Costello on his debut album, My Aim Is True, recording through late 1976 and early 1977 in a tiny studio in Islington, London. Due to contractual issues (another British band was already named Clover), they used the pseudonym The Sham Rocks and were never credited on the original album release. "So Riviera found a band called Clover, who had come over from America, and were pretty much broke as they tried to or the UK."
**Post-Breakup Member Destinies** : After Clover broke up in 1978, its members pursued various paths. One individual joined The Doobie Brothers before eventually quitting music to become a chiropractor. Another member achieved recognition by writing the song "867-5309/Jenny" for Tommy Tutone. Additionally, two members, along with Clover's singer (who had merely hung around during the Elvis Costello sessions), went on to form the successful band Huey Lewis and the News. "Clover broke up in 1978. One guy joined the Dubie Brothers before quitting music to become a chiropractor, and another guy wrote the song 867-5309-Geni for Tommy Tutone."

**I'm a Cult Hero (1979)** : This experimental seven-inch single was the A-side released by Cult Hero in 1979 on Fiction Records. Robert Smith deliberately chose not to sing on the track to obscure the band's true identity, instead enlisting his mailman, Frank Bell, for lead vocals. The song served as a "job application" to test out Simon Gallup as a potential new bassist for The Cure. It also had a Canadian single release in 1982. ## curiosities
**The Cure's "Job Application" Band** : Cult Hero was essentially The Cure performing under a pseudonym, specifically designed by Robert Smith as a way to test out new bassist Simon Gallup in 1979 without official commitment. Robert, Simon, Robert's two sisters, and other friends secretly recorded a single, "I'm a Cult Hero," with Robert's mailman, Frank Bell, on lead vocals to further conceal their identities. "That's essentially the cure trading as Cult Hero, with Robert Smith's postman on lead vocals."

# curiosity

**Paul McCartney's Influence on The Ramones' Name** : Paul McCartney famously used the pseudonym "Paul Ramone" when checking into hotels to avoid unwanted attention. This practice later served as the direct inspiration for the band Ramones when they were seeking a name for their own group. "Well, when he used to sign into hotels, he used the name Paul Ramone. So it was not to raise any suspicions and when the band Ramones were looking for a name, that's where they got their inspiration."
**Music Industry Funding for Creative Whims (1980s-1990s)** : In the 1980s and 1990s, the music industry had significantly more money circulating, which allowed record companies to indulge artists' creative whims, even when it involved releasing albums under fake names or obscured identities purely for fun. This financial climate facilitated projects like The Dukes of Stratosphear (XTC) and Passengers (U2). "But like I've been saying, back in the 90s there were still lots of money floating around in the music industry, and if your artist had a creative whim, you indulged it."
