## albums
**Do Little (1989)** : This album, released in 1989, is today considered a classic of Alt Rock. ## songs
**Debaser (1989)** : This song from their 1989 album "Do Little" played a pivotal role in inspiring Perry Farrell to create Lollapalooza. Farrell witnessed 30,000 people jumping and screaming to "Debaser" at the 1990 Reading Festival in England, prompting his vision for a similar festival experience in America. "He saw 30,000 kids jumping up and down, screaming their brains out to Debacer by the Pixies."

## curiosities
**Limited Fanbase, Major Influence** : Despite not having a large fanbase at the time, The Pixies were highly regarded by influential figures. "But there were a few key people who thought they were amazing."

## albums
**Goo (1990)** : Released on June 26, 1990, this was Sonic Youth's major label debut with DGC Records, a subsidiary of Universal. Despite concerns from their indie fanbase about "selling out," the album was scrutinized and found to be as noisy and non-mainstream as their previous independent releases. "But the truth was that goo was every bit as noisy and as non-mainstream as anything they had done up until that point."

## curiosities
**Most Respected Indie Band** : By 1990, Sonic Youth was widely regarded as perhaps the most respected indie band in America, known for selling a decent number of records to their non-mainstream alternative rock constituency. "By 1990, they were probably the most respected indie band in America."
**Major Label Signing for Security** : Facing financial difficulties despite their respect and decent record sales, Sonic Youth made the "tough call" to sign with a major label, DGC (a subsidiary of Universal Records), in 1990. Their decision was driven by the desire for security and benefits like health care, rather than a large upfront payment, which was not yet common for alternative bands. "We went to DGC Records because we sort of wanted the security. We were able to get health care by being on a major label."
**Major Label Debut Scrutiny** : Their move to DGC shocked and horrified some indie fans who feared they had "sold out" to the establishment, particularly as DGC was also the label for artists like Cher and Aerosmith. However, their major label debut, "Goo," proved to be as noisy and non-mainstream as their earlier work. "And when it came out, it was scrutinized by the faithful for any sign that Sonic Youth had been tamed by the establishment."
**Influence on Nirvana's Label Choice** : Sonic Youth played a direct role in Nirvana's decision to sign with DGC. Having just signed with DGC themselves and having toured with Nirvana, they advised Kurt Cobain and the band that the label and its staff, including Ray Farrell from SST Records, seemed "pretty cool." "I think when the guys at DGC went to sort of talk to them about doing a record with them, we were, we had just signed, yeah. And we had toured with them. And they were like, we said it was, you know, it seems pretty cool."

## albums
**Bleach (1990)** : Nirvana's first album for Subpop, "Bleach," was recorded in June 1990. The total cost to produce the album was a mere $606.17, showcasing the band's humble beginnings. "In June of 1990, they recorded Bleach, their first album for Subpop. Total cost to make $606.17."
**Nevermind (1991)** : This album, produced by Butch Vig, was released on September 24, 1991, with an initial distribution of 46,251 copies to record stores. The band initially hoped to sell around 200,000 copies, but "Nevermind" became an unprecedented success, selling 300,000 copies a week by Christmas. It sold its first million copies in just 65 days and doubled that figure in another 41 days, ultimately staying on the Billboard charts for 92 uninterrupted weeks and generating over $100 million in gross revenues. "Released to radio on August 27th, 1991, and on album to everyone on September the 24th, 1991, smells like teen spirits. The album of course was never mind."
**Nevermind (1991) - Cultural Impact** : "Nevermind" was a pivotal album that exploded everything that had been building in indie and alternative music throughout the 1980s. It refined the sound pioneered by bands like Husker Dew and Sonic Youth, incorporating influences from Kiss, Black Sabbath, and The Beatles. It was one of the first alternative records to achieve success on both radio and MTV, establishing Nirvana as a bridge between the underground and mainstream rock scenes. "Everything that had been building through the 1980s, as far as indie and alternative and underground was concerned, exploded with this record."

## songs
**Smells Like Teen Spirit (1991)** : Released to radio on August 27, 1991, this song was Track One on the "Nevermind" album and was initially intended as a "throw away" single to "soften people up" for what the band believed would be the album's main hit, Track Three. However, it unexpectedly became a massive success and a defining anthem of the grunge movement. "It was decided that Track One would be the first single, song that would kind of soften people up for what was going to be the real hit from the album, which was Track Three. However, it didn't quite work out as planned. That throw away for single ended up, well you know."

## curiosities
**Melvins Fandom and Early Career** : Kurt Cobain, a stoner and former janitor, was such a dedicated fan of The Melvins that he would sometimes roadie for them for free, highlighting his deep roots in the local Pacific Northwest music scene before Nirvana's rise. "Nirvana, featuring of course Kurt Cobain, a stoner and former janitor who was such a big fan of the melvins that he's sometimes rowdy for them for free."
**Subpop Singles Club Debut** : Nirvana's very first single was featured as the inaugural release of the Subpop Singles Club, which began arriving in subscribers' mailboxes in November of 1988, marking one of their earliest official recordings. "The very first single of the month started arriving in mailboxes in November of 1988. It was from an obscure trio from the tiny logging town of Aberdeen, Washington. They were called Nirvana."
**Settling on Dave Grohl** : By October 1990, Nirvana solidified its lineup by finally settling on Dave Grohl, a musician from Washington, D.C., as their permanent drummer. "By October of 1990, they had finally settled on a drummer, this kid from DC named Dave Grohl."
**Move to DGC Records (1991)** : Facing financial constraints from Subpop despite high hopes for the band, Nirvana signed a contract with DGC on January 4, 1991. Their decision was influenced by Sonic Youth's earlier move to the same label, with Kurt Cobain believing if it was good enough for Sonic Youth, it was good enough for Nirvana. "So on January 4th, 1991, it was announced that Nirvana had signed a contract with DGC. Now why DGC? Well, like Thurston Moore said, it was because Sonic Youth had chosen that company as their major label home."
**Producer Butch Vig Collaboration** : Following their signing with DGC, Nirvana spent nine months working on their new album, "Nevermind," with producer Butch Vig, a collaboration that proved instrumental in shaping their breakthrough sound. "The next nine months were spent working on a new album with producer Butch Vic."
**Unexpected Global Phenomenon** : Nirvana's transformation from an unknown cult band from Aberdeen, Washington, into one of the biggest and most important bands in rock history was an "fabulous artistic, economic, demographic, and sociological question." Their rise coincided with Generation X's disillusionment, a recession, and a desire for authentic, anti-star musicians. "But we have to ask, how did this unknown cult band from nowhere Washington suddenly become not only one of the biggest bands in the world, but also one of the most important in the history of rock?"
**Punk Credentials Maintained** : Even after achieving multi-platinum success, Nirvana managed to retain the support of alternative music fans due to their "impeccable punk rock credentials." This authenticity allowed them to bridge the gap between underground and mainstream audiences. "And with impeccable punk rock credentials, Nirvana continued to enjoy the support of alternative music fans even after they went multi-platinum."
**British Early Champions** : It is often overlooked that some grunge bands, most notably Nirvana, were initially championed and supported more by British audiences than by Americans. "What's forgotten in all this is that some Grunge bands, most notably Nirvana, were first champions not by Americans, but by the British."

# genre - Alt Rock

## curiosities
**Seismic Years of Transformation (1990-1991)** : The years 1990 and 1991 are described as "absolutely crucial" and "every bit as seismic" as the birth and rise of punk in 1976 and 1977 for the history of Alt Rock. This period marked a significant turning point, especially with the explosion of alternative music into the mainstream. "The years 1990 and 1991 are absolutely crucial to the history of Alt Rock. They are every bit as seismic as what we saw with the birth and rise of punk in 1976 and 1977."
**Birth of the Alternative Nation** : The concept of the "alternative nation," coined by Perry Farrell, was born around 1991, signifying a growing movement where alternative music gained significant traction and began to challenge mainstream rock. This period marked a rapid shift from niche appeal to a broader cultural phenomenon. "The alternative nation was being born more in a moment."
**Alternative Rule (1991-1995)** : From mid-1991 through the end of 1995, alternative rock, fueled by grunge at its core and sustained by various other genres, dominated the music scene. This era introduced Generation X to a wide array of subgenres, paralleling the "endless" supply of fresh bands during the New Wave era. "And for the next five years, Alt Rock ruled, spread by the ideals of Lola Palusa and fueled by Grunge at its core and sustained by all kinds of other music."
**Mainstream Acceptance (1994)** : By 1994, alternative rock had grown so large that it was no longer an "alternative" but had supplanted traditional rock players and become the mainstream itself. This shift, while not immediately problematic, signaled a significant change in the rock music landscape. "Things got so big that by 1994, alternative rock wasn't so much of an alternative anymore. It had supplanted the traditional players in rock and had become the mainstream."
**North American vs. British Scenes** : The history of alt-rock in 1990-1991 must be viewed through the lens of developments in both North America and Britain, as distinct forces and movements were shaping the genre on both continents. "And again, we have to divide things between what was happening in North America versus what was going on in Britain."
**Diverse Genre Introduction** : Through the mainstream success of grunge and grunge-like music, Generation X was exposed to a multitude of genres including industrial, goth, punk-funk, jangle-pop, hardcore, dream-pop, and old folk, broadening their musical horizons. "Through Grunge and Grunge-like music, Gen X at large was introduced to industrial, goth, punk-funk, jangle-pop, hardcore, dream-pop, old folk, and a million other genres."

# genre - Grunge

## curiosities
**Seattle Origins and Unique Scene** : Grunge emerged from Seattle, a city distinct from the rest of the West Coast due to its geographical distance from other major American centers, fostering a unique arts and culture scene. Locals consumed rock differently, embracing a blend of traditional hard rock, hardcore punk, and noisy indie influences. "Well, Seattle is in the same time zone as Los Angeles. # label - Musak Corporation

## curiosities
**Origin in "Wired Radio"** : The Musak Corporation originated from Brigadier General George Owen Squire's invention of "wired radio," a technology designed to compete with wireless radio by delivering music directly into homes and businesses via wires. Squire later renamed his invention "Music," playing on the words "music" and "Kodak." "Back before he died, in 1934, he came up with a new name for his invention. **Business Model and Success** : While wired radio didn't appeal to consumers, businesses embraced the concept of "close circuit music," tailored to their environment 24 hours a day without interruption or static. The company became the world's biggest supplier of "elevator music," earning over $400 million a year by the 1970s distributing music worldwide from its Seattle headquarters. "Back before he died, in 1934, he came up with a new name for his invention. Playing with the words music and Kodak as in Kodak film, he came up with music."

"In fact, for a time, music executives thought this was a really good, unofficial slogan. Boring work is made less boring by boring music."

**Brigadier General George Owen Squire's "Wired Radio"** : Born March 21, 1865, Brigadier General George Owen Squire, an Army officer with a PhD in electrical science, invented "wired radio" to compete with wireless radio. He considered wireless radio "useless" due to static and fadeouts, envisioning a system like modern cable TV or early telephones to deliver music via wires into homes and businesses. "He invented the technology designed to compete with this new thing called radio. Wireless radio, he figured, was absolutely useless."
**Squire's "Music" Invention and Musak's Origins** : Before his death in 1934, George Owen Squire coined the term "Music" for his wired radio invention, combining "music" and "Kodak" (as in Kodak film). While the consumer market didn't adopt wired radio, businesses found closed-circuit music appealing, leading to the Musak Corporation becoming the world's largest supplier of "elevator music" and earning over $400 million annually by the 1970s. "Back before he died, in 1934, he came up with a new name for his invention. Playing with the words music and Kodak as in Kodak film, he came up with music."
**1990-1991: A Seismic Shift in Alt Rock** : The years 1990 and 1991 were "absolutely crucial" and "seismic" for the history of Alt Rock, marking a transformation as significant as the birth of punk in 1976-1977. This period saw a divide between North American and British developments, with pivotal decisions like Sonic Youth signing a major label deal impacting the trajectory of the genre. "The years 1990 and 1991 are absolutely crucial to the history of Alt Rock. They are every bit as seismic as what we saw with the birth and rise of punk in 1976 and 1977."
**Generation X's Disillusionment and the Rise of Grunge** : The early 1990s saw a brutal recession and the coming-of-age of Generation X (ages 15-25), many of whom were overeducated, underemployed, disillusioned, jaded, and scared about their future. This demographic, facing the first Gulf War and a third consecutive Republican presidency, sought authentic music that reflected their anger and anxieties, rejecting the perceived meaninglessness of 80s pop, hair metal excess, and their parents' classic rock. "Much of Gen X was overeducated and under-employed. There was a real concern amongst the members of Generation X that they would not be able to achieve a standard of living equal to or better than their parents."
**Political Climate and Music Trends** : Historical patterns suggest that during Republican presidencies, music tends to become heavier, angrier, and more rock-oriented. The early 1990s, following two terms of Ronald Reagan and into George H.W. Bush's presidency, saw Generation X embracing this trend, further fueling the rise of grunge and alternative music. "And if you match up history with politics, you'll see that whenever there's a Republican in the White House, music tends to turn heavier, angrier, and more rocky."
**Gen X's Rejection of Mainstream Music** : Generation X actively rejected the music popular with their parents and older siblings, including the "one long parade of classic rock bands" like Van Halen and Aerosmith, as well as the "meaningless, soulless crap" of late 80s pop (New Kids on the Block, Tiffany, Debbie Gibson) and the "excess, makeup, costumes, and endless stupid power ballads" of hair metal. They craved authentic music that addressed their specific attitudes and issues. "Gen X was not going to buy into this. They wanted music of their own, music by their own people that dealt with their attitudes and issues."
**Slacker Culture and Anti-Stardom** : The early 1990s embraced a "slacker culture" that blended apathy with egalitarianism, valuing authenticity, humbleness, and street cred in bands. Stardom was to be shunned, avoided, and downplayed, reflecting a return to the punk ethos where barriers between performer and audience were dismantled, and music was made by "regular people, for regular people." "Slacker culture with its strange mix of apathy and egalitarianism took over. It was supposed to be about the people, about the fans, and about real, honest music."
**Music Industry's Recession Boost (1991)** : The music industry, struggling with a recession like other sectors, received a desperate and much-needed boost from the emergence of grunge in 1991. The genre offered rock music with both "loads of street cred and a consumer-friendly sound," providing a commercial face for alternative music. "This was exactly the boost for which the music industry was desperate. Like everything else, it was being hit by the recession. Grunge was just what it needed."
**1991: The Greatest Year in Rock (in Retrospect)** : In retrospect, 1991 is considered one of the greatest years in the history of rock due to the simultaneous breakthrough of three pivotal grunge albums: Pearl Jam's "Ten" (August 8), Nirvana's "Nevermind" (September 24), and Soundgarden's "Bad Motor Finger" (October 8). At the time, however, the full impact of these events was not yet understood. "In retrospect, this makes 1991 look like one of the greatest years in the history of rock. Well, maybe, but only in retrospect. In all honesty, we had no idea what was going on at the time."
**British Reaction to Grunge (1991-1995)** : The immense success and global reach of grunge and its related subgenres between mid-1991 and late 1995 posed a significant "problem for certain people in the UK." The British music scene felt threatened by what was perceived as American dominance, leading to a defensive cultural movement known as Britpop, designed to "repulse" the "yanks." "Grunge and its cousins were certainly a problem for certain people in the UK. And this is what we're headed for on Chapter 12 of our complete history of Alt Rock."
