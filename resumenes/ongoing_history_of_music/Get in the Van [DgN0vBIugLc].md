## curiosities
**Stage Performance Motivation** : Shirley Manson finds stepping on stage to be like a "bolt in the arm," instantly rejuvenating and energizing her. This feeling makes all the "weird things" and difficulties of touring worthwhile, especially when she sees the lit-up faces of the fans enjoying the show. "The minute you step on stage, it's like a bolt in the arm."
**Russian Customs Incident (1999)** : On February 4, 1999, Garbage faced problems with Russian customs agents when attempting to cross the border to perform in Tallinn, Estonia. Their road cases were stenciled with the band's name, "Garbage," which Russian customs officials interpreted literally as a declaration of "trash" for export. Despite the band's repeated explanations that it was their name, the agents refused to clear them, leading to the cancellation of their show in Tallinn. "The kids in Tallinn, Estonia did not get their garbage show that night."

**Van Theft & Polish Mafia (Poland)** : While on tour in Poland one Christmas, NoMeansNo had their rented van stolen after a show in Warsaw, containing about $10,000 worth of merchandise. With local police being unhelpful and the Dutch rental company concerned about insurance coverage outside Holland, a fan connected them with the Polish Mafia. Band member Rob Wright met two heavily armed men in a church basement and negotiated the van's return for 2000 German marks. The next day, the van and all its contents were recovered from a barn outside Warsaw. However, the Warsaw police later demanded another 2000 German marks for operating outside legal channels. "The next day the van was given the name of a village about 100 kilometers outside of Warsaw and told to wait in the town square."

**Spanish Bus Crash & Dryden Mitchell's Injury (2002)** : In May 2002, Alien Ant Farm's bus crashed into a parked car about 100 miles outside Madrid, Spain. The driver was killed instantly, and the band suffered multiple broken bones. Lead singer Dryden Mitchell sustained the most severe injury, breaking his C2 vertebrae, an injury compared to Christopher Reeve's. He underwent fusion surgery, had to wear a halo brace on his head for three months (secured by screws drilled into his skull), and endured months of therapy. He still experiences nerve damage, including reduced feeling in his fingertips, making guitar playing very difficult, and temporary arm numbness if he moves his head down. "The most seriously injured was singer Dryden Mitchell. He broke his neck. Vertabrae C2, the same one that paralyzed actor Christopher Reeve."

**Dave Williams' Death on Tour (2002)** : On August 14, 2002, during Ozzfest, Drowning Pool's lead singer Dave Williams was found dead in his bunk on the tour bus, having died in his sleep. His death was attributed to cardiomyopathy, a disorder affecting the heart muscle often caused by viral infections or high blood pressure, thus determined to be from natural causes. The band later released a memorial DVD, with proceeds directed towards achieving Williams' dream of buying his parents a house. "He died of something called cardiomyopathy, a disorder that affects the heart muscle caused by things like viral infections or high blood pressure."

# curiosity

**Tour Finance and Advances** : When signing a recording contract, bands often immediately go into debt due to an advance check from the record company, which can range from a few thousand to several million dollars. This money is intended to cover expenses such as new gear, stage clothes, album recording, and tour financing (including transportation, accommodation, and road crew). An advance is a loan that must be repaid, typically through album sales, meaning a band may not see any profit from album sales for years as the record company "claws back" the advance. Touring is a strategy to counteract this debt by encouraging album sales and generating direct income from tickets, cover charges, bar sales percentages, and merchandise like t-shirts, which bands get to keep. "An advance is nothing more than a loan that has to be repaid."
**Touring Van Experience** : The cheapest and most common method for bands, especially in their early careers, to travel between gigs is by using a used van. These vehicles are often described as crowded, stinky, and prone to breaking down, yet they are the starting point for virtually every band. "The cheapest way is with a used van. That's virtually every band gets going."
**Tour Bus Experience** : Graduating from a van to a tour bus is considered a significant upgrade, offering more comfort, reduced accommodation costs, and providing a private, sanctuary-like space that becomes a home for months. Tour coaches can range from basic setups with bunks, a TV, and VCR to luxurious "penthouses on wheels" equipped with full bathrooms, complete kitchens, video game systems, and state-of-the-art audio/video systems with satellite dishes that function even when moving at 60 mph. "If your career is going well enough, you may be able to graduate from traveling in a van to your own tour bus."
**Newell Coach 2002 Tour Bus** : The 2002 Newell Coach is identified as the tour bus of choice for those with sufficient funds, costing $1.5 million. This luxury vehicle functions as a 450-square-foot "condo on wheels," featuring a 500-horsepower diesel engine, one bedroom, one and a half baths, a Marantz home theater system with two 40-inch plasma screens, a twin satellite system operational at 60 miles an hour, a full kitchen, mood lighting, and seating for groupies. "If you have the coin, the tour bus of choice right now is the 2002 Newell Coach."
**General Tour Bus Accident (2002)** : In February 2002, a band's bus was rolling through Kentucky when a front-end tire blowout caused it to swerve violently across the highway. Fortunately, the driver was able to bring the vehicle under control, and there was no other traffic nearby, preventing what could have been a much more serious accident. "There's bus was rolling through Kentucky in February 2002 when there was a front end tire blowouts and he gets swirving all over the highway."
**Christopher Reeve Context** : Christopher Reeve, the actor famous for playing Superman, is mentioned to provide a clear context for the severity of Dryden Mitchell's C2 vertebrae injury. Mitchell's injury was the same type of vertebral damage that paralyzed Reeve, highlighting its life-altering potential. "Vertabrae C2, the same one that paralyzed actor Christopher Reeve."
**Bus Tour Dangers and Accidents** : Life on the road for musicians is fraught with dangers, especially when driving at night, in all kinds of weather, and through various traffic conditions, often while heavy on the gas to make up for lost time. Inevitable mechanical failures further contribute to the risk, leading to situations where "things go really, really wrong and people get hurt," including severe injuries and fatalities for band members, crew, and drivers. "Sometimes things go really, really wrong and people get hurt."
