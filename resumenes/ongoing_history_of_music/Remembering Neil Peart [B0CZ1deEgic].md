## curiosities
**Early Drumming Beginnings** : Neil Peart was born in Hamilton on September 12, 1952, and his first home was on the family dairy farm near Hager'sville. He started drumming at an early age, reportedly using chopsticks on his sister's playpen. The family later moved to St. Catharines, where at age 13, Neil began taking formal drum lessons using practice pads. After a year of committed practice, his parents bought him his first proper red drum kit. "Neil apparently liked to drum chopsticks on his sister's playpin."
**Early Influences** : While growing up and playing in local bands, Neil Peart was deeply influenced by prominent drummers across different genres. He admired Keith Moon of The Who, Ginger Baker of Cream, John Bonham of Led Zeppelin, and Mitch Mitchell of The Jimi Hendrix Experience. Additionally, he was fascinated by big band drummers such as Gene Krupa and Buddy Rich. "He loved Keith Moon of the Who, ginger baker of cream, John Bonham of Zeppelin, and Mitch Mitchell of Jimmy Hendrix's groove."
**Attempt to Break into UK Music Scene** : At 18 years old in 1970, Neil Peart moved to England with the ambition of entering its vibrant music scene. Although this attempt did not result in a musical breakthrough, he gained valuable experience and learned a lot. By 1972, approximately 18 months later, he returned home to St. Catharines, where he worked at his father's farm equipment dealership while continuing to play in local bands. "Neil moved to England to see if you could get involved in the music scene there. That didn't work out, but he learned a lot."
**Rush Audition and Unique Appearance** : In 1974, Neil Peart auditioned for Rush as one of five drummers, replacing founding member John Rutsey who was leaving due to health issues and a musical mismatch. At the audition, Alex Lifeson and Geddy Lee initially found Neil's appearance "goofy" because he had short hair during a time when long hair was fashionable. He also brought an unusual Slingerland double bass drum kit, notable for its unusually small bass drums. "Alex and Gettie thought Neil looked goofy."
**Joining Rush** : Neil Peart formally became a member of Rush on July 29th, 1974, a date which coincided with Geddy Lee's 21st birthday. He secured the position after auditioning, replacing founding drummer John Rutsey. His first gig with the band was shortly after, on August 14th, 1974, in Pittsburgh, where Rush opened for Uriah Heep and Manfred Mann. "He got the job and formally became a member of Rush on July 29th, 1974, which just happened to be Gettie Lee's 21st birthday."
**Personality and Public Persona** : Despite his powerful and loud drumming style, Neil Peart was known as the "quiet one" within Rush, often described as an introvert and a "reluctant drumming institution." He maintained a very private life, with interviews being rare, and fan meet-and-greets typically handled by Alex Lifeson and Geddy Lee. He was highly articulate, extremely well-read, a deep thinker, and possessed a wicked sense of humor, earning unending respect for his abilities as a drummer, lyricist, and writer. "For someone who played so loud and so hard, Neil was the quiet one."
**Health Struggles and Retirement from Touring** : Neil Peart faced significant health challenges late in his career, including arthritis, carpal tunnel syndrome, chronic tendonitis, shoulder problems, and lower back issues—common ailments for hard-hitting drummers. A severe foot infection made playing Rush's R-42R tour very painful. He initially resisted embarking on this final tour, agreeing only after being persuaded, stating, "one more time and then I'm out." His last show was in Los Angeles on August 1st, 2015, after which he announced his retirement from Rush and from drumming entirely. "Neil hadn't been well, but it was all about arthritis and carpal tunnel, two things that hard-hitting drummers often suffer after years behind the kit."
**Reflections on Drumming as an Athlete** : In December 2015, Neil Peart authored an article for *Drumhead* magazine titled "Neil Peart Reflects on 50 Years of Hitting Things with Sticks," where he discussed the physically demanding nature of his craft. He acknowledged drumming as largely an "athletic undertaking" and recognized that, like all athletes, there comes a time to step away from the game. He preferred to retire rather than face the decline described in Rush's song "Losing It" from the 1982 *Signals* album. "The reality is that my style of drumming is largely an athletic undertaking."
**Personal Tragedies and Temporary Retirement from Rush** : The late 1990s were an exceptionally difficult period for Neil Peart, leading to a temporary retirement from Rush. On August 10, 1997, his 19-year-old daughter, Selina, died in a single-car accident while driving to university. Just five months later, his common-law wife of 23 years, Jackie, passed away from cancer. This double trauma prompted him to temporarily step away from the band to cope, embarking on a motorcycle tour across North America to help process his grief. "An August 10th, 1997, Neil's 19 year old daughter, Selina, died in a single car accident."
**Post-Trauma Writing Career and Remarriage** : Following his temporary retirement from Rush due to personal tragedies, Neil Peart entered a new phase of his writing career. He produced memoirs, novels, book reviews, essays, and later, blogs, with books being a favorite subject on his website under the heading "Bubba's Book Club." He also became widely known as an avid motorcyclist. This hiatus proved beneficial, as he remarried to Kerry in 2000 and subsequently returned to Rush to record the *Vapor Trails* album in 2001. "The result was a new phase of his writing career, with memoirs, novels, book reviews, essays, and soon after blogs."
**Drumming Style Evolution and Lessons** : In 1994, Neil Peart sought further drumming instruction from teacher Freddie Gruber, an experience that significantly refined his technique. He altered his stick-holding method, adopted different movements, and evolved his playing style, often discovering that "less was more." This refinement contributed to his highly musical approach to drumming, which extended beyond mere timekeeping to include a wide array of percussion instruments and fluid movements. "In 1994, he went back to taking lessons from a teacher named Freddie Groober."
**Private Battle with Brain Cancer** : Sometime in 2016, Neil Peart was diagnosed with glioblastoma, a form of brain cancer that he battled for over three years. His illness was kept entirely private, with family, friends, and management closing ranks to respect his privacy. He passed away quietly on January 7, 2020, at the age of 67. The announcement of his death was delayed until January 10, maintaining a low-key and dignified farewell, true to his introverted nature. "sometime in 2016, Neil was diagnosed as having brain cancer. He battled it for more than three years."
**Diverse Interests** : Beyond his musical contributions, Neil Peart harbored a wide range of intellectual and personal interests. He had a profound love for motorcycles and a passion for big band music. He was also a hockey fan, enjoyed reading books extensively, and was dedicated to studying philosophy. "He loved to read books and study philosophy."

## albums
**Fly By Night (1975)** : This was Rush's second album, released on February 15th, 1975. It was recorded over five days in the same month that the band played their first Canadian show with Neil Peart. The album marked a significant shift in the band's musical direction, moving towards more complex arrangements compared to their debut. The record label initially expressed dissatisfaction with this new sound, preferring more heavy blues rock akin to Led Zeppelin, similar to songs like "In the Mood" and "Working Man" from their first album. "Fly by night was recorded over five days later that month."
**Caress of Steel (unknown year)** : This album, Rush's third, was described as "something of a bomb." Its commercial failure placed significant pressure on the band's subsequent record, *2112*, which had to be a hit to prevent the risk of losing their record deal. "Their third album, Currestive Steel, was something of a bomb."
**2112 (unknown year)** : This album was a "make or break" record for Rush, following the commercial disappointment of their third album, *Caress of Steel*. It proved to be a major success, described as "hitting it out of the park." The entire first side of the album is a concept piece, blending Prog Rock with sci-fi themes. It draws inspiration from Ayn Rand's 1937 novel *Anthem*, portraying a dystopian society where music and individualism are outlawed. The album's narrative is set in the future city of Megadon, where priests and computers suppress creativity, before the Solar Federation and an unnamed entity take control. The album is fundamentally a song cycle about the rediscovery of music. "This was a maker break record for Rush."
**Farewell to Kings (1977)** : Released in 1977, this album features the multi-part song "Cygnus X-1," which showcases Rush's musical chops and Neil Peart's lyrical abilities. The track's narrative about an astronaut exploring black holes was inspired by a story Neil Peart read in *Time* magazine. "Next track on my list of Neil Peer Decentral is from the Farewell the Kings album in 1977."
**Hemispheres (1978)** : Released in 1978, this album features "Cygnus X-1 Book II," which concludes the narrative begun in "Cygnus X-1" from *Farewell to Kings*. It also includes "La Villa Strangiato," Rush's first purely instrumental piece, composed of 12 distinct movements. The recording of "La Villa Strangiato" alone took longer than the entire *Fly By Night* album. "Signus X1 Book 2 on the Hemisphere's album in 1978."
**Permanent Waves (1980)** : Released in 1980, this album marked a significant commercial success for Rush, becoming their biggest album to that point. It features the song "The Spirit of Radio," which was inspired by Toronto radio station CFNY FM, then known by its slogan "The Spirit of Radio" while operating from a small yellow house in Brampton, Ontario. "Permanent Waves was Rush's biggest album to that point."
**Moving Pictures (1981)** : Released in 1981, this album is considered the band's best-selling record. It achieved significant sales, approximately one million copies in Canada, five million in the US, and many more worldwide. The album produced several major rock radio hits, including "Tom Sawyer" and "YYZ." "I think 1981's Moving Pictures is the band's best selling record."
**Signals (1982)** : This album, released in 1982, features the songs "Losing It" and "Subdivisions." "Losing It" is referenced by Neil Peart in an article where he likened his drumming career to an athletic undertaking, stating he'd rather retire than face the predicament described in the song's lyrics, which contemplate the sadness of watching one's dreams die. "Subdivisions" is noted for being the first keyboard-based Rush song and offers an autobiographical take on suburban culture and social conformity. "That's a track from the 1982 signals album."
**Power Windows (unknown year)** : This album features the song "The Big Money," which addresses themes of corporate greed prevalent in the 1980s. Neil Peart's lyrics for the song were inspired by a 1936 novel from John Dos Passos, part of a trilogy depicting 12 characters navigating the Great Depression, with Peart adapting these sentiments to the global economy of his time. "Take for example this track from Power Windows."
**Hold Your Fire (1987)** : Released in 1987, this album includes the song "Time Stand Still." The lyrics are autobiographical for Neil Peart, reflecting on the band's nearly 20-year career by 1993 and the passing of time spent on the road. The song is a plea to appreciate the present moment and a warning against excessive nostalgia, born from Peart's reflection on neglecting friends and family during intense touring in the 1970s. "The track ended up on the 1987 album Hold Your Fire."
**Roll the Bones (1991)** : Released in 1991, this album features the instrumental track "Where's My Thing?" This song served as a vehicle for Neil Peart to showcase his extensive drum solos during live performances, allowing him to "bust out" with his percussive virtuosity. "And there's an instrumental on the Roll the Bones album from 1991 until Where's My Thing?"
**Vapor Trails (2001)** : Released in 2001, this album marked Neil Peart's return to Rush after a temporary retirement caused by profound personal tragedies in the late 1990s. His return followed a period of motorcycle touring and a new phase in his writing career, culminating in his remarriage to Kerry in 2000. "Neil remarried to a woman named Kerry in 2000, and soon returned to Rush to record the vapor trails album in 2001."
**Clockwork Angels (2012)** : Released in 2012, this album demonstrated Rush's continued ability to "seriously rock in a very modern and contemporary way" even as its members were approaching their senior years. The album highlights Neil Peart's evolved drumming style, which had been refined through lessons he began taking again in 1994, emphasizing a "less was more" approach to his playing. "I want to skip way ahead to the Clockwork Angels album in 2012."

## songs
**By-Tor & The Snow Dog (1975)** : Featured on the *Fly By Night* album, this song is noted for Neil Peart's drumming, particularly three distinct drum breaks that have been described as captivating listeners and inspiring drummers. "Bytour and the Snow Dog from Rush's Fly By Night album."
**Anthem (1975)** : The first song on Side One, Track One of Rush's 1975 album, *Fly By Night*, which was their second record. This track was partially constructed before Neil Peart joined the band, but his addition made the arrangement more complex. Peart also wrote the lyrics, drawing inspiration from Objectivist philosopher Ayn Rand's book of the same name, a theme that would later influence *2112*. Alex Lifeson and Geddy Lee preferred to focus on the music. "Anthem, the first song in Rush's 1975 album, their second record, Fly By Night."
**Overture (unknown year)** : This is the opening section of the concept album *2112*. It features trippy synth sounds played on an ARP Odyssey keyboard by Hugh Syme, who also designed Rush's artwork and the famous Starman logo, which debuted on *2112*. The "Overture" is famously recognized as the piece that "launched a million air drummers" due to Neil Peart's drumming. "The overture from 2112 for me."
**Temples of Syrinx (unknown year)** : This track is part of the *2112* album, contributing to the dystopian narrative of a society where individualism and creativity are suppressed by priests, temples, and computers in the city of Megadon. It is a key segment in the song cycle about the rediscovery of music. "The overture and temples of Syring's from the 2112 album."
**Cygnus X-1 (1977)** : A multi-part song from the 1977 album *Farewell to Kings*. This track showcases Rush's musical prowess and Neil Peart's lyrical capabilities. The story within the song, detailing an astronaut's journey to investigate a black hole, was conceived by Peart after he read an article on black holes in *Time* magazine. The narrative continues with "Cygnus X-1 Book II" on the *Hemispheres* album. "Signus X1 from the Farewell the Kings album."
**Cygnus X-1 Book II (1978)** : This song, featured on the 1978 album *Hemispheres*, serves as the conclusion to the multi-part story initiated by "Cygnus X-1" from the *Farewell to Kings* album. "a piece that was concluded with a track called Signus X1 Book 2 on the Hemisphere's album in 1978."
**La Villa Strangiato (1978)** : Featured on the 1978 album *Hemispheres*, this was Rush's first purely instrumental piece and is composed of 12 distinct movements. The song originated from a dream that guitarist Alex Lifeson had. Its recording was an extensive process, reportedly taking longer to complete than the entire *Fly By Night* album. "One of the greatest Rush instruments of all time is La Villa Strangato from 1978's Hemispheres."
**The Spirit of Radio (1980)** : The first track on Side One of the 1980 album *Permanent Waves*. This song was directly inspired by Toronto's CFNY FM radio station, which at the time operated from a "little yellow house in Brampton, Ontario" and used "The Spirit of Radio" as its slogan. A note in the lyric sheet explicitly stated, "inspired by The Spirit of Radio in Toronto, alive and well so far." "Inspired by CFNY FM, now called one on two point one, the edge in Toronto, when it was still a little yellow house in Brampton, Ontario, and using the slogan The Spirit of Radio."
**Tom Sawyer (1981)** : A prominent song from the 1981 album *Moving Pictures*. The track is famously referenced in the 2009 movie *I Love You, Man*, where characters discuss Neil Peart's drumming prowess. "Rush and Tom Sawyer from 1981's Moving Pictures."
**YYZ (1981)** : An instrumental track from the 1981 album *Moving Pictures*. This piece is notable for its complex time signature of 10/8, making it challenging for dancing. The song's title, YYZ, is the IATA airport code for Toronto Pearson International Airport, and this code is ingeniously woven into the music using Morse code. "The track is hard to dance due because it's in the time signature of 10-8."
**Losing It (1982)** : This song from the 1982 *Signals* album is lyrically significant for its exploration of decline and regret. Neil Peart referenced its themes when discussing his own retirement from drumming, stating he'd rather step away than "face the predicament described in our song, losing it," which includes lyrics like, "Sadder still to watch it die than never to have known it." "That's a track from the 1982 signals album."
**Subdivisions (1982)** : From the 1982 *Signals* album, this song is the first Rush track to be keyboard-based. Lyrically, it offers Neil Peart's autobiographical observations on suburban culture, social expectations, and cliques, serving as a keen commentary on the human condition and the pressures to conform or be cast out. "This was also the first rush song to be keyboard based."
**The Big Money (unknown year)** : Featured on the *Power Windows* album, this song's lyrics by Neil Peart were influenced by a 1936 novel from John Dos Passos, part of a trilogy about 12 characters during the Great Depression. Peart adapted the novel's sentiments to reflect the growing corporate greed of the 1980s global economy, presenting a theme not typical of rock and roll. "The big money was inspired by a 1936 novel from John DePasso's."
**Time Stand Still (1987)** : An autobiographical song for Neil Peart, released on the 1987 album *Hold Your Fire*. By 1993, with the band having been together for nearly 20 years, Peart reflected on how time had "slipped away" during their extensive touring in the 1970s, which felt "like a dark tunnel" and led to neglecting friends and family. The song serves as a "plea for the present," urging listeners to stop and enjoy the moment rather than dwelling on the past. "So the song is about stopping to enjoy that, with a warning against too much looking back, instead of getting nostalgic about the past, it's more a plea for the present."
**Where's My Thing? (1991)** : An instrumental track from the 1991 *Roll the Bones* album. This song was specifically designed as a showcase for Neil Peart's drum solos during live performances, allowing him to "bust out" and display his extensive percussive skills and instruments. "And there's an instrumental on the Roll the Bones album from 1991 until Where's My Thing?"
**Be You To Be (2012)** : A track from the 2012 *Clockwork Angels* album. This song highlights Neil Peart's evolved drumming style, particularly the "groove" he lays down, which reflects his refined playing and the "less was more" approach he developed after returning to drum lessons in 1994. "Note the groove Neil lays down in this track called, Be You To Be."

## curiosities
**Band's Musical Evolution (Post-New Wave)** : In the post-new wave era, Rush's music underwent a notable transformation. Their songs became shorter, tighter, and incorporated a greater emphasis on keyboards. Despite these changes, Neil Peart consistently provided highly erudite lyrics, distinctive for their intellectual depth within rock music. "The songs grew shorter, tighter, and with more keyboards."
**Starman Logo** : The iconic Starman logo, famously associated with Rush, was created by Hugh Syme. It made its first appearance on the *2112* album, becoming a defining visual symbol for the band. "He's also the guy who created the famous Starman logo, which first appeared on 2112."

The album was a critical and commercial success, solidifying Rush's place in the genre. "The entire first side is absolutely brilliant. # instrument - Drums

## curiosities
**Drummers' Lack of Respect and Standouts** : Over the decades, drummers have often been subjected to jokes and a general lack of respect within the music industry. Despite this, some drummers, like Neil Peart, stand out significantly and are admired, even worshipped, by fans, other drummers, and musicians alike for their exceptional talent. "Over the decades, drummers haven't really received a lot of respect."
**Common Ailments for Hard-Hitting Drummers** : Hard-hitting drummers often suffer from specific physical ailments after years of playing. Neil Peart experienced several of these, including arthritis, carpal tunnel syndrome, chronic tendonitis, shoulder problems, and lower back issues, which made performing particularly painful during his later tours. "arthritis and carpal tunnel, two things that hard-hitting drummers often suffer after years behind the kit."
**Neil Peart's Musical Approach** : Neil Peart was renowned as a highly musical drummer who did far more than simply keep time. His setup included an extensive array of percussion instruments, such as chimes, gongs, special cymbals, temple blocks, octobans, rototoms, blocking shields, and various electronic percussion devices. He utilized exquisitely tuned drums of different sizes and depths, performing with fluid movements and no wasted motion, constantly refining his playing even later in his career. "He was a very musical drummer. He didn't just keep time."
