# genre - Rock and Roll

## curiosities
**Early Development and Instrumentation** : Early rock and roll evolved from rhythm and blues combos in the early 1950s. While electric guitars were present in many combos, the fundamental "honkin rhythm" primarily originated from saxophones and pianos, with the piano incorporating elements of jazz, boogie-woogie, barrel house, and juke-joint energy. "The earliest rock evolved out of rhythm and blues combos. By the early 1950s, many of them featured some kind of electric guitar, but the honken rhythm came from saxophones and pianos, which were pounded into matchsticks."
**Pre-Guitar Hero Era** : Throughout the 1950s, the concept of a "guitar hero" was largely absent in the world of rock and roll, with Chuck Berry being a notable exception. Instead, the genre's early pioneers were celebrated as "piano heroes," highlighting the piano's central and dominant role in its nascent stages. "And even through the 1950s, the construct known as the guitar hero was largely absent for the world of rock and roll outside of Chuck Berry, of course. Instead, the early pioneers were piano heroes."
**Shift from Piano to Electric Guitar Dominance** : Although the piano was instrumental in creating rock and roll, its prominence was largely supplanted by the electric guitar as guitars became louder, produced "dirtier" sounds, and wailed more powerfully. This shift caused many rock and roll piano heroes to recede into the background, even though the piano remained a vital instrument for songwriting and performance in popular music. "But when guitars got louder, started sounding dirtier and began to wail more powerfully, the number of rock and roll piano heroes were ungunned and began to recede into the background. And while the piano helped create rock and roll, it was soon largely supplanted by the electric guitar."

# genre - Jazz

## curiosities
**Origin in New Orleans** : Jazz emerged in and around New Orleans, taking the piano in a new musical direction. This new musical form introduced a completely different type of groove, fundamentally altering how music was structured and performed. "Others took the piano in a different direction, especially in and around New Orleans, where a new form of music was born. They called it jazz."
**Impact on Time Signatures** : Jazz, particularly its early form known as ragtime, played a significant role in precipitating a "great time signature shift." Ragtime was among the first new musical forms to utilize the left hand on lower notes to provide a steady rhythm, which influenced the standardization of four-four time as the primary beat for most popular songs in the 20th century. "And along with having a completely different sort of groove, jazz also helped precipitate the great time signature shift. But with the rise of the piano and the spread of jazz, especially in early form called ragtime, which was among the first new musical forms to use the left hand and the lower notes to supply a steady rhythm to what the right hand was playing, time signatures fell into four four time, the standard beat for most of today's popular songs."
**Bebop Development in the 1940s** : In the 1940s, a form of jazz called bebop developed, which was characterized by the abilities of smaller combos typically consisting of three to five players, often including a piano player. This development occurred as larger dance bands downsized during World War II due to members being drafted and increased costs. "In the 1940s, a form of jazz based on the abilities of the combo called the bebop developed, and such groups often had a piano player."

# instrument - Piano

## curiosities
**Historical Accessibility and Mass Production** : For many decades, pianos were expensive, bespoke instruments largely out of reach for most people. However, by the mid-1800s, the Industrial Revolution made mass production possible, transforming the piano into an item for the masses and making it fashionable for every home. "For many, many decades, pianos were expensive bespoke instruments that were out of reach for most people. But by the middle 1800s, the Industrial Revolution had made mass production possible, and one of the items made for the masses was the piano."
**Pre-Radio Role in Home Entertainment** : Before the advent of radio and recorded music, the piano served as the primary means to "summon music on demand" in homes, typically through live performance. This made the piano an essential center of home entertainment and cultural life. "Now, keep in mind that this was before radio and before any sort of recorded music. So the only way you could summon music on demand in your home was to have someone perform it, often on a piano."
**Catalyst for Professional Songwriting** : The widespread popularity of the piano spurred the development of a professional songwriting class tasked with creating music for people to play at home. These compositions were among the first pop music "ditties" sold on sheet music, establishing the piano as "the musical instrument of the people" before World War I. "The piano craze spurred the development of a professional songwriting class whose job it was to come up with music that people could play in their homes. These were the first pop music diddies that were sold on sheet music and arranged specifically for the piano."
**Integral to Popular Music Evolution and Various Venues** : The piano became an essential instrument not only in homes but also in musicals, movie theaters, bars, restaurants, juke joints, and even brothels. It served as the foundation upon which much of popular music evolved, becoming a ubiquitous presence in entertainment settings throughout the 20th century. "Because it seemed that everyone had a piano, it became an essential instrument, not only in homes, but in musicals and movie theaters and bars and restaurants and juke joints and even brothels. And it was on the piano that much of popular music evolved."
**Role in Smaller Combos Post-WWII** : During World War II, as big dance bands were stripped of members due to drafting and economic constraints, smaller combos of three to five players emerged. The piano often played a key role in these groups, particularly in the development of bebop jazz in the 1940s. "During World War II, the big dance bands of the era were stripped of their members as many were drafted, maintaining a group of ten or twelve or more members became too expensive. And the big bands gave way to smaller combos of three, four or five players. In the 1940s, a form of jazz based on the abilities of the combo called the bebop developed, and such groups often had a piano player."
**Enduring Importance in Songwriting** : While the electric guitar largely supplanted the piano in rock and roll performance, the piano remains a vital instrument for writing and performing across all areas of popular music. Many songs, even those ultimately recorded with different instruments, are initially composed on a standard piano. "But it is still a vital instrument for writing and performing in all areas of popular music. Songs that are ultimately performed and recorded using different instruments are often written on a standard piano."

# curiosity

**Evolution of Time Signatures in Popular Music** : Up until the early 1900s, the beat of a song was not a primary focus in music genres like classical, folk, or religious music. Waltz time (3/4) and marching music (2/4) were common. However, with the rise of the piano and the spread of jazz, particularly ragtime's use of the left hand for a steady rhythm, four-four time became the standardized beat for most popular songs throughout the 20th century. "Up until the early 1900s, the beat of a song really wasn't that much of a big deal. The most common time signature was three quarter time, wallstime, followed by the two four time of marching music, which was a really, really big deal until sometime after 1900. But with the rise of the piano and the spread of jazz, especially in early form called ragtime, which was among the first new musical forms to use the left hand and the lower notes to supply a steady rhythm to what the right hand was playing, time signatures fell into four four time, the standard beat for most of today's popular songs. Number four was the beat of the 20th century."
**World War II's Impact on Big Dance Bands** : During World War II, big dance bands experienced a significant decline as many of their members were drafted into military service. The prohibitive cost of maintaining large groups of ten or more musicians led to the emergence of smaller combos of three to five players in the 1940s. "During World War II, the big dance bands of the era were stripped of their members as many were drafted, maintaining a group of ten or twelve or more members became too expensive. And the big bands gave way to smaller combos of three, four or five players."
**Chromesthesia Condition** : Chromesthesia is a neurological condition where music or sound is perceived as structures of light or color. Tori Amos is cited as an individual who experiences this, seeing music as colors, which reportedly aided her piano playing by allowing her to follow the colors in addition to the sounds of the notes. "music appeared to her as structures of light, which is a condition known as chromatizia. This helped her play because in addition to the sounds of the notes, she could just follow the colors."
