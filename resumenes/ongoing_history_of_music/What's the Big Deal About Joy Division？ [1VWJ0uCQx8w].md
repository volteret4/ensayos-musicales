## curiosities
**Early Life and Influences** : Born in Manchester on July 15, 1956, Ian Curtis's father was a police detective, and he had a younger sister named Carol. Raised in Hudsfield, outside Manchester, he sang in the church choir and was an avid reader, delving into Dostoyevsky, Sartre, and other philosophers, as well as literature and poetry, alongside a fascination with military history, particularly Nazi Germany. "Ian was born in Manchester on July 15, 1956. His father was a police detective and he had a younger sister named Carol."

**School Dislike and Early Drug Use** : Despite his intellectual interests, Ian hated school, preferring to stay home with his books and records by Iggy Pop, The Doors, David Bowie, and Lou Reed rather than attend class. Obligatory visits to local retirement homes with classmates were depressing but also led to rifling through medicine cabinets for medication. "Ian hated school. He would rather stay home with his books and Iggy Pop and Doors and David Bowie and Lou Reed records than go to class."

**Expulsion and Substance Abuse** : Ian was expelled from school for coming to class high on cough medicine and was also a fan of solvents, Valium, and various barbiturates. With no money to buy records, he regularly stole them from local record stores. "He was also a fan of solvents, volume, and various barbituits. With no money to buy records, he regularly stole them from local record stores."

**Early Marriage and Poetic Preoccupation** : On August 23, 1974, Ian married his girlfriend, Deborah, whom he had met when he was 16. Deborah found him mysterious and cool, and was intrigued by a box full of poetry he had written, some of which contained romantic fantasies about him dying young. "But then again, some of that poetry contained romantic fantasies involving Ian dying young."

**Day Jobs and Punk Discovery** : After finding a house with Deborah (eventually settling at 77 Barton Street in Macclesfield, where he had a room for his poetry), Ian initially worked in a record store in downtown Manchester, where he discovered punk rock. He later secured more stable employment within the British Civil Service, moving between several parts of the bureaucracy, such as the Ministry of Defense and an unemployment office. "He got a day job in a record store at downtown Manchester, which is where he discovered punk rock."

**Formation of the Band** : Ian's interest in music intensified after being one of a few dozen people to see the Sex Pistols play the Manchester Free Trade Hall on July 20, 1976, easily identifiable by his jacket with the word "hate" on the back. He then formed a band with Peter Hook and Bernard Albrecht, two guys he kept bumping into again and again at concerts, including that Pistols gig. "His interest in music really took off when he was one of a few dozen people in the audience, when the sex pistols played the Manchester Free Hall on July 20th, 1976."

**Epilepsy Diagnosis** : After collapsing at a gig as a teenager from strobe lights and learning about epilepsy through his civil service job in 1977, Ian experienced his first confirmed epileptic seizure on December 27, 1978, in the band's van returning from their first London gig. He was hospitalized, and after being on a waiting list, saw a specialist on January 23, 1978 (transcribed year, likely 1979 in context), who prescribed anti-convulsive drugs. "This was Ian's first confirmed epileptic seizure."

**Allergies and Mood Swings** : Ian suffered from health challenges beyond drug-related incidents, including an allergy to sunlight (polyformus-like eruption) that caused his hands to swell like "big red balloons," requiring bandaging. He also exhibited "Jekyll and Hyde mood swings," once throwing a drink at Deborah at their engagement party, and in the band, his erratic behavior was termed "alter Ian," leading to strange actions like kicking in a door or getting into fights. "For example, he was allergic to sunlight. He had something called polyformus-like eruption."

**Dancing as a Symptom** : Ian's distinctive "herky-jerky" dancing during live performances of Joy Division was often so erratic that it was difficult to distinguish from an actual epileptic seizure, leading to confusion among audiences and even band members about his true condition. "When joint division played live, Ian would dance in a very herky jirky sort of way, almost as if he was being electrocuted or something. Was he dancing or was he suffering another seizure?"

**Darkening Lyrics and Poetry** : Ian consistently carried a plastic bag filled with notebooks, where he would write poetry and lyrics whenever inspiration struck. As these writings were adapted into Joy Division's music, the band's sound became increasingly somber, dark, and depressing, with nearly all compositions written in a minor key. "And finally, more people began to notice that Ian's lyrics and poetry were becoming darker and more depressing."

**Affair with Annik Honoré** : On October 16, 1979, while playing a gig at Plan K in Brussels, Belgium, Ian met Annik Honoré, a woman described as resembling Siouxsie Sioux. This encounter quickly evolved into a full-blown affair, with Ian frequently fabricating excuses to meet Annik, who subsequently moved to London and worked at the Belgian Embassy, and even toured with the band on their European dates in early 1980. "On October 16, 1979, the band played a gig at a place called Plan K in Brussels, Belgium. This is where Ian met a woman named Anik on a ray."

**Voice Transformation for 'Closer'** : During the recording of the "Closer" album in March 1980, producer Martin Hannett and Factory Records head Tony Wilson coached Ian to achieve a deeper and richer vocal delivery by having him listen extensively to Frank Sinatra records, which resulted in a more haunting and authoritative sound that made his portrayals of pain and alienation highly credible. "Why? Because they made him listen to a lot of Frank Sinatra records."

**Increased Seizures and Mounting Pressures** : As Joy Division's popularity increased, the more gigs they played and the harder they worked, the worse and more frequent Ian's epileptic attacks became, suffering four grand mal seizures in one night in May 1979. By April 1980, the intense pressures from recording the "Closer" album, an ongoing tour, an impending North American tour, his failing marriage, guilt over fatherhood, his demanding mistress, and his continued use of alcohol and drugs exacerbated his epilepsy, making his mood swings unpredictable and leading to a state where he couldn't fall asleep until he had a seizure. "It got to the point where he couldn't fall asleep until he had a seizure."

**On-Stage Collapse and Overdose Attempt** : On April 4, 1980, during a show with The Stranglers at London's Rainbow venue, Ian suffered three epileptic attacks within the first five songs before completely collapsing; the band struggled to intervene earlier due to the difficulty of distinguishing his seizures from his characteristic dancing. Three days later, on April 7, Ian deliberately overdosed on his epilepsy medication, leaving a suicide note, and required a stomach pump at the hospital, yet a psychiatrist controversially declared him "not suicidal." "Three days later, Ian purposely overdosed on his epilepsy medication."

**Suicide by Hanging** : After a tumultuous period marked by a collapsing marriage, his mistress's demands, and severe anxiety about the upcoming North American tour, Ian spent the night of May 17, 1980, alone at 77 Barton Street. He watched the 1977 film "Strozek," drank strong coffee and whiskey, wrote a long note to Deborah with pictures of his wife and baby daughter nearby, then put on Iggy Pop's "The Idiot," and, as the sun rose on Sunday, May 18th, he took a length of rope from the clothesline and hanged himself at the age of 23, discovered by Deborah hours later. "Then he went out into the garden and got himself a length of rope from the clothesline. And as the sun came up and he sang, Ian hanged himself."

**Headstone Inscription** : Following the inquest into his death, which officially ruled "Asphyxia with ligature around the neck," Ian Curtis's body was cremated. His wife, Deborah, chose the poignant inscription for his headstone in Macclesfield Cemetery: "Ian Curtis, 18580, Love will Terrorist Apart" (transcribed verbatim from the source). "His body was cremated, and it was Deborah, who selected the inscription for Ian's headstone, laid in Maclisfield Cemetery. It reads, Ian Curtis, 18580, Love will Terrorist Apart."

## albums
**An Ideal for Living (1978) - 7-inch EP** : This four-track, seven-inch EP was the band's first release as Joy Division, featuring a controversial cover image of a Hitler Youth kid banging on a drum. It included the song "Warsaw." "From the four track seven-inch EP entitled An Ideal for Living, this is Joy Division with a song called Warsaw."

**Factory Sampler (1979) - Compilation Album** : In January 1979, Factory Records, founded by Tony Wilson with an inheritance, issued this record featuring material by some of the new bands on his label, including Joy Division's song "Digital," marking one of their early official releases under the new name. "And here's one of their songs from that factory sampler album. This is called Digital."

**Unknown Pleasures (1979) - Debut Album** : Recorded in April 1979 at Strawberry Studios with producer Martin Hannett, this album, financed by Tony Wilson for 8,500 pounds for 10,000 copies, was hailed by reviews as the "most radical post-punk record of the year" and a "masterpiece," immediately selling out its first pressing. Its distinctive sound was achieved by Hannett's innovative production techniques, including recording drums on the studio roof and employing "cold intimacy." "The name of the album was unknown pleasures. Joy Division and Disorder, for unknown pleasures, one of the most important post-punk alternative records of all time."

**Closer (1980) - Second Studio Album** : Recorded between March 18 and 20, 1980, this album featured a deeper, richer, and more haunting vocal delivery from Ian Curtis, achieved through producer Martin Hannett and Tony Wilson making him listen to Frank Sinatra records. It marked the band's artistic progression and intensified their sound of pain and alienation. "Between March 18 and 20, 1980, they started recording a new album that would eventually be called Closer."

## curiosities
**Early Name "Stiff Kittens"** : The band's initial name was "the stiff kittens," which Ian Curtis disliked because he felt it made them sound like generic punk bands. "At first, they were called the stiff kittens, but Ian hated that name because he felt it made them sound like any other punk band."

**Name "Warsaw" Inspiration** : After rejecting "Stiff Kittens," the band settled on "Warsaw," inspired by the song "Warzawe" from David Bowie's "Low" album, reflecting Ian Curtis's admiration for Bowie. "Besides, they were inspired by a song called Warzawe on David Bowie's low album."

**First Gig as Warsaw** : Warsaw played their debut gig on May 29, 1977, at the Electric Circus, a Manchester club. Reviews were mixed, but some music papers acknowledged "an elusive spark of dissimilarity," suggesting potential. "Warsaw played their first gig on May 29th, 1977, and a Manchester club called the Electric Circus."

**Finding a Permanent Drummer** : The band struggled to find a permanent drummer until August 1977, when Stephen Morris was found. He was the only individual to reply to Warsaw's advertisement for a drummer in a local music magazine, proving to be a good fit. "The next big date in Jordy Vision history was in August, when they finally found a permanent drummer. His name was Stephen Morris."

**Funding the First Record** : To finance the recording and pressing of 1200 copies of their first record, Ian Curtis deceitfully told his bank manager he needed 400 pounds for "dining room furniture," with the money instead going directly to the band's musical endeavor, ready by July 1978. "He told his bank manager that he needed 400 pounds to buy some dining room furniture. But when the money came through, it went straight towards the recording and pressing of 1200 copies of the first Jordy Vision record."

**Name Change from Warsaw** : The band changed its name from "Warsaw" because it was frequently confused with a London metal band called Warsaw Pact. To avoid conflict, they just thought they would change their name. "Warsaw was always getting confused with a London metal band called Warsaw Pact. And rather than get into some kind of fight, they just thought they would change their name."

**Origin of "Joy Division" Name** : The name "Joy Division" was chosen by Ian Curtis, inspired by a passage in Carol Satinsky's 1971 book "House of Dolls," which described "Joy Division" as a section in a concentration camp where women were forced to perform sexual favors for Nazi officers on leave, reflecting Ian's fascination with Nazi Germany. "The Joy Division was where women were forced to do sexual favors for Nazi officers on leave. I mean, how dark is that?"

**Scrapped RCA Album Attempt** : In early 1978, Joy Division attempted to record an album for RCA Records, but the project ultimately "didn't work out" and was completely scrapped before anything significant was accomplished. "They tried recording an album for RCA Records in early 1978, but that didn't work out, and the whole thing was scrapped before anything significant was accomplished."

**Battle of the Bands and Tony Wilson** : On April 14, 1978, Joy Division competed in a Battle of the Bands at Rafters, hoping to secure a record deal. Ian Curtis, believing Tony Wilson (host of the TV show "So It Goes") needed to know about the band, approached him with an "angry, insulting letter" rather than polite conversation, which unexpectedly impressed Wilson enough to book Joy Division on his show. "Ian believed that Tony definitely needed to know about Joy Division. But instead of being all polite and respectful, Ian just slammed the guy, complete with a very angry, very insulting letter."

**Rob Gretton Becomes Manager** : Joy Division's performance on Tony Wilson's TV show "So It Goes" made a strong impression on Rob Gretton, who subsequently became the band's manager. "When they made it to TV, their performance impressed another guy, Rob Gretton, the person who would become Joy Division's manager."

**Post-Punk Era Pioneers** : By early 1979, as the original punk rock scene declined, Joy Division found themselves perfectly positioned within the emerging post-punk era, where new sounds and attitudes constantly appeared, and their dark, moody, and depressing style resonated with a growing audience. "Joint division was perfectly positioned to take advantage of this. They found that there was an audience for their brand of dark moody depressing stuff."

**NME Cover and Fan Influence** : Ian Curtis's appearance on the cover of NME in January 1979, wearing a dark raincoat, inspired die-hard fans to begin attending Joy Division gigs dressed in the same manner, highlighting the band's burgeoning visual impact and cult following. "When Ian appeared on the cover of the enemy in January 1979 wearing a dark raincoat, die-hard fans started attending gigs dressed the same way."

**BBC John Peel Show Session** : On January 31, 1979, Joy Division recorded four songs for the John Peel Show on BBC Radio 1, with the tapes subsequently broadcast on Valentine's Day, significantly boosting their exposure and reputation. "Four songs were recorded for the John Peel Show on BBC Radio 1. The session took place on the 31st of January and the tapes were broadcast on Valentine's Day."

**Martin Hannett's Production Genius** : For the "Unknown Pleasures" album, producer Martin Hannett (described as a "genius, but a certified wacko") radically cleaned up the band's sound and created its distinctive drum sound by rebuilding Stephen Morris's kit, reassembling it on the studio roof, and adding echo, reverb, and sound effects, isolating instruments to achieve an intimate yet cold, detached, and distant atmosphere, which became known as "cold intimacy." "Not only did he clean up the band's sound, but he's the guy who found that distinctive drum sound."

**'Unknown Pleasures' Cover Art** : The striking black cover design for "Unknown Pleasures" by Factory Records in-house art director Peter Saville featured minimal text and an image that appeared to be a mountain range but was actually a graphical representation (ridge line plot) of 100 consecutive pulses from CP1919, the first pulsar ever discovered, found in the Cambridge Encyclopedia of Astronomy. Saville reversed the original black-on-white diagram to white-on-black, creating an iconic image that launched countless t-shirts and a 2003 clothing line. "It was originally black on white, but Savile reversed it to be white on black. But what is this? It's a graphical representation, what's known as a ridge line plot, of 100 consecutive pulses from a pulsar known as CP1919."

**Reflecting Manchester's Mood** : The "Unknown Pleasures" album perfectly captured the grim atmosphere of Manchester in the summer of 1979, a period marked by intense heat, a severe economic downturn, widespread unemployment, and the unpleasant odors stemming from the city's crumbling ancient sewer system. "Unknown pleasures captured the mood of Manchester that summer. It was very hot. The economy had gone very bad, and many people were out of work."

**Ian's Fear of Hurting Daughter** : Following the birth of his daughter, Natalie, Ian Curtis loved his baby but was afraid to pick her up or even touch her due to the intensifying frequency and severity of his epileptic seizures, fearing he might inadvertently harm her during an attack. "He loved his baby, but he was afraid to pick her up or even touch her for fear that he would have a seizure and hurt her."

**Unwritten Rule for Wives and Girlfriends** : By October 1979, an "unwritten rule" had been established among the members of Joy Division, prohibiting wives and girlfriends from attending gigs, suggesting a growing internal tension or a desire to maintain a specific atmosphere around the band's performances. "Plus it had become an unwritten rule among the members of Georgia Vision that wives and girlfriends were no longer welcome at gigs."

**Mini Riot at Derby Hall** : On April 8, 1980, after Ian Curtis's overdose attempt, the band played at Derby Hall, where a shaky Ian was only able to sing two songs. The crowd reacted so negatively that a mini-riot ensued, resulting in five people being hospitalized. "Ian was so shaky that he was only able to sing two songs, and the crowd freaked out. Five people ended up being taken to the hospital after a mini riot."

**Ian's Desire to Leave the Band** : Following a period of intense illness and depression, Ian Curtis announced his intention to leave Joy Division, expressing a desire to move to Holland and open a bookshop, although he retracted this statement just a day later as if he had never said it. "Then he announced he was leaving the band. He was going to move to Holland to open a bookshop."

**Fainting at Final Gig** : At Joy Division's last gig on May 2, 1980, at Birmingham University, Ian fainted on stage during the performance, and the cheering crowd mistakenly believed it was part of the act, when in reality, he was suffering another epileptic seizure. "The crowd is reacting to Ian fainting. They thought it was all part of the act, but the truth is, as we listen to this, Ian's having another seizure."

**North American Tour Cancellation** : The band was scheduled to depart for a North American tour on Monday, May 19, 1980, a tour that had every indication of making them famous. Ian's death the day before (May 18) meant the tour was canceled and the opportunity lost. "On Monday May 19, Joy Division was scheduled to leave for North American tour, and there was every indication that this was the tour that was going to make them famous."

**Suicide Pact and Band Dissolution** : A solemn suicide pact existed among the members of Joy Division, stipulating that if any member were to leave the band for any reason, Joy Division would cease to exist. Consequently, upon Ian Curtis's death, Peter Hook, Barney Sumner, and Stephen Morris honored their word, retiring Joy Division and beginning work on their next project, New Order. "The deal was that should any member leave for any reason joy division would be broken up. So once Ian was gone, Peter Hook, Barney Sumner, and Stephen Morris kept their word."

**Post-Mortem Legacy and Influence** : Since Ian Curtis's death in May 1980, Joy Division has achieved legendary status, with continued album sales, radio play, streams, and hundreds of bootlegs in circulation. Their sound and style profoundly influenced the development of Goth, industrial music, Emo, and the alternative music scene of the 80s and 90s (through New Order), demonstrating how rock could be emotionally powerful, stripped down, intellectually serious, and a study in mental illness and emotional paralysis. "Since that day in May 1980, joy division has become legendary."

**Enduring Visual Identity** : The band's visual identity, especially the iconic "Unknown Pleasures" album cover, is noted for its power and timelessness, contributing to the band's lasting appeal and repeated rediscovery by subsequent generations. "And people noted the power in their visual identity, especially with that unknown pleasure's album cover."

**Limited Output Preserves Legacy** : The fact that Joy Division released only two albums is highlighted as a significant factor in their enduring legend, as it "froze them in time," concentrating their entire output into a powerful, finite body of work that never had the chance to "grow stale and boring." "And the fact that the band released just two albums is important too. Joy Division is forever frozen in time, with everything concentrated into those two records."

**Documentaries and Biographies** : Joy Division's story is chronicled in Deborah Curtis's 1995 book "Touching From a Distance," whose title comes from the song "Transmission," and the band was also a significant part of the movie "24 Hour Party People." A feature film on Ian's life, "Control," was directed by U2 photographer Anton Corbijn and executive produced by Tony Wilson. "And one of the best rock biographies you'll ever read is a 1995 book written by Deborah Curtis called Touching From a Distance."

**Proposed Museum at Barton Street** : There has been a persistent interest in transforming the house at 77 Barton Street in Macclesfield, where Ian Curtis lived and died, into a Joy Division museum, reflecting the ongoing fascination with the band and its frontman. "And the last I heard, some people still want to turn the house at 77 Barton Street in Mackelsfield into a Joy Division museum."

# label - Factory Records
## curiosities
**Founding with Inheritance** : Tony Wilson, a TV personality, started Factory Records using a modest inheritance he received after a relative died. In January 1979, the label issued its first record, "Factory Sampler," featuring material from new bands, including Joy Division. "Soon after that battle, the band showed one of Tony Wilson's relatives die, leaving him a modest inheritance. And what did he do with that money? He started a record company called Factory."

**Funding 'Unknown Pleasures'** : Tony Wilson was so impressed with the recording of Joy Division's "Unknown Pleasures" that he invested all of Factory Records' money, 8,500 pounds, to press up 10,000 copies of the album. "Tony Wilson, they had a factory, was so thrilled with the results that he put up 8,500 pounds, all the money factory had to press up 10,000 copies."

**In-House Art Director Peter Saville** : Peter Saville served as Factory Records' in-house art director, responsible for creating the iconic black cover design for Joy Division's "Unknown Pleasures" album, featuring the ridge line plot of a pulsar. "One of the most striking features of the album was the cover design by Factory Records in-house art director Peter Savile."

# instrument - Drum
## curiosities
**Martin Hannett's Innovative Recording** : For Joy Division's "Unknown Pleasures" album, producer Martin Hannett created the distinctive drum sound by radically rebuilding drummer Stephen Morris's kit and then reassembling it on the studio roof to record him playing, adding echo, reverb, and sound effects for an intimate yet cold atmosphere. "Not only did he clean up the band's sound, but he's the guy who found that distinctive drum sound. He do radical things by completely rebuild Stephen Morris' kit and then reassemble it on the roof of the studio and record him playing up there."

"Over the last half century, Ian Curtis has become a tragic mythical figure among the lines of Kurt Cobain, Sid Vicious and Jim Morrison."

**Thorazine Overdose Incident** : At 16, Ian Curtis had a particularly scary incident where he fell unconscious after taking a very large hit of Thorazine, an anti-psychotic drug, requiring his stomach to be pumped. This was part of a pattern of rifling through medicine cabinets at local retirement homes. "Ian ended up with having a stomach pump because he fell unconscious after a very large hit of thorazine, which is used as an anti-psychotic drug."

**'House of Dolls' Book Influence** : Ian Curtis's fascination with Nazi Germany led him to read the 1971 book "House of Dolls" by Carol Satinsky, which described life inside a concentration camp and contained the passage mentioning "Joy Division" as a section where women were forced to perform sexual favors for Nazi officers, providing the inspiration for the band's name. "He had read a book published in 1971 called House of Dolls by a guy named Carol Satinsky."

**Tony Wilson's Inheritance** : Tony Wilson, a relative of one of the band's attendees, received a modest inheritance after their death, which he then used to start Factory Records, signing Joy Division and releasing their music. "Soon after that battle, the band showed one of Tony Wilson's relatives die, leaving him a modest inheritance."

**'Cold Intimacy' Production Term** : Producer Martin Hannett's radical recording techniques for Joy Division's "Unknown Pleasures," which involved isolating instruments and adding echo/reverb to create an intimate yet detached sound, became recognized by the production term "cold intimacy." "There's even a production term for this now, cold intimacy."

**Manchester's Grim Summer of 1979** : The mood captured by Joy Division's "Unknown Pleasures" album perfectly reflected Manchester in the summer of 1979, characterized by intense heat, a struggling economy, high unemployment, and an unpleasant odor emanating from the city's crumbling ancient sewer system. "Unknown pleasures captured the mood of Manchester that summer. It was very hot. The economy had gone very bad, and many people were out of work."

**Doctor's Suicide** : The doctor responsible for treating Ian Curtis for his epilepsy and depression tragically shot himself several weeks after an appointment with Ian and Deborah, leaving them feeling that "something was wrong with this guy." "The doctor who was supposed to be treating Ian for epilepsy and depression shot himself several weeks after that appointment."

**Ian Curtis Faking Seizures** : By October 1979, the frequency and unpredictability of Ian Curtis's epileptic seizures led to uncertainty among those around him, as no one could truly discern whether he was experiencing a genuine seizure or if he was faking an attack to manipulate people. "At the same time, no one really knew when it was a real seizure, or if it was one of those times when Ian faked it, just so he could manipulate people."

**Bernard Sumner's Hypnosis** : Joy Division guitarist Bernard Sumner, who was "pretty good at this stuff," decided to hypnotize Ian Curtis. During the trance, Ian reportedly regressed into a previous life as a 17th-century French peasant on his deathbed, living in a hut. "Joy Division guitarist Bernard Sumner decided to hypnotize Ian, who, believe it or not, is pretty good at this stuff."

**'Strozek' Film on Suicide Night** : On the night he died, Ian Curtis watched "Strozek," a 1977 film about a musician and former mental patient who travels to the US and struggles to choose between the two women in his life, a narrative that eerily mirrored aspects of his own life and emotional state. "Once she was gone, Ian sat down to watch his movie, a 1977 film in total Strozek. It's all about a musician and a former mental patient who travels to the US and is unable to decide between the two women in his life."

**'Touching From a Distance' Biography** : Deborah Curtis's 1995 book, "Touching From a Distance," is described as one of the best rock biographies and chronicles Ian's life, with its title taken from a line in the Joy Division song "Transmission." "And one of the best rock biographies you'll ever read is a 1995 book written by Deborah Curtis called Touching From a Distance."

**'24 Hour Party People' Movie** : Joy Division played a significant role in the movie "24 Hour Party People," which explored the Manchester music scene and the history of Factory Records. "They were a big part of the movie 24-hour party people, which is excellent."

**'Control' Feature Film** : A feature film about Ian Curtis's life, titled "Control," was directed by renowned U2 photographer Anton Corbijn and executive produced by Tony Wilson, offering a cinematic portrayal of the frontman's story. "There's a feature film on Ian's life called Control, directed by famed U2 photographer Anton Corbin, and executive produced by Tony Wilson."

**Proposed Joy Division Museum** : There is ongoing interest in converting the house at 77 Barton Street in Macclesfield, where Ian Curtis resided and ultimately died, into a museum dedicated to Joy Division. "And the last I heard, some people still want to turn the house at 77 Barton Street in Mackelsfield into a Joy Division museum."
