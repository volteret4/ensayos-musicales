# song - Happy Birthday

## curiosities
**Most Performed Song (Last 100 Years)** : "Happy Birthday" is considered the most common, recognized, and universal song in the English language, and is used in at least half a dozen other cultures. Its simple melody, words, and sentiments contribute to its universal and timeless appeal. "It's the most common, most recognized, and most universal song in the English language."
**Origin Story (1893)** : The song originated from two American kindergarten teachers, sisters Patty and Mildred Hill from Louisville, Kentucky. In 1893, they created a song for children titled "Good Morning to You," which later morphed into "Happy Birthday to You." "In 1893, they came up with a song for the children that they called Good Morning to You."
**Copyright War and Ownership** : A copyright war over the ownership of "Happy Birthday" has existed since at least 1912. In 1990, Warner Chappell, a music publishing company, acquired the rights for $5 million and claims the copyright will not expire until 2030. "People have been fighting over the ownership of Happy Birthday since at least 1912."
**Royalties and Public Performances** : Unauthorized public performances of "Happy Birthday" are considered illegal unless royalties are paid to Warner Chappell. The company reportedly nets $10,000 for each performance on film or TV. Even singing it at a friend's birthday party in front of a group is technically considered a public performance, making participants liable for a royalty payment. "This means whenever you hear Happy Birthday in a movie or a TV show, Warner Chappell is due money."
**Impact on Media and Entertainment** : Copyright issues surrounding "Happy Birthday" have led many movies to substitute it with public domain songs like "He's a Jolly Good Fellow" in birthday scenes. Instances of the song in films, TV shows, and documentaries have also prevented their re-showing or re-release due to unresolved copyright problems. "Instances of Happy Birthday in films and TV shows and documentaries have actually prevented them from being re-shown or re-released because the attendant copyright problems haven't been sorted out."
**Restaurant Avoidance** : Restaurants often have servers sing a unique, corporate song at birthday tables to avoid singing "Happy Birthday" in public and incurring liability for copyright infringement. "One of these sings a weird corporate song, so they don't have to sing Happy Birthday in public and be liable for copyright infringement."
**International Incident (2008)** : When a crowd in Beijing sang "Happy Birthday" to Usain Bolt after he won his 200-meter gold medal in August 2008, it raised questions about copyright fees, especially as TV networks worldwide broadcast the singing, though China is unlikely to pay such fees. "I wonder what happened when the crowd in Beijing sang Happy Birthday to Usain Bolt after he won his 200 meter gold medal in August of 2008."

## songs
**Smells Like Teen Spirit (1991)** : Kurt Cobain aimed to write a song in the style of the Pixies, characterized by loud/soft/loud/soft dynamics, later admitting it was an attempt to "rip off" the Pixies. It began much faster and more punk rock, with Kurt providing the riff and chorus. Chris Novoselic suggested slowing the verses, and Dave Grohl changed the drum beat, leading to the song's distinctive structure. "Kurt was looking to write a song in the style of the Pixies, which made a lot of loud, soft, loud, soft dynamics."
**Smells Like Teen Spirit - Collaborative Writing** : Unlike many other Nirvana songs, "Smells Like Teen Spirit" is one of the few credits shared by all three band members: Kurt Cobain, Chris Novoselic, and Dave Grohl, reflecting their individual contributions to its development. "It's also why, unlike so many other Nirvana songs, all three members are credited as having written it."
**Smells Like Teen Spirit - Title Origin** : The title originated from a remark made by Kathleen Hannah of Bikini Kill. After a night of heavy discussion and drinking with Kurt Cobain, she spray-painted "Kurt smells like Teen Spirit" on his apartment wall, referring to the fruity-smelling deodorant favored by Toby Vale, Kurt's girlfriend at the time. "It was there that Kathleen remarked that Kurt smelled like teen spirit."
**Smells Like Teen Spirit - Cobain's Misunderstanding** : Kurt Cobain, who reportedly never wore deodorant, initially misinterpreted Kathleen Hannah's comment as a compliment related to their discussions on punk and politics. He only understood the true meaning months after the song's release. "He thought Kathleen was actually paying him a compliment."
**Smells Like Teen Spirit - Recording (1991)** : Three takes of "Smells Like Teen Spirit" were recorded in the studio, with the final version being essentially the middle take (take two of three). More takes might have been done, but Kurt Cobain's voice was "shot from all the yelling and screaming." The single was released on September 10th, 1991. "So what we hear is essentially the middle one take two of three."

## curiosities
**Pixies Influence** : Kurt Cobain specifically sought to write "Smells Like Teen Spirit" in the style of the Pixies, known for their distinct loud/soft/loud/soft dynamic shifts in their music. He later candidly described this creative process as an attempt to "rip off the Pixies." "He later called the whole exercise an attempt to rip off the Pixies."

# artist - Sex Pistols

## members
Johnny Rotten

## genres

## songs
**Anarchy in the UK (1976)** : Released on November 26th, 1976, "Anarchy in the UK" is historically recognized as the second-ever punk rock single in the UK. The song expresses Johnny Rotten's discussions about 1970s Britain, which was marked by a terrible economy, widespread strikes, lost hope among young people, and the ongoing crumble of the British Empire amid a strong class system. "It was released on November 26th, 1976, and has gone down in history as the second ever punk rock single in the UK."
**Anarchy in the UK - British References** : Many lyrics in "Anarchy in the UK" are highly specific to 1970s Britain and can be confusing to non-British audiences or those unfamiliar with the era. Johnny Rotten uses various initials in the lyrics that reference political and media entities of the time. "But there are aspects of the song that are very British and don't make a lot of sense to people outside the country or outside of that era back in the 1970s."
**Anarchy in the UK - NME Reference** : In the song, Johnny Rotten's mention of "NME" refers not to an enemy, but to the New Musical Express, a prominent weekly British music paper. He used this reference to signify his use of media to disseminate his message of anarchy. "He's not talking about the enemy. He means the new musical express, one of the big weekly British music papers."
**Anarchy in the UK - MPLA Reference** : The "MPLA" referenced in the lyrics stands for the Popular Movement for the Liberation of Angola, an African group that violently expelled the Portuguese in 1975 to establish an independent Angolan state. This group was supported by communist allies and achieved its goals through a form of anarchy. "When he mentions the MPLA, this is the popular movement for the liberation of Angola."
**Anarchy in the UK - UDA Reference** : Johnny Rotten also mentions the "UDA," which refers to the Ulster Defense Association, a loyalist terrorist group in Northern Ireland. The UDA was dedicated to remaining loyal to England and actively opposed any unification with the Republic of Ireland, carrying out bombings, shootings, and killings until 2007. "This was the Ulster Defense Association, a terrorist group in Northern Ireland."
**Anarchy in the UK - IRA Reference** : The "IRA," or Irish Republican Army, is also referenced in the song. This group aimed to overthrow the Northern Irish government by any means necessary and was the sworn enemy of the UDA, reflecting the widespread "anarchy" Johnny Rotten observed both domestically and internationally. "Their aim was to overthrow the government of Northern Ireland by whatever means necessary, and they were the sworn enemies of the UDA."
**Anarchy in the UK - Megadeth Cover** : When Megadeth covered "Anarchy in the UK" in 1998, singer Dave Mustaine did not fully understand all of Johnny Rotten's original lyrics and consequently made up some new ones. "Funny thing is that when the band Megadeff covered Anarchy in the UK in 1998, singer Dave Mustain didn't quite understand all the words that Johnny was singing, so we made up a few new ones."

# artist - U2

## members
Bono

## songs
**I Will Follow (1980)** : The song's emotional core is rooted in the unconditional love a mother has for a son, but it also delves into the pain of a son being separated or "taken away" from his mother. Bono was actually singing the song from his mother's point of view, and the lyrics can be interpreted as resembling a suicide note, a possibility Bono himself acknowledges. "Bono was actually singing the song from his mother's point of view."
**I Will Follow - Origin Story (1974)** : The song's genesis dates back to September 10th, 1974, when Bono's mother, Iris Hussin, passed away from a cerebral hemorrhage, only days after her father (Bono's grandfather, Alex) also died. This double tragedy profoundly affected a 13-year-old Bono, leading him into a period of anger, confusion, and isolation, which became the emotional wellspring for the lyrics. "This is where I will follow comes from."
**I Will Follow - Recording Anecdote** : The song emerged from a particularly "loud and violent argument" Bono had with The Edge during rehearsals. Bono reportedly felt The Edge was playing his guitar parts "too gentle," so he snatched the guitar and demonstrated how it should be played, leading to the song's distinctive sound. "The song itself came out of a very loud and very violent argument that Bono had with the edge during rehearsals."

## curiosities
**Bono's Family Tragedy (1974)** : On September 10th, 1974, Bono's mother, Iris Hussin, fainted at her father Alex's burial service and suffered a cerebral hemorrhage, dying four days later after being declared brain dead and having life support turned off by Bono's father, Bob. This left Bono, his brother Norman, and his father as "three hardcore Irish bachelor types" suddenly forced to manage on their own. "On September 10th, 1974, Iris Hussin, Bono's mum, gathered the family together and went to the funeral for her father, Bono's grandfather."
**Bono's Psychological Impact** : The sudden loss of both his grandfather and mother within days in 1974 had a significant psychological effect on the 13-year-old Bono. He became very angry and confused, developing into a loner and a "strange kid" who spent time in a "pretty dark place." "This naturally had a huge psychological effect on Bono."

## albums
**Love (1985)** : This album marked a significant shift for The Cult, presenting a cleaner and leaner sound compared to their previous, more niche gothic and heavy records. It featured a better production quality with a blend of guitar heft and psychedelia, sharper songwriting, and included their hit single "She Sells Sanctuary." "It was cleaner and leaner than the other records."

## songs
**She Sells Sanctuary (1985)** : This enduring alt-rock song from the 80s was the big single from The Cult's "Love" album. It was recorded in March 1985, and despite its success, the song was initially buried deep on the record, appearing as Track 9 on the CD (Side 2 on the album). "You didn't get to She Sells Sanctuary until Track 9 on the CD."
**She Sells Sanctuary - Drunk Drumming Incident** : During the recording of "She Sells Sanctuary," drummer Nigel Preston, who had a severe drinking problem, showed up intoxicated and struggled to play. Singer Ian Astbury and guitarist Billy Duffy gradually removed pieces of his drum set, leaving him with just a snare, bass drum, and hi-hats. This limitation forced a much more rhythmic, straight-ahead 4/4 beat with minimal snare fills, inadvertently creating the song's distinctive, effective drumming. "The result was a song that was much more rhythmic, much more straight ahead than anything to call it ever done."

## curiosities
**Band Name Evolution** : Before settling on "The Cult," the band went by two other names: Southern Death Cult (for one album) and Death Cult (for another). By the end of 1983, they were simply "The Cult." "Up until that record came out, The Cult went by two other names."
**Nigel Preston's Drinking Problem** : Drummer Nigel Preston had a serious drinking problem that made him increasingly unreliable during recording sessions, often showing up late or too drunk to play. This unreliability directly led to the minimalist drumming on "She Sells Sanctuary." "Nigel however had a serious drinking problem and was becoming more and more unreliable."
**Nigel Preston's Firing and Death** : Although "Sanctuary" and the "Love" album were major hits, Nigel Preston was fired shortly after his parts were recorded due to his unreliability. He later died in 1992, but his drumming on "She Sells Sanctuary" remains a part of The Cult's legacy. "Nigel wasn't invited to stick around. He was fired soon after all his parts were recorded."
**Mark Brachecki as Video Drummer** : The drummer seen in the music video for "She Sells Sanctuary" was Mark Brachecki, who was on loan from the band Big Country, as Nigel Preston had already been fired. "The drummer we see in the video for Sanctuary is a dude named Mark Brachecki, who was on loan from the band Big Country."

**London Calling (1979)** : Recorded in late summer 1979 with producer Guy Stevens, the album's tone was set by the recording of the first song, "Brand New Cadillac." ## albums
**Blur (1997)** : The self-titled album marked a deliberate shift away from the band's Britpop sound, influenced by Graham Coxon's deep dive into American indie and lo-fi bands like Pavement. This change in direction was widely accepted by the band members, resulting in a distinctly different sound for their fifth record. "Weirdly, just about everyone agreed with him, which is why the fifth Blur record sounded so different than the Brit pop stuff that came before it."

## songs
**Song 2 (1997)** : This track, always known as "Song 2," never received a proper title, coincidentally being the second song on the album, two minutes and two seconds long, and reaching number two on the British charts. It was conceived and recorded in under 30 minutes, performed and recorded in real time. "Yes, it's the second song on the record. And yes, it's two minutes and two seconds long. And yes, it reached number two in the British charts, but all those things are just coincidences."
**Song 2 - Recording Process** : During recording, two drum kits, intentionally tuned to sound "cheap and trashy," were set up facing each other, played by Graham Coxon and Dave Rowntree. The band decided to play basic parts in the verses and "go to town" in the chorus. Damon Albarn, having only scraps of lyrics, ad-libbed the famous "woohoo!" parts, which were kept due to their unexpected effectiveness. "No one knew they were coming, but they sounded good so they kept them."
**Song 2 - Commercial Success and Licensing** : Despite its quick creation, "Song 2" became Blur's most enduring hit, generating millions in royalties. The ad-libbed "woohoo!" parts were especially valuable. It has been covered by numerous artists and extensively licensed for TV commercials (Toyota, BMW, Labatt, Intel), video games, movie trailers, TV shows, and almost every type of sporting event. "Turns out those ad-hawk woohoo's were worth millions and millions of dollars."
**Song 2 - US Military Refusal** : Damon Albarn notably refused to license "Song 2" for use in a commercial for the US military, demonstrating a boundary for its commercial applications despite its widespread usage elsewhere. "The only place where the song didn't reach was a commercial for the US military. Damon Alburn refused to let them have it."

## curiosities
**Britpop Rejection** : Blur became "really, really tired of the whole Brit pop thing" and sought a completely new musical direction. Graham Coxon, deeply immersed in the American indie scene and lo-fi bands like Pavement, advocated for this change, which the band embraced for their self-titled album. "See, Blur was really, really tired of the whole Brit pop thing and they wanted to do something completely different."

## songs
**Fairy Tale of New York (1987)** : Despite becoming a Christmas favorite, the song is not traditionally Christmassy, focusing instead on a fight between a drunken couple on Christmas Eve. It took over two years to write and record, starting as a piece by banjo player Jim Finer in 1985. "Not very Christmassy in the traditional sense, but still one of the biggest alt rock Christmas songs ever, if not of all time."
**Fairy Tale of New York - Long Development** : Jim Finer first started developing the song in 1985, showing it to Shane McGowan. For the next two years, they struggled with the lyrics, which initially centered on a lonely sailor, a concept deemed "crap." The band blew off recording it for Christmas 1985 and went on tour in America. "It first started to come together as a piece sometime in 1985 by Jim Feiner, the Pogs Banjo player."
**Fairy Tale of New York - Inspiration and Concept Shift** : Returning to England in March 1986, the song's concept changed from a sailor to a drunken couple's Christmas Eve fight. Part of the inspiration came from a book Shane McGowan read on tour, "A Fairy Tale of New York," about an Irish man returning to New York after studying in Ireland. "Part of the inspiration came from a book that Shane had read on tour called A Fairy Tale of New York, which dealt with an Irish dude returning to New York after going to school in Ireland."
**Fairy Tale of New York - Vocalist Challenges** : The Pogues faced difficulty finding a suitable female vocalist for the duet. It was initially written for bass player Cait O'Riordan, but she left the band after running off with Elvis Costello, shelving the song for Christmas 1986. "Besides, the Pogs really couldn't find anybody they liked to sing the female part."
**Fairy Tale of New York - Kirsty MacColl's Contribution (1987)** : In March 1987, Shane McGowan recorded both male and female parts for "Fairy Tale of New York" as a demo during sessions for "If I Should Fall From Grace With God." Producer Steve Lillywhite, who had worked with U2, took this rough cut to his wife, singer Kirsty MacColl, in August 1987. She recorded her parts in the studio alone, surprising the band with the results, and Shane then re-recorded his parts to complement hers. "Steve brought her into the studio one night to do her bits, and then she went home to look after the kids."
**Fairy Tale of New York - Separate Vocal Recordings** : A notable curiosity of "Fairy Tale of New York" is that, despite being a duet, Shane McGowan and Kirsty MacColl never recorded their parts together in the same studio. MacColl recorded her vocals separately based on Shane's demo, and then Shane adjusted his vocals. "As you listen to this, remember that although the song was written as a duet and sung as a duet, Shane and Kirsty were never ever in the same studio together."

## curiosities
**Cait O'Riordan's Departure** : Cait O'Riordan, the Pogues' bass player for whom the female part of "Fairy Tale of New York" was originally written, left the band after she "had run off with Elvis Castell." This departure forced the song to be shelved for Christmas 1986. "It had been written for Cater Reardon, a Pogs bass player, but she had run off with Elvis Castell, so that shelled the song for Christmas 1986."
