# curiosity
**A&R (Artists and Repertoire)** : This department of a record label is responsible for discovering new artists and songs, signing them to contracts, assisting with material selection for recording, and guiding them through the entire recording process. Historically, A&R involved extensive searching in bars and sifting through physical demos, but now heavily utilizes online platforms like YouTube, Facebook, SoundCloud, and Bandcamp for talent discovery. The role is highly competitive, and the success of an A&R person is crucial for the artists they champion. "A and R stands for artists and repertoire. This is the department of a record label tasked with finding new artists and new songs."
**Advance** : An advance is an upfront sum of money paid by a record label to an artist upon signing a contract, intended to help them get started. This money is considered a recoupable loan, meaning it must be paid back from the artist's future earnings generated by the record deal. For many acts, this can lead to a cycle of debt, as they may not earn enough to pay off the initial advance, leading to deeper financial holes with subsequent advances. Labels view advances as a high-risk investment, with a quoted ratio suggesting only 3 out of 30 signings will generate enough profit to cover all others. "When an A and R person signs an artist to a contract, the deal includes paying the ax some money up front to help get them started until money starts flowing in from the record deal. It's alone. This money has to be paid back."
**Recoupable Advance** : This term refers to an advance payment made by a label to an artist that the label has the right to "claw back" from the artist's future earnings. The advance is essentially a loan that is paid off by garnishing wages from any money the artist makes from their record deal until the loan is fully repaid. "This is called a recoupable advance. The label has the right to claw back the money. It lent you to get you started."
**Label Risk Ratio** : The music industry is characterized by high financial risk for record labels. A commonly cited ratio indicates that out of 30 artists signed to a label, 20 will lose money, 7 will break even, and only 3 will make enough money to offset the losses from all other signings. This demonstrates the significant financial gamble labels take on new acts. "out of 30 signings to a record label, 20 will lose money, seven will break even, and the last three will make enough money to pay for everybody else."
**Cancon (Canadian Content)** : Cancon is an abbreviation for Canadian content, a regulatory requirement in Canada since 1971. Canadian radio stations are mandated to allocate a certain percentage of their playlist to music of Canadian origin. This policy was enacted to prevent Canadian musicians from being overshadowed by foreign music, ensure Canadian music receives airplay within its own country, and fostered the growth and eventual international success of the Canadian music industry, which was nascent before 1971. "Cancon is short for Canadian content. And since 1971, Canadian radio stations have been required to devote a percentage of their playlist to music of Canadian origin."
**MAPL (Music, Artist, Production, Lyrics)** : MAPL is a system used to define official Canadian content, typically identified by a four-quartered circle symbol on CDs or vinyl. For a song to qualify as Canadian content, it must meet at least two of the four criteria: the Music written by a Canadian, the Artist is Canadian, the Production recorded in Canada or by a Canadian, and the Lyrics written by a Canadian. This bureaucratic system can lead to unusual classifications. "MAPL, which is spelled M-A-P-L. This stands for Music, Artist, Production and Lyrikes... For a song to be officially considered Canadian content, it has to meet two of those criteria."
**Catalog Department** : This division within a record company manages older, non-current music. It includes material from artists who are no longer active and music that has not been actively promoted for at least 18 to 24 months. The catalog department essentially takes over once the A&R department's initial work is complete, with the goal of selling and exploiting older music for the long-term benefit of both the company and the artist. "This is the division of a record company that deals with older, non-current music. This covers material by artists who are no longer active, and it also means any music that is no longer being actively promoted, which means it's at least 18 to 24 months old."
**Legacy Artists and Touring** : Due to the collapse of physical record sales and the proliferation of streaming services, older acts, referred to as "legacy artists," no longer earn significant income from catalog sales as they once did. Consequently, many artists in their 60s and 70s are compelled to continue touring, as live performances have become their primary source of income. "But with the collapse of record sales and the rise of streaming legacy artists... aren't making anywhere near what they used to from sales. So this explains why so many artists, people who were in their 60s and 70s now are still on the road playing gigs. Touring has become their main source of income."
**Box Sets** : Box sets are lucrative, high-margin projects in the music industry, designed to appeal to "super fans." These collections often include rare or exclusive material such as remixes, live tracks, demos, books, expanded liner notes, swag (posters, stickers, fake backstage passes), 5.1 mixes, DVDs, and Blu-ray discs. While they don't sell in massive numbers, the profits generated from these specialized, often expensive, editions can be extraordinary due to their dedicated fanbase. "Now box sets don't sell in huge numbers, but super fans buy them and the profits can be extraordinary."
**Contract (Standard)** : A contract is a formal written agreement between two parties outlining their obligations and responsibilities within a business arrangement for a specific term. Historically, record contracts primarily focused on the details of making and selling records, as this was the main revenue stream for labels. "This is a written agreement between two parties that spells out the obligations and responsibility each agrees to fulfill in a business arrangement that will run for a certain term."
**360 Deal** : A 360 deal is a modern record contract that grants the label a share of all the artist's income streams, not just record sales. This includes revenue from touring, merchandise, publishing, and streaming. While it appears to give the label a deeper cut of the artist's earnings, it also provides the label with a greater incentive to invest in the artist's overall success across all platforms, potentially offering the artist more long-term security than traditional contracts. "Basically signing a deal today will see the label take a piece of the action not just from records sales, but from all income streams. That means a kind of touring revenue, money from merch, money from publishing, money from streaming, the whole thing."
**Copyright** : Copyright is a fundamental principle in creative industries, literally meaning "the right to copy." It is a form of intellectual property rights that grants the owner the exclusive legal right to control who can copy or use creative works, such as music, for financial gain. In music, copyright protects composers from unauthorized use of their work. Artists typically assign their music's copyright to their label, which then holds exclusive rights to copy and distribute it, requiring payment for any third-party usage. "This is one of the very foundational principles of any creative industry. copyright literally means the right to copy. Whoever owns the copyright on something has the absolute legal and exclusive right to determine who can and cannot copy that something or who can and cannot use that something for financial gain."
**Podcast Music Copyright Issues** : Podcasts often face limitations in including full songs from radio shows due to copyright restrictions. Even snippets of melodies cannot be reproduced without permission, as they fall under copyright. This highlights the complexities and strictures of music copyright in digital distribution, where mechanisms for acquiring appropriate permissions for podcasts are still evolving or unavailable. "Just because we cannot get copyright permission to include the songs in the podcast, we just include a little clip for demonstration purposes... because there is yet no mechanism for us to acquire copyright permission for including music in something like a podcast."
**Demo (Demonstration Recording)** : A demo is a recording created by an artist to showcase their abilities and musical style. Demos vary widely in production quality, ranging from simple bedroom recordings on cassettes or laptop programs like GarageBand to self-financed full studio productions. They can serve as works in progress, aiding a song's evolution, or as tools to shop artists to record labels, managers, and venues. While fans appreciate demos for their insight into the creative process, some artists are hesitant to release unfinished material. Labels value demos for potential use in reissues, expanded editions, and box sets. "Demo is short for demonstration. It's a recording made by an artist to demonstrate what they can do and what their music sounds like."
**ISBN (International Standard Book Number)** : The ISBN is a unique 10 or 13-digit identifier assigned to every book, acting as a global standard for tracking publications since 1967. Each edition and variation of a book (e.g., hardcover, paperback, e-book) receives its own distinct ISBN, facilitating the organization and identification of the world's literary works. "If you look at any book... you will find a 10 or 13 digit number... This is called the ISBN, the international standard book number. Every book has one."
**ISRC (International Standard Recording Code)** : The ISRC is a 12-character alphanumeric code introduced in 1986, serving as a unique global identifier for sound recordings, similar to how ISBNs work for books. Each distinct recording, including every remix, edit, and version of a song, receives its own ISRC. The ultimate goal is to create a universal database where any recording can be immediately identified for copyright purposes. ISRCs are crucial in the digital age for ensuring that all parties involved in a recording—artists, producers, remixers, composers, labels, and publishers—are properly paid for its use, as unidentified recordings risk non-payment. "ISRC stands for International Standard Recording Code. And it does the same thing for music as ISBNs do for books. This numbering scheme, there are 12 characters in each code, came into use starting in 1986."
**ISRC Code Breakdown** : An ISRC (International Standard Recording Code) is a 12-character identifier for audio recordings. It typically consists of a two-character country code (e.g., "Gb" for Great Britain), followed by a three-character registrant code representing the right holder (e.g., "A-H-T" for Helium 3), a two-digit year of assignment (e.g., "17" for 2017), and a five-digit unique designation code for that specific recording. This granular detail allows for precise tracking and identification of every version of an audio track. "The Gb is the two character code denoting which country issued the number. Gb stands for Great Britain. So pretty straightforward. The A-H-T stands for the entity that registered this number, the right holder of this recording. In this case, I think it stands for Helium 3, the band's label, which is distributed by Warner Music Group. The 17 is the year the number was assigned. And the 00303 is the unique designation code assigned to that song."
