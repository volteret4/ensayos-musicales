## albums
**Sonic Highways (2014)** : This album features eight songs recorded in eight different cities across eight different studios, a process also documented in an HBO TV series. The band would absorb local vibes and talk with musicians in each city, with Dave Grohl then crafting lyrics from transcriptions of these conversations. The album was designed to lay bare the influences on its songs and the band members themselves. "Sonic Highways. Eight songs recorded in eight different cities in eight different studios."

## songs
**Something from Nothing (2014)** : This was the first single released from the "Sonic Highways" album. It was recorded at Electrical Audio in Chicago, a studio owned by Steve Albini, who previously recorded Nirvana's "In Utero." The track features guest guitar contributions from Rick Nielsen of Cheap Trick, a band that was a major favorite of the Foo Fighters members during their youth. "Something from nothing, the first single from the Foo Fighters 2014 album, Sonic Highways."
**Outside (2014)** : This song from the "Sonic Highways" album was recorded in the desert, specifically at a studio in Joshua Tree, California. This studio doubles as the owner's house, with clients typically bunking at the owner's place. The track features guest guitar contributions from Joe Walsh of The Eagles. "This is called Outside."
**Congregation (2014)** : Recorded at Southern Ground Studios in Nashville, this track is part of the "Sonic Highways" album. The recording location underscores the importance of Nashville in American music, and the song itself reflects the musical landscape of the city. "This next track was made into a place called Southern Ground Studios. It's called Congregation."
**Feast or Famine (2014)** : Featured on the "Sonic Highways" album, this song was recorded at Inner Ears Studios in Arlington, Virginia. This location is very close to where Dave Grohl grew up, and Inner Ears Studios is significant as many of Grohl's heroes made records there. The track includes guest musicianship from The Bad Brains, a legendary DC band and one of Grohl's all-time favorites. "It's called Feast or Famine."
**Subterranean (2014)** : This track from the "Sonic Highways" album was recorded at Robert Lang Studios, located north of Seattle. This studio is historically significant for the Foo Fighters as it's where their very first record was made, and it was also a favorite spot for Alice in Chains. Additionally, Nirvana recorded their last ever completed song, "You Know Your Right," at this same studio. The track features local guest musician Ben Gibbert of Death Cab for Cutie. "It's called Subterranean."
**Winnebago** : This Foo Fighters song is cited by Dave Grohl as featuring open guitar chords inspired by Rush, similar to many "crazy fills" from Rush records influencing Taylor Hawkins' drumming. "Like we have songs like Winnebago was one. Winnebago's the biggest."
**Times Like These** : Dave Grohl notes this Foo Fighters song for its sustained notes that "ring through a riff pattern," a guitar style he attributes to his listening to Rush. "And times like these sounds kind of like. Times like these like all of the sustained notes that sort of ring through a riff pattern. I got that from listening to Rush."

## curiosities
**Sonic Highways HBO TV Series** : Alongside the "Sonic Highways" album, the band produced an eight-week HBO TV series that meticulously documented the entire recording process. Each episode focused on a different city and song, exploring the local music scene and influences. "they also made an HBO TV series documenting the whole process."
**Dave Grohl's Songwriting Method for Sonic Highways** : For the "Sonic Highways" album, Dave Grohl developed a unique songwriting technique. After spending a week in each city talking with local musicians and absorbing the environment, he would compile a transcription of these conversations. He then cut and pasted words and phrases from these interviews to form the lyrics for that city's song. "Dave would sit down with a transcription of the conversations he'd had. And then sort of cut and paste words and phrases from those conversations and interviews into what would become the lyrics for that city's song."
**Band Members' Live Performance Joy** : The band is noted for the immense joy and fun they visibly exude during their live performances. The interviewer observed that there has never been a time when the band didn't appear to be having more fun than anyone should be allowed. "there are five guys on stage having more fun than anybody should ever be allowed to have."
**Dave Grohl's Filmmaking Endeavors** : Dave Grohl directed the "Sound City" documentary and was involved in the "Sonic Highways" HBO series. Despite success, he prefers his "day job" as a musician, stating that the filmmaking projects "just sort of fell into my lap" and that he likes getting "paid tons of money" for rock and roll. "I don't want to quit my day job because I like it a lot. Because we get paid tons of money."
**606 Studios Location** : The Foo Fighters' recording studio, 606 Studios, is located in Northridge, California. Surveillance audio taken on September 30, 2014, captured the outside of the studio, describing it as looking "like a fortress" in a "rough neighborhood." "This is playback of surveillance audio outside 606 Studios, the home of the food fighters, September 30th, 2014, 1216 PM, Pacific Daylight Time."

**Santa Barbara Music Scene** : Chris Schifflett grew up in Santa Barbara, California, a town he initially considered boring but later recognized for its vibrant music scene. The area hosted weekly keg parties with bands, a barn venue in Isle Vista, and was close enough to LA for him to attend shows. "But we got a great music scene. **Early Influences and Shift to Punk** : Chris Schifflett's early influences came from his brothers' music, including The Beatles, The Stones, Bowie, Led Zeppelin, and Aerosmith. In high school, he discovered punk rock, which was the prevalent live music scene in Santa Barbara and offered a more accessible social outlet compared to larger rock shows. "It wasn't until high school that I really got turned on to punk rock. And it was just like, because that was, you know, if you wanted to go to a show, there was no big rock show in Santa Barbara."

## curiosities
**Cousin Tracy's Influence** : At age 13, Dave Grohl visited his cousin Tracy in Chicago, who had recently become a punk rocker. She took him to see a punk band at a small, dingy club called The Cubby Bear, across from Wrigley Field. This experience, filled with raw energy and loud music, completely changed his life and redirected his musical aspirations from being a "guitar virtuoso God" on a poster to being a part of a dynamic, accessible punk band. "my cousin Tracy had just turned into a punk rocker and she said, hey, I'm going out tonight. Do you want to see a band?"
**Rush's 2112 Introduction** : Dave Grohl's cousin gave him the Rush album "2112" when he was in third grade, which was his first exposure to music beyond The Beatles and AM Radio. He was captivated by the unique sound and the prominent drums, but felt apprehensive about sharing it with friends due to the band's appearance, including their kimonos and Alex Lifeson's "camel toe." "I remember my, I had a cousin, another cousin, give me 2112, Um, maybe when I was about, I remember I was in third grade and um, up until that point, it had pretty much only been in the Beatles and AM Radio for me."
**Neil Peart as an Idol** : Dave Grohl identifies Rush drummer Neil Peart as a significant influence, stating that Peart was "my guy on the wall" when discussing his early musical heroes. This highlights the impact of Peart's drumming on Grohl's own musical development and appreciation. "Meal Perk was my guy on the wall."
**Alex Lifeson's Guitar Influence** : Dave Grohl admits to regularly incorporating elements of Alex Lifeson's guitar playing into Foo Fighters songs, particularly "anything weird" he does on guitar. He specifically notes that the sustained, ringing notes in riff patterns found in songs like "Times Like These" are directly inspired by listening to Rush. "to this day, I still rip off Alex Lifeson's guitar playing in a lot of Who Fight or Song."
**DC Hardcore as a Reason to Live** : Growing up in Virginia, Dave Grohl found immense personal and musical significance in the DC Hardcore scene, stating it "basically gave him a reason to live." This genre and local scene were so influential that the second episode of the "Sonic Highways" HBO series is dedicated to exploring its impact. "Dave Grohl grew up next door in Virginia. And DC Hardcore basically gave him a reason to live."

**Orange County Music Scene** : Growing up in Orange County, Laguna Beach, California, Taylor Hawkins observed a predominant reggae music scene, where hard rock was "frowned upon." He felt compelled to hide his Rush and Queen records from his brother and his friends to avoid being labeled a "hesher." "everybody played reggae music. Which was where? Orange County, Laguna Beach, California, and everybody liked reggae."
**First Paid Gig Experience** : Taylor Hawkins recounted his first experience getting paid for playing music in a cover band at a wedding. When the band wasn't paid, his mother went to the event organizer's workplace and demanded their money, successfully securing their payment. This marked his first financial gain from music, making him realize the potential for a career. "my mom went down to the people. The guy that worked, where the guy worked and spreeb. I want to be on a money. Amazing. Oh yeah. Mom got your money? Yeah, my mom got her money for us."
**Jane's Addiction as Personal "Punk Rock"** : Taylor Hawkins was disillusioned with the "blah music" of hair metal (like Warrant) during his high school years. Hearing Jane's Addiction was a transformative experience, as he found them "disgusting," "awesome," and "scary," providing a raw energy that resonated with him much like punk rock did for others. "And then I heard Jane's addiction and it was like, dude, I could play like the dudes in the poster. But they were a mess and they were disgusting and they were awesome and they were scary."
**Neil Peart's Drumming Influence** : Taylor Hawkins credits Neil Peart's drumming from Rush records for influencing "any crazy fill" he performs on the drums. This highlights Peart's lasting impact on Hawkins' percussive style within the Foo Fighters. "A lot of like anything weird that I do on the drums is usually ripped off from a rush record, like any crazy fill."

# genre - Punk Rock

## curiosities
**Simplicity and Accessibility** : Punk rock is characterized by its simplicity, often utilizing just three chords, making it highly accessible for aspiring musicians who may be intimidated by virtuoso rock stars. This ease of entry allowed kids to start bands and write songs about their immediate surroundings and concerns. "And then you hear a Ramones song and it's three chords and you can do that and then you have friends that might not play drums like Buddy Rich or but they can play drums like the guy from the Sex Bistels or because it's so simple, it becomes available to you like, oh cool, well, I could start a band if we do it like this."
**Comparison to Folk Music** : Dave Grohl likens punk rock to folk music, emphasizing its role in allowing young people to write about topics important to them, such as their neighborhood or home life, rather than generic rock themes. He suggests this makes it a form of "reporting" on real-life experiences. "it is like folk music in that you have these kids writing about things that are important to them. You're not going to have a kid in Springfield, Virginia, writing songs about Jack Daniels and mud wrestlers and limousines. You're going to be writing about what's going on in your neighborhood or your home and in that like folk music, it's kind of like reporting, you know, like you're writing about things that really happen to you."
