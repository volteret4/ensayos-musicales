# curiosity

**Music Cycles and Revivals** : Music often follows cycles where a sound gains popularity, peaks, fades, but rarely disappears completely. Instead, many rock sounds lie in wait for a generation or two before being rediscovered, reactivated, and given a "sonic update," enjoying a new period of popularity before the cycle repeats. "A sound and style will be big for a while, reach a peak with the public, and then slowly fade out." "Instead, what we've seen, rockabilly aside, after enjoying a time at the forefront of music, many of the cycle-prone rock sounds were seeded into the shadows, never really go away, lying in wait until someone comes along, often a generation or two later, to rediscover and reactivate it."
**2010s Combacks as Industry Health Indicator** : The comebacks of established "monsters of rock" in the 2010s, such as Tool, Guns N' Roses, Pearl Jam, and Metallica, were crucial for the health of both the recorded music industry and the touring industry, as well as music in general. These acts continued to fuel massive tours and album sales, largely supported by Boomers and Gen Xers. "The comebacks that we saw in the 2010s were an extremely important part of the health of the recorded music industry, also the touring industry, and you know what, music in general."
**Dominance of Pre-Internet Rock Bands in Touring** : In the 2010s, the biggest rock bands capable of filling arenas, amphitheaters, and stadiums were overwhelmingly those who had established their careers and achieved many big hits before the internet disrupted the music industry around 2000. Very few new rock acts emerged post-2000 with the same touring power. "The biggest rock bands in the world were still those who managed to establish careers with many big hits before the internet came along and disrupted everything." "And since 2000, very few new rock acts, relatively speaking when compared to the olden days, have emerged who can do the same thing."
**Boomer and Gen X Fuel for Massive Tours and Album Sales** : Boomers and Gen Xers continued to be the primary demographic fueling massive rock tours and purchasing a significant number of albums in the 2010s. This sustained the commercial viability of established acts and their comebacks. "The BOOMers and Gen Xers continue to fuel these massive tours while continuing to buy non-insignificant numbers of albums."
**Rock's Overall Consumption in the 2010s** : Despite hip hop being the dominant cultural force in many territories, rock was still performing well in the 2010s in terms of overall consumption, which included records sold, songs streamed, and videos watched. Furthermore, rock remained the most consumed genre by concert tickets sold in countries like Canada, the US, and the UK. "rock was still doing well in terms of overall consumption."
**Cross-Generational Music Consumption** : In the 2010s, music became significantly more cross-generational than ever before, with audiences at shows by bands like Metallica and Soundgarden spanning a wide range of ages, from "grey hairs to preteens." While generation gaps still existed, it became increasingly common for parents and children to share a love for the same music and artists. "Music is now far more cross-generational than it's ever been." "But now, more than ever, it's very common for a parent and their child to share a love for the same music, the same act."
**Technological Advancements and Progressive Rock** : The increasing complexity of progressive rock in the late 1960s and early 1970s was enabled by technological advancements. Musicians became more skilled, leading to a focus on virtuosity, and new equipment alongside more sophisticated recording studios allowed for the creation of new and intricate sounds. "New equipment allowed for new sounds, and recording studios installed ever more sophisticated gear."
**DIY Ethic in Music Strengthened by Technology** : The do-it-yourself (DIY) ethic in music, particularly within genres like punk, has become stronger due to technology democratizing and decentralizing music. While record labels once assisted with promotion, the intense competition and "noise" in the modern music landscape necessitate artists working harder than ever to get noticed independently. "In fact, I think that DIY ethic has become stronger since technology democratized and decentralized all of music."
