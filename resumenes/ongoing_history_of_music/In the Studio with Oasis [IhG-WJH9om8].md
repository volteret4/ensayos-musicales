## songs
**Columbia (1993) - First Recorded Single & Noel's First Song for Oasis** : This was the very first single Oasis recorded and the first song Noel Gallagher wrote for the band. It was inspired by an infamous hotel in London, known for its lenient management, where Oasis was eventually banned for life after throwing bar furniture out a window. Musically, it appears to have been inspired by "Swamp Thing" (1986) by The Comelians and "Axe Corner" by Tortuga. It was first performed in early 1992, either January 14th or Valentine's Day, and later featured in their pivotal King Tut's Wawa Hut set on May 31, 1993. A white label demo was released in December 1993, recorded in Real People's studios, and included samples from an unidentified movie, unknown chants, and a BBC interview clip of British politician Tony Benn. This initial release was 100% independent, with music on only one side of a 12-inch vinyl, accompanied by a plain wrapper sticker. Very few copies were pressed, primarily going to journalists and radio DJs. "Columbia was the first Oasis song ever played on the radio. It was featured on a BBC Radio One show called Evening Session."
**Bring It Down - Unreleased Early Track** : This song was a massive hit with audiences when Oasis played at the Boardwalk club in Manchester. It was the original goal for their first studio sessions at Pink Museum Studio, but the band struggled to capture its live magic in the studio, with Noel Gallagher blaming drummer Tony McCarroll for failing to keep a steady beat. "The original goal was to record a song called Bring It Down, a massive hit with audiences when Oasis was playing at that boardwalk club in Manchester."
**Supersonic (1994) - Quick Creation and Noel's Favorite** : This song, one of the big singles from the debut album "Definitely Maybe," was written and completed in less than 12 hours. It originated when Noel Gallagher, frustrated by unproductive sessions for "Bring It Down" at Pink Museum Studio, retreated to a back room while the rest of the band and producer ate Chinese food, and started noodling with a riff. The lyric "a girl called Elsa, she's into Elka Celtser" is a direct reference to Elsa, the studio's farty resident Rottweiler. Noel considers it his favorite Oasis song. "Even though Noll was constantly being pestered by Elsa, the studio's extremely farty resident rotwiler, which explains the lyric, a girl called Elsa, she's into Elka Celtser."
**Live Forever (1994) - An Answer to Nirvana** : Noel Gallagher claimed this as the first good song he wrote for Oasis, with origins tracing back to 1991 when he was working for British Gas in Manchester. After a foot injury, he was reassigned to a parts desk storeroom, a job so undemanding that he began bringing his guitar to work and writing songs. The song took years to develop, starting as a mess of chords and a rough melody. The opening chords were first played repeatedly during soundchecks for Inspiral Carpets. Inspiration struck while listening to The Rolling Stones' "Shine a Light" from "Exile on Main Street," prompting the phrase "maybe I don't really want to know." Noel intended it as an answer to Nirvana's "I Hate Myself and Want to Die," annoyed by Kurt Cobain's misery despite his fame. When presented to the band, they initially disbelieved Noel had written it due to its quality. It was recorded at Clear studio in Manchester and released on August 8, 1994, as the third advance single for "Definitely Maybe," bringing Oasis their first worldwide attention. "He'd later say that Live Forever is supposed to be an answer to the Nirvana song I hate myself and want to die."
**Some Might Say (1995) - First Single from Morning Glory** : This song was the first single released from the "What's the Story Morning Glory" album. It serves as a prime example of Noel Gallagher's prolific songwriting, where even the A-sides were exceptional, and Noel was committed to its release as a single. "The hit with this CD single was Some Might Say, the first single from the What's the Story Morning glory album, and it's a great example of how Noll had so many great songs even the B-sides and bonus tracks were better than most band singles."
**Acquiesce (1995) - B-Side with a Breakthrough Story** : Originally a B-side to "Some Might Say," this song, like "Supersonic," came together at the very last minute. In February 1995, Noel Gallagher was traveling by train to Loco studio in Wales when the train broke down in the Severant Tunnel, leaving him stuck for four hours. He overheard a passenger use the word "Aquies" during a phone call, liked the sound, and began strumming the opening chords, working out 90% of the arrangement despite a fellow passenger's protest to stop playing. Liam Gallagher sang the verses, and Noel sang the chorus because Liam, recovering from a throat infection after dates in Japan, couldn't hit the high notes. Alan McGee was so impressed he wanted it as the single, but Noel insisted on "Some Might Say." It eventually gained recognition, promoted as the single for the 1998 compilation "The Masterplan" and also appearing on "Stop the Clocks." "When the song made it to the studio, it was decided that Liam would sing the verses and Noll would take care of the chorus because Liam just couldn't hit those high notes."
**Wonderwall (1995) - Iconic Hit and Noel's Daily Earner** : Originally titled "Wishing Stone," the meaning of this song has evolved over time; initially about Noel Gallagher's then-girlfriend Meg Matthews, it later morphed into being about an imaginary savior and was even connected to George Harrison's 1968 album "Wonder Wall Music." Written quickly in the months leading up to the "What's the Story Morning Glory" album, Noel sought a stripped-down vibe inspired by Portishead's "Dummy." Recorded at Rockfield Studios in rural Wales in May 1995 over half a day, the title may also refer to a brick wall on the studio grounds where Noel would sit and strum his guitar. Noel played guitar and bass, Alan White provided a shuffling groove, and producer Owen Morris added intensity with "brick walling," a cello, and a four-note piano motif. Liam Gallagher chose to sing this song over "Don't Look Back In Anger." It was first heard backstage at Glastonbury on June 24, 1995, for a BBC TV special and was performed sporadically that summer. Released as the fourth single from "Morning Glory," it reached number two in the UK, number one in Canada, Australia, and New Zealand, and number eight on the Billboard Hot 100 in the US, where it was the number one alternative song. "Noll once told me that he makes about 3,000 pounds a day from the song."
**Don't Look Back in Anger (1996) - A Second British National Anthem** : The fifth single from "Morning Glory," this was the first Oasis single sung exclusively by Noel Gallagher. He wrote it on Tuesday, April 18, 1995, on a rainy night in Paris after returning from a strip club, while Oasis was opening for The Verve. Inspiration came from David Bowie's "All the Young Dudes" (performed by Mott the Hoople) and a bootlegged tape of John Lennon's memoirs (stolen after his murder on December 8, 1980), particularly Lennon's quote about "trying to start a revolution from me bed." The piano sounds also share similarities with Lennon's "Imagine" and "Watching the Wheels." Six days later, Liam Gallagher misheard Noel singing "Sally can wait" during a soundcheck for their first arena headlining gig in Sheffield; Noel, not having specific lyrics, then finished the song in the dressing room with the iconic "don't look back and anger." Noel performed it live acoustically hours later. "Sally" is ambiguous, possibly a nod to The Stone Roses' "Sally Cinnamon." Recorded in the same two-week span as "Wonderwall" in Wales, its official single release was February 19, 1996. It has since become a "second British national anthem," sung at funerals, weddings, and as a healing song after the Manchester concert bombing on May 22, 2017. "And it even became a healing song for the general public after the horrendous Manchester concert bombing on May 22nd, 2017."
**Champagne Supernova (1996) - Psychedelic Closer with Cosmic Retrospect** : The longest recording in the Oasis catalog, this song closed "What's the Story Morning Glory" and was its sixth and final single. The title was inspired by the Pixies' 1990 album "Bossanova" combined with something Noel Gallagher misheard on a BBC documentary about Champagne. Noel admits he has no idea what the song is about and considers it his most psychedelic track. Paul Weller made a notable guest appearance, playing lead guitar, whistling, and contributing background vocals. In a curious retrospective link, on April 24, 2003, astronomers discovered SN 2003 FG, a Type IA Supernova 3.5 billion light-years away, and named it the "Champagne Supernova." "No one, including Noll has any idea what the song is about. Whatever you get out of it and how are you interpreted is just fine with him."

## curiosities
**Noel Gallagher's Songwriting Prolificacy** : Noel Gallagher went through a period in the 90s where he consistently produced hits, with even Oasis B-sides and bonus tracks often surpassing the official singles of other bands in quality. "Noel Gallagher went through a period where he cranked out hit after hit after hit, even B-sides and bonus tracks could be better than another band's official singles."
**Formation and First Gig** : Noel Gallagher, while working as a roadie for Inspiral Carpets, wrote songs in his downtime, amassing home demos by September 1989. In April 1990, the band Rain was co-founded by Paul "Bonehead" Arthurs and Chris Hutton, playing gigs at the Boardwalk in Manchester. Noel joined mid-1990, took over, and his younger brother became the singer. The group's first official gig as Oasis was on August 9, 1991. "The group's first gig as Oasis was on August 9, 1991."
**Early Studio Sessions & Song Recycling** : Oasis engaged in several recording sessions in 1992, early in their career. Many of the songs recorded during these initial sessions would later appear on their subsequent albums. "There were also a few recording sessions along the way, and many of the songs would end up on subsequent albums."
**Hotel Ban in London** : The band Oasis once emptied out a bar in an infamous London hotel by throwing all the furniture out the window. This incident led to them receiving a lifetime ban from the establishment. "By that, I mean, they threw all the furniture in the bar out the window. Not so lenient. That got him a lifetime ban from the place."
**Discovery and Signing by Alan McGee** : On May 31, 1993, Oasis, after hitching a ride from Manchester with 18 Wheeler, literally bullied the owner of King Tut's Wawa Hut in Glasgow into allowing them to play a four-song set. Alan McGee, head of Creation Records, accidentally arrived early that night and inadvertently caught their performance, which was enough to prompt him to sign the band. "This was the night Alan McGee, the head of creation records, accidentally showed up early and inadvertently against his will, caught the Oasis set. But it was enough for him to sign the band."
**White Label Demo Release** : In December 1993, Oasis released a white label demo of "Columbia." This was a 100% independent release with no record company affiliation at all, distributed on a 12-inch vinyl with music on only one side. The plain wrapper bore a sticker that read, "not wishing to wind everybody up, but Oasis have got everything here for yourself, and this is the only demo. Anyone up for more?" "In this case, there was music on just one side of this 12 inch. The sticker on the plain wrapper red, not wishing to wind everybody up, but Oasis have got everything here for yourself, and this is the only demo."
**Noel Gallagher's Inspiration from Studio Dog** : During the creation of "Supersonic" at Pink Museum Studio, Noel Gallagher was continuously pestered by Elsa, the studio's extremely farty resident Rottweiler. This dog directly inspired the lyric "a girl called Elsa, she's into Elka Celtser" in the song. "Even though Noll was constantly being pestered by Elsa, the studio's extremely farty resident rotwiler, which explains the lyric, a girl called Elsa, she's into Elka Celtser."
**Noel Gallagher's British Gas Injury and Songwriting** : In 1991, while working for British Gas in Manchester, Noel Gallagher suffered a serious foot injury when a large steel cap from a gas pipe fell on his foot. This led to his reassignment to the parts desk storeroom, a non-strenuous job where he often had downtime, allowing him to bring his guitar to work and write songs, including early ideas for "Live Forever." "A big steel cap from a large gas pipe had fallen on his foot, causing a serious injury. So he was reassigned to the parts desk store room."
**Band's Disbelief in Noel's Songwriting Talent** : When Noel Gallagher first presented "Live Forever" to the band, they initially did not believe he had written it, as they considered the song "too good" for him to have composed. "When he finally presented the song to the band, they didn't believe that he'd written it. It was too good in their opinion."
**Noel Gallagher's Lack of Driver's License** : Noel Gallagher did not have a driver's license in February 1995 when he was taking a train to Loco studio in Wales, and he still does not possess one. "Noll, of course, didn't have a driver's license and he still doesn't."
**Passenger's Frustration During "Acquiesce" Creation** : While Noel Gallagher was stuck in the Severant Tunnel and continuously strumming the opening chords of what would become "Acquiesce," a fellow passenger eventually screamed, "The Amine mate. We've heard enough of the guitar now." "something that eventually prompted one of the passengers to scream, the Amine mate. We've heard enough of the guitar now."
**Liam Gallagher's Vocal Challenges During "Acquiesce" Recording** : During the recording of "Acquiesce," Liam Gallagher, who had just returned from a series of dates in Japan, was recovering from a throat infection. This condition prevented him from hitting the high notes, leading to Noel Gallagher taking over the chorus vocals. "The band had just come back from a series of dates in Japan and Liam was recovering from a throat infection."
**Noel Gallagher's Dismissal of "Brotherly Love" Narrative** : Despite "Acquiesce" often being perceived as a symbol of brotherly love between Noel and Liam Gallagher, Noel himself dismissed this interpretation, stating it was "just BS" but that he goes along with the story anyway. "No, Noll says that it's just BS, but he's going along with the story anyway."
**"Wonderwall" Title Interpretations** : The title "Wonderwall" has multiple potential origins and meanings according to Noel Gallagher. Initially, it was about his then-girlfriend Meg Matthews. After their 2001 divorce, it morphed into being about an imaginary person who would save him. He later connected it to George Harrison's 1968 album "Wonder Wall Music." Additionally, it may refer to a brick wall at Rockfield Studios where he would sit and play guitar, potentially seeing it as his "songwriting Wonder Wall." "The brick construction was perhaps his songwriting Wonder Wall."
**Vocal Choice for Liam Gallagher** : For the recording of "Wonderwall," Noel Gallagher presented Liam with a choice between singing "Wonderwall" or "Don't Look Back In Anger," stating that Noel would sing whichever one Liam did not choose. Liam opted for "Wonderwall." "When it came to recording the vocals, Noll gave Liam a choice. You do this one or don't look back an anger. Whichever one you don't choose, I'll do."
**First Public Performance of "Wonderwall"** : The song "Wonderwall" was first heard backstage at Glastonbury on June 24, 1995, during a behind-the-scenes BBC TV special. Oasis had headlined the night before and chosen to remain for the weekend. "The first time anyone heard this song was backstage at Glastonbury on June 24th, 1995, for a behind-the-scenes BBC TV special."
**Noel Gallagher's Earnings from "Wonderwall"** : Due to the immense popularity and iconic status of "Wonderwall," Noel Gallagher once stated that he earns approximately £3,000 per day from the song. "Noll once told me that he makes about 3,000 pounds a day from the song."
**Don't Look Back In Anger Origin in Paris Strip Club** : Noel Gallagher wrote "Don't Look Back In Anger" on a rainy night in Paris, specifically after returning from a strip club, on April 18, 1995, while Oasis was opening for The Verve. "or even more specifically, after Noll had come back from a strip club."
**John Lennon's Bootlegged Memoirs as Inspiration** : Noel Gallagher was inspired by a bootlegged tape of John Lennon's memoirs, which had been stolen from his Dakota apartment after his murder on December 8, 1980. The tape, widely circulated, contained Lennon's quote about "trying to start a revolution from me bed," which deeply impressed Noel and influenced "Don't Look Back In Anger." "at a tape that had been apparently stolen from John Lenin's apartment at the Dakota after he was murdered on December 8, 1980."
**"Sally Can Wait" Origin** : The phrase "Sally can wait" in "Don't Look Back In Anger" originated from Liam Gallagher mishearing Noel singing nonsense lyrics during a soundcheck. While Noel was merely keeping the melody with made-up words, Liam's question "are you saying Sally can wait?" prompted Noel to finish the lyrics in the dressing room. "And Liam asked, are you saying Sally can wait? And Noll says, I don't know what I'm singing."
**Noel Gallagher's Initial Regret for "Don't Look Back In Anger"** : Early in the song's history, Noel Gallagher expressed that if he had known the extent to which "Don't Look Back In Anger" would be used at funerals and weddings, he might never have finished it due to the immense pressure. Despite this, it later became one of his all-time favorite Oasis songs. "Noll has been quoted as saying back in the beginning, that'll be pretty good when we record it. But if I'd known that night what I know now about people playing it at funerals and weddings, I'd never have finished this song too much pressure."
**Paul Weller's Guest Appearance on "Champagne Supernova"** : Noel Gallagher idolized Paul Weller's work since childhood in The Jam, The Style Council, and his solo career. They became friends and even lived across from each other in London. Weller contributed lead guitar, whistling, and background vocals to "Champagne Supernova." "Paul Weller. Ever since he was a kid, no idolized Willers work in the jam, the style council, and his solo work."

**Discovery of the "Champagne Supernova" (SN 2003 FG)** : On April 24, 2003, two deep space telescopes in Hawaii (one co-built by Canada) discovered SN 2003 FG, a Type IA Supernova, in a galaxy 3.5 billion light-years away in the constellation of Boötes. Researchers at the University of Toronto made the discovery, announced by David Branch. Due to its impressive nature, astronomers named this celestial event the "Champagne Supernova," connecting it posthumously to the Oasis song. "On April 24, 2003, two deep space telescopes in Hawaii, and one co-built by Canada, discovered SN 2003 FG, a Type I A Supernova, and a galaxy, 3.5 billion light years away in the constellation of Boots."
