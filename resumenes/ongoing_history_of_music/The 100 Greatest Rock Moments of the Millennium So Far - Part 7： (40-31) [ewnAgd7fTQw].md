## curiosities
**Lawsuit Against Napster (2000)** : Metallica, notably with drummer Lars Ulrich as the public face, filed a lawsuit against Napster on April 30, 2000, after discovering their unreleased demo "I Disappear" and their entire catalog were being freely traded online. Lars Ulrich physically delivered a 60,000-page list of 335,435 alleged Metallica song pirates to Napster's offices. The band sought at least $10 million in damages, a ban for all users trading Metallica songs, and university crackdowns on student downloading. This action generated intense fan backlash, damaging Metallica's reputation as "rich rock stars, bullying fans." Despite this, Napster was ultimately shut down in July 2001 after settling with Metallica and Dr. Dre, confirming that its practices violated copyright. "On April 30, 2000, they made a big deal of launching a lawsuit with drummer Lars Alrich becoming the face of the proceedings."

# instrument - Electric Guitar

## curiosities
**Decline in Sales (2010s)** : In the 2010s, electric guitar sales plummeted, a significant shift from its historical status as a primary instrument in rock. This decline was attributed to several factors: the economic downturn making instruments expensive, the rise of non-guitar-centric genres (hip hop, pop, EDM, R&B), a decrease in inspiring guitar heroes, and the focus of TV talent shows on fame over craft. Additionally, the proliferation of digital music production tools like laptops, GarageBand, Ableton Live, and MIDI controllers allowed for complex music creation without traditional instrument practice, while declining music education in schools and social media's short attention spans reduced interest in learning guitar. The traditional consumer base, older white men, also began buying less. "For most of rock history, the electric guitar was the go-to instrument for millions of one of the rock stars. But in the 2010s, guitar sales plummeted."
**Sales Reversal (Late 2010s/Early 2020s)** : The decline in electric guitar sales has shown signs of reversal, partly due to the COVID-19 pandemic, which led people to learn guitar out of boredom and enjoy the physical nature of playing. The continued popularity of rock, and the use of guitars in certain hip hop and pop styles, also contributed. Manufacturers responded by offering more affordable instruments and amps. Crucially, a new generation of guitar heroes emerged, including John Mayer, Greta Van Fleet, Tim Henson of Polyphia, and a notable number of female artists such as H.E.R., Yvette Young, Courtney Barnett, Orianthi, Ellie Rowsell of Wolf Alice, Lari Basilio, and St. Vincent, whose talent and success helped inspire new interest. "There are, however, signs that this trend has been reversed."

**AI's Growing Influence in Music** : Artificial intelligence has rapidly grown in influence within the music world, democratizing music creation with tools like MuseNet, Suno, and Magenta, and enabling text-to-music capabilities and sound design experimentation. The technology has led to the emergence of "virtual artists" and the concept of "digital music slaves." AI is also integral to algorithms for song recommendations, market prediction for industry companies, and new fan engagement strategies for artists. However, its rise has also introduced ambiguities around originality, authenticity, copyright infringement, and ethical concerns regarding deep fakes and unauthorized voice use. Additionally, AI is used in dynamic pricing for concert tickets and in creating soundscapes for music therapy. "The growing influence affects an interdependence between AI and music."
**Music Biopic Trend (Last 20 Years)** : Over the past two decades, music biopics have become a major phenomenon, chronicling the lives of artists such as Elton John, Johnny Cash, Bob Dylan, Amy Winehouse, Bob Marley, Whitney Houston, David Bowie, Tupac, Notorious B.I.G., Kurt Cobain, Jimi Hendrix, Ray Charles, James Brown, Aretha Franklin, N.W.A., The Runaways, and Joy Division's Ian Curtis. The popularity is driven by nostalgia, fascinating artist stories, cross-generational appeal, younger audiences' curiosity about legends, and significant box office success. **Napster's Impact and Lawsuits (1999-2001)** : Napster, launched by college student Sean Fanning on June 1, 1999, quickly amassed millions of users by facilitating online music sharing via MP3s. This caught the recorded music industry, accustomed to selling physical CDs, completely off guard. Artists, including Metallica and Dr. Dre, found their music being pirated and subsequently filed lawsuits. Metallica's suit, fronted by Lars Ulrich, notably targeted 335,435 users and sought $10 million in damages. While facing intense fan backlash, the legal actions ultimately led to Napster's shutdown in July 2001, proving its copyright violations. Despite Napster's demise, numerous copycat services emerged (Rockster, AudioGalaxy, LimeWire, KaZaA), forcing the industry to reluctantly embrace the digital age and eventually leading to new models like the iTunes Music Store. "Napster was created by Sean Fanning, a college student who wanted to find a better way of finding, organizing, and sharing music online using this new thing called the MP3."
