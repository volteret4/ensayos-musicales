## curiosities
**Birth and Early Life (1964)** : Gordon Edgar Downey was born on February 6, 1964, in Amherstville, Ontario, located across Collins Bay from Kingston Proper. His father, also named Edgar, was initially a traveling salesman before transitioning into real estate, while his mother, Lorna, cared for him and his siblings. Gord was the second youngest among his brothers Mike and Patrick, and sisters Charlotte and Paula. "Gordon Edgar Downey was born on February 6, 1964 in Amherstville, Ontario."
**Family and Hockey Influence** : Gord's godfather was Harry Sinden, a family friend who later became the coach of Team Canada in the 1972 Canada-Russia series and subsequently worked as coach and general manager for the Boston Bruins. This connection influenced Gord's childhood passion for the Bruins, leading him to wear number four like Bobby Orr, before switching to play goal, a position he thoroughly enjoyed. "His godfather was Harry Sinden, a friend of the family. Harry would later go on to be the coach of Team Canada and the 72 Canada Russia series and then work as coach in GM with the Boston Bruins. And this is why as a kid, Gord was such a Bruins fan."
**Hockey Career** : As a bantam player, Gord played for Ernestown, located west of Kingston, where he backstopped his team to a provincial B championship. Hockey remained a lifelong part of his identity. "As a bantam, he played for Ernestown, which is just a little further to the West and backstop the team to a provincial B championship."
**High School Athletics and Music** : While attending Kingston Collegiate and Vocational School, Gord attempted basketball but found being in a band to be a much greater priority. He was known to skip games and practices to perform with his groups. "In high school, he tried basketball at Kingston, Collegiate and Vocational School. But by that time being in a band was way more important."
**Enrollment in Film Studies** : After finishing high school and playing with The Filters, Gord grew tired of performing punky covers and decided to enroll in film studies at Queen's University. It was there that he reconnected with Rob Baker and Gord Sinclair, leading to the formation of The Tragically Hip. "Eventually Gord got tired of playing the punky covers and left to enroll in film studies at Queens, and that's where he ran into Rob Baker and Gord Sinclair again."
**Death (2017)** : Gord Downie passed away on October 18, 2017. His death was met with widespread national mourning, with one poignant tweet summarizing the sentiment as "Canada closed, death in the family." "And when Gord died on October 18, 2017, it seemed like the whole country went into morning."
**Hopefulness in Songwriting** : Gord expressed a personal artistic inclination to incorporate a sense of hopefulness into his songs, even when exploring bleak sentiments. He found it challenging at his age and stage to leave a song entirely without a glimmer of positivity, believing listeners would desire some form of eventual resolution or recovery. "It'd be hard for me now at this age and stage to leave a song without a glimmer of hope fullness."
**Live Performance Philosophy** : When performing live, Gord described feeling both thrilled and nervous, akin to his initial rehearsals as a teenager. He embraced the improvisational nature of live shows, viewing mistakes or equipment failures as opportunities for spontaneous action, such as twirling a broken mic stand like a baton. "It's very improvisational. But it's sort of, and I like to say that if something goes wrong, it's almost better."
**Future Remembrance** : Future plans include exploring Gord Downie's solo career, his activism, poetry, and the story of his final months, alongside the band's journey through the 1990s. "On part two of our remembrance of Gord Downey in the tragically hip, we'll follow the band through the 1990s. And then when we get to the 2000s, we'll veer into Gord's solo world, his activism, his poetry, and ultimately the story of his last months."

## albums
**Up To Here (1989)** : This was The Tragically Hip's debut album, released on September 5, 1989. After a gradual start, it achieved sustained success, selling over a million copies in Canada across CD, vinyl, and digital formats. The album introduced audiences to the band's distinctive style and featured a song known for extended jams and lyrical stream-of-consciousness rants. "The hip's first album was up to here. It arrived on September 5th, 1989, and after a bit of a slow start, it began a burn that continues today."
**Road Apples (1991)** : Released in 1991, this album continued the band's commercial success, also selling a million copies in Canada. It marked a significant delve into Canadiana, with Gord Downie drawing deep inspiration from Canadian places, people, and locations. The original working title for the album was "Saskadelpia," but it was ultimately released under the more rural Canadian term "Road Apples," referring to horse manure. "Road apples in 1991. It also sold a million copies in Canada."
**Fully Completely (1992)** : Released in 1992, this album is regarded by some as The Tragically Hip's best work, serving as an extensive lesson in Canadian history and geography. It weaves together references to historical figures, political events, and geographical locations across Canada. "After road apples came what is probably the best tragically hip album, fully completely in 1992."

**Locked in the Trunk of a Car (1992)** : This song from "Fully Completely" draws its inspiration from the 1970 kidnapping and murder of Quebec deputy premier Pierre Laporte during the FLQ crisis. ## curiosities
**Formation (1984)** : The Tragically Hip was formed in 1984 when Gord Downie reconnected with Rob Baker and Gord Sinclair at Queen's University. They recruited high school student Johnny Fay on drums and a more experienced saxophone player from British Columbia named Davis Manning. "This was 1984."
**Band Name Origin** : The band adopted the name "The Tragically Hip" from a throwaway phrase found in a VHS tape filled with skits by Mike Nesmith, formerly of The Monkees. Despite not considering it a "great name," they needed a name for an upcoming gig. "And it was then that they settled on a name, the tragically hip, which was a throwaway phrase in a VHS tape full of skits, released by Mike Nessmith, formerly of the monkeys."
**Lineup Change (1986)** : In 1986, Davis Manning, the saxophone player who was about 15 years older than the other members and brought valuable experience, left the band. He was replaced by Paul Langua, who became the new rhythm guitarist and was noted for his skill in playing Rolling Stones songs, shifting the band's sound from saxophone-driven to more guitar-oriented. "In 1986, Manning left, and his replacement was Paul Langua. So instead of a saxophone player, the hip had a new rhythm guitarist, who was pretty good at playing some stone songs."
**Early Gigs and Demos** : The band spent considerable time on the road, playing venues like the Terrapin Tavern and the Lakeview Manor in Kingston, the latter described as a "not great spot" hosting strippers by day and touring bands by night. After packing the Men at Home in Kingston, they gained the confidence and material to record demos, which they sent to radio stations, battle of the bands contests, and potential venues. "The hip started drawing crowds of places like the Terrapin Tavern in Kingston, and then the Lakeview Manor."
**Management Deal** : The band's big break came when a friend gave their demos to Hugh Seagull, a former senator, who then passed them to conservative political figure Alan Gray. Gray, a music enthusiast and partner in a management company with Jake Gold, was impressed by the demos and signed on to manage the band after seeing them live. "The hip got a big break when a friend gave a copy of their demos to Hugh Seagall, a friend and former senator in Ottawa. He delivered it to conservative poster Alan Gray, who was very much into music."
**Debut EP and Record Deal** : The Tragically Hip recorded a debut EP in Toronto, which served as their calling card to generate interest from record labels. They were subsequently signed to MCA Records after a showcase performance at the Horseshoe Tavern in Toronto. MCA Records reissued their debut EP, marking the true beginning of their story. "The tragically hip was signed to MCA Records after they showcased their stuff at the horseshoe tavern in Toronto. And the first thing that the label did was reissue that debut EP."
**Live Performance Style** : Live performances frequently featured extended instrumental jams and Gord Downie's lyrical stream-of-consciousness rants. These often involved storytelling, such as the "famous Killer Whale Tank tale," or spontaneous interpolations of other songs, and served as a workshop for developing new material, including songs like "Nautical Disaster" and "Ahead by a Century." "Sometimes he tell a story in the middle of the song, like the famous Killer Whale Tank tale. Sometimes Gord would just go off and start seeing another song."
**The 1990s - Golden Age** : The 1990s are identified as the "golden age" for The Tragically Hip, during which they released commercially successful and critically acclaimed albums like "Up To Here," "Road Apples," and "Fully Completely," each selling over a million copies in Canada. "The 1990s were the band's golden age."
**Canadiana in Music** : Beginning notably with "Road Apples" and intensifying with "Fully Completely," The Tragically Hip delved deeply into "Canadiana," extensively referencing Canadian places, people, and historical events. This approach was a significant aspect of Gord Downie's lyrical inspiration. "It's on this album that Gord and the hip really started to dig deep into Canadiana, this idea of name checking places, people, and locations across the country."
**Band Humor and Support** : The band's internal humor often emerged from difficult "bottom of the barrel moments" that would be crushing if experienced alone. This shared humor and mutual support allowed them to navigate challenges, with the brain's natural tendency to forget hardships aiding in their resilience. "Generally, it's really funny stuff, and that humor, I think, comes from these bare bottom of the barrel moments that would crush people, even if they knew that they happened to you."
**Autobiographical Reticence** : The Tragically Hip consistently avoided creating chronological autobiographies or officially authorizing any books about the band throughout their three-decade career, leaving many stories untold despite their dedicated fanbase. "They've never been interested in any kind of chronological autobiography, and no book about them has ever received official authorization from the band."
