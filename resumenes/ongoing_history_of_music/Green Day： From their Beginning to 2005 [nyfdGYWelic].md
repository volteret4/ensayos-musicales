## albums
**Sweet Children EP (1988)** : First record released by the band under the name Sweet Children, issued on Skin Records.
**1000 Hours (1989)** : First 7-inch EP released by the band under the name Green Day on local indie label Lookout Records.
**Slappy (1990)** : A four-song, 7-inch EP released by Green Day.
**39 Smooth (1990)** : Vinyl-only album for Lookout Records, one of the first two albums to sell approximately 70,000 copies combined with Kerplunk.
**Kerplunk (1992)** : Indie album released on Lookout Records on January 17, 1992, containing an early version of "Welcome to Paradise." Originally called "Liquid Duckey." Its CD booklet contains a false story about a girl murdering her parents to see Green Day. It was one of the first two albums to sell approximately 70,000 copies combined with 39 Smooth.
**Dookie (1994)** : Released on February 1, 1994, this album stayed on the charts for two years and sold over 10 million copies, establishing Green Day as superstars and paving the way for numerous pop punk bands.
**Insomniac (1995)** : The follow-up to Dookie, named because Billy Joe Armstrong's oldest son Joey cried so much as a baby, keeping his parents up all night. Sales were "okay" compared to Dookie.
**Nimrod (1997)** : Album whose sales were "okay" in the shadow of Dookie.
**Warning (2000)** : Album whose sales were "okay" in the shadow of Dookie.
**Lost Album (2002)** : Master tapes for an album with about 20 recorded songs vanished from the studio, making it forever the "lost Green Day album."
**American Idiot (2004)** : Described as a "brilliant" comeback album and a 13-track punk rock opera, released in 2004. It proved that "old punks don't necessarily have to fade away" and sold several million copies in a very short order. It features a complex storyline involving characters like St. Jimmy and an unnamed narrator, exploring themes of nihilism, crisis of faith, consumerism, paranoia, alienation, war, neurosis, and introspection.

## curiosities
**Unlikely Origins (Late 1980s)** : The band was formed by a high school dropout (Billy Joe Armstrong), the son of a heroin addict (Mike Dirnt), and a 12-year-old wannabe rock star (Tré Cool, implied), making their longevity highly improbable.
**Initial Band Names** : Billy Joe Armstrong's band was called "At Socrates" before changing to "Sweet Children," which then became "Green Day."
**Green Day Name Origin** : The band changed its name to "Green Day" from "Sweet Children" because "Green Day" was a song title Billy Joe Armstrong had written on his jacket, believing it was a "better name for a band." The term also privately refers to a day spent smoking up.
**Birth Year of Members (1972)** : All three core members of Green Day were born in 1972.
**Billy Joe Armstrong's Childhood Prodigy** : As a child, Billy Joe Armstrong was a musical prodigy, taught show tunes by his jazz drummer father and singing at old age homes and children's hospitals by age five. He started piano at eight.
**Billy Joe Armstrong's Father's Death (1982)** : His father died of cancer when Billy Joe was ten, leaving his mother to raise five kids as a waitress at Rod's Hickory Pit.
**Mike Dirnt's Traumatic Childhood** : Born Michael Pritchard, his mother was a heroin addict. He was placed in an adoptive home, divorced at seven, lived with a stepfather, and returned to his birth mother where he smoked his first joint at age seven.
**Mike Dirnt Leaves Home (1987)** : At age 15, Mike Dirnt left home, living in a truck before moving into Billy Joe Armstrong's place.
**Billy Joe Armstrong's "2 Dollar Bill" Nickname** : Billy Joe earned extra money by selling joints at school for two dollars apiece, leading to his nickname.
**Sweet Children's First Gig (1988)** : The band's first gig was at Rod's Hickory Pit, where Billy Joe Armstrong's mother worked, playing to about 30 people.
**Billy Joe's Brief Rancid Membership** : Billy Joe Armstrong was a member of the band Rancid for an "extremely short period of time," described as a "blip hardly worth mentioning."
**Billy Joe Armstrong Drops Out of High School (1990)** : On February 16, 1990, the day before his 18th birthday, Billy Joe Armstrong dropped out of high school to pursue music full-time, supporting himself by selling newspaper subscriptions.
**Mike Dirnt Graduates High School (1990)** : Mike Dirnt hung in until June 1990, graduating just before Green Day embarked on their first tour.
**Drummer John Kiffmeyer Quits (1990)** : After Green Day's first short tour, drummer John Kiffmeyer quit the band to go to college, which in retrospect, was "probably wasn't a great career move."
**Tré Cool's Introduction to the Band** : Tré Cool (Frank Edwin Wright III) was John Kiffmeyer's drum teacher and was a regular at the punk club 924 Gilman Street, joining Green Day just after John's departure.
**Tré Cool's Renaming** : Frank Edwin Wright III was renamed Tré Cool by Lawrence Livermore, founder of Lookout Records.
**Tré Cool's Father's Support** : Tré Cool's father, a Vietnam vet who owned a trucking company, overhauled an old library bookmobile to serve as Green Day's first tour bus and acted as the official driver for their first three tours. The band also practiced in his living room.
**Major Label Attention (Mid-1992)** : Green Day's reputation grew following sales of about 70,000 copies of *39 Smooth* and *Kerplunk*, attracting the attention of major labels.
**Lucrative Lookout Records Deal** : Under their deal with Lookout Records, Green Day received a generous 60% of gross profits, earning over $150,000 from their indie records.
**Warner Brothers Signing (1993)** : Rob Cavallo, a Warner Brothers rep, signed Green Day for $300,000 without ever seeing them play live, after being convinced by Elliot Kahn. The band insisted on a studio within biking distance of their homes.
**The "Beatles, Joints, and Indian Food" Deal** : Rob Cavallo sealed the deal by visiting Tré Cool's place, jamming on Beatles tunes, smoking joints, and going out for Indian food with the band.
**Gilman Street Backlash (1994)** : Old school Green Day fans picketed some shows, angered that the band signed a major label record deal, feeling alienated from their "de facto families and support systems" at 924 Gilman Street.
**Dookie's Cultural Impact (1994)** : *Dookie* was referred to by some as "the punk rock version of The Joshua Tree," while others called it "Fluxy" as in "what a fluke that was."
**Band Members Marriages (Mid-1990s)** : By the time all members were 25, they were married with families, a potential response to feeling alienated by their newfound superstardom.
**Mike Dirnt's Health Issues (Early 2000s)** : Mike Dirnt suffered from minor heart problems causing shortness of breath and searing chest pain, publicly attributed to panic attacks, but is now under control.
**Tré Cool's Divorces** : Tré Cool has been divorced twice and has one child with each ex-wife, also having a failed romance with The Donnas drummer Tori Castellano.
**Billy Joe Armstrong's Enduring Marriage** : Billy Joe Armstrong's marriage to Adrian (a sociology graduate who works for a punk merchandise company) is the only one among the band members that has survived. Adrian often tours with the band, and Billy Joe's mother (the ex-waitress from Rod's Hickory Pit) looks after their sons, Joey and Jacob Danger Armstrong.
**Post-Dookie Sales Dip (Late 1990s-Early 2000s)** : Albums *Insomniac*, *Nimrod*, and *Warning* sold "okay" but were in the shadow of Dookie, leading people to question Green Day's relevance.
**Co-Headlining Tour with Blink 182 (2001)** : A tour with Blink 182 saved Green Day's career by cementing them as "punk rock elder statesmen" for a younger audience, which was aided by the simultaneous release of a greatest hits album.
**Creative Recovery After Lost Album (2002)** : After the master tapes for their album vanished, the band members undertook a creative exercise of writing 30-second songs, which they strung together to form longer tracks like "Homecoming" and "Jesus of Suburbia," leading to the creation of *American Idiot*.
**American Idiot Movie Adaptation Possibility** : Green Day's manager has stated that a movie adaptation of the *American Idiot* album's storyline is a possibility.
**Billy Joe Armstrong's Vocal Cord Damage** : Billy Joe Armstrong's vocal cords were severely ulcerated, requiring him to give up coffee, alcohol, Mexican food, and Italian food, but he is now okay.
**Band Members Still Live in the Oakland Area** : All three members of Green Day still reside in the Oakland area.
**Mike Dirnt's Stand-Up Comedy** : Mike Dirnt pursues stand-up comedy, trying out material in front of audiences at venues like The Comedy Store on Sunset Boulevard in Los Angeles.

# curiosity

**Aging in Music Industry** : The challenge for "young, loud, and snotty" bands to gracefully transition into older, successful musicians without becoming "phony" or having their "act get old," a downfall for many musicians.
**Music Producer's Role in Signing (1993)** : Rob Cavallo's strategy of connecting with Green Day on a personal level (jamming, smoking, sharing a meal) rather than just business proved crucial in securing their major label deal.
**Impact of Major Label Deals on Fanbase** : Signing with a major label can lead to alienation from an artist's original, grassroots fanbase, as seen with Green Day and the fans at 924 Gilman Street.
**Creative Problem-Solving in Music Production** : The method of assigning band members to create 30-second song snippets and then stringing them together was a successful creative exercise Green Day used to overcome the loss of an entire album's master tapes.
