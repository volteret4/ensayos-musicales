# artist - Mamie Smith

## genres

## songs
**Crazy Blues (1920)** : Recorded in a New York studio on August 10, 1920, this song addressed the mistreatment of black people, with a hidden meaning relating to racial injustice rather than just domestic violence. It achieved immediate success, selling 75,000 copies in its first month and over two million copies overall, and was one of the first songs to establish the blues as a popular recorded art form, laying foundations for future blues, R&B, and ultimately rock music. "It was also one of the first songs to establish the blues as a popular art form, at least as in a recorded sense."

## curiosities
**Pioneering Black Female Singer** : Mamie Smith was among the first in a line of black female singers who challenged conventional expectations for female vocalists, helping to sell millions of blues records throughout the 1920s and 1930s and significantly contributing to the evolution of R&B and rock music. "Mamie Smith was one of the first of a long line of black female singers who went beyond the bounds of what female singers were supposed to do and supposed to sound like."

"In 1929, she was discovered by someone from Columbia Records while playing outside of Barber Shop."
**Retirement and Posthumous Recognition** : After decades of recording numerous songs, her declining health in the 1950s, including a series of strokes that impaired her ability to play guitar, forced her to retire. She passed away in a nursing home in 1973, but in 1996, Bonnie Raitt funded a headstone for her, inscribed with an epitaph praising her singular contribution to the blues. "A series of strokes destroyed her ability to play guitar, and she died in a nursing home in 1973."

# artist - Daphne Orham

## genres
Electronic music

## labels
BBC

## instruments
Sine wave oscillators
Magnetic tape recorders

## songs
**Pulse Persephone (1959)** : Created in 1959, this piece by Daphne Oram served as an example of how magnetic tape could be utilized to manipulate sound, showcasing her innovative experimental techniques in electronic music. "It's called Pulse Persephone from 1959, which showed how tape could be used to manipulate sound."

## curiosities
**Junior Studio Engineer at BBC** : In 1942, Daphne Oram became a junior studio engineer at the BBC, where her initial responsibility involved ensuring continuous music broadcasts by seamlessly switching to recorded music during potential Nazi bombing raids, serving as both a morale booster and a psychological tactic. "Her first job was to make sure that music broadcasts continued should things be interrupted by Nazi bombing runs."
**Pioneering Electronic Composition** : After World War II, Oram deeply explored composing with synthetic sounds, utilizing esoteric audio equipment like sine wave oscillators combined with magnetic tape recorders, as synthesizers had not yet been invented. Her studio was described as resembling something from a science fiction movie. "So she used things like sine wave oscillators combined with magnetic tape recorders. Her studio must have looked like something out of a science fiction movie."
**First All-Electronic Score** : In 1957, Daphne Oram composed the soundtrack for the play *Amphitrium 38*, which is recognized as the first-ever entirely electronic score for any production. "In 1957, she created the soundtrack for a play called Amphitrium 38, which was the first ever all electronic score for anything."
**BBC Radiophonic Workshop and Independent Studio** : She successfully advocated for the BBC to establish an electronic workshop, which became the BBC Radiophonic Workshop. Upon leaving the BBC in 1959, she founded her own studio, dedicated to the creation of new sounds, further advancing her work in electronic music. "Once she convinced the BBC to establish an electronic workshop, they called it the BBC Radiophonic Workshop, more experimentation followed."
**James Bond Electronic Sounds** : Daphne Oram was responsible for creating the distinctive electronic sounds heard in three James Bond movies: *Dr. No*, *From Russia with Love*, and *Goldfinger*. "For example, the electronic sounds you hear in three James Bond movies, Dr. No from Russia with Love and Goldfinger, were all created by Daphne Orham."

She continued her experimental compositions until 1975, making significant contributions to the history of electronic music. "She stayed for 11 years, working on electronic music for close to 200 radio and TV programs using tape machines and oscillators and sine wave generators."
