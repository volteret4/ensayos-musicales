## albums
**10,000 Days (2006) - Release Date Context** : The album was released on April 28, 2006. At the time of the transcript's recording, it had been approximately 11 years and five months since its release. Its release preceded the introduction of the iPhone by over a year, and occurred in the same year NASA launched the New Horizons probe towards Pluto, highlighting the significant time elapsed without new material. "That record came out on April 28th of 2006."
**Opiate EP (1993) - Debut EP & Metal Marketing** : Tool's first EP was released in March 1993 by Zoo Entertainment. The label marketed it heavily to the metal crowd, a strategy Tool disliked because they feared being perceived as trying to capitalize on the waning "hair metal" trend of the 1980s. Four years later, Tool had received almost no royalties from the EP. "Zoo released Tools Opiate EP in March of 1993, pushing it to the metal crowd."
**Undertow (1993) - Debut Album Release** : Tool's debut full-length album was released in April 1993 by Zoo Entertainment. Its release coincided with Zoo Entertainment facing significant financial difficulties. "Zoo released Undertoe, the debut album in April of 1993."
**Anima (1996) - Second Album & Royalty Issues** : This "big selling record" was Tool's second album, released while the band was under Zoo Entertainment, which at that point was integrated with Volcano Entertainment. Despite the album's strong sales, Tool became very unhappy with the label due to consistently slow royalty checks. When the first check finally arrived, it was a meager $10,000, significantly less than what the band believed they were owed. "Zoo got custody of the second Tool album, Anima."
**Salival (2000) - Compilation Stopgap** : Released on December 12, 2000, this compilation served as a stopgap measure, combining audio and video material. It featured numerous live recordings, believed to have been captured during a 1998 World Tour, and was available as a CD bundled with either a DVD or a VHS tape. The CD comprised nine tracks: three new songs, two covers, and four older songs presented with new arrangements, some of which seemed to have been recorded during the *Anima* sessions. "It was a compilation record called Salival, which featured a lot of live recordings, which we think were recorded on a 1998 World Tour."
**Lateralus (2001) - Post-Legal Battle Comeback** : Released on May 15, 2001, just six months after *Salival*, this album came out on the Volcano label, specifically through Dissectional, Tool's co-owned sub-label. Despite emerging after four prolonged years of legal disputes, the album demonstrated the strength of Tool's dedicated fanbase by selling over half a million copies in its first seven days. The band toured extensively in support of *Lateralus* throughout 2001 and 2002. "Lateralus was released on May 15th of 2000, just six months after Salival."
**10,000 Days (2006) - Fourth Album & Touring** : This was Tool's fourth full album, released at the end of April 2006. Following its release, the band embarked on extensive tours, performing in 2006, 2007, 2008, and 2009. "It was called 10,000 Days, released like I said at the end of April of 2006."

## songs
**The Pot (2006)** : This track was the second of three singles released from Tool's fourth album, *10,000 Days*, in the spring of 2006. "That is Tool with the pot, the second of three singles from their fourth album, 10,000 days from the spring of 2006."

According to frontman Maynard James Keenan, the song alludes to the theoretical evolution of humans from having 46 chromosomes to 48, a concept rooted in the theories of Carl Jung. "A typically confounding tool song called 46 and 2, which according to Maynard, alludes to how humans will eventually evolve from having 46 chromosomes to 48."
**Stinkfist (2000) - Salival Version** : This particular version of "Stinkfist" was included on the *Salival* compilation record, which was released in 2000. "Tool and the version of stinkfist from the Salival compilation of 2000."
**Schism (2001) - Grammy Winner** : A prominent track from the album *Lateralus*, "Schism" earned Tool a Grammy Award in 2001. This achievement marked a significant recovery and moment of recognition for the band after navigating four arduous years embroiled in legal difficulties. "There's tool from La Doraus with Schism, a song that earned them a Grammy in 2001."

## curiosities
**Long Intervals Between Albums** : The band has been noted for extremely long intervals between album releases. For instance, between *10,000 Days* (2006) and the time of the transcript's recording, over 11 years and five months had passed without new material. This period was humorously contextualized by noting that the introduction of the iPhone was still over a year away when *10,000 Days* came out, Bob Barker was still hosting *The Price is Right*, and NASA launched the New Horizons probe towards Pluto, which has since traveled out of the solar system, in less time than it takes Tool to make an album. "In other words, you can travel out of the solar system in less time than it takes tool to make an album."
**Early Formation & Industry Attention** : Tool coalesced in Los Angeles in 1990. After two years of dedicated rehearsing and gigging, the band successfully attracted the attention of several record companies, who were impressed by what they saw and heard. "Tool coalesced in Los Angeles in 1990."
**Initial Legal Odyssey - Contract Breach** : A protracted legal battle began after the *Anima* album cycle, when Tool became unhappy with Zoo/Volcano due to slow royalty payments and the label's failure to exercise its option to renew Tool's contract. Zoo sued Tool for breach of contract, despite their own oversight, prompting Tool to file a counter-suit asserting that the label had forfeited its right by forgetting the option. This marked the beginning of a "weird legal odyssey" that would span nearly two decades. "And this is just the start of a weird legal odyssey that would last for nearly two decades."
**Band Paralysis Due to Label Changes** : During the ongoing legal disputes, the band was effectively paralyzed by the frequent and confusing name changes of their record label, which transitioned from Volcano to Free World, then back to Zoo Entertainment, and finally just Zoo. This constant flux meant the band was often forced to discuss lawyers, business, and financial matters, rather than creating music, to the point where they considered disbanding. "Imagine if your tool, they're completely paralyzed."
**Period of Inactivity Due to Legal Battles** : Tool was largely out of action throughout 1998, 1999, and 2000 as their complex legal issues were being sorted out, significantly impacting their musical output during this time. "Tool was largely out of action in 1998, 1999, and 2000, as all this legal stuff got sorted."
**Lawsuit with Former Manager Ted Gardner** : In 2000, Tool collectively fired their manager, Ted Gardner. Following this, Gardner initiated a lawsuit against the band, alleging fraud, non-payment of commission, and negligent misrepresentation, among other claims, and sought over $5 million in damages. "Tool collectively fired their manager Ted Gardner in 2000."
**Maynard's Role in Creative Process** : In November 2016, Maynard James Keenan explained his specific role in Tool's creative process, stating that he contributes the lyrics and vocals only after the other three band members have fully completed the instrumental music. He emphasized that until their musical composition is finished, he must simply wait. "He dodged the issue, saying that he comes in with the lyrics and the vocals after the other three guys have completed the music."
**Artwork & Insurance Lawsuit** : In 2007, a year after *10,000 Days* was released, a friend of guitarist Adam Jones, Cam De Leon (a well-known digital illustrator who worked on films like *Ghostbusters*), claimed credit for some artwork for the band and demanded recognition. Subsequently, Tool's insurance company, which was expected to defend the band against such claims, instead sued Tool on technical grounds to avoid dealing with De Leon's lawsuit. This forced Tool to counter-sue their own insurance company. This multi-level lawsuit, involving multiple parties, dragged on for eight years, consuming millions in legal fees and preventing the band from releasing new music, though they continued to tour. "A friend of guitarist Adam Jones named Cam De Leon, claimed that he created some artwork for the band and wanted some credit."
**Resolution of Artwork/Insurance Lawsuit** : After eight years of litigation and numerous postponements, a trial date was finally set for March 2015. The judge ultimately ruled in Tool's favor, determining that Cam De Leon's claim had no merit. Consequently, the insurance company's case against Tool and Tool's counter-suit against the insurance company were also dismissed. By May 2015, Tool was declared "free and clear" of all legal issues stemming from this dispute, finally enabling them to release new music. "Bottom line? As of May 2015, Tool was free and clear of any and all legal issues and was now able to release new music."
**Post-Lawsuit Album Delays** : Following the resolution of all legal issues in May 2015, guitarist Adam Jones announced that a new album would be released by the end of that year; however, this did not materialize. Subsequent predictions and reports of a new album in 2013, early 2014, and 2017 also proved to be unfounded, despite the band touring again in January 2016 and May 2017. "Adam Jones declared that they would be a new album by the end of the year. End of 2015. And there wasn't."
**Band Cohesion Amidst Adversity** : Despite the immense tensions, significant financial costs, and the sheer duration of many years of legal battles that would have torn apart a lesser group, Tool has remained together. "The tensions and costs of so many years of legal battles would have torn a lesser group apart."

## curiosities
**Early Affiliation and Artist Roster** : Zoo Entertainment was the label Tool initially chose, affiliated at the time with BMG, which was a standalone major label. It also served as the label for Green Jelly, providing a level of comfort for Tool's drummer Danny Carey, who had played with Green Jelly. "Tool decided to go with a label called Zoo Entertainment."
**Financial Troubles & Acquisitions** : By April 1993, when Tool's debut album *Undertow* was released, Zoo was already experiencing financial trouble. In 1995, its parent label BMG laid off numerous staff, leading to Zoo's sale to Volcano Entertainment in 1996. For a period, Zoo and Volcano co-existed as a partnership, with Zoo retaining custody of Tool's second album, *Anima*. "Zoo was in financial trouble."
**Royalty & Contract Disputes** : Zoo was criticized for being "really slow with the royalty checks" for the briskly selling *Anima* album, and the initial payment was significantly smaller than expected. Critically, Zoo (then absorbed into Volcano) "forgot to pick up the option" for Tool's contract renewal. This oversight, attributed to the formality falling through the cracks, led to Tool deciding they no longer wished to deal with the company, which in turn prompted Zoo to sue Tool for breach of contract. "Then, Tool found out that Zoo, Volcano, forgot, forgot to pick up the option for Tools Contract."
**Confusing Name Changes** : After being absorbed into Volcano, the entity underwent confusing name changes, including Volcano changing to Free World, then Free World Entertainment changing back to Zoo Entertainment, and then simply Zoo. These changes caused significant confusion and paralysis for Tool. "And then just for kicks, Free World Entertainment changed its name back to Zoo Entertainment, and then just back to Zoo."
**Acquisition by Zamba** : In the spring of 1998, Zoo was acquired by Zamba, at which point Zoo's name was changed to Volcano. This acquisition and subsequent renaming marked another shift in the complex corporate ownership Tool faced. "In the spring of 1998, Zoo was bought by Zamba, whereupon Zoo's name was changed to Volcano."

## curiosities
**Acquisition of Zoo & Sony Patronage** : Volcano Entertainment acquired Zoo Entertainment in 1996, becoming the new home for Tool. Volcano's major label patron was Sony Records. For a period, Zoo and Volcano co-existed as a partnership, with Zoo retaining custody of Tool's second album, *Anima*. "Volcano Entertainment in 1996."
**Contract & Royalty Disputes with Tool** : Volcano, or Zoo (which became more or less the same entity), was central to Tool's legal troubles. They were criticized for being slow with royalty checks for the successful *Anima* album and, critically, "forgot to pick up the option" on Tool's contract, leading to a lawsuit against the band for breach of contract, and a counter-suit from Tool. "Tool's label Volcano, or Zoo, which more or less the same thing, is suing the band for bailing on their contract."
**Confusing Name Changes** : Volcano underwent a series of confusing name changes, first to Free World, then changing back to Zoo Entertainment, and then simply Zoo, contributing to significant confusion and legal complications for Tool. "Volcano had changed its name to Free World."
**Acquisition by Zamba** : In the spring of 1998, Volcano was bought by Zamba. This was part of a broader corporate restructuring that eventually led to Q Prime acquiring a stake in the new Volcano, intending to provide a stable home for Tool. "In the spring of 1998, Zoo was bought by Zamba, whereupon Zoo's name was changed to Volcano."
**Formation of Volcano 2 & Dissectional** : After Q Prime acquired a 50% stake in the new Volcano (from Zamba) to provide Tool with a stable label environment and resolve ongoing lawsuits, a new company called Volcano 2 was formed. This structure included giving Tool its own sub-label, Dissectional, which was set up as a joint company to ensure Tool had a direct say in its operations and prevent future disputes. "This new company, now called Volcano 2, was structured to give Tool its own sub-label, which they called dissectional."
**Return to BMG Ownership** : In 2002, Volcano's parent company, Zamba, was acquired by BMG. This meant that Volcano, along with all its artists (including Tool), effectively returned to the ownership of BMG, the company that had previously owned Zoo Entertainment, Tool's original label. "In 2002, Zamba was bought by BMG, which returned Volcano, and all the other artists on the roster, back to the company that had owned it when it was still called Zoo Entertainment."
