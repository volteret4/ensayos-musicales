**1981 American Tour - Jovan Musk Sponsorship** : This tour marked an early instance of major corporate sponsorship in rock music, with Jovan Musk, a men's cologne company, paying $3 million for exclusive rights. The Jovan name appeared on over two million concert tickets and was mentioned whenever the tour was, such as "Jovan presents the Rolling Stones 1981 American tour." The deal was designed to market Jovan as a "hip product" to rock fans aged 16 to 34, successfully boosting both the band's earnings (grossing $50 million) and Jovan's sales, despite initial public criticism as "crass and corporate." "For $3 million, Jovan Musk, a men's cologne and toiletries company bought the exclusive rights to the tour."

## songs
**Revolution (1968) - Nike Commercial Controversy** : The original recording of "Revolution" was used in a highly controversial 1987 Nike commercial for their "Air Revolution" line of shoes. This use of what was considered "a sacred call to action" by John Lennon to sell shoes sparked immense public outrage. The ad agency, Wieden+Kennedy, chose the Beatles' song over an alternative by the Georgia Satellites. The licensing deal, worth between $7 and $10 million, was negotiated with Michael Jackson, who had acquired a significant portion of the Beatles' publishing catalog in 1985, and Yoko Ono, who supported the deal to expose her late husband's music to new generations. The remaining three Beatles (Paul, Ringo, and George) "went ballistic," suing Nike for $50 million, though the matter was settled out of court. The campaign significantly boosted Nike and became one of advertising's most influential spots, marking a turning point in commercial music licensing. "And what's more, this sacred call to action that John Lennon wrote in the 1960s was being used to sell shoes."

# curiosity

**Definition of "Selling Out"** : "Selling out" is broadly defined as when the pursuit of money compromises the "pursuit of truth and beauty and all the purity and goodness that is supposed to flow from art." There are varying interpretations, including taking money for any art, allowing music to be influenced by riches, becoming too popular for original fans' comfort, or using music to sell unrelated products in commercials. "The most basic definition of selling out is when the pursuit of money compromises corrupts or otherwise interrupts the pursuit of truth and beauty and all the purity and goodness that is supposed to flow from art."
**Changing Value of Music** : Since the advent of the internet and streaming services like YouTube, the perceived value of music, in terms of how much people are willing to pay for it, has "dropped to near zero," making almost all music "available for free." This forces musicians to find alternative revenue streams. "Thanks to streaming in YouTube, almost all the music ever created in the history of humankind is pretty much available for free."
**Rising Costs for Musicians** : Being a working musician has become "ridiculously expensive," encompassing costs beyond basic living expenses like vans, gas, gear (guitar strings, sticks, heads), recording, managers, and website maintenance. These escalating costs, combined with record labels cutting budgets and declining record sales, drive artists to seek new revenue sources like sponsorships and licensing. "Secondly, being a musician is ridiculously expensive."
**Patronage vs. Sponsorship** : Historically, artists like Shakespeare, Michelangelo (supported by Popes Julius II and Leo X), and Mozart (by Emperor Joseph II of Austria) relied on wealthy patrons. Modern musicians now have "patrons" in the form of sponsors, who may provide equipment (guitars, amps, mics, drums) in exchange for album liner note thanks, magazine endorsements, or appearances at trade shows. "And today's musicians have patrons as well, except that we call them sponsors."
**Escalating Tour Costs** : Staging major concert tours has become increasingly expensive, requiring substantial upfront capital for stage design, contracts, crew, transportation, accommodation, and insurance before any revenue is generated. The pressure for each tour to be "bigger and better" than the last has led to the widespread adoption of corporate sponsors like Budweiser, Pepsi, Tommy Hilfiger, Honda, and Citibank. "As time went on, staging a major tour required bigger and bigger amounts of capital up front."
**Napster and Decline of Record Sales (Early 2000s)** : The early 21st century saw record sales "go into free fall" with the emergence of Napster and other file-sharing programs. This loss of traditional revenue forced artists to seek alternative income streams, such as licensing music for commercials, to compensate for piracy-related losses. "The beginning of the 21st century was when record sales went into free fall, following the introduction of Napster and all the other file sharing programs."
**Japanese Commercials as "Selling Out" Avoidance** : In the past, famous celebrities like Harrison Ford, Arnold Schwarzenegger, and Brad Pitt would agree to do commercials in Japan, believing that their endorsement would not be seen by their Western audience, thus allowing them to avoid the perception of "selling out" at home. "You may remember stories of famous celebrities agreeing to do a commercial in Japan because now they figure that no one would ever find out."

I've just got to get the other lads to agree"), discussing the "crust first" approach to eating their new product. "Stuffed crust pizza from pizza hot with cheese baked into a new thinner crust, you'll want to eat it crust first."
