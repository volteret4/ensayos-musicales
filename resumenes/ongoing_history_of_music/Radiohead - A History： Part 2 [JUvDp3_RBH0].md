## albums
**Pablo Honey** : Radiohead's first album, which "The Bends" started slow like, implying similar initial reception or impact. It is mentioned as the album preceding "The Bends." "many members of the group came down with some kind of health problem during the years between Pablo Honey and The Bends."
**The Bends (1995)** : Radiohead's second album, which started slow in the post-grunge world, contrasting with the Britpop era dominated by bands like Blur and Oasis. It was initially "a flop" but gained critical and commercial success through extensive touring and support from Michael Stipe of R.E.M. The album's title refers to what happens to deep-sea divers coming up too fast, reflecting the band's feeling of immense pressure at the time. Its medical imagery is linked to Tom Yorke's childhood surgery and health issues among band members. "The Bends is what happens to deep-sea divers when you come up to the surface too fast and the change in pressure does terrible things to your bloodstream."
**Okay Computer (1997)** : Radiohead's third album, released on July 1, 1997. It became a massively successful and influential album, with its first single "Paranoid Android" reaching number three on the UK singles charts and the album itself hitting number one. The album's title is a reference to a quote from Zaphod Beeblebrox in "The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy" series. There is a theory, based on numbers on the back cover (1857-6397), that the album was finished at 6:57 PM on March 6, 1997. The cover image is an interchange of Interstates 84 and 91, photographed by Tom Yorke from a Hilton Hotel window in Hartford, Connecticut, on August 20, 1996. "A CD called Okay Computing."
**Airbag / How Am I Driving? (1998)** : An EP released in 1998, which consisted of extra material recorded during the "Okay Computer" sessions. This EP served to buy the band some time amidst rising expectations for their fourth album, but ultimately only delayed the pressure. "They bought a little time with a 1998 EP entitled Airbag How Am I Driving, which basically consisted of extra material recording during the OK computer sessions, but that really just kicked the whole can down the road."
**Kid A (2000)** : Radiohead's fourth album, released on October 3, 2000. It emerged after a period of intense pressure and creative block, taking nearly three years to complete following "Okay Computer." The album marked a radical departure in sound, becoming an electronic record with loops, samples, artificial beats, and treated vocals, heavily influenced by techno artists like Aphex Twin and Autechre. During its recording, Ed O'Brien and Jonny Greenwood barely played guitar, and Phil Selway focused more on computer programming. Tom Yorke created the title track's vocals using a self-written computer program, transforming his voice into an instrument. The recording sessions were difficult, moving from Paris to Copenhagen to London and finally to their Oxford private studio. "The fourth album emerged as what can be best described as an electronic record."
**Amnesiac (2001)** : Radiohead's fifth album, released barely ten months after "Kid A." It originated from material recorded simultaneously with "Kid A" but was "saved and held back" from that album, rather than being "left over." "Amnesiac" is described as more conventional than "Kid A," featuring guitars, real drums, and more normal song structures. It also included recordings of songs that had become live favorites. Despite its conventional approach, some fans were left feeling unsatisfied, desiring a return to the style of "Okay Computer." "In other words, this new album was recorded pretty much simultaneously with Kid A but is designed to be taken separately."
**I Might Be Wrong: Live Recordings (2001)** : A live album released for Christmas 2001. It was a short record, containing 8 songs and lasting 40 minutes, which annoyed some Radiohead fans due to its brevity. However, it was significant as it was the first time Radiohead's live performance abilities were officially and properly documented on CD. "For Christmas 2001, it was decided that a live album was required."
**Hail to the Thief (2003)** : Radiohead's sixth album, recorded at Oceanway Recording in Hollywood in September 2002. It was their last album under their original contract with EMI Records. The band decided to pull back on experimentation and return to basics, featuring more live-in-the-studio takes rather than computer-generated loops and samples. Recording was fast-paced, about a track a day, and mixing was completed in two weeks. Tom Yorke's lyrics were inspired by the post-9/11 world, including right-wing politics, the War on Terror, and the Bush administration. The album was catastrophically leaked online in an unmixed and unmastered form from March to June, 10 weeks before its official release. "This record, all about performance."

## songs
**Creep (1992)** : An early song that initially gave Radiohead the reputation of being a "one-hit wonder." Despite this, it was instrumental in their early career and the pressure to follow it up was a significant challenge for the band. "they were able to overcome the pressure of following up the potential one-hit wonder-ness of a song called Creep."
**Just** : A single from "The Bends" album, notable for its video directed by Jamie Thraves. The video, set near Liverpool Station in London, features a well-dressed man lying on the street, refusing help, and eventually revealing the reason to a gathering crowd, but his words are subtitled and then censored, leading the crowd to also lie down. The band and director have kept the meaning a mystery. The song was a "famous radio hit song" but was "never officially released as a single in North America." "The video for Just was directed by a guy named Jamie Thraves."
**Paranoid Android (1997)** : The first single from "Okay Computer," released on May 26, 1997. It is described by Tom Yorke as "several half finished songs stitched together" and in its original form was over 11 minutes long with an "over-the-top organ solo," which was later cut. Drummer Phil Selway arranged the three separate parts into the final 6 minute, 22 second single. The song is allegedly based on The Beatles' "Happiness Is a Warm Gun" and "the Fall of the Roman Empire." Its title comes from Marvin the Paranoid Android from "The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy." A section of the song is in a 7/8 time signature, which "just kind of happened." The lyrics' "Gucci little piggy" refers to an incident Tom Yorke witnessed in an LA bar where a woman reacted violently to a spilled drink on her Gucci outfit. Radiohead initially thought it would "never gonna make it on the radio," but it became their biggest British hit ever, climbing to number three on the UK singles charts. "According to Tom, paranoid Android is nothing more than several half finished songs stitched together."
**Karma Police (1997)** : A track from "Okay Computer," released on July 1, 1997. Its title originated from a running joke within the band, a threat members would make to each other ("Somebody was always afraid of a call from the Karma police") if someone did something wrong. "The title comes from a threat that members of Radiohead would make to each other if they did something terrible."
**Everything in Its Right Place (2000)** : The opening song and lead-off track from "Kid A." It was the first song Tom Yorke wrote on a brand new grand piano, despite the instrument not being heard in the final track. When fans first heard it, it clearly signaled the band's new, different direction. It was written in the same week as "Pyramid Song." "It was the first song that Tom wrote on a brand new grand piano."
**Nothing to Fear (1999) / Pyramid Song (2001)** : Originally called "Nothing to Fear" in 1999, it became "Pyramid Song" within two years and was released on the "Amnesiac" album. Tom Yorke wrote this song in the same week as "Everything in Its Right Place," inspired by a museum exhibition he visited in Copenhagen. "Back in 1999, this single was originally called Nothing to Fear."
**2 + 2 = 5 (2003)** : One of the singles from "Hail to the Thief." Like other songs on the album, it has an alternative title, "The Luke Warm." "Most people know this as 2 plus 2 equals 5, but you can also call it the Luke Warm if you want."

## curiosities
**Early Career Pressure** : Radiohead faced significant pressure early in their career, being labeled as potential "one-hit wonders" after the success of "Creep." They overcame this with the help of producer John Leckie and a tour opening for Alanis Morissette. "But this is exactly the sort of thing radiohead faced earlier in their career."
**The Bends Medical Imagery** : The album "The Bends" is filled with medical imagery, stemming from Tom Yorke's childhood eye surgery and various health problems suffered by band members between the "Pablo Honey" and "The Bends" albums. Tom Yorke, in particular, suffered from intestinal issues and "had a rep of being the most sickly person in rock at that time." Jonny Greenwood also had a serious repetitive strain problem from playing guitar, requiring an arm brace, and his ears were so damaged by volume that they bled, forcing him to wear industrial-strength headphones on stage. "There's all kinds of medical imagery on the Benz."
**The Bends Album Cover** : The cover art for "The Bends" is a digital blend of two photos: one of a plastic dummy used by hospital staff in Oxford for CPR practice, and another of Tom Yorke. This image was created to suggest the band's feeling of needing "some kind of recestitation" due to the pressure they were under. "It's a picture of a plastic dummy used by hospital staff in Oxford to practice CPR or cessitation."
**R.E.M. Tour Support** : "The Bends" initially flopped but gained traction after Michael Stipe of R.E.M. took Radiohead "under REM's wing," offering them support slots on their tour. This provided crucial exposure and advice from R.E.M. The two bands "got along famously" and engaged in pranks, such as Michael Stipe sending a radio-controlled car onto the stage during Radiohead's set. "It never really caught on until Michael Steip took Radiohead under REM's wing and offered them support slots on their monster tour."
**Touring Fatigue and Health Issues (The Bends era)** : During the extensive touring for "The Bends," Tom Yorke frequently fell sick. His ears suffered from constant pressure changes during flights, leading to fluid buildup and hearing trouble, which necessitated special earplugs. The stress was immense, leading him to beg the manager to send him home to England. The entire band was exhausted, with only two weeks off in 1994 and five days in mid-1995. "His ears couldn't take the constant pressurizing and depressurizing that comes with takeoffs and landing."
**Gear Theft in Denver** : At a tour stop in Denver in 1995, all of Radiohead's gear was stolen and never recovered. "Then at a tour stop in Denver, all their gear was stolen, everything."
**Tom Yorke's Blackout in Munich** : On November 25, 1995, during a concert in Munich, Germany, Tom Yorke lost his voice three songs into the set and then blacked out on stage, hitting the floor. This incident effectively ended their touring for the year. "Then on November 25th in Munich, Germany, Tom not only lost his voice, three songs into the band set, but he blocked out."
**Post-Bends Pressure and Hiatus** : After the success of "The Bends," Radiohead faced immense pressure to create an even better album, being compared to U2 and R.E.M. To cope, the band members scattered: Ed O'Brien went to India, Tom Yorke traveled solo in Europe, and others stayed home in England. They eventually regrouped in January 1996. "They were being called the next U2 and the next REM."
**Self-Built Studio and Album Scrapping** : On February 1, 1996, Radiohead returned to their rehearsal space, Kanda Plaas, an old farmhouse in Sutton Courtney, Oxfordshire. At their manager's insistence, they invested $250,000 in recording gear, intending to save money by no longer renting expensive studios. They wrote and rehearsed extensively, completing an entire album, but then scrapped it, believing they could do better, and started over. "The bank account took a $250,000 hit."
**Record Company Flexibility** : Radiohead's record company gave them "a rare pledge," promising "not to impose a hard deadline for the delivery of a new album," allowing them creative freedom to take as long as they needed. "Their record company had promised not to impose a hard deadline for the delivery of a new album."
**Recording at St Catherine's Court** : Due to the lack of toilets at their Kanda Plaas rehearsal space, Radiohead moved to St Catherine's Court, a 14th-century castle in Bath, once owned by Henry VIII and rented out by actress Jane Seymour. The quiet, out-of-the-way location and the acoustics in its ballroom and library made it worth the significant rent of 10,000 pounds per week (or 175,000 Canadian dollars for their two-month stay). "Well, it's just dumb reason, but no one had ever installed toilets at Kandaplas."
**Influences During Okay Computer Sessions** : During the recording of what would become "Okay Computer," Jonny Greenwood was heavily influenced by Pink Floyd, listening to their old albums and watching performance films. Tom Yorke, meanwhile, was entranced by old Johnny Cash records. "Johnny Greenwood was into a big pink void trip at the time, fortunate to listen to all these old albums and demanding that they watch old pink void performance films."
**Discomfort with Okay Computer Fame** : Following the massive success of "Okay Computer," Radiohead found the "huge crowds and tons of press coverage" to be unsettling. Drummer Phil Selway coined the phrase "don't read your press, weigh it," and Tom Yorke wore a T-shirt saying in French, "don't swallow your press," reflecting their unease. "All of this attention, however, made the band more than a little uneasy, uncomfortable."
**Post-Okay Computer Awards and Touring** : Radiohead spent 1997 and 1998 touring and accepting numerous awards, including "best album awards, band of the year awards," and Grammy awards (e.g., Best Alternative Album in the States and a Danish Grammy). This intense period contributed to their exhaustion. "They picked up one in the States for best alternative album, but they also want a Danish Grammy."
**Meeting People Is Easy Documentary** : The toll of fame on Radiohead is documented in the film "Meeting People is Easy," which portrays how "fame can be so boring and so draining" as the band traveled through cities like Barcelona, Paris, New York, and Tokyo. This film helps explain why it took nearly three years for them to release their fourth album. "If you want to see what the band went through, source out the Radiohead documentary film, Meeting People is Easy."
**Creative Block and Internal Disagreement (Kid A era)** : Facing immense pressure for their fourth album, Tom Yorke suffered from burnout and a creative block, unable to write anything. In a January 1999 meeting in Paris, the band had no clear direction. Ed O'Brien advocated for a return to "three minute guitar charge pop songs," a view supported by management. However, the Greenwood brothers and Phil Selway felt "lost," leading to difficult and haphazard recording sessions. "Tom was so freaked out and so burned out that he couldn't seem to write anything."
**Intentional Sonic Reinvention (Kid A)** : For "Kid A," Radiohead intentionally "burned it all down and they started from scratch," fundamentally changing their sound to an electronic record. This involved minimal guitar use by Ed O'Brien and Jonny Greenwood, Phil Selway acting more as a computer programmer, and Tom Yorke creating vocals via self-written computer programs. They successfully "destroyed themselves and rebuilt the band as something brand new." "they burned it all down and they started from scratch, which is not an easy thing to do."
**EMI Contract End and Industry Crossroads** : With the release of "Hail to the Thief," Radiohead fulfilled their obligations to EMI Records. EMI strongly wanted to re-sign them, but Radiohead was dissatisfied with their treatment and payment, and concerned about EMI's management and ownership flux. They opted to "give things a long, hard think" about whether to stay with a major label or go independent, a decision that "shook the music industry" given the erosion of traditional label hegemony, rising piracy, and the shift to online music in the early 2000s. "With the release of Hail to the Thief, Radiohead's obligations to EMI came to an end."

# curiosity

**One-Hit Wonder Concept** : Becoming a one-hit wonder is described as "quite an accomplishment" because it signifies achieving success, however brief, unlike many bands who work for years without any. However, the label itself is disliked, implying a "moralizing" judgment of an artist having only "one good song." "When you think about it, it's quite an accomplishment to become a one-hit wonder."
**The Fall of the Roman Empire** : This historical event is cited as one of the alleged inspirations for Radiohead's song "Paranoid Android." "And also the Fall of the Roman Empire."
**Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy References** : Douglas Adams' "Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy" series provided inspiration for Radiohead. The character Marvin the Paranoid Android lent his name to the song "Paranoid Android," and another character, Zaphod Beeblebrox, provided the quote, "okay, computer, I want full manual control now," which is referenced in the album title "Okay Computer." "It comes from Marvin the Paranoid Android, one of the central characters in the Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy series by Douglas Adams."
**Bill Hicks Dedication** : Radiohead's album "The Bends" was dedicated to comedian Bill Hicks, who died of pancreatic cancer at age 32. He is mentioned as "the same guy, tool reviewers." "The album, the Benz, was dedicated to comedian Bill Hicks, the same guy, tool reviewers."
**Jane Seymour's St Catherine's Court** : Actress Jane Seymour (known as "Dr. Quinn, Medicine Woman") rents out her 14th-century castle, St Catherine's Court in Bath, to bands looking for a "funky sort of place" to record. Radiohead rented it for two months to record part of their third album. The castle boasts "Eight bedrooms, six and a half bathrooms, stables, a tennis court, and even a full church," surrounded by 14 acres of gardens, available for 10,000 pounds a week at the time. "It was actress Jane Seymour's house."
**Kanda Plaas Rehearsal Space** : Radiohead's rehearsal space, located at an old farmhouse in Sutton Courtney, Oxfordshire. After spending $250,000 to equip it as a studio, the band ultimately had to leave for St Catherine's Court because "no one had ever installed toilets at Kandaplas." "Radio had went back to Kanda Plaas, their rehearsal space at the old farmhouse in Sutton, Cortney in Oxfordshire."
**Internet Leaks Era** : The early 2000s are described as "the era of the catastrophic internet leak." This affected Radiohead when their album "Hail to the Thief" was "released into the wild a full 10 weeks before the official release date" as an unmixed and unmastered version, from March until June, with the leakers never being caught. "This was also the era of the catastrophic internet leak."
**Music Industry Shifts (Early 21st Century)** : By the mid-2000s, the "traditional label of hegemony over the recorded music industry was eroding," primarily due to piracy "killing CD sales" and a growing movement towards online music. Lawsuits and threats failed to stop this shift, forcing artists like Radiohead to consider major label contracts versus independent options. "the traditional label of hegemony over the recorded music industry was eroding, piracy with killing CD sales."
