# artist - The Middle Class

## labels
Joke Records

## albums
**Out of Vogue (1979) – First West Coast Hardcore Record** : Released in January 1979, this 4-track EP is considered the first hardcore record from the American West Coast. The entire EP was just 4 minutes and 55 seconds long, with its four songs finishing in less than five minutes, embodying the stripped-down, fast, and short style of early hardcore. Because major labels and commercial radio wouldn't consider such music, The Middle Class formed their own indie label, Joke Records, to release it. "The first hardcore record from the American West Coast seems to be by a band from Santa Anna called The Middle Class that appeared in January 1979. It was a 4 track EP that was over in 4 minutes and 55 seconds. 4 songs done in less than 5 minutes."

## songs
**Out of Vogue (1979)** : This is the title track from The Middle Class's 1979 debut EP, which is considered the first hardcore record from the American West Coast. The rapid-fire nature of the song and EP (4 songs in under 5 minutes) exemplified the new, stripped-down punk sound. "This is the title track, and it's called Out of Vote."

# artist - D.O.A.

## labels
Sudden Death Records

## albums
**Hardcore 81 (1981) – Naming the Genre** : Released on April 22, 1981, this was D.O.A.'s second album. The band toured extensively to support it, and the album's title, "Hardcore 81," is credited with giving the entire subgenre of punk its name. "On April 22, 1981, the group released their second album. They called it Hardcore 81 and then they toured the hell out of it. The term Hardcore caught on and ever since, that's been the name for this subgenre of punk."

## songs
**D.O.A. (1981)** : This song is the namesake track from D.O.A.'s 1981 album, "Hardcore 81." The album itself is famously credited with coining the term "hardcore" for the subgenre of punk. "DOA with their namesake song from their album Hardcore 81."

## curiosities
**West Coast Touring Strategy** : D.O.A., based in Vancouver, primarily toured north-south along the West Coast from San Diego to Vancouver, finding it easier and more affordable than east-west travel due to the Rocky Mountains. They would then head east once they got south. "Vancouver's DOA was very much part of this and while they did tour through Canada, it was often easier to go up and down the west coast playing the same gigs as their hardcore brother."
**"The List" of Venues** : D.O.A. compiled and traded "the list," which was a growing catalog of punk-friendly venues and places to stay across North America. This list was shared among dozens, if not hundreds, of like-minded bands, significantly expanding the reach of punk and hardcore across the continent. "DOA compiled something called the list. This was a growing list of punk-friendly venues and places to stay across North America that was made available and then traded among dozens, maybe hundreds of other like-minded bands."
**Coined "Hardcore" Term** : D.O.A. is credited with giving the new, harder, faster, louder, and more aggressive form of punk its name. They achieved this with the release of their second album, "Hardcore 81," on April 22, 1981, after which the term caught on and became the official name for the subgenre. "Second, it was DOA that gave this new, harder, faster, louder and more aggressive form of punk its name. On April 22, 1981, the group released their second album. They called it Hardcore 81 and then they toured the hell out of it."

## songs
**Pay to Cum (1980) – First East Coast Hardcore Release** : Released in 1980, this track is considered the very first East Coast hardcore release. By the time it came out, Bad Brains had evolved from a jazz fusion group, Mind Power, to a hardcore band with blistering tempos, inspired by the Ramones' song "Bad Brain." "This track is probably the very first East Coast hardcore release. It's called Heat Come. Bad Brains. Or the Bad Brains, take your pick. ## curiosities
**Initial Band Name and Genre Shift** : Bad Brains originally started as a jazz fusion group named "Mind Power." As they continued to play, their music became progressively faster and harder, leading them to change their name, inspired by the Ramones' song "Bad Brain," and embrace hardcore. "They started as a jazz fusion group known as Mindpower, but the more they played, the faster and harder the music got. And that's when they changed their name, inspired by the Ramonesong, Bad Brain."
**Racial and Religious Identity** : Every member of Bad Brains was African American and identified as Rastafarians, a distinctive feature for a hardcore band. "Another thing, every member of Bad Brains was African American. They were all Rastafarians too."
**Genre Blending and Influence** : Being big Bob Marley fans, Bad Brains eventually incorporated Reggae into their sound, exposing the genre to a new generation of kids. Later, they further diversified their music by introducing elements of funk, soul, and even hip hop, making them hugely important on multiple levels. "And because they were big Bob Marley fans, they also eventually incorporated Reggae into their sound, bringing that genre to a whole new generation of kids. Later, funk and soul and even hip hop was introduced. Again, hugely important on so many levels."
**Influence on Major Artists** : Bad Brains were tremendously influential to punk kids in the DC area, including a young Dave Grohl, who was "completely besotted with them." They also influenced the Beastie Boys, Red Hot Chili Peppers, No Doubt, and even Guns N' Roses. "And that included a young Dave Grohl, who became completely besotted with him. So with the Beastie boys and the Chili Peppers and no doubt, and even Guns and Roses, that Bad Brains were great."

**DIY Ethic and Label Principles** : Minor Threat's leader, Ian MacKaye, became a punk rock legend due to the band's music and the strong ethics they established. They adopted a serious do-it-yourself (DIY) ethic, founding Dischord Records, which would release material for other hardcore bands. Dischord had strict policies of keeping record prices low and affordable. "Leader Ian McKay became a punk rock legend, two reasons. First of all, Minor Threats music found a lot of fans, but just as important was the ethics that he and the band laid down. They had a serious do-it-yourself ethic, founding a record label called Discord that would eventually release material by other hardcore bands. Second, they had a strict policy of keeping record prices low and affordable."
**All-Ages Gigs Policy** : Minor Threat maintained a policy of only playing all-ages gigs and keeping ticket prices cheap, ensuring accessibility for a wider audience, particularly younger fans. "Third, they would only play all ages gigs with cheap ticket prices."
**Influence on Emo** : Minor Threat is credited with pointing in the direction of what would become the emo genre as early as the mid-1980s, highlighting their foundational role in subsequent musical developments. "Fourth, if you dig back far enough, you'll see that Minor Threat pointed in the direction of what would become emo as early as the mid-1980s."
**Origin of Straight Edge Movement** : Ian MacKaye was a central figure in the straight edge movement, a philosophy advocating no alcohol, no drugs of any kind, and no promiscuous sex. This lifestyle, and the term itself, originated from a Minor Threat song of the same name and continues to be a lifestyle for many. "And fifth, McKay was a big part of this straight edge movement. No alcohol, no drugs of any kind, no promiscuous sex. And the straight edge philosophy continues to this day. It's a lifestyle for a lot of people."

# genre - Hardcore

## curiosities
**Definition and Grievances** : Hardcore punk, or hardcore, is a subgenre of punk rock. Its creators were drawn to the faster and heavier aspects of original punk bands and sought to express grievances against societal norms. These grievances included disgust with yuppies and their culture, broken homes, ruined families, capitalist exploitation, consumerism, and corrupt politicians. They also disliked "high and mighty rock stars," New Wave aesthetics (skinny ties, eyeliner), and the established music industry, sometimes just wanting to "get wasted." "Hardcore punk, or just hardcore, if you prefer, is a subgenre, obviously, of punk rock. Its creators love the faster and heavier end of the original punk rock bands, but they also had, well, they had some grievances that they needed to express. Epis and their culture disgusted them, broken homes and ruined families, destroyed them, capitalist exploitation, yuppies, consumerism, corrupt politicians. They also aren't crazy about high and muddy rock stars, or the posters of New Wave with their skinny ties and eyeliner, the weasels of the established recorded music industry, and sometimes the people behind Hardcore just wanted to get wasted."
**Musical Characteristics** : The aim of hardcore was to play harder, faster, and louder than anyone else. Technical skill and virtuosity were only valued if they contributed to this goal. Melody was often considered "wimpy," with shouting and screaming deemed more important for vocal delivery. "The aim was to play harder, faster, and louder than everyone else. Technique and virtuosity? Only if it helped you play harder, faster, and louder. Melody? Eh, wimpy. Shouting and screaming was more important."
**Geographic Origins and Spread (Late 1970s)** : Hardcore first took root simultaneously on both the East and West Coasts of North America in the late 1970s, specifically in Vancouver, San Francisco, Southern California, Washington, D.C., Boston, and New York City. From these North American scenes, it spread to the UK, Italy, and Japan. "Hardcore first took root on the East and West Coast of North America in the late 1970s, specifically in Vancouver, San Francisco, Southern California, Washington, D.C., Boston, and New York City. The UK also had its own Hardcore scenes, and from there it spread to Italy and Japan."
**Early West Coast Sound** : In Los Angeles and Southern California, the original punk rock scene had faded by 1979, replaced by a new, even more stripped-down type of punk. This sound typically featured guitars, bass, drums, and a vocalist, with traditional verse-chorus structures often abandoned in favor of extremely short songs, sometimes under 32 seconds. "The original punk rock scene was gone by about 1979, and in its place was a new type of punk that was stripped down even further. Guitar's bass drums vocalist, that's it. The traditional first chorus first song structure wasn't necessary. In fact, if everything could be said in 32 seconds or less, so be it."
**California as Hardcore Center** : California, particularly Los Angeles and Orange County, served as a central hub for hardcore culture, fostering numerous influential bands in the main cities and surrounding suburbs. "California was the center of hardcore culture with so many bands, an LA and Orange County and the suburbs to the North, the Minutemen, the descendants, Agent Orange, the adolescents, social distortion, Red Cross."
**Bay Area Hardcore (San Francisco)** : Despite San Francisco's hippie history, or perhaps because of it, the Bay Area became a significant magnet for hardcore punk bands, with Dead Kennedys being particularly important. "Although the place had a hippie history, or maybe because of it, the Bay Area became a magnet for hardcore punk bands, and no other band was more important than the dead Kennedys."
**Impact of Rocky Mountains on West Coast Touring** : The presence of the Rocky Mountains made touring north-south along the West Coast (from San Diego to Vancouver) much easier and more affordable for bands than touring east-west across North America. This geographic reality fostered a semi-distinct ecosystem for West Coast bands and influenced the spread of hardcore. "If you're in a band on the west coast, it is much easier and much more affordable to tour north south than it is east west. You can blame the Rocky Mountains. It takes time to cross the range and there aren't that many places to play between the western and eastern slopes. So that means for a certain level of band, you inhabit a semi distinct ecosystem. You're not cut off from the rest of the continent, but it does affect how you tour."
**Touring Underserved Cities** : The north-south touring flow of punk and hardcore bands meant they frequently played in underserved cities and towns, including the Seattle area, where major touring acts were infrequent. This created opportunities for local scenes to flourish and for unique cross-pollination of musical styles. "The North South flow of punk and hardcore bands meant that these groups played a lot of underserved cities and towns when it came to tours. They had to play somewhere so they would play anywhere. And that included the Seattle area. At the time, the Pacific Northwest was sort of off the grid when it came to major touring acts. So if you wanted to see live music, it was much easier to catch a punk show than anything else."
**New York Hardcore Scene Characteristics** : The hardcore music in New York City had a distinct sound, sometimes referred to as the "New York Hardcore sound." This scene was home to early Beastie Boys, Agnostic Front, Cro-Mags, and the Misfits. "From DC, we head to New York City. The music was a little different too. Some people called this the New York Hardcore Tug."
**Boston Hardcore Scene and "The Boston Crew"** : Boston's hardcore scene included bands influenced by Ian MacKaye's straight edge philosophy. The "Boston Crew" were self-appointed "moral police" who would often physically confront anyone seen drinking or doing drugs at shows, creating an intense atmosphere. "Some of these bands were influenced by Ian McKay's straight edge philosophy. They were so intense that the straight edge guys would often brawl with anyone they saw drinking or doing drugs at their shows. Those self-affointed more police were locally known as the Boston Crow."
**Midwest Hardcore Hub: Minneapolis** : Minneapolis in the Midwest was a crucial hub for hardcore, producing highly influential bands like The Replacements and Hüsker Dü, who later became significant forces in alt-rock. "There were other American cities that got cut up in hardcore, Detroit, Phoenix, various spots in Texas, North Carolina, Chicago, and maybe most importantly in the Midwest Minneapolis."
**International Expansion in the 1980s** : Hardcore punk rapidly spread globally in the 1980s, driven by bands relentlessly touring and playing inexpensive all-ages shows in towns and cities that mainstream acts often overlooked. This allowed kids seeking a musical scene to connect with the genre. "It's amazing the number of places where hardcore punk caught on during the 1980s. And then again, maybe not because all these bands toured relentlessly, often playing inexpensive all-ager shows and towns and cities with kids who wanted to belong to some kind of musical scene, but had been left behind by the Bigger Acts."
**Canadian Hardcore Scenes** : Canada had several important hardcore bands beyond D.O.A., with notable scenes in Toronto (e.g., Fucked Up from Kensington Market), Montreal (The Asexuals), Edmonton (SNFU), and particularly the West Coast (e.g., The Subhumans, Dayglo Abortions, NoMeansNo). "Canada wasn't immune. We've already talked about DOA, but they were far from the only important hardcore band from Canada. In Toronto, there was a bunch of effing goofs who came out of the Kensington Market neighborhood. Montreal gave us the asexuals. SNFU came from Edmonton. But the West Coast had the most fertile ground, the subhumans and dayglow abortions, for example."
**Hardcore in Eastern Europe** : Before the fall of the Iron Curtain, hardcore was a potent underground force in Eastern Europe, demonstrating its ability to thrive in politically restrictive environments. "Hardcore was a powerful underground force in Eastern Europe before the fall of the Iron Curtain."
**Early Reach to Japan** : Hardcore reached Japan surprisingly early, establishing a scene there as far back as 1977. "It even reached Japan as early as 1977."
**UK Hardcore and D-Beat** : The UK developed its own hardcore derivative called D-Beat, characterized by a distinctive drum pattern. UK Hardcore was a fusion of British punk (like the Sex Pistols) and the New Wave of British Heavy Metal (NWOBHM) bands that emerged in the early 1980s, with Motörhead being a major influence. "Then we have the UK, home of a hardcore derivative called Debeat, which referred to a distinctive sort of drum pattern that went with a lot of the songs. UK Hardcore was an amalgamation of British punk, especially the Sex Bistels, and the new wave of British heavy metal bands that appeared at the beginning of the 1980s. The biggest influence from that scene was Motorhead."
**Influence on Other Genres** : Hardcore has had a significant and undeniable influence on various other music forms, including metal, post-hardcore, skate punk, emo, Riot Grrrl, and grunge. "First of all, we've seen the influence this music has had on metal and in creating post-hardcore music as well as skate punk and emo. Metal, emo, riot girls, grunge is undeniable."
**Political Engagement** : Hardcore inspired people across the political spectrum to engage in politics, fostering activism in places like the Middle East and Indonesia, where Muslim hardcore acts used the genre to protest human rights injustices. "People got into politics because of hardcore, and we're talking all sides of the political spectrum. This includes Muslim hardcore acts in places like the Middle East and Indonesia, who used this style of music to rail against human rights and justices."
**Diversity in Contributors** : While hardcore was largely composed of white males, some of its most important contributors were Black and Latino, highlighting the genre's internal diversity despite its typical demographic profile. "Hardcore was largely made up of white males, but some of the sounds most important contributors were black and Latino."
**Catalyst for Alternative Music** : Hardcore was instrumental in establishing the concept of alternative music in the early and mid-1980s. This movement eventually led major labels to sign influential bands who, in turn, shaped subsequent generations of music. "Hardcore helped create the whole notion of alternative music in early and middle 1980s, eventually leading major labels to some great bands who in turn influenced the next generation."
**Essential for Grunge** : Hardcore's influence was so profound that it is considered essential to the emergence of grunge, implying that without hardcore, grunge would not have developed as it did. "Honestly, no hardcore, no grunge."
**Continued Global Presence** : Hardcore continues to thrive globally today, manifesting in a diverse range of sounds and styles, exemplified by modern bands like Baltimore's Turnstile, who blend hardcore roots with experimental directions. "And hardcore lives today around the world in a variety of sounds and flavors. For example, there's Baltimore's Turn Style, a group with serious hardcore roots or taking things in a more experimental direction."

# curiosity

**Rise of Indie Record Labels** : Hardcore music played an essential role as a catalyst for the rise of indie record labels in an industry traditionally dominated by major corporations. Many early hardcore bands formed their own labels due to a lack of interest from commercial radio or major labels, popularizing the idea of a do-it-yourself approach. "it is part of a much larger community of music that's been influential in many different ways throughout the decades, including being an essential catalyst for the rise of indie record labels in a world dominated by majors."
**West Coast Hardcore Tour Network** : Hardcore scenes across the West Coast, though emerging almost simultaneously, were interconnected through bands that toured relentlessly. This was facilitated by a network of hardcore-friendly venues and fans, ensuring the genre's widespread reach. "These scenes all emerged practically simultaneously, and were linked together by bands who toured non-stop using a network of hardcore friendly venues and fans."
**First Moshpits and Slam Dancing** : Hardcore music is associated with the emergence of the first moshpits and slam dancing. These intense forms of audience participation were initially observed in California and Washington, D.C. "It was with hardcore that we saw the first Moshpits with slam dancing, and was first seen in California and Washington, D.C."
**Hardcore's Role in Popularizing DIY Labels** : The hardcore scene was instrumental in popularizing the concept of forming one's own record label. This do-it-yourself approach became a widespread strategy for bands unable to secure deals with mainstream labels. "It was through hardcore that the idea of forming your own record label was popularized."
**Riot Grrrl Movement as a Reaction** : The Riot Grrrl movement was created, at least in part, as a reaction to the male-dominated, testosterone-heavy, and often exclusive world of hardcore, demonstrating how social dynamics within genres can spur new movements. "Some women repulsed by the testosterone of hardcore, and its exclusive women took matters into their own hands and created the entire Riot Girl movement as a response. Yes, that was, at least in part, a reaction to the male-dominated world of hardcore, and look at all the stuff that came out of the Riot Girl movement."
**Straight Edge Movement** : The straight edge movement, a lifestyle based on abstaining from alcohol, drugs, and promiscuous sex, originated directly from the hardcore scene, particularly from Ian MacKaye and a Minor Threat song of the same name. This philosophy continues to be a way of life for many. "The idea of straight edge and living a life without alcohol and drugs, totally a hardcore thing."
