## albums
**Fly Swatter (Demo Cassette)** : An early demo tape recorded in Scott Rainer's bedroom, consisting of a mix of original songs and covers. It was distributed on cassette and notably featured the track "Rebok Commercial." "It was a mix of original songs and covers recorded in Scott's bedroom that was distributed on cassette."
**Demo Number Two (Demo Cassette)** : A follow-up cassette demo that was also recorded in Scott Rainer's bedroom. This tape included the track "Marble Man," which the transcript notes began to showcase the developing Blink 182 sound. "The track is Marble Man and again it's pretty rough, but we start to hear the Blink 182 sound start to come through."
**Boota (1994) – Indie Release** : A 15-track independent release from January 1994, recorded in approximately two days. It served as a collection of all the material the band had written up to that point, presented with significantly improved audio quality, and featured the song "Carousel." "It was basically a collection of everything they had written to that point, but with much better audio quality."
**Cheshire Cat (1995) – Debut Studio Album** : Released on February 17, 1995, by Cargo Records. This album was recorded over six intense days, with the band often working 12-hour shifts to produce 16 tracks, many recorded live, which contributed to its "frantic feel." Initially available only on cassette, it transitioned to CD after fans began bootlegging CD burns, selling around 3,000 copies, and featured singles like "M&Ms" and "Wasting Time." "Released on February 17, 1995, there's Blink 182, who remember, still known as Blink at the time, with M&Ms, a song from their album called Cheshire Cat."
**Dude Ranch (1997) – Major Label Debut** : Released on June 17, 1997, as the band's first record with MCA. The recording process was rushed, complicated by drummer Scott Rainer's broken heels and Mark Hoppas's strained voice. MCA executives were initially hesitant about the album's "punk thing" and "goofy sense of humor," but it eventually sold half a million copies within a year after the single "Dammit" gained radio promotion. "The record was called Dude Ranch. And to be honest, the people at MCA weren't crazy about this record."
**Enema of the State (1999) – Breakthrough Album** : This album, originally conceived as "Turn Your Head and Cough," began recording in January 1999 and continued for three months at various California studios, produced by Jerry Finn. It achieved global sales exceeding 15 million copies, largely propelled by hit singles like "What's My Age Again?", "All the Small Things," and "Adam's Song." The album cemented the band's success during a time when pop punk had become very popular with fans. "Nursery rhymes on steroids. That's how Tom described the songs in the album that was once known as Turn Your Head and Cough. But somehow it would end up being retitled, Enema of the State."

## songs
**Rebok Commercial (Demo)** : This song was the opening track from Blink's very first demo cassette, "Fly Swatter," which was recorded in drummer Scott Rainer's bedroom. "It led off with a song called Rebok Commercial."
**Marble Man (Demo)** : Featured on their second demo tape, "Demo Number Two," this track, also recorded in Scott Rainer's bedroom, was noted as one of the first songs where the distinctive Blink 182 sound began to emerge. "The track is Marble Man and again it's pretty rough, but we start to hear the Blink 182 sound start to come through."
**Carousel (1994)** : A song included on "Boota," Blink's first professional recording, an indie release from 1994. It was part of a collection that showcased all the material they had written up to that point with improved audio quality. "This is called Carousel."
**M&Ms (1995)** : The official single from the "Cheshire Cat" album, written by Tom DeLong in his parents' garage. It managed to become a minor hit in certain areas of California. "It became a minor hit single in some parts of California."
**Wasting Time (1995)** : This track served as the second single released from the "Cheshire Cat" album. "The second single from the Cheshire Cat album that's Wasting Time."
**Dammit (1997)** : Identified as the breakthrough single for Blink-182, originating from their major label debut album, "Dude Ranch." The song, about a fictional breakup, features Mark Hoppas's strained vocals due to oversinging during sessions, which led Tom DeLong to perform the chorus during live shows. "That is the breakthrough single for Blink 182."
**Josie (1997)** : The fourth and final single from the 1997 album "Dude Ranch." The song is actually about a dog named Josie, owned by Elise Rogers, a singer in the Scott Punk band Dance Hall Crashers. Its music video featured actress Alyssa Milano and included a reference to a real San Diego restaurant, Sombrero's. "There is no such person named Josie. It's actually about a dog owned by Elise Rogers, one of the singers in the Scott Punk band Dance All Crashers."
**What's My Age Again? (1999)** : The lead single from "Enema of the State," infamous for its music video where the band members ran around Los Angeles seemingly naked, though they wore flesh-colored speedos. The band later regretted this image, not wanting to be perceived as a "joke band" or associated with the Red Hot Chili Peppers' use of nudity, vowing to exert more creative control over their public image. It was also part of an early experiment in digital distribution in 1999. "The video was famous for the band running around LA naked."
**All the Small Things (1999)** : Written by Tom DeLong for his girlfriend, Jennifer, this song was deliberately crafted to be catchy and become a hit. It successfully sold over 500,000 copies in the U.S. through 2000, reached number six in America, number two in the U.K. (selling half a million copies there too), and achieved top ten status in Canada, Italy, New Zealand, and Australia, significantly contributing to "Enema of the State"'s global sales. "All the small things, Tom DeLong wrote that for his girlfriend, Jennifer."
**Adam's Song (1999)** : A very serious song written by Mark Hoppas, reflecting on the loneliness he experienced on tour, feelings of depression, and suicidal ideation, partially inspired by a suicide letter he had read in a teen magazine. The song became controversial when a Columbine survivor, Greg Barnes, left it on repeat before taking his own life, although the band intended it as an anti-suicide message and received mail from teens stating it helped them choose not to end their lives. "Mark Hopper's remembered how lonely he got on tour."

## curiosities
**Early Group Names**: Before adopting the name Blink, the band was known by several other names, including Duck Tape and Figure Eight, along with other names that have since been forgotten. "The band, which was called Duck Tape and Figure Eight and some other forgotten names, put together a demo tape that they ended up calling Fly Swatter."
**San Diego Skate Park Origins**: The band's early activities and formation were deeply rooted in the skate park suburbs of San Diego, a region identified as a hub where music began "brewing" that would ultimately lead to 35 million records sold. "But down in the skate park suburbs of San Diego, something began brewing that would end up being responsible for selling 35 million records."
**From Zero to Worldwide Superstars**: The band experienced a rapid ascent from obscurity to becoming worldwide superstars, selling 35 million records, before facing significant internal and external challenges that nearly brought them "back to zero again," requiring them to "claw back." "These guys went from zero to worldwide superstars, and then almost back to zero again before clawing back."
**Filter Records Dispute**: An issue arose concerning Filter Records, the label founded by Mark Hoppas's boss with his savings, leading to a legal dispute over the ownership rights of the early demo tapes that were released through the label. "Now that would later be an issue when there was a dispute over who actually owned these tapes."
**Sleeping Arrangements during Cheshire Cat Recording**: During the six-day recording sessions for "Cheshire Cat," the band members conserved resources by staying in a single hotel room that contained only one king-size bed, forcing them to sleep "three across" for a few hours each night. "They stayed at a hotel down the street in a room with exactly one king-size bed. So they slept a few hours each night, sleeping three across."
**Bootlegging of Cheshire Cat**: Due to enterprising fans creating and distributing CD burns of the cassette version of "Cheshire Cat," Cargo Records was compelled to release an official CD version of the album. "They had to put out the CD because enterprising fans started bootling the cassette as CD burns."
**Manager Rick DeVoe**: The band secured Rick DeVoe as their manager, a professional who also managed groups like The Offspring, No Effects, and Pennywise, and who played a significant role in elevating Blink's profile, particularly within the snowboard crowd. "The band attracted a manager, a guy by the name of Rick DeVoe, who also worked with groups like The Offspring and No Effects in Pennywise."
**Millennium Falcon Van**: After accumulating enough funds from their gigs, the band was able to purchase their own van, which they playfully named the "Millennium Falcon." "They soon had enough money to buy their own van, which they dubbed the Millennium Falcon."
**Pennywise's Financial Support**: When Blink-182 received an invitation to perform in Australia but lacked the necessary funds for travel, the members of the band Pennywise stepped in and purchased their plane tickets. "So the guys in the band Pennywise bought them their tickets."
**"Sellout" Backlash from Fans**: Upon signing with MCA, a major record label, Blink-182 faced considerable criticism and "an awful lot of grief" from their fanbase, who accused them of being "sellouts" for abandoning their independent punk roots. "But Blink's fans gave them an awful lot of grief for signing with a major label. You know, that whole sellout thing."
**Scott Rainer's Broken Heels**: During the recording sessions for "Dude Ranch," drummer Scott Rainer, under the influence of alcohol, believed he could fly and jumped off an unspecified object, resulting in him breaking both his heels. Consequently, he recorded most of his drum parts while in a wheelchair or on crutches. "One night Scott got a little drunk and was convinced he could fly. So he jumped off something and broke both his heels."
**Original Name Dispute with Irish Band**: The band, initially known simply as "Blink," encountered a legal challenge over their name from an Irish band that had used the same name since recording new tracks in June 1991, predating the San Diego band's usage by several months and also having music available for sale earlier. "Back in June of 1991, an Irish band, who used to go with the name Red and Dino, recorded some new tracks under the name Blink."
**Blink-182 Name Change Origin**: To resolve the legal dispute with the Irish band also named Blink, the San Diego band agreed to append their name with the number 182. While popular myths link the number to movie references (like the F-bomb count in "Scarface" or the film "Turk-182"), the band maintains that the number "means nothing." "The truth is the number means nothing."
**Blink-182 Name Formatting**: For correct spelling, the band's name should be written with a lowercase 'b' for Blink and a hyphen between "Blink" and "182." Early versions of the "Cheshire Cat" album credited to just "Blink" (without "182") are considered collectible due to this eventual name change. "This means that if you want something collectible, look for early versions of the Cheshire Cat album credited to just Blink and not Blink 182."
**Jerry Finn's Production Influence**: Jerry Finn, who had previously produced Green Day's "Ducky" album, produced Blink-182's "Enema of the State." He brought a wealth of new studio knowledge to the band, significantly influencing their sound at a pivotal time when pop punk was gaining widespread popularity. "He brought in a ton of studio knowledge that was all new to the band and pop punk had become a real thing with fans."
**Nudity in "What's My Age Again?" Video**: The music video for "What's My Age Again?" depicted the band members running around Los Angeles appearing naked, though they were actually wearing flesh-colored speedos. The band later expressed dissatisfaction with this image, aiming to avoid being typecast as a "joke band" or compared to the Red Hot Chili Peppers, and vowed to take more creative control over their public image. "They wanted to leave nudity to the Red Hot Chili Peppers, and they did not want to be viewed as a joke band, so they vowed to take more creative control when it came to their public image."
**Early Digital Distribution Experiment**: "What's My Age Again?" was part of a pioneering digital distribution experiment in 1999. Thirty-second clips of the song, along with two others, were made available online through a company called A to B music, which was considered "pretty radical stuff back then." "Thirty-second clips of that song and two others were available to anyone online in 1999. Pretty radical stuff back then."
**Enema of the State Album Cover Model**: The woman featured on the cover of the "Enema of the State" album is Janine Lindemueller, who was a prominent porn star at the time. The band claimed they were unaware of her identity, stating they simply chose her photo from a stack of options to depict a "sexy nurse" character suitable for the album's title. "The woman on the cover of Enema of the State is Janine Lindemueller. She was a major porn star at the time."
**Enema of the State Cover Art Censorship**: The "Enema of the State" album cover underwent three different versions due to regulatory issues. The initial version featured a red cross on Lindemueller's hat and a capitalized 'B' in "Blink." The 'B' was corrected to lowercase, and subsequently, the American Red Cross intervened, citing the use of their symbol as a violation of the Geneva Convention, leading to its complete removal in the third and final version of the cover. "The American red cross saw the album and said using the red cross symbol in any other way than to signify the world of the real red cross is a violation of the Geneva convention."

Blink-182

## curiosities
**Early Life and Family Trauma**: Born in Washington, D.C., his family relocated to San Diego when his father, a Navy member, was transferred to a base there. This move coincided with his parents' divorce, a "broken family thing" that scarred him and led him to move around with his dad while his sister remained in San Diego with their mother. "Mark's father was in the Navy. He was born in Washington, DC, but ended up in San Diego when his father was transferred to the base there. That's when there was a divorce."
**Musical Discoveries**: As a teenager, he found solace in music, discovering and becoming influenced by groups such as The Cure and The Smiths. He dedicated a significant portion of his earnings from working at Little Caesar's pizza to purchasing CDs. "He was scarred by the whole broken family thing, which explains why he discovered groups like the cure and the Smiths as a teenager."
**School Expulsion**: During eighth grade, he was expelled from his school after showing up drunk to a basketball game. This expulsion serendipitously led him to register at another school for a semester, a key event that ultimately connected him with Tom DeLong. "One night he showed up at his school for a basketball game. He was in grade eight and he was drunk. That got him expelled, which meant he had to register at another school for one semester."
**Previous Bands and Connection to Tom**: Before joining Blink, Mark, who was a few years older than Tom DeLong and Scott Rainer, had already been a member of "a bunch of bands." He connected with Tom through his sister, who was dating one of Tom's friends, leading to their collaboration in songwriting. "Mark's sister was going out with one of Tom's friends, a connection was made and they started writing songs together."
**Construction Job**: While Blink was in its formative years, Mark maintained a day job at a construction site, where he spent his days hauling bags of cement and sand. "Mark, for example, had a gig where he hauled bags of cement and sand at a construction site all day."
**Voice Strain during Dude Ranch**: During the recording sessions for "Dude Ranch," Mark severely strained his voice by attempting to sing the album's first single, which was outside his vocal range. This injury accounts for his distinct vocal quality on the song "Dammit" and why Tom DeLong often sang the chorus when the band performed it live. "Mark had really screwed up his voice by trying to sing the first single from the song which was outside his range."
**"Adam's Song" Inspiration**: He composed "Adam's Song" based on his experiences of loneliness while touring, particularly as a single individual among bandmates with girlfriends. The lyrical themes of depression and suicide were significantly influenced by a suicide letter he had encountered in a teen magazine. "Mark Hopper's remembered how lonely he got on tour."

# artist - Scott Rainer

## member of
Blink-182

## curiosities
**Initial Meeting with Tom DeLong**: Scott Rainer, a drummer whose current band was "falling apart," was drawn to Tom DeLong's solo acoustic performance at a local battle of the bands. He later met Tom at a party, which eventually led to their musical collaboration. "And this attracted the attention of a drummer named Scott Rainer who was in a band that was in the process of falling apart."
**Bedroom Recording Studio**: His bedroom served as the primary recording venue for Blink's early demo tapes, including "Fly Swatter" and "Demo Number Two," where a mix of original songs and covers were produced. "It was a mix of original songs and covers recorded in Scott's bedroom that was distributed on cassette."
**Family Relocation and Living Arrangements**: His family moved to Reno, Nevada, which created a logistical challenge for him to participate in recording "Cheshire Cat" with the band. To overcome this, he moved in with his sister in San Diego temporarily. "To make matters even more complicated, Scott's family had just moved to Reno, Nevada. So he moved in with his sister in San Diego while the band made the record."
**Underage for Contract**: When Blink signed with Cargo Records, Scott was under the age of 18, making him legally ineligible to sign the contract himself. "The only member of the band who could sign the contract was Mark. He was old enough. Tom wasn't around because he was work and Scott was under 18, so he was ineligible."
**Disagreement over Major Label Deal**: He strongly believed that signing with MCA, a major label, constituted a "very unpunk move," creating significant friction and disagreement within the band, particularly with Mark Hoppas and Tom DeLong who were supportive of the deal. "Scott? Not so much. He thought that signing with MCA was a very unpunk move."
**Temporary Replacement by Mark Crawl**: Due to ongoing issues with his living arrangements, Scott was temporarily replaced by his high school friend, Mark Crawl. During this period, the band pooled money to facilitate Scott's travel back and forth between Reno and San Diego. "For a while, the band pulled money so we could fly back and forth to Reno. And for a time, he was replaced by a dude named Mark Crawl, who was Scott's high school buddy."
**Alcohol Abuse and Band Tensions**: By 1998, after three straight years of intense touring with little time off, Scott's drinking problems became severe, leading to escalating tensions, fights, and a breakdown in communication among band members. "But his problems with drinking continued, something had to give."
**Fired from Blink-182**: In May 1998, despite agreeing to enter rehab, Tom DeLong and Mark Hoppas felt that Scott's behavior was negatively impacting the band's performances, leading to his dismissal. While some accounts suggest he was fired over the phone, he was ultimately replaced by Travis Barker. "So Scott was fired. Some story said he was fired over the phone, but whatever the case is, he was out and Travis was in."
**Unfinished High School and College Thoughts**: Due to his commitments with the band, Scott never completed high school. Around the time of his departure from Blink-182, he began to consider attending college, indicating a period of personal conflict and reflection. "Even though because of his work with a man, he never even finished high school."

"Punk had always been there, waiting to be discovered by a whole new generation. And finally, in the 90s, the time was right."

"All different flavors too. There was pop punk, punk, punk, hardcore, garage punk, glam punk, queer core, riot girl, scop punk."
**California as a Center**: A significant hub for punk rock activity during the 1990s was California, with major scenes flourishing in areas such as Orange County, the Bay Area, and the skate park suburbs of San Diego. "A lot of the big action seemed to be centered in California, Orange County, the Bay Area."
**"Pop Punk" Emergence**: By the time Blink-182 released "Enema of the State," "pop punk" had solidified its place as "a real thing with fans," indicating its mainstream appeal and commercial viability, aligning perfectly with the band's sound and attitude. "And pop punk had become a real thing with fans. So Blink had this right sound and this right attitude at exactly the right time."
**Market for Adolescent, Sophomore, Goofy Pop Punk**: There existed a "stupidly huge market for adolescent, Sophomore, Goofy, Pop, Punk," which played a significant role in the massive global sales of albums like Blink-182's "Enema of the State." "On the other hand, there was a stupidly huge market for adolescent, Sophomore, Goofy, Pop, Punk."

# curiosity

**Post-Grunge Music Landscape**: In the early 1990s, following the massive success of grunge, the traditional barriers between mainstream rock and alternative music collapsed entirely, creating an environment where previously niche genres like Goth, industrial, various forms of electronica, and especially punk rock, began to emerge and gain wider attention. "After Grunge blew up in the early 1990s, the walls between mainstream rock and the alternative universe crumbled completely."
**San Diego's Music Scene in 1994**: In 1994, San Diego was not considered the central hub for alternative music; aspiring artists often had to relocate to Los Angeles, as Scott Wyland did with Stone Temple Pilots, to achieve significant success. "San Diego wasn't exactly the epicenter of alternative music in 1994. If you wanted to make it big, you'd have to be like Scott Wyland and Lee for Los Angeles."
**A to B Music Digital Distribution**: In 1999, A to B music was a digital company that pioneered an early form of online distribution, making 30-second clips of songs such as Blink-182's "What's My Age Again?" available to online users, a practice considered "pretty radical stuff back then." "Thirty-second clips of that song and two others were available to anyone online in 1999. Pretty radical stuff back then."
