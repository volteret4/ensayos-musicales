**Perceived Uncompromising Ideology (1976-1977)** : In the nascent stages of British punk, particularly around 1976 and 1977, The Sex Pistols were widely regarded as the most uncompromising and ideologically pure of all the bands in the movement. This perception stood in stark contrast to other bands like The Clash, who faced criticism for signing major label deals. "The clash was seen as the most uncompromising and the most ideologically pure of all the British punk bands."

Despite selling tickets for only 50 pence, fewer than 50 people attended, making it a "failed money losing gig." Nonetheless, this concert proved to be a transformative moment for English rock, as the small audience included future members of influential bands such as the Buzzcocks, Pet Shop Boys, Joy Division, and New Order. "Didn't seem like much of the time, but this failed money losing gig became a turning point for English rock."

The tour was marred by hostile audiences, Sid Vicious's extreme intoxication and altercations, Malcolm McLaren's difficult management, and deep internal animosity, all of which were captured in the film "The Great Rock and Roll Swindle." This show marked the last time the original lineup would perform together for nearly 18 years, and the final appearance with Sid Vicious before his death 13 months later. "It shows a band at the end of their rope and literally ready to blow apart."

# genre - New Rock
## curiosities
**Origin of the Term "Punk" (1976)** : The modern usage of the word "punk" as an adjective for hard, fast, and primitive rock emerged from a fanzine created by Legs McNeil in 1976. The magazine, named "Punk" after The Dictators' album art, featured bands like The Dictators, Lou Reed, and The Ramones, leading to the term becoming synonymous with their musical style. "The group that inspired the modern usage of the word punk when it came to rock and roll."
**Evolution of "Punk" Terminology** : The popularity of "Punk" magazine led to bands featured within its pages being referred to as "punk bands." This shorthand quickly evolved into common usage, where people would simply say they liked "punk music" or "punk rock," illustrating how a seemingly small cultural artifact can trigger a cascading series of changes, akin to the butterfly effect. "Pretty soon anyone who was featured in the magazine was being referred to as a punk band. They were shorthand when someone asked what kind of music you like, you could reply, I like groups that they write about in punk magazine."
