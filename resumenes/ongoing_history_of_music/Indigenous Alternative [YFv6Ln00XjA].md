# artist - Link Wray

## genres

## instruments

## curiosities
**Shawnee Heritage Obscured** : Link Wray was born in North Carolina to parents from the Shawnee tribe, making him a Native American. However, census documents of the time identified him as white, obscuring his true ethnicity. "His name was Link Ray, and he was born in North Carolina to parents from the Shawnee tribe. But that ethnicity was obscured by census documents that identified him as white, but he was definitely Native American."
**Inventor of the Power Chord** : Link Wray is credited with popularizing the idea of playing power chords on an electric guitar, a technique that would profoundly influence rock music. He is often called the "inventor of the power cord." "He has been called the inventor of the power cord, Jimmy Page, Pete Townsend, guys like that picked up the guitar in part because of Link Ray."
**Influence on Guitar Legends** : His innovative guitar playing style, particularly his use of power chords, directly influenced iconic guitarists such as Jimmy Page and Pete Townshend, who picked up the guitar in part because of his sound. "Jimmy Page, Pete Townsend, guys like that picked up the guitar in part because of Link Ray."

## songs
**Rumble (1958)** : This was Link Wray's first single, released in 1958, and was a fuzzy, distorted instrumental, highly unique for its era, predating fuzz pedals. Wray achieved its "dirty and dangerous sound" by poking holes in his amplifier's speaker, after initially experimenting with a composition titled "Oddball." "In 1958, he released his first single, which was a fuzzy, distorted instrumental, very unique for its day because it was recorded years before there was such a thing as a fuzz pedal for the guitar."
**Radio Ban for "Glorified Juvenile Delinquency"** : "Rumble" was banned by radio stations in New York and Boston because its gritty, street-fight-like sound was perceived as "glorified juvenile delinquency," despite being an instrumental track. Wray himself retitled the composition "Rumble" because he thought it sounded like a street fight. "He retied the composition rumble because he thought it sounded like a street fight. And it did because it was banned by radio stations in both New York and Boston because it sounded like, and this is a, quote, glorified juvenile delinquency."

# curiosity

**Director X's Music Video Career** : Director X is one of the biggest music video directors globally, responsible for "game-changing videos" from artists like Drake, Coldplay, Kendrick Lamar, Alicia Keys, Puff Daddy, Sisqo, Destiny's Child, Justin Bieber, 2 Chainz, Rosalia, Iggy Azalea, Shaggy, Beenie Man, Ariana Grande, and John Mayer. He has been active in the industry since the late 1990s and remains relevant. "These guys are responsible for game-changing videos from artists like Drake and Coldplay and Kendrick Lamar and so many more."
**Fella Production Company Breakthrough** : Director X and his collaborator broke their production company, Fella, with the music video for DJ Khaled, Drake, and Justin Bieber's song "Popstar." This moment marked a significant milestone in their "crazy journey" from Brampton, Ontario, to global recognition. "We broke our production company, Fella, with this music video for DJ Khaled, Drake and Bieber called Popstar."
**Art Context Podcast Concept** : Art Context is a new podcast hosted by Director X and another director named Director X (Tadges Christelot, although not explicitly named as Director X), which focuses on "storytellers that come from music videos." The podcast aims to give "amazing creative artists" the recognition they deserve by exploring their journeys, processes, and influences in creating iconic music videos, films, and content. "Our show is pretty much about, it's in the world of music. It's pretty much us sitting down with storytellers that come from music videos."
**Art Context Podcast Content** : The podcast "Art Context" explores directors' "come up," what drew them to filmmaking, their influences, highs, lows, and breakthrough moments, including the behind-the-scenes process like storyboards, art direction, and collaboration. The debut episode featured director Dave Meyers, discussing his approach to art and broader issues like Black Lives Matter. "The first podcast the debut of the show was with Dave Myers. Another guy that's been in the game for a long, long time. And just talking about that philosophy behind his approach to art, the work he's done. And as well digging into some of the larger world issues out there."
**Inspiration from 1980s Music Videos** : Director X was inspired to enter the field of music video direction by the 1980s era, watching videos from artists such as Madonna, Peter Gabriel, Phil Collins, Michael Jackson, and Aerosmith. He was fascinated by the storytelling, dancing, and style of these videos. "I came up in the 80s era where that's what made me fall in love with music videos, right? I was watching music videos by like Madonna and Peter Gabriel and like Phil Collins and Michael Jackson and and Errol Smith."
**Duran Duran's "Hungry Like the Wolf" as an Example** : The video for Duran Duran's "Hungry Like the Wolf" is cited as an example of the kind of imaginative, complex storytelling that captivated Director X and that their podcast seeks to explore the creation of. "So imagine if you could go back in the days and sit down with the Rand Rand and talk about the hungry like a wolf video, like what the hell? I can tell you guys being this jungle and and and just going through this crazy, crazy story."
**Hard Rock Empire Ownership** : The entire Hard Rock Empire, which includes 185 cafes, 25 hotels, and 12 casinos across 74 countries, is owned by the Seminole Tribe of Florida. "I should point out, speaking of the Hard Rock Hotel, that the entire Hard Rock Empire is actually owned by the Seminal Tribe of Florida. 185 cafes, 25 hotels, 12 casinos, 74 countries."
**Indigenous Peoples in Rock Music** : Over the last decade, there have been long overdue attempts at reconciliation with First Nations and Indigenous Peoples of North America, leading to increased learning about their music and contributions to the world of rock. This growing awareness is partly credited to Gord Downie's activism and the Polaris Music Prize in Canada. "The treatment of Indigenous Peoples makes for some really ugly history. But it has also been enlightening in positive ways. For example, we now have been learning more about First Nations music and the role people who identify as Native Canadians and Americans have played in the world of rock."
**Global Indigenous Music Contributions** : While the discussion focuses on Indigenous peoples of North America, the original peoples of other countries like Brazil, Australia, and New Zealand have also made their own contributions to rock music. "And keep in mind that we've only focused on the Indigenous peoples of North America. The original people of places like Brazil and Australia and New Zealand, so many other countries, have made their own contributions to rock."
