## curiosities
**Horror Movie and Memorabilia Passion** : Kirk Hammett has maintained a lifelong passion for horror movies and horror memorabilia, with his primary obsession being posters from old sci-fi and horror films. This interest began in his childhood, around the age of five or six. "Kirk has always been a fan of horror movies. And Kirk is a fan of horror memorabilia. But his biggest passion are posters from old sci-fi and horror movies."
**"It's Alive" Exhibition at ROM** : Hammett's extensive, comprehensive, and well-preserved collection of classic horror movie posters and artwork is deemed significant enough to be featured in major museum exhibitions, including "It's Alive: Classic Horror and Sci-Fi Art from the Kirk Hammett Collection" at the Royal Ontario Museum (ROM) in Toronto. This exhibition ran from July 12, 2019, until January 5, 2020, with plans to tour other museums globally thereafter. "This collection is so big and so comprehensive and so well preserved that it's the subject of exhibitions at major museums, including the Royal Ontario Museum in Toronto."
**Childhood Interest in Horror** : Hammett's fascination with horror began around age five or six, in the late 1960s or early 1970s. He was initially drawn to the genre because it offered a distinctly different feel and atmosphere compared to the prevalent Disney movies and cartoons that defined much of the culture he experienced as a child. "It was something that I was attracted to initially because it was so different and had a different sort of feel and atmosphere to a lot of the culture I was getting at the time as a 5 or 6 year old, you know, that would have entailed a lot of Disney movies and cartoons."
**First Horror Movie Experience** : His first horror movie experience was watching "Day of the Triffids," likely from the late 1950s or early 1960s. This viewing made a strong impression on him, prompting him to actively seek out more horror-related stories across various media such as comic books, magazines, and books. "Yes, a movie called Day of the Triffids."
**Collection Focus on Universal Monsters** : While he enjoys most horror movies from the turn of the last century to modern times, Hammett primarily focuses his collection on classic Universal horror monsters and movies from the early 1930s. His key interest lies in characters such as Frankenstein, Dracula, The Mummy, Wolfman, Phantom of the Opera, Creature from the Black Lagoon, and The Hunchback. "But I try to keep it to what I really love and what means the most to me, which is the classic universal horror monsters and those movies that came out in the early 30s."
**Preference for Thematic Horror** : Hammett prefers horror movies with supernatural, monster, outer space, or paranormal themes, tending to avoid overtly violent horror. He sees "torture porn" as a "cheap thrill" that often lacks the plot and intrigue he values, making it difficult for him to recognize any artistry in it. "I tend to shy away from horror movies that are overtly violent. You know, I like horror but I don't like horror for the sake of it."
**Diverse Horror Memorabilia** : Beyond movie posters, Hammett's collection encompasses a wide array of horror memorabilia, including props (items used in movies), toys (both vintage and continuously produced modern ones), masks, artwork, paintings, comics, original art, and sculptures. He differentiates his collecting from genres like Star Wars or Star Trek, strictly adhering to horror. "I, I tried to keep it to a horror movie posters, props, which are, you know, items that were used in certain movies. I love, I love the toys."
**Exhibition Content** : The "It's Alive" exhibition at the Royal Ontario Museum features his "core collection," specifically "my hundred best pieces," which totals over a hundred items. This selection broadly represents the horror genre from the turn of the last century to modern times, designed to illustrate the evolution of horror movie posters and the cultural trends reflected in them. "The collection that, of things that are going to, to the Toronto Museum is my core collection or in other words, my hundred best pieces."
**Early Entry into Collecting** : Hammett began collecting movie posters over 30 years ago, when the hobby was still underground and had relatively few participants. This allowed him to acquire available pieces at "sensible" prices because there was less competition among collectors. "I got into this hobby when it was so underground, over 30 years ago, there weren't a lot of movie poster collectors."
**Discovery of Rare Posters** : When Hammett started collecting over three decades ago, the existence of a "Frankenstein 3 sheet" or a "Mummy 3 sheet" poster was entirely unknown. He subsequently found both, and these pieces currently represent the only known copies of their kind in the world. "when I first started 30 years ago, no one knew that there was a Frankenstein three sheet out there or a mummy three sheet. I mean, I found both."
**Holy Grail Items** : Hammett's "holy grail" items, pieces he would love to find but which have not yet been discovered, include a "Bride of Frankenstein 3 sheet" and a "Dracula 3 sheet." These are billboard-sized posters from the 1920s that, if found, would be incredible additions to his collection. "Well, there's a lot of pieces out there that I would love to have that have not just been found at all, you know, like a bright of Frankenstein, three sheet, you know, Dracula, three sheet."
**Exhibition Goals** : Hammett hopes that visitors to his exhibition will first and foremost recognize the beauty and grandeur of the collected works and be "blown away" by seeing such a concentration of great pieces together. Beyond aesthetic appreciation, he aims for the exhibition to educate the public about the art of movie posters, their historical contribution to culture, and their evolution from the turn of the last century to the present day. "Well, first and foremost, I hope that people will see these things and just get, we'll recognize the beauty of it and the grand tour of it and just get blown away by seeing all this stuff in one spot because when you have all these really great pieces and they're all together, you get the synergy going and it's for me, it's a really cool energy and it feels really good and it's overwhelmingly just cool just to be surround by cool horror pieces if you're a fan."
**Favorite Piece: The Mummy 3 Sheet** : Hammett's absolute favorite piece in his collection, irrespective of its monetary value, is "The Mummy 3 sheet" poster, an image so significant to him that it is also depicted on his guitar. He finds himself completely engrossed by its colors, composition, and overall richness, describing it as having an "amazing effect" that allows his mind to dive in totally and get lost. "You know, it probably would be the Mummy 3 sheet. I love that image. It's on my guitar. You know, when I look at it, I just get lost in the colors of it, the composition, just the luscious of it."

## curiosities
**Horror Genre Evolution** : The horror genre underwent significant and diverse developments throughout the 20th century, experiencing distinct phases and themes from the 1930s through the 1970s. These changes were influenced by cultural shifts, leading various themes to fall in and out of fashion over the decades. "from there, the horror genre kind of exploded out and became what it initially became during the 30s, the 40s, the 50s, the 60s, the 70s."

# curiosity
**1930s Horror Poster Design** : During the 1930s, horror movie posters were characterized by vivid colors, lush aesthetics, and deep tones. Studios deliberately designed these posters to resemble romance movie posters, which were the most successful genre at the time, as a marketing strategy to attract a broader audience and boost ticket sales. "I mean, for instance, the movie posters from the 30s are very vivid and have very lush, deep tones. And you know, they, when you, I look at them, I get a sense of like romance and, and what not. And it makes sense because back in the 30s, the most successful, successful type of movie were romance movies, you know, relationship movies."
**1940s and 1950s Poster Trends** : The stylistic evolution of horror movie posters continued, with the 1940s introducing a distinctive "stylized look." By the 1950s, a new trend became prominent, often featuring women in bathing suits or lingerie being pursued by monsters, as notably seen in posters for films like "Creature from the Black Lagoon." "And then the 40s, the trend, trend, it changed again, and into a very sore stylized look. And then a change to get in the 50s and so on."
**Movie Posters as Cultural Reflections** : Movie posters serve as compelling historical indicators, inherently reflecting the prevailing socio-economic and cultural climate of their creation period. Their design and thematic elements were frequently crafted to mirror or engage with societal norms and trends of the era. "I mean, it really, it really is a sign of just whatever, whatever the social economic cultural thing was going on at the time."
**Rarity of Old Movie Posters** : Old movie posters are exceptionally rare due to widespread disposal practices and historical recycling efforts. After their theatrical runs, posters were routinely discarded and collected during "paper drives" throughout World War I and World War II, massive recycling initiatives that gathered paper from movie theaters and distribution centers (poster exchanges), resulting in very few surviving examples. "Exactly what happened. And to make matters worse, over the first world war and a second world war, there is these things called paper drives, which were basically big recycling efforts to get, you know, to the, all the paper that people weren't using and to put it in big trucks and wagons or whatever and haul it off to be recycled."
**Poster Exchanges** : Poster exchanges functioned as centralized distribution hubs where movie theaters would return used posters and from which new posters were disseminated to various cinemas within a town or county. These facilities were specifically targeted during paper drives, further contributing to the scarcity of surviving historical posters. "poster exchanges were these distribution centers where the theaters would send all the posters and they would distribute the posters to all the different theaters in the town or in the county."
**Unusual Survival Locations** : The survival of early movie posters is often attributed to serendipitous circumstances, with many found in unexpected places. Common discovery sites include being found between walls, where they were used as insulation, or in foreign countries such as Sweden, New Zealand, or Iran. "You know, a lot of them have been found in between walls as used as insulation. A lot of them have been found outside the country and weird places like Sweden or New Zealand or Iran."
**"Frankenstein 3 sheet" Discovery in Canada** : Approximately three years prior to the interview, a remarkable "Frankenstein 3 sheet" poster was discovered in Canada. This significant piece was found affixed to a wall within a boarded-up projection room of an old theater, a room that had remained sealed for 40 or 50 years, and its existence was previously unknown to collectors. "a major piece was found three years ago in Canada. It was founded, it was founded a boarded up projection room that no one had opened up for like last 40 or 50 years."
**Movie Poster Authentication Methods** : Authenticating rare movie posters involves multiple steps, akin to verifying other art forms. "Press books" published with the original movies provide crucial visual references for how posters appeared. Scientific testing of paper stock helps determine if it matches types used in the 1920s and 1930s. Additionally, robust "provenance," or a detailed history of the poster's origin and ownership, is essential for verification. "Well, when these movies came out, there are these scenes called press books and they had pictures of all the movie posters in the back. And so you had a direct sort of like visual reference from the studio of what these posters look like."
**Movie Poster Market Inflation** : The market for horror movie posters has experienced an exponential surge in value and price over the past 30 to 35 years. This inflation is largely driven by the extreme rarity of many posters—some existing as only a single known copy globally—and a growing influx of collectors and dealers willing to pay premium prices for such unique artifacts. "And you know, I've seen this every year for like the last 30, 35 years and exponentially it's brought the entire horror genre of movie posters way up in value and in price."
**Methods for Poster Collecting** : Aspiring movie poster collectors generally pursue items through two primary channels: purchasing from online dealers, or actively "hitting the road" to search in physical locations. This latter method involves exploring old movie theaters, antique stores, and flea markets, often targeting boarded-up cinemas where forgotten posters might still be hidden away. "if you want to become a movie poster collector, there's two ways to do it. You go out online and you buy from dealers or you hit the road, you go out there and you start hitting up movie theaters and, you know, antique stores and flea markets and just, you know, anything, everything."
**Uncredited American Movie Poster Artists** : A notable characteristic of American movie poster history is the frequent anonymity of the artists, with many remaining unknown or uncredited in official records. This contrasts with European artists, a greater number of whom were recognized and credited for their contributions to the medium. "Well, a lot of the artists that, that, that made the American movie posters are, are unknown or uncredited, but there's quite a few European artists that were known and credited."
**Roland Codon - French Artist** : Roland Codon was a distinguished French artist renowned for his contributions to French movie posters between the 1930s and 1950s. He was also highly active in creating contemporary advertisements, cementing his status as a very famous artist of his time. "One of the most famous ones was a guy named Roland Codon. And he was a French artist and he did a lot of the French movie posters from like 30s to the 50s. And he also did a lot, a lot of contemporary advertisements too."
**Toulouse L'Attract - Early French Poster Artist** : Toulouse L'Attract created early movie posters in France during the turn of the 20th century, predating many of the later artists and trends in the genre. "Toulouse L'Attract did early movie posters in France, you know, during the turn of the 20th century."
**Frank Fizzetta - 1960s Poster Artist** : Frank Fizzetta, a celebrated illustration artist, began designing movie posters in the early 1960s. His involvement marked a period in the 1950s and 1960s when other famous illustrators also contributed their talents to the movie poster medium. "Frank Fizzetta, you know, who's a famous illustration artist. He started doing movie posters in the early 60s, along with just the whole host of other illustrators who are, you know, famous for other reasons these days."
