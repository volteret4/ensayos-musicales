This strategy demonstrated that established artists could thrive without conventional record deals. "They realize that they're big enough that they don't really need a record deal."

"Pay whatever you want for it. If you choose to pay nothing, well, you know what, that's your choice."
**Financial Success of "In Rainbows" Model** : The unconventional "tip jar" approach for "In Rainbows" was a financial triumph for Radiohead. **Rock vs. Pop Cycle Theory** : A theory posits that rock and pop music are in a cyclical "death struggle" that repeats every 13 years. Rock music rises in popularity and creativity, peaks, then declines, while pop music follows an inverse pattern, dropping as rock ascends and rising as rock falls. "That theory contends that rock and pop music are locked in a death struggle that plays itself out like clockwork every 13 years."
**Rock vs. Pop Cycle Timeline (1951-2010)** : The rock versus pop cycle theory suggests four complete cycles have occurred since 1951. In 2010, the music world was in its fifth cycle, which began in the spring of 2003, with rock peaking in 2005 and hitting bottom in the preceding couple of years. "the rock versus pop cycle has played itself out through four complete cycles since 1951. If you want to know why, you don't have to go back and review chapter 14."
**"Rock is Dead" Claims (1960-1999)** : Throughout history, there have been recurring declarations that "rock is dead," notably in 1960, 1972, 1988, and 1999. These claims often coincide with periods when pop music dominates the forefront of the industry. "Just like we heard in 1999, 1988, 1972, and 1960."
**MP3 Invention and Perfection (1987-1991)** : The technology enabling MP3s originated from German engineers using an acapella track from Suzanne Vega's 1987 album "Solitude Standing" to perfect an algorithm for shrinking audio files. This invention, initially called ISO MP1 Audio Lera 3, was perfected by 1991, making it possible to transmit music via the internet. "This new invention was called ISO MP1 Audio Lera 3, which is MP3 to you and me. That was 1991 by the time it was perfected."
**Early Internet Music Sharing Challenges (Pre-1999)** : Initially, using the internet for music sharing was cumbersome due to slow uploading/downloading speeds and clunky, awful software. This made early digital music distribution awkward and inefficient for users. "It was awkward at first because uploading and downloading took forever and the software you had to use was clunky and awful."
**Internet Technology Improvements (Summer 1999)** : By the summer of 1999, significant advancements in internet technology had occurred. Computer modems were faster, networks improved, hard drive costs decreased, and web browsers became more user-friendly, setting the stage for more widespread online music activity. "By the summer of 1999 things had improved a lot. Computer modems were better, networks were faster, the cost of hard drives had begun to drop, and browsers were better and easier to use."
**Fan Dissatisfaction with Music Industry (Late 1990s)** : In the late 1990s, music fans, particularly college students, expressed growing dissatisfaction with the music industry due to high CD prices and limited selection. Rock fans were especially discouraged by the dominance of pop music like Backstreet Boys and Britney Spears. "At the same time, there was growing dissatisfaction by music fans about the price and selection of music, especially amongst college students."
**Decline of Early 1990s Alt-Rock Scenes** : By the late 1990s, the "awesomeness" of early 1990s alternative rock subgenres such as grunge, industrial, Manchester, and Britpop had faded. This left a void and a sense of disillusionment among fans, exacerbated by the lingering specter of Kurt Cobain's suicide, and rock was "flailing" to find the "next big thing." "The awesomeness of the alternation of the early 1990s, with grunge and industrial and Manchester and Britpop, had faded away."
**CD Single Phasing Out in North America** : The phasing out of the CD single in North America contributed to fan frustration. It meant that to acquire a single track, even from a one-hit wonder, consumers often had to purchase an entire album costing $18 or more, despite promises of falling CD prices. "the CD single was being phased out of North America. That meant that even if you did like a song put up by one of those one-hit wonders, you had to pony up 18 bucks or more just to get that one track."
**Napster's Launch and Impact (June 1, 1999)** : On June 1st, 1999, amateur programmer Sean Fanning released Napster to 30 friends, marking a pivotal moment in music history. This peer-to-peer file-sharing invention fundamentally changed how music was distributed and consumed, making it easier for millions to access music. "But everything changed on June 1st, 1999, a date that is every bit as important to the history of rock of all music, actually, than anything else you can imagine. This was the date an amateur computer programmer named Sean Fanning gave something he developed to 30 friends on the promise that they not share it with anyone else. He called his invention Napster."
**Proliferation of Peer-to-Peer File Sharing Sites** : Following Napster's success, numerous other peer-to-peer file-sharing sites emerged, including Scour, Qtmx, Freenet, Imesh, Bear Share, Edonkey, Audio Galaxy, Rapstar, Rockstar, Limewire, and Kazaa. This proliferation significantly amplified the scale of unauthorized music distribution. "There were dozens of other peer-to-peer file sharing sites. It was Scour and Qtmx and Freenet and Imesh, Bear Share, Edonkey, Audio Galaxy, Rapstar, Rockstar, Limewara, Kazah, endless list of these things."
**Legality of Portable MP3 Players (June 1999)** : A court ruling in June 1999, concurrent with Napster's release, declared portable MP3 players legal. This decision, coupled with the entry of major consumer electronics companies like Apple (with the iPod in October 2001), solidified MP3s as the dominant format and signaled the obsolescence of CDs. "A court ruling came down in June of 1999, almost exactly the same time Sean Fanning set Napster free. And this court ruling asserted that portable MP3 players were in fact legal."
**Apple iPod Launch (October 2001)** : Apple introduced the iPod in October 2001, entering the portable MP3 player market. The iPod's success, combined with the legalization of portable MP3 players, accelerated the shift from CDs to MP3s as the preferred music format. "including Apple and their little invention called the iPod in October of 2001, when they entered this space, MP3s were in and CDs on their way out."
**Collapse of Recorded Music Sales (2000-2009)** : Between 2000 and 2009, sales of recorded music experienced a dramatic collapse after decades of growth. CD sales were cut in half globally, with the U.S. market dropping 52% and annual sales plummeting from nearly a billion discs to 374 million by 2009. "Between 2000 and the start of 2009, CD sales were cut in half in just about every territory around the world."
**High School Musical Soundtrack Sales (2006)** : The "High School Musical" soundtrack was a top-selling album in 2006, selling 3.7 million copies. Its commercial success was notable during a period of overall declining recorded music sales, highlighting its specific appeal. "2006, high school musical, 3.7 million."
**Rock Music Peak and Decline (2005-2006)** : According to the rock vs. pop cycle theory, rock music peaked in 2005 and continued its momentum for about a year, but by the end of 2006, it was clearly in a slow decline. However, the period of 2006-2007 also saw a slight opening up of musical variety within rock. "2005, the last year rock music peaked as far as our theory of rock versus pop cycles are concerned. That was Green Day's American Idiot sold 3.4 million copies."
**Music Creation and Consumption Boom (2000s)** : Despite a challenging business environment in the latter half of the 2000s, more people than ever before were making, distributing, and consuming music. This surge was enabled by new technologies and the bypassing of traditional gatekeepers like record labels and radio stations. "At no point in history, were more people making music, distributing music, and consuming music."
**Decline of Record Stores and Rise of Online Retailers** : The necessity of visiting physical record stores diminished with the advent of online platforms like iTunes, P2P programs, and torrent sites, which offered instant access to music. Online ordering also provided a more convenient way to obtain physical copies, eliminating stock issues. "Many people really don't feel the need to go to a record store anymore. iTunes, P2P programs, and torrent sites give us almost instant access to whatever we want, often whenever the spirit moves us and wherever we happen to be."
**A La Carte Music Purchasing (iTunes)** : The introduction of online retailers like iTunes allowed consumers the freedom to purchase individual songs rather than entire albums, with few exceptions. This "a la carte" model transformed how people bought music, even if they chose not to pay for it at all. "with the introduction of online retailers like iTunes, we no longer have to buy an entire album if we just want one song with a few exceptions of course."
**Demise of Digital Rights Management (DRM)** : Digital Rights Management (DRM), which previously restricted the sharing and transfer of legitimately purchased music across devices, became obsolete. The music industry's efforts to lock down music and sue file-sharers ultimately failed, leading to a landscape free of these technological barriers. "digital rights management, those locks that made it very, very difficult to share music, even when it came to moving legitimately purchased songs from one device to another that you all own, that's all dead. DRM is done."
**Social Media's Role in Music Discovery and Sharing** : Social media platforms significantly altered how people discover, consume, and share music. Fans could easily search bands on Google, read blog reviews, check MySpace pages, watch YouTube videos, and share findings with friends via Facebook or Twitter, often accessing free downloads directly from artists. "social media is changing the way we find out about music, consume it and share it with others."
**360 Deal Business Model** : The "360 deal" emerged as a new business relationship where a music industry entity (label, promoter, etc.) provides upfront financial support for an artist in exchange for a percentage of all revenue streams. This includes traditional income, as well as concert ticket sales, merchandise, and licensing, aiming for mutual survival and growth in a changing market. "We began to hear of something called the 360 deal. This is a new type of business relationship between an artist or some kind of music industry entity."
**1995 vs. Today's Internet Access** : The level of global internet access for music distribution in the late 2000s was "the stuff of science fiction" in 1995. The ability to instantly upload a song or video and have it accessible worldwide became a taken-for-granted reality. "That kind of access was the stuff of science fiction in 1995 and today we just take it for granted."
**Breakdown of Music Silos** : The rigid "us versus them" tribalism that characterized rock music for decades, where fans were either alternative or mainstream, has diminished. Today, individuals can enjoy diverse music genres without stigma, exemplified by liking both The Beatles and a Norwegian death metal band. "all the silos have broken down. Johnny Rotten walked into Malcolm McLaren's clothing shop in 1975. He was immediately considered for the job of lead singer of the sex pistols, based on the fact that he was wearing an eye-hate pink Floyd t-shirt."
**Technology as a Driving Force** : Technology is an unparalleled driver in the creation, distribution, and consumption of music, shaping expectations that any song should be accessible anytime, anywhere, on any device. This fundamentally alters behaviors across the entire music ecosystem, including artists, fans, promoters, record companies, and stores. "technology is driving the creation of music, the distribution of music, and the consumption of music as never before."
**Sony Walkman's Impact** : The Sony Walkman significantly influenced music consumption by transforming listeners into "headphone-wearing zombies" within their individual musical bubbles. This marked an early shift towards personalized and isolated listening experiences. "Who would have guessed that the Sony Walkman would turn us all into headphone-waring zombies inside our individual bubbles of music?"
**CD Player Skip Button's Impact** : The "skip button" on CD players profoundly affected listener attention spans and tolerance levels for music. It accustomed listeners to quickly bypass disliked tracks, influencing how entire albums are consumed. "Think about what the skip button on CD players did for our attention spans and tolerances when it came to music."
**Napster's Sociological Component** : Beyond providing free music, Napster fostered a unique sociological connection. Early file-sharers could discover strangers globally who shared identical musical tastes, creating a powerful sense of not being alone in their preferences—a feeling now taken for granted. "Napster and his descendants not only allowed us to get music for free, but it also had a sociological component. Someone who remembers those early days of file sharing will no doubt recall a time when you found a stranger somewhere on the planet who had musical tastes that were just like yours."
**Changing Attitudes to Music Possession** : Modern attitudes towards music ownership are shifting, with less emphasis on physical possession. The ability to access any song on demand from "the cloud" reduces the perceived need to personally own the music, especially with seamless internet access in environments like cars. "If I can access any song I want any time I feel like it, who says I actually have to have that music on my person? Why can't I just get it from the cloud that mysterious part of the internet that has the ability to store anything?"
**Enduring Desire for Physical Music** : A segment of the audience continues to desire physical music ownership. Bands and labels respond by issuing special editions and features, such as T-shirts or posters, that cannot be digitally downloaded, catering to collectors and those who value tangible media. "A portion of us will still want to own music in a physical form. Both bands and record labels will continue to issue special editions and feature stuff that you can't download."
**Music Overload and Curation Needs** : The infinite supply of music online has led to "music overload," causing fatigue from the extensive time required to sort, research, find, and organize music. This results in faster consumption and burnout of bands and sounds, suggesting a need for trusted filters or curated lists. "after a period of time when we had an infinite amount of music to choose from, we're all getting a little tired of the time it takes to sort through it all."
**Social Media's Increasing Role in Music** : Social media platforms like Facebook, YouTube, and Twitter are destined to play an even larger role in music, influencing its creation, spread, and consumption. This evolving landscape suggests significant opportunities for new, successful ideas and wealth creation. "social media, Facebook, YouTube, Twitter and whatever comes next will play a bigger and bigger part in music. It's creation, it's spread and it's consumption."
**Future of the Music Business** : The music business will continue to exist, but its "old ways of doing things are gone forever." The industry faces ongoing challenges in monetizing music and ensuring artists are paid while meeting consumer demands. Solutions will require copyright reform, tariff adjustments, and international agreements beyond just price reductions. "the music business will continue to exist, although the old ways of doing things are gone forever. There are still years of trying to figure out how to make music pay and how musicians can get paid while still filling the needs, wishes and demands of the consumer."
**Rock Music Longevity** : Rock music is "never, ever going to go away," despite periodic downturns or claims of its demise. This resilience is attributed to a fundamental human need to create music, the increasing volume of music being made and consumed in diverse forms, and record attendance at live shows. "rock music, despite how bad it may look at times, is never, ever going to go away. Humans have a hardwired need to make music."
