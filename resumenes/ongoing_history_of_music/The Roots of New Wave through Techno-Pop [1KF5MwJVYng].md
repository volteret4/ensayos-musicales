## curiosities
**Boston-Based 20-Somethings** : The group is based in Boston and is composed of musicians in their twenties who have developed a significant obsession with a specific type of sound from the 1980s. "Passion Pit is a Boston-based group of 20-somethings who, even though they weren't even born until halfway through the decade, have developed a major obsession with a certain type of sound from the 1980s."
**Analog Sound Discovery** : The band discovered analog sound through sources like "Switched On Bach" and newer bands like Boards of Canada, finding warmth and character in the out-of-tune, drifting nature of analog synths. "For me, personally, it was switched on back and there's already stuff in that. There's bands like Bords of Canada that are newer, but they had that same kind of analog sound."
**Apartment Studio Setup** : One member records in a soundproofed two-bedroom apartment on the Lower East Side, using the larger bedroom as a studio. The soundproofing includes 3,000 pounds of sand under the floor and insulated walls to prevent disturbing neighbors. "Well, I have a two bedroom apartment and a lower east side, and I sleep in the smaller bedroom, and the slightly larger bedroom is my studio, but I've had it soundproofed."
**Hoarding Old Equipment** : The band actively acquires vintage gear, including analog drum machines, spring reverb units, tape delays, and old synths, often through platforms like eBay. "I'm sort of a hoarder when it comes to old equipment."
**Yamaha SK Line Preference** : The band expresses a strong preference for the Yamaha SK line of synthesizers, specifically the SK15, SK20, and SK30, valuing their expressive capabilities which are modeled after ARP synths. "We love the Yamaha SK line. 15, 20, 30."
**SK30's Hidden Value** : The Yamaha SK30 is highly valued by the band because it contains a CS80 chip, which is usually found in much more expensive synthesizers. One member acquired an SK30 for free. "Their chip inside of them is... The SK30 is a CS80 chip, which is a lot more expensive synthesizer than the SK30."
**Analog Tuning Challenges** : While analog keyboards offer depth, they require a lot of tuning during live performances, which is a trade-off compared to sterile digital sampled keyboards. "Yeah, but once in a while I see why, because we're on stage, there's a lot of tuning going on. But it makes the sounds much more depth to it."

# genre - Technopop

## genres
Synthpop
Industrial
Cold Wave
Electric Body Music (EBM)

## curiosities
**Simultaneous Independent Discovery** : Described as a specific genre of 80s music that was simultaneously and independently discovered by a number of artists, representing an important part of alt-rock history. "What we're hearing is a number of artists simultaneously and independently discovering a specific genre of Aedes music. Back then, it was called Technopop, although synthpop was also acceptable. Some people loved it. Some people hated it. But whatever the opinion, it was a very important part of alt-rock history."
**First Wave (1977-mid 1980) – Cold and Robotic** : This initial period was characterized by a cold, robotic musical approach, laying the foundational groundwork for what would later be known as industrial music. "The first was from 1977 through to about the middle of 1980. That was when the cold, robotic approach was invoked. Songs like this formed the very basic foundations of what we would later call industrial music."
**Emotional Characteristics** : The emotions conveyed by this music were often cold, bleak, paranoid, and hollow, reflecting the political, social, and economic environment of the late 70s and early 80s, marked by recession, unemployment, and the Cold War. "But the emotions it conveyed were pretty cold and bleak and paranoid and hollow. And again, this was in complete step with the political, social, and economic environment of the day. Recession, unemployment, the Cold War."
**Antithesis of Punk Fashion** : The genre developed its own distinct look, which was the opposite of scruffy punk fashion, often featuring artists in suits with short hair and ties. "This sound also came with its own look. In fact, it was the antithesis of Scruffy Punk Fashion."
**Second Wave (Post-mid 1980) – Joyous and Danceable** : This later period shifted towards electronic music that was danceable, joyous, and fun, reflecting an atmosphere of returning optimism as economic recession began to dissipate. "And we got into our second wave which features the kind of electronic music you can dance to. Synthesizing music didn't have to be all gloomy and detached. It could also be joyous and fun."
**Evolution by 1984** : By 1984, the genre had evolved from its cold, robotic origins to encompass a wide spectrum of emotions, styles, and fashion senses. "By 1984, Tecno Pop had evolved from being this cold, robotic music into something that covered a wide range of emotions and styles and fashion senses."
**Decline and Revival (1990s-2004)** : Interest in 80s-style Technopop declined significantly in the early 1990s with the rise of grunge and hip hop. However, starting around late 2004, these early 80s sounds began to reappear in popular music, leading to a "current Tecno Pop revival" and an explosion of synth-pop bands. "When Grunge and Hip Hop came along in the early 1990s, general interest in synth pop really kind of just declined and it stayed that way for years. It did, however, live in some respects in the music of nine-inch nails, de Pesh mode, radio head, and Bjork. But as for 80-style Tecno Pop, it was done. Or was it? But starting somewhere around late 2004, these early 80s sounds began to creep back into popular music."

# genre - Synthpop

## curiosities
**Alternative Name for Technopop** : This term was also an acceptable alternative name for the genre known as Technopop during its original emergence. "although synthpop was also acceptable."
**Prevalence of Two-Person Model** : Many bands in this genre, especially from the UK, commonly featured a two-person lineup: a singer or front person and a composer/programmer/operator. "Many of them had this two-person model. You had the singer or front person and at the back, the composer, programmer, operator."
**MTV Programming Staple** : Its stylish nature made it perfectly suited for MTV upon its August 1981 launch. British synthpop acts provided a significant source of programming for the new channel due to their extensive backlog of performance videos. "And they were all very stylish which was perfect because there was this new thing called MTV. And it needed to be fit. See when MTV first went on the air in August of 1981, they had a grand total of 250 videos, most of which were from Britain. So the UK had this long history of bands performing on TV, so they had a big backlog of stuff to choose from. So until North American record labels in bands started investing in making videos, a big source of programming for MTV were clips from these stylish synth pop bands from the UK."
**Chicago House Music Influence** : Synthpop influences later migrated to Chicago, becoming an integral part of the development and rise of house music in the city. "In North America, synth pop influences settled in Chicago, where the sounds became integral to the rise of house music."

# genre - New Wave

## curiosities
**Post-Punk Catch-All Phrase** : By the late 1970s, new wave became a predominant trend, serving as a broad term to describe music emerging after the punk explosion. It was identifiable as not being punk, yet clearly demonstrated punk's influence. "The big thing was new wave. This catch-all phrase to describe some of the music that was coming after punk. And the best definition of new wave I've ever heard was, it wasn't punk, but by listening to it, you could tell that punk had happened."
**Opened Musical Possibilities** : Punk had previously opened up new avenues for musicians, emphasizing that raw desire and a message were more important than exceptional technical skill, a philosophy that informed new wave. "See, punk had opened everyone's eyes to possibilities. The message was, you didn't have to be the world's greatest musician. But if you had the desire and something to say, well then you should say it, dammit."

# instrument - Keyboard Synthesizer

## curiosities
**Development and Affordability** : These instruments, in development since the early 1940s, became significantly cheaper by the late 1970s. This made them accessible to a wider audience, moving beyond traditional studios or university labs. "At around this time, keyboard synthesizers, inventions that had been in development since the early 1940s, started to fall in price. Instead of being confined to a studio or a lab on a university campus somewhere, it was possible to pick up a reasonably powerful keyboard for a reasonable price."
**Unprecedented Sound Capabilities** : Synthesizers were capable of generating entirely new and unimagined noises, including unique square waveforms, which made their sounds artificial and distinct from the curvy or spiky waveforms of traditional instruments. "These synthesizers could not only make noises that had never been heard before, but they made sounds that no one had ever even imagined before. And with a little experimenting, you could build a kind of music that was utterly unique. ... See up until this point, all musical instruments generated sounds that could be represented with curvy or spiky waveforms. synthesizers could generate sounds with square waveforms. This made them sound artificial and unreal."
**Democratizing Music Production** : The ease of use, with simple knob twiddling yielding brilliant sounds, meant musicians didn't need to be highly skilled. This made the technology accessible and versatile, serving as a "great equalizer" that allowed professional-sounding music to be made even in a bedroom by the late 70s. "You know, you twiddle a couple of knobs on these new machines and you sounded brilliant. It was easy to be awesome. You didn't have to be pink Floyd, some huge prog rock band to access this technology. ... And by the late 70s it was possible to make a full professional sounding song in your bedroom. ... And synthesizers became a great equalizer for a great number of one of the musicians."
**Vintage Revival and Value** : By the late 2000s, the price of vintage analog synthesizers had dramatically increased. These older machines produce unique sounds unattainable from modern digital ones, offering a warmth and depth despite requiring more frequent tuning. "Meanwhile, the price of vintage synthesizers has skyrocketed. These old analog machines have sounds that you just can't get from modern digital ones. ... But it makes the sounds much more depth to it."

# curiosity

**Punk's Influence on Music Creation** : By the late 1970s, the punk explosion had highlighted that one didn't need to be a technically proficient musician to create music, provided there was desire and something meaningful to express. "See, punk had opened everyone's eyes to possibilities. The message was, you didn't have to be the world's greatest musician. But if you had the desire and something to say, well then you should say it, dammit."
**MTV's Early Programming (August 1981)** : Upon its launch, MTV had a limited library of only 250 videos, mostly from Britain. This made British bands, particularly stylish synth-pop acts with existing performance videos, a crucial source of programming until North American labels began investing in video production. "See when MTV first went on the air in August of 1981, they had a grand total of 250 videos, most of which were from Britain. So the UK had this long history of bands performing on TV, so they had a big backlog of stuff to choose from. So until North American record labels in bands started investing in making videos, a big source of programming for MTV were clips from these stylish synth pop bands from the UK."
**Socio-Economic Environment and Dehumanized Music** : The brutal recession and Cold War of the late 1970s and early 1980s made the concept of dehumanizing music by entrusting its creation to machines an appealing artistic statement for some. "After the all-too-real intensity of punk rock and the brutal recession of the late 70s and early 80s, the idea of dehumanizing music by turning it over to machines became pretty appealing to some people."
**Decline and Resurgence of Vintage Synths** : People initially perceived old analog keyboards as junk, preferring sterile, digitally sampled alternatives. However, by the late 2000s, producers grew tired of digital sounds and sought out vintage 80s analog keyboards, driving up their prices due to their unique depth and character. "People think they're junk, so they throw them out, and they prefer that sterile, digitally sampled keyboards, which sound terrible. ... These old analog machines have sounds that you just can't get from modern digital ones."
