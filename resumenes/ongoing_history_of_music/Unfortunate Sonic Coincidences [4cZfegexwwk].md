**Guitar Riff Copyright** : Guitar riffs are generally not copyrightable, which allows similar riffs to appear in multiple songs across different artists and eras without legal infringement, as seen with songs like Killing Joke's "Eighties" and Nirvana's "Come As You Are," or Green Day's "Brain Stew," Chicago's "25 or 6 to 4," and Led Zeppelin's "Babe I'm Gonna Leave You." "You can't copyright a guitar riff, which also takes us back to that Food Fighters Rony James D.O. case."

# curiosity

**Earworm** : This term describes a phenomenon where a snippet or clip of a song repeatedly plays in one's head, often feeling like it's on a continuous loop. "That's when a clip of a song keeps running through your head over and over and over again on a loop and a drive you crazy."
**Mondegreen** : This refers to a misheard lyric, where a listener incorrectly interprets a song's words. A notable example is in Jimi Hendrix's "Purple Haze," where "Excuse me while I kiss the sky" is often misheard as "Excuse me while I kiss this guy." "That's a misheard lyric and a great example is in Jimmy Hendrix's purple haze."
**Music Theory and Finite Combinations** : Western music is built upon a 12-note scale, with notes mathematically related by semitone intervals, forming chords, melodies, and harmonies. Despite theoretically vast numbers of possible note combinations (over a quintillion when considering durations), the requirement for music to sound "pleasing to the ear" significantly limits viable arrangements, leading to inevitable duplication of musical ideas over time. "Western music is based on a scale of 12 notes."
**Definition of Plagiarism** : Plagiarism is defined as "the act of appropriating someone else's work and passing it off as your own." It distinguishes from unfortunate sonic coincidences, where intent is often a key factor. "The act of appropriating someone else's work and passing it off as your own."
**Copyright Limitations** : Certain musical elements are not protected by copyright, including song titles, rhythms and beats (e.g., the "Bo Diddley beat" or the "shave and a haircut, two bits" rhythm), and chord progressions. This means these elements can be used by different artists without legal repercussions. "One title is for one. If you wanted to, you could write a piano ballad and call it smells like teen spirit and there is nothing anybody could do."
**Shave and a Haircut, Two Bits Rhythm** : An old, seven-note rhythmic pattern that predates formal copyright laws, cited as an example of a rhythm that cannot be copyrighted. "And before that, there's the old seven note shaven a haircut, two bits rhythm."
**Fortunate Sonic Coincidence and Royalty Deal** : An anecdote detailing a friend who co-wrote a multi-platinum, Top 20 song in 1985. Years later, a European band with a similar song, expecting it to be a massive hit, approached him for a deal to avoid a lawsuit, offering royalties based on album sales and single chart performance. The European album sold 8 million copies, and the song became a number one hit in America, providing the friend with significant and ongoing royalties for a song he didn't write. "For him, this unfortunate sonic coincidence was very fortunate."
**Legal Standard for Copyright Infringement** : In court cases regarding musical copyright, the judge and jury primarily consider the "note by note" comparison of songs as they appear on sheet music, rather than their recorded sound. Elements like production techniques and outboard effects that might make recordings sound similar are disregarded, which can lead to findings of non-infringement even when audible similarities exist, as in the Led Zeppelin vs. Spirit case. "The only thing that matters are just the notes, how the song looks when it's plotted out on sheet music."
