# artist - The Beatles

## albums

**Abby Road** : This album became the accidental home of the first hidden track, "Her Majesty". The track was initially intended to be removed from the final cut by Paul McCartney, but due to Abbey Road Studios' policy of leaving edited material at the end of the master tape and a subsequent oversight by the mastering engineer, it was inadvertently included after 20 seconds of silence, a surprise the band ultimately decided to keep. "When the Beatles reviewed the master and were surprised by that song coming out of the silence, they thought it was really cool. Leave it in, they said. And so the first hidden track came to be as the result of an accident."

**Her Majesty** : This song became the first accidental hidden track in music history, appearing at the very end of the "Abbey Road" album after 20 seconds of silence. Initially, Paul McCartney had requested its removal, but tape assistant John Kerlander, following studio rules, spliced it to the end of the master tape with a gap, a note for the mastering engineer that was either unread or misunderstood, leading to its unexpected inclusion which the band approved upon hearing. "Paul McCartney, he said, great, but you know, take that last track, a song called Her Majesty Out. It just doesn't work. Just drop it."

He was responsible for compiling the rough mixes for the 16-minute medley featured on side two of the "Abbey Road" album. His adherence to studio rules regarding excised material led to the accidental creation of the first hidden track, "Her Majesty." "A guy with a name of John Kerlander was a tape assistant at Abby Road Studios. At just 18 years of age, he was the guy in charge of putting together the rough mixes for the 16-minute medley on side two of the album."
**Abbey Road Studios' Editing Policy** : A specific rule at Abbey Road Studios mandated that any material edited out from a master tape had to be retained at the end of the tape for future reference. This policy, followed by tape assistant John Kerlander when Paul McCartney requested "Her Majesty" be removed from "Abbey Road", inadvertently led to the creation of the album's hidden track. "The rules at Abby Road said that any material edited out from a master tape had to be left at the end of the tape for reference."
**"Her Majesty" - Accidental Hidden Track** : The inclusion of "Her Majesty" as a hidden track on "Abbey Road" was an unintended consequence of studio procedure. After Paul McCartney requested the song's removal, tape assistant John Kerlander snipped it out and spliced it to the end of the master tape with 20 seconds of blank tape, in accordance with Abbey Road's rules for edited material. A mastering engineer either overlooked or misread Kerlander's note, resulting in the song's presence on the final master, which the Beatles found "really cool" and decided to keep. "And so the first hidden track came to be as the result of an accident."

# curiosity

**Hidden Audio on CDs - Common Type** : The most frequently encountered form of hidden audio on compact discs involves tracks positioned at the very end of a series of unlisted songs. These unlisted segments only become apparent after listeners believe the album has concluded its official runtime, as exemplified by the Catherine Wheel's "like cats and dogs" album. "That's the most common sort of hidden audio on CDs."
**Hidden Audio on CDs - Pre-gap Method** : The "pre-gap" or "Track 0" method is a technique used to conceal a song *before* the official beginning of a CD, specifically in the space preceding Track 1. This hidden audio is typically accessed by inserting the CD into a CD player (this method is less reliable on computers) and repeatedly pressing the rewind button from Track 1 until the hidden content is reached. "You can hide a song before the beginning of a CD. This is called a pre-gap. That's the space and the wrist space before Track 1."
**CDs vs. Vinyl for Hidden Audio** : The advent of Compact Discs in the early 1980s significantly simplified the practice of embedding hidden audio compared to vinyl records. While vinyl allowed visual counting of song bands on a side, the CD's blank, shiny surface provided an ideal medium to render material invisible to everything except the CD player itself, making hidden tracks much easier to implement covertly. "When CDs came along in the early 1980s, hidden audio became much easier. I mean, if you look at a vinyl record, you can count the bands and see exactly how many songs are on a side. But with a CD, you can't."
**"Throwaway" Tracks Becoming Hits** : A recurring phenomenon in music history where tracks initially considered "throwaways" or simply unlisted by bands (such as The Clash's "Train in Vane" or Cracker's "Euro Trash Girl") surprisingly achieved commercial success, either by becoming Top 40 hits or being released as singles, once they were discovered by the public. "And once again, we have an instance of a throwaway track becoming a hit song."
**Run-Out Grooves on Vinyl - Hidden Audio** : A historical practice on vinyl records involved artists creatively utilizing the run-out grooves to embed brief audio snippets that would play in an endless loop. Examples include a dog whistle on The Beatles' "Sgt. Pepper", "tripping crickets" on a Brian Eno solo album, a dripping tap at the end of Pink Floyd's "Atom Heart Mother", and an unending loop of applause on ABBA's "Super Trouper". "Other bands had fun with run-out grooves too."
