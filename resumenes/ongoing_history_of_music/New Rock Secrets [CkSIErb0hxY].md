# artist - Elvis Costello

## albums
**My Aim is True (1977) – Debut Album with Clover** : When Stiff Records signed Elvis Costello, they needed to release material quickly. Jake Riviera, head of Stiff, recruited the band Clover to provide energy and anger to Costello's acoustic, twangy demos at Pathway Studios. Everyone from Clover, except their lead singer, played on the debut album, with the band officially changing their name to The Shamrocks for the contract to avoid legal issues with another group named Clover.
"a record that could not have been made without the help from, say it again, Huey Lewis and the news."

## curiosities
**Early Backup Band (Clover/The Shamrocks)** : Stiff Records expedited the release of Elvis Costello's debut album, *My Aim is True*, and due to time constraints, a permanent band could not be assembled. Jake Riviera, the head of Stiff, brought in a touring group named Clover to provide instrumentation for Costello's acoustic demos at Pathway Studios. They played under the name The Shamrocks for the official contract to avoid being sued by another band called Clover.
"But there wasn't enough time to get a real band together."

**Clover's Transformation into Huey Lewis and the News** : The band Clover provided backup instrumentation for Elvis Costello's debut album, *My Aim is True*, performing under the name The Shamrocks. # artist - Ramones

## members

## genres
Punk Rock

## albums
**Road to Ruin (1978) – Thick Guitar Sound** : The Ramones' album *Road to Ruin*, released in the summer of 1978, featured a noticeably "nice and fat and thick" guitar sound. This was a result of producer Ed Stasium's preference for "big thick guitars," leading to more layered guitar tracks on the band's albums over time.
"One of the most thickly layered Ramon songs to that point was I Want to Be Sedated."

## songs
**I Want to Be Sedated (Year Implied: 1978)** : This iconic punk rock song features one of the most famous one-note guitar solos in punk history. However, this elegant solo was not played by Johnny Ramone, but by the album's producer, Ed Stasium, who also contributed a muted rhythm part with his Fender Stratocaster to build up the thick guitar layers on the track.
"It's not Johnny. It's Ed."

## curiosities
**Johnny Ramone's Conservative Politics** : Johnny Ramone was known as a hardcore Republican, one of the most conservative punk rockers. He openly admired and worshipped Ronald Reagan. This political stance often put him at odds with Joey Ramone, especially when he had to perform anti-Reagan songs written by Joey, such as "Bonzo Goes to Bitburg."
"As politics go, he was about the most conservative punk rocker you could find anywhere."
**Joey Ramone's Capitalism** : Despite embodying the image of an "archetypical punk," Joey Ramone was, in fact, a hardcore capitalist. In the years leading up to his death, he became very skilled at stock trading over the internet. He reportedly amassed a considerable fortune by diligently following the Wall Street Journal, watching CNBC, and utilizing his online trading account.
"He apparently amassed quite a little fortune by reading the Wall Street Journal, watching CNBC and using his online trading account."

## songs
**God Save the Queen (Year Implied: 1977)** : Glenn Matlock, one of the co-writers of this and other Sex Pistols songs, later adopted a stance on public swearing that aligned with his parents' views. This marked a significant shift from the band's notoriously provocative image and behavior.
"At least one of the people who co-wrote this song has officially adopted the attitude of his parents."

## curiosities
**Bill Grundy Interview (1976)** : On December 1, 1976, the Sex Pistols appeared on Bill Grundy's afternoon talk show as last-minute substitutes. The band arrived drunk and surly, leading to an infamous on-air interview filled with obscenities. This controversial broadcast, likened to the "Janet Jackson Nipplegate scandal of its day," transformed the Sex Pistols from mere curiosities into national and international sensations.
"Still, it was the thing that turned the sex pistols from curiosities to national and international sensations."
**Glenn Matlock's Changed Views on Swearing** : Sex Pistols bassist Glenn Matlock, now a middle-aged father of two, has publicly stated that he believes swearing on television should be banned. In an early 2005 TV interview, he expressed his dislike for his children hearing obscenities on TV and advocated for government intervention to address the issue.
"It's pathetic when people swear for the sake of it. Something ought to be done about it."
**Sid Vicious's Inability to Play Bass** : Glenn Matlock was fired from the Sex Pistols in 1977 for allegedly liking The Beatles, which was considered a "crime" within the band. His replacement was a fan named Sid Vicious, who was chosen for his cool look and punk-rock attitude, even though he could not play the bass. During the band's last tour in America in late 1977 and their final original show at the Winterland Ballroom in San Francisco on January 14, 1978, Sid appeared on stage but did not play a note; roadies were instructed to unplug him, and another musician played his bass parts behind the amplifiers.
"Unfortunately, though, Sid lacked one qualification. He couldn't play the bass."

**Cigarettes & Alcohol EP (1994) – "I Am the Walrus" Bonus Track Hoax** : The 1994 *Cigarettes & Alcohol* EP included a bonus track of Oasis performing "I Am the Walrus," which was marketed as a live recording from the Cat House in Glasgow, Scotland, in June 1994. This track was initially celebrated as a prime example of early live Oasis but was later revealed to be a fabrication.
"Its Oasis performing I Am the Walrus at some place called the Cat House in Glasgow, Scotland in June of 1994."

## songs
**I Am the Walrus (1994) – Falsified Live Recording** : The track presented as a live recording of "I Am the Walrus" on the 1994 *Cigarettes & Alcohol* EP was a hoax. Noel Gallagher disclosed that it was actually recorded during a sound check for a Sony convention at the Glen Eagles Hotel in 1993, with the band being "very, very, very drunk." The band deliberately lied about its origin, claiming it was from a gig at the Cat House in Glasgow to appear "far cooler," and even "nicked" the crowd noise from an Eric Clapton and The Faces bootleg recorded at the Roundhouse around 1973. The truth was later acknowledged on *The Masterplan* album.
"The problem is that it was a hoax."
