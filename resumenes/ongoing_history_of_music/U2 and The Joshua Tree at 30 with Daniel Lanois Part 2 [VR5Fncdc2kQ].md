## songs
**Radio Mining Town** : This song was already being discussed as part of side two of The Joshua Tree album. "Actually, we already started that with Radio Mining Town."
**In God's Country** : Bono reportedly sought to "rock up" the album, leading to the creation of this song. It features a vibrant, propelling groove, considered one of The Edge's best with its ECHO rhythms, and a great groove from Larry. Released as the fourth single in North America, approximately three months after "Where the Streets Have No Name," it may be about Ireland or America, and Bono later dedicated it to the Statue of Liberty. "It's rock and roll but it's speed's along."
**Trip Through Your Wires** : This is track 9 on The Joshua Tree album, featuring Bono on harmonica. It was released as a single only in Australia, limited to 500 hand-numbered vinyl copies with "wires" on one side and three songs on side two; a copy of this rare single now sells for around $50. The song, in a 6/8 time signature, has not been played live since The Joshua Tree tour, and The Edge feels it doesn't make sense within the album's context without "The Sweetest Thing." "6'8", grooves are hard to deliver live."
**One Tree Hill** : Track 10 on the album, this emotional song is dedicated to Greg Carroll, a Maori man who was Bono's personal assistant and a beloved member of the band's crew. It commemorates Carroll's standout presence in Auckland and his untimely death. The vocal take used on the album was the only one Bono recorded, due to his intense grief. The song also critiques human rights atrocities in Chile under Augusto Pinochet and America's involvement, forming a trilogy with "Bullet the Blue Sky" and "Mothers of the Disappeared." It is rarely played in concert but is performed during special occasions and whenever the band plays in New Zealand. "It's a lovely, tidal, a lovely image, isn't it?"
**Exit** : Track 10 on The Joshua Tree, this song was created on the very last day of the album's sessions from a long jam that was edited down, captured in one take. It lyrically draws from Norman Mailer's "The Executioner Song" (about Gary Gilmore) and Truman Capote's "In Cold Blood," leading Bono to describe the character as a "serial killer, sort of." The song was performed on all 109 of the original Joshua Tree tour shows, but was played only one other time until the 30th anniversary tour. "There's something that happens when YouTube bashes it out in the band room and sometimes things get out of control, sonically, in a good way."
**Mothers of the Disappeared** : The eleventh and final song on The Joshua Tree, this track is part of a trilogy with "Bullet the Blue Sky" and "One Tree Hill," criticizing the Pinochet dictatorship in Chile and America's involvement, as well as political instability in Nicaragua and El Salvador. It also references the Madre's Deplaced de Mayo in Argentina, a group of women whose children were forcibly disappeared by military governments between 1976 and 1983. Part of its melody was conceived by Bono on a Spanish guitar during a humanitarian mission in Ethiopia, where he wrote a ditty to teach children hygiene. Powerful live performances occurred on February 5th and 11th, 1998, in Argentina (with some original mothers on stage) and Chile (in a former Pinochet prison camp), where Bono demanded information about the disappeared children, contributing to protests against Pinochet. In Guatemala, it was notably released as the B-side to "With or Without You," a political statement. "This was nothing short of a human rights atrocity."
**The Sweetest Thing (1987)** : This song, originally left off The Joshua Tree, was written by Bono for his wife, Allie, after he forgot her birthday during the album's recording. Allie claimed the publishing rights as a gift and donated them to a Ukrainian Chernobyl charity. Initially a B-side for "Where the Streets Have No Name" in 1987, it gained significant radio airplay. Re-recorded and re-released in 1998, it became a number one hit in Canada and Ireland, reached number six in the UK, but only number 63 in the US. In Europe, the 1998 single was promoted with gold-foiled "sweetest thing chocolate bars," now coveted collector's items. "The original recording of the Sweetest Thing rejected for inclusion on the Joshua Tree, but a big hit as a stand-alone single when it was re-recorded more than a decade later."
**Luminous Times (Hold on to Love)** : Co-written with producer Brian Eno (with some sources listing Eno as sole songwriter, which is unusual for U2), this song was released as a B-side with "With or Without You" because it was deemed "not enough to make the album." "Luminous times hold on to love is a co-write with Brian Eno."
**With or Without You** : This song was mentioned as coming together "very quickly" and was already "quite formed" before its recording. It served as the A-side for "Luminous Times (Hold on to Love)" and had "Mothers of the Disappeared" as a B-side in Guatemala. "With or without you came very quickly. You know, it was already quite formed."
**Silver and Gold** : This track, considered a live favorite, was recorded for The Joshua Tree but ultimately cut because it "just wasn't done." An earlier version was co-written by Bono with Keith Richards and Ron Wood for the "Sun City" anti-apartheid collection, making a last-minute inclusion there. It was later rewritten and re-recorded during The Joshua Tree sessions, then became the second half of the B-side for the "Where the Streets Have No Name" single in 1987, alongside "The Sweetest Thing." "This song predated the Joshua Tree by about two years."
**Where the Streets Have No Name** : This song was released as a single, three months before "In God's Country," and was the A-side to "The Sweetest Thing" and "Silver and Gold" on a 1987 single.

## curiosities
**Greg Carroll's Relationship with U2** : Greg Carroll, a Maori man, met U2 in Auckland, New Zealand, during The Unforgettable Fire tour in 1984. Bono, suffering from jet lag, befriended Carroll during a night out with locals. Carroll, a soundman for a comedy troupe, impressed Bono with his humor and work ethic, leading to his hiring on the spot. He completed the world tour, acquired a passport, and became Bono's personal stage assistant by 1985, appearing next to Bono at Live Aid. By 1986, he was Bono's full-time personal assistant, living in Dublin, and a close friend of Bono's wife, Allie. Carroll also researched locations for The Joshua Tree album artwork and recording studios. "He was one of the crew. And the thing about you two is they have really devoted people around them."
**Greg Carroll's Death and "One Tree Hill" Origin** : On July 3, 1986, Greg Carroll died at age 26 in Dublin after a motorcycle accident while returning Bono's bike on a rainy night. Bono, upon hearing the news in Texas, immediately flew back to Ireland, and the U2 organization arranged for Carroll's body to be flown back to New Zealand. At his traditional Maori funeral, Bono sang The Beatles' "Let It Be" and Bob Dylan's "Knockin' On Heaven's Door," before writing "One Tree Hill" as a dedication. The album's vocal take for "One Tree Hill" was Bono's only one due to his overwhelming grief. "He was killed instantly. And he was just 26."
**"Exit" Lyrics Allegedly Influence Killer** : On July 19, 1989, Robert John Bardo, the killer of actress Rebecca Schaefer, reportedly claimed that U2's "Exit" lyrics influenced his actions. The song was played during Bardo's trial, where he allegedly "bopped along" to it, and his lawyer used it as part of the defense, though Bardo was still convicted of first-degree murder. "Bartow allegedly claimed that the lyrics to exit influence his thinking."
**Bono Forgets Wife's Birthday, Inspires "The Sweetest Thing"** : Bono wrote "The Sweetest Thing" for his wife, Allie, as an apology for forgetting her birthday while engrossed in the recording of The Joshua Tree. Allie requested the publishing rights as a personal gift and subsequently donated them to a Ukrainian Chernobyl charity. "Bono writes the song for his wife because he forgot her birthday because he was so tied up in the recording of the Joshua Tree."
**Producer's Struggle to Finish Albums** : According to producer Steve Lillywhite, U2 is "great at making records" but "terrible at finishing them," largely because they compose in the studio, making it challenging to determine when a song is truly complete. "He says that you two is great at making records. They're terrible at finishing them."
**Daniel N. Waw Interview at Hard Rock Cafe** : On March 12, 2017, co-producer Daniel N. Waw discussed the making of The Joshua Tree in front of a live audience of hardcore U2 fans at the Hard Rock Cafe in Toronto, examining the record track by track, beginning with side two. "Daniel N. Waw to talk about the making of the record and we did it kind of differently. It was in front of a live audience of hardcore YouTube fans at the Hard Rock Cafe in Toronto and we're examining the record track by track."
**Speed Chess Mixing Technique** : During the production of The Joshua Tree, a "tag team mixing" technique was employed where two producers would each work on a mix for 15 minutes, with the rule that "nothing sacred" from the previous person's work is off-limits for alteration. This rapid-fire process results in four different mixes within an hour and is likened to "speed chess." "You get 15 minutes to mix. You finish a mix. You leave the room. The other guy comes in, and the only rule is nothing sacred."
**"Sweetest Thing" Chocolate Bars** : In Europe, the re-released 1998 single of "The Sweetest Thing" was promoted with actual gold-foiled chocolate bars. These chocolate bars are now highly sought-after by collectors of U2 memorabilia. "In Europe, the 1998 single was promoted with sweetest thing chocolate bars."

# curiosity

**6/8 Time Signature Challenges in Music** : Songs written in a 6/8 time signature are noted for being difficult to perform effectively live, are seldom played on radio, and are considered "tough to dance to," often described as a "double waltz." "6'8", grooves are hard to deliver live."
**Album Abandonment vs. Completion** : Many artists face situations where albums are not fully completed but rather "forced to abandon" them due to budget constraints or strict deadlines. Often, the songs that received the most creative effort and "labor poured into them" are ironically the ones that do not make the final cut. "oftentimes, the songs that have had the most labor poured into them are the ones that don't get on."
**Tag Team Mixing Technique** : A collaborative mixing method was described where two individuals take 15-minute turns at the mixing board, with the key rule that "nothing sacred" from the previous person's work is off-limits for alteration. This rapid-fire process results in four different mixes within an hour and is likened to "speed chess." "You get 15 minutes to mix. You finish a mix. You leave the room. The other guy comes in, and the only rule is nothing sacred."
**South American Human Rights Atrocities** : The transcript highlights severe human rights abuses in South America, specifically referencing the forced disappearances of children by military governments in Argentina and Chile between 1976 and 1983. This was exemplified by groups like the Madre's Deplaced de Mayo. U2's songs, particularly "Mothers of the Disappeared," "Bullet the Blue Sky," and "One Tree Hill," were written to address these atrocities, including the Pinochet dictatorship in Chile, political instability in Nicaragua and El Salvador, and American involvement in these regimes. "This was a group of women whose children had been forcibly disappeared by the military governments in both that country and Chile between 1976 and 1983. This was nothing short of a human rights atrocity."
**Influence of The Executioner's Song and In Cold Blood on Lyrics** : The lyrics for the U2 song "Exit" were directly inspired by Norman Mailer's book "The Executioner Song," which recounts the real-life story of Gary Gilmore's execution by firing squad, and Truman Capote's novel "In Cold Blood." "Lyrically, the song draws from a book by Norman Mailer called the Executioner Song, which tells the real-life story of Gary Gilmore, who was executed by firing squad, and also by the Truman Capote novel in Cold Blood."
**Cultural Impact of Pop Albums** : Some albums achieve a level of success that extends beyond critical acclaim and commercial sales, becoming cultural phenomena that have a lasting impact for years, even decades, shaping public consciousness and perception. "An album is a critical success. It turns into a commercial smash and every once in a while, every once in a long, long while, it turns into a cultural phenomenon with an impact that last years, maybe decades."
