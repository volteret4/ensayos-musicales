## albums
**The Joshua Tree (1987) – Critical and Commercial Smash** : Released March 9, 1987. It surpassed expectations, becoming a critical success, commercial smash, and a cultural phenomenon. The album has sold beyond 25 million copies and is considered one of the most significant rock releases of all time. Daniel Landwell co-produced the album. Over 30 songs were written for it, but only 11 made the final cut.
**The Joshua Tree (2007) – 20th Anniversary Edition** : Released to commemorate the album's 20th anniversary.
**The Joshua Tree (2017) – 30th Anniversary Edition** : Released for the album's 30th anniversary. Daniel Landwell was involved in discussions regarding this edition.

## songs
**In God's Country (1987)** : Track 8 on The Joshua Tree. Bono reportedly wanted to "rock this record up a little bit," resulting in a vibrant and propelling groove. It features what is considered one of The Edge's best performances with echo rhythms and a great groove from Larry Mullen Jr. Released as the album's fourth single in North America, approximately three months after "Where the Streets Have No Name." While its meaning is open to interpretation (Ireland or America), Bono later dedicated the song to the Statue of Liberty.
**Trip Through Your Wires (1987)** : Track 9 on The Joshua Tree. This song features Bono playing harmonica. It was released as a single exclusively in Australia, limited to 500 hand-numbered vinyl copies with "Wires" on side one and three other songs on side two. A copy of this limited Australian single now sells for around $50. The song has not been played live since The Joshua Tree tour. It is characterized by a 6/8 groove, which is noted as challenging to deliver live, seldom broadcast on radio, and not communicating well in live performance settings. The Edge suggests it makes more sense when heard in the context of another song, "The Sweetest Thing," which was ultimately left off the album.
**One Tree Hill (1987)** : Track 10 on The Joshua Tree. This emotional song is dedicated to Greg Carroll, Bono's personal assistant, a Māori man who died in a motorcycle accident in Dublin on July 3, 1986, at the age of 26. Bono's vocal take on the album was his only one due to the overwhelming grief he experienced. String parts for the song were recorded by Nick Paul and Adele Arman at Grant Avenue Studios in Hamilton, Canada, directed remotely by Daniel Landwell and The Edge during a six-hour transatlantic phone call. Lyrically, the song also addresses human rights atrocities in Chile under the dictator Augusto Pinochet. It forms a thematic trilogy with "Bullet the Blue Sky" and "Mothers of the Disappeared," serving as a critique of United States involvement in the Chilean regime. The song is not frequently played in concert but is always performed when U2 is in New Zealand and on other special occasions.
**Exit (1987)** : Track 11 on The Joshua Tree. This song came together on the very last day of The Joshua Tree recording sessions, originating as a long jam that was edited down to its album version in a single take. Daniel Landwell described the recording process as a moment when the band's playing got "out of control, sonically, in a good way," taking on a life of its own. Lyrically, the song draws inspiration from Norman Mailer's book "The Executioner's Song," which tells the real-life story of Gary Gilmore, and Truman Capote's novel "In Cold Blood." The song became a subject of controversy on July 19, 1989, when Robert John Bardo, the killer of actress Rebecca Schaeffer, allegedly claimed its lyrics influenced his actions. It was played at his trial, where Bardo reportedly bopped along, and his lawyer unsuccessfully used the song as part of the defense. "Exit" was performed on all 109 shows of the original Joshua Tree tour, but was reportedly played only one other time until the 30th anniversary tour.
**Mothers of the Disappeared (1987)** : Track 12, the final song on The Joshua Tree. It is intended to be understood in the context of "Bullet the Blue Sky" and "One Tree Hill," serving as a powerful criticism of the Pinochet dictatorship in Chile and America's involvement with it. The song also reflects Bono's concerns about political instability in Nicaragua and El Salvador. It draws inspiration from the Madres de Plaza de Mayo, a group of women in Argentina whose children were forcibly disappeared by military governments between 1976 and 1983, an event described as a human rights atrocity. Daniel Landwell suggested the song likely emerged from the band's experiences during the "Conspiracy of Hope" tour. The melody was partly lifted from a Spanish guitar ditty Bono wrote on a humanitarian mission in Ethiopia, designed to teach young children about personal hygiene. The song was featured in powerful live performances on February 5, 1998, in Argentina, with original Mothers on stage, and on February 11, 1998, in a Chilean stadium (previously a Pinochet prison camp), where the Mothers joined the band holding pictures of their still-missing children. The Chilean gig was broadcast on national television, with Bono directly demanding the government reveal the location of the children's remains. This performance had a real-world effect, contributing to mass protests against Pinochet, who was subsequently hindered from being named Senator for Life. In Guatemala, the song appeared as a B-side to "With or Without You," a move interpreted as a political statement.
**The Sweetest Thing (1987)** : Originally released as a B-side to the 1987 single "Where the Streets Have No Name," this song was intentionally left off The Joshua Tree album because it was not considered complete at the time. Bono wrote it as an apology to his wife, Ali, for forgetting her birthday due to his intense focus on recording The Joshua Tree. Despite its B-side status, the song received unusual radio airplay and became popular with fans. It was re-recorded and re-released in 1998, achieving massive success as a number one hit in Canada and Ireland. It reached number 6 in the UK but only number 63 in the US. For its 1998 European promotion, actual "Sweetest Thing" chocolate bars, wrapped in gold foil, were produced and are now highly coveted by U2 memorabilia collectors. Ali, Bono's wife, received the publishing rights to the song and donated them to a Ukrainian Chernobyl charity.
**Luminous Times (Hold On to Love) (1987)** : This song was co-written with Brian Eno, though some sources list Eno as the sole songwriter, which would be highly unusual for a U2 song. It was packaged as a B-side for the "With or Without You" single but was not considered strong enough for inclusion on The Joshua Tree album.
**Silver and Gold (1987)** : A live favorite that was not included on The Joshua Tree, primarily because it was not finished in time for the album's release. An earlier version of the song was written by Bono with Keith Richards and Ron Wood of The Rolling Stones, making a last-minute inclusion on the "Sun City Artists Against Apartheid Collection" approximately two years before The Joshua Tree. It was subsequently rewritten and re-recorded during The Joshua Tree sessions. After being cut from the album, it became the second B-side for the "Where the Streets Have No Name" single in 1987, alongside "The Sweetest Thing."
**Where the Streets Have No Name (1987)** : This song was released as a single in 1987. Its B-sides included "In God's Country" (in North America), "The Sweetest Thing," and "Silver and Gold."
**With or Without You (1987)** : This song was tag-team mixed by Daniel Landwell and another producer, where they took 15-minute turns, adhering to a "nothing sacred" rule. Its B-sides included "Luminous Times (Hold On to Love)" and, in Guatemala, "Mothers of the Disappeared" as a political statement.

## curiosities
**Greg Carroll's Relationship with U2** : Greg Carroll, a Māori man, first met U2 in Auckland, New Zealand, during their 1984 Unforgettable Fire tour. Bono, experiencing jet lag, encountered Carroll during a night out. Greg, then a soundman for a comedy troupe, bonded with Bono over his humor and impressive work ethic. Carroll took Bono to One Tree Hill in Auckland, a site described as a spiritually significant volcanic peak. He was subsequently hired on the spot, completed the remainder of that world tour, and later served as Bono's personal stage assistant in 1985 (appearing next to Bono at Live Aid) and then as his full-time personal assistant in 1986, also becoming a close friend of Bono's wife, Ali. Carroll was tasked with researching locations for the album artwork and potential recording studios for The Joshua Tree.
**Greg Carroll's Death and Funeral** : On July 3, 1986, at just 26 years old, Greg Carroll was killed instantly in a motorcycle accident in Dublin while returning Bono's motorcycle to his home. Bono, who had landed in Texas for a recording session with Willie Nelson only an hour earlier, immediately flew back to Ireland upon hearing the news. The entire U2 organization facilitated the return of Carroll's body to New Zealand for a traditional Māori funeral. At the service, Bono sang The Beatles' "Let It Be" and Bob Dylan's "Knockin' On Heaven's Door" before composing "One Tree Hill" in Carroll's memory.
**"Exit" and Robert John Bardo Controversy** : On July 19, 1989, actress Rebecca Schaeffer was murdered by Robert John Bardo, who allegedly claimed that the lyrics to U2's song "Exit" had influenced his actions. During Bardo's trial, the song was played in the courtroom, and he reportedly bopped along to it. His lawyer attempted to use the song as part of the defense, but Bardo was ultimately convicted of first-degree murder.
**"Mothers of the Disappeared" Impact on Pinochet** : A powerful live performance of "Mothers of the Disappeared" by U2 on February 11, 1998, in a Chilean stadium that had previously been used as a prison camp by the Pinochet regime, had significant real-world consequences. With the Madres de Plaza de Mayo joining the band on stage holding pictures of their missing children, and the performance broadcast nationally, Bono publicly demanded that the government disclose the whereabouts of the children's remains. This event contributed to mass protests against Augusto Pinochet, ultimately hindering his political advancement to Senator for Life.
**"The Sweetest Thing" Chocolate Bars** : As part of the European promotion for the re-recorded 1998 single of "The Sweetest Thing," actual chocolate bars wrapped in gold foil and bearing the song's title were produced. These limited-edition items have since become highly coveted by U2 memorabilia collectors.
**Bono's Humanitarian Melody** : The melody for the song "Mothers of the Disappeared" was partially derived from a small ditty Bono had composed on a Spanish guitar during a humanitarian mission in Ethiopia. This tune was originally intended to help teach young children about personal hygiene.

## curiosities
**Co-producer of The Joshua Tree** : Daniel Landwell served as a co-producer for U2's iconic album, The Joshua Tree.
**"The Joshua Tree" 30th Anniversary Interview (2017)** : On March 12, 2017, Daniel Landwell participated in a live interview at the Hard Rock Cafe in Toronto, discussing the making of U2's The Joshua Tree album in a track-by-track format for its 30th anniversary.
**Tag-Team Mixing Technique** : Daniel Landwell engaged in a unique "tag-team mixing" approach, notably for U2's "With or Without You." This technique involved taking 15-minute turns mixing a track with another producer (implied, not named in this context), with the rule that "nothing is sacred," leading to multiple mixes in a short period (e.g., four in an hour). He likened this process to "speed chess."
**Remote String Direction for "One Tree Hill"** : Daniel Landwell, alongside The Edge, remotely directed Nick Paul and Adele Arman during a six-hour transatlantic phone call for the recording of the string parts for U2's "One Tree Hill" at Grant Avenue Studios in Hamilton.
**Involvement in Anniversary Editions** : Daniel Landwell has been involved in recent discussions concerning the 20th and 30th-anniversary editions of The Joshua Tree, hinting at a potential "surprise on the horizon."

# curiosity

**Album Success Predictability** : The success of an album is unpredictable; despite significant investment, there is no guarantee a release will become a commercial smash or cultural phenomenon. Success hinges on public reaction, which cannot be foreseen, though artists continue to strive for remarkable outcomes.
**Producer Tag-Team Mixing Technique** : A collaborative mixing method where two producers take turns in short intervals (e.g., 15 minutes) to work on a track, with the critical rule that "nothing is sacred." This allows for rapid iteration and multiple perspectives on the mix, described as being akin to "speed chess," and was employed for U2's "With or Without You."
**U2's Record Finishing Habits (Steve Lillywhite Quote)** : Producer Steve Lillywhite observed that U2 excels at making records but struggles with finishing them. This suggests that the band often composes in the studio, making it difficult for them to determine when a song is truly complete, sometimes leading to songs being "abandoned" due to budgets or deadlines rather than fully finished.
**Amnesty International's "Conspiracy of Hope" Tour (1986)** : This tour, in which U2 participated during the recording of The Joshua Tree, exposed Bono to significant human rights issues, particularly in South America, which profoundly influenced his songwriting, notably for "Mothers of the Disappeared."
**"Sun City Artists Against Apartheid Collection"** : This compilation album, created to protest apartheid in South Africa, featured "Silver and Gold," an early version of which was a last-minute inclusion and co-written by Bono, Keith Richards, and Ron Wood.
