## albums
**Station to Station (1976)** : Bowie admitted to having no memory of making this album due to severe cocaine use from late 1975 through to late 1976. Despite yielding only one major hit ("Golden Years"), it is now considered one of his most important albums, having been positively re-evaluated over the years, and it hinted at his musical direction for the latter half of the 70s. "a record that Bowie had no memory of making because by his own admission, he was so coked out during that entire period from late 1975 through to sometime in late 1976 that he doesn't remember a thing."
**Low (1977)** : The first album of Bowie's "Berlin trilogy," recorded in Berlin. This experimental and electronic work was influenced by "The Man Who Fell to Earth," "Station to Station," and the burgeoning Berlin scene, resulting in a colder sound even than "Station to Station." Produced while Bowie was struggling to overcome cocaine addiction, Side 1 featured short song fragments, while Side 2 was entirely instrumental, incorporating significant contributions from Brian Eno and pioneering art rock. "The first was Low, an experimental and often very electronic album that was part man who fell to earth, part station to station, and part whatever was happening in Berlin at the time."
**Heroes (1977)** : Recorded at Hansa Studio, this album continued Bowie's exploration of Krautrock influences, with its title attributed to a song by the German band Neu! and featuring "V2 Schneider" as a tribute to Florian Schneider of Kraftwerk. Released ten months after "Low," it contained the earliest elements of what would become New Wave music and is historically recognized as one of Bowie's all-time best records. "Heroes was also recorded at Hansa. Bowie continued to follow his Crout Rock Muse."
**Lodger (1979)** : Although the third album in Bowie's "Berlin trilogy," it was recorded in Switzerland and New York, yet maintained the stylistic feel of his German period with Brian Eno contributing (Tony Visconti produced). Initially the least commercially successful of the three and puzzling to critics and fans upon its spring 1979 release, it has since been re-evaluated as an underrated, artsy, and experimental album, anticipating new wave and post-punk music. "Lager was the least commercially successful of these three albums."
**Scary Monsters and Super Creeps (1980)** : This album was intentionally commercial, partly due to Bowie's financial difficulties stemming from a detrimental management contract with Tony DeFrees. It served as a pivotal transition from the 70s to the 80s, centered around the song "Ashes to Ashes," and became highly influential on the emerging New Romantics scene and many future bands like Duran Duran and Depeche Mode. "Scary monsters and supercrepes was a great record, perhaps the perfect way to end the 70s and welcome the 1980s."
**Let's Dance (1983)** : Recorded in just 17 days with a new session band, this album was a massive commercial success, selling over 6 million copies and becoming Bowie's best-selling album ever. The album, a hybrid of rock, dance, and funk, was groundbreaking for 1983 and led to the "Serious Moonlight Tour" which drew 2.6 million people across 16 countries. "Let's Dance was a massive success for Bowie, and drew 2.6 million people to the serious moonlight tour as it wound its way through 16 countries."
**Tonight (1984)** : This album, featuring the song "Blue Jean," was a rushed follow-up to "Let's Dance," intended to capitalize on his new audience but failed to connect. Critics widely dismissed it as merely "Let's Dance Part 2," noting a stark absence of the innovation typically expected from Bowie. "The big knock against that record was that it wasn't anything more than Let's Dance Part 2."
**Never Let Me Down (1987)** : This album, released in 1987, was negatively received and further hampered by the "ill-conceived Glass Spiders tour." It contributed to a period in Bowie's career where critics perceived his work as "tired" and lacking genuine enthusiasm. "It didn't get better with Never Let Me Down Again in 1987, which wasn't helped by the ill-conceived Glass Spiders tour."
**Earthling (1997)** : This album garnered plenty of positive reviews and was supported by a successful "Love in the World tour." It featured the song "I'm Afraid of Americans," which included a Nine Inch Nails remix and a music video cameo by Trent Reznor. "Bowie and a 9-inch nails remix of I'm Afraid of Americans, originally from the 1997 album Earthling."

## songs
**Golden Years (1976)** : This song, the only major hit from the album "Station to Station," explored Bowie's feelings of isolation during the intense period of its creation. "the song Golden Years, which talked about how isolated he felt during that time."
**TVC-15 (1976)** : Featured on "Station to Station," this song was inspired by a drug-induced hallucination or dream Bowie had with Iggy Pop, wherein an unidentified girlfriend was consumed by a holographic television, model TVC-15. It also reflected Bowie's immersion in the doomed spaceman character from "The Man Who Fell to Earth" and hinted at his future musical direction in the late 70s. "It was inspired by a dream, more likely a drug-induced hallucination with his friend, Ike Pop, where an unidentified girlfriend was eaten by a holographic television, model number, TVC-15."
**Warzawa (1977)** : An instrumental track from Side 2 of the album "Low," "Warzawa" exemplified Bowie's experimental direction at the time. Initially perceived as radically avant-garde, it is now recognized as a visionary piece that helped spread the idea of synthesizer music. "This is an instrumental track called Warzawa."
**V2 Schneider (1977)** : A song from the album "Heroes," "V2 Schneider" was a direct nod to Florian Schneider, one of the co-founders of the influential German electronic band Kraftwerk. "There's also a song in the record called V2 Schneider, which is a nod to Florian Schneider, one of the founders of Kraftwerk."
**Heroes (1977)** : The title track of its album, "Heroes" had several alleged inspirations including a young couple kissing by the Berlin Wall (possibly producer Tony Visconti and Antonia Maass), Otto Mueller's 1916 painting "Lovers Between Garden Walls," a book called "A Grave for Adolfin," or time spent with a woman named Claire Shenstone. Bowie recorded the song in English, German, and French, reflecting his time in Berlin. "The title track was allegedly inspired by something Bowie saw from the studio window one day. The young couple, meaning by the Berlin Wall for a kiss."
**Ashes to Ashes (1980)** : This song was the centerpiece of "Scary Monsters and Super Creeps," picking up the narrative of Major Tom, a character Bowie first introduced ten years prior in "Space Oddity." Its accompanying music video was unprecedentedly expensive at 250,000 pounds, making it the most costly video ever made up to that point, and it effectively tapped into Britain's emerging New Romantics scene, featuring several figures from London's Blitz Club. "The centerpiece of Scary Monsters was ashes to ashes, a song that not only picked up the story of Major Tom from Space Audity, a character that Bowie had introduced 10 years earlier, but was also made into a hideously expensive music video."
**Blue Jean (1984)** : Featured on the album "Tonight," "Blue Jean" was part of Bowie's attempt to replicate the commercial success of "Let's Dance," but it ultimately failed to deliver the expected innovation. "Bowie with Blue Jean from the Tonight Album in 1984."
**I'm Afraid of Americans (1997)** : From the album "Earthling," this song featured a Nine Inch Nails remix and an accompanying music video that starred Trent Reznor in a "creepy stalker" role, visually compared to the film "Taxi Driver." Bowie also toured with Trent Reznor during this period. "Bowie and a 9-inch nails remix of I'm Afraid of Americans, originally from the 1997 album Earthling."

## curiosities
**Influential Rock Artist** : David Bowie is widely considered one of the most influential artists in the history of rock, with many musical paths tracing back to his work. His importance has grown even more significantly since his passing. "The more you study the history of rock, the more you realize just how many roads lead back to Bowie in some way or another."
**Golden Period - 1970-1983** : Bowie experienced an incredibly prolific and impactful "golden period" from 1970 to 1983, during which he released 13 albums, many of which became not only classics but milestones in rock history. "He had a golden period that stretched roughly from 1970 through to 1983, 13 albums in those 13 years, and many of them remained not just classics, but milestones in the history of rock."
**Early 70s Album Output** : From the release of "Hunky Dory," Bowie was exceptionally prolific, releasing 11 studio albums and two live albums in just over eight and a half years, in addition to collaborations with other artists like Iggy Pop. "he had released 11 studio albums in just over 8 and a half years, plus two live albums."
**British Punk Favorite - 1975** : By 1975, Bowie had become a favorite among British punks, who admired not only his music but also his non-status quo fashion sense and his defiant attitude. His influence was so strong that it was difficult to find a punk enthusiast in London who wasn't also a Bowie fan. "By 1975, he'd become a favorite among British punks."
**Glam Stage Evolution** : By the time of "Station to Station," Bowie had moved beyond his iconic glam rock stage, which was exemplified by his "Ziggy Stardust" and "Aladdin Sane" personas. "Bowie had moved beyond his glam stage, exemplified by his Ziggy Stardust and Aladdin Sane years."
**Image Changes and Public Imagination** : Throughout his career, Bowie made a series of daring image changes that consistently captivated the public's imagination, marking distinct phases of his artistic journey. "He'd made a series of daring image changes, all of which captured the public's imagination."
**The Thin White Duke Persona - 1976** : The "Station to Station" era was defined by Bowie's "Thin White Duke" persona, a character described as hollow, amoral, emotionless, and damaged, yet elegantly dressed in a white shirt and waistcoat. "Station to Station was the era of his thin white duke face."
**Theatrical Concert Tours** : Bowie's concert tours were renowned for being among the most theatrical and ambitious ever to be taken on the road, showcasing his elaborate stage presentations. "His concert tours were among the most theatrical and ambitious things ever taken on the road."
**Severe Addiction and Health Crisis - 1975-1976** : During the period from late 1975 to late 1976, Bowie was heavily drinking and consuming tremendous amounts of cocaine, while subsisting on little more than milk and peppers. His weight plummeted to less than 90 pounds, indicating a severe health crisis, and he was described as "cracking up." "Bowie was drinking very heavily and using tremendous amounts of cocaine while eating a little more than milk and peppers."
**Paranoid Delusions - 1970s** : During a particularly difficult period, Bowie exhibited severe paranoia, storing urine in his fridge due to fears that witches were attempting to steal his "vital essence" and touring with a telescope, convinced that aliens in an orbiting mothership were coming for him. "He did crazy things like store urine in the fridge because he was afraid unspecified witches were out to steal his vital essence."
**Move to West Berlin with Iggy Pop** : Deeply influenced by German electronic groups like Kraftwerk, Can, and Neu!, and envisioning electronic music as the future, Bowie moved to West Berlin with Iggy Pop to immerse themselves in the local scene and simultaneously recover from their addictions. They settled in a modest place at Hopstresser 155 in the Schöneberg area, motivated by a belief that remaining in Los Angeles would lead to their demise. "He began to think that these electronic methods of producing music were the future. And what better way to follow this muse than to move to where it all seemed to be happening? Germany."
**Hansa Studio 2 - Berlin** : Bowie and Iggy Pop made music seriously at Hansa Studio 2 in Berlin, located approximately 100 meters from the Berlin Wall, a place where Bowie produced Iggy's album "The Idiot" and recorded two of his own albums. "Berlin also had a very good recording studio called Hansa, all of 100-ish meters from where the Berlin wall ran through the city."
**Financial Troubles with Tony DeFrees** : Since the early 1970s, Bowie was entangled in a detrimental management agreement with Tony DeFrees, which left him financially "skint" despite platinum albums and chart-topping singles. Although he parted ways with DeFrees in 1975, the contract allowed DeFrees to collect royalties on Bowie's music until 1996, leading to a protracted legal battle that was a major financial gamble. "Seeing the early 70s, he entered a terrible arrangement with manager Tony DeFrees, and even with all the platinum albums and chart-topping singles, he was close to Skint, he had nothing."
**Longest Album Gap** : Following the release of "Scary Monsters and Super Creeps," Bowie experienced the longest gap between albums in his career, lasting over two and a half years, during which he focused on other artistic endeavors. "The gap following the release of Bowie's scary monsters was the longest gap between albums in his career, a little more than two and a half years."
**Acting Career - 1980s** : Bowie embarked on a significant acting career throughout the 1980s, taking on roles that were unusual for rock stars. His notable projects included John Merrick in the Broadway production of "The Elephant Man," films such as "Christine F" (1983), "Ball" (1983), "Merry Christmas Mr. Lawrence" (1983), "Yellowbeard" (1983), and a vampire role in "The Hunger" (1983) which resonated with Goth fans. He also starred in "Into the Night" (1985), "Labyrinth" (1985, for which he did the soundtrack), "Absolute Beginners" (1985), and "The Last Temptation of Christ" (1988), demonstrating a versatility unmatched by other artists like Elvis. "He had been in two movies at that time and was just embarking on his role as John Merrick in the stage production of The Elephant Man, which took him to Broadway."
**$17.5 Million EMI Record Deal - 1983** : In 1983, Bowie secured a monumental $17.5 million record deal with EMI Records, one of the largest in the industry at that time. In exchange, he committed to creating a more commercially accessible album. "And then there was the new 17.5 million dollar record deal with EMI Records, one of the biggest deals in the industry to that time."
**Post-Let's Dance Audience Alienation** : Following the immense commercial success of "Let's Dance," Bowie felt a profound sense of alienation from his new audience, questioning their connection to his artistic roots. This psychological impact marked the beginning of a creative "spiral" that continued throughout the rest of the 1980s, as his subsequent work was often judged against his previous innovations. "I remember looking out over these waves of people who were coming to hear this record played live and thinking, I wonder how many velvet underground albums these people have in their record collections. I suddenly felt very apart from my audience, and it was depressing because I didn't know what they wanted."
**Critical Decline in the 80s** : During the mid-to-late 1980s, Bowie's music and performances, including albums like "Tonight" and "Never Let Me Down," and the "Glass Spiders tour," faced harsh criticism, with descriptions like "tired" and "fake enthusiasm" being used to characterize his work, suggesting a departure from his earlier innovative spirit. "Words like tired and phrases like fake enthusiasm were thrown around."
**90s Reinvention - Sound and Vision Tour** : Bowie initiated his 90s reinvention with the "Sound and Vision Tour," a two-stage process beginning with a promise to perform his classic hits—such as "Space Oddity," "Ziggy Stardust," "Rebel Rebel," "Young Americans," "Heroes," and "Let's Dance"—one last time before permanently retiring them. His motivation was to clear away past expectations and start fresh. "The first was something called the Sound and Vision Tour, where Bowie promised to play his old hits one more time and then retire them forever."
**Financial Freedom - Post-1996** : After his problematic contract with former manager Tony DeFrees finally expired in 1996, all royalties from Bowie's music began flowing directly to him, granting him the financial freedom to make music on his own terms once again. "Remember that bad deal with his old manager, Tony DeFrees? It expired in 1996, and all royalties from Bowie's music started flowing to Bowie."
**Influencer of 90s Alt-Rock** : Bowie was a significant acknowledged influence on the emerging Alt Rock stars of the 1990s, including bands like the Pixies, Nirvana, The Smashing Pumpkins, Suede, Oasis, and Blur, which helped introduce his earlier work to a new generation of fans. "The new Alt Rock stars of the 90s constantly acknowledged Bowie as a major influence."
**Hero to Outsiders** : Bowie served as a hero for outsiders, weirdos, punks, alternative kids, gay people, and anyone who felt they didn't fit into societal norms, demonstrating that individuality could be a strength. "Bowie was a hero of outsiders and weirdos, punks, alternative kids, gay people, and basically anyone who didn't fit into normal society."
**Master of Cultural Synthesis** : For about 15 years, starting in 1974, Bowie's career showcased a masterclass in cultural synthesis. He uniquely absorbed diverse influences from various forms of music, literature, movies, and painting, integrating them seamlessly into his musical output. "For about 15 years, from 1974, Bowie's career was a masterclass in cultural synthesis, more than anyone else."
**Artistic Dedication over Commercial Success** : Bowie was a role model who exemplified loyalty and dedication to one's art as being more important than commercial success, encouraging others to take artistic risks. "He showed that if you were loyal and dedicated to your art, that was more important than commercial success."
**Life as a Work of Art** : It has been remarked that Bowie approached his entire existence as a continuous work of art in itself. "Bowie lived life as a work of art in itself."

# genre - Goth

## curiosities
**Bowie's Role in "The Hunger" and Goth Appeal** : David Bowie's portrayal of a vampire in the 1983 film "The Hunger" endeared him to Goth fans, further solidifying his connection and influence within that subculture. "He played a vampire in the hunger, something that endeared him to Goth fans."

# concert - Serious Moonlight Tour

## curiosities
**Massive Global Success - 1983** : The "Serious Moonlight Tour," accompanying David Bowie's "Let's Dance" album, was a massive global success in 1983. It drew 2.6 million people as it traveled through 16 countries. "Let's Dance was a massive success for Bowie, and drew 2.6 million people to the serious moonlight tour as it wound its way through 16 countries."

# concert - Sound and Vision Tour

## curiosities
**Bowie's Hits Retirement Strategy - 90s** : The "Sound and Vision Tour" was the first stage of David Bowie's 90s reinvention, during which he promised to play his most famous old hits, including "Space Oddity," "Ziggy Stardust," "Rebel Rebel," "Young Americans," "Heroes," and "Let's Dance," one last time before retiring them permanently. His goal was to shed past expectations and embark on a fresh artistic start. "The first was something called the Sound and Vision Tour, where Bowie promised to play his old hits one more time and then retire them forever."

## curiosities
**Support for Earthling Album - 1997** : The "Love in the World Tour" accompanied David Bowie's 1997 album "Earthling," which received plenty of positive reviews. "Plenty of positive reviews and lots of love in the world tour that went along with it."

# curiosity

**Music Videos in Their Infancy** : In the era before MTV, music videos were in their early stages of development, primarily serving as promotional tools to be sent to TV shows like "Top of the Pops" so that artists could avoid personal appearances. They also allowed artists to carefully control their public image, offering more command than live television. "Music videos were still in their infancy. Their main purpose was to send them to TV shows like Top of the Pops so that the artist didn't have to go in person."
**Most Expensive Music Video (to its time) - Ashes to Ashes** : David Bowie's music video for "Ashes to Ashes" (1980) cost 250,000 pounds, which was well over half a million dollars in contemporary money, making it the most expensive music video ever made up to that point. This was a significant financial gamble for Bowie, who was still dealing with an ugly legal battle over royalties. "It cost 250,000 pounds, so well over half a million dollars in today's money. ... The most expensive video ever made to that point."
**The Berlin Wall's Proximity to Hansa Studio** : Hansa Studio 2 in Berlin, where David Bowie and Iggy Pop recorded music, was located a mere 100-ish meters from where the Berlin Wall ran through the city, creating a unique historical context for their creative work. "Berlin also had a very good recording studio called Hansa, all of 100-ish meters from where the Berlin wall ran through the city."
**Alleged Inspiration for Bowie's "Heroes" - Berlin Wall** : One of the alleged inspirations for David Bowie's iconic song "Heroes" was the sight of a young couple kissing by the Berlin Wall from the studio window, a poignant image against a backdrop of division. "The young couple, meaning by the Berlin Wall for a kiss."
