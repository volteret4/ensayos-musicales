## albums
**License to Ill (Year unspecified)** : This album was initially perceived as a parody of hip hop, though a good one. It contributed to the band's early image as "spoiled, snotty, frat boys, writing goofy songs and making funny videos." "Rolling Stone described the album as three idiots make a masterpiece."
**Paul's Boutique (Year unspecified)** : This second album suffered an "underwhelming commercial reception" and was considered a "sales disaster" upon its release, confusing fans who expected more of the sound from their debut. Despite its initial commercial failure, it is now regarded as a "masterpiece of production and engineering." "Adam Yach remember as being told by the president of their label that they couldn't afford to sink any more money into promoting the album because, wow, they had a new, donny, osman record coming out."
**Check Your Head (1992)** : Released on April 7, 1992, this album marked a significant shift for the band, representing their "comeback album" and solidifying their musical credibility after the commercial disappointment of their previous record. The album sold "big time, reaching into the top 10 of the US album charts." "The thinking was, they'd taken the sampling thing about as far as they could, so it was time for a different approach."
**Ill Communication (1994)** : This fourth album saw the band continue to evolve, with its lead single "Sabotage" being released four months prior to the album itself. The Beastie Boys toured extensively in support of the album, including a stint on Lollapalooza in 1994 and their successful Quadrophonic Joystick Action Tour, which famously sold out Madison Square Garden in 30 minutes. "And if you remember the 1994 MTV Music Video Awards, the sabotage clip lost out to R.E.M.'s Everybody Hearts."
**Aglio Iolio (Year unspecified)** : This was a "quicky hardcore EP" released after *Ill Communication*, featuring "8 songs in just 11 minutes."
**The In Sound from Way Out (Year unspecified)** : Released after *Ill Communication*, this CD was "full of funky jazz," showcasing the band's diverse musical interests.
**Hello Nasty (1998)** : Released on July 14, 1998, this album was another commercial success, reaching number one on the charts and selling 681,000 copies in its first week in the U.S. alone. "Another number one. This time selling 681,000 copies in its first week in the U.S. along."
**To the 5 Buroughs (2004)** : Released after a six-year gap, this album served as a "love letter, and tribute to New York" in the aftermath of 9/11 and other global events. The album's creation involved a unique recording process where the band would start with beats, add rough vocals, and then, if a track sounded good, proceed to record final vocals using "fancy and nicer mics." "This album was really like New York City last, like everything has happened in New York City since like 2002, 2001, or whatever, you know what I mean?"
**The Mix-Up (2007)** : Released on June 26, 2007, this "straight instrumental record" was an idea the band had considered since the early 1990s but dismissed as "non-commercial and frankly rather crazy" at the time. It was intended as a "statement about the status of hip-hop music." "It didn't sell very well, but it did win a Grammy for Best Instrumental Album."
**Hot Sauce Committee Part One (2009)** : This album was originally slated for release on September 15, 2009, as the first part of a two-part set. However, its release was indefinitely postponed due to Adam Yauch's cancer diagnosis, ultimately being shelved. "The first release date for the new album was set as September 15, 2009. It was going to be called Hot Sauce Committee Part One, the first of a two-part set."
**Hot Sauce Committee Part Two (2011)** : Released on April 27, 2011, more than a year and a half later than originally planned for "Part One," this became the "last ever Beastie Boys album." The album's release and promotion were significantly impacted by Adam Yauch's ongoing battle with cancer, preventing a tour and his appearance in any of the album's videos. "Make some noise from the last ever Beastie Boys album, Hot Sauce Committee Part Two."

## songs
**Body Movin' (1998)** : This song was one of the singles released from their 1998 album, *Hello Nasty*. "The Beastie boys and Body Movin, one of the singles from their 1998 album Hello Nasty."
**Pass the Mic (1992)** : Released on April 7, 1992, this was the first single from their comeback album, *Check Your Head*. The track was "built around a guitar sample from a bad brain song called Big Takeover" and featured a distinctive "big drum sound" achieved by using "a big 10-foot tunnel in front of the bass drum and then miking it at the end of that long barrel."
**Sabotage (1994)** : This iconic song was released as a single four full months before its parent album, *Ill Communication*. The accompanying video, directed by former pro skateboarder Spike Jonze, famously featured the band members in fake hair and beards, a decision made because Mike D was "folically challenged" and couldn't grow facial hair for the shoot. "At first, everybody thought that this was a big risk, something that would make the video look cheesy, but in the end, the cheesiness made it work even better."
**Intergalactic (1998)** : Featured on *Hello Nasty*, this song is notable for its intricate sampling. It incorporates an electronic album from 1968 by Les Baxter, a bass part from Houston Jazz Soul Fusion band The Crusaders, and a duet from The Stovle Sisters' 1970 song "Hang On In There." "Now, if you're wondering about the samples in that song, let's break some of them down."
**An Open Letter to NYC (2004)** : This track served as the first single from their 2004 album, *To the 5 Buroughs*, an album heavily influenced by their experiences in New York City after 9/11. "The first single from to the 5 Burrows."
**Triple Trouble (2004)** : Another single from *To the 5 Buroughs*, this song notably features samples from the Sugarhill Gang's 1979 track "Rapper's Delight." Its music video is also known for a cameo appearance by Kanye West at the very end. "Lots of sampling of the Sugarhill Gang, Rapper's Delight from 1979 and this one."
**Make Some Noise (2011)** : This song appeared on *Hot Sauce Committee Part Two*, which ultimately became the final Beastie Boys album. "Make some noise from the last ever Beastie Boys album, Hot Sauce Committee Part Two."

## curiosities
**Early Career Perception** : For the first eight years of their career, the Beastie Boys were not taken seriously by many, often perceived as "spoiled, snotty, frat boys, writing goofy songs and making funny videos." "No one took them seriously for the first eight years of their career."
**Artistic and Business Evolution** : Over their career, the Beastie Boys underwent a significant transformation, growing "as artists," "as businessmen," and "as humans," taking risks, experimenting, and branching out beyond their initial novelty act image. "But then something changed. The Beastie boys grew up."
**Move to Los Angeles and Self-Sufficiency** : After the commercial disappointment of *Paul's Boutique*, the Beastie Boys moved to Los Angeles, where they established their own label, Grand Royal, and built their private studio, G-Son, using profits from *License to Ill*. "The Beastie boys moved to LA. They set up their own label called Grand Royal."
**G-Son Studio Origin and Setup** : The Beastie Boys' studio, G-Son, was located in Atwater Village, north of downtown and east of Hollywood, on the second floor above a drugstore at 3218 and a half, Glendale Boulevard. The name G-Son came from a dilapidated plumbing store next door, Gilson's, which had lost the "I" and "L" from its sign, leading owners to simply hyphenate G-S-O-N. "This place became their business office, their private skate park, and their official home basketball court. Rent was less than $1,500 a month."
**Shift Away from Heavy Sampling** : For their album *Check Your Head*, the band consciously moved away from their previous heavy reliance on sampling, as their accountant advised them that "the cost of clearing those samples would break them." This decision was both an "economic one as well as an artistic one."
**Recording "Check Your Head" at Night** : Much of the recording for *Check Your Head* at their G-Son studio was done at night because "the people at the drug store on the ground floor kept complaining about all the noise during business hours."
**Lack of Label Supervision for "Check Your Head"** : During the creation of *Check Your Head*, the Beastie Boys experienced "almost any record company supervision," as they "d been written off by the labels, so they didn't take a lot of interest in the project." "Rick Rubin wasn't there to guide things. The dust brothers weren't there to take control, so it was just the Beastie Boys doing their Beastie Boys thing."
**Expansion of the Beastie Empire** : Beyond their music, the Beastie Boys expanded their ventures significantly; their label Grand Royal signed other acts like Luscious Jackson, launched a magazine and mailing list, and they created a clothing company called X Large, specializing in Skate and Hip Hop Wear, born from their desire to design their own "cool clothes." "The Beastie Empire began to grow."
**Adam Yauch's Trip to Tibet and Activism** : Adam Yauch's trip to Tibet "changed his life," leading him to become a Buddhist and a strong activist for pro-Tibet and anti-China political causes. This commitment led him to establish the Milarepa Fund, a non-profit organization dedicated to raising awareness of Chinese oppression in Tibet, and the band also supported AIDS awareness and pro-choice causes. "Adam Yacht took a trip to Tibet, the changed his life."
**Nathaniel Hornblower Incident at MTV Awards** : At the 1994 MTV Music Video Awards, where their "Sabotage" video lost to R.E.M.'s "Everybody Hearts," a "drunk freak named Nathaniel Hornblower rushed the stage in a drunk in protest." Officially Adam Yauch's uncle, the figure "looks suspiciously like Adam himself" behind the beard. "Officially, that was Adam Yox's uncle, but if you look past the beard, he looks suspiciously like Adam himself."
**Milarepa Fund Donations from Tour** : During their successful Quadrophonic Joystick Action Tour, the Beastie Boys committed to donating one dollar from each ticket sold to the Milarepa Fund. "And even more important was that one dollar from each ticket was donated to the Miller Rapafund."
**Beastie Boys and the Early Internet** : In the period between 1995 and 1999, the Beastie Boys "spent a lot of time getting into this new thing called The Internet, and we're in fact one of the first bands to set up a proper sophisticated website."
**Mike D's Bike Accident** : Around the early 2000s, Mike D suffered a "bike accident" that resulted in a "separated shoulder that needed surgery and rehab," impacting the band's activities. "Mike D had a bike accident, a separated shoulder that needed surgery and rehab."
**Adam Yauch's Film Endeavors** : Adam Yauch delved deeper into film, establishing a studio named A Siloscope Laboratories in Manhattan, further expanding his artistic reach beyond music. "Adam Yox started getting deeper into film, setting up a studio called A Siloscope Laboratories in Manhattan."
**Copy Protection Controversy with "To the 5 Buroughs"** : In Europe, the release of *To the 5 Buroughs* was marred by a controversy involving MacroVision CDS2000, a copy protection software used by Capitol Records, which "mimicked a virus." This caused "lots of accusations" and issues for the band, particularly in light of Sony being "busted" for installing rootkit malware on their own disks around the same time. "Somebody came up with a bright idea of installing some malware on some disks, ripped the CD to your computer, and a piece of software called a root kit installed itself, screwing with the operating system."
**Grand Royal Label Collapse** : The Beastie Boys' own record label, Grand Royal, ultimately "ran up to many debts and went out of business in 2001." "The biggest exception was the band's record label, Grand Royal. It ran up to many debts and went out of business in 2001."
**Adam Yauch's Cancer Diagnosis and Treatment** : On July 20, 2009, Adam Yauch publicly announced that he had a "little lump in my throat" which was diagnosed as "a form of cancer in the gland that's over here," specifically the parotid gland and a lymph node. He underwent "surgery probably next week coming up. And then after that, have to have some radiation done localized in that area." "But the good news is that it's, that it is, they did scans of my whole body and it's only localized in this one area."
**Adam Yauch's Illness and Final Album Promotion** : Although MCA received treatment and was well enough for *Hot Sauce Committee Part Two* to be released, his cancer was not as treatable as initially thought, preventing him from participating in a tour or appearing in any of the album's videos. "There was no tour and he wasn't well enough to appear in any of the videos from the album."
**Adam Yauch's Rock and Roll Hall of Fame Letter** : When the Beastie Boys were inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in April 2012, Adam Yauch was too ill to attend. The other two members read a heartfelt letter he had prepared, dedicating the award to his bandmates, fans, early collaborators like Kate Shellenbach and John Berry, his supportive parents, and most of all, his wife Dechen and daughter Lose. "When the Beastie Boys were inducted into the Rock and Wall Hall of Fame in April 2012, MCA was too sick to attend. The other two guys read his letter from the stage."
**Adam Yauch's Death** : Adam Yauch was admitted to New York Presbyterian Hospital in Manhattan on April 14, 2012, and died at 9 AM on May 4, 2012, at the age of 47. The official cause of death was "Salivary gland Neoplasm, cancer of the Salivary glands." "And at nine in the morning on May 4th, 2012, he died at the age of 47."
**End of the Beastie Boys** : Following Adam Yauch's death, there was "never any question" that Mike D and Adrock would not continue as the Beastie Boys, honoring his legacy. "That of course was the end of the Beastie Boys."
**Adam Yauch's Will Stipulation** : Adam Yauch's will contained a handwritten stipulation near the end, stating "that no Beastie Boys song should ever be used in advertising," a wish that the other two band members had "no problem with." "His will stipulates that no Beastie Boys song should ever be used in advertising."
**Adam Yauch Park** : In Brooklyn Heights, a few blocks from where Adam Yauch grew up and learned to ride his bike as a child, a park was named in his honor. "And if you go to Brooklyn Heights, you'll find Adam Yauk Park, a few blocks from where he grew up."
**Post-Beastie Boys Careers** : After the band's end, Mike D pursued interior design and record producing, married television, film, and music video director Tamara Davis, and had children. Adam Horowitz became a remixer under the name 41 Small Stars, played bass with cabaret singer Bridget Everett, expressed interest in more acting, and was married to Kathleen Hannah of the Riot Girl band Bikini Kill. "Adam Yauk was committed to his fight for the people of Tibet. He was a big supporter of feminist causes and LGBT rights."
**Kathleen Hannah's Influence on Kurt Cobain** : Adam Horowitz's wife, Kathleen Hannah of Bikini Kill, is noted as the woman who "accidentally inspired Kurt Cobain to write a song called Smells Like Teen Spirit."

# curiosity

**Difficulty of Transitioning from Novelty Act to Serious Artist** : It is "even more difficult to move from being pigeonholed as a novelty act to one that carries gravitas and serious artistic merit." "Yet, that's what the Beastie boys managed to do."
**Impact of 9-11 on Artists** : The 9/11 attacks had a profound effect on the Beastie Boys, "which messed them up. A lot," contributing to the reflective and tribute-like nature of their album *To the 5 Buroughs*. "There was Coachella, and there was 9-11, which messed them up. A lot."
**Music Industry Response to File Sharing (2004)** : In 2004, the music industry was grappling with "rampant" legal file sharing and was actively "looking to do something, anything, to keep people from stealing music." "The legal file sharing was rampant and the industry was looking to do something, anything, to keep people from stealing music."
**Music Industry Experimentation with Malware/Spyware (2004)** : In an attempt to combat piracy around 2004, "Somebody came up with a bright idea of installing some malware on some disks," such as a rootkit, which would install itself when a CD was ripped to a computer, disrupting the operating system. "Somebody came up with a bright idea of installing some malware on some disks, ripped the CD to your computer, and a piece of software called a root kit installed itself, screwing with the operating system."
**Importance of Sample Manipulation** : The effectiveness of sampling often lies not just in using original sources but in the "genius" of those who can "weave these things, these sounds into something brand new, either as is, or by altering it or manipulating it or mutilating it in some way." "I know that these samples don't seem like much on their own, but that's the genius of the people who can do it right."
**Artistic Reflection in Challenging Times** : The creation of albums during troubled times, such as the period following 9/11 for the Beastie Boys, often involves a balance of reflecting on serious events while also seeking "a way to have a good time, to enjoy life and live life as well." "Having fun and troubled times. That's a good way."
