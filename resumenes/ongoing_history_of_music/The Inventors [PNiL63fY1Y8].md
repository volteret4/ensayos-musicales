**Mechanical Recording Limitations (Pre-Mid-1920s)** : Before the mid-1920s, sound recording was a purely mechanical process. It involved musicians and singers performing around a large, horn-shaped sound collector that funneled sound to a needle. This needle vibrated and cut a groove into a rotating disc made of soft material, which served as the master. This method produced awful sound quality, offered no editing, overdubs, or special effects, and required restarting from scratch if any mistake occurred. "Up until the mid-1920s, recording sound was a very mechanical process."

**Foundations of Vinyl Record Specifications (1927)** : In 1927, JP Maxfield conducted calculations crucial for sound-on-film technology that inadvertently established the physical standards for vinyl records. He determined that to achieve maximum sound fidelity and volume with minimum noise over 10 to 11 minute film reels, the grooves on a 12-inch disc needed to be packed at approximately 100 grooves per inch, rotating at 33 and a third revolutions per minute. These dimensions and speed remain standard for vinyl albums today. "JP did some calculations in 1927 and he worked out that sustaining maximum sound fidelity and volume and minimum noise over 10 or 11 minutes required the grooves on the record to be packed in at about 100 to the inch on a 12 inch disc rotating at 33 and a third revolutions per minute."

"The invention of the MP3 has generated revenues of more than $150 million since it was released.
