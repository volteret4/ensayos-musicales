# artist - Tony Iommi

## curiosities
**Factory Accident and Finger Injury (1966)** : At age 17, while working in a Birmingham factory that manufactured metal rings, Iommi suffered an industrial accident on his last day of work. Reassigned to a huge press that operated like a guillotine due to a colleague's absence, his right hand was caught, severing the ends of his middle and ring fingers. Doctors informed him he might as well forget about playing the guitar. "bang, it came down. It just took the ends off my fingers. I actually pulled them off."
**Inspiration from Django Reinhart** : After his factory accident, Iommi's foreman, feeling sympathetic, played him a recording by guitarist Django Reinhart. Reinhart, despite terrible burns to his hand leaving him with only two workable fingers, became a master of the guitar, inspiring Iommi to continue playing despite his own injury. "That inspired Tony to keep going, but it was painful."
**DIY Prosthetic Thimbles** : To manage the pain and difficulty of playing guitar after his injury, Iommi fashioned plastic thimbles from a dishwashing soap bottle and wrapped them in leather from an old jacket. These thimbles allowed him to play but made it difficult to feel the strings, requiring him to press down very hard. "he fashioned a couple of plastic thimbles from a bottle of dishwashing soap and wrapped them in leather from an old leather jacket."
**Development of Signature Sound** : Due to his finger injury and the use of thimbles, Iommi found soloing tough and concentrated on playing heavy power chords. He couldn't bend standard guitar strings well, so he used banjo strings and tuned his guitar low to C sharp (drop C sharp) to make them looser and easier to play. This combination of techniques—heavy power chords, banjo strings, and low tuning—created the distinctive heavy, sinister, and powerful guitar sound that made Black Sabbath famous and significantly contributed to the sound of heavy metal. "Put this all together, and you have the distinctive guitar sound that made his band, Black Sabbath, famous."
**Influence on Grunge** : More than a decade after Black Sabbath's rise, a new generation of metal-adjacent bands from the Pacific Northwest, influenced by Tony Iommi, adopted his "no fingers tuning," specifically drop D or drop C sharp. This tuning became a foundational element of the grunge genre. "That dropped D or in Tony's case, drop C sharp became the foundation of grunge."

# genre - Ska

## curiosities
**Emergence in Jamaica (Late 1950s)** : Ska, initially nicknamed "SCOP," developed in Jamaica from late 1959. It originated from a mix of calypso, soul music, American blues, and R&B, fostered by sound system culture in Kingston's expanding ghettos. This cultural shift was a social effect of wide-scale bauxite mining in Jamaica, which displaced thousands and led to high unemployment. "Starting at about late 1959, this mix of music began to coalesce into something with a distinctive Jamaican sound. Long story short, it was nicknamed SCOP."
**National Music of Independent Jamaica (1962)** : Upon Jamaica's independence in 1962, Ska music, being homegrown and representing a "good time music" vibe, became a significant cultural symbol for the new nation. "In 1962, Jamaica became an independent nation, and this new SCOP music was exactly what the country needed. It was good time music, and it was homegrown."
**Spread to Britain and Beyond (1964)** : Jamaican immigrants brought Ska to Britain, where a diaspora was serviced by at least three record labels by 1964. The music was then discovered by white British youth, especially mods, and further popularized by Millie Small's worldwide hit "My Boy Lollipop." "By 1964, the Jamaican diaspora in Britain was being serviced by at least three record labels that brought music from home. Eventually, it was discovered by white kids, especially those identifying as mods, and spread the sounds even further."
**Second Wave/Revival (Late 1970s)** : After a period of receding popularity, Ska, also known as Blue Beat, was rediscovered by white youth in Coventry, England. They revitalized the working-class Jamaican music by infusing it with working-class punk from England, leading to a new wave of popularity in the late 1970s and early 1980s. "until it was discovered by some white kids from Coventry, who gave this working class music from Jamaica a new twist by adding working class punk from England."
**Enduring Popularity** : Ska experienced a two-year period of significant popularity in the UK, burning out in the early 1980s. However, the genre never disappeared and has continued to resurface repeatedly over the past 40 years, remaining a popular and universal sound globally. "For two years, SCOP was one of the biggest sounds in the UK, but it burned out in the early 1980s. However, SCOP never went away, and has popped up again and again and again over the last 40 years."
