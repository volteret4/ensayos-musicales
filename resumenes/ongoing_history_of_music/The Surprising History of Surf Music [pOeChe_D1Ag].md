# instrument - Reverb

## curiosities
**Dreamy, Twangy Guitar Effect** : Reverb, a fast echo introduced in the recording studio, can make guitar sounds even more dreamy and twangy. "It could be made even more dreamy and twangy by adding reverb, a fast echo introduced in the recording studio."
**1930s Development** : The first reverb effects were developed in the 1930s, primarily by American producer Bill Putnam. "The first reverb effects were developed in the 1930s, largely by an American producer named Bill Putnam."
**Purpose in Music** : Reverb enhances sound, making music sound better. Ancient Greeks created amphitheaters for slight delays, and great concert halls were praised for their natural reverb. Reverb adds life to otherwise dry recordings. "Now why would you want reverb on a recording? Because music can sound better with it."
**Live Sound Enhancement** : Reverb was extensively used to enliven many early rock and roll recordings, particularly on vocals. "Reverb was used to live up a lot of early rock and roll recordings, especially on the vocals."

# instrument - Reverb unit

## curiosities
**Echo Chamber Design** : Bill Putnam's first reverb unit, or echo chamber, consisted of a speaker and a microphone placed in a tiled bathroom, which provided a natural echo effect. "Putnam's first reverb unit or echo chamber was a speaker and a microphone hanging in a tile bathroom."
**Studio Integration** : Soon after its initial use, recording studios globally started designing special rooms specifically for creating reverb effects, such as the reverb room at the back of World Famous Studio 2 at Abbey Road Studios, or in basements, stairwells, and loading docks. Tiled bathrooms remained a fallback option. "And soon recording studios around the world had special rooms designed just to create that effect."
**Plate Reverbs** : Plate reverbs were a method of creating reverb using plates of sheet metal hung in special rooms. "The two most important being plate reverbs, which used plates of sheet metal hung in one of those special rooms."
**Spring Reverbs** : Spring reverbs were more portable and were first found in Hammond Organs in the 1930s. This innovation allowed the replication of natural church-like reverb in smaller spaces or homes, where organs were commonly experienced. "But more important was the spring reverb, which were portable. The first units were found in Hammond Organs in the 1930s."
**Wet Reverb Terminology** : Early surf guitarists appreciated the dueling effect of Fender's external reverb tank, which they felt made the guitar sound "wet," akin to waves. This term, "wet reverb," is still used today. "The early surf guitars picked up on this due effect, and to them it made the guitar sound wet, just like the waves. In fact we still call this a wet reverb."

**Branches of Surf Music** : The surf music phenomenon developed into distinct branches, primarily original guitar-heavy surf music and vocal surf (e.g., The Beach Boys, Jan and Dean). A subset known as Hot Rod Rock also emerged, shifting the focus from the beach to the street. "Here's a split. There was the original surf music that was heavy on the guitar. And then we have Vocal Surf, which is where we find the Beach Boys, Jan and Dean, the RIP Chords and the Riviera."
**Surf Music's Enduring Influence** : Surf music's sound and aesthetic have permeated various other genres, including punk rock, hardcore punk, and even indie rock, demonstrating its adaptability and lasting impact on the rock ecosystem. Its influence is noted in amplifier design and recording studio innovations. "You can see how a form of music can worm its way through the rock ecosystem."
