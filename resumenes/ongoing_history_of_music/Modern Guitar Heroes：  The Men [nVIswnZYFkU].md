## albums

## songs
**House of Cards (2007)** : A track from the "In Rainbows" album, highlighted as an example of Johnny Greenwood's distinct guitar style. "This is from the In Rainbows album, and it's called House of Cards."

## curiosities
**Joining Radiohead** : Johnny Greenwood, the youngest member, joined the band by consistently accompanying his older brother, Colin, until he was eventually integrated into the group. "he only made it into the band because he kept hanging around with his older brother, calling so much that he just was sort of folded into the group."
**Multi-Instrumentalist and Composer** : While primarily a guitarist, Johnny Greenwood is proficient in piano, electronic instruments, viola, harmonica, banjo, and peculiar instruments like the Ondes Martenot and Indian Tampura. He has also developed a significant career as a composer for film, TV, and documentaries, working across rock, indie, jazz, chamber orchestra, and classical styles. "Not only has Johnny been a key element to the Radiohead sound, but he's also become quite the composer on his own, especially when it comes to soundtracks for movies, TV shows, and documentaries."
**Music History and Genre Interests** : Greenwood is an expert in the history of recorded music and is particularly interested in Krautrock, a German rock style from the 1970s, and the noise music of bands like Sonic Youth. "Johnny is also very much into things like Crot Rock, that very German sort of rock that came about in the 1970s, and the noise we get from bands like Sonic Youth."
**Rejection of "Guitar Hero" Label** : Johnny Greenwood actively rejects the concept of a "guitar hero," viewing the guitar simply as a tool for making music, akin to a typewriter for writing or a vacuum cleaner for housework. "He also refuses to buy into this whole idea of the guitar hero. To him, guitars are just for making music, like a typewriter was used to write novels or a vacuum cleaner is used for housework."

# artist - Queens of the Stone Age

## members
Josh Homme

## member of
Kyuss (evolved into Queens of the Stone Age)

## genres
Stoner Rock

## instruments
Electric Guitar

## curiosities
**Guitar Solo Satire** : Josh Homme frequently plays guitar solos with the intention of "making fun of guitar solos," highlighting his unique, instantly recognizable style over flashy technique. "He even says that he often plays guitar solos to make fun of guitar solos."
**Polish Polka Training** : His first music lessons were on guitar, but his Polish teacher insisted he learn polkas, a musical foundation that would later contribute to his distinctive rhythmic approach. "His first music lessons were on guitar, but his Polish teacher insisted on having him learn polkas."
**Influences from Guitar Legends** : He was influenced by Jimi Hendrix, Jimmy Page, and profoundly by Billy Gibbons of ZZ Top, drawing inspiration from Gibbons' riffage and his technique of tapping individual notes. "Billy Gibbons said it made a huge impact on Josh, not just with his riffage, but how he had a way of tapping out individual notes in the middle of everything."
**Polka's Rhythmic Impact** : The two-four time signature and "bounce" characteristic of polkas, which he learned at ages 9-11, implicitly influenced the rhythmic feel of his playing. "Many of them are written in two, four times, so they have a particular sort of bounce to them."
**Thumb and Finger Picking Technique** : During his early learning, he developed a unique technique of plucking strings with his thumb and fingers instead of a pick. "He didn't use a pick. Instead, he developed a technique of plucking the strings with his thumb and fingers."
**Subtractive Chord Playing** : At age 13, he discovered that removing a note from a standard chord produced a "cooler sound" with a "darker sort of tone," which became a signature element of his style, along with his finger picking. "he later discovered at age 13 that if he took a note out of a proper chord, he ended up with a cooler sound, a darker sort of tone, and that, along with the finger picking, became part of his signature sound."

# artist - Red Hot Chili Peppers

## members
John Frusciante
Flea

## instruments

## albums
**Blood Sugar Sex Magik (1991)** : This album was recorded in "The Mansion," a reportedly haunted house, and its artwork is said to include a "weird glowing orb" in a group photo, which Frusciante attributes to poltergeist activity influencing his guitar work. "In fact, if you look carefully at the artwork for the Chili Peppers album Blood Sugar Sex Magic, you'll see what looks like a weird glowing orb that's part of a group photo."

## songs
**(Untitled Jam Session Song) (Early 1990)** : A song that originated from an early 1990 jam session with Flea and was recorded at "The Mansion," showcasing Frusciante's talent. "This song, I think, represents one of the greatest showcases of John's talent. It came out of a jam session with Flea, sometime in early 1990, and the album on which it appears was recorded at a place called The Mansion that is said to be haunted."

## curiosities
**Song-Serving Philosophy** : John Frusciante believes his guitar playing should always serve the song, focusing on tone, melody, rhythm, and structure rather than flashy, attention-grabbing techniques, especially prominent in his initial tenure with the band. "He seems to believe that his playing must serve the song and not in any way draw attention to what he's doing."
**Critique of Speed-Focused Playing** : Frusciante argues that focusing on playing faster and creating new techniques hinders emotional progress in music, failing to transmit feeling to listeners and leaving players at an emotional level similar to where Hendrix was decades ago. "People believe that by playing faster and creating new playing techniques, you can progress forward, but then they realize that emotionally, they don't progress at all."
**Exclusive Vintage Guitar Use** : He almost exclusively uses vintage guitars, rejecting any made after 1970. His favorites include a 1962 Red Fender Jaguar, a 1962 Sunburst Fender Stratocaster, a 1955 Stratocaster, a 1955 Gretsch White Falcon, a Yamaha SG, and a 1950s Martin 010 for acoustic work. "Much of his sound is derived from the fact that he plays almost exclusively vintage guitars. If it was made after 1970, he wants nothing to do with it."
**Loss of Guitars in House Fire (1996)** : In 1996, John Frusciante lost a significant portion of his guitar collection when his house was destroyed by fire. "John had a lot more guitars, but he lost all of them when his house burned down in 1996."
**Embracing Imperfections and "Mistreatment"** : He purposefully seeks out "wonky, distorted, and weird" tones, believing that imperfections and "mistreating" his guitar can generate cool new sounds, rejecting the pursuit of perfectly clean tones. "John believes that imperfections and mistakes can actually make a musical work better. Trying to get clean tones from a guitar is a bad idea."
**Diverse Musical Inspirations** : His inspirations include Jimi Hendrix, Joe Strummer, Captain Beefheart, Frank Zappa, Robert Johnson, Bernard Sumner of New Order, and significantly, John McGeoch of Siouxsie and the Banshees, admired for his varied soundscapes. "And who does he look for for inspiration? Well, we talked about Hendrix, but also Joe Strummer, Captain B. Fart, Frank Zappa, loose legend Robert Johnson, Bernard Sumner of New Order."
**Haunted Recording Studio Influence** : The band recorded at "The Mansion," a house reputed to be haunted where the owner allegedly pushed his girlfriend to her death in 1918. Stories of poltergeist activity, changing room temperatures, and "gremlins" in electronics at the site inspired Frusciante to conjure "otherworld of guitars" in his songs. "It is said that in 1918, the owner of the house pushed his girlfriend to her death off the balcony, and then that original house burned down and then had to be rebuilt."

# artist - Tool

## members
Adam Jones

## instruments
Gibson Les Paul (many)
1979 Silverburst Les Paul (special edition, his favorite)
Old school Marshall tube amps
Electric Guitar

## albums
**Ænima (1996)** : This album features the song "Stinkfist," showcasing Adam Jones' guitar work and diverse techniques. "Tool and stinkfist from the Enema album."

## songs
**Stinkfist (1996)** : A track from the "Ænima" album, highlighted as a prime example of Adam Jones' varied techniques and his continuous search for interesting sounds. "Tool and stinkfist from the Enema album."

## curiosities
**Band Founder and Leader** : Adam Jones is the founder and leader of Tool, exercising significant control over the band's direction. "Toul is Adam's band. He formed it, he runs him."
**Suzuki Method Musical Training** : Jones received formal music training using the Suzuki method, which emphasizes learning by ear, public performance from a young age, and "tonalization"—the ability to recognize and produce beautiful tones from an instrument—a core concept in his work with Tool. "He learned all about music through something known as the Suzuki method, which has been around for decades. It involves learning to play by ear rather than from sheet music."
**Classical and Multi-Instrumental Background** : His first instrument was the violin, a Suzuki method staple, through which he learned classical music. He later moved to bass before picking up the six-string guitar. "His first instrument was the violin, which is a Suzuki method staple, and he learned to play classical music, which of course makes sense if you're at all familiar with Toul's music, right?"
**"Sounds Over Notes" Philosophy** : Adam Jones prioritizes creating unique sounds over adhering to a specific playing style. He employs various techniques like power chords, polyrhythmic riffs, triplets, and arpeggios as required by each song, and readily utilizes outboard gear to achieve his desired tones. "Again, it's not just the notes, it's the sounds, very Suzuki method."
**Silverburst Les Paul Mythology** : His favored 1979 Silverburst Les Paul is subject to myths; one claims the paint's weight imbues a special tone, while another suggests a "straight screw or nail" hidden within it provides an extra special quality. "It is said that the weight of the paint used for the 1979 model, which is what Adam has, imbues a special sort of tone to the guitar."
**Old Marshall Amp Preference** : He prefers old-school Marshall tube amps, noting that each one sounds unique, prompting him to keep any that produce a good tone. "He loves old school marshals, the kind with the tubes. He says that no two martial two-bamps sound the same."
**Drop-D Tuning Consistency** : Almost every Tool song is performed in drop-D tuning, a consistent characteristic of their guitar sound. "Almost every song tool does seems to be in drop-dee tuning."
**Additive Chord Approach** : In contrast to Josh Homme's subtractive method, Adam Jones often adds extra notes to his chords, which contributes to the thick, dense sound characteristic of Tool's guitar parts. "Adam adds additional notes to a lot of the chords he plays."

## members

## instruments

Gretsch guitars

## albums
**Elephant (2003)** : This album features the globally recognized song "Seven Nation Army," known for its distinctive riff. "And maybe the best example is Seven Nation Army from the white stripes 2003 album Elephant."

## songs
**Seven Nation Army (2003)** : The iconic seven-note riff was played on a late 1950s/early 1960s K Archtop guitar. Its bass-like sound was achieved by funneling it through a whammy pedal set to transpose the sound down one octave, and it was amplified by a mid-1960s Sears department store amplifier. "The 7 note riff was played on that K Archtop. And the reason it sounds like a bass guitar is because it's funneled through a whammy pedal set to transpose the sound down one octave."

## curiosities
**Analog Preferences and "Old Soul"** : Jack White often states he feels like he was born in the wrong generation, preferring the warmth and authenticity of analog recordings from earlier eras (1930s or late 1800s) over digital technology. "He often said that he was born in the wrong generation. He feels that as a musician, he has an old soul."
**Analog Recording Process** : While he uses digital programs like Pro Tools for editing, he always records music onto old-school magnetic tape, employing two ancient stutter tape machines that record eight tracks each onto two-inch tape. "He says that they have a warmth and authenticity that you just can't get with digital technology. For example, while he may edit his music in a digital program like Pro Tools, he always records the music onto old school magnetic tape."
**"It Might Get Loud" Documentary (2008) Appearance** : Jack White was featured in this 2008 documentary alongside The Edge of U2 and Jimmy Page of Led Zeppelin, where they discussed their relationships with the electric guitar. "watch the 2008 documentary It Might Get Loud, which features Jack, the Edge from U2 and Jimmy Page of Led Zeppelin, all trying to explain the relationships they have with the electric guitar."
**Signature Montgomery Ward Airline Guitar** : His favorite and signature guitar is a red and white 1964 Montgomery Ward JB Hutto Airline, which he frequently used with The White Stripes. The company ceased operations in 1968. "His favorite guitar is still a 1964 Montgomery Ward JB Hato airline. That's the red and white guitar that we saw him playing all the time with the white stripes."
**Resourceful Use of Inexpensive Gear** : The recording of "Seven Nation Army" with a mid-1960s Sears department store amplifier exemplifies his belief that creative sound production depends on what can be done with available gear, not its cost or quantity. "Proving again that it's often not about how much gear you got or how expensive that gear is, but what you can do with the gear you have."
**"Seven Nation Army" as a Sports Anthem** : "Seven Nation Army" went on to become a worldwide sports anthem for decades. "A song that will be a worldwide sports anthem for decades."
**Recording at Toe Rags Studio** : "Seven Nation Army" was recorded at Toe Rags, an old eight-track recording studio located in London. "And one recorded using a Sears amplifier in an old eight track recording studio in London called Toe Rags."

## songs
**Bulls on Parade (1996)** : This 1996 track showcases Tom Morello playing his "Arm the Homeless" guitar through a whammy pedal, demonstrating his innovative effects usage. "Let's sample something from 1996. This is Tom with Rage Against the Machine. He's playing the Arm the Homeless guitar through a whammy pedal on Bulls on Parade."

## curiosities
**Early Guitar Start and Band Experience** : Tom Morello picked up a cheap Japanese K guitar at age 13 for about $100, and within weeks he was playing in a Led Zeppelin cover band. He began seriously studying the guitar around age 20. "Tom first picked up a guitar at age 13, a K guitar, a cheapo Japanese thing that cost about 100 bucks. And within weeks he was playing in a Led Zeppelin cover band."
**Harvard Attendance with Gibson Explorer** : He took his Gibson Explorer with him when he enrolled at Harvard University and still owns the instrument. "And this Gibson Explorer was the guitar he took with him when he enrolled in Harvard. He still has him."
**Hip Hop and "Scratching" Guitar Technique** : Influenced by hip hop and rap acts like Public Enemy and Run DMC, he developed a unique technique of "scratching" his guitar strings, mimicking a DJ on a turntable. "He got deeper into hip hop and rap, public enemy and run DMC were big influences, which also moved him to start scratching his guitar strings like a DJ wood on a turntable."
**Tony Iommi's Influence on Restraint** : He studied old Black Sabbath records to observe how Tony Iommi effectively used restraint, recognizing the importance of the "space between the notes" in his playing. "He also spent a lot of time listening to old black Sabbath records, noting how guitarist Tony Iomey often exhibited restraint, utilizing that the space between the notes could be as important as the notes themselves."
**High Guitar Position for Maneuverability** : Morello deliberately holds his guitar high on his body, providing more room to move his hands along the strings and create unusual sounds on the guitar's body and fretboard. "He plays it way up high on the body. And that's done deliberately because it gives him more room to move his hands up and down the strings and to create all sorts of unusual sounds on the body of the guitar and the fretboard."
**"Arm the Homeless Electric" Guitar** : His most famous guitar is a custom instrument called the "Arm the Homeless Electric," a slogan he carved into its body in the early 1990s. He purchased the wood body from a luthier in Hollywood in 1986, then customized all its components to his liking, making it his main guitar except during his Audioslave period. "His most famous is probably a custom job that he calls his Arm the Homeless Electric. He calls it this because he carved that slogan, Arm the Homeless, into the body of a guitar sometime in the early 1990s."

# genre - Alt Rock

## curiosities
**Evolving Definition of Guitar Hero (Post-1990s)** : From the early 1990s onward, the definition of an Alt Rock guitar hero shifted from solely speed-based solos to emphasize great technique, creativity (new sounds, unusual tunings, innovative use of effects), distinctive presentation, and a certain mystique. "They're just different, you know? Let's make a list, shall we?"
**Emphasis on Creativity and Sound** : Alt Rock guitarists prioritize technique expressed through creative new sounds, unusual tunings, innovative outboard technologies (pedals and processors), and novel approaches to rhythm and melody construction, moving beyond the classic rock metric of "100 notes a second." "Technique was expressed in other ways other than solos that were measured by speed, you know, a number of notes per second. This leads to a discussion of creativity, you know? New sounds, unusual tunings, different outboard technologies like pedals and processors, new ways of playing rhythm and construction melodies."
**Importance of Presentation and Mystique** : An Alt Rock guitar hero needs a unique sense of style and performance, where "how they play is just as important as what they play," combined with an intriguing mystique about their methods, ideas, equipment, and personality. "Now guitar heroes have to have a certain sense of style and performance that's really hard to quantify how they play is just as important as what they play."

# curiosity

**Definition of a "Guitar Hero" (Pre-Video Game)** : Before the advent of the video game, a "guitar hero" was understood as a male musician who achieved a seemingly supernatural mastery of the electric guitar, inspiring other experts and pushing the boundaries of the instrument, amplifiers, and effects pedals. "A guitar hero was a guy, and it was almost always a guy who achieved a seemingly supernatural mastery of the electric guitar."
**Alt Rock Guitar Hero Criteria** : The definition of an Alt Rock guitar hero from the 1990s onward encompasses great technique (not solely speed-focused), creativity (new sounds, unusual tunings, innovative technologies), a distinct sense of style and performance, and a certain mystique about their methods and gear. "First of all, you have to have great technique. You might show off that technique by shredding through solos, but not necessarily. That's an old metric."
**The Mansion Recording Studio** : A reportedly haunted house where the Red Hot Chili Peppers recorded an album. It is said that in 1918, the owner pushed his girlfriend to her death from a balcony, leading to the original house burning down and being rebuilt. Stories include forces changing room temperatures and "gremlins" affecting electronics. "It is said that in 1918, the owner of the house pushed his girlfriend to her death off the balcony, and then that original house burned down and then had to be rebuilt."
**Suzuki Method of Music Education** : A long-standing method of music education emphasizing learning by ear, public performance from a young age, and "tonalization"—the ability to find and recognize beautiful and interesting tones from an instrument. Adam Jones of Tool received formal training through this method. "He learned all about music through something known as the Suzuki method, which has been around for decades. It involves learning to play by ear rather than from sheet music."
**"It Might Get Loud" Documentary (2008)** : A 2008 documentary featuring Jack White, The Edge from U2, and Jimmy Page from Led Zeppelin, where they discuss their individual relationships with the electric guitar. "watch the 2008 documentary It Might Get Loud, which features Jack, the Edge from U2 and Jimmy Page of Led Zeppelin, all trying to explain the relationships they have with the electric guitar."
**Analog Recording Preference** : Jack White strongly prefers analog recordings, believing they offer a warmth and authenticity that digital technology cannot replicate, despite his use of digital programs for editing. "He believes in analog recordings. Even old scratchy 78s made in the late 1920s. He says that they have a warmth and authenticity that you just can't get with digital technology."
**Two-inch Magnetic Tape Recording** : Jack White utilizes two ancient stutter tape machines to record eight tracks each onto two-inch magnetic tape, showcasing his dedication to old-school analog recording methods. "He has two ancient stutter tape machines that record eight tracks each onto two-inch tape."
**Definition of a Luthier** : A luthier is defined as a guitar maker, as mentioned in the context of Tom Morello purchasing his "Arm the Homeless" guitar. "He bought it in 1986 from a Luther, that's what you call a guitar maker."
