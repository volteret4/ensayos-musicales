# genre - Rock

## curiosities
**12-Year Rock and Pop Cycle** : Historically, popular music followed a 12-year cycle where rock's popularity and influence would ascend while pop descended, and vice versa. This pattern can be traced back to the early 1950s, with rock gaining traction around 1951, peaking by 1957, and then declining as pop surged. The cycle reversed in 1964 with The Beatles' arrival in America, sustaining until about 1970 when pop again took over. Rock saw a resurgence with punk in 1976, faded by 1982-83 with new pop stars, and returned around 1990 with alternative and grunge until 1996. After another pop resurgence and rock being "declared dead," rock experienced a final comeback in 2000-2001, remaining dominant until about 2008 before falling off a cliff. This cycle, which historically predicted a rock revival after 2014 (and even after Donald Trump's 2016 election, which usually correlated with rock's resurgence), no longer held, suggesting it has been irreparably broken by streaming and the decline of traditional music gatekeepers. "This is a dance that extends back to the early 1950s. It says that when rock is on the ascended, in terms of popularity and influence, pop is on the descendant and vice versa."

# label - Apple

## curiosities
**iTunes Music Store and iPod Launch (2003)** : Apple, under Steve Jobs, emerged as the savior of the recorded music industry by introducing the iTunes music store in April 2003 (initially Mac-only, then Windows in October). This followed the earlier release of the iTunes media player and the iPod, which was marketed as holding "a thousand songs in your pocket." Jobs convinced the five major record labels in 2002 to license their music, selling songs for 99 cents with Apple taking 30%. This move changed everything, leading to the iPod becoming the fastest-selling consumer electronic product and providing the first effective solution to music piracy by offering legal, clean digital tracks. "the iTunes music store was introduced in April 2003."
**Stock Price Surge (2001-2007)** : Before the iTunes music player was revealed on January 1, 2001, Apple's stock price was 39 cents. It remained around 39 cents in 2002 and dropped to 32 cents in 2003 when the iTunes music store went online. However, by 2004, it rose to 116 cents, then 254 cents in 2005, 297 cents by the end of 2006, and 597 cents a year later. The iPod and iTunes ecosystem saved both the music industry and Apple itself, which at one point controlled 70% of all digital music sales worldwide. Today, Apple boasts a market cap of approximately $3.5 trillion. "A January 1st, 2001, about 10 days before the iTunes music player was revealed, Apple stock price was just 39 cents."

# label - Spotify

## curiosities
**Streaming Dominance and Scale** : Spotify has become a dominant force in the streaming music industry, boasting 625 million monthly users and valuing the company at $125 billion, a figure greater than the entire recorded music industry combined. It provides access to approximately 202 million songs, available for free or at a very low cost. "Spotify now has 625 million users every month and is a 125 billion dollar company."
**Low Payouts and Artist Compensation** : Spotify, like other streaming platforms, offers significantly lower payouts to artists compared to traditional physical sales. While it provides global reach for a song, the revenue per stream is often equivalent to radio play. This economic model has led to a "thin layer of very successful artists on top" and millions of struggling artists at the bottom, forcing many to rely on alternative income streams like merchandise, touring, and licensing. "Streaming pays out much less than the sales of physical formats."
**Influence on Song Composition** : The platform's payment structure, which only compensates artists after a song runs for at least 30 seconds, has influenced how music is composed. Artists now prioritize holding a listener's attention during this initial half-minute, leading to shorter intros, front-loaded choruses, and an abundance of "sugary hooks" in the first 30 seconds. "no one gets paid until a song runs for at least 30 seconds."
**Listener Behavior and "Lean Back Listening"** : Streaming has altered music consumption habits, with many using music as background "soundtrack" to fill silence, rather than actively listening. This phenomenon, termed "lean back listening," involves passively letting music come to the listener by simply clicking play, contrasting with "lean forward music," which involves actively seeking out, absorbing, and appreciating music. This shift has impacted how algorithms work, the kind of music composers create, and general attitudes toward the abundant music supply. "We've created a situation where a lot of us are hearing music, but not listening."
**Skipping Behavior Data** : Spotify usage data indicates a significant tendency for users to skip songs: 25% of users skip an unfamiliar new song within the first five seconds, another 29% skip within 10 seconds, and 35% are gone by the 30-second mark. This behavior contributes to the devaluation of music as both an art form and a commodity. "According to some Spotify numbers I saw while back, 25% of users will skip an unfamiliar new song in the first five seconds."

# concert - Record Store Day

## curiosities
**Origin and Purpose (2008)** : Record Store Day was conceived in 2007 by a group of independent record store owners in Baltimore, who were struggling with declining sales due to digital music. Inspired by "Free Comic Book Day," they sought an initiative to draw people back to stores to buy records. The first event was held on April 19, 2008, with approximately 200 participating stores, and its positive reception was highly encouraging. "Thus was born record store day, an annual celebration of vinyl and the stores that sell it. It's held on the third Saturday in April. The first record store day was on April 19th, 2008, and about 200 stores participated."
**Expansion and Impact** : The success of Record Store Day led to major retailers like Best Buy, Target, Walmart, and Urban Outfitters beginning to stock vinyl as a defensive strategy against plummeting CD sales caused by piracy. Since its inception, vinyl sales have seen double-digit growth year after year. The event is now supported by around 1500 stores globally, with backing from prominent artists such as the Foo Fighters, Jack White, Paul McCartney, Billie Eilish, and Taylor Swift. A Black Friday edition was later introduced, and during the pandemic, multiple Record Store Days were organized to help sustain independent record stores. "In fact, it was so encouraging that other retailers like Best Buy, Target, Walmart, and Urban Outfitters started stocking vinyl as a hedge against declining CD sales caused by music piracy."
**Metallica's Endorsement (2008)** : A significant endorsement for the inaugural Record Store Day came from Metallica, who hosted an autograph session at Rasputin Music in San Francisco on April 19, 2008. Fans waited up to three days to meet the band, demonstrating the event's immediate appeal and legitimacy. "The biggest endorsement happened at a San Francisco record store called Respeutian Music, when Metallica showed up for an autograph session."

**Human Brain Memory Capacity** : The average adult human brain possesses a storage capacity of approximately 2.5 petabytes, which equates to 2.5 million gigabytes. This vast capacity stores personal memories, facts, academic knowledge, learned behaviors, muscle memory, and specialized areas for musical memories and lyrics. While some memories remain robust, new experiences can cause others to fade or become corrupted over time. "The average adult brain has a capacity of about 2.5 petabytes."
**John Lennon's Philosophy on Life** : John Lennon famously encapsulated the unpredictable nature of existence, stating that life often unfolds while individuals are occupied with making other plans. "John Lennon said, life is what happens when you're making other plans."
**Modern Music Piracy and Its Consequences** : Modern music piracy, which began in the early 1990s and became rampant by 2000, was fueled by consumer frustration over high CD prices, the phasing out of CD singles, and albums often containing only a few good songs. The music industry's attempts to sue fans for stealing music exacerbated the problem. This resulted in a catastrophic decline in sales, with 2.5 billion CDs sold globally in 2000 crashing by over 75% in the subsequent decade. The impact included massive revenue losses, job cuts, artists being dropped from rosters, and the destruction of relationships between labels and record stores. The industry, paralyzed by its lack of technological understanding and hindered by antitrust laws (after being scrutinized for CD price-fixing), struggled to adapt until Steve Jobs' iTunes offered a new path. "Modern music piracy began in the early 1990s. And by the time we reached January 1st 2000, it's starting to really get out of control."
**Vinyl Resurrection from Near Death** : Despite predictions in the late 1980s and early 1990s that vinyl was dying, with CDs dominating the market, vinyl experienced an improbable comeback. By 1999, new turntables were thought to be nearing obsolescence, and for the next 8-9 years, vinyl was on "life support" due to CDs, digital downloads, and piracy. However, around 2006-2007, indie bands began ironically embracing vinyl, and audiophiles continued to resist digital formats. This contrarian movement, combined with the inspiration of "Free Comic Book Day" leading to the creation of Record Store Day in 2008, sparked a consistent double-digit growth in vinyl sales, ultimately leading to vinyl outselling CDs in revenue and units in some countries by 2020, and in Canada by 2024 for the first time in 35 years. "around 2006 and 2007, indie bands started being contrarian and maybe ironic by embracing vinyl."
**Major Labels' Failed Digital Initiatives** : In response to the Napster crisis and widespread piracy by 2000, major record labels, initially dismissive of MP3s, found themselves technologically unprepared (most executives lacked computers). Their attempts to create their own online stores failed due to conflicting loyalties with retail partners, consumer demand for one-stop shops stocking all labels, and antitrust laws preventing a unified store (especially given past CD price-fixing scrutiny). This led to two disastrous ventures: "Press Play" (Universal/Sony), which lasted two years until March 2003, and "MusicNet" (BMG/EMI/Warner), which was sold off by 2005. Both services were criticized for being difficult to use, restrictive, and laden with complicated digital rights management (DRM). "Well, a couple of problems with that. First, the record labels had to think about their retail partners, the thousands of stores that for decades had sold their product."
**Social Media's Transformative Impact on Music** : Social media, beginning with MySpace and later significantly impacted by YouTube, has fundamentally reshaped the music industry through platforms like X, Threads, Blue Sky, TikTok, Instagram Reels, and YouTube Shorts. These platforms facilitate songs going viral rapidly, with algorithms pushing trending sounds globally, enabling artists to build fan bases directly without traditional labels or marketing. This includes diverse content like dance trends, memes, collaborations, and live streaming via Instagram Live, TikTok Live, and Twitch, fostering unprecedented fan engagement and community. However, it has also led to a "snippet culture" emphasizing short songs and hooks, pressure on artists for constant viral content leading to burnout, intense competition, and a "flattening of music" where trends are chased, resulting in similar-sounding material. Social media has also augmented or replaced traditional A&R talent scouting. "Social media and music really began to come together with the debut of MySpace."
**COVID-19 Pandemic's Acceleration of Digital Music Transition** : The COVID-19 pandemic, causing a two-year halt in live music (concerts, festivals), severely impacted artists and venues. It forced a rapid acceleration of the music industry's digital transition, leading to a surge in virtual concerts, live streams, and the explosion of TikTok for music discovery. Revenue shifted to crowdfunding, merchandise, and platforms like Patreon, while streaming services like Spotify, Apple Music, and YouTube saw massive user spikes. While some artists created "COVID music" about isolation and resilience, widespread home production using similar software led to a "flattening of music." Post-pandemic, ticket prices surged due to inflation, and logistical issues like tour bus shortages emerged. Despite its devastation, COVID-19 significantly propelled the industry and fan consumption deeper into the digital realm. "COVID-19 was unspeakably awful. But it did accelerate the music industry's transition into the digital realm, and the effects will be felt not only on the industry, but also on music fans."
**Shift from Physical to Streaming Media (2000s-2016)** : The early 2000s began with robust sales of physical music media, but the rise of the internet and piracy led to a drastic decline. After roughly 15 years of adaptation, the industry embraced streaming, leading to revenue growth starting in 2016. Today, streaming accounts for at least 75% of record label revenue, with platforms like Spotify boasting massive user bases and market capitalization. This shift has resulted in lower artist payouts compared to physical sales, forcing artists to diversify income through merch, touring, and licensing. It also created a top-heavy economic structure with many struggling artists. Streaming has influenced song composition (e.g., shorter intros to grab attention for the first 30 seconds to qualify for payouts) and fostered a "lean back listening" culture where music often serves as background "organized noise" rather than being actively engaged with, unlike the detailed experience of listening to a CD with artwork and liner notes. "revenues started to go up because the industry embraced streaming."
**Wholesale Devaluation of Music in the 21st Century** : The most significant shift in music this century is the wholesale devaluation of its monetary worth. In the 2000s, CDs cost $10-$20, requiring financial investment and careful selection by consumers who would then listen repeatedly. Today, music is an oversupplied product, effectively free or very cheap through streaming services offering millions of songs and 100,000 new uploads daily. This abundance leads to consumers skipping songs frequently (e.g., 25% skip unfamiliar songs in 5 seconds, 35% by 30 seconds), indicating a lack of financial investment, risk, and short attention spans. While music retains emotional and cultural value, its monetary worth has plummeted. Despite complaints about low artist payouts, many users utilize free tiers or pay minimal subscription fees, often unwilling to pay more to better compensate artists. This situation has made music a low-cost, low-margin commodity, impacting how and by whom music is made for the foreseeable future. "So this, the devaluation of music is my pick for the most important thing to happen to rock at all music so far in this century."
