# genre - Rock

## curiosities
**12-Year Pop and Rock Cycle** : For over 50 years, dating back to the early 1950s (birth date estimated around 1951), rock and pop popularity fluctuated in an inverse 12-year cycle. Rock ascended from 1951-1957, then declined until 1964 (Beatles). It surged again until 1970, then faded for pop. It returned with punk in 1976, faded 1982-83 (new pop stars). Another comeback in 1990 with alternative/grunge until 1996, then pop returned, and rock was declared "dead" until 2000-2001. Remained dominant until 2008, then fell off.
**Death of 12-Year Cycle** : This cycle, which should have seen a rock revival after 2014, died. This is attributed to streaming and the decline of traditional gatekeepers (radio, record stores, labels, music magazines, cable music video channels), leading to a fragmented, non-monocultural music landscape.
**Waking from Slumber** : Regressive, reactionary Republican administrations are noted to historically "wake rock from its slumber," though this did not happen after Donald Trump's election in 2016, further indicating the death of the 12-year cycle.

# curiosity

**Human Brain Music Memory Capacity** : The average adult brain has a capacity of about 2.5 petabytes (2.5 million gigs), with special areas dedicated to storing musical memories and lyrics.
**Memory Corruption and Forgetting** : The human brain is subject to memories fading or becoming corrupted over time due to new experiences or circumstances, making eyewitness accounts often unreliable.
**"F4 Refresh" Mental Maintenance** : The concept of performing "maintenance" on the brain's "hard drive" by revisiting history and past experiences is likened to repeatedly pressing F4 on a keyboard for a refresh.
**Alan Cross Podcast Series** : The program, "The ongoing history of New Music Podcasts with Alan Cross," is part 10 of a series titled "100 greatest rock moments of the millennium so far."
**Subjectivity of Music Rankings** : Ranking music moments by importance, influence, or quality is inherently subjective, allowing for significant argument and dissent.
**First 25 Years of the 21st Century Focus** : The list specifically ranks the 10 most important events in music during the first 25 years of the millennium.
**12-Year Pop and Rock Cycle Death** : The traditional 12-year cycle of popularity shifts between rock and pop, which had persisted for over 50 years (since the early 1950s), died around 2014-2016.
**Cause of Cycle Death** : The demise of the 12-year cycle is attributed to the rise of streaming and the decline in influence of traditional music gatekeepers, including radio, record stores, record labels, music magazines, and cable music video channels.
**Monoculture End** : The internet and streaming have ended the monoculture in music that previously created universal listening trends, leading to a fragmented landscape with more genres and diverse consumption.
**Modern Music Piracy Origins (Early 1990s)** : While music piracy has a centuries-long history, modern music piracy began in the early 1990s and was "out of control" by January 1, 2000.
**Consumer Grievances Leading to Piracy (Early 2000s)** : Music consumers were motivated to engage in piracy due to high CD prices, the phasing out of CD singles, and the perception that albums often contained only one or two good songs.
**CD Sales Collapse (2000s)** : Global CD sales, which peaked at approximately 2.5 billion in 2000, crashed by over 75% within the next decade due to widespread music piracy and file sharing.
**Music Industry Financial Crisis (2001-2014)** : The music industry experienced massive revenue declines, job losses, artist roster cuts, and destroyed label-retailer relationships, with revenues dropping every year from 2001 to 2014.
**Record Labels' Technological Ignorance (2000s)** : In 2000, most record label employees and executives lacked understanding of new digital technology, with many not even having computers on their desks.
**Antitrust Laws and Label-Owned Stores** : Antitrust laws, combined with labels already under scrutiny for a CD price-fixing scheme, prevented major labels from jointly creating a single online music store.
**Pressplay (2000-2003)** : A disastrous joint venture online music store launched by Universal and Sony around 2000, criticized for its difficulty of use, restrictions, and heavy digital rights management (DRM), leading to its closure in March 2003.
**MusicNet (2000-2005)** : An online music store handling BMG releases around 2000, also difficult to use with DRM, but lasted longer than Pressplay before being sold off in 2005.
**Steve Jobs' Intervention (2002)** : Steve Jobs approached major record labels in 2002, proposing iTunes as a solution to digital music distribution, offering to sell songs for 99 cents (Apple taking 30%, labels 70%).
**iTunes Music Store Launch (April 2003)** : Launched in April 2003 (Mac only initially, then Windows in October), it allowed legal digital music purchases, becoming a crucial step in solving the piracy problem.
**iPod Sales Phenomenon** : The iPod became the fastest-selling consumer electronic product to that point, driven by the ability to fill it with legally purchased songs from the iTunes Music Store.
**Apple's Resurgence and Market Cap** : The success of the iPod and iTunes saved Apple, transforming it into a technological powerhouse, with its stock price soaring from 32 cents in 2003 to 597 cents in 2007, eventually reaching a market cap of approximately $3.5 trillion.
**MySpace and Early Social Media Music (Episode 1)** : The debut of MySpace was highlighted as the beginning of the significant intersection of social media and music in episode one of this series.
**YouTube's Impact on Music (Episode 8)** : The massive impact of YouTube on music was the dedicated topic of episode eight of this series.
**Current Social Media Platforms for Music** : X, Threads, Blue Sky, TikTok, Instagram Reels, and YouTube Shorts are identified as current platforms facilitating songs going viral.
**Algorithms and Viral Trends** : Social media algorithms detect and push trending sounds globally, enabling even old, forgotten, or obscure songs to quickly achieve global hit status.
**Artist Empowerment through Social Media** : Social media empowers artists to reach global audiences and build fan bases without needing traditional record labels, marketing, or promotion.
**Direct Artist-Fan Connections** : Platforms like SoundCloud, Bandcamp, Spotify, Apple Music, and Amazon Music enable artists to establish direct connections with their fan bases.
**Social Media Marketing Strategies** : Artists use social media for dance trends, memes, rollouts, trailers, teasers, collaborations, and interactive campaigns.
**Live Streaming for Fan Engagement** : Instagram Live, TikTok Live, and Twitch provide artists with platforms for live-streamed performances, interviews, and highly personal interactions with fans.
**Negative Effects of Social Media on Music Consumption** : Social media encourages short attention-span content consumption, favoring playlists over albums, leading to shorter songs and a greater emphasis on hooks (termed "snippet culture").
**Social Media Pressure on Artists** : Labels often demand a constant stream of social media posts and pressure artists to regularly go viral, contributing to quick burnout.
**A&R Shift to Social Media Scouting** : Traditional A&R (Artist and Repertoire) scouting, which involved attending clubs and reviewing demos, has been augmented and often replaced by interns monitoring social media for emerging artists.
**"Flattening of Music" via Social Media** : Social media's tendency to favor certain styles leads to trends being chased by many artists, resulting in periods where much music sounds similar.
**COVID-19 Pandemic Impact on Live Music (2020-2022)** : The pandemic caused a two-year halt in concerts, festivals, and live music, severely impacting artist finances and venue operations.
**Rise of Virtual Events During COVID-19** : The absence of live music led to a surge in virtual concerts and controlled live-stream events utilizing platforms like YouTube, Twitch, Zoom, Instagram Live, and TikTok.
**TikTok's Explosion During COVID-19** : TikTok experienced an explosion in popularity as a primary platform for new music discovery during the pandemic lockdowns.
**New Artist Revenue Models (COVID-19)** : Artists turned to crowdfunding, intensified merchandise sales, and platforms like Patreon to generate revenue during the pandemic.
**Streaming Service User Spikes (COVID-19)** : Spotify, Apple Music, and YouTube saw significant increases in users as people consumed more music at home during lockdowns.
**"COVID Music" Era** : The pandemic inspired an era of "COVID music," with many songs reflecting themes of isolation, alienation, lockdowns, and resilience.
**Home Music Production Surge (COVID-19)** : Thousands of individuals began producing music at home during lockdowns, contributing to a "flattening of music" as many relied on the same software and plugins.
**Hybrid Music Experiences (Post-COVID-19)** : The pandemic spurred the development of new hybrid models blending virtual and live music experiences.
**NFTs and Direct-to-Fan Models (COVID-19)** : The rise of NFTs (non-fungible tokens) is identified as a direct result of new direct-to-fan models accelerated by the pandemic.
**Post-COVID Concert Attendance Boom** : Following the lifting of restrictions, people flocked to live music events in record numbers.
**Post-COVID Concert Ticket Price Inflation** : Concert ticket prices significantly increased due to post-COVID inflation affecting all sectors, including the music business.
**Streaming as Dominant Industry Revenue (Current)** : Currently, streaming accounts for at least 75% of all record label revenue.
**Spotify User Base and Valuation** : Spotify boasts 625 million monthly users and is valued at $125 billion.
**Vastness of Streaming Libraries** : Approximately 202 million songs are available for streaming, with an estimated 100,000 new songs uploaded daily.
**Low Streaming Payouts for Artists** : Streaming platforms generally pay artists significantly less per play than traditional physical sales, with payouts often comparable to radio plays, despite global reach.
**Economic Inequality in Streaming Era** : The new economic realities of streaming have created a "thin layer" of highly successful artists atop millions of struggling ones.
**Streaming's Influence on Song Composition** : To ensure payment (which typically begins after 30 seconds of play), song composition has evolved to include shorter intros, immediate choruses, and numerous hooks within the first 30 seconds to capture listener attention.
**Streaming's Impact on Music Consumption Habits** : The ease and low cost of streaming have led to music often being consumed as background noise ("hearing" rather than "listening") to fill silence.
**Lean Back vs. Lean Forward Listening** : "Lean back listening" describes the passive consumption of music (e.g., clicking play and letting algorithms dictate), contrasting with "lean forward listening," which involves actively seeking out, absorbing, studying, and appreciating music.
**Wholesale Devaluation of Music** : The most significant development in music this century is identified as the wholesale devaluation of music, transforming it from a relatively expensive commodity (CDs at $10-20) to a nearly free and oversupplied digital product.
**Consumer Willingness to Pay** : Despite complaints about low artist payouts from streaming, many users of free tiers are unwilling to pay more for music (less than $20/month for premium services), indicating a low perceived monetary value.
**"Skip Button" Culture on Streaming** : Streaming fosters a "skip button" culture, with data indicating high skip rates for unfamiliar new songs: 25% skipped in the first 5 seconds, 29% within 10 seconds, and 35% within 30 seconds. This lack of investment contributes to devaluation.

## curiosities
**12-Year Pop and Rock Cycle** : For over 50 years, dating back to the early 1950s (birth date estimated around 1951), rock and pop popularity fluctuated in an inverse 12-year cycle. Rock ascended from 1951-1957, then declined until 1964 (Beatles). It surged again until 1970, then faded for pop. It returned with punk in 1976, faded 1982-83 (new pop stars). Another comeback in 1990 with alternative/grunge until 1996, then pop returned, and rock was declared "dead" until 2000-2001. Remained dominant until 2008, then fell off.
**Death of 12-Year Cycle** : This cycle, which should have seen a rock revival after 2014, died. This is attributed to streaming and the decline of traditional gatekeepers (radio, record stores, labels, music magazines, cable music video channels), leading to a fragmented, non-monocultural music landscape.
**Waking from Slumber** : Regressive, reactionary Republican administrations are noted to historically "wake rock from its slumber," though this did not happen after Donald Trump's election in 2016, further indicating the death of the 12-year cycle.

# curiosity

**Human Brain Music Memory Capacity** : The average adult brain has a capacity of about 2.5 petabytes (2.5 million gigs), with special areas dedicated to storing musical memories and lyrics.
**Memory Corruption and Forgetting** : The human brain is subject to memories fading or becoming corrupted over time due to new experiences or circumstances, making eyewitness accounts often unreliable.
**"F4 Refresh" Mental Maintenance** : The concept of performing "maintenance" on the brain's "hard drive" by revisiting history and past experiences is likened to repeatedly pressing F4 on a keyboard for a refresh.
**Alan Cross Podcast Series** : The program, "The ongoing history of New Music Podcasts with Alan Cross," is part 10 of a series titled "100 greatest rock moments of the millennium so far."
**Subjectivity of Music Rankings** : Ranking music moments by importance, influence, or quality is inherently subjective, allowing for significant argument and dissent.
**First 25 Years of the 21st Century Focus** : The list specifically ranks the 10 most important events in music during the first 25 years of the millennium.
**Decline of Traditional Gatekeepers** : The decline in influence of radio, record stores, record labels, music magazines, and cable music video channels is cited as a reason for the death of the 12-year pop and rock cycle.
**Monoculture End** : The internet and streaming have ended the monoculture in music that previously created universal listening trends, leading to a fragmented landscape with more genres and diverse consumption.
**Modern Music Piracy Origins (Early 1990s)** : While music piracy has a centuries-long history, modern music piracy began in the early 1990s and was "out of control" by January 1, 2000.
**Consumer Grievances Leading to Piracy (Early 2000s)** : Music consumers were motivated to engage in piracy due to high CD prices, the phasing out of CD singles, and the perception that albums often contained only one or two good songs.
**CD Sales Collapse (2000s)** : Global CD sales, which peaked at approximately 2.5 billion in 2000, crashed by over 75% within the next decade due to widespread music piracy and file sharing.
**Music Industry Financial Crisis (2001-2014)** : The music industry experienced massive revenue declines, job losses, artist roster cuts, and destroyed label-retailer relationships, with revenues dropping every year from 2001 to 2014.
**Record Labels' Technological Ignorance (2000s)** : In 2000, most record label employees and executives lacked understanding of new digital technology, with many not even having computers on their desks.
**Antitrust Laws and Label-Owned Stores** : Antitrust laws, combined with labels already under scrutiny for a CD price-fixing scheme, prevented major labels from jointly creating a single online music store.
**iTunes Music Store Launch (April 2003)** : Launched in April 2003 (Mac only initially, then Windows in October), it allowed legal digital music purchases, becoming a crucial step in solving the piracy problem.
**iPod Sales Phenomenon** : The iPod became the fastest-selling consumer electronic product of its time, driven by the ability to fill it with legally purchased songs from the iTunes Music Store.
**Apple's Resurgence and Market Cap** : The success of the iPod and iTunes saved Apple, transforming it into a technological powerhouse, with its stock price soaring from 32 cents in 2003 to 597 cents in 2007, eventually reaching a market cap of approximately $3.5 trillion.
**Streaming Revenue Overtakes Physical Sales (2016)** : In 2016, as streaming became more mainstream, it surpassed physical sales (CDs and vinyl) in terms of revenue.
**MySpace and Early Social Media Music (Episode 1)** : The debut of MySpace was highlighted as the beginning of the significant intersection of social media and music in episode one of this series.
**YouTube's Impact on Music (Episode 8)** : The massive impact of YouTube on music was the dedicated topic of episode eight of this series.
**Current Social Media Platforms for Music** : X, Threads, Blue Sky, TikTok, Instagram Reels, and YouTube Shorts are identified as current platforms facilitating songs going viral.
**Algorithms and Viral Trends** : Social media algorithms detect and push trending sounds globally, enabling even old, forgotten, or obscure songs to quickly achieve global hit status.
**Artist Empowerment through Social Media** : Social media empowers artists to reach global audiences and build fan bases without needing traditional record labels, marketing, or promotion.
**Direct Artist-Fan Connections** : Platforms like SoundCloud, Bandcamp, Spotify, Apple Music, and Amazon Music enable artists to establish direct connections with their fan bases.
**Social Media Marketing Strategies** : Artists use social media for dance trends, memes, rollouts, trailers, teasers, collaborations, and interactive campaigns.
**Live Streaming for Fan Engagement** : Instagram Live, TikTok Live, and Twitch provide artists with platforms for live-streamed performances, interviews, and highly personal interactions with fans.
**Negative Effects of Social Media on Music Consumption** : Social media encourages short attention-span content consumption, favoring playlists over albums, leading to shorter songs and a greater emphasis on hooks (termed "snippet culture").
**Social Media Pressure on Artists** : Labels often demand a constant stream of social media posts and pressure artists to regularly go viral, contributing to quick burnout.
**A&R Shift to Social Media Scouting** : Traditional A&R (Artist and Repertoire) scouting, which involved attending clubs and reviewing demos, has been augmented and often replaced by interns monitoring social media for emerging artists.
**"Flattening of Music" via Social Media** : Social media's tendency to favor certain styles leads to trends being chased by many artists, resulting in periods where much music sounds similar.
**COVID-19 Pandemic Impact on Live Music (2020-2022)** : The pandemic caused a two-year halt in concerts, festivals, and live music, severely impacting artist finances and venue operations.
**Rise of Virtual Events During COVID-19** : The absence of live music led to a surge in virtual concerts and controlled live-stream events utilizing platforms like YouTube, Twitch, Zoom, Instagram Live, and TikTok.
**TikTok's Explosion During COVID-19** : TikTok experienced an explosion in popularity as a primary platform for new music discovery during the pandemic lockdowns.
**New Artist Revenue Models (COVID-19)** : Artists turned to crowdfunding, intensified merchandise sales, and platforms like Patreon to generate revenue during the pandemic.
**Streaming Service User Spikes (COVID-19)** : Spotify, Apple Music, and YouTube saw significant increases in users as people consumed more music at home during lockdowns.
**"COVID Music" Era** : The pandemic inspired an era of "COVID music," with many songs reflecting themes of isolation, alienation, lockdowns, and resilience.
**Home Music Production Surge (COVID-19)** : Thousands of individuals began producing music at home during lockdowns, contributing to a "flattening of music" as many relied on the same software and plugins.
**Hybrid Music Experiences (Post-COVID-19)** : The pandemic spurred the development of new hybrid models blending virtual and live music experiences.
**NFTs and Direct-to-Fan Models (COVID-19)** : The rise of NFTs (non-fungible tokens) is identified as a direct result of new direct-to-fan models accelerated by the pandemic.
**Post-COVID Concert Attendance Boom** : Following the lifting of restrictions, people flocked to live music events in record numbers.
**Post-COVID Concert Ticket Price Inflation** : Concert ticket prices significantly increased due to post-COVID inflation affecting all sectors, including the music business.
**Streaming as Dominant Industry Revenue (Current)** : Currently, streaming accounts for at least 75% of all record label revenue.
**Spotify User Base and Valuation** : Spotify boasts 625 million monthly users and is valued at $125 billion.
**Vastness of Streaming Libraries** : Approximately 202 million songs are available for streaming, with an estimated 100,000 new songs uploaded daily.
**Low Streaming Payouts for Artists** : Streaming platforms generally pay artists significantly less per play than traditional physical sales, with payouts often comparable to radio plays, despite global reach.
**Economic Inequality in Streaming Era** : The new economic realities of streaming have created a "thin layer" of highly successful artists atop millions of struggling ones.
**Streaming's Influence on Song Composition** : To ensure payment (which typically begins after 30 seconds of play), song composition has evolved to include shorter intros, immediate choruses, and numerous hooks within the first 30 seconds to capture listener attention.
**Streaming's Impact on Music Consumption Habits** : The ease and low cost of streaming have led to music often being consumed as background noise ("hearing" rather than "listening") to fill silence.
**Lean Back vs. Lean Forward Listening** : "Lean back listening" describes the passive consumption of music (e.g., clicking play and letting algorithms dictate), contrasting with "lean forward listening," which involves actively seeking out, absorbing, studying, and appreciating music.
**Wholesale Devaluation of Music** : The most significant development in music this century is identified as the wholesale devaluation of music, transforming it from a relatively expensive commodity (CDs at $10-20) to a nearly free and oversupplied digital product.
**Consumer Willingness to Pay** : Despite complaints about low artist payouts from streaming, many users of free tiers are unwilling to pay more for music (less than $20/month for premium services), indicating a low perceived monetary value.
**"Skip Button" Culture on Streaming** : Streaming fosters a "skip button" culture, with data indicating high skip rates for unfamiliar new songs: 25% skipped in the first 5 seconds, 29% within 10 seconds, and 35% within 30 seconds. This lack of investment contributes to devaluation.
