# instrument - Record Groove
## curiosities
**Living Stereo Groove Technology (Late 1950s)** : "Living Stereo" records featured a revolutionary V-shaped groove, with each side cut at a 45-degree angle. This design allowed for two separate and distinct soundtracks to be impressed into a single groove. During playback, a specialized stereo pickup with two ceramic bars would read the impressions from both sides simultaneously, reproducing the sound from the left and right sections of an orchestra through separate speakers. "Two separate and distinct sound tracks. One from one side of the orchestra or one speaker system. Another track from the other side of the orchestra for the second speaker system. Yet both are coming from one record, one groove, picked up by one stylus."

## curiosities
**Koss SP3s (1958)** : The world's first stereo headphones, invented in 1958 by jazz musician John Koss and engineer Martin Lang Jr. Originally conceived as an accessory for a portable turntable, the SP3s quickly became the star attraction at audio shows, revolutionizing personal listening by introducing stereo sound directly to the ears. "These first headphones were the Koss SP3s, and frankly they weren't supposed to be that big of a deal. They were simply an accessory for a portable turntable, but whenever one saw and heard them at an audio show, all people wanted were the headphones."
**Evolution of Headphone Types** : Over several decades, headphones have evolved into distinct categories. Circum-aural headphones, the original stereo type, feature large pads that fully enclose the ears. Supra-aural headphones have pads that rest directly on top of the ears. Earbuds, a more recent development, are designed to be worn inside the ear canal. "The first and original stereo headphones are called Circum Oral. That means the ears fit inside two big pads. Super oral headphones have pads that fit against the ears. They just kind of ride on top of your ears. And then we have earbuds, which are worn inside the ear canal."
**Headphone Technologies** : A diverse range of technologies are employed in modern headphones to produce sound. These include moving coil, electrostatic, electric, planar magnetic, and thermoacoustic principles, among others. Depending on a user's budget, it is possible to achieve "exquisitely good" sound quality with current headphone offerings. "There are a variety of technologies used by headphones, moving coil, electrostatic, electric, planar magnetic, thermochoustic, and a few others."
**Stereo Experience Debate** : A debate exists regarding whether headphones and earbuds truly provide a proper stereo experience comparable to listening through external speakers. Unlike speakers, where the brain can analyze incoming audio to construct a spatial soundstage with height, depth, and distance, headphones deliver audio discretely and separately to each ear. This can lead to a diminished or absent perceived center of the stereo image, instruments appearing in only one ear, or sounds seemingly coming from behind, as the brain struggles to spatially locate the audio. "There is a debate, however, with all headphones and all earbuds. Do they offer a proper stereo experience?"

**Tiatrophone (1800s)** : An early two-channel radio system from the 1800s, described as an audio-only precursor to cable TV. It provided two-channel audio but became obsolete and went out of business with the advent of wireless radio in the 1920s. "Two channel radio goes all the way back to the 1800s with something called the Tiatrophone, which was basically like cable TV with just audio."
**FM Radio Invention (1930s)** : FM radio was invented in the 1930s by Edwin Howard Armstrong. Despite its invention, it struggled to gain significant adoption and public traction until the late 1950s. "FM radio was invented in the 1930s by Edwin Howard Armstrong, but really didn't gain any traction until sometime in the late 1950s."
**Multiplexing for FM Stereo (Late 1950s - Early 1960s)** : Multiplexing was the technique that ultimately resolved the challenges of broadcasting stereo radio signals. It involved combining left and right channels at the source, transmitting them via a single transmitter, and then allowing a receiver to separate the signal back into discrete left and right channels. This system was also mono-compatible. After extensive testing in the late 1950s and early 1960s, the closely related multiplex systems proposed by General Electric and Zenith were adopted as the industry standard, forming the basis for modern FM stereo broadcasts. "This involved combining left and right channels from a source, using one transmitter to beam out the signal, and then having a receiver separate the signal back into left and right channels."
**First FM Stereo Broadcasts (Year Unknown)** : The first two radio stations known to broadcast in the modern form of FM stereo using multiplexing technology were W E FM in Chicago and W FGM in Schenectady, New York. "As far as we can tell, the first two stations to broadcast in this modern form of FM was W E FM in Chicago and W FGM in Schenectady, New York."
**Mono Button on FM Receivers** : Many modern FM receivers still include a "mono" button. This feature is designed to address situations where a stereo FM signal is weak, allowing the listener to switch the broadcast down to mono to eliminate distortion and improve listenability. "That's which is a weak stereo FM signal down to mono, so you can listen without distortion."

**AM Stereo Development (Late 1970s)** : In the late 1970s, efforts were made to popularize AM radio by introducing stereo capabilities, in response to FM's increasing listenership. This development process was contentious, characterized by lawsuits and political disputes, eventually leading to two main competing systems: the ISB system and Motorola's CQAM system. "The development process was a mess devolving into a series of lawsuits and political squabbles."
**Motorola CQAM System Dominance (1984-1992)** : Motorola's CQAM system achieved global market dominance for AM stereo. It was adopted by GM, Ford, and Chrysler cars starting in 1984. International adoption followed, with Australian stations beginning in 1985, Canada and Mexico in 1988, and Japan in 1992. While it offered better sound than traditional mono AM, it wasn't as good as FM, and its widespread adoption was hindered by the need for stations and listeners to invest in new, compatible equipment. "This gave Motorola's CQAM system total market dominance worldwide."
**Quadrphonic Sound Concept (1970s)** : Quadraphonic sound emerged in the 1970s as an attempt to enhance realism in music reproduction beyond two-channel stereo. The concept aimed to replicate the way sound arrives from all directions in the real world, including delayed reflections, by using four speakers (front left, front right, rear left, rear right) to create a more immersive listening experience. "To reproduce this realism, you need more than two speakers. And this is where we encounter something called quadrphonic sound."
**RCA Quad-8 Tapes (1970)** : In 1970, RCA introduced "Quad-8" quadraphonic 8-track tapes. These tapes were ultimately unsuccessful due to their poor quality and were withdrawn from the market by 1976. "In 1970, RCA introduced a quad-8-track tape they called the Quad-8. But they weren't very good and disappeared from the marketplace by 1976."
**Competing Quadraphonic Record Formats (1970s)** : The development of quadraphonic sound for vinyl records involved many companies like Sony, Columbia Records, and EMI, who agreed on systems like SQ. However, numerous other incompatible formats such as QS, EB4, Quadru disk, UD4, and USQ emerged. This fragmentation created a dilemma for consumers, who had to choose a system without knowing which would become the industry standard, often requiring special cartridges, amps, and four matching speakers. "But they were competing systems, QS, EB4, Quadru disk, UD4, USQ, and a few others."
**Quadrasonic Demonstration Disk (1973)** : A demonstration disk issued in 1973 showcased the capabilities of quadraphonic sound. The demo included varied soundscapes, such as a race course, humpback whales off Bermuda, and Broadway performances, to illustrate the immersive experience. "For distinct channels from an LP, you can play with a standard stereo cartridge. And whatever the sounds are, whatever the source, you the listener are in the middle of its natural environment."

**Quadraphonic Sound Commercial Failure (Late 1970s)** : Quadraphonic sound ultimately failed commercially by the end of the 1970s. Its demise was attributed to several factors: the high cost of acquiring the necessary equipment, the confusion caused by numerous competing and incompatible systems, and the resulting scarcity of music available in the quad format. "But in the end, Quadro Fonic Sound was a total commercial failure."
**TV/FM Simulcast (Pre-1980s)** : Before stereo television became standard, a common workaround for listening to stereo audio for TV programs was the TV/FM simulcast. TV stations would partner with local FM stations to broadcast the stereo soundtrack of a concert or other event. Viewers would then mute their TV and listen to the stereo audio through their radio, providing a superior sound experience compared to the TV's small, mono speaker. "The idea was for people to turn down their TVs and turn up their radios."
**WTTW Telesonics Stereo TV Experiments (1976)** : Proper stereo television broadcasting began with experiments conducted in 1976 by the public station WTTW in Chicago, utilizing a system called Telesonics. These successful experiments laid the groundwork for technical standards that were eventually adopted by the wider industry. "Proper stereo TV began with some experiments in Chicago with the public station WTTW using a system called Telesonics in 1976."
**First Network Stereo TV Broadcast (July 26, 1984)** : The Tonight Show Starring Johnny Carson was the first network TV show to be broadcast in stereo, on July 26, 1984. This initial broadcast was limited to New York, as it was the only affiliate at the time that had acquired and installed the necessary stereo broadcasting equipment. "As far as I can tell, the first network TV show to be broadcast in stereo was, the tonight show with Johnny Carson, July 26, 1984, but only in New York because that was the only affiliate that had purchased and installed the new stereo broadcasting equipment."
**Evolution of Video Formats for Stereo Audio** : The transition to stereo television audio was complemented by advancements in video playback formats. Early VCRs, initially mono-only, eventually became stereo-capable. Subsequently, laserdiscs emerged, resembling 12-inch CDs, which offered digital video alongside digital audio, paving the way for more advanced home theater systems. "VCRs, which were only capable of mono at first, went stereo, then came laser disks, which looked like CDs, but were 12 inches in diameter, and contained digital video as well as audio."
**Modern Surround Sound Systems** : Contemporary television sets are equipped with circuitry to decode a variety of surround sound systems, including Dolby Surround, Dolby Atmos, and DTS. These systems extend beyond two-channel stereo, typically starting with a minimum of three channels (left, right, center) and expanding into configurations like 5.1 (five speakers plus a subwoofer), 6.1, 7.1, 9.2, 10.2, and even 22.2, offering increasingly immersive audio experiences depending on the investment. "TVs became equipped with new circuitry that decoded a number of what were called surround sound systems, and today we have Dolby Surround, Dolby Atmos, DTS, and so on."
**MP3 Impact on Audio Quality (1990s)** : The introduction of the MP3 in the 1990s marked a significant shift in audio consumption. While digital formats were initially promoted for greater accuracy, MP3s, despite being in stereo, "dumbed down" audio quality by stripping away considerable information to create smaller file sizes. The convenience of these files, often played on computers with subpar speakers, outweighed the loss in fidelity for many users. "These music files were in stereo, but because the encoding algorithm stripped away so much information in order to make the files smaller, they just didn't sound as good as uncompressed files."
**Smart Speakers and Audio Quality (2020s)** : Listening to music through a single smart speaker does not provide a true stereo experience, which requires two separate sound sources for realism. Even the best single smart speaker offers an audio experience comparable to a mono FM table radio from 1965, representing a regression in audio quality despite the convenience. A stereo system from 1979 would typically provide better audio than a smart speaker purchased in 2020. "Listening to music even through the best smart speaker gives you at best the same experience as someone listening to a mono FM table radio back in 1965."
**Headphone Origins (Late 19th Century)** : The lineage of modern headphones can be traced back to the earpieces utilized by telephone operators in the late 19th century. Early applications included radio operators in the military, ship personnel, and radio hobbyists who required focused listening for faint signals. This technology later evolved for use in hearing aids—notably the first transistorized devices—and the earliest transistor radios in the 1950s, though these were mono and often single-ear. "Everything we use today is descended from earpieces worn by telephone operators beginning in the late 19th century."
**Invention of First Stereo Headphones (1958)** : The first stereo headphones were invented in 1958 by Milwaukee-based jazz musician John Koss, in collaboration with engineer Martin Lang Jr. This innovation coincided with the emergence of the first proper stereo recordings, reel-to-reel tapes, and LPs, ushering in the boom of home high-fidelity equipment. The initial model, the Koss SP3s, was designed as a turntable accessory but became a standalone sensation at audio shows. "It wasn't until 1958 when a jazz musician from Milwaukee named John Koss, together with an engineer named Martin Lang Jr, invented the first stereo headphones."
