## albums
**Ramones (1976) – Debut Album** : Recorded from February 2nd to February 19th, 1976, at Plaza City Sound, a large studio on the 8th floor of Radio City Music Hall. The total cost for recording was $6,400, which was $400 more than their advance. When the master was sent to the record plant, it was so loud that it blew a cutter head right off the machine. The album was released on April 23rd, 1976. Its cover photo, featuring the band against a brick wall, is now considered an all-time rock and roll classic, taken by photographer Roberta Bailey near CBGB. Critical reception was mixed, and almost no radio station would play it, but it became highly influential in the UK. The album finally sold over 500,000 copies in early 2014, earning a gold record status. "The cover photo of the band's Denny against a brick wall was now considered to be an all-time rock and roll classic."
**Ramones Leave Home (1977) – Second Album** : Released on January 10th, 1977. Tommy Ramone served as co-producer alongside Tony Bon Jovi, who is the cousin of John Bon Jovi. While considered "a little more poppy" than their debut, it was still far from mainstream pop. "It was a little more poppy than the first one, but this isn't exactly mainstream pop."
**Rocket to Russia (1977) – Third Album** : Released on November 4th, 1977, just 11 months after *Ramones Leave Home*, completing a streak of three albums in 18 months. Tommy Ramone co-produced this album as well. Its release coincided with the peak of punk rock, making it a "seriously important record" for the time. Johnny Ramone was highly motivated by the Sex Pistols' "God Save the Queen," feeling they had "ripped off the Ramone sound." The album features iconic songs that would forever be staples in the Ramones' setlist, including "Rockaway Beach," "Sheena Is a Punk Rocker," and "Cretin Hop (We're a Happy Family)." This album marked Tommy Ramone's last as the band's drummer. "A fine record, but it would be the last with drummer Tommy Ramone."

## songs
**Rockaway Beach (1977)** : This song is featured on the Ramones' third album, *Rocket to Russia*, which was released on November 4th, 1977. It was performed by the band's original classic lineup and became one of the tracks that would forever be a part of the Ramones' live setlist. "Rockaway Beach from their third album, Rocket to Russia, released on November 4th, 1977, and featuring the original classic lineup of the band."
**I Don't Care (Early 1970s)** : This song was originally written by Joey Ramone (under the name Jeff Starship) for his first band, Sniper, which was active from 1972 to 1974. Joey carried the song with him when the Ramones were formed, and it became part of their repertoire. "The remones were the song carried over from the time Joey was the singer in a band called Sniper."
**I Don't Want To Get Involved With You (1974)** : This was the very first song that the Ramones wrote together as a band. It set a trend for their early songwriting, often starting with the negative phrase "I don't want to." "The first song they wrote together was, I don't want to get involved with you."
**I Don't Want To Walk Around With You (1974)** : Following their initial collaborative effort, this was the second song written by the Ramones. It continued the pattern of songs beginning with the negative phrase "I don't want to." "The second was, I don't want to walk around with you."
**I Don't Want To Be Learned, I Don't Want To Be Tamed (1974)** : This song was part of a sequence of early Ramones compositions that adopted a consistent naming convention, following "I Don't Want To Walk Around With You" in their songwriting process. "That was followed by, I don't want to be learned, I don't want to be tamed."
**I Don't Want To Go Down To The Basement (1974)** : This track holds the distinction of being the fourth song the Ramones ever wrote together as a band. It later found its place on their self-titled debut album. "The fourth song that Ramon's ever wrote together and a track from their debut album."
**I Wanna Sniff Some Glue (Early 1970s)** : This song was the Ramones' first "positive" song, if it could be called that, after a series of compositions starting with "I don't want to." It famously has a running time of only 94 seconds, embodying the band's philosophy of "never believing in belaboring the point." "Now I want to sniff some glue, running time 94 seconds."
**Beat On The Brat (1976)** : The inspiration for this punk anthem came from an incident Joey Ramone witnessed in the playground outside his apartment building, Birchwood Towers. He observed a "crazy, undisciplined child" from a rich family disrupting others and mentally wished he had a baseball bat. The song eventually appeared on their 1976 debut record. "I wonder if that kid knows he inspired a punk anthem? Probably not."
**53rd & 3rd (1976)** : Written by Dee Dee Ramone, this song is a stark account of his experiences "turning tricks for drugs" in a specific area of New York City. The track was included on the Ramones' self-titled debut record, offering a glimpse into the darker realities of his life. "53rd and 3rd, written by D.D. Ramone about the days he spent turning tricks for drugs in that part of New York."
**Teenage Lobotomy (1977)** : Featured on the *Rocket to Russia* album, this song led off side two of the original vinyl release. It contributes to the Ramones' recurring theme of mental illness in their song catalog. "What would a Ramones album be without a couple of songs about mental illness? That's teenage lobotomy from Rocket to Russia."
**Song about being on the road (1978)** : Joey Ramone began writing this song after suffering second and third-degree burns from a makeshift humidifier backstage at the Capitol Theater, an incident that occurred before he had to play a show. The song was about the relentless touring schedule, specifically "having to be on the road, 365 days a year," and later appeared on the band's fourth album. "A little later, Joey started writing a song that would end up on the band's fourth album, a song about having to be on the road, 365 days a year."

## curiosities
**First Public Performance (CBGB)** : On Friday, August 16, 1974, four guys in leather jackets, t-shirts, torn jeans, and Converse high-tops got up on the tiny stage at CBGB. Ligs McNeil remembered their sound as "just this wall of noise" and noted their striking, non-hippie appearance. They played their entire repertoire in just 15 minutes, with arguments about song order and broken guitar strings, scaring the owner's dog. "15 minutes after their set started, it was over. They have blown through all their songs."
**CBGB Owner's Reaction to First Gig** : After their debut 15-minute set at CBGB, the owner expressed skepticism about their appeal but promised to have them back. The band returned the following night and continued to play there frequently, totaling 74 gigs by the end of 1974. "When it was over, the owner said to the band, listen, nobody's going to like you. But you know what, I'll have you back."
**Influence on Rock Music** : The Ramones are credited with profoundly influencing countless rock bands over several decades, with a direct lineage tracing back to their original version for bands like Rise Against, Metallica, Blink 182, Motorhead, Green Day, Guns N' Roses, The Clash, Sex Pistols, Rancid, The Pixies, The Beastie Boys, Alice in Chains, Pearl Jam, Soundgarden, The Strokes, The Offspring, and Red Hot Chili Peppers. "virtually any rock band of the last 25 years, 35 years, and you can draw a line back into the past, and that line will inevitably lead back to the Ramones."
**Limited Commercial Success** : Despite their immense influence, the Ramones achieved only two gold records in their 22-year career. One was a greatest hits compilation, and their self-titled debut album only surpassed the 500,000 sales mark in early 2014. They were "really annoyed at the band's lack of commercial success," with almost no radio airplay. "Only two Ramones albums have ever sold more than 500,000 copies."
**Financial Struggles** : The band struggled financially despite constant touring and merchandise sales, earning only $750 to $1,000 per show. Each member was living on a mere $125 per week, with Dee Dee Ramone famously spending $100 of that on heroin. "Each member of the band was living on a hundred and twenty five dollars a week."
**Joey Ramone's Marfan Syndrome** : Jeffrey Hyman (Joey Ramone) suffered from Marfan Syndrome, an inherited disorder affecting connective tissue, leading to his tall, gangly physique with long limbs and fingers. This condition can also cause serious heart problems and, if untreated, typically limits life expectancy to 32 years. "Young Jeffrey grew up with all sorts of health problems. He was tall and gauky as the result of something called Marfan Syndrome, which is an inherited disorder living on chromosome 15 that messes with connective tissue."
**Joey Ramone's OCD** : Diagnosed with OCD at age 18, Joey Ramone's life was plagued by the disorder. This manifested in specific rituals, such as needing to step on and off a curb a certain number of times before moving on, and elaborate routines required before leaving his apartment. "For example, he sometimes had to step on and off a curb a prescribed number of times before he could move on."
**Joey Ramone's Early Glam Phase** : Around his 21st birthday, Joey became deeply immersed in glam culture, idolizing David Bowie and Alice Cooper. He would dress in satin jumpsuits adorned with rhinestones and wear knee-high platform boots, which exaggerated his height to six feet eight inches. "He also became a big fan of Alice Cooper. His first real band was called Sniper."
**Johnny Ramone's Discipline and Baseball Fandom** : Johnny Cummings (Johnny Ramone) came from a blue-collar background and attended military schools, instilling in him a strong sense of discipline. He was a lifelong, fervent fan of the New York Yankees, with stories of him having a TV set up just off stage to watch games while performing gigs. "His parents hoped that he turned out to be a pro baseball player. And there were stories about how when there was a big game on, Johnny would have a TV set up just off stage so he could play the gig and watch the game at the same time."
**Johnny Ramone and The Beatles** : An anecdote recounts Johnny Ramone attending The Beatles' concert at Shea Stadium on August 23, 1966, armed with a bag of rocks, which he then threw at the band. "there's another story that says that when the Beatles played Shay Stadium in August 23, 1966, Johnny went to the show with a big bag of rocks, which she then proceeded to throw at the band."
**Dee Dee Ramone's Difficult Background** : Douglas Colvin (Dee Dee Ramone) grew up as an "army brat" in a profoundly unhappy military family, where domestic violence was common. After living on various American military bases in Germany, he moved to New York, where he descended into drug use and resorted to prostitution to fund his addiction. "Douglas was an army brat from a very unhappy military family where domestic violence was the order of the day."
**Dee Dee Ramone's Mental Health and Mood Swings** : Dee Dee Ramone was reportedly diagnosed as bipolar but chose not to seek treatment. He was known as a "very scary dude prone to huge mood swings." "There's one story that he was diagnosed as being bipolar, but he didn't seek any treatment for it."
**Tommy Ramone's Early Career and Production Role** : Tommy Erdelyi (Tommy Ramone) escaped communism as a child, fleeing Hungary with his parents to New York. He was a skilled guitarist and, significantly, worked as a recording engineer at The Record Plant, where he notably worked with Jimi Hendrix. This experience led him to produce the Ramones' first three albums, and later co-produced their fourth and eighth albums. "Not only did he play guitar, but he also found a very sweet gig working as a recording engineer in a studio called the Record Plant. One of the very first people he got to work for was Jimmy Hendricks."
**Formation of the Band** : The four original members connected through a series of introductions: Jeffrey Hyman (Joey) met Douglas Colvin (Dee Dee) at a Sniper show; Douglas introduced Jeffrey to John Cummings (Johnny); and Johnny introduced them to Tommy Erdelyi (Tommy), who already knew Joey from the neighborhood. Tommy suggested they form a band, offering to manage them, which they did. "It was Tommy that told the other three guys that they should form a band, all advise and manage. And so they did."
**Early Rehearsals and Lineup Changes** : The Ramones' first rehearsal took place at Johnny's apartment with bassist Richie Stern, who quickly quit. A more formal rehearsal followed on January 24th, 1974, at the Performance Studio, with Jeffrey on drums, Johnny on guitar, and Douglas on lead vocals and bass. They were initially "terrible," unable to play their instruments well or covers, only knowing their own simple songs. Douglas quickly lost his voice, and Jeffrey, a loud but slow drummer, moved to vocals, leading to Tommy eventually taking over drums in July 1974. "They were not ready, not even close. They had next to no idea how to play their instruments."
**Origin of the Band Name** : Douglas Colvin, a huge Paul McCartney fan, knew McCartney sometimes used the alias "Paul Ramon" (after producer Phil Ramon) on hotel registers. Douglas adopted "D.D. Ramon." The plural, "Ramones," sounded like a gang, and as no other names clicked, it stuck. To reinforce a "gang mentality," all members adopted "Ramone" as their surname, deciding to appear as brothers, and wore leather jackets, jeans, and t-shirts. "Douglas liked the sound of that, so he started calling himself D.D. Ramon... The plural of Ramon, Ramon's, sounded like it could be the name of a gang."
**Joey's Name Change** : Jeffrey Hyman decided to change his stage name to Joey Ramone, believing it sounded "much cooler" than Jeff Ramon, further cementing the band's unified identity. "The last change came when Jeffrey decided that he'd rather go by Joey. Joey Ramon just sounded so much cooler than Jeff Ramon."
**Early Songwriting Themes** : The Ramones began writing songs about what they knew, which largely involved being "near-to-wells" and "hoodlums" who were better at "hanging out, drinking, getting in fist fights, and doing drugs" than actual crime. Their early songs often began with the phrase "I don't want to," such as "I Don't Want To Get Involved With You," "I Don't Want To Walk Around With You," "I Don't Want To Be Learned, I Don't Want To Be Tamed," and "I Don't Want To Go Down To The Basement." "They started to write about what they knew, which was, well what exactly? Well, they were all a bunch of near-to-wells, a bunch of hoodlums."
**Music Style Template** : The Ramones' musical template was characterized by loud, fast, and fuzzy sounds, essentially "amped up 60 style pop songs." Their style featured simple, sing-along melodies, no overt political messages, no guitar solos, and strict adherence to songs under three minutes in length. "But if you really pay attention, there's nothing more than really amped up 60 style pop songs."
**UK Debut and Impact** : On July 4th, 1976, while America celebrated its Bicentennial, the Ramones played second on the bill at the Roundhouse in North London. This event is widely credited with doing "more to kick start British punk than anything else that had happened up until that point." "That event did more to kick start British punk than anything else that had happened up until that point."
**UK Reception Compared to US** : Upon their first visit to England in 1976, the Ramones were treated "like Royal Teal," playing to 3,000 enthusiastic fans who were already "tuned in" to their sound, a stark contrast to their smaller, earlier gigs at CBGB in New York. "we were like treated like Royal Teal we got there."
**Tommy Ramone's Departure** : Tommy Ramone played his final show with the band on New Year's Eve 1977. He decided to leave his position as drummer to focus on his career in the recording studio, frustrated by the band's lack of commercial success, zero radio airplay, and constant touring demands. He also grew depressed with "idiot fans" and issues with Johnny's strict discipline. "Tommy got very depressed at the situation. And at some of the idiot fans that were starting to dominate gigs, there were issues with Johnny and his hardcore discipline style, something that Tommy didn't really appreciate."
**The Teapot Incident** : Backstage at the Capitol Theater in Passaic, New Jersey, Joey Ramone, suffering from a cold, rigged a makeshift humidifier using a teapot on a hot plate in the dressing room. The teapot "ended up blowing up in Joey's face," causing second and third-degree burns. Despite his injuries, he was rushed to the hospital for treatment and then returned to the venue to perform the show. "Somehow it ended up blowing up in Joey's face and engsculting water all over him."
**Final Gig and Legacy** : The Ramones' official end came on August 6, 1996, after a gig at The Palace in Hollywood. Over their 22-year career, they played 2,236 gigs since their first performance as a trio in March 1974. They are often called the "Johnny Apple Seeds of Rock" for inspiring thousands of new bands wherever they played. "until the official end of the band on August 6, 1996 after a gig at the palace in Hollywood. 22 years and 2,236 gigs since that first performance as a trio in March of 1974."
**Original Members' Last Reunion** : The four original members of the Ramones were last together on July 20, 1999, when they made an appearance for an autograph session at the Virgin Megastore in Times Square. "The last time they were all together was on July 20, 1999, when they appeared for an autograph session at the Virgin Megastore in Times Square."
**Joey Ramone's Death and Memorial** : Diagnosed with lymphoma in 1995, Joey Ramone's cancer worsened after he broke his hip in a fall in late 2000, which necessitated coming off his chemotherapy. He died on April 15, 2001, just one month before his 50th birthday. The corner of East Second Street and Bowery, near CBGB and his last apartment, is now officially named "Joey Ramone Place." He is buried in Hillside Cemetery in New Jersey, overlooking Manhattan. "he died on April 15, 2001, a month short of his 50th birthday. The corner of East Second Street in Bahri is now officially known as Joey Ramon Place."
**Dee Dee Ramone's Death and Memorial** : Dee Dee Ramone, who left the band in 1989 to pursue a solo career, play with other bands, and write a book, was found dead of a heroin overdose by his wife at his Hollywood apartment on June 5, 2002. He was 50 years old and is buried in the Hollywood Forever Cemetery. "On June 5, 2002, he was found dead of a heroin overdose by his wife at his department in Hollywood."
**Johnny Ramone's Death and Memorial** : Johnny Ramone died on October 8, 2004, at the age of 55, after a five-year battle with prostate cancer. He was cremated, but an eight-foot bronze statue of him stands in Hollywood Forever Cemetery, not far from Dee Dee's grave. "He died on October 8, 2004, after suffering from prostate cancer for five years. He was 55."
**Tommy Ramone's Death and Later Career** : After leaving the Ramones, Tommy Ramone co-produced two more of their albums (*Road to Ruin* and *Too Tough to Die*) and produced albums for bands like The Replacements and Red Cross, before forming the bluegrass folk band Uncle Monk. He was diagnosed with bile duct cancer, which spread throughout his body despite treatment, and he died in hospice care on July 11, 2014, at the age of 65. "Tommy was diagnosed with bile duct cancer and spread throughout his body despite treatment. And he died at hospice care on July 11, 2014 at the age of 65."

# genre - Punk Rock

## curiosities
**Emergence in the UK** : When the Ramones' debut album was released in April 1976, punk was nascent in the UK, described as "stirring" but not yet "coalesced into anything firm." The Ramones' performance at the Roundhouse in North London on July 4, 1976, is credited with doing "more to kick start British punk than anything else that had happened up until that point." "Punk was stirring, but it really hadn't coalesced into anything firm."
**New York Times Description** : A music critic for the New York Times described the Ramones as "a highly stylized extension of the punk idiom," noting their "non-stop set of brisk, monochromatically intense songs, where conventional considerations of pace and variety are thrown calculatedly to the wind." "The Ramones are a highly stylized extension of the punk idiom."
**Peak of the Genre** : The release of the Ramones' third album, *Rocket to Russia*, on November 4, 1977, occurred just as punk rock was peaking, cementing the album's importance. "Rocket to Russia was out just as punk rock peaked."

# concert - CBGB

## curiosities
**Setting of Ramones' Debut** : On Friday, August 16, 1974, CBGB was a "dive bar" located at 315 Bowery at Bleeker in a rather uncivilized, slum-like part of New York City, surrounded by garbage, abandoned buildings, drug addicts, and homeless people. The owner, Hilly Crystal, an ex-Marine, had started allowing young rock bands to play there since spring 1974, transitioning from its original focus as a "country blues and bluegrass bar." "This part of the city was, to be honest, rather uncivilized. OK, it was a slum."
**Ramones' Home Venue** : CBGB became the Ramones' primary venue, their "home," where they played an impressive 74 times by the end of 1974. In 1975, they performed there 33 times, sometimes alone, other times on bills with bands like Talking Heads, Blondie, or Tuftarts. "CBGBC was the Ramones home. They played elsewhere, but in 1975, they played CB's 33 times."
**Growth and Notoriety** : Following the Ramones' frequent performances, "words started to spread about these leather-jacketed weirdos," and crowds began to grow. The venue gained enough notoriety that people from the "rich side of town" started visiting to "slum it," although some, like Linda Ronstadt, lasted only minutes. "Crowds started to get bigger and people started to get it. There's a song where the Ramones' 60s pop influences really shine through it."

# concert - Mothers

## curiosities
**Ramones Record Deal Catalyst** : This "gay bar on 23rd Street" hosted the Ramones for a three-night stand in October 1975. Seymour Stein, the head of Sire Records, attended one of these performances, which directly led to the band signing a record deal with his label for $6,000. "Stein went to see the band at a three-night standard Mothers, which was this gay bar on 23rd Street that October."

# concert - Roundhouse

## curiosities
**Catalyst for British Punk** : On July 4th, 1976, while America celebrated its Bicentennial, the Ramones were second on a bill at the Roundhouse in North London. This event is credited with doing "more to kick start British punk than anything else that had happened up until that point." "On July 4th, 1976, while America was celebrating the Big Bison Tenio, the Ramones were second on a bill at the Roundhouse in North London."

# concert - Dingwalls

## curiosities
**Meeting Point for Future Punk Bands** : During their first visit to England in 1976, the Ramones played at this club. "And then we did this club Dingwalls. And basically the whole makeup of Dingwalls were all these kids that would later form these kind of groundbreaking bands of their own."

# concert - Capitol Theater (Paseg, New Jersey)

## curiosities
**Site of Teapot Incident** : Backstage at this venue, Joey Ramone sustained second and third-degree burns to his face when a makeshift humidifier (a teapot on a hot plate) exploded. Despite the severe injury, he was rushed from the hospital back to the venue to play the show. "It was backstage at the Capitol Theater in Paseg, New Jersey. Joey had a cold and rigged up a makeshift humidifier by heating up the teapot on a hot plate in the dressing room."

# curiosity

**Marfan Syndrome** : This is an inherited disorder, linked to chromosome 15, that affects the body's connective tissue. Individuals with Marfan Syndrome tend to be tall and thin, with unusually long arms, legs, and fingers. It can lead to serious heart problems, and if left undiagnosed and untreated, the average lifespan is typically 32 years. Joey Ramone was afflicted with this condition. "Young Jeffrey grew up with all sorts of health problems. He was tall and gauky as the result of something called Marfan Syndrome, which is an inherited disorder living on chromosome 15 that messes with connective tissue."
**ADHD or OCD as Marfan's Symptom** : ADHD or OCD can manifest as another symptom of Marfan Syndrome. Joey Ramone was diagnosed with OCD at 18, and it continued to affect him throughout his life. "Another symptom of the disorder can be ADHD or OCD."
**Joey Ramone's OCD Rituals** : Joey Ramone's lifelong struggle with OCD, diagnosed at 18, manifested in specific and compulsive behaviors. These included having to step on and off a curb a predetermined number of times before he could proceed, and following a strict ritual every time he left his apartment, making these everyday activities a struggle. "For example, he sometimes had to step on and off a curb a prescribed number of times before he could move on."
**Ramones' First Gig Sound** : At their debut performance at CBGB on August 16, 1974, the Ramones' sound was described by scene-ster Ligs McNeil as "just this wall of noise." Their appearance, featuring leather jackets, t-shirts, torn jeans, and Converse high-tops, struck him as "completely new" and distinctly non-hippie. "And it was just this wall of noise. They look so striking. These guys were not hippies. This was something completely new."
**August 16, 1974 - A Turning Point for Music** : The night of the Ramones' first gig at CBGB, August 16, 1974, is cited as the moment "music began to change forever." This seemingly insignificant event, with a bass player counting in the first song, is equated to a monumental "let there be light" moment for music. "But back to August 16th, that was the night music began to change forever."
**Dee Dee Ramone's Failed Robbery Attempt** : In his youth, Douglas Colvin (Dee Dee Ramone) attempted to rob a neighborhood drug store. However, he made a mistake and ended up breaking into the laundromat located next door instead. "One of them, I think it was DD, once tried to rob a neighborhood drug store, it broke into the laundromat next door of a mistake."
**"Johnny Apple Seeds of Rock"** : The Ramones earned this nickname because their influence inspired thousands of young people to pick up guitars. Wherever the band played, new local bands seemed to emerge in their wake, spreading their musical legacy. "Some people call them the Johnny Apple Seeds of Rock because wherever they stop to play, new bands sprung up in their wake."
