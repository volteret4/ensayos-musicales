# curiosity

**Music and Technology Interaction (Centuries)** : Throughout centuries, music and technology have engaged in a constant "dance," with each influencing the other. This dynamic illustrates that progress in technology inevitably shapes musical forms and consumption patterns, as seen from medieval church architecture influencing sacred music to modern recording limits defining song length. "For centuries, there's always been this dance between music and technology, with each affecting the other in some kind of way."
**Catholic Church Architecture and Sacred Music (Middle Ages)** : In the Middle Ages, the design of large, echoey cathedrals built by the Catholic Church influenced sacred music. The music adapted to these new architectural spaces, utilizing the natural reverb to enhance its sound and spiritual impact. "when the Catholic Church built big, echoey cathedrals in the Middle Ages, the sacred music in those buildings adapted to this new architecture so that it made use of the natural reverb."
**Recording Limits and Standard Song Length (Late 19th Century)** : The three-minute recording limit of early devices like Thomas Edison's talking machine (1877) and Emil Berliner's gramophone (1887) directly established the standard length for popular songs. This shows how technological constraints can dictate creative norms in music. "But because of that recording limit, the standard length of a popular song became about three minutes. So the music bent to the limitations of the technology."
**Radio's Impact on Music Consumption** : Radio fundamentally altered how music was consumed, marketed, and sold. It broadened reach and created new pathways for commercial success, demonstrating its transformative power on the music industry. "Radio changed the way music was consumed, marketed, and sold."
**Electricity's Impact on Musical Instruments** : The advent of electricity was a revolutionary force, enabling the creation of amplifiers and the electric guitar. This technological leap expanded the possibilities of musical sound and performance significantly. "Electricity gave us amplifiers in the electric guitar."
**Sampling - Initial Controversy and Acceptance** : Sampling in music was initially regarded as "evil and illegal," facing widespread condemnation. However, over time, it became an accepted and integrated practice within the music industry, demonstrating how new technologies often overcome initial resistance. "Sampling. Well, that was thought to be evil and illegal at first, but we worked that out."
**MP3 File Sharing and Streaming's Resolution** : The advent of MP3 file sharing initially led to concerns that people would never pay for music again, posing a significant threat to the industry. However, the rise of streaming services eventually provided a legal and convenient alternative, with hundreds of millions now paying for streaming access. "File sharing of MP3s meant that no one would ever pay for music again, but now hundreds of millions of people are paying for streaming."
**Music Videos - Shift to On-Demand Digital (Mid-2000s to 2010s)** : The consumption of music videos dramatically shifted from television channels like MTV and MuchMusic in the 80s and 90s to on-demand digital platforms like YouTube starting in the mid-2000s. By 2010, traditional channels had largely pivoted away from music videos, and viewers accessed content instantly on smartphones. "In the 80s and 90s, MTV and much music were the only places we could see music videos. That all began to change in the mid-2000s with the debut of YouTube. By 2010, both channels were barely running any music videos, if at all, pivoting instead to becoming lifestyle channels."
**Music Video Budgets - Decline and Democratization (2010s)** : Music video budgets dramatically decreased from multi-million dollar productions (e.g., Michael Jackson's $7M "Scream" in 1995) to significantly more modest scopes in the 2010s. "Those million dollar plus videos that we once saw became more modest in scope. But because everyone had access to high quality video cameras and very powerful video editing software on laptops or even phones, people could create pro-level productions."
**Resurrection of Vinyl (2008 onwards)** : Vinyl, once deemed on the brink of extinction by 1999, experienced a dramatic resurrection starting in 2008. Initiated by independent record store owners in Baltimore through "Record Store Day," vinyl sales increased by double digits annually in many territories, eventually eclipsing CD revenue in some countries by the 2020s. "One of the big success stories of the 2010s was the resurrection of vinyl."
**Record Store Day Foundation (2008)** : In 2008, independent record store owners in Baltimore, seeking to save their businesses, conceived "Record Store Day." Inspired by Comic Book Day, the initiative involved artists and labels participating with special vinyl releases, leading to a sustained, double-digit annual increase in vinyl sales globally. "A bunch of independent record store owners in Baltimore got together to try to save their businesses. The solution was to copy Comic Book Day... We could call it record store day."
**CD Sales Decline (2001-2019)** : Compact disc sales saw a precipitous decline from 2001, falling every single year. In the United States, sales plummeted from 942.5 million in 2000 to less than 50 million by 2019, representing a 95% crash over 20 years. A slight year-over-year increase was only observed in 2021. "Starting in 2001, the number of CDs purchased has fallen every single year. If we look at just the United States, the largest music market in the world, 942.5 million CDs were sold in 2000... By 2019, that number had gone from a billion to less than 50 million, which is a fall of 95% over those 20 years."
**Belief in Music Ownership vs. Access Shift** : A widely held belief in the 2000s was that people would always want to "possess" or "own" music, whether on CD or in digital form, supported by the "long tail theory" that songs would be available for purchase indefinitely. However, the 2010s proved this wrong, as the market overwhelmingly shifted to streaming, prioritizing access over ownership. "These developments fly in the face of two things we believe with all our hearts back in the odds. First, the people would always want to possess music, to own music, be it on CD or in digital form."
