# genre - Punk Rock
## curiosities
**1970s Origins as Revival** : The original punk rock explosion in the mid-1970s was itself a revival, a "reactionary" movement that stripped rock back to its essence. It emerged as a response to the perceived excesses of prog rock, the detachment of stadium rock, and the blandness of AM radio music, re-emphasizing simplicity, attitude, and accessibility regardless of musical ability. "Punk rock was not revolutionary. It was in fact reactionary. Rock was stripped back to its essence in terms of instrumentation, arrangement, performance, and attitude."
**DIY Ethos** : A core tenet of punk rock was the "doing it yourself" philosophy, which persisted even when the genre receded into the margins. This ethos ingrained itself into many subsequent genres, including post-punk. "And the central tenets of doing it yourself, engraved itself into many other genres loosely grouped under the umbrella of post punk."
**1990s Revival Factors** : The punk rock revival of the 1990s was facilitated by several factors: the groundwork laid by grunge (which name-checked punk influences), the music industry's "herd mentality" to sign similar acts, the death of Kurt Cobain which shifted focus away from grunge, and a broader socio-economic climate of optimism (end of Cold War, economic recovery). "But more important was the herd mentality of the record industry. You know how it works. Once a successful trend is identified, there's a stampede to sign and promote acts for that space."
**Survival in Margins** : Despite being overshadowed by synthesizer bands, hair metal, and pop music in the 1980s, punk rock music and its attitude never truly died. It mutated into sub-genres like hardcore, oi, and anarcho punk, surviving in local enclaves across North America, supported by dedicated communities. "It's not like punk was dead, but it had receded into the margins, drowned up by the waves of synthesizer bands, hair-middle groups, and various floods of pop music."

# curiosity
**Cycles of Music Revivals** : The universe, including music, moves in cycles where things are born, build up, peak, and then fade into a period of stasis or hibernation before something triggers a rebirth. **1970s Rock Decline** : By 1973, there was a widespread perception that the original promise of rock music had "gone off the rails," leading to a desire for a "complete reboot." This was attributed to the excesses of prog rock, the perceived detachment of stadium rock bands, and the "awful," "safe and bland and dull" music prevalent on AM radio, exemplified by the #1 song of 1973, "Tie a Yellow Ribbon around the Old Oak Tree." "Things got so dire that a small group of people decided that rock and roll needed an enema, a complete reboot."
**AM Radio Dominance (1970s)** : In the 1970s, AM radio was the primary source for most people to get their music. Despite the emergence of FM rock stations in some cities and more cars equipped with FM radios, AM still "ruled," broadcasting music that was often described as "safe and bland and dull." "AM radio was where most people still got their music back then."
**Record Industry "Herd Mentality"** : The record industry exhibits a "herd mentality," wherein once a successful trend like grunge or punk is identified, there is an immediate "stampede" to sign and promote similar acts. This often progresses from original bands to "like bands," then "adjacent bands," and finally "any sort of band that could be classified as alternative." "Once a successful trend is identified, there's a stampede to sign and promote acts for that space."
**Challenges of Indie Labels for Bands** : Running an indie band and label is difficult due to the extensive business demands: getting albums into stores, promoting releases, tracking sales, and collecting money. These tasks are a significant drain on time and energy, which often drove bands like Sonic Youth, R.E.M., and Hüsker Dü to sign with major labels. "The business of getting your albums into the stores, promoting releases, and going through the process of tracking albums sold and the hassle of collecting that money, that's just a huge drain on time and energy and everything else."
**Kurt Cobain's Death and Punk Revival** : Kurt Cobain's death on April 8, 1994, was a pivotal event that indirectly fueled the 1990s punk revival. In hindsight, it marked the end of the industry's laser focus on grunge, creating a need for new musical trends. This transition smoothly shifted attention to emerging punk bands like Green Day, whose album "Dookie" then soared in popularity. "The world was shocked by the death of Kurt Cobain. And in hindsight, we can see that this event marked the beginning of the end of the public's and the recording industries laser focus on grunge."
**Socio-Economic Context for 90s Punk Revival** : The mid-1990s punk revival was supported by a more optimistic global environment. The world economy had emerged from an early 90s recession, the Cold War was over, and political leaders like the US President and UK Prime Minister were described as music-loving or ex-musicians. This upbeat mood made the "gloom of grunge" seem "out of date" and created a demand for the more energetic punk sound. "Besides, the world economy had emerged from a terrible recession in the early 90s, about when grunge started. And everyone started to feel pretty good about things."
