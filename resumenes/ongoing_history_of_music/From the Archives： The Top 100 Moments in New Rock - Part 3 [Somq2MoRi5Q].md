**Money (1973) – Tape Loop Introduction** : The introduction of this 1973 song features a famous cash register sequence, which serves as a prime example of old-school sound manipulation. This effect was created using reels of magnetic tape, where the desired sound bites were carefully measured, cut with a razor blade, and then pieced together using splicing tape to form a loop. The process was described as mind-numbing and took hours to complete. "This whole cash register sequence was measured out, cut up, and then carefully looped."

**Pump Up The Volume (1987) – Groundbreaking Sample-Based Single** : Created by five London-based producers and musicians (Martin, Alex, Rudy, Ivo Watts Russell, and Stephen, whose initials formed the band name M/A/R/R/S), this single was a groundbreaking musical collage. It extensively used samples from diverse sources including Israeli singer Ofra Haza, James Brown, the Iranian Revolutionary Army chorus, and rap duo Eric B. & Rakim, with the latter's sample providing the song's title. Initially distributed anonymously on 500 white labels to club DJs in the fall of 1987, its sounds and ideas rapidly spread through dance clubs and eventually into mainstream pop and rock, making a long-lasting impact despite not being the first sampled hit. "The song was issued under a name made up of the initials of the five main guys behind the project."

This strategy allowed the track's sounds and ideas to spread organically through dance clubs before breaking into mainstream pop and rock, contributing to its significant and lasting impact. "This song was mailed out on an anonymous white label to 500 club DJs, and from there the sounds and ideas spread, first through the dance clubs, and finally into mainstream pop and rock."

Deeply affected by the images of starvation, Geldof felt compelled to take action, leading to the inception of the Band-Aid project. "Bob was stunned by the images of starving people, and he was suddenly moved to do something, anything to help these poor people."

This self-assessment, however, fueled his determination to find a way to make a difference, ultimately leading to the creation of Band-Aid. "As he wrote in his autobiography, I was only a pop singer, and by now not a very successful pop singer, all I could do was make records that no one bought."

# instrument - Turntable

## curiosities
**Technics SP-10 (1970) – First Direct Drive Turntable** : Introduced in 1970 by Technics, a division of Japan's Panasonic, the SP-10 was the world's first direct drive turntable. It was developed by a shadowy group of engineers and scientists known as the Special Products Group. This design eliminated belts and pulleys, significantly reducing low-frequency rumble and allowing the platter to reach operating speed much faster. The SP-10 used a linear frequency generator for stable speed, which could be pitched up or down by as much as 8%. "This was significant because it was the world's first direct drive turntable."
**Technics SL-1200 (1972) – DJ Standard** : Introduced in 1972 as the successor to the SP-10, the Technics SL-1200 quickly became the turntable of choice for DJs worldwide due to its power, ease of use, versatility, and indestructibility. Its importance was further cemented by the introduction of the Mark II model around 1979, which featured a beefier motor and the iconic slide pitch control. The SL-1200 was instrumental in the development of club culture, DJ culture, beat mixing, 12-inch records, and extended remixes. Even in the digital age, thousands of SL-1200s are still sold annually, all hand-made in Osaka, Japan. "The techniques SL-1200 was so powerful, so easy to use, so versatile, and so bloody indestructible that the SL-1200 became the turntable of choice for DJs around the world."

# instrument - Sampler

## curiosities
**Fairlight Computer Musical Instrument (CMI) (1980) – First Keyboard Digital Sampler** : Developed by the Australian company Fairlight throughout the 1970s, the CMI made its debut in 1980 as the world's first keyboard-based digital sampler. This groundbreaking instrument allowed users to digitally record any acoustic sound, store it on a floppy disk, visualize its waveform on a monitor, manipulate it with a light pen, and then play it back using a standard 88-note keyboard. This capability meant sounds could be shaped, molded, and transformed into entirely new sonic creations. Historically, the first sound ever digitally sampled on a Fairlight was a barking dog. "The Fairlight allowed the operator to digitally record any acoustic sound, stored on a floppy disc, display a visual image of that sound on a monitor, manipulate that waveform using a light pen, and then play it back using a standard sized 88-note keyboard."
**Fairlight CMI Cost and Limitations (1980)** : Despite its innovative capabilities, the Fairlight CMI was prohibitively expensive upon its debut in 1980, costing between $25,000 and $36,000. Additionally, the technology had significant limitations, with sampled soundfiles restricted to a maximum size of 20 kilobytes. Despite these drawbacks, over 300 studios and record labels invested in this cutting-edge technology. "Getting on options, these massive things cost anywhere from $25 to $36,000."
**Akai S1000 (1988) – Industry Standard Sampler** : Following earlier samplers like the Emulator and Sinclair Vier, the Japanese company Akai introduced a line of rack-mounted samplers, including the S1000 in 1988. This model was particularly significant because it was very affordable and quickly became the industry standard for digital sampling. Its accessibility played a crucial role in making sampling technology more widespread among musicians. "But then a Japanese company called Akai got into the act, with a line of rack-mounted samplers, including the very affordable S1000 in 1988, and it became the industry standard."

# curiosity

**Introduction of Ecstasy (1985) – Impact on Music** : Ecstasy (methylene dioxianne methyl-emphetamine), a stimulant derived from sassafras oil that also broke down emotional inhibitions, was introduced into the UK and European club scene around 1985. By 1988, it became the substance of choice in clubs and at parties, surpassing even beer. Its widespread popularity fundamentally changed the nature of music over the following years, fostering the rise of raves and shifting musical focus from melody to rhythm, with DJs and the dance floor becoming central to a new youth revolution. "The DJ's beats reign supreme as ecstasy roared through the bloodstream."
**Rise of Raves in England** : In the late 1980s, driven by the popularity of ecstasy, gigantic and often illegal outdoor parties known as raves began to emerge across England. These events were held in various unconventional locations such as fields, warehouses, and abandoned buildings, becoming a significant part of a new youth revolution centered around dance music and DJ culture. "Raves, gigantic and sometimes illegal outdoor parties started popping up across England, fields, warehouses, abandoned buildings."
**Emergence of AIDS Epidemic** : In 1981, a mysterious disease initially known as GRID (Gay-Related Immune Deficiency) began to appear, affecting gay men, hemophiliacs, and Haitian immigrants, though it received limited media attention. By July 1982, it was renamed AIDS (Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndrome), and in May 1983, the causative virus, HIV, was isolated. The epidemic rapidly escalated, with 7,669 AIDS cases reported in the U.S. by the end of 1984, a number that nearly doubled to 15,948 just months later. "By the end of 1984, a total of 7,669 AIDS cases have been reported in the United States."
**Rock Hudson's Death (October 3, 1985) – First Famous AIDS Fatality** : Actor Rock Hudson passed away on October 3, 1985, becoming the first famous public figure to die from AIDS. His death brought significant, albeit delayed, public attention to the escalating health crisis. "Actor Rock Hudson passed away on October 3, 1985."
