## albums
**Pills 'n' Thrills and Bellyaches (1990)** : Released in 1990, this album was a massive sensation both at home in the UK and internationally, contributing to Factory Records' high-flying status. It is characterized as the "everything" album, representing a period of extreme excess, whatever the band could get their hands on in LA. "Pills and Fruits was everything, was the LA, everything, whatever you can get all of in LA album."
**Yes Please (1992)** : This album was a follow-up recorded in Barbados at Eddy Grant's studio, but its production was plagued with problems. Shaun Ryder was largely absent for vocals, contributing lyrics for only one song, although he typically wrote lyrics later and didn't involve himself in music composition. Despite receiving good reviews and reaching number three on the charts, it is considered the record that largely marked the end of the Madchester era and was recorded amid significant band chaos including car wreckage and drug use. "It actually got good reviews, he went to number three, it did okay, it's, out of there, I think there's 10 songs in it, there's six that are good, I think there's four that, that really laid it down."
**Bummed (1988)** : This album is characterized as "the excessy album," marking a period of significant indulgence for the band. "Bund was the, was the excessy album."

## curiosities
**Factory Records Contract** : Contrary to popular myth, Happy Mondays did have a contract with Factory Records, though it might have been an unusual arrangement that allowed them considerable freedom. Tony Wilson, head of Factory Records, loved the band and let them operate with this leeway, despite much of the rest of the label, including other bands and managers, not taking to them. "We did have a contract... Tony loved us. Everyone hated us."
**Factory Records Bankruptcy Myth** : The band did not bankrupt Factory Records, a claim often associated with them. Instead, Factory Records owed the Happy Mondays a substantial amount of money when the label went under, with the bankruptcy attributed to bad management. "No, we didn't. That's another myth. We didn't."
**Barbados Album Recording Chaos** : The recording of their follow-up album, "Yes Please," in Barbados at Eddy Grant's studio was marked by extreme chaos and excess. The band and their families, a party of about 30 people, were on the remote, non-tourist Atlantic side of the island, which contributed to the uncontrolled environment. "It didn't go well at all."
**Car Destruction in Barbados** : During their Barbados recording session for "Yes Please," multiple cars were destroyed due to reckless behavior and poor road conditions on the island's east side. Estimates for the number of destroyed cars vary from nine to "more." "how many cars were destroyed? ... I thought it was nine, but then someone told me it was more."
**Kicked Out of Sam Lord's Castle** : On their first night in Barbados, the band was kicked out of Sam Lord's Castle, their initial accommodation. This happened after their keyboard player, PDR, assaulted a member of a cabaret band called Spice or The Spice Boys due to annoyance, leading to his escort by police. "the first night we arrived, we stayed at this place called Sam Lod's castle... we got kicked out."
**Gazz Wheeling's Barbados Activities** : During the Barbados recording sessions for "Yes Please," drummer Gazz Wheeling finished recording his parts in the first week. Subsequently, he spent his time drinking local rum and self-medicating with local herb, enjoying the chaotic environment from a distance while his wife and other families joined the party. "I was drinking a lot of, a lot of local rum. I was, uh, self-medicating with a lot of local herb."
**Car Crash into Bar** : One night in Barbados, after Gazz Wheeling finished drumming, he witnessed a car crash directly through the front of a small shack bar where he was drinking. The car was driven by an ex-West Indian cricketer, who immediately offered to pay for the damages. "this car comes through the front, the front bit of the bar... The car drove into the bar."
**Band Recoupment for Damages** : The band ultimately had to pay for all damages and expenses incurred during the Barbados recording, including the car crashes, as these costs were recouped from them by the label. "The bandled up paying for everything, don't they?"
**Bez's Jeep Accident and Injuries** : During the Barbados sessions, Bez crushed himself in a Jeep accident in the marshlands near the studio. He sustained serious arm injuries requiring pins but was reportedly jet-skiing and re-injuring it just two weeks later. "Bez with his arm in some kind of, he was, uh, medical device... he was crushed and he had all these pins and his arm."
**Sun Lounges as Crack Den** : Band members converted sun loungers by the pool near Eddy Grant's studio in Barbados into a makeshift crack den, a place where many of them spent their time. "they turned the sun, the sun lounges... They turned and they made it cracked and out of it. It stopped open to a crack there."
**Shaun Ryder's Unconventional Recording Process** : Shaun Ryder typically did not get involved in the music composition, preferring to add lyrics later, often after the music for an entire album was written. For "Yes Please," he only contributed lyrics for one song, which was unusual even for his late-stage lyric writing habits. "he'd always did the lyrics later, he never got involved in the music, ever."
**Bez's Favorite Album** : Despite its chaotic production and perceived failure by some, "Yes Please" is Bez's favorite Happy Mondays album. "it's best his favorite album."
**Reunions Not for Tax Reasons** : Shaun Ryder claimed the band reunited multiple times for tax reasons, but Gazz Wheeling stated this was a "safety net" to avoid appearing as if they genuinely wanted or needed to reunite, reflecting a "Mancunian" attitude of not showing enjoyment for commercial endeavors. "No, that was, that was just Sean saying that to kind of, ever, as a bit of a, a, a safety net in case it all went perjokes, just so it looked like we, the thing, well, it's kind of like not trying to look like we needed to get back all we wanted to."
**Gazz Wheeling's Departure** : Gazz Wheeling decided to leave the Happy Mondays after a tour in Australia because Shaun Ryder was cutting set times to as little as 20 minutes, despite headlining festivals requiring 90-minute sets, and expecting to be paid to record new music. Wheeling felt Shaun Ryder was doing "what he wanted to do," and there was mutual fatigue among the members. "Sean, you know, was cutting the set, so he wanted to do 20 minutes... he'd pay me to come in the studio to do recordings."

# genre - Madchester

## curiosities
**Definition and Flourishing Period** : Madchester was a crazy and wildly popular music scene that flourished in Manchester, England, in the late 1980s and early 1990s. It was exemplified by bands like the Happy Mondays, Inspiral Carpets, and The Stone Roses, collectively known as the "Holy Trinity." "that crazy and wildly popular scene in Manchester, England that flourished in the late 80s and early 90s."
**Influence on British Indie Music** : Madchester music set the stage for much of the British indie music that emerged over the subsequent 15 years, with its impact still felt today. "This was music that set the stage for much of the British indie music that was to come for the next 15 years."
**Duration and Decline** : The Madchester era burned brightly and hard for about four years, primarily ending around 1992 or 1993. Its decline was attributed to bands burning out and public interest waning. "the Madchester thing comes to an end somewhere around, what he figured, 92, 93."
**Foundations** : The foundations of Madchester music are rooted in Northern Soul and similar genres. "Madchester's foundations are originally Northern Seoul and stuff."
**Bridge to Britpop** : After a brief lull following Madchester's decline, Britpop emerged, with its foundations essentially built upon the Madchester scene. "then there's a bit of a law and then Brickpop comes along. But Brickpop's foundations were essentially Manchester."
**Londoners Traveling to Manchester** : During the Madchester era, it became a bizarre phenomenon for people from London to travel by coach or train to Manchester's clubs, particularly on Fridays and Saturdays. This was unusual for Londoners, who typically did not travel to provincial cities for entertainment. "the first time ever you'd been a club, and you'd hear people would come up from London, you'd get a coach."

**Relationship with Happy Mondays** : Factory Records had a complex relationship with the Happy Mondays; while head Tony Wilson loved the band, many others at the label, including other bands and managers, did not take to them. "Everyone at Factory hated us, apart from Tony loved us."

# curiosity

**Manchester's Enduring Musical Legacy** : Manchester has consistently been a significant musical hub, producing a wide array of influential artists and bands across different genres and eras, from the Bee Gees and Davy Jones (The Monkees) to Graham Nash, Buzzcocks, The Fall, James, Godley & Creme, Badly Drawn Boy, Elbow, John Mayall, Starsailor, The Verve, Phil Lynott, The Smiths, and Simply Red. "mancise has always been, I don't know why mancise has been this great musical set, you know, bands from, you know, from the Bee Gees to, you know, the, you know, the singer of David Jones, the monkeys."
**Manchester's Portrayal in UK Media** : Even after the Madchester era, Manchester and its "Mancunian" stereotype continue to be a common setting and subject for musicians, TV shows, and comedy shows in the UK, such as "Shameless." "every TV show, every comedy show in the UK, if you want to portray any musician, if it's all setting man to something, everything's setting man to do."
**Manchester's Urban Transformation** : Manchester has undergone significant gentrification and urbanization, now featuring three universities (University of Manchester, Manchester Metropolitan University, University of Salford), Europe's largest student population, and the biggest gay community outside London. It has become a European city, often called the "capital of the north" or "London of the north," though its past "greatness" is perceived by some as having changed. "it's got three universes, Manchester, Manchester, Met and Sulford, it's had the biggest gay community outside London, biggest student population in Europe."
**Manchester's Cultural Pillars** : Growing up in Manchester, three things were paramount to the city's identity: music, football, and fashion. These three elements were considered equally important and intertwined, mutually influencing each other. "when we grew up, it was, there's three things, it's the older tunit, it's a, it's music, it's football, it's fashion, then three things are really important, no more important than the other, and the go together, the go together."
**Historical Football Allegiances in Manchester** : Historically, football allegiances in Manchester were geographically and socially divided: Western and North Manchester were primarily United fans, while South and East were City fans. Manchester United was originally seen as the Catholic team and a working man's club supported by trade unions, whereas Manchester City was initially the Protestant team and seen as a richer club. These divisions have largely blurred over time due to modern influences and club ownership changes. "Western North Manchester was mainly United, South and East was City. United was originally the Catholic team, City was, the post-it team, City initially was a rich club, United was a working man's club to sell by the trade unions, but that doesn't, it's not, that's not the case now."
**Football Allegiance as Identity** : For Mancunians, supporting a football team is deeply tied to identity and is seen as an unchangeable part of who one is, reflecting more than just the sport itself. However, this aspect has also evolved with the influx of money and international ownership in recent years. "your football team says different about what you're about, but times of change, the money, last 10 years, it's all kind of got, you know, lost in the..."
**Mancunian "Not Enjoying It" Attitude** : There is a specific English, and particularly Mancunian, working-class attitude that dictates it's "not cool" to appear to be enjoying success or public attention too much. This can manifest as downplaying achievements or even feigning disinterest. "Sean's got this thing where he doesn't like to enjoy it. If he looks like he's enjoying it, he's not, that's a real man's to think, is where he got a lot of you not enjoying it, if you're enjoying it, it's not very cool, you know."
**English Working-Class "Know Your Place" Mentality** : A deep-seated class dynamic in England, especially prevalent in the working class, instills a sense of "knowing your place" and not getting "above your station," even when achieving success. This can lead to insecurity among working-class individuals in positions of authority or fame. "if you're working class and you're, and you make something, you know, you do something... you've got to know, you know, you know, you know, you know, you're place, it's strange."
**Class Prejudice in England** : An anecdotal example illustrates class prejudice where one might feel uncomfortable with a working-class Mancunian pilot but reassured by a pilot named "Giles from Rugby" (implying an upper-class background), suggesting that background affects perception of competence. This reflects the class dynamics prevalent during the Madchester era. "if I got an airplane in the guy, the pilot says we're going to fly... he's got a mancha of tracks and I might get me off this plane... But if he's called Giles and he speaks from rugby, I feel okay."
