# genre - Nerd Rock

## curiosities
**Definition and Scope** : Nerd rock is a genre of music that aligns squarely with alternative music, often characterized by a sound, style, look, attitude, or personality of the musicians that embodies "nerdishness." It encompasses numerous subgenres, with geek rock being a prominent one. "Nerdishness is now so widespread that even nerds have their own genre of music."
**1990s Explosion** : The 1990s saw a significant boom in nerd rock, driven by the broader explosion in alternative music. This era created an egalitarian environment where diverse ideas, attitudes, and sounds from the fringes could enter the mainstream, encouraging and celebrating artists who were perceived as weird. "The early 1990s saw a huge bloom in nerdy bands coming to the forefront."
**Characteristics** : Nerd rock bands, particularly in the early to mid-90s, did not necessarily share a common sound but were unified by their humor, fun, self-deprecation, appreciation of the mundane, and extensive use of irony. "There wasn't really a common sound, but they were all about humor and fun and self-deprecation, appreciation of the mundane and loads and loads of irony."
**Evolution from Folk to Electronic** : The music genre initially began as a form of folk music (FILK Rock), then incorporated surrealism, and later integrated elements of synth-based new wave and post-punk, especially as electronic instruments became more accessible and powerful. "The music started as a form of folk music, moved into surrealism and then parts of the synth based new wave and post punk world."
**Early 80s Electronic Roots** : In the early 1980s, nerd rock became heavily rooted in electronics, utilizing keyboards, synthesizers, sequencers, and drum machines, as tech-minded people uninterested in traditional instruments were drawn to the new technology, and synthesizers were equated with the music of the future and science fiction. "For a time, nerd rock was heavily rooted in electronics, keyboards synthesizers, sequencers, drum machines, and so on."

It consists of folk songs specifically favored by fans of science fiction, horror movies, and fantasy. "The name itself is a typo, I is next to O on the keyboard. These were folk songs, favored by certain fans of science fiction and horror movies and fantasy."
**Convention Origins** : The trend of FILK music originated at sci-fi conventions, where participants would play FILK songs at gatherings known as "House Filks," often performed by musicians in "FILK circles" using acoustic guitars. "The trend seems to have started at sci-fi conventions as participants would play FILK songs and gatherings called House Filks where musicians played in FILK circles, usually with acoustic guitars."
**Enduring Legacy** : FILK music has an enduring legacy, evidenced by its own dedicated awards program, the Pegasus Awards, and a FILK Hall of Fame. "FILK music is so enduring that it has its own awards program called the Pegasus Awards. And there's a FILK Hall of Fame."

# curiosity

**Etymology of "Nerd"** : The word "nerd" first appeared in a 1500s book titled "a true discourse of the assault committed upon the most noble prince Prince William of Orange, County of NASA, Marquess de la Verre and C," written by John Jarkuy Speniel. It fell out of use around 1725 before being revived by Dr. Seuss in 1950 to describe a peculiar creature. "According to these nerds, nerd, the word, shows up for the first time in a book called a true discourse of the assault committed upon the most noble prince Prince William of Orange, County of NASA, Marquess de la Verre and C, written by John Jarkuy Speniel."
**Modern Usage of "Nerd"** : In 1951, Newsweek magazine reported that the term "nerd" was used in Detroit to describe an "awkward sort of dude who really wasn't very cool." The word gained significant popularity in 1974 with the TV series "Happy Days," where Fonzie often called Richie and Potsy "nerds." By the late 1970s, coinciding with the rise of personal computers, consumer technology, and science fiction, its use became widespread, evolving into a compliment in modern technological society, with figures like Bill Gates, Elon Musk, and Mark Zuckerberg celebrated as "nerds." "But the following year, Newsweek magazine reported that nerd was being used in Detroit to describe an awkward sort of dude who really wasn't very cool."
**Electronics and Future Music** : The increasing affordability and power of synthesizers and related electronics in the 1980s attracted tech-minded musicians uninterested in traditional instruments. This segment of the music world equated synthesizers with the "music of the future," which in turn connected to science fiction, a realm strongly associated with nerds. **Egalitarianism in Alt Rock** : The explosion of alternative music in the 1990s fostered a sense of egalitarianism, where previously fringe ideas and sounds were welcomed into the mainstream. Being "weird" was not only tolerated but encouraged, leading to the celebration and promotion of artists who challenged mainstream tastes with their strangeness. "From the earliest days, being weird was not only tolerated but encouraged. People who annoyed the mainstream with their strangeness were celebrated and promoted."
**SNK "The Nerds" Sketch** : The phrase "nerd rock" as uttered by Tom Davis after Elvis Costello's SNL performance led to the creation of "The Nerds," a recurring sketch on Saturday Night Live. The sketches featured characters played by Gilder Radner, Jane Curtain, and Bill Murray, solidifying the cultural recognition of "nerd" archetypes. "And that led to the creation of the nerds, a reoccurring set of sketches featuring characters played by Gilder Radner, Jane Curtain and Bill Murray."
