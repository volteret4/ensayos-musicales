## albums
**The Man Who Sold the World (1970)** : This album's cover featured David Bowie wearing a flowing gown designed by Mr. Fish, also known as Michael Fish, a prominent British fashion maven. Bowie insisted on wearing the dress in public and to interviews, which was not widely encouraged in 1970. Certain US stores refused to display the record due to "a dude in a dress," causing controversy that hurt sales until new editions with different covers were quickly released in markets like the US and Germany. "Inside though, it was apparent that the floodgates had begun to open when it came to new songs."
**Hunky Dory (1971)** : Released 13 months after "The Man Who Sold the World," this album marked a significant step forward for Bowie, giving him another hit with the song "Changes." The album was a clear sign that "something was happening" with Bowie's career after years of searching for his niche. "When the Hunky Dory album came out, Bowie had become friends with Mark Bowen, a former folkie who had taken up electric guitars and formed a psych band called T-Rex."
**Aladdin Sane (1973)** : Released on April 13, 1973, this album was Bowie's first as a "genuine rock star." The "Aladdin Sane" character served as a transitional persona, evolving the "Ziggy Stardust" concept to a "higher realm" and being further developed through subsequent albums, even if not explicitly named Ziggy. "The Gene Genie written in part about his even crazier friend, Eiki Pop, and inspired by Jean Genie. Another friend who was a writer, poet, and political activist."
**Pin Ups (1973)** : This album, released in the same year as "Aladdin Sane," was a collection of cover songs. It could be described as an extension of the Ziggy Stardust persona, offering a new interpretation of existing tracks through Bowie's evolving artistic lens. "The pin-up album, a collection of covers also from 1973, could be called... Well, let's just call it Ziggy karaoke."
**Diamond Dogs (1974)** : This was Bowie's fifth studio album released in a period of just two years and four months, showcasing his remarkable prolificness. The album was loosely based on George Orwell's dystopian novel "1984," an ambitious and unusual concept for rock music at the time, which Bowie successfully executed. "Right an album loosely based on George Orwell's novel 1984? Try, nobody did that kind of thing."
**David Live (1974)** : A double-live album recorded in October 1974, capturing performances from the ambitious "Diamond Dogs" tour. This release contributed to Bowie's incredible output of five studio albums and one live record within a span of just two years and ten months. "That fall, October 1974, a double-live album was taken from the tour that made for five studio albums and one live record in just two years and ten months."
**Young Americans (1975)** : Released on March 7, 1975, this album marked the debut of yet another Bowie persona: the "blue-eyed plastic soul singer." It was his sixth album in three years and two months. The album showcased Bowie's new interest in American soul and R&B, featuring a smoother, funkier sound developed during studio breaks on the "Diamond Dogs" tour. "Young Americans introduced yet another Bowie. The blue-eyed plastic soul singer."
**Blackstar (2016)** : This album was released on Friday, January 8, 2016, which also coincided with David Bowie's 69th birthday. It was his final album, arriving just three days before his death. "The man had just released a brand new album that Friday. It was barely three days old."

## songs
**Liza Jane (1964)** : This song marked David Bowie's (then David Jones) first ever appearance on record, released on June 5, 1964, with the group David Jones and the King Bees. It was an early attempt by Bowie to make a mark in the music industry. "The first time David Bowie ever appeared on record, the song is Liza Jane."
**The Laughing Gnome (1967)** : Released on April 14, 1967, this song is cited as an example of Bowie's early musical experiments during a period when he was "very, very lost in the 1960s." It represents his struggle to find a definitive style before later breakthroughs. "That's the laughing gnome released on April 14, 1967."
**Space Oddity (1969)** : Emerging after Bowie saw Stanley Kubrick's "2001: Space Odyssey" while stoned in 1968, this song was inspired by the film's character David Bowman and possibly Tom Major, a failed British trapeze artist who also made garden gnomes. Initially rejected multiple times as a "novelty song," it became Bowie's first hit due to the BBC using it as a theme for their Apollo Moon mission coverage. It reached number five on the UK charts, but only peaked at number 124 in North America initially, before a 1973 re-release saw it reach number 15 in the US and number 16 in Canada. "That song became David Bowie's first ever hit. And only because of some lucky timing."
**Changes (1971)** : A hit song from the "Hunky Dory" album, released in 1971. This track helped solidify Bowie's rising status and showcased his evolving musical style, moving beyond his earlier experimental phases. "He released an album called Hunky Dory and it gave him another hit in the form of a song called Changes."
**The Jean Genie (1973)** : This song from the "Aladdin Sane" album was written in part about his friend Iggy Pop and inspired by Jean Genet, a writer, poet, and political activist. The track captured the essence of Bowie's transitional "Aladdin Sane" persona, which took the Ziggy Stardust concept to a new level. "The Gene Genie written in part about his even crazier friend, Eiki Pop, and inspired by Jean Genie. Another friend who was a writer, poet, and political activist."
**Rebel Rebel (1974)** : This song is from Bowie's "Diamond Dogs" album. It exemplifies the bold and theatrical aesthetic of the era, fitting within an album loosely based on George Orwell's "1984." "Rebel, Rebel, be on your dress. Rebel, Rebel, your face is on this."
**Fame (1975)** : Co-written by David Bowie and John Lennon, this song originated from a conversation they had about the difficulties of being famous, with the lyrics co-written in about 20 minutes. It features Bowie's lead vocals and Lennon's background vocals (emphasizing "fame" in the verses). Although Bowie reportedly didn't like the song much and it was tacked onto the "Young Americans" album at the last minute, it was released as a single and became his first ever number one hit in America. "And it became Bowie's first ever number one in America."
**Lazarus (2016)** : This song was one of the singles from David Bowie's final album, "Blackstar," which was released on his 69th birthday, January 8, 2016. Its release shortly before his death added significant poignant meaning to the track. "David Bowie and Lazarus, one of the singles from his final album, released on Friday, January 8, 2016, which also happened to be his 69th birthday."

## curiosities
**Death and Diagnosis (January 11, 2016)** : David Bowie died at 2:30 in the morning of Monday, January 11, 2016, due to liver cancer. His illness had been diagnosed approximately 18 months prior to his passing, a fact kept largely private from the public. "Bowie was gone, a victim of liver cancer that had been diagnosed about 18 months earlier."
**Initial Reaction to Death** : Upon hearing the news of his death, many initially believed it to be an internet hoax, given Bowie's special status and the fact he had just released a new album three days prior. The disbelief stemmed from his recent musical activity and perceived invincibility. "My initial reaction was this surely was another internet hoax. This was Bowie. He was special, probably immortal."
**Post-Death World Deterioration** : Many people observed a noticeable decline in global affairs after Bowie's death, leading to a "meme" that the world "kind of started going a hell" after he died. This includes political, social, economic, and technological shifts like Brexit, Putin's actions, the Ukraine conflict, COVID, Gaza, and the rise of AI. "I'm sure it's all a coincidence, but it's no exaggeration to say that since Bowie died, we've transitioned into a completely different world."
**Toronto Concert After Death** : After Bowie's death, a planned concert in Toronto with the surviving members of his Spiders from Mars band proceeded. The mood at this show was described as "stunned," with plenty of denial evident among fans as the truth of his passing began to sink in. "I was at that Tuesday show in Toronto, and the word to describe the mood was stunned."
**Influence and Impact** : Bowie's legacy extends beyond music, influencing image, the concert arena, fashion, film, stage, painting, museums, and career management. He was a foundational figure for genres like rock, punk, new wave, goth, industrial, electronic dance music, grunge, and indie rock, appealing to diverse artists from Lady Gaga to the Sex Pistols. "Yes, his music has been extraordinarily influential, but that's only part of it."
**Importance for Gay People** : David Bowie was one of the most significant figures for gay people in pop culture, having tremendous social and political implications, especially in the UK. His public statements and gender-bending image provided representation and courage to many marginalized individuals. "David Bowie was one of the most important figures for gay people that pop culture has ever seen."
**Internet Pioneer** : Bowie was noted as an internet pioneer, suggesting his involvement or foresight in the early development and use of the internet within his career. "Bowie was an internet pioneer."
**Original Name: David Jones** : Before adopting his famous stage name, David Bowie was known by his birth name, David Jones. He began his musical career under this name and initially struggled to make an impact. "It was pretty, you know, spacious Bowie or David Jones as he was known back then, which is his real name, tried real hard to make something happen with music."
**Early TV Appearance (November 12, 1964)** : At 17 years old, David Jones appeared on television as a spokesman for "The League for the Protection of Animal Filenet." This was a completely fictitious support group created by him and his friends for men with long hair, designed as a scam to get on TV and earn about five pounds each. "In 1964, at the age of 17, he appeared as a spokesman for the league for the protection of animal filenet, a totally fictitious support group for men who chose to wear their hair long."
**Name Change Motivation** : David Jones changed his name to David Bowie to avoid confusion with two other notable figures: a poet and painter of some renown in South London also named David Jones, and David Jones, the short kid who moved to America and joined the TV band The Monkees. "First, there was a poet and painter of some renowned and South London named David Jones. And then there was that short kid who moved to America and joined a TV band called The Monkeys."
**Inspiration for "Bowie" Name** : David Jones chose the name "Bowie" after becoming somewhat obsessed with the character Jim Bowie, played by actor Richard Widmark in the movie "The Alamo." Jim Bowie was a real-life American pioneer, soldier, smuggler, and slave trader famous during the Battle of the Alamo in Texas. "Our David Jones had just seen a movie called The Alamo. He became somewhat obsessed with the character played by actor Richard Widmark. That character's name was Jim Bowie."
**Correct Pronunciation of "Bowie"** : The correct pronunciation of his adopted surname is "Bowie," not "Bowie" or "Bow-ee." Bowie himself affirmed this pronunciation, despite guitarist Mick Ronson and others sometimes mispronouncing it. "It should be pronounced Bowie. And trust me, I spoke to the man himself."
**Early Career Experiments (1967-1969)** : During this period, Bowie extensively experimented to find his artistic niche. His endeavors included living in a Buddhist monastery, taking dance lessons, learning to become a mime, working in a printing shop, and being cast in an ice cream commercial. The commercial was notably directed by a then-unknown Ridley Scott. "Between 1967 and 1969, Bowie tried to find his niche. He experimented with all kinds of different things."
**Confidence from "Space Oddity" Success** : The success of "Space Oddity," particularly its high chart position in the UK, provided Bowie with much-needed confidence. This newfound self-assurance encouraged him to be even bolder with his experiments in image and presentation, leading to his iconic gender-bending styles. "This success gave Bowie some much needed confidence. He felt he could even be more bold with his experiments with the image and presentation, which often involves some serious gender bending."
**Glam Rock Genesis** : David Bowie was significantly inspired by Mark Bolan, a former folk musician who formed the psych band T-Rex. Bolan's appearance on "Top of the Pops" in March 1971, wearing satin clothes covered with glitter dust, struck Bowie as brilliant and marked the beginning of Glam Rock. "This was the beginning of Glam Rock."
**Glam Rock Inspirations** : Bowie's full dive into Glam Rock was inspired by Mark Bolan, his "crazy new friend" Iggy Pop, another friend Lou Reed, and a 1968 seven-inch single called "Paralyzed" by "The Legendary Stardust Cowboy." Drawing on his background as an actor and mime, Bowie meticulously experimented with makeup and costuming. "Inspired by Bowen, his crazy new friend Iggy Pop, Lou Reed, another new friend, and a seven inch single from 1968 called Paralyzed by a guy calling himself the legendary Stardust Cowboy. Bowie went all in."
**Iconic Haircut and Stylists** : Bowie's new haircut was revolutionary and as historically significant as Elvis's curls or The Beatles' mop tops. Inspired by a model in a magazine wearing designs by Japanese fashion figure Kansai Yamamoto, the cut was replicated on Bowie by stylist Susie Fussy (his mom's hairdresser). It featured razor-cut sides and back, backcombed into a puffy fluff ball, and dyed a bright reddish-orange, transforming him into the "Elfin Cute Boy in the Dress." "Bowie's new haircut was every bit as important to rock history as Elvis's curls or the Beatles mop tops."
**Ziggy Stardust Persona** : Bowie immersed himself fully into the persona of Ziggy Stardust, a "doomed pansexual alien." This character was a "pre-packaged synthetic ultra glamorous, ultra theatrical, fantastical rock star" that fundamentally changed the presentation of rock music. "Bowie immersed himself into this new persona, this doomed pansexual alien, and he invented a new name for himself. It was Ziggy Stardust."
**Incalculable Impact of Ziggy Stardust** : The impact of Bowie's Ziggy Stardust persona on rock music is considered "almost incalculable." In 1972, rock was dominated by jeans, t-shirts, and long hair, but Ziggy's makeup, costumes, and hair completely revolutionized the visual aspect of rock. "Now take it from me, the impact of Bowie's Ziggy Stardust on rock is almost incalculable, and here's why."
**Concerts as Theater** : With Ziggy Stardust, Bowie transformed rock concerts from mere performances by musicians standing on a low stage into theatrical spectacles. These shows incorporated choreography and elaborate costuming, making the concert an integral part of the "show" itself, blending hard rock with vaudeville, glitter pop with real theater, and fashion with technology. "Back then, concerts were basically a bunch of dude standing around on a low stage. But with Ziggy, the concert was theater, a spectacle with its choreography and costuming."
**Feminization of Male Rock Stars** : Bowie was the first to openly and unabashedly feminize the macho male rock and roll star. His pioneering embrace of androgyny led to a subsequent wave of acts that were androgynous, bisexual, gay, or "plain femme looking," including artists like Depeche Mode, Gary Numan, Soft Cell, and Eurythmics. "Bowie was the first to unabashedly and openly feminize the macho male rock and roll star."
**Flippant "Gay" Quote (January 1972)** : In January 1972, Bowie gave a flippant quote to a British music paper stating he had "always been gay." This statement was an enormous "bomb" at the time, given that homosexuality had only been decriminalized in Britain for over-21s in England and Wales in 1967, and was still criminalized elsewhere in the UK. This public declaration, despite its flippant nature, profoundly resonated with thousands of gay and confused individuals. "In January, 1972, Bowie gave a flippant quote to a British music paper about having always been gay."
**Inspiration for LGBTQ+ Individuals** : Bowie's public comments about his sexuality in 1972 struck a chord with thousands who were afraid to come out, giving them courage and a sense of shared identity with a famous figure. Figures like Boy George of Culture Club and Holly Johnson of Frankie Goes to Hollywood have stated that Bowie's comments changed their lives. "Bowie gave them the courage to be who they were."
**Recanting Sexuality Claims** : Bowie later recanted his claims of being gay or even bisexual. This retraction led to significant criticism from gay rights activists, who felt he had withdrawn support from the community he initially inspired. "Bowie would later recant claims of being gay or even bisexual, something that resulted in plenty of criticism from the gay rights activists."
**Influence on Punk Rock** : Bowie's "outsider-ness" in the early 1970s resonated with marginalized individuals, including the working class, and had a profound effect on the development of punk rock. The first generation of British punks admired his short, sharp songs, wild fashion sense, and defiant attitude. "David Bowie's outsider-ness of the early 1970s endeared him to other people who felt alienated and marginalized."
**Steve Jones' Theft of Bowie's Gear (July 3, 1973)** : Following Bowie's final Ziggy Stardust show at the Hammersmith Odeon in London on July 2, 1973, a young thief named Steve Jones (who would later form The Sex Pistols) broke into the venue. While a night watchman slept, Jones stole microphones, amps, and cymbals, some of which would be used to start his new band. "A young thief named Steve Jones somehow got inside and managed to help himself to whatever he could carry out, mostly some microphones and some amps along with a couple of symbols."
**Admiration by British Punks** : The early British punks loved Bowie, admiring his concise songs, bold fashion, and rebellious attitude of not caring what others thought, even in the face of ridicule. His friendships with "cool" figures like Iggy Pop and Lou Reed, and his willingness to "kill himself off" creatively, also resonated deeply with them. "The kids who would become the first generation of British punks loved Bowie. They admired the short, sharp songs, his wild fashion sense, and the way he acted like he didn't care about what anybody thought."
**"Retirement" of Ziggy Stardust** : At the end of the Ziggy Stardust tour, Bowie made a speech implying retirement, which many people mistook for his own. However, he actually meant that he was "killing off his Ziggy," already planning a new persona to channel his creative energies. "What he really meant though was that he was killing off his Ziggy."
**Inspiration for "Aladdin Sane" Name** : The name for Bowie's new persona, "Aladdin Sane," was a phonetic play on "A Lad Insane," offering a glimpse into his mental state after the intense "Ziggy" experience and his concerns for his half-brother, Terry, who suffered from severe mental health issues. "He called his new image a Latin scene. Separate that to a lad in scene and you'll get the tiniest idea of where his head was at."
**Personal Impact of Terry's Mental Health** : The "Ziggy" experience was incredibly intense for all involved, and Bowie was also deeply affected by his adored half-brother, Terry, who suffered terribly from manic depression and schizophrenia and required hospitalization. Terry later died by suicide more than a decade later, by lying down in front of a train. "Not only had the Ziggy experience been crazy for all involved, but he also had a half brother. He adored named Terry, who was suffering terribly with mental health issues."
**Prolific Output (1970s)** : Bowie's rise to stardom was characterized by an incredible prolificness, releasing "album after album, single after single, character after character, tour after tour." This constant stream of new material was remarkable, especially as his records were commercial and critical successes and each was distinct from the last, creating immense mystery and public curiosity. "That prolificness was a big part of Bowie's rise to start him."
**Ambitious Diamond Dogs Tour (1974)** : The tour accompanying the "Diamond Dogs" album was one of the most ambitious rock had ever seen at the time. It featured elaborate sets, frequent costume changes, complex choreography, and a cherry picker boom that lifted Bowie over the audience during "Space Oddity." This hugely expensive and complex spectacle was groundbreaking, taking the basic rock and roll show to unprecedented levels of theater and spectacle, and audiences loved it. "The tour they came with the Diamond Dogs album was one of the most ambitious things Rock had ever seen."
**Transition to "Elegant" Persona** : During the "Diamond Dogs" tour, fans observed Bowie undergoing a transition, moving from his "spaceman" image to a more elegant aesthetic. This included shorter hair, tailored suits, and an overall air of sophistication, reflecting a shift in both his personal style and musical direction towards American soul and R&B. "Bowie became less space man and more elegant. Elder hair, tailored suits and this air of sophistication."
**Collaboration with John Lennon** : By 1975, Bowie was famous enough that John Lennon was willing to collaborate, co-writing the song "Fame." Lennon, in the middle of his "lost weekend" separation from Yoko Ono, happily partied and worked with Bowie. Their professional and personal relationships extended to their associates, with Lennon's assistant, May Pang, eventually marrying Bowie's producer, Tony Visconti. "By this time Bowie was big enough that someone like John Lennon was willing to hang out and even do a little co-writing."
**Blue-Eyed Plastic Soul Singer** : The "Young Americans" album introduced a new Bowie persona: the "blue-eyed plastic soul singer." This look featured a new haircut with a part on the right, baggy pants, and stylishly baggy sweatshirts, embodying a professed love for black music. This endorsement encouraged many of his fans to explore soul and R&B. "Young Americans introduced yet another Bowie. The blue-eyed plastic soul singer."
**Widespread Musical Appeal** : Bowie's music had such widespread appeal that it was played across diverse radio formats, including Top 40 stations and rock stations. It was considered "cool" in punk clubs, and his songs like "Fame" or "Young Americans" were often mixed in with dance music at disco venues, demonstrating his broad influence across genres. "And Bowie's cache had so much widespread appeal that his music was played on both top 40 stations and rock stations."
**Personal Struggles (Mid-1970s)** : Despite his global superstardom, Bowie was in a terrible personal state in the mid-1970s. He was drinking heavily and had developed a powerful cocaine addiction, leading him to believe he was "going quite insane" and worrying about witches and UFOs. Paradoxically, these struggles did not diminish his influence, but rather made him appear even more mysterious and, consequently, bigger. "He was going quite insane, worried about witches and UFOs."
**Leading Visionary of Rock (Late 1970s)** : As the 1970s drew to a close, Bowie was widely regarded as the leading visionary in rock music. He was consistently several steps ahead of his contemporaries, possessing acute instincts for future trends, or perhaps, actively shaping the direction in which he wanted music to evolve. "He was always several steps ahead of everybody else. And his instincts for what was to come next were bang on."

# genre - Glam Rock

## curiosities
**Origin and Aesthetics** : Glam Rock began with Mark Bolan of T-Rex appearing on "Top of the Pops" in March 1971, wearing satin clothes covered with glitter dust, which David Bowie found brilliant. Bowie, inspired by Bolan, Iggy Pop, Lou Reed, and The Legendary Stardust Cowboy, fully embraced the genre by using his acting and mime lessons, experimenting with makeup and costuming, and adopting a revolutionary haircut. "This was the beginning of Glam Rock."

# curiosity

**Homosexuality Criminalization in Britain** : Before 1967, it was a crime to be a homosexual in Britain, with individuals facing charges and imprisonment under the Sexual Offences Act. Many lives were ruined by this law. Even after decriminalization in 1967, the new conditions only applied to people over the age of 21 in England and Wales. Homosexuality remained a crime in Scotland until 1980 and in Northern Ireland until 1982, and for members of the armed forces in the UK. Post-1967, police arrests for "gross indecency" even tripled, indicating continued fear and persecution. "Until 1967, it was a crime to be a homosexual in Britain."
