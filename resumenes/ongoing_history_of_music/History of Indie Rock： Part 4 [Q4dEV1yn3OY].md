# curiosity
**Internet's Impact on Music Industry (Late 1990s)** : The advent of the internet, with its file-sharing and illegal downloading capabilities in the late 1990s, was likened to a "mass extinction event" for the music industry, severely exacerbating already difficult conditions for rock music. "the internet came along with its file sharing an illegal downloading. It was like some kind of mass extinction event."
**Indie Resilience vs. Major Label Struggles (Early 2000s)** : In the early 21st century, while major labels were battered by file sharing and falling market share, indie labels demonstrated remarkable resilience by "bobbin and weven," adapting to survive and, in doing so, producing "pretty amazing music" that captured substantial public attention. "While the majors were being battered by file sharing and falling market share, the Indies were just bobbin and weven doing whatever they had to do to survive. And in the process, they gave us some pretty amazing music that caught the attention of a substantial number of people."
**Public Perception of Indie vs. Major Labels (Early 21st Century)** : In the early 21st century, a widespread sentiment among music fans depicted indie labels as performing "God's work" by caring about music, while major labels were seen as being "punished" for perceived decades of "sins," including overpriced CDs, one-sided deals, and prioritizing money over art. This fostered an "Indie good major bad attitude." "there was a sense among a significant number of music fans that the Indies were doing God's work. They were of the opinion that Rath from on high was visiting itself upon the majors for decades of sins over price CDs, one-sided record deals, disregard for artists and fans, a bunch of money-grubbing bean counters who cared about money more than music and needed to be punished."
**Independent Music's Resurgence (Early 21st Century)** : Independent labels and the music they release achieved unprecedented importance for the overall health of the music industry in decades, profoundly influencing the market. This led major labels to adopt indie aesthetics, and former indie labels were acquired by majors, often retaining their operations while gaining financial security. "Indie labels and the music they release hasn't been this important to the overall health of the music industry in decades, and their influence on the market overall has been pronounced."
**Convergence of Major and Indie Labels** : The traditional "war" between major and indie labels has largely ended, giving way to an era of mutual cooperation. This shift has resulted in more music from more bands reaching the public, a trend further amplified by artists increasingly bypassing labels entirely thanks to technology and the internet. "The war between major versus indie has pretty much ended. Instead, we're into an era of mutual cooperation, which is good since it's meant that more music for more bands is getting out there. And when you factor in all the artists who are trying to avoid any kind of label arrangement thanks to technology in the internet, the amount of music that we have to choose from these days has exploded."
**Core Values of Indie Artists** : The fundamental values driving every indie artist remain consistent: a strong desire for self-expression and a conviction that "I got something to say, and I'm going to find a way to say it no matter what." This ethos, coupled with the belief that there will always be those willing to help and listen, has redefined what it means to be a musician and a music business person in the 21st century. "What hasn't changed though is the core values that run through the veins of every indie artist. I got something to say, and I'm going to find a way to say it no matter what. And there will always be those willing to help and willing to listen. Indie has redefined what it means to be a musician and a music business person in the 21st century."
