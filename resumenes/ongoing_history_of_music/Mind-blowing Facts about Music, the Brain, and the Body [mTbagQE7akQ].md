# concert - Live Performance
## curiosities
**Neurological Synchronization and Entrainment** : The transcendent feeling experienced at an amazing live show, where performers and audience seem entirely in sync, is a neurological response stemming from mirror neurons in the right hemisphere of the brain. These neurons facilitate synchronicity with the thoughts and actions of others, making music an excellent vehicle for this state. This process, known as "entrainment," fosters a collective bond that enhances individual enjoyment, with research even suggesting it's better for well-being than practicing yoga. "That's a neurological response generated by what's called mirror neurons in the right hemisphere of your brain. They allow us to get in sync with the thoughts and actions of others."

**Time Perception Warping** : During a live music performance, if the music is compelling, the brain can readjust and recalibrate temporal perception, causing minutes or even hours to pass in a flash. This phenomenon, where time seems to lose meaning, is attributed to music-induced chemicals in our brains literally distorting our perception of time. "If the music is just right, the brain will readjust and recalibrate temporal perception. This explains why time sometimes has no meaning when you're listening to music."

**Band Chemistry as Literal Chemistry** : When musicians play together, they can literally get on the same wavelength, with their brains generating precise amounts of specific chemicals across no fewer than 12 distinct parts of the brain. This neurological synchronicity provides a literal basis for the common phrase "band chemistry," referring to a real mix of chemicals within their beings. "Everyone literally gets on the same wavelength. Their brains generating the correct chemicals and exactly the correct amounts using no fewer than 12 distinct parts of the brain."

**Post-Concert Hearing Impairment** : Exposure to very loud music for extended periods at concerts can result in a temporary muffled, "underwater" sensation in hearing. This is caused by powerful sound waves damaging the cilia, tiny hairs in the inner ears that convert acoustic energy into electrical impulses for the brain. It typically takes about three days for cilia to regenerate and hearing to return to normal, but repeated damage can permanently impair this regenerative ability, leading to irreversible hearing loss. "Too much volume destroys or impairs Cilia, resulting in an inability for them to do their job. It takes about three days for them to regenerate, which is when you're hearing returns."

# curiosity

**Brain's Musical Hardwiring** : The human brain, a three-pound organ containing billions of neurons, appears to be naturally hardwired for music, possessing special areas exclusively devoted to processing musical information. Possible evolutionary theories suggest this might be a result of ancient ancestors imitating bird song, a development related to language, a tool for storytelling (as details are easier to remember with music), or a phenomenon that evolved alongside religious rituals and chants. "There is no obvious biological reason for it, but our brain seemed to be hardwired for music. There were special areas of the brain devoted to just dealing with music and nothing else."

**Music and Brain Lobes** : The experience of music actively engages all four major lobes of the brain, with different regions responsible for specific functions such as emotional reactions, memory formation, and movement motivated by music. Scientists have only recently begun to precisely map which brain parts are dedicated to processing music. "Experiencing music requires all four of the brain's major lobes."

**Temporal Lobe and Music Recognition** : The temporal lobe, situated centrally in the brain, has two hemispheres: the left hemisphere is responsible for interpreting speech, while the right hemisphere is crucial for recognizing the structures and patterns inherent in music. This right-side region is interconnected with the auditory cortex. "The temporal lobe, a hunk of brain right in the middle, has two hemispheres. One, the one on the left, interprets speech. The one on the right helps us recognize the structures and patterns of music."

**Auditory Cortex Function** : Located directly behind the ears and approximately the size of a thumb, the auditory cortex serves as a primary filter and sorter for all incoming audio information. It breaks down what we hear into fundamental components and then funnels these bits to the appropriate processing areas of the brain, working in conjunction with the cerebellum. "It's the thing that filters and sorts all the audio information coming at us. It breaks down what we hear into rudimentary bits and funnels those bits to the appropriate parts of the brain."

**Singing-Specific Neurons** : Within the auditory cortex, there exists a unique group of neurons that activate and align themselves exclusively when the brain perceives singing. These specialized neurons do not respond to spoken words or purely instrumental music, and their specific function and reason for this selectivity remain unknown to scientists. "the auditory cortex contains a group of neurons that line up only when we hear singing. That's all they do."

**Musical Memories in the Hippocampus** : Musical memories are stored in the hippocampus, the brain region responsible for all memory formation, yet they are distinct and separate from other life memories. Studies on individuals suffering from irreversible amnesia due to disease or accident have shown that while they lose all personal memories, including their name, their ability to remember favorite songs, play instruments, and read music (if previously learned) astonishingly remains intact. "musical memories are personal database of familiar songs, are, while still in the hippocampus, separate from all our other memories."

**Broca's Area and Musical Expression** : Broca's area, located between the frontal and temporal lobes, is a crucial brain region involved not only in the production of speech but also in the expression of music, such as singing or playing an instrument. It is situated in close proximity to Wernicke's area and the auditory cortex. "This is the spot that not only helps us produce speech, but it's what we use to express music, like playing an instrument or singing."

**Wernicke's Area and Music Appreciation** : Wernicke's area, positioned near Broca's area and the auditory cortex, is a region of the brain that plays a role in the analysis and enjoyment of music. "We use this part of the brain to analyze and enjoy music."

**Nucleus Accumbens and Dopamine Release** : The nucleus accumbens, identified as the pleasure-seeking center of the brain, releases a blast of dopamine—the body's natural "feel-good" hormone—when an individual encounters something they like, including a favorite song. This biological mechanism implies a design within our brains that seeks out pleasurable experiences akin to "sex, drugs, and rock and roll." "When we find something we like, the nucleus accumbens give us a blast of a hormone called dopamine, which is the body's natural feel-good drug."

**Amygdala and Emotional Processing** : The amygdala, interconnected with the nucleus accumbens, is the brain region where all emotions are processed and triggered. The sensation of "chills" experienced when listening to a great song originates here, manifesting as a direct result of a dopamine rush. "This is where all our emotions are processed and triggered. Again, when we hear a great song and you feel chills, this is where they come from. That's a dopamine rush."

**Caudate and Musical Anticipation** : The caudate, a specific sub-region within the brain, possesses the remarkable ability to anticipate the emotional climax of a song up to 15 seconds before it actually occurs. This predictive capacity is why songs that progressively build intensity before a significant drop or release evoke such a strong, pleasurable sensation, as the brain instinctively senses a forthcoming "big payoff." "the co-date can somehow anticipate the emotional climax of a song up to 15 seconds before it happens."

**Hypothalamus and Physiological Responses to Music** : The hypothalamus, a brain region that regulates essential bodily functions such as thirst, hunger, sleep, heart rate, body temperature, and mood, reacts directly to music. Playing relaxing music causes the hypothalamus to produce hormones that reduce heart rate and blood pressure, while high-energy music triggers the opposite effect, increasing these rates and inducing a burst of energy. "If you play some relaxing music, the hypothalamus reacts by producing essential hormones that produce heart rate and blood pressure."

**Putamen and Movement to Music** : The putamen, an area of the brain responsible for processing rhythm, body movement, and coordination, becomes active when music-generated dopamine flows into it. This often results in a spontaneous need to move with the music, such as tapping a foot, banging a beat, or dancing, as the body instinctively synchronizes. "Once music-generated dopamine gets into this area of the brain, we may feel a spontaneous need to move with the music."

**Cordiospinal Excitability (Urge to Dance)** : A song featuring a compelling beat or groove can promote something known as "cordiospinal excitability," which is essentially the urge to dance. This physiological response involves pumping extra blood to the legs and changes to heart rate and breathing patterns, allowing the body to instinctively sync with the music. "if a song has a good beat or a good groove, it promotes something called cordiospinal excitability, otherwise known as the urge to dance."

**Individual Dance Styles** : Research conducted by Finnish scientists has concluded that every individual possesses a distinct and unique style of dancing. The specific way a person moves to music is apparently as unique and identifiable as their fingerprints. "more Finnish researchers concluded that everyone has their own unique style of dancing. How you move to music is apparently as distinctive as your fingerprints."

**Speed of Song Recognition** : The human brain is extraordinarily fast at recognizing songs, capable of retrieving virtually all associated information from its musical database in the hippocampus. The typical time required to retrieve a musical memory is approximately 100 milliseconds, which is quicker than the blink of an eye and faster than a beam of light could travel around the Earth's equator. "Science says that the typical amount of time it takes us to retrieve a musical memory is about a hundred milliseconds. That's faster than the blink of an eye."

**Brain's Ability to Correctly Identify Reversed Music** : An experiment demonstrated that even when presented with a half-second clip of a song played in reverse, the brain's extremely fast processing of musical memories allowed for correct identification of the song. This illustrates the remarkable efficiency and robustness of our memory system for music. "Yet your brain would have none of that. It worked so fast with your musical memories that it was able to correctly identify the song even though it was hearing it wrong."

**13 Universal Emotions Evoked by Music** : A study involving 2500 participants across North America and China revealed that music can consistently elicit exactly 13 distinct emotions, irrespective of an individual's musical preferences or cultural background. These emotions include amusement, joy, eroticism, beauty, relaxation, sadness, dreaminess, triumph, anxiety, scaringness, annoyance, defiance, and feeling pumped up. "Scientists say that music can bring forth exactly 13 different emotions, regardless of who you are or what kind of music you like, or it seems your cultural background."

**Music-Induced Chills (Piloerection)** : A 2020 study by French researchers, using EEG machines on volunteers who regularly experience chills from music, identified specific electrical activity in the Orbital Frontal Cortex (involved in emotion processing), the supplementary motor area (controlling movement), and the audio processing areas of the right temporal lobe (related to music appreciation). These regions collectively prompt the amygdala to release more dopamine, and combined with anticipation, result in a tingly chill emanating from the cerebellum, indicating heightened brain connectivity. "They found that certain music created very specific electrical activity in an area called the Orbital Frontal Cortex, a region that we use for processing emotions."

**Brain Connectivity and Chills** : Research from California indicates that individuals who frequently experience chills when listening to music possess a greater number of connecting fibers between their auditory cortex and the brain regions responsible for processing emotions. This enhanced communication allows for a more fluid interplay between music and emotions, leading to a distinct physical response, suggesting such individuals have an "extra special brain." "people who get chills when listening to music have more fibers connecting the auditory cortex to those parts of the brain that deal with processing emotions."

**Queen Mary University Chills Playlist** : Queen Mary University in London compiled an extensive Spotify playlist featuring 700 songs that were found to be most likely to create a chills and/or goosebumps response in listeners. Their research highlighted that sadder, slower, less intense, and more instrumental songs were generally the most effective in inducing these physiological reactions. "Queen Mary University in London went so far as to create a Spotify playlist of the 700 songs, most likely to create a chills and or goosebumps response in people."

**Sound Symbolism and Lyrics** : Humans possess the capacity to appreciate and enjoy sounds, including singing, without necessarily needing to understand the literal meaning of the lyrics. The brain is primarily evolved to respond to sound first, treating lyrics as enjoyable sounds rather than words. This explains why people can appreciate songs in foreign languages or derive pleasure from music without concentrating on lyrics in their native tongue. "Basically, you could appreciate and enjoy sounds, like singing, without really needing to understand what's being sung. We're just enjoying the lyrics as sounds, not as words."

**Coffee and Post-Concert Hearing Impairment** : Following exposure to high volumes of music at a concert which can cause temporary hearing impairment, consuming coffee is ill-advised. The combination of caffeine and audio overstimulation has been shown to extend the duration required for hearing to return to its normal state. "Caffeine plus audio overstimulation equals more time for your hearing to get back to normal."

**Musical Taste and Parent's Height** : Research suggests a curious link between an individual's musical taste and the height of the parent who took primary responsibility for their upbringing. The theory proposes that babies attuned to a shorter parent's quicker walking pace (due to shorter legs) might develop a preference for faster musical rhythms, whereas those with taller parents (longer strides, slower pace) might lean towards slower music. "Your taste in music may be related to the height of your parents, or specifically, the parent which did most of the parenting."

**Music Appreciation While High (Cannabis)** : The perception that music sounds better when under the influence of cannabis is not an illusion. Cannabis activates CB1 receptors in the brain, causing them to become hyperactive, which in turn enhances activity in the metalymbic dopamine system when music is present. This leads to increased dopamine secretion for every note heard, intensifying the "feel-good" sensation. Additionally, cannabis interferes with short-term memory, fostering a heightened focus on the present moment and suppressing the brain's natural tendency to anticipate what comes next, allowing listeners to perceive previously unnoticed details in both complex and simple arrangements. "Music plus cannabis enhances activity in the metalymbic dopamine system. That means for every note of music we hear, we get extra dopamine secretion caused by cannabis."

**Music for Pain Management** : Research from the University of Utah indicates that listening to music, specifically Mozart, can significantly augment the pain-reducing effects of medication like ibuprofen. While ibuprofen alone reduced inflammation by 70%, its efficacy increased to 90% when combined with listening to Mozart in mouse trials, suggesting a promising avenue for new human pain management strategies. "when they gave pain sufferers ibuprofen and had them listen to Mozart at the same time, that inflammation decreased when from 70% to 90%, which meant even less pain."

**Optimal Daily Music Listening for Health** : A study conducted in France has determined that listening to music for exactly 78 minutes daily can contribute positively to overall health. For maximum health benefits, this daily listening should be structured into specific emotional categories: 14 minutes of uplifting music for happiness, 16 minutes of calming music, 16 minutes of music to counteract sadness, 15 minutes of motivational music for concentration, and 17 minutes of music specifically for dealing with anger. "A study from France determined the exact amount of music that we should listen to every day so we stay in good health, and that number is exactly 78 minutes."

**Health Benefits of Singing in the Shower** : A small study conducted in the UK suggests that the simple act of singing in the shower every morning can improve both physical and mental well-being. Participants reported feeling generally better about everything after just five days of this routine. "Singing in the shower might actually be good for your physical and mental health."

**Increased Noise Sensitivity Post-Pandemic** : Studies indicate that individuals who experienced pandemic-related lockdowns have developed a heightened sensitivity to noise. One particular study found that 43% of participants reported they could no longer tolerate loud music to the same degree as they could prior to the pandemic. "43% of the people in one study say that they just can't tolerate loud music the way they used to."

**Manipulating Musical Taste** : Researchers at McGill University in Montreal made a discovery that musical taste can be manipulated within minutes using a technique called transcranial magnetic stimulation (a "magnetic zap") to the front of the brain. This method could either increase or decrease the amount of enjoyment individuals derived from music, a process believed to involve the regulation of dopamine within the brain, though the precise mechanism is not yet fully understood. "musical taste can be manipulated in minutes just by a little magnetic zap to the front of the brain."
