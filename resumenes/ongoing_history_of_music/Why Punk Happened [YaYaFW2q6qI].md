## genres

## curiosities
**Mid-1970s Emergence** : Punk rock rose in the middle of the 1970s, initially seen as an "aberration," a niche for "weirdos and misfits and outliers," and dismissed as "just noise" by rock purists who thought it would fade away. However, it persisted and is still discussed today. "There's one other event that we need to include on this list, and that's the rise of punk rock in the middle of the 1970s."
**Beyond Music** : Punk evolved beyond a mere musical form, becoming "a way of thinking and acting and creating and presenting" that permeated music, film, visual art, literature, dance, and politics, significantly altering Western thought with its "screw you, I'm going to do it anyway" ethos. "And punk became more than just a form of music. It became a way of thinking and acting and creating and presenting."
**Socio-economic Roots (1974)** : The deconstruction of punk's origins begins in 1974, examining the socio-economic factors that served as a "great musical Big Bang" in history. "Let's start this deconstruction by looking at the socio-economic factors behind one of the great musical Big Bangs in history. And we're going to begin in 1974."
**New York Origins** : Punk initially began in New York City as a "low-key, artsy sort of thing," cultivated by alienated artists in economically depressed areas like the Bowery, who gravitated to venues like CBGB. "To recap, punk started in New York as a low-key artsy sort of thing."
**UK Transformation** : Upon reaching the UK, punk rock took on a distinct identity, intensely fueled by political grievances and the entrenched class system, contrasting with its more artistic New York counterpart. "In England though, things were rather different. English punk was very, very different from what was happening in America. Well, punk was more of an art statement in New York. It was fueled by politics and the class system in the UK."
**British Punk Ethos** : British punk became a vehicle for rebellion, aiming to "kick against the upper classes and the monarchy," shock older generations, challenge established institutions, and destroy the status quo, effectively giving power to the powerless. "British punk went beyond just mere music. It involved kicking against the upper classes in the monarchy. It was about shocking the older generations. It was about challenging all manner of established institutions."
**Counterculture Development (1978-79)** : During the "winter of discontent" (1978-79), exacerbated by widespread strikes and extreme cold, punk rock intensified, transforming "from being this innocuous sort of thing into a genuine counterculture." "All this simply increased the heat of punk. It went from being this innocuous sort of thing into a genuine counterculture."
**Toronto Scene** : The punk scene in Toronto, while tiny, was significant enough to form the "third leg of a triangle that extended through both New York and London." It was not as artsy as New York punk, nor as political or class-conscious as UK punk, but it was characterized by its raw anger. "Toronto punk wasn't as artsy as New York punk. And it certainly wasn't as political or as class conscious as what they were doing in the UK. But man, it could still be really, really angry."
**Los Angeles Adoption** : Los Angeles, a natural home for the alienated and unconventional, quickly adopted punk rock once records from New York bands like the Ramones and singles from the Sex Pistols began circulating. The scene was driven by themes of alienation, hopelessness, and a desire to challenge the status quo. "LA had always been a place for the alienated the outcast and the weird, so it was a natural place for punk to land."
**San Francisco Eclecticism** : San Francisco punk was arguably the "most eclectic" of all the early punk scenes, uniquely blending elements of the artsy, the angry, and the city's established hippie, drug culture, and left-wing political traditions, which paved the way for later rock, new wave, and technopop sounds. "It often combined the best of the artsy side of things with the best of the angry side. And of course, there was the hippie side with its drug culture and left-wing politics."

# curiosity
**Rise of Teenagers (1950s)** : In the 1950s, millions of post-war children emerged as a new demographic construct known as "teenagers." Their gravitation towards new radio stations playing a mix of blues, country, and R&B and its subsequent annoyance to their parents, made this new music dangerous and attractive. "Elvis came along in the 1950s, just as millions of post-war kids, these new constructs that were now being called teenagers, began gravitating to new radio stations that played music derived from a mix of the blues, country and R&B."
**Music Video Transformation (Early 1980s)** : The appearance of music videos in the early 1980s marked a pivotal moment that fundamentally transformed the entire music industry. "You might remember the appearance of the music video in the early 1980s, which transformed the industry."
**Collapse of 1960s Promises (Mid-1970s)** : By the mid-1970s, the idealistic "hippie values of peace and love" associated with the 1960s had largely "collapsed," contributing to a turbulent societal atmosphere. "The promises of the 1960s with its hippie values of peace and love had collapsed."
**Richard Nixon's Resignation** : President Richard Nixon was identified as a criminal who resigned from office in disgrace, contributing to the era's political turbulence. "where Jornixson was a criminal and resigned in disgrace."
**Vietnam War Disaster** : The Vietnam War was unequivocally described as a "disaster," adding to the widespread disillusionment and societal unrest of the mid-1970s. "The Vietnam War, a disaster."
**1973 Oil Crisis** : The oil crisis of 1973 had a severe global impact, crippling the economies of the world and also significantly derailing the welfare state system in the UK. "The oil crisis had crippled the economies of the world and the Middle East blew up into war with regularity."
**Cold War Tensions** : The Cold War between the United States and the Soviet Union continued, with the two superpowers "always snarling at each other," maintaining an atmosphere of international tension and threat. "And of course there was the Cold War with the US and the Soviet Union, always snarling at each other."
**New York City's Decline (1974)** : By 1974, New York City was in a severe state of decay, plagued by "crime, corruption, sleaze, filth, drugs, racial tensions," and was literally on the verge of bankruptcy, with many neighborhoods falling into ruin. The federal government notably refused to provide assistance, telling the city to solve its own problems. "As cities went, New York was especially rotten. Crime, corruption, sleaze, filth, drugs, racial tensions. The city was literally going bankrupt and many neighborhoods were falling into decay and ruin."
**Music Landscape in 1974** : The music scene in 1974 was considered to be in "bad shape," characterized by inaccessible prog rock, millionaire rock stars detached from reality, "soft" California rock, and irrelevant AM radio pop songs like "The Way We Were" and "The Entertainer." "Music was in bad shape too. There was Prague Rock with its virtuoso performances, that was a big thing, but it was hard for the average person to identify with that."
**Artist Living Conditions in NYC (1974)** : Artists struggling to afford housing in New York City were forced to live in undesirable, cheap areas like the Bowery, often in lofts that could cost around $150 a month but lacked basic amenities like toilets, showers, or winter heating. "Because you couldn't afford anything in the good parts of town, you ended up living in the parts of the city that weren't so good. Like the bowry."
**CBGB's Genesis (March 1974)** : Originally a "crappy, scuzzy country, bluegrass and blues club," CBGB began allowing newer rock-oriented bands to play in March 1974, with bands earning door money while the bar kept alcohol sales. Its unique atmosphere, long and narrow room, sparse stage, and lack of dressing rooms, combined with cheap rents in the Bowery, attracted a community of disaffected, left-wing, artsy, anti-establishment artists. "This was the situation. When a crappy, scuzzy country, bluegrass and blues club called CBGB started allowing some newer rock-oriented bands to play."
**UK Economic Devastation (1975)** : In 1975, Great Britain faced an economy even worse than that of the US, still recovering 30 years after World War II with visible bombed-out buildings. The government, burdened by a welfare state derailed by the 1973 oil crisis, ran out of money. Industrial production was in steep decline, leading to three-day workweeks and widespread union strikes, culminating in the Bank of England exhausting its foreign reserves and requiring an IMF bailout. "As bad as the economy in the US was, it was worse in the UK. Even though the war had been over for 30 years, the empire had still not recovered."
**UK Scheduled Blackouts (1970s)** : As a consequence of the UK's economic crisis, scheduled rolling blackouts were implemented, with areas receiving prior notification of when their electricity would be cut, forcing residents to adapt daily routines like cooking around these outages. "There were scheduled rolling blackouts. Areas would receive notification as to when the electricity would be cut."
**Entrenched UK Class System (1970s)** : The class system in the UK was "firmly entrenched," with opportunities for "electricity and advancement" primarily linked to birth rather than individual ability. "The class system was firmly entrenched. The electricity in advancement was linked to birth, not ability."
**Monarchy's Influence in UK (1970s)** : The British monarchy held significant ceremonial power; during the Queen's annual Christmas Day message, all activity in homes across the kingdom would cease, with some older individuals even standing and saluting the TV until her majesty finished speaking. "When the queen delivered her annual message on Christmas Day, all activity in houses across the kingdom would stop."
**UK Unemployment and Hopelessness (1970s)** : The severe recession in the UK led to soaring unemployment, crushing many people with hopelessness and forcing millions to "resign themselves to a life of living on the dole." "Some people were crushed by this recession. They couldn't find work, unemployment, soared, hopelessness set in."
**Tiny Early UK Punk Scene (Late 1975)** : Before the Sex Pistols' TV appearance, word of the New York punk scene reached the UK by late 1975, attracting only "dozens" of young people who kept mostly to themselves and were largely ignored by others, forming a "tiny, tiny, tiny scene." "By the end of 1975, word had drifted across the Atlantic that something was happening in New York.
