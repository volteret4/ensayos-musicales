# artist - The Carpenters

## members
Richard Carpenter
Paul Williams

## songs
**We've Only Just Begun (1970) – Former Bank Jingle** : This song, released in late 1970, started as a failed jingle for the Crocker National Bank of California, part of a campaign designed by Hal Reiner and Partners to appeal to young couples. Songwriter Paul Williams, struggling at the time, was tasked with writing the jingle, which initially had only two verses and a bridge. Richard Carpenter saw the TV commercial, got excited, and tracked Williams down, leading to a finished version of the song. It sold a million copies, spent seven weeks at number one, ten weeks in the top ten, and earned the Carpenters two Grammy Awards. "This song, this giant hit, this multi-million dollar success started as a failed jingle in a bank commercial."

## curiosities
**Crocker National Bank Campaign Failure** : The "We've Only Just Begun" jingle was created for a Crocker National Bank of California campaign aimed at young couples seeking loans. While the TV commercial featuring the jingle was popular, it did not achieve its desired effect as the many young people interested in loans lacked the necessary collateral. Consequently, Crocker suspended the campaign. "So the whole thing was a flop. And Crocker suspended the campaign."
**Jingle Licensing to Recoup Costs** : After Crocker National Bank suspended its campaign, the agency Hal Reiner and Partners, rather than taking a financial hit for the work done, attempted to recoup costs by licensing the "We've Only Just Begun" jingle to other banks. This move inadvertently led to Richard Carpenter discovering the song. "Rather than take the financial hit for all the work, writing and partners had done, they tried to recoup as much as they could by licensing the jingle to other banks."

# artist - David Bowie

## albums
**Heroes (1977) – Berlin Trilogy Album** : This album, released in 1977, is part of David Bowie's "Berlin trilogy" of records. The entire record was made in Studio Two of Hansa Studios, a location about 150 yards from the Berlin Wall, which added to the album's distinct atmosphere. "The whole record was made in Studio Two of Hansa Studios, which was maybe 150 yards from the Berlin Wall."

## songs
**Heroes (1977) – Multiple Inspirations** : This powerful title track from Bowie's 1977 album features artsy lyrics about two lovers finding optimism amid a world collapsing into death, including references to swimming with dolphins. Its meaning is layered, drawing inspiration from producer Tony Visconti's embrace with German singer Antonia Moss by the Berlin Wall, the Otto Mueller painting "Lovers Between Garden Walls," Alberto Denti di Pagano's 1956 short story "A Grave for a Dolphin," and a dream Bowie had about swimming with dolphins followed by a day with his girlfriend Claire Shenstone in East Berlin, watching soldiers. Bowie confirmed seeing Visconti and Moss, and Shenstone claimed their day directly inspired elements like dolphins, the wall, and gun shooting. "So which is true? All of them, apparently."
**Heroes (1977) – Verbatim Lyric Fragment** : This fragment appears to be a partial lyric or an echoing of the song's theme. "I can remember the man, the standing by the wall, the fire."

## curiosities
**Hansa Studios' Historical Significance** : Studio Two of Hansa Studios, where David Bowie recorded his *Heroes* album, has a unique historical background. The building was originally a concert hall that was later used by the Gestapo as a ballroom during World War II. It was then repurposed into a recording studio by a pair of brothers in 1962. "The building was a former concert hall used by the Gestapo during World War II as a ballroom."
**Visconti and Moss Inspiration for "Heroes"** : Producer Tony Visconti provided a long-standing explanation for the lyrics of "Heroes," stating that David Bowie saw him embracing German singer Antonia Moss by the Berlin Wall. Visconti, from the West, and Moss, from the East, were depicted as hating the divided city but insisting on kissing by the wall as bullets flew from guard towers. Bowie later confirmed seeing the couple. "We've been told that one of the lovers is from the west, which is Visconti, and the other from the east, which is Moss."
**Otto Mueller Painting Influence** : David Bowie was inspired by a painting titled "Lovers Between Garden Walls" by Otto Mueller. He and Iggy Pop encountered this artwork in a Berlin museum around the time *Heroes* was being recorded, adding another layer of artistic influence to the song's lyrical themes. "Bowie and his buddy Iggy Pop saw in a Berlin museum around this time."
**"A Grave for a Dolphin" Story Connection** : The reference to swimming with dolphins in "Heroes" lyrics has a connection to a 1956 short story by Alberto Denti di Pagano, titled "A Grave for a Dolphin." This story told of an Italian soldier and a girl from Somalia involved in a doomed relationship during World War II. "Bowie remembered a short story written in 1956 by Alberto Denti di Pagano, entitled A Grave for a Dolphin."
**Claire Shenstone's Day Out with Bowie** : During the recording of the *Heroes* album, Bowie spent a day with his then-girlfriend, model, and artist Claire Shenstone. The night before, Bowie had dreamt he was swimming with dolphins. The next day, they visited East Berlin and observed soldiers marching, an experience which Shenstone claims directly inspired the song's lyrics, specifically the references to dolphins, standing by the wall, and gunshots. "Shenstone claims that the song's lyrics were directly inspired by that day they spent together."

# artist - Joy Division

## members
Ian Curtis
Steven Morris

## labels
Factory Records

## songs
**Love Will Tear Us Apart (1980) – Posthumous Release & "Suicide Note"** : This most famous Joy Division track was one of their last, recorded in March 1980 by producer Martin Hannett at Strawberry studio outside Manchester. It was released on June 27, 1980, approximately five weeks after lead singer Ian Curtis took his own life. The song originated from an August 1979 jam session, with Curtis's lyrics reflecting the intense turmoil of his life, including worsening epilepsy exacerbated by various lifestyle factors, a failing marriage to Deborah, and an affair with Anaconda Rae. Despite its happy-sounding, major key arrangement that initially puzzled the band, its profound lyrical depth and Hannett's meticulous production make it a poignant reflection of Curtis's final weeks, leading it to be called Joy Division's "Suicide Note Song." "It wouldn't be released until June 27, 1980, about five weeks after singer Ian Curtis took his own life."

## curiosities
**Ian Curtis's Personal Turmoil** : The lyrics for "Love Will Tear Us Apart" were deeply personal, drawn from the considerable turmoil in Ian Curtis's life. This included his epilepsy, which was growing worse due to drugs, alcohol, late nights, smoking bad food, and the stress of holding down a day job while being in a struggling band. His failing marriage to Deborah and an affair with a Belgian woman named Anaconda Rae also contributed significantly to his emotional state reflected in the song. "Curtis's lyrics were drawn from the turmoil in his life."
**Lyrics Reflecting Relationships** : Specific lines in "Love Will Tear Us Apart," such as "why is the bedroom so cold and turned away on your side," are understood to be direct expressions from Ian Curtis, speaking to either his wife Deborah or his girlfriend Anaconda Rae, or both. This reveals the deeply personal and autobiographical nature of the song's emotional core. "Lines like why is the bedroom so cold and turned away on your side aren't just lines plucked from somewhere, he's speaking to one or both women in his life."
**Band's Initial Puzzlement over Song Tone** : The other members of Joy Division were initially puzzled by the sound of "Love Will Tear Us Apart," noting its unusually happy tone for a band known for its dark and glum reputation. It was strange to them that a Joy Division song was written in a major key, contrasting sharply with their established style. "It was an awfully happy sounding song for a band with a reputation of being dark and glum."
**Tony Wilson's Vocal Suggestion** : Tony Wilson of Factory Records suggested to Ian Curtis that he try using his baritone range when singing "Love Will Tear Us Apart," in a manner similar to Frank Sinatra. Curtis adopted this suggestion, adding a "very old-school crooning" element to his vocal performance on the track. "Tony Wilson of the band's factory records made a suggestion. Ian tried using the baritone range in the same way Frank Sinatra would when you sing this song."
**Martin Hannett's Production Obsession** : During the recording sessions for "Love Will Tear Us Apart," producer Martin Hannett became intensely obsessed with the song. He meticulously recorded each instrument separately and spent a significant amount of time perfecting Ian Curtis's voice in the mix. His dedication was so extreme that he once called drummer Steven Morris at 4 AM, demanding he come to the studio immediately because he couldn't get the snare drum sound right, and frequently called the band in the middle of the night to hear the latest mix. "Hannet mixed and remix the song again and again and again."
**Strawberry Studio Recording Location** : Joy Division recorded their famous track "Love Will Tear Us Apart" at Strawberry studio, which is located just outside Manchester, in March of 1980. This studio served as the site for one of the band's final and most impactful recording sessions. "It was recorded by producer Martin Hannet in a studio called Strawberry, just outside Manchester in March of 1980."

## songs
**Mr. Brightside (2003) – Breakthrough Betrayal Anthem** : Released in the fall of 2003, "Mr. Brightside" is one of the 21st century's biggest songs, demonstrating incredible staying power, especially in the UK where it's charted for over 450 weeks. It has been streamed nearly 2.5 billion times on Spotify, won numerous awards, and is a staple at sporting events, generating millions for The Killers. The song's origin traces back to Brandon Flowers' heartbreak as a 19-year-old, after he saw his ex-girlfriend with another man at The Crown and Anchor bar in Las Vegas, leading him to furiously write down his spiraling imaginations of their actions later that night, attempting to put on a brave face by calling himself "Mr. Brightside." The music was already written by guitarist Dave Keuning, and their crude demo with a drum machine helped them secure a record deal. "As Brandon has said, who knows that betrayal could sound so good."
**Mr. Brightside (2003) – Verbatim Lyric Fragment** : This lyric captures the song's theme of imagined infidelity and heartbreak. "It was only a kiss. ## curiosities
**Early Songwriting Success** : "Mr. Brightside" was one of the first three songs ever written by The Killers, proving to be an almost instant success that helped launch their career and secure a record deal. Its massive popularity and enduring presence are remarkable for such an early composition. "It's not bad considering it was one of the first three songs the band ever wrote."
**Brandon Flowers' Personal Inspiration** : The genesis of "Mr. Brightside" came from a deeply personal experience for singer Brandon Flowers. As a 19-year-old, he was living in Las Vegas with his sister, renting a room for $200 a month, and was heartbroken after walking into The Crown and Anchor bar and seeing his ex-girlfriend, his first serious relationship, with another man. This betrayal inspired the song's narrative. "Somewhere at the root of all this, at all these achievements and profits is a real woman."
**The Crown and Anchor Bar Encounter** : Brandon Flowers' painful encounter that inspired "Mr. Brightside" occurred when he walked into The Crown and Anchor bar in Las Vegas and saw his ex-girlfriend with another man. He had been trying to avoid her to prevent such a situation, but his fears were realized that day. "One day he walked into a bar called the Crown and Anchor and sitting there having a drink was his girlfriend. Actually, it was his ex-girlfriend and the person involved in Brandon's first proper, serious relationship. She was there with another dude."
**"Mr. Brightside" as a Coping Mechanism** : After seeing his ex-girlfriend with another man, Brandon Flowers immediately left the bar but couldn't shake the image from his mind. He then spiraled into imagining what they would be doing later that night, writing everything down on a pad of paper in an attempt to convince himself to put on a brave face, hence calling himself "Mr. Brightside." "He wrote everything out on a pad of paper hoping to convince himself to put on a brave face with the situation."
**Crude Demo's Role in Record Deal** : The initial composition of "Mr. Brightside" involved Brandon Flowers writing the lyrics over music already composed by guitarist Dave Keuning. They then created a crude demo using a drum machine, and this early version of the song was instrumental in helping the full Killers lineup secure their first record deal. "When the full killer's lineup came together, this was one of the songs that landed them a record deal."

## songs
**Jeremy (1991) – School Shooting Narratives** : One of Pearl Jam's biggest songs from their *Ten* album, "Jeremy" intricately weaves together two distinct school shooting stories. The first involves a troubled seventh grader named Brian from Eddie Vedder's junior high in San Diego, who brought a sawed-off shotgun to school in the 1970s and shot up a fish tank, an event that deeply affected Vedder. The second, more central narrative, details the life and tragic suicide of 15-year-old Jeremy Dell of Richardson, Texas, who, after a history of psychiatric issues, suicidal attempts, and school disciplinary problems, ultimately shot himself in front of his English class on January 8, 1991. The song was inspired after Eddie Vedder read a newspaper story about Jeremy Dell. "It tells two stories that are intertwined into one and both involve school shootings."

## curiosities
**Brian's Junior High Shooting Incident** : One of the inspirations for "Jeremy" came from Eddie Vedder's own junior high experience in San Diego during the 1970s. A troubled seventh-grader named Brian, with whom Vedder had some confrontations, brought a sawed-off shotgun to school, hid it in bushes until lunchtime, and then shot up a 1,000-gallon fish tank. Although no one was injured or died, the event deeply shook Vedder, prompting him to consider what if he had been in the fish tank room at that moment. "No one died, no one was injured. But I mentioned that kind of thing happening in an elementary school back in the 1970s."
**Jeremy Dell's Early Life and Struggles** : Jeremy Dell, a 15-year-old from Richardson, Texas, who inspired part of the song "Jeremy," was described as a quiet, sad, but good kid who struggled under a heavy load of problems and could get very angry. He also had a lighter side, enjoying jokes and being a talented artist who won an award for drawing at age seven. Despite appearing to be an ordinary teenager to most, he had previously undergone a psychiatric evaluation and been admitted for a time while attending another school. "People describe Jeremy as a sad kid. He was always very quiet. He was also said to be a good kid, but stuck under a giant load of problems and he could get very angry."
**Jeremy Dell's Suicide Attempts (April-June 1990)** : Between April and June 1990, Jeremy Dell attempted suicide at least three times, utilizing different methods including pills, threatening to jump off a church roof, and making threats to shoot himself. This history indicated a clear need for additional help and treatment that he evidently did not receive. "Between April and June 1990, he attempted suicide at least three times, pills, threatening to jump off the roof of a church, and a threat to shoot himself."
**Jeremy Dell's Family Situation and School Transfer** : Jeremy Dell changed schools and started at Richardson High on October 16, 1990. His parents had divorced the year before, and he was living with his father and his father's considerably younger girlfriend, while his mother, Wanda, lived elsewhere but remained in regular contact. His attendance at Richardson High immediately became a problem, leading to counseling with his father and meetings with the principal. "His parents divorced the year before, so Jeremy was living with his dad and his dad's girlfriend who was quite a bit younger."
**Prohibited Items and Disciplinary Issues** : While at Richardson High, Jeremy Dell was found with prohibited items in his locker, including a book titled "Cults That Kill" and three bottles of White Out, which he may have been sniffing to get high. These infractions, along with smoking on school grounds and alleged verbal threats against his algebra teacher, frequently led to him being placed on in-school suspension. "On a couple of occasions, he was found to have some prohibited items in his locker, including a book entitled, Cults That Kill, and three bottles of white out, which he may have been sniffing to get high."
**Escalating Threats and Suicide Talk (Nov-Dec 1990)** : In November 1990, Jeremy Dell began casually discussing suicide with classmates. The following month, he was again on in-school suspension for making more threats against teachers, had stolen money from a cash box at a basketball game, continued talking about suicide, and made a specific threat about "blowing the head off a particular teacher." These incidents marked a clear escalation of his troubling behavior and mental state. "The following month, he was back on in-school suspension for making more threats against teachers."
**The Stolen Gun and Suicide Notes** : Around Christmas, Jeremy Dell falsely claimed he received a gun as a present, but it appears he stole a .357 Magnum from a drawer at his dad's girlfriend's house. In early January 1991, he started writing and sending suicide notes to friends, some thoughts even recorded on cassette. One letter ominously read, "This letter was written before I committed suicide," concluding with a postscript: "PS. Call my dad for funeral info." "Sometime around Christmas, Jeremy mentioned that he was given a gun as a present. This is not true. It appears that he stole it from a drawer at the house of dad's girlfriend."
**The Day of Jeremy Dell's Suicide (January 8, 1991)** : On the morning of Tuesday, January 8, 1991, Jeremy Dell woke at 7:10 AM, called a friend, Nancy, and mentioned he was already high from smoking weed, appearing happy. He arrived at Richardson High at 8:20 AM and gave other students a letter to be mailed, stating he wouldn't be around after school. After missing his 9 AM class, he arrived late to his second-period English class at 9:35 AM. When teacher Fay Barnette followed procedure to send him to the principal's office for a late slip, Jeremy instead went to his locker (number 540), retrieved the stolen .357 Magnum, returned to the classroom, placed his hall pass on the teacher's desk, and, turning to her, stated, "Miss, I got what I really went for." He then put the gun in his mouth and pulled the trigger in front of approximately 30 classmates, with the bang heard throughout the school. "He then got out the gun, put it in his mouth, and pulled the trigger. There were about 30 other kids in the room at the time."
**Preceding School Suicides in the Area** : The tragic events of Jeremy Dell's suicide were not isolated in the Richardson area. In the years leading up to January 8, 1991, three other students in Richardson had taken their own lives. Furthermore, in 1985, a 17-year-old student had killed himself in front of his fellow drama students in nearby Arlington, highlighting a troubling pattern. "It could be because in the years previous to January 8, 1991, three students in Richardson had taken their own lives."
**Wanda Richardson's Public Statement (2018)** : Jeremy Dell's mother, Wanda Richardson, gave her first public interview about her son's death to a local ABC affiliate, WFAA, in 2018. She stated that "That day that he died did not define his life," emphasizing his identity as a son, brother, nephew, cousin, grandson, friend, and talented individual. Initially in shock and disbelief, she later reflected on how she thinks of other mothers and sisters affected by school shootings, wanting to comfort them, although she still isn't sure of the exact "why" behind her son's actions. "That day that he died did not define his life."
**Wanda Richardson's Grief Support Work** : Today, Wanda Richardson, the mother of the real-life Jeremy, actively helps run a grief support group at her local church, channeling her personal tragedy into supporting others. "These days, Wanda Richardson, the mother of the real-life Jeremy, helps run a grief support group at her local church."

# artist - Fastball

## members

## songs
**The Way (1998) – Romanticized Tragedy** : This one-hit wonder from 1998 was written by Austin-based songwriter Tony Scalzo after he discovered the tragic story of Leila and Ray Howard. Scalzo characterized the song as a romanticized account of what might have happened to the elderly Texas couple, who suffered from cognitive decline and went missing during a short drive, only to be found dead two weeks later almost 450 miles from home after driving off a cliff. "He wrote this song for his band Fastball, and he characterizes the song as a romanticized account of what might have happened."

## curiosities
**The Disappearance of Leila and Ray Howard** : In 1997, 83-year-old Leila Howard, suffering from early-stage Alzheimer's, and 88-year-old Ray Howard, recovering from brain surgery and a stroke, decided to drive 17 miles from Salado, Texas, to Temple for Pioneer Days. Their son offered to drive, but they declined, asserting they knew the way. They left on Sunday, June 29th, and disappeared, later spotted at a Walmart. This tragic journey inspired Fastball's song "The Way." "The previous year, an elderly couple named Leila and Ray Howard of Solado, Texas decided to go for a drive."
**Multiple Police Stops in Arkansas** : After going missing from Texas, Leila and Ray Howard were pulled over twice by different cops in rural Arkansas on July 2nd. The first stop was for Leila driving with her headlights off, where the trooper noted they were polite but heading in the wrong direction, and Leila seemed to remember their home address. Half an hour later, they were stopped again for driving with high beams on. In both instances, they were let go with a warning, as neither officer realized they were dealing with missing persons. "Neither cop had any idea that they were dealing with a couple of missing persons."
**Discovery of Neglect at Howard Home** : On July 3rd, a welfare check at the Howard's home revealed clear signs of cognitive decline, including their hearing aids being left behind and their cat, Happy, found abandoned. This discovery prompted a sick bulletin, a reward offer for their safe return, and extensive media coverage, though it ultimately led to no immediate success in finding the couple. "Given the state of the place, there were obvious signs that the couple were suffering from cognitive decline."
**Tragic Death in Arkansas Ravine** : Two weeks after leaving their Salado, Texas home, on July 12, 1997, Leila and Ray Howard were found dead in their car at the bottom of a ravine near Hot Springs, Arkansas, almost 450 miles from their house. It is believed Leila may have been trying to find a former vacation spot, and with no skid marks, it appears she drove straight over the 25-foot cliff edge. Ray was found in the passenger seat, while Leila's body was discovered about 20 feet away, still holding her purse and car keys, with the duration their bodies lay there unknown. "Leila and Howard were found dead in their car at the bottom of a ravine near Hot Springs, Arkansas, almost 450 miles from their house."

## songs
**Buddy Holly (1994) – Platonic Defense Anthem** : This song, released as a single from Weezer's 1994 debut album, was written by Rivers Cuomo out of anger because other band members were making fun of his Korean friend, King He, whom he felt protective of due to past bullying. Cuomo stated the song was about his platonic commitment to her, their relationship, and his willingness to defend her. Initially deemed "too cheesy" and an "outlier" by Cuomo, it was eventually included on the album after strong encouragement from producer Ric Ocasek and the band, with lyrics evolving from Ginger Rogers and Fred Astaire to Mary Tyler Moore and Buddy Holly, and rap slang removed, with the tempo increased during production. "It's about my commitment to her, our relationship, and my willingness to defend her. It's very platonic, not a romantic thing at all."
**Buddy Holly (1994) – Verbatim Lyric Fragment** : This fragment represents a key, albeit possibly mistranscribed, line from the song. "weew, I look just like Buddy Holly"

## curiosities
**Weezer's Early Identity Struggle** : When Weezer first started, the band members were uncertain about their musical direction, exploring genres like metal, PowerPop, and grungy sounds. They remained a "work in progress" for a significant period before the release of their 1994 debut album. "When Weezer was first starting out, the band wasn't exactly sure what they wanted to be."
**Rivers Cuomo's Friendship with King He** : The song "Buddy Holly" was a direct result of Rivers Cuomo's protectiveness over his Korean friend, King He. Cuomo became "really pissed off" when other band members made fun of her, recalling how she was often picked on in high school. The song was written as an expression of his commitment to their platonic relationship and his readiness to defend her. "It's about a particular girl I knew."
**Songwriting Process on Santa Monica College Campus** : Rivers Cuomo developed the lyrics for "Buddy Holly" while walking through the campus of Santa Monica College, where both he and King He were attending school. The iconic line "weew, I look just like Buddy Holly" was conceived at the same speed as his walking pace, indicating an organic, real-time inspiration. "He came up with the lyrics while walking through the campus of Santa Monica College where he and King He were attending school."
**Rivers Cuomo's Initial Rejection of "Buddy Holly"** : Rivers Cuomo initially refused to consider including "Buddy Holly" on Weezer's debut album, deeming it "too cheesy" and an outlier compared to his other compositions. He felt it didn't fit the band's intended sound. "For the longest time, he refused to consider including the song and Weezer's debut album because it was too cheesy."
**Producer Ric Ocasek's Persuasion** : Despite Rivers Cuomo's strong reservations, producer Ric Ocasek, leader of The Cars, was adamant about including "Buddy Holly" on the debut album. He, along with the other band members, insisted on its inclusion. Ocasek eventually convinced Cuomo by suggesting they record it, and if Cuomo still disliked it, they could leave it off, but warning him it would be "stupid not to include it." "But Rico Kasek, the leader of the cars and the guy tapped to produce the record, would have none of that."
**Studio Dynamics and "Buddy Holly" Notes** : During the recording sessions for Weezer's debut album, notes reading "weew, what, Buddy Holly" began to appear around the studio. This suggests the other band members and producer Ric Ocasek were playfully but persistently advocating for the song's inclusion, despite Rivers Cuomo's initial reluctance.
