# instrument - Guitar
## curiosities
**Handedness History (16th Century to Present)** : The traditional right-handed configuration of the guitar, where the right hand strums/plucks and the left forms chords, dates back to 16th-century instruments and earlier stringed predecessors (Lyre, Ud, Lute, Vihuela). This setup was influenced by historical stigmas against left-handedness. The electric guitar emerged in the 1930s, and intricate left-hand solos gained prominence from the 1890s with Flamenco guitar. "Bottom line, Ray, is that the guitar and all the instruments that came before were built with right hand in this in mind because of the way they were originally played with the right hand doing all the work and the left hand just forming chords."
**Early Appearance (16th Century)** : The first proper guitars, or instruments recognizable as such, began to appear in the 16th century, with the modern six-string version dating to the 18th century. # curiosity
**Cover Version Origin (1940s-1960s)** : The term and practice of "cover versions" originated from the segregation and racism prevalent in the music industry during the 1940s, 50s, and 60s. White artists would record toned-down, sanitized versions of songs by black musicians, whose original recordings were deemed vulgar or destabilizing by white radio stations, allowing the music to reach a wider white audience and "cover the rest of the marketplace." "This whole idea goes back to the days when there was horrible segregation and racism in the music industry, specifically in the 40s, 50s and 60s."
**Sample Definition (N/A)** : A "sample" is defined as surgically excising a part of an existing song's exact audio recording for use in a brand new composition. "A sample is when you surgically excise part of another song, an existing composition, for use in one that is brand new."
**Sample Legality (N/A)** : To legitimately use a sample, one must obtain permission from the original composer and pay two distinct fees: one for the master recording rights (for the actual sound recording used) and another for the publishing rights (for the use of the composition itself). "two fees have to be paid when you sample somebody. There's the master recording rights, which is the actual sound recording that you're using, and the publishing rights for use of the composition itself, the people that own that composition."
**Interpolation Definition (N/A)** : An "interpolation" involves re-recording or recreating a part of another song within a new song, exactly copying the writing (composition) rather than using the original recording's audio. "This is a re-recording or recreation of something from another song without actually excising it from the original."
**Interpolation Legality (N/A)** : Interpolations are cheaper than samples because they only require payment for the composition rights, not the master rights, as a new master recording is created. Acknowledging and paying for the privilege is necessary to avoid plagiarism. "This makes an interpolation cheaper than the sample, because in this case you only have to pay the rights for the composition, not the master, because you are making a new master."
**Plagiarism in Music (N/A)** : Failing to acknowledge and pay for the use of another artist's composition, whether through sampling or interpolation, constitutes plagiarism, which is legally considered theft. "If you don't, you step over a very fine legal line into plagiarism which is theft."
**Music Copyright (N/A)** : When an artist writes a song, they hold the copyright, meaning no one can copy it in any form without their compensation. "When an artist writes a song, he or she has the copyright on it."
**Publishing Deals (N/A)** : Artists typically sign publishing deals, usually involving a 50-50 split of proceeds, wherein a publisher polices all uses of the artist's song—including radio airplay, record sales, streaming fees, covers, and public performances—and ensures appropriate royalties are collected and paid. "the publisher polices all use of the artist's song and makes sure that the appropriate royalties are collected and paid."
**Publishing Rights vs. Master Rights (N/A)** : Publishing rights pertain to the songwriting composition itself and are extremely valuable to the artist, generating royalties. Master rights, conversely, grant ownership of the actual finished sound recording, which record labels almost always retain in a deal, giving them control over physical and digital copies. "Publishing is different from holding the master rights. If you hold the master rights, you own the actual finished recording, which is different from the rights to the songwriting."
**Catalog Sales Trend (mid-2010s)** : Since the mid-2010s, there has been a significant surge in well-financed companies purchasing the publishing rights held by artists, with hundreds of such deals occurring. "Since the mid-2010s, there has been a rush by a well-financed group of companies to buy up the publishing rights held by artists."
**Why Companies Buy Catalogs (N/A)** : Companies invest in music catalogs because great songs are considered valuable, long-term assets that theoretically generate royalties for a very long time, making them a viable long-term investment. "Music is valuable. Great songs will always be great. Therefore they, in theory, should generate royalties for a very, very long time."
**Why Artists Sell Catalogs (N/A)** : Artists sell their publishing catalogs primarily for financial freedom, receiving a lump sum that can sustain them for life and be used for new music, investments, activism, philanthropy, or estate planning. "entering into such a deal gives the artist financial freedom."
**Catalog Valuation Method (N/A)** : Buyers value music catalogs by determining the annual income generated from all sources and then negotiating a multiple (e.g., 5x, 10x, 20x) of that annual figure to establish the purchase price, effectively giving artists future earnings upfront. "the buyer will look at the artist's work and determine how much it generates from all sources over the course of a year. Then they'll negotiate the multiple of that number."
**Copyright Duration (N/A)** : In most countries, copyright for a song is retained by an artist or their estate for 75 years after the artist's death. For multiple songwriters, rights extend until 75 years after the last surviving member dies, after which the song enters the public domain. "the way copyright works in most countries is that an artist or their estate retains control of a song up until 75 years after the artist has died."
**Tax Advantages of Catalog Sales (N/A)** : Selling a music catalog allows artists to receive future earnings as a lump sum, which is typically taxed as capital gains at a much lower rate (e.g., 15% in US, 20% in UK) compared to the higher income tax rates (35-50%) applied to regular royalty checks. Canada's 50% capital gains rate disincentivizes Canadian musicians from cashing in unless they reside in lower-tax jurisdictions. "if the artist takes all that future money, all at once, governments consider that to be capital gains. Taxes on such things are generally much lower."
**Catalog Revenue Streams for Buyers (N/A)** : Companies that acquire music catalogs recoup their investments by actively encouraging revenue generation through radio and video airplay, streaming, public performances, covers by other artists, licensing for movies, television, and commercials, and revenue from samples and interpolations. "there's radio airplay, video airplay, streaming, public performances, other artists covering those songs, licensing of those songs for movies, television and commercials, and revenue will come from samples and interpolations."
**Performing Live After Catalog Sale (N/A)** : Artists who have sold their music catalog do not need to ask for permission to perform their own songs live, as this right is covered by the publisher and performing rights organizations. "Do they have to ask permission to perform what was once their own stuff? No, that's covered by the publisher in performing rights organizations."
**Restrictions on Catalog Use (N/A)** : Purchase contracts for music catalogs typically include clauses that stipulate what the acquired songs can and cannot be used for, such as prohibiting use in partisan political contexts or for advertising alcohol. "There will be clauses in the purchase contract and stipulate what the songs can and cannot be used for."
**Future Songs After Catalog Sale (N/A)** : Generally, any new songs written and released by an artist after selling their catalog are not covered by the sale and remain the property of the artist. "Generally, those songs are not covered by the sale and stay in the possession of the artist."
**Buyer's Incentive to Promote Catalog (N/A)** : Companies that purchase music catalogs have a vested interest in actively promoting and keeping the songs alive and in the public mind for decades to come, thereby maximizing their investment and returns. "And it is in their best interest to keep these songs alive and in the mind of the public for decades and decades to come."
**Multiple Songwriter Credits (Writing by Committee) (N/A)** : The modern music industry often sees 10-15 or more people credited on a single song due to an industrialized, "factory-like" approach by labels and publishers. This involves assembling teams of writers, producers, top-liners (for melodies/lyrics), and beat-makers in "songwriting camps" or remotely, to collaboratively craft songs with maximum commercial appeal and hooks, thus reducing the risk of a flop. "The headline is that much of today's music is extremely industrialized, and almost factory-like in its construction, and it works."
**Stigma Against Left-Handedness (Mid-20th Century)** : Historically, left-handed individuals were subjected to a long-standing stigma, associated with bad luck and even witchcraft. This led to efforts to force southpaws to become right-handed, continuing in schools until the mid-20th century. "There was also a long-standing stigma against southpaws, against lefties. They were associated with bad luck and even witchcraft, so to avoid being shunned or stoned or burned at the stake, southpaws work to become right-handed."
