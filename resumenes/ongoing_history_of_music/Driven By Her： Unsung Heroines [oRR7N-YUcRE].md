# artist - Claire Torry

## songs
**The Great Gig in the Sky (1973)** : Claire Torry's extraordinary improvised vocal performance on Pink Floyd's "The Great Gig in the Sky" was recorded in 1973 at Abbey Road Studios. Engineer Alan Parsons directed her to listen to the music, feel it, and "let her rip" without words, pretending to be a musical instrument, resulting in a compilation of about half a dozen takes that became legendary. "That was the sum total of the direction she was given, and what came out was extraordinary."

## curiosities
**Compensation and Royalties Dispute** : For her iconic performance on "The Great Gig in the Sky," Claire Torry was paid a standard flat fee of 30 pounds, which is equivalent to approximately $400 today, with no royalties. In 2004, she sued Pink Floyd, arguing that her performance was so integral to the song that she deserved a songwriting credit and additional compensation, highlighting issues of artist compensation. "For that performance, which, by the way, is a compilation of maybe half a dozen takes, Claire was paid a standard flat fee of 30 pounds with no royalties."
**Acquired Songwriting Credit** : Following her 2004 lawsuit against Pink Floyd, the case was settled out of court for an undisclosed sum. As a result of this settlement, all editions of "Dark Side of the Moon" issued after 2005 include the credit "vocal composition by Claire Torry" in the liner notes, acknowledging her creative contribution to the song. "In 2004, Claire sued Pink Floyd on the basis that her performance, something that she created, was such an integral part of the song that she was do some cash."

# genre - Rock and Roll

## curiosities
**Origin of the Term by Alan Freed** : The term "rock and roll" was popularized by disc jockey Alan Freed in the 1950s. After losing a lawsuit and needing a new catchphrase, Freed, who played R&B records by Black performers for a white audience, adopted "rock and roll" from blues and R&B slang. He decided this would be the name for all the music he played on his radio show, despite an unsuccessful attempt to copyright the term. "After night of brainstorming with some buddies, okay, heavy drinking is probably more like it, he decided that rock and roll would be his thing, his catchphrase."
**First Secular Use in a Song** : The words "rocking and rolling" were first used in a secular song, referring to sex, by Trixie Smith in her September 1922 recording "My Man Rocks Me with One Steady Roll." This predates the popularization of the genre name itself, establishing a direct linguistic lineage for the term. "And as far as anyone can tell, this song, which is about sex in case you miss the memo, is the first example of the use of rocking and rolling to be used in a secular song."
**"60 Minute Man" and "Rock 'Em, Roll 'Em" Lyric** : The term "rock and roll" also appeared in songs like "60 Minute Man" by Billy Ward and His Dominoes. This song included the explicit line, "I Rock'em, I Roll'em, all night long," further illustrating the early, suggestive use of the phrase within R&B music before its adoption as a genre label. "Including Billy Ward and his Dominoes, who released a song called 60-Minute Man that included the line, I Rock'em, I Roll'em, all night long."

**Long-standing Female Producer** : For nearly four decades, Ethel Gabriel was the only woman producer at RCA Records, a period spanning from the late 1950s. She produced over 2,500 albums and earned at least 15 gold records during her extensive career with the label, making her a formidable figure in music production. "And for nearly four decades, she was the only woman producer at RCA Records."
**Establishment of Nashville Studio** : Ethel Gabriel worked with RCA to establish their renowned studio in Nashville, playing a key role in developing the label's presence in a vital music hub. Her involvement extended beyond production into infrastructure development and the label's strategic growth. "She worked with RCA to establish their famous studio as a Nashville."
**Pioneering Stereo and Disco Releases** : RCA Records was at the forefront of technical innovation with Ethel Gabriel's involvement in the first stereophonic recordings, including supervising Bing Crosby's first stereo session. The label also embraced new genres, releasing its first disco records with Gabriel's contributions, demonstrating forward-thinking in both technology and genre. "She even supervised the first-ever stereo recording session for Bing Crosby, and she was involved with RCA's first-ever disco releases."

# label - Northern Recording Company

## curiosities
**First Record Label Owned by a Woman** : The Northern Recording Company was founded in 1958 by Sadie DuPouy (Johnny May Matthews), who borrowed $85 to start the venture. Based in Detroit, it holds the distinction of being, as far as records show, the first record label owned by a woman, marking a significant and pioneering step in the industry. "In 1958, she borrowed $85 to form a label called Northern Recording Company. And as far as I can tell, that made her the first woman to ever own her own record label."

# instrument - Moog Synthesizer

## curiosities
**Early Design Collaboration with Wendy Carlos** : Bob Moog worked closely with Wendy Carlos during the early development of his synthesizers, benefiting from her musician's perspective. Carlos's input was critical in shaping design elements such as the inclusion of a keyboard and the tactile feel of individual keys, making the instrument more appealing and intuitive for musicians. "She worked carefully with Bob Mogue, offering a musician's perspective, and how his new electronic instruments should look and feel."
**Used for "Switched-On Bach" Album** : The Moog synthesizer was the sole instrument used by Wendy Carlos for her landmark 1968 album "Switched-On Bach." This giant modular unit, supplied by Bob Moog, demonstrated the synthesizer's capability for complex classical arrangements, despite the laborious process of programming each sound one note at a time using switches, knobs, and patch cords. "The result was an album entitled Switched On Bach, 10 pieces by Johann Sebastian Bach, and everything was played on a giant modular synthesizer unit supplied by Bob Mogue."
**BBC Demonstration of Waveforms** : Wendy Carlos provided a detailed demonstration of the Moog synthesizer for the BBC, explaining how various sounds are generated from basic waveforms. She illustrated sine, sawtooth, triangular, pulse, and square waves, emphasizing the creative process of "building every sound" from these simple elements. "Let me start and show you how we get some of these sounds."

# instrument - Bass

## curiosities
**Not a "Lady's Instrument" in 1963** : When Carol Kaye switched from guitar to bass in 1963 to pursue session work, it was uncommon for women to play the instrument, as it was generally not considered "a lady's instrument." Her decision defied gender norms of the time, paving the way for other female instrumentalists. "She started working in studios in 1957, switching from guitar to bass in 1963. And let's say no woman played bass in 1963. She was just not a lady's instrument, but she didn't care if there were gigs to be had as a session bass player."

# instrument - Eight-track recorder

## curiosities
**Custom-built for "Switched-On Bach"** : Wendy Carlos's "Switched-On Bach" album was recorded on a custom-built eight-track recorder. This specialized equipment was necessary for the extensive overdubbing required to create the complex, multi-layered classical arrangements using a monophonic synthesizer, highlighting the technical challenges of early electronic music production. "I can't imagine how many overdubs were required to make something like this, especially considering that it was reported on an eight-track recorder that had to be custom-built."

# curiosity

**Early Use of "Rocking and Rolling" in Secular Music** : Trixie Smith's September 1922 record, "My Man Rocks Me with One Steady Roll," is recognized as the first example of the phrase "rocking and rolling" being used in a secular song, explicitly referring to sex. This historical detail provides a direct lineage for the term that would later define the rock and roll genre. "And as far as anyone can tell, this song, which is about sex in case you miss the memo, is the first example of the use of rocking and rolling to be used in a secular song."
**Alan Freed and the Popularization of "Rock and Roll"** : In 1956, disc jockey Alan Freed, after losing a lawsuit, needed a new catchphrase for his radio show where he played R&B records for a largely white audience. Drawing on his knowledge of blues and R&B slang, he adopted "rock and roll" as his new identifier, subsequently labeling all the music he played as "rock and roll records," despite an unsuccessful attempt to copyright the term. "And all the music he would play on his radio show would forever be referred to as rock and roll."
**Low Percentage of Women in Production and Engineering** : The music industry still suffers from a severe underrepresentation of women in production and engineering roles, with percentages estimated to be far less than 10%, possibly as low as 2%. This highlights the exceptional accomplishments of female producers like Ethel Gabriel and Sylvia Massy in a male-dominated field. "The percentage of women who work as producers and engineers is, in this no way, rounded, ismo, way less than 10% of all possessions."
