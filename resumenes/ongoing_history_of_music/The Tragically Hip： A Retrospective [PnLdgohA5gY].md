## albums
**The Tragically Hip (1987) - Indie EP** : This self-titled Indie EP was the band's first proper, official, and authorized record, released on December 11, 1987. It was primarily stocked by a few record stores in Kingston but still managed to sell 2000 copies locally within a couple of weeks. "The first proper official and authorized hip record was released on December 11, 1987. It was a cell titled IndieP stocked only by a couple of record stores in Kingston, but still the damn thing sold 2000 copies in just a couple of weeks, all locally."
**Road Apples (Year unstated) - Second Record** : This album's title refers to "road apples," which is a chunk of horse manure. Historically, a frozen road apple could be used as a substitute for a puck when one was unavailable, highlighting a hockey connection. "The title of their second record, Road Apples. What exactly is a Road Apple? Well it's a chunk of horse poo. And back in the day, a frozen Road Apple made for a decent substitute when a puck couldn't be found."
**Fully Completely (1992) - Third Full Album** : This was the band's third full album. The video for the song "Courage" from this album features Gord Downie wearing a Boston Bruins sweater, a nod to his godfather, Harry Sinden, who was a legendary Bruins coach and general manager. "The hip from 1992 and their third full album Fully Completely. Gord Downey's Bruins sweater is not to his godfather Harry Sinden."
**Phantom Power (Year unstated)** : This album features the song "Fireworks," which contains lyrics referencing Paul Henderson's famous goal in the 1972 Canada-Russia Series. It also includes the song "Thompson Girl." "lines from the song Fireworks from the Phantom Power album that talks about Paul Henderson's famous goal."
**World Container (2006)** : This album, released in 2006, contains the song "Lonely End of the Rink," reflecting the band's deep connection to hockey themes. "The World Container album from 2006 contained a song called Lonely End of the Rink."
**In Violet Light (Year unstated)** : This album includes the song "Silverjet," which name-checks specific Canadian geographical locations such as Cape Spear in Newfoundland, Northumberland County in Ontario, and Clay Quot Sound in British Columbia. "a song called Silverjet from In Violet Light, name checks Cape Spear Newfoundland, Northumberland County in Ontario and Clay Quot Sound in BC."
**Now For Plan A (2012) - 12th Studio Album** : This album, released in 2012, is the band's 12th studio album and features the song "Transformation." "Transformation from the Hips 12th studio album and now for Plan A from 2012."

## songs
**50 Mission Cap (Year unstated)** : This song tells the story of Bill Barilko, a defenseman for the Toronto Maple Leafs who scored the winning goal in the 1951 Stanley Cup finals but tragically died in a plane crash shortly thereafter. "Forget that everybody in the band has always played. Forget the hockey pools staged by the band in the crew every season. Forget that they're friends with a bunch of NHLers. And you can even forget the song 50 Mission Cap, which tells the story of Bill Barilco, the doomed defenseman from the 1951 Stanley Cup winning Toronto Maple Leafs."
**Courage (1992) - For Hugh MacLeanen** : The full title of this song is "Courage for Hugh MacLeanen," a dedication to Canadian author and English professor Hugh MacLeanen. A passage from MacLeanen's novel, "The Watch That Ends the Night," which features a character based on Norman Bethune, is used in the song. "The full title of the song Courage from Fully Completely Is Courage for Hugh MacLeanen."
**Fireworks (Year unstated)** : This song from the album *Phantom Power* contains lyrics that reference Paul Henderson's famous goal in the 1972 Canada-Russia Series, demonstrating the band's integration of Canadian hockey history into their music. "lines from the song Fireworks from the Phantom Power album that talks about Paul Henderson's famous goal."
**Lonely End of the Rink (2006)** : Featured on the *World Container* album (2006), this song is another example of The Tragically Hip's enduring connection to and thematic exploration of hockey. "The World Container album from 2006 contained a song called Lonely End of the Rink."
**Heaven is a Better Place Today (Year unstated)** : This song, from the album *In Between Evolution*, is about Dan Snyder, a member of the Atlanta Thrashers hockey team who tragically died in a car accident. "In between evolution includes a song called Heaven is a Better Place Today, which is about Dan Snyder, a member of the Atlanta Thrashers who was killed in a car accident."
**Thompson Girl (Year unstated)** : This song, presumably from *Phantom Power*, is believed to be about a girl from the northern Manitoba town of Thompson, reflecting the band's tendency to reference Canadian locations in their lyrics. "Thompson Girl from Phantom Power, I'm assuming, is about a girl from that northern Manitoba town."
**Bob Cajun (Year unstated)** : This song is about the town of Bobcaygeon in Ontario, further illustrating The Tragically Hip's lyrical connection to Canadian geography. "Bob Cajun, that's about a town in Ontario."
**Silverjet (Year unstated)** : From the album *In Violet Light*, this song name-checks three distinct Canadian geographical locations: Cape Spear in Newfoundland, Northumberland County in Ontario, and Clay Quot Sound in British Columbia. "a song called Silverjet from In Violet Light, name checks Cape Spear Newfoundland, Northumberland County in Ontario and Clay Quot Sound in BC."
**Nautical Disaster (Year unstated)** : This song is a World War II epic about the Dieppe Raid, an attack on the German-occupied port in France on August 19, 1942, where 4,000 people, mostly Canadian, died, were wounded, or captured, with nothing significant accomplished. The song emerged from one of Gord Downie's stream-of-consciousness live tangents and was performed on Saturday Night Live on March 27, 1997. "This is a song about the de-apparade, an attack on the German occupied port in France on August 19th, 1942. The 4,000 people died, were wounded or captured. Most of them were Canadian. And really nothing was accomplished. Many survivors of the attack died in the water while awaiting rescue. It really was a complete, nautical disaster."
**New Orleans is Sinking (Year unstated)** : This song features a "long, weird, improvised experimental jam" in its middle during live performances. The most famous extended live version, known for the "Killer Whale Tank story," was recorded at the Roxy in Los Angeles in May 1991. This recording was a highly sought-after bonus track on the "long time running" single, which was originally distributed only to radio stations and not sold to the public. "The long, weird, improvised experimental jam in the middle of New Orleans's sinking. The most famous of all the extended live versions of New Orleans's sinking is the one with the Killer Whale Tank story."
**Transformation (Year unstated)** : This song is found on the band's 12th studio album, *Now For Plan A* (2012). Lyrics from the song include "I want to help you lift enormous things. I've been shifting, I've been laughing. As the earth revolves around us." "At Transformation from the Hips 12th studio album and now for Plan A from 2012."
**Blow at High Doe (Year unstated)** : This song contains a line about shooting a movie in Gord Downie's hometown, although no such movie ever actually happened. Other lyrics include "Nothing more. Yeah, I can get behind anything. Get it out, get it all out. Yeah, stretch that thing." "You know the line in Blow at High Doe about shooting a movie in Gord's hometown? Yeah, I'd never happened. There was no movie."

## curiosities
**Gord Downie's Glioblastoma Diagnosis (2016)** : In 2016, Gord Downie was diagnosed with glioblastoma, an aggressive and terminal form of brain cancer, with a prognosis of one to five years of survival. "Gordowning was dying. Brain cancer. Something called glioblastoma. The prognosis wasn't good. The condition was terminal. One year, maybe two, five, at best."
**Man Machine Poem Tour (2016) - National Celebration** : Following Gord Downie's diagnosis, The Tragically Hip embarked on the Man Machine Poem Tour in 2016. This tour quickly transformed into a national celebration of Gord, the band, rock music, and Canadian identity. The final show, broadcast on a Saturday night in August, was watched by tens of millions of Canadians, bringing the country to a standstill. "that same news conference also announced that the hip was going on tour again, the man machine poem tour. That turned into a national celebration of all things. Gord, all things hip, all things rock, and all things Canadian. Tens of millions of people stop what they were doing and watch the final show that Saturday night and August."
**Gord Downie's Post-Diagnosis Activities (2017)** : After his diagnosis but before his death, Gord Downie remained active, conducting a couple of interviews, releasing his *Secret Path* documentary and album, and appearing to receive an Order of Canada. Sessions for a solo album were wrapped up in February, but no news followed until his death. "A couple of interviews, his secret path documentary and album showing up to receive an order of Canada. ... Sessions for that album wrapped up in February. And since then, radio silence."
**Gord Downie's Death (October 18, 2017)** : Gord Downie passed away on the morning of October 18, 2017. His death immediately led to a widespread and sustained outpouring of affection and grief across Canada, with his passing covered similarly to that of a beloved head of state. "And then on the morning of October 18th, we got the news. Gord was gone. The outpouring of affection and grief was immediate and sustained. Hip music started playing everywhere. We covered his death like other countries might cover the death of a beloved head of state."
**Band Longevity and Lineup Stability** : The Tragically Hip's roots date back 30 years, and throughout their career, which includes multiple studio records, two live albums, and over 50 singles, they have maintained remarkable lineup stability with only one change. "The hip's roots go back 30 years. Since then, there's really only been one lineup change among all their studio records, their two live albums and 50 odd singles."
**Awards and Honors** : The Tragically Hip has received numerous accolades, including 14 Juno Awards, induction into the Canadian Music Hall of Fame, induction into Canada's Walk of Fame, an honorary fellowship from the Royal Conservatory of Music, and a Governor-General's Performing Arts Award. "I count 14 June Awards, one induction into the Canadian Music Hall of Fame, one induction into Canada's Walk of Fame, one honorary fellowship from the Royal Conservatory of Music, and one Governor-General's Performing Arts Award."
**Founding Members and Early Friendship** : The core of the band, Gord Downie and Bobby Baker, grew up across the street from each other in Kingston, Ontario, and were friends from birth. They both started playing guitar at age 13. "At the beginning, the core of the band was Gord Downey and Bobby Baker. They grew up across the street from each other in Kingston, Ontario, and have essentially been friends pretty much from the day that they were born. They were both 13 when they picked up guitars for the first time."
**Only Lineup Change (1986)** : The earliest version of the band included Davis Manning on saxophone. In 1986, Manning was replaced by Paul Langlois due to "girlfriend issues," marking the only lineup change in the band's history. "The earliest version of the band featured a guy named Davis Manning. He played saxophone, but he had some girlfriend issues and was replaced in 1986 by Paul Langua, and that was the only, only lineup change."
**Band Name Origin** : The name "The Tragically Hip" was taken from a line in a 1981 video collection of music and comedy by Mike Nesmith (formerly of The Monkees). The specific skit involved raising money for "poor yuppies" to fund their Lamborghinis and cocaine. "That was stolen from a line in a 1981 video collection of music and comedy by Mike Nessmith, ex of the monkeys. The skit had to do with raising money for poor yuppies to keep them in Lamborghini's and cocaine. They were the so-called tragically hip."
**First Gig (Kingston Artists Association)** : The band's first public performance was at Queen's University in Kingston for the Kingston Artists Association. "First gig, Queen's University Kingston for the Kingston Artists Association."
**First Known Recording (Mid-1987)** : The band's first known recording was made at Grand Central Station in Ottawa sometime in mid-1987. "It was recorded at a place called Grand Central Station in Ottawa sometime in mid-1987."
**Discovery at CMJ Music Conference (1989)** : In 1989, The Tragically Hip gained a significant break after playing the CMJ Music Conference in New York. Their inclusion on a delegate CD, common for highlighted artists at the time, led to their discovery by Canadian record company representatives who heard the disc back in Toronto. "The hip played the CMJ music conference in New York. And as was common back in those days, the organizers distributed a CD featuring some highlighted artists to all the delegates. The hip were on that disc. And that's what the Canadian record people company heard."
**Deal Signed at Horseshoe Tavern (1989)** : Following their discovery at CMJ and subsequent discussions, The Tragically Hip secured a record deal after a particularly powerful live show at the Horseshoe Tavern on Queen Street in Toronto. "When everybody got back to Toronto, it was time to discuss a deal. And after one particularly powerful show at the Horseshoe Tavern on Queen Street, the deal was done."
**Live Performance at CNE (August 1989)** : In August 1989, The Tragically Hip performed live in beer tents at the CNE (Canadian National Exhibition) in Toronto. "The hip playing live in a beer tents at the C&E in Toronto in August 1989."
**Extensive Touring History** : The Tragically Hip has played thousands of gigs throughout their career. Their largest concert attendance was approximately 450,000 people at the Park Pop Festival in Holland. Conversely, they experienced very small shows, including one in 1989 at Jakes in Bloomington, Indiana, where only six people were present, and some nights with zero attendees. "Since those days, the hip has played thousands of gigs. The biggest was probably at the Park Pop Festival in Holland, which featured about 450,000. The smallest? Well, there were some very bad nights where they showed up to play and no one was in the bar. So attendance zero."
**Diamond Club Firing (Winnipeg)** : The band was booked for a Monday to Saturday engagement, playing three sets nightly, at the Diamond Club in Winnipeg but was fired after only the Monday performance. This unexpected event ultimately led to other bar owners inviting them to play, and the individual who fired them reportedly "has never been able to live that one down." "Then there was the time they were booked to play a place called the Diamond Club in Winnipeg. The deal was for the standard Monday to Saturday stand with three sets of night. But something went wrong and they were fired after playing just the Monday."
**Early Performance Earnings** : In their early career, specifically during the period of their first album, The Tragically Hip typically earned about $200 per night, occasionally supplemented by a case of beer. "Back then, and we're talking the days of the first album up to here, the hip made about 200 bucks a night. Plus maybe a case of beer if they were lucky."
**Deep Hockey Connections** : The band has a profound love for hockey, with connections running deeper than common knowledge. Gord Downie's godfather is Harry Sinden, the legendary Boston Bruins coach and general manager. Downie wore a Bruins sweater in the "Courage" video, and songs like "50 Mission Cap," "Fireworks," "Lonely End of the Rink," and "Heaven is a Better Place Today" all include hockey themes or references. "To know the tragically hip is to know that they're huge hockey fans. And that love for hockey runs a lot deeper than most people know. ... Hockey literally runs in the blood of the tragically hip. Well in the veins of Gord Downey anyway."
**Performance at Air Canada Center Opening** : The Tragically Hip holds the distinction of being the first band to perform a concert at the Air Canada Center when it opened in Toronto. "Name the first band to play a concert when the air Canada Center opened in Toronto."
**Authentic Canadian Identity in Lyrics** : The Tragically Hip is regarded as the quintessential Canadian band for its unique ability to incorporate Canadian references—places, history, and figures—into its songs without sounding artificial or "cheesy." Their lyrical content is so rich with Canadian detail that it could form the basis of a Canadian history or geography course. "they're one of the few bands who can make references to Canadian things without sounding cheesy about it. ... You could teach a Canadian history course, or a geography course using only tragically hip songs."

**Saturday Night Live Performance (March 27, 1997)** : The Tragically Hip appeared as a special musical guest on Saturday Night Live on March 27, 1997, performing two songs, including "Nautical Disaster." This high-profile national US television exposure was facilitated by Kingston native Dan Aykroyd, who was seen wearing a Tragically Hip t-shirt in the 1992 movie *Sneakers*. Despite this major vehicle, the performance did not result in a significant breakthrough in the American market. "That was one of two songs they performed on Saturday Night Live on March 27th, 1997. ... The answer is Dan Acroid. Dan is from Kingston. **US Market Struggles (1990s onward)** : The Tragically Hip never achieved major stardom in the American market, a phenomenon that has become an area of study. While they have pockets of support, their timing often did not align with prevailing American rock trends. For instance, in the 1990s, American rock was dominated by grunge, then hip hop, and later indie rock (like The Strokes or The White Stripes), none of which perfectly matched the Hip's style. "Why has the tragically hip never been able to break through in the States? While this isn't entirely true, the hip do have pockets of support in America. But unlike Brian Adams or Lannis Morsehsett or Nickelback, it just seems that their timing was off. Their music was great, but whenever they were hot, American rock and roll attitudes were somewhere else. In the 90s, it was grunge, and the hip are not grunge. Then came hip hop. Hip didn't belong there. Then it was Indy Rock and the flavor of the strokes, so the white stripes and the hip didn't really fit in there either."
**Gord Downie's Live Stream-of-Consciousness Tangents** : Lead singer Gord Downie was renowned for his "weird stream of consciousness tangents" during live performances. These improvised and experimental detours were not only entertaining but also served as a valuable source for writing new songs, as exemplified by "Nautical Disaster." Many of these tangents originated from the extended jam section of "New Orleans is Sinking." "singer Gord Downey can go off in these weird stream of consciousness tangents. These can be very entertaining, but they're also quite useful for writing new songs. For example, Nautical Disaster was born out of one of these tangents. And many of these detours begin in the same place. The long, weird, improvised experimental jam in the middle of New Orleans's sinking."
**"Killer Whale Tank" Story (May 1991)** : The most famous extended live version of "New Orleans is Sinking" features the "Killer Whale Tank" story, recorded at the Roxy in Los Angeles in May 1991. This recording was a highly sought-after bonus track on the "long time running" single, which was initially distributed exclusively to radio stations and not made available for public sale. "The most famous of all the extended live versions of New Orleans's sinking is the one with the Killer Whale Tank story. It was recorded at the Roxy in Los Angeles in May of 1991. And for years fans long to get their hands on this recording, but it was completely unavailable. And that's because it was only available as a bonus track on the long time running single. And this single was only distributed to radio stations. It wasn't designed to be sold to the public."

# curiosity

**Hugh MacLeanen - Canadian Author and Professor** : Hugh MacLeanen was a Canadian author and a professor of English at McGill University. He was instrumental in establishing the concept of Canadian identity through his novels, including "Two Solitudes" (1945), which addressed the relationship between Quebec and the rest of Canada, and his writings on the 1917 Halifax Explosion. His novel "The Watch That Ends the Night" featured a character inspired by Norman Bethune, and a passage from this book is used in The Tragically Hip's song "Courage." Leonard Cohen was also a student of MacLeanen's at McGill. "Hugh was a Canadian author and professor of English at McGill. He did a lot to establish the concept of Canadian identity through novels. ... that was the name of MacLeanen's 1945 novel, Two Solitudes. He also wrote about the 1917 Halifax Explosion. Another novel called The Watch That Ends the Night has a character patterned on Norman Bethune, Big Hero in China. A passage from that book is used in the hip song Courage. And oh, you know who was a student of MacLeanen's at McGill? Some kid named Leonard Cohen."
**David Milgaard - Wrongful Conviction** : David Milgaard was a man wrongfully convicted of murder and subsequently spent over 20 years imprisoned at Stony Mountain Penitentiary in Manitoba. His story is referenced in The Tragically Hip's song "Wheat Kings." "David Milgard, a guy convicted of a murder he didn't commit and spent over 20 years in Stony Mountain Penitentiary in Manitoba."
**Pierre LaPorte - FLQ Kidnapping and Murder (October 1970)** : Pierre LaPorte was the Quebec Deputy Premier and Minister of Labor who was kidnapped and murdered by the FLQ (Front de libération du Québec) in October 1970. His body was discovered in the trunk of a car, an event from which The Tragically Hip's song "Locked in the Trunk of a Car" is written, taking his perspective. "Locked in the trunk of a car was written from the perspective of Pierre LaPorte, the Quebec Deputy Premier and Minister of Labor who was kidnapped and murdered by the FLQ in October of 1970. And his body was found in the trunk of a car."
**My Music @ Work - Toronto Radio Station Slogan** : "My Music @ Work" was a slogan utilized by a radio station in Toronto. "My music at work was a slogan used by Toronto Radio Station"
**Canada Lee - Black Canadian Actor and Civil Rights Champion (1930s)** : Canada Lee was a Black Canadian actor who was a prominent champion of civil rights in Hollywood and across America during the 1930s. He is referenced in songs by The Tragically Hip. "a black Canadian actor named Canada Lee, who was a champion of civil rights in Hollywood specifically and America had large back in the 1930s."
**Dieppe Raid (August 19, 1942)** : The Dieppe Raid was a World War II attack on the German-occupied port in France on August 19, 1942. This disastrous operation resulted in approximately 4,000 casualties (dead, wounded, or captured), with most of them being Canadian, and ultimately accomplished little. Many survivors perished in the water while awaiting rescue. "an attack on the German occupied port in France on August 19th, 1942. The 4,000 people died, were wounded or captured. Most of them were Canadian. And really nothing was accomplished. Many survivors of the attack died in the water while awaiting rescue. It really was a complete, nautical disaster."
**Dan Aykroyd - Kingston Native and Supporter of The Tragically Hip** : Dan Aykroyd, originally from Kingston, Ontario, played a crucial role in securing The Tragically Hip's appearance on Saturday Night Live. His support was also evident in 1992 when he wore a Tragically Hip t-shirt under a sport coat in the movie *Sneakers*. "The answer is Dan Acroid. Dan is from Kingston. In fact, if you go back to the movie Sneakers with Acroid and Robert Redford and Ben Kingsley, Sydney Ponce, and River Phoenix in 1992, you'll see Dan wearing a tragically hip t-shirt under a sport coat."
**Harry Sinden - Legendary Hockey Coach/GM** : Harry Sinden is a legendary figure in hockey, having served as a coach, general manager, and president for the Boston Bruins. He also coached the Canadian team in the historic 1972 Canada-Russia Series. Sinden is Gord Downie's godfather. "Harry Sinden, the legendary coach, general manager and president of the Bruins. He was also the coach for the Canadian team in the 72 Canada Rushes series."
**Paul Henderson's Goal - 1972 Canada-Russia Series** : Paul Henderson scored the famous game-winning goal in the 1972 Canada-Russia Series, an iconic moment in Canadian hockey history. This event is referenced in The Tragically Hip's song "Fireworks." "Paul Henderson's famous goal. If there's a goal that we all remember was back at old 72, we all squeeze the stick and we all pull the trigger."
**Bill Barilko - Doomed Toronto Maple Leafs Defenseman (1951)** : Bill Barilko was a defenseman for the Toronto Maple Leafs who famously scored the 1951 Stanley Cup-winning goal. He tragically disappeared and was found deceased in a plane crash shortly after, an event commemorated in The Tragically Hip's song "50 Mission Cap." "Bill Barilco, the doomed defenseman from the 1951 Stanley Cup winning Toronto Maple Leafs."
**Dr. Seuss Quote in Remembrance** : An adage often attributed to Dr. Seuss, "Don't cry because it's over. Smile because it happened," was used to express sentiment following Gord Downie's passing. "He said, don't cry because it's over. Smile because it happened."
