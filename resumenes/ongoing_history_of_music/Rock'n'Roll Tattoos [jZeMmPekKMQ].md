## curiosities
**Travis Barker's Extensive Tattoo Coverage** : Travis Barker has 117 distinct tattoos, covering over 70% of his body, extending from the top of his head down to his toes.
**Travis Barker's Band Tattoos – Can I Say / Field Day** : He has a tattoo for his band "Can I Say," which later became "Field Day," located on his chest. He also has a gas mask tattoo as a nod to The Transplants, a band he co-founded in 2002.
**Travis Barker's Vehicle Collection Tattoos** : He has multiple Cadillac tattoos reflecting his collection of Cadillacs, and a large boom box tattooed on his abdomen due to his collection of boom boxes.
**Travis Barker's Family Tattoos** : He has the names of his children, Alabama and London, tattooed on him. Additionally, he has a skull tattoo covering the name of his ex-wife, Shanna Moakler, and the name of another ex-wife is also tattooed on his body.
**Travis Barker's Hand Tattoos** : He has "self" tattooed on one hand and "made" on the other.
**Travis Barker's Personal Motto Tattoo** : Just below his Adam's apple, he has the phrase "don't trust anyone" tattooed.
**Travis Barker's Plane Crash and Re-inking (2008)** : Following a severe plane crash in 2008 that left him with third-degree burns, many of his existing tattoos were wiped out by skin grafts. Once healed, he had the new skin re-inked.

# curiosity
**Ancient Origins of Tattoos** : Tattoos are an ancient form of body art, with archaeological evidence including mummified remains thousands of years old. Examples include "Otsul," discovered in the Swiss Alps, who had 61 tattoos and lived over 5,000 years ago, as well as Egyptian mummies, Pacific Islanders, members of ancient African communities, bodies from Iron Age Britain, early Japanese societies, and indigenous peoples of North and South America.
**Historical Functions of Tattoos** : Beyond aesthetic and cultural expression, tattoos have historically been used for identification (e.g., prisoners, slaves), to display religious connections, and to signify associations with armies, navies, biker gangs, and criminal gangs.
**Societal Stigma of Tattoos (Historical)** : For a significant period, tattoos carried a strong stigma, often associated with "deviance and weirdos."
**Tattoos Going Mainstream (Early 21st Century)** : The perception and acceptance of tattoos dramatically shifted in the last 60 years, particularly since the beginning of the 21st century (around 2001-2002), moving from counter-culture to mainstream acceptance. In contemporary society, a lack of tattoos can sometimes make one an "outsider."
**Influences on Tattoo Mainstreaming** : The popularization of music video channels like MTV and VH1 played a crucial role, as viewers saw musicians with tattoos and sought to emulate their rock star idols. The proliferation of tattoo shops, now as common as Starbucks in many cities, also made tattoos more accessible.
**Blackwork Tattoo Style** : Blackwork tattoos are characterized by large, dense areas of solid black ink, often forming warrior-like or tribal patterns. This style has roots thousands of years old, used by ancient cultures to promote identity, loyalties, and authority.
**"Modern Primitives" Book's Impact (Late 1980s)** : The publication of the book "Modern Primitives" towards the end of the 1980s, which extensively featured blackwork tattoos, helped integrate this style into modern tattoo culture.
**Pain and Addictive Nature of Tattoos** : Tattoos are inherently painful, especially on bony areas like elbows and knees. However, the process can induce an "adrenaline rush" and "endorphin rush," creating a pleasurable and potentially addictive experience that causes many people to desire more tattoos.
**Significant Financial and Time Investment in Tattoos** : Tattoos are expensive; Darren Fyfer estimated spending approximately "50 grand" and "a week" in the tattoo chair over his lifetime.
**Tattoos as a "Safe Addiction"** : Darren Fyfer contrasted his personal "safe addiction" to tattoos with other common pitfalls in the rocker world, such as "drugs and whatnot, alcoholism."
**Permanence of Tattoos vs. Other Trends** : Unlike fashion trends or subcultural affiliations (e.g., biker, punker) that can be adopted and discarded, tattoos are permanent. This lifelong commitment makes them a "riskier trend to follow."
**Advances in Tattoo Health and Safety** : Modern tattooing benefits from improved techniques, equipment, inks, and health and safety standards, significantly reducing risks like infection and gangrene that were common in earlier eras.
**Future Trends in Tattoos: Increased Mainstreaming** : The future of tattoos is predicted to involve even greater mainstream integration and trendiness. The proliferation of tattoo parlors in malls across America exemplifies this, allowing individuals to easily acquire a "fully rock and roll" aesthetic.
**Loss of Uniqueness for Tattooed Individuals** : As tattoos become commonplace, some long-time enthusiasts, like Darren Fyfer, express a sense of loss regarding the "specialness" or uniqueness that once accompanied being tattooed before the early 2000s.
**Family-Inspired Tattoo Planning** : Darren Fyfer plans to get tattoos of his two dogs when they pass away, following his existing tattoos commemorating his mother and father, demonstrating a trend of personal and family-oriented ink.
**Family Connections in Tattooing** : Darren Fyfer benefits from free tattoos provided by his nephew, Nick Fyfer, who owns a tattoo parlor in Buffalo, making the expensive hobby more accessible.
**Unwritten History of Rock and Roll Tattoos** : There is no comprehensive, established history specifically detailing tattoos within rock and roll, making it challenging to definitively identify the earliest rock stars with visible ink prior to figures like Ozzy Osbourne.
**Prominent Women in Rock with Tattoos** : Noted female musicians with tattoos include Brody Dalle of The Distillers, Courtney Love, and Amy Winehouse.
