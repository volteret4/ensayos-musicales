# genre - New Wave

## curiosities
**Early Application to Film (1950s)** : The term "new wave" ("nouvelle vague" in French) originally applied to a new generation of French filmmakers, including Jean-Luc Godard and Francois Truffaut, who emerged in the late 1950s and rejected traditional cinema. "Now that first applied to the work of a new generation of French filmmakers who emerged in the late 1950s, guys like Jean de Goudard and Francois Truffaut, had a different way of looking at cinema, rejecting many of the traditional ways of making movies."
**Early Application to Music (Early 1970s)** : In the early 1970s, some writers used the term "new wave" to describe groups like The Velvet Underground and The New York Dolls, identifying them as artists who rejected the norms of regular blues-based rock and roll, akin to its film application. "the term had been banied about since the early 1970s, when some writers began referring to groups like the Velvet Underground and the New York dolls as new wave, or at least a new wave in rock."
**Malcolm McLaren's Usage (1977)** : By the late 1970s, Malcolm McLaren, a former manager of The New York Dolls, began using the term to describe "cool new bands that weren't quite punk," leading to interchangeable use of "punk" and "new wave" by the end of 1977. "Malcolm McLaren, a one-time manager of the New York dolls, started throwing at the term when he was trying to describe cool new bands that weren't quite punk."
**Defining Characteristics (UK, 1977-78)** : The first era of British New Wave (around 1977-78) was described as spiky, angular, and somewhat agitated, representing a shift from aggressive punk to a more existential despair ("from being about F you to we're F'd"). Some bands were also upbeat and fun, characterized by sharp suits, skinny ties, and choppy, sharp-sounding power pop with clever or geeky lyrics. "One of the best descriptions I've ever read about this era of British music is that things went from being about F you to we're F'd."
**American Version (Late 1970s)** : In North America, punk was deemed too political, crass, and violent. Sire Records, led by Seymour Stein, deliberately co-opted "New Wave" for its new signings (like The Ramones and Talking Heads) through a "Don't Call It Punk" marketing campaign, to make the music more palatable to the American audience. "Sire launched a marketing campaign for these new signings under the banner, don't call it punk."
**Two Versions Merge (Late 1970s)** : By the end of the 1970s, there were distinct UK and American versions of New Wave, characterized as an energetic, guitar-based, slightly jittery brand of power pop, often featuring musicians in suit jackets and skinny ties. "By the end of the 70s, there were two slightly different versions of New Wave, one for America and one for the UK."
**Second Wave and Synthesizers (Early 1980s)** : The second wave of New Wave began around 1980 with the widespread adoption of synthesizers, which became cheaper, more portable, and powerful. Musicians, often without formal training, experimented with synths to create new and futuristic sounds, leading to the birth of synth-pop. "The second wave began in earnest in about 1980 with the widespread introduction of synthesizers."
**MTV's Role in Definition (Early 1980s)** : The debut of MTV on August 1, 1981, was a defining moment. New Wave bands, particularly those of the second wave, were perfectly suited for television with their visual appeal (makeup, wild clothes, personas), leading to a "second British invasion" as MTV acquired more British promotional films. MTV essentially established what New Wave meant in the early 1980s, encompassing anything with a progressive sound, closer to punk than traditional blues-based rock and roll. "MTV and other video outlets established what new wave was in the early 1980s."
**Subgenres and Decline (Mid-1980s)** : New Wave became a broad category, leading to the emergence of subgenres like synthpop (including New Romantics), dark guitar-focused bands (Public Image Limited, Joy Division, The Cure), "dorky" bands (Devo, B-52s, Oingo Bongo), and post-disco new wave (New Order, Dead or Alive, Eurythmics, The Human League). Some acts, like Culture Club and Wham!, stripped away punk DNA. By 1985, the concept of New Wave became too unwieldy and meaningless due to blurring genre differences, leading to its decline. "by 1985, the genre differences were just too blurry in all the commonalities had been wiped out."

# genre - Punk Rock

## curiosities
**Origins of New Wave** : New Wave emerged as punk rockers raged in the mid-to-late 1970s. Musicians were drawn to punk's free expression but not its anger, aggression, violence, and politics. New Wave music could not have existed without punk, indicating an inextricable relationship. "This was music that wasn't punk, but could not have happened unless punk had gotten their first."
**North American Reception (1970s)** : Punk did not gain traction in North America as it did in the UK. It was seen as too political, class-oriented, crass, vulgar, and violent, leading to radio stations refusing to play bands like The Ramones and record labels fearing to release records by The Clash in America. Critics also dismissed it as a fad. "One could never really cut on in North America, the same way that it did in the UK."

# curiosity

**Periods of Cultural Revival** : History shows a cyclical pattern where old productions, movies, TV shows, and musical sounds are constantly brought back and renewed, sometimes by generations removed from the original. Examples include Broadway revivals (West Side Story, Porgy and Bess), movie reboots (Ghostbusters, King Kong, Spider-Man, A Star Is Born), and TV remakes (90210, Twin Peaks, Star Trek). "It is a fact of life that there are periods where everything old is new again."
**Music as a Recycled Art Form** : Music is consistently recycled and renewed, with scenes and sounds reappearing, sometimes discovered independently or stumbled upon through older records. This ongoing process highlights how music continually evolves by referencing its past. "Whatever the case and whatever the source, music is constantly being recycled and renewed."
**"Rock is sick and living in London" (1977)** : This headline appeared on the cover of Rolling Stone magazine on October 20th, 1977, featuring The Sex Pistols. It captured the critical sentiment and controversy surrounding punk rock at the time, with some critics branding it a short-sighted fad. "The October 20th, 1977 edition of Rolling Stone magazine had the sex pistols on the cover, but the headline, Rock is sick and living in London."
**Synthesizers in Early 1980s** : Around 1980, synthesizers became widespread because the gear became sufficiently cheap, portable, and powerful for everyday musicians. This accessibility fostered a "punk" approach to the technology, as many users lacked formal musical training but enjoyed experimenting to create cool, unprecedented sounds. "it took until the beginning of the 80s for the gear to become cheap enough, portable enough, and powerful enough to be taken up by everyday musicians."
**Second British Invasion (1980s)** : With MTV promoting British music videos, a surge of British artists appeared on American TV screens, creating what was literally called a "second British invasion." These acts, deemed part of the New Wave, greatly benefited from the visual medium. "It was literally a second British invasion."
**New Wave Revival of the 2000s** : Beginning in the early 2000s, a new generation of bands emerged, drawing heavily from their parents' or older siblings' record collections. They rediscovered and were inspired by classic New Wave artists like New Order, The Cure, Duran Duran, David Bowie, Depeche Mode, The Smiths, Joy Division, and Blondie, creating a sound that was fresh yet familiar. "At the beginning of the 2000s, we began to hear from a new generation of young bands who obviously spent a lot of time going through their parents or older siblings' record collections."
**"New New Wave" Term (Early 2000s)** : British music papers frequently used the term "New New Wave" to describe the new wave revivalist bands emerging from the UK in the early 2000s, such as Bloc Party, The Kaiser Chiefs, Ladytron, The Ting Tings, and The Foals. "In fact, if you read any write-ups in the British music papers, you'll have seen them refer to as New New Wave."
**New Wave Revivalists in New York (Early 2000s)** : On the American side, many new wave revivalists in the early 2000s were based in New York, including The Yeah Yeah Yeahs, Interpol, The Rapture, and The Bravery. "On this side of the Atlantic, a lot of new wave revivalists seem to be headquartered in New York, the Ye-E-S, Interpol, the Rapture, and maybe the best of them all, or at least the most obvious of them all, the bravery."
**Book: Rip It Up and Start Again, Post Punk 1978 to 1984** : Written by Simon Frith, this book is an excellent recommended reading that delves deeply into the concepts of New Wave and Post Punk, covering the period from 1978 to 1984. "there's an excellent book on the whole concept of New Wave and Post Punk called Rip It Up and Start Again, Post Punk 1978 to 1984 by Simon Frith."
**Enduring Musical Influences** : New Wave's influences continue to be felt today, fragmenting into various subgenres like Electroclash, Cold Wave, Dark Wave, Synth Wave, and Chill Wave, as well as terms like Post Disco, New Pop, and New Wave. "New Wave's influences continue to be felt today."
**Future Revivals** : While scenes like Industrial, Goth, Shoegaze, and Britpop have never truly disappeared, they might again be adopted en masse by new generations, much like Punk, Ska, Emo, Garage Rock, and New Wave, taking these sounds to new places. "Who knows what sounds might come around again in the future to dominate our attention again."
