## albums
**16 Stone (1994) – Breakthrough Album** : Released on December 6, 1994, this album, once discovered by the public, sold 50,000 copies a week for months on end. Its success was a significant factor in the band selling approximately 20 million records worldwide during the 1990s. "That's Bush and Machine Head from 16 Stone, an album released on December 6, 1994."
**Razorblade Suitcase (1996)** : This album also achieved multi-million sales, indicating its commercial success. Gavin Rossdale referenced doing a lot of "research and swallowing" from this record, suggesting a deep immersion in its creation or impact. "And so I was doing a lot of research and swallowing from 1996's Razorblade suitcase."
**The Science of Things (1999)** : Released in October 1999, this album hit the market about four months after Napster launched, placing it in a period of significant industry change due to digital file sharing. "The chemicals between us, from the 1999 album, The Science of Things."
**Sea of Memories (2011) – Self-Funded Comeback** : This record was largely recorded while Gavin Rossdale was still signed to Interscope, with support from Jimmy Iovine. Nearing completion, Rossdale left Interscope and personally funded the album's setup and distribution. It successfully reached number one in America, marking the first time a self-released record achieved this feat, and also performed well in Canada, eventually repaying his initial investment through touring. "First self-released ever to do that here in America."
**Man On the Run (2014) – Personal Label Release** : This album was released on Gavin Rossdale's personal label, Zoomer Rock Records. Initially self-funded, a distribution deal was later secured with Red after Rossdale's manager, Lenny Beer, initiated talks and Red expressed interest beyond simple distribution, eventually taking over further aspects of the release. "I worked really hard on Man On the Run, this record that we have now."

## curiosities
**Career Transition from 90s Rock Star to Indie Musician** : Gavin Rossdale's journey with Bush exemplifies the transition from multi-platinum success in the 1990s to adapting to the contemporary, post-CD music business. He consciously embraced the new realities, including digital distribution and self-funding, as a means to continue making music. "How can a musician who enjoyed multi-platinum glory days in the 1990s adjust to the new realities of the music business today?"
**Departure from Interscope and Self-Funding** : Rossdale made a proactive decision to leave Interscope after a conversation with Jimmy Iovine, opting to fund his album "Sea of Memories" himself rather than wait to be "tapped on the shoulder." Interscope was generous in providing previous recordings, but the bulk of the album's setup and execution was a personal investment. "I had a conversation with Jimmy and I wanted to leave before I was tapped on the shoulder into whatever."
**Entrepreneurial Approach and Personal Label** : Rossdale has adopted an entrepreneurial mindset, effectively running his own music business with the assistance of managers and distribution deals. The release of his 2014 album "Man On the Run" on his personal label, Zoomer Rock Records, signifies a return to the independent artist model, reminiscent of his early career. "So you've turned into a real entrepreneur. You're doing things, you're running your own business essentially right now with help from a couple of managers and you've got a distribution deal and that's the thing."
**Embracing Digital Consumption and Streaming** : Rossdale actively uses Spotify Premium and purchases music from Beatport for harder-to-find tracks, acknowledging the collective effort required in the new music ecosystem. He views streaming as "absolutely incredible" for fostering interest and providing instant access to music. "So I fully embraced the way that music is now, and I have my Spotify premium."
**Pledge Music for Fan Engagement** : Bush utilized Pledge Music as a crowdfunding platform, not primarily for financing, but to generate excitement and anticipation for new releases. This strategy helps to navigate a fragmented media landscape by directly engaging fans and ensuring awareness, compensating for the decline of traditional promotional channels like MTV or numerous alternative radio stations. "pledge music is an incredible company. This solely set up to create excitement and anticipation that we used to get when there was incredible, at least we were, I mean, I'm going to talk about America and other than the encounter, we did so great and many more stations than there are now."
**Mentorship Role on The Voice** : Gavin Rossdale served as a mentor on "The Voice," recognizing it as an "incredibly gracious platform" to promote his new record. He appreciated the hard work and discipline demonstrated by the aspiring singers, noting their different path compared to the traditional pub/club circuit he experienced. "You're a mentor on the voice. What does that mean? It means an incredibly gracious platform with which to announce that I have a new record."
**Artist Philosophy on Performance and Creation** : Rossdale's artistic philosophy centers on pouring maximum effort into making the best possible record, free from commercial expectations or statistical pressures. He performs every live show with the intensity of it being his last, maintaining the same energy and intention for audiences of 400 or 40,000, believing this commitment is vital for an artist's longevity and to earn audience support. "The main thing my only job and my only concern is to pour over that record and make it the best that I can so that if God forbid, somebody takes this time and doesn't do the whole thing on speakers with somebody else, they can enjoy it together."
**Early Career Gamble and Indispensability Avoidance** : In his early career, Rossdale deliberately chose menial labor jobs that paid no more than $50 a day and where he could not become indispensable. This strategic choice was made to prevent any alternative career path from becoming too enticing and diverting him from his primary goal of pursuing music, a significant gamble that ultimately paid off. "Every job I did was only mean your labor and it was only in places where I knew that they would never, I could not become indispensable."

# curiosity

**Musicians' Riches from Plastic Sales (Pre-Digital)** : In a past era, musicians could amass considerable wealth by selling physical "pieces of plastic" (records). These recordings were highly valued as the exclusive method of music distribution, with their supply and price tightly controlled by the music industry. Major acts could achieve sales of two million albums in a single week in the United States, leading to extravagant lifestyles for artists and substantial profits for labels. "Their riches came by selling pieces of plastic to those who enjoyed their music."
**Music Industry Overlords' Wealth** : Historically, the "overlords" of the music industry (record labels) accumulated immense wealth from music sales, often far exceeding that of the artists. These powerful entities were reluctant to make changes that might undermine their established kingdoms or reduce their profits. "The overlords enjoyed their immense wealth and were loathed to do anything that might jeopardize the power of their kingdoms."
**Digital Era Impact on Music Sales** : The advent of the digital era irrevocably ended the "glory days" of physical music sales. Physical album sales are in decline, digital album sales have plateaued and are now falling, and profit margins on music sales have become "razor-thin." Streaming services are further "cannibalizing" any remaining sales, forcing a return to pre-1965 business models for many artists. "Then came the digital era, which killed off those glory days forever."
**Shift from Sales to Live Performance and Streaming Metrics** : In the modern music industry, artists can no longer reliably expect significant record sales. Success is now often measured by digital metrics such as YouTube views, Pandora plays, and Spotify streams. Live performances have become a crucial and primary income source, with recorded music primarily serving as a "calling card" to promote tours. "Obviously it was incredible to have that opportunity to make that kind of money."
**Decline of Traditional Music Promotion Channels** : The traditional avenues for music promotion have significantly diminished: the number of alternative radio stations in America has drastically decreased from roughly a thousand to around 180, and MTV and VH1 no longer primarily broadcast music videos. The number of music magazines and physical record stores has also dwindled. This fragmentation necessitates artists finding new methods, such as crowdfunding platforms like Pledge Music or social media, to raise awareness for their music. "So if I, excuse me if I use an American example, but when we began it back and there, I think there were a thousand alternative stations."
**Napster's Impact on Music Industry (1999)** : Napster, launched in 1999, approximately four months before Bush's "The Science of Things" album, was a transformative force that fundamentally altered the music industry by enabling widespread and free file sharing. Its arrival marked a significant turning point for how music was consumed and distributed. "It came out in October of that year, which was about four months after this thing called Napster hit the interwebs. And we all know what happened after that."
**Beats by Dre and Audio Quality** : Jimmy Iovine's involvement in creating Beats with Dr. Dre was partly a strategic business move, but also motivated by a concern for the "lack of quality in the headphones" available at the time, particularly earbuds. The venture aimed to address this perceived deficiency in audio consumption. "Part of the whole premise of Jimmy doing beats with Dre was some of it was business, which says paid off, Jimmy's quite a good marketing guy, was because of the lack of quality in the headphones."
**The Voice as a Platform** : The television show "The Voice" serves as an "incredibly gracious platform" for artists, especially mentors, to promote new records and gain public visibility. It offers a contemporary, albeit unconventional, route for aspiring singers to develop their careers, differing from traditional club circuits. "It means an incredibly gracious platform with which to announce that I have a new record."
**Artist's Role as a "Wandering Minister"** : The role of a musician is analogized to an "ancient job," resembling a "wandering minister" from 10,000 years ago who would travel, play songs, and offer remedies. This perspective emphasizes the timeless human need for communication, creativity, and artistic expression, irrespective of modern industry structures or motivations for fame. "I always say it's like a wandering minister, my job in this modern world."
**Artists vs. Labels Profit Share** : Historically, the music industry was structured to heavily favor record labels, which reaped significantly more profit than artists. Even when bands achieved moderate success after accounting for expenses and splits, their earnings were considerably less than the wealth accumulated by label executives like Jimmy Iovine. "But that's how it goes and that's how the industry was built upon the sort of massacre of the artists and the profit of the labels."
