# genre - Goth Music

## curiosities
**Origin as a Subculture** : Goth music culture is one of the biggest musical subcultures globally, with its explosion beginning after the original punk era of the 1970s. It's speculated to be the second biggest music-related subculture after metal, encompassing music, fashion, art, lifestyle, and sometimes politics and religion. "goth music culture is one of the biggest musical subcultures the planet has ever seen."
**British Invention with Early American Practitioners** : Goth largely emerged in Britain in the late 1970s after punk rock declined, though early American artists like The Doors and The Velvet Underground had some sinister sounds. "goth is very much a British invention, with one tiny exception."
**Meaning of "Gothic" in Music** : The word "gothic" was first used to describe modern music in relation to The Doors' first album, due to its dark, mysterious, and violent qualities. "As far as we can tell, the first use of the word gothic to describe modern music was used in relation to the doors, first album."
**Etymology of "Goths"** : One story suggests British punk refugees who made gloomy music were called "goths" because they lived in an apartment building named Visigoth Towers in Brixton, London. Alternatively, music journalist Nick Kent may have coined the term. "One story says they became known as goths because a bunch of them all lived in the same apartment building, a place called Visigoth Towers in the Brixton area of London."
**Post-Punk Genesis** : After the original punk scene fragmented, darker-minded punks gravitated towards artists already making dark music. This music, often less aggressive and more introspective and artsy than punk, incorporated existentialist philosophy, romanticism, art history, nihilism, and macabre horror elements. "As the original punk scene lost its legs, it began shattering into many different pieces. The darker-minded punk slowly coalesced around artists who were already making dark music."
**Fashion Aesthetics** : Goth fashion, often called "menacing" or "disconcerting" to outsiders, is characterized by wearing all black, except for occasional shades of purple or bright red (especially lipstick). Styles often harken back to 19th-century British artsy class attire, some androgenous, others BDSM-inspired, creating an erotic, mysterious, "dressed for a never-ending funeral" look. "This insistence on wearing black everything became part of the glue that held the new scene together."
**Reclaiming the Term "Goth"** : Initially an insult, the term "Goth" was reclaimed by the scene by the summer of 1982, becoming a self-identifier. "At the beginning, to be called Goth was an insult. But by the summer of 1982, the scene reclaimed that word as their own."
**Musical Characteristics** : Early Goth music featured tribal drums, bass players working high on the fretboard (sometimes carrying the melody), sharp, slashing, thin guitar sounds, and vocals ranging from growls to yelps to operatic. The overall effect was often described as mesmerizing. "In the beginning, there was almost a Goth sound, tribal drums, bass players working high on the fretboard, sometimes carrying the melody, which of course was just not done in proper circles."
**Evolution and Influence (Mid-80s)** : By the mid-1980s, Goth's influence extended to other genres, including new industrial bands and electronic groups, and even some metal acts, demonstrating its pervasive and lasting impact. "By the mid-80s Goth's influence was spilling over into other genres."
**Resilience and Longevity** : Goth has proven to be one of the most resilient and long-lasting alternative rock subgenres to emerge from the post-punk era, consistently remaining in the cultural background and influencing new generations of bands with a darker perspective on life. "Goth has proven to be one of the most resilient and long-lasting of the alt-rock subgenres that emerged from the post-punk era."

# concert - The Batcave

## curiosities
**Soho London Club (July 1982)** : Opened in July 1982 in London's Soho district by members of the band Specimen, The Batcave became the essential headquarters and central hub for the emerging goth scene. "In July 1982, a new club opened in the Soho district of London. It was called the Batcave."
**Theatrical Decor and Policy** : Decorated with black walls, leather, lace, and design cues from 1930s-40s black and white horror films, the club enforced an unofficial dress code and a strict music policy that excluded funky or poppy sounds, focusing on a reinvention of glam-era David Bowie crossed with Tim Curry's Dr. Frank N. Furter from The Rocky Horror Picture Show. "Specimen's club had black walls and was decorated with leather and lace and other design cues from black and white horror films from the 30s and 40s. # curiosity

**Mount Tambora Eruption (April 10, 1815)** : Ejected nearly 200 cubic kilometers of debris into the atmosphere, causing global cooling and making 1816 "the year without a summer." This eruption was the biggest in 1300 years. "All that dust circled the earth, blocking out a significant amount of sunlight."
**Year Without a Summer (1816)** : Resulted from the Mount Tambora eruption, causing a global temperature drop of almost a full degree. Led to food shortages, famines, disease outbreaks, and severe storms in Europe, providing the dark atmosphere for the birth of gothic fiction. "1816 has gone down in history as the year without a summer."
**Villa Diodati Gathering (Summer 1816)** : During the "year without a summer," four artsy types, including John William Polidori and Mary Shelley, were holed up in a mansion near Geneva, Switzerland. To entertain themselves during the dark, cold, wet, rainy days, they drank, had sex, took opium, and competed to create the best horror story, laying the foundation for gothic fiction. "To entertain themselves through these dark, cold, wet, rainy days, these people drank, had sex, and tocopium. And they tried to outdo each other by coming up with the best horror story."
**Nick Kent - Music Journalist** : Possibly coined the term "gothic" when describing the early gloomy music and associated fans made by British punk refugees. "Or it might have been music journalist Nick Kent, who started throwing the word about when describing these people."
**English Mood and Goth Music** : The gloomy music made by early goths was seen as reflective of the mood in England during the late 1970s and early 1980s, characterized by high unemployment, endless strikes, recession, and Margaret Thatcher's policies, especially impacting young people. "Whatever the case, these music fans began making music that was equal to the mood in England at the time. There was high unemployment, there were endless strikes, a recession, market thatcher, very popular, high unemployment, especially for young people."
**Bromley Contingent** : A group of dedicated fans who followed the Sex Pistols everywhere, including to the famous Bill Grundy TV show in December 1976. Susan Ballion (Siouxsie Sioux) was a member of this contingent. "When the Sex Pistols were on the Ascendant, they had a group of fans that followed them everywhere. They were called the Bromley contingent."
**Bill Grundy TV Show (December 1976)** : A famous television appearance by the Sex Pistols, accompanied by their fans, the Bromley Contingent, which included Susan Ballion. "including to the famous Bill Grundy TV show in December 1976."
**Bela Lugosi - Vampire Actor** : The world's most famous vampire actor, whose portrayal of Dracula in movies inspired the Bauhaus song "Bela Lugosi's Dead," a foundational track for the goth scene due to its themes of vampires, death, bats, and bell towers. "And of course, Bella Legosi, the actor famous for playing Dracula in the movies."
**Goth's Big Break (Early 1983)** : The British music media, weary of happy, bouncy, lightweight technopop that had been everywhere for a couple of years, declared it "boring and dead" in early 1983. This declaration positioned goth as the new sound and led to songs featuring black-clad artists with distinctive makeup and hair filling the charts. "Goth's Big Break came in early 1983. And we're getting a little weary of the happy, bouncy, lightweight techno pop that had been everywhere for the past couple of years."
**British Music Media Influence** : In the early 1980s, the British music media was exceptionally powerful and influential, capable of shaping music trends and chart performance. This was evident when they declared technopop dead and goth the new sound, immediately resulting in goth artists populating the charts. "And because the British music media was so powerful and influential back then, the charts immediately began to fill with songs featuring people dressed in black and wearing mascara, and deathly white makeup and big teased black hair, often carefully crimped."
**Goth Fashion (Detailed)** : Goth fashion, a form of artistic expression, includes dog collars, lace, leather, velvet, fishnet stockings, ruffled shirts, stilettos or Doc Martens, and often black painted nails. These elements contribute to the distinctive "dressed for a never-ending funeral" aesthetic. "They wore dog collars and lace and leather and velvet and fishnet stockings and ruffled shirts and stilettos or dock martens and painted nails, often black."
**"Positive Punks" Term (1984)** : By 1984, music papers began using the term "positive punks" non-ironically to describe some goth groups. This indicated that these bands focused on art that provoked introspection and examination rather than any kind of revolution. "By 1984, there were Goth bands all over England. The music papers started calling some of these groups positive punks in a completely non-eronic way."
**Jim Steinman - Songwriter/Producer** : Known for his work on Meat Loaf's "Bat Out of Hell" and Bonnie Tyler's "Total Eclipse of the Heart," Steinman collaborated with The Sisters of Mercy in the late 1980s, cross-pollinating musical styles. "That was most evident in the late 1980s when they teamed up with songwriter Jim Steinman, the guy behind Meatloaf's Battle of Hell."
**"Gothic Goblins" Term** : Ian Astbury, who would later front The Cult, coined the term "Gothic Goblins" to describe the followers of Sex Gang Children, contributing to the evolving lexicon of the goth scene. "The term Goth stuck a little harder after Ian Aspery, who would later front the cult, called the followers of a group called Sex Gang Children, Gothic Goblins."
**Tim Burton's Influence** : The goth scene is credited as a foundational influence for film director Tim Burton's aesthetic and cinematic style. "Would there be a Tim Burton, if not for the Goth scene? Nope."
**Batman's Gothic Form** : The current form of Batman's aesthetic is noted as being influenced by the goth look, indicating the genre's crossover into mainstream fashion and pop culture. "Would Batman exist in his current form without Goth? Nope."
