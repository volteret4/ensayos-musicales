# genre - Gothic Fiction

## curiosities
**Foundation in 1816** : Gothic fiction was founded in 1816, known as "the year without a summer." During this period, four artsy individuals, including John William Polidori and Mary Shelley, were holed up in Villa Diodati near Geneva, Switzerland. To pass the time amidst dark, cold, wet, rainy days, they drank, had sex, took opium, and competed to create the best horror story. Polidori conceived "The Vampyre," about undead bloodsuckers, 80 years before Bram Stoker's *Dracula*. Mary Shelley, then 22, envisioned *Frankenstein*, about a mad scientist creating a being from dead body parts. These stories laid the groundwork for a type of horror that endures in novels, movies, comic books, fashion, and music.

# genre - Goth Music

## curiosities
**Global Subculture (Post-Punk Era)** : Goth music culture is described as one of the biggest musical subcultures globally, speculated to be the largest music-related subculture after metal, with numerous adherents worldwide. Its explosion began in the wake of the original punk era of the 1970s.
**British Invention** : Goth is predominantly a British invention that emerged in Britain in the late 1970s as a byproduct of punk rock burning out, despite some early American practitioners.
**Visigoth Towers Origin Story** : One anecdote suggests that early Goths became known by the name because a group of them lived in an apartment building called Visigoth Towers in the Brixton area of London.
**Nick Kent Origin Story** : Alternatively, music journalist Nick Kent is credited with popularizing the term "gothic" to describe these individuals.
**Reflection of UK Mood (Late 1970s)** : The gloomy music made by early goth fans mirrored the mood in England at the time, characterized by high unemployment (especially for young people), endless strikes, a recession, and the unpopularity of Margaret Thatcher.
**Intellectual Components** : Goth music was less aggressive and more introspective and artsy than punk. It contained an intellectual dimension, incorporating elements of existentialist philosophy, romanticism, art history, nihilism, and the trappings of horror and the macabre, while still possessing a strength to endure rather than being whiny.
**Initial Insult to Reclaimed Identity (Early 1980s)** : Initially, being called "Goth" was considered an insult. However, by the summer of 1982, the scene reclaimed the word as its own identity.
**Aesthetic and Fashion (Early 1980s)** : Goth developed a look that was both understated and menacing or disconcerting to outsiders. The insistence on wearing "black everything" was a unifying element. Styles often harked back to British artsy classes of the 1800s, reminiscent of Mary Shelley's era. Looks ranged from androgynous to BDSM-inspired, described as erotic, mysterious, and "dressed for a never-ending funeral." The primary color was black, with exceptions only for some shades of purple and bright red (especially for lipstick). By early 1983, with goth gaining mainstream attention, charts filled with artists in black attire, mascara, deathly white makeup, big teased and crimped black hair, dog collars, lace, leather, velvet, fishnet stockings, ruffled shirts, stilettos or Doc Martens, and often black painted nails. This fashion was considered as much artistic expression as style.
**Big Break and Media Recognition (Early 1983)** : Goth's big break came in early 1983 when the British music media, tired of "happy, bouncy, lightweight technopop," declared technopop "boring and therefore dead." The media then proclaimed goth as the "new sound."
**"Positive Punks" Label (1984)** : By 1984, as goth bands proliferated across England, music papers began calling some groups "positive punks" in a non-ironic way. This term signified that these bands were not advocating revolution but rather creating art that provoked introspection and examination.
**Musical Evolution (Early Days)** : Early goth music had a distinct sound characterized by tribal drums, bass players working high on the fretboard (sometimes carrying the melody, which was unconventional), and sharp, slashing, thin guitar sounds. Vocal styles ranged from growls and yelps to operatic deliveries, creating a mesmerizing effect.
**Global Expansion (Early 1980s)** : Goth expanded globally in the early 1980s, with significant American acts like Christian Death and 45 Grave (from Southern California, more punk-influenced), The Cramps (New York City), Type O Negative (Brooklyn), and Clan of Xymox (Netherlands). A notable goth scene also emerged in Florida, influencing artists like Marilyn Manson and The Marked (Billy Corgan's pre-Smashing Pumpkins band). Despite this, the UK remained goth's spiritual center.
**Influence on Other Genres and Arts (Mid-1980s onwards)** : By the mid-1980s, goth's influence spread to other genres. New industrial bands, such as Nine Inch Nails (led by Trent Reznor), adopted gothic trappings. Some electronic and even metal acts started to look and sound gothic. This influence intensified, ensuring goth remained an omnipresent background force, occasionally resurfacing with new generations of bands. Goth also infiltrated other art forms, notably influencing filmmakers like Tim Burton and even the modern aesthetic of Batman.
**Sociological Study and Resilience** : The complexity of the goth scene has led to intense sociological study, with books and theses dedicated to understanding its adherents. Goth is considered one of the most resilient and long-lasting alt-rock subgenres to emerge from the post-punk era, often described as an "undead" and "immortal" subculture that will endure indefinitely.

# venue - Batcave

## curiosities
**Headquarters of Early Goth (July 1982)** : The Batcave was a club that opened in July 1982 in London's Soho district, becoming the essential "headquarters" and "ground zero for Goth proper." It was founded by members of the band Specimen.
**Aesthetic and Policy** : The club featured black walls decorated with leather, lace, and design cues from 1930s and 1940s black-and-white horror films. It had an unofficial dress code and a strict music policy: "Nothing funky, nothing poppy." The musical and aesthetic focus was on reinventing glam-era David Bowie (e.g., *Ziggy Stardust* or *Aladdin Sane*) crossed with Tim Curry's Dr. Frank-N-Furter from *The Rocky Horror Picture Show*, fostering an atmosphere of sexual inclusivity (straight, gay, and everything in between).
**Influence and Expansion** : The Batcave became incredibly popular, leading to franchising. "Batcave nights" were staged at other clubs around the UK, maintaining the same look, feel, and sound. It even established a residency in New York at a place called Danceteria. The Batcave played a crucial role, arguably more than any other entity, in spreading the "gospel of Goth" in its early days.

# curiosity

**Mount Tambora Eruption (April 10, 1815)** : A massive volcanic eruption occurred on April 10, 1815, in the central Indonesian archipelago. Mount Tambora ejected nearly 200 cubic kilometers of debris into the atmosphere, causing dust to circle the earth and block significant sunlight. This event led to a nearly full-degree drop in global average temperature.
**The Year Without a Summer (1816)** : The climatic effects of the 1815 Mount Tambora eruption resulted in 1816 being historically known as "the year without a summer." This period saw widespread food shortages, famines, disease outbreaks, extreme cold, and severe storms across much of Europe. These conditions indirectly fostered the environment that gave birth to gothic fiction, which in turn influenced goth music.
**UK Social and Economic Conditions (Late 1970s/Early 1980s)** : The mood in England during the late 1970s and early 1980s was characterized by high unemployment, endless strikes, a recession, and the unpopularity of Margaret Thatcher. These bleak conditions, particularly high unemployment among young people, provided fertile ground for the emergence of gloomy music genres like goth, as punk rock began to fragment.
**Music Video Directors - Director X & Taj Krischlo** : Director X and Taj Krischlo are described as two of the biggest music video directors globally, known for creating "game-changing videos" for artists such as Drake, Coldplay, and Kendrick Lamar. Taj Krischlo specifically praised Director X as "the goat" for his enduring relevance since the late 1990s. **Art Context Podcast** : This is a new podcast, hosted by Director X and Taj Krischlo, titled "Art Context: The Architects of Art." It focuses on music video directors as "storytellers," aiming to give these creative artists the recognition they deserve by delving into their "come-ups, their journey, their process of creating some of the most iconic music videos, films and content on the planet." The podcast explores the behind-the-scenes aspects of music video creation, including storyboards, art direction, and collaboration. The first episode featured Dave Myers and discussed topics such as his philosophy toward art and broader world issues like Black Lives Matter from a white director's perspective in a Black music world. The hosts were inspired by 1980s music videos from artists like Madonna, Peter Gabriel, Phil Collins, Michael Jackson, and Aerosmith. The podcast can be followed at @architects_pods.
