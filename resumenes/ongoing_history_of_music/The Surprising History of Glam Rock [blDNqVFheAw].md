# artist - T-Rex

## members
Mark Bowen

## genres
Glam Rock

## concerts

## albums
**Electric Warrior (1971)** : Released in September 1971, this album elevated the sound and attitude established by "Ride a White Swan." It was characterized as fun, funny, cartoony, fantastical, and provocatively naughty, making it a significant album that rocked with a distinct glam rock style and sound, and is considered the first glam rock album. "Electric Warrior, I think we can say, was the first glam rock album."

## songs
**20th Century Boy (1973)** : A major glam rock hit released in the spring of 1973. "a major glam rock hit from the spring of 1973."
**Ride a White Swan (1970)** : Released in October 1970, this song became a significant British hit in late 1970 and early 1971, accidentally defining the sound of glam rock. T-Rex performed it twice on Top of the Pops. "Not only was it a hit, but it accidentally found the sound for glam rock."
**Bang a Gong (Get It On) (1971)** : A major hit single from Mark Bowen and T-Rex in the UK, which also became a radio hit for FM rock stations in North America. This song helped define the sound of the *Electric Warrior* album. "Bang a gong, a major hit single from Mark Bowen and T-Rex in the UK, and a radio hit for FM Rock stations in North America."

## albums
**The Rise and Fall of Ziggy Stardust and The Spiders from Mars (1972)** : This album was the major hit Bowie had sought, launching glam rock to prominence. The accompanying tour (January 29, 1972 - July 3, 1973) was rock's most theatrical to date, featuring costume changes, choreography, makeup, tall boots, high platform shoes, and daring sexual ambiguity. "The album, The Rise and Fall of Ziggy Stardust and The Spiders from Mars, was the hit he'd been looking for."

## songs
**Space Oddity (1969)** : Bowie had a minor hit in 1969 with a song about an astronaut named Major Tom, predating his glam rock persona. "In 1969, he had a minor hit about an astronaut named Major Tom."
**Rebel Rebel (1974)** : This song, with its lyric "Your Mother Can't Tell if you're a boy or a girl," explicitly showcases glam rock's connection to the LGBT community and its exploration of sexual ambiguity. "Your Mother Can't Tell if you're a boy or a girl."

## curiosities
**Early Influence from Noel Coward** : As a young man, David Bowie was introduced to the work of Noel Coward, who was still making music for the West End and Broadway in the mid-1960s, contributing to Bowie's understanding of style and showmanship. "And this is how a new generation learned about him, and that included a young David Bowie."
**Fan of Marc Bolan and Electric Warrior** : David Bowie was Mark Bowen's (Bolan's) biggest fan, indicating the strong mutual influence between the two emerging glam artists. "But Bowen's biggest fan was an emerging artist named David Bowie."
**Folk Singer Origins** : David Bowie started his career as a folk singer in the 1960s before transitioning to other styles. "he too had been a folk singer in the 1960s."
**Friendship with Marc Bolan** : Bowie and Mark Bowen (Bolan) became close friends, fostering a mutual influence as both artists sensed a shift in the musical landscape. "Bowie and Bowen became close friends."
**Appreciation for Pop Songs** : Through his evolving artistic direction, Bowie developed a new appreciation for "short, sharp pop songs." "He developed a new appreciation for short, sharp pop songs."
**Developing Fashion Sense** : Bowie was actively cultivating a daring and interesting fashion sense, moving away from the prevailing hippie aesthetic of long hair and jeans. "Bowie was also developing a very interesting and daring fashion sense."
**Hype Costumes (1970)** : In 1970, with his band Hype, Bowie began experimenting with stage costumes. They wore superhero outfits made from shiny material, thigh-high boots, and Bowie's own costume included a cape and the word "rainbow man" sewn on his chest, a stark contrast to the hippie fashion of the time. "In 1970, around the time Mark Bowen was gaining fame with Ride-A-White Swan, Bowie had a band called Hype. They began appearing in interesting costumes."
**Creation of Ziggy Stardust** : Building on glam rock concepts, Bowie developed the character Ziggy Stardust. This persona was inspired by the outrageous behavior of Iggy Pop and The Stooges, the artsy "scummy" persona of Lou Reed and the Velvet Underground, the "god-alien" self-perception of Vince Taylor, and the "space travel obsessed" persona of Norman Odom, also known as the Legendary Stardust Cowboy. Bowie combined these influences, changing "Iggy" to "Ziggy" and adopting "Stardust" as the surname. "He invented an entire character completely rooted in glam rock concepts that borrowed from the outrageous behavior of another new friend, Iggy Pop... So Bowie, taking all this information, changed Iggy to Ziggy and adopted Stardust as a surname and thus Ziggy Stardust was born."
**Influence of Iggy Pop** : Bowie incorporated elements from the "outrageous behavior" of his friend Iggy Pop, who performed extreme acts with his band The Stooges in America, into the Ziggy Stardust persona. "borrowed from the outrageous behavior of another new friend, Iggy Pop, who was doing all kinds of extreme things in America with his band, The Stooges."
**Influence of Lou Reed and Velvet Underground** : Bowie was a fan of Lou Reed's band, The Velvet Underground, and Reed's "New York Artsy Scummy solo persona," which informed the development of Ziggy Stardust. "Bowie had also met Lou Reed. He was a fan of both Reed's band, the Velvet Underground, and Reed's New York Artsy Scummy solo persona."
**Influence of Vince Taylor** : Bowie was inspired by Vince Taylor, an English-American singer who, following a mental crisis, believed himself to be an entity between a god and an alien, contributing to Ziggy's otherworldly aspect. "He'd also discovered Vince Taylor, an English American singer, with some kind of mental crisis and now believed that he existed somewhere between a god and an alien."
**Influence of Norman Odom (Legendary Stardust Cowboy)** : Norman Odom, a "wacky space travel obsessed Texas singer" known as the Legendary Stardust Cowboy, provided inspiration for the "Stardust" surname and the cosmic elements of Bowie's Ziggy persona. "He also got inspiration from Norman Odom, a wacky space travel obsessed Texas singer who went by the name the legendary Stardust cowboy."
**Ziggy Stardust - Breakthrough and Theatricality** : The creation of Ziggy Stardust brought Bowie out of almost a decade of obscurity, elevating rock's theatricality to new levels. The character was presented as a "doomed spaceman with a Messiah concept," potentially involved in populating Earth with "Japanese style kabuki actors." Bowie used Ziggy to explore fashion, utilize his professional mime training from the 1960s, and experiment with art and sexuality. "this was the move that brought Bowie out of obscurity after almost a decade of dead ends."
**Mime Training** : David Bowie had professional mime training in the 1960s, a skill he integrated into his theatrical performances as Ziggy Stardust. "Bowie did infect train with professional mime in the 60s."
**Glam Rock Leadership** : Bowie recognized his role as a pioneer and leader in the glam rock movement, finding it a fitting categorization for his artistic output. "I think glam rock is a lovely way to categorize me and it's even nicer to be one of the leaders of it."
**Departure from Hippie Culture** : Ziggy Stardust marked a distinct departure from the prevailing hippie culture of the 1960s, which was preoccupied with issues like war, environment, advertising, big business, television, and global artificiality. "Ziggy was also a sharp move away from the hippie culture of the 1960s."
**Ziggy Stardust Costume Inspiration** : Ziggy Stardust's costumes drew inspiration from the main characters of Stanley Kubrick's controversial film *A Clockwork Orange*, which was banned in the UK for its depictions of violence and sex, as well as from Japanese designer Yasuko Hayashi. "Ziggy's clothes were inspired in equal parts by what we saw with the main characters in the film, a clockwork orange... And a Japanese designer named Yasuko Hayashi."
**Ziggy Stardust Hairdo Creation** : Bowie's iconic "Ziggy Stardust puff ball" hairstyle, a striking mullet, was conceived from photos in a fashion magazine called Honey (for the front) and French Vogue (for the back and sides). His mother's stylist, Susie Fussy, from Evelyn Page salon, helped create it, dyeing it "Schwartzkoft red" and stiffening it with an anti-dandruff treatment called Guard. This unisex haircut became a defining style of the era. "They created the famous Ziggy Stardust puff ball."
**Melody Maker Interview (January 1972)** : In early 1972, Bowie gave a front-page interview to British music weekly Melody Maker, titled "Oh, You Pretty Thing." The article described his image as a "swishy queen, a gorgeously effeminate boy," noting his "camp" demeanor while he maintained he was "ungay." This interview showcased his deliberate push for shocking outrage and sexual ambiguity. "He gave an interview to Melody Maker, one of the big British music weeklies. The title of the article was, oh, you pretty thing."
**Ziggy Stardust Debut (February 1972)** : David Bowie formally debuted the Ziggy Stardust character on February 10, 1972, at the Torquay Jug Pub in London. At the time, he was still a minor pop star, resulting in low attendance, but this performance marked the beginning of Ziggy's ascent. "Then on February 10th, 1972, David Bowie debuted the Ziggy character at the Torey Jug Pub in London."
**Ziggy Stardust Tour Theatricality** : The tour supporting "The Rise and Fall of Ziggy Stardust and The Spiders from Mars" (January 29, 1972 - July 3, 1973) set a new standard for theatricality in rock, incorporating "big end themic songs, costume changes, choreography, makeup, tall boots, and ridiculously high platform shoes" along with "daring sexual ambiguity." "the tour, which ran from January 29th, 1972 to July 3rd, 1973, was the most theatrical thing that rock had ever seen to that point."
**Top of the Pops Performance (July 6, 1972)** : Bowie's seminal performance on Top of the Pops on July 6, 1972, with The Spiders from Mars, was highly influential. Dressed in a quilted jumpsuit, red boxing boots with green laces, and bright red-orange hair, Bowie's anisocoria (permanently dilated pupil from a childhood fight) added to his "otherworldly look." His public display of affection towards guitarist Mick Ronson (draping his arm around his shoulders) was considered "super duper gay" for the era and had a profound impact, leading to the immediate appearance of "Ziggy Clones" and helping young people feel accepted in their sexuality. "One especially interesting performance was on top of the pops on the BBC, July 6th, 1972."
**Anisocoria (Eye Condition)** : David Bowie had anisocoria, a condition where one pupil was permanently dilated due to being punched in the eye by a childhood friend during a fight over a girl. This gave him an "otherworldly look" that contributed to his unique visual persona as Ziggy Stardust. "Bowie had something called anisocoria. One pupil was permanently dilated because he'd been punched in the eye by a childhood friend in a fight over a girl."
**Equipment Theft by Steve Jones (July 1973)** : During a multi-night stand at the Hammersmith Odeon in London in July 1973, David Bowie and The Spiders from Mars had microphones and amplifiers stolen by a young thief named Steve Jones, who later became the guitarist for the Sex Pistols. It is speculated that this stolen gear was used by the Sex Pistols in their early rehearsals and gigs in 1975, contributing to the punk rock era. "When Bowie and the spiders from Mars loaded into the Hammersmith Odien in London in July 1973 for a multi-night stand, a young thief named Steve Jones broke into the theatre and made off with a bunch of microphones and amplifiers."
**Transition to Aladdin Sane** : Around 1974, Bowie moved from his Ziggy Stardust persona to the Aladdin Sane character, which largely followed a similar glam-oriented trajectory. "we dumped a Ziggy for his Aladdin Sane character which continued down or less the same path."
**Post-Aladdin Sane Era** : After his Aladdin Sane persona, David Bowie transitioned away from glam to become a "decidedly non-glam blue-eyed soul singer" by 1976-1977. "Bowie had moved on from Aladdin's saying to a stint as a decidedly non-glam blue-eyed soul singer."

## curiosities
**Early Career - Taranosaurus Rex** : Mark Bowen, a London-based singer-songwriter and poet, began his career with the psychedelic folk band Taranosaurus Rex. This band released four largely acoustic albums in the 1960s but did not achieve significant hit success. "Bowen was a London-based singer-songwriter and poet. He started with a psychedelic folk band called Taranosaurus Rex, who released four largely acoustic albums in the 1960s without managing to have a proper hit."
**Transformation to T-Rex** : Frustrated by a lack of success or sensing a shift in the musical landscape, Bowen shortened the band's name to T-Rex and transitioned to playing an electric guitar, which marked a turning point in his career. "Bowen shortened the name to T-Rex and picked up an electric guitar."
**Glam Rock Appearance on Top of the Pops** : In March 1971, after "Ride a White Swan" became a hit, Mark Bowen appeared on Top of the Pops wearing satin clothes and glitter. This striking visual, in a "grey recession-ridden still in the post-war Doldrums England," was seen as fresh and new, further enhanced by Bowen's "petite and cute and non-threatening" appearance which had a "fluid" quality about it. "But in March 1971, Bowen appeared on the show wearing satin clothes and glitter."
**Impact on Sexual Identity** : Mark Bowen's glittery, satiny, and somewhat sexually ambiguous public persona on TV, especially given his height of five foot four, resonated deeply in early 1970s Britain, where homosexuality had only recently been decriminalized (1967 Sexual Offences Act) but public displays remained illegal. For some, his appearance pushed boundaries, while for others, it offered a liberating affirmation of their own non-binary feelings. "Now, for some, a pretty glittery, satiny, five foot four Mark Bowen on TV was really close to that line about doing something gay in public. For others, though, it was not just a breath of fresh air. It was also like, hey, that's me. I feel like that. And it's okay to be that way."
**Death (September 16, 1977)** : Mark Bowen died on September 16, 1977, when his girlfriend, Gloria Jones, crashed their Mini Cooper into a tree in southwest London. "Mark Bowlin died on September 16, 1977, when his girlfriend Gloria Jones drove their mini-cooper into a tree in southwest London."

## albums
**Roxy Music (1972)** : This self-titled debut album was released on June 16, 1972. It was characterized by its fashionable, glamorous, and artsy approach, incorporating film and cultural references, and venturing into the avant-garde with futuristic and retro, sexy and standoffish, silly and serious elements. Its experimental nature, particularly its heavy reliance on synthesizer on Side Two as noted by a Cream Magazine review in 1973, divided critics but appealed to a segment of British music fans, including future punks. "Their cell titled debut album was released on June 16th, 1972."

## songs
**Virginia Plain (1972)** : This song, released as a standalone single in the UK and part of the North American release of their debut album, notably served as a single about a brand of cigarettes, reflecting the band's unique and often peculiar approach. "Roxy Music and Virginia Plane, part of the North American release of their debut album, and a standalone single on the UK."
**Love Is the Drug (1975)** : A song that captures the essence of glam, with lyrics like "love is the drug for me." "love is the drug for me."

## curiosities
**Early Influence from Noel Coward** : The young man who would later form Roxy Music was influenced by Noel Coward's work, which was still popular on the West End and Broadway in the mid-1960s. "and the young man who would later form Roxy Music."
**Formation and Early Struggles** : Roxy Music was formed in 1970 as an experimental art rock outfit, with Brian Ferry fronting the band after being fired from his job teaching ceramics. After a slow start, they secured a record deal in 1971. "They were formed as an experimental art rock outfit in 1970, an out front was Brian Ferry, who had just been fired from his job teaching ceramics at a girl's school."
**Influence on British Punks** : Similar to David Bowie, Roxy Music garnered the attention of young people who would later become British punks, partly because their artifice and experimental sound, especially the synthesizer-heavy Side Two, were disliked by mainstream critics. "Like Bowie, Roxy Music attracted the attention of kids who would soon become Britain's punks. They especially liked that the critics weren't crazy about Roxy Music."
**Cream Magazine Review (1973)** : A 1973 review in Detroit's Cream Magazine criticized Roxy Music's debut album, describing its artifice as potentially "unhealthy as the sheen on a piece of rotten meat" and noting Side Two's excessive reliance on Brian Eno's synthesizer, which "leans a little too heavily on the synthesizer... without the saving grace of drums and bass line." Despite the harsh critique, this divisiveness made them appealing to a niche audience. "From the drag queen on the corner, to the fop finery in the centerfold, to the polished deformity of the music on the record, this celebrates the kind of artifice that could come to seem as unhealthy as the sheen on a piece of rotten meat."
**Highly Influential British Band** : Roxy Music is considered one of the most influential British bands of all time, with a profound impact on British punks, goth kids, New Romantics, and avant-garde artists. Their influence is evident in bands like Sex Pistols, Siouxsie and the Banshees, Duran Duran, The Smiths, Talking Heads, Radiohead, and Human League. "it's not an exaggeration to say that Roxy is one of the most influential British bands of all time."

# genre - Glam Rock

## curiosities
**Introduction and Influence** : Glam rock emerged in the late 1960s and early 1970s as a highly shocking and outrageous style, fundamentally altering rock music despite its relatively short lifespan of four to five years. Its influence is still observable and felt today. "This is the surprising history of glam rock. It still lives and can still make things very interesting."
**Definition and Characteristics** : Glam rock was a theatrical form of rock, a distinct musical sub-genre, and a significant movement in fashion, culture, and non-binary sexuality. It served as a bridge between 1960s rock and the developments of the 1970s and beyond. Predominantly a British phenomenon in the early 1970s, its influence (1971-1975) is still pervasive in rock worldwide, contributing to the existence of many important and long-lasting genres today. "Glam was rock at its most theatrical. Not only was it a sound, a proper musical sub-shin, but it was also a movement in fashion, the clothes, the hair, the makeup."
**Geographical and Temporal Scope** : Primarily a British phenomenon that emerged in the early 1970s, with its core influential period spanning from about 1971 to 1975. Despite its relatively short duration and regional focus, its influence spread globally. "Yes, this was a thing in the early 70s and yes, it was mostly a British thing."
**Peak and Widespread Influence** : Glam rock reached its peak around 1974, extending its influence beyond punk and post-punk into various rock acts. Artists like Elton John, Queen (with Freddie Mercury's flamboyance), and Kiss (with their makeup) visibly adopted glam aesthetics. Richard O'Brien, author of *The Rocky Horror Picture Show*, reportedly listened exclusively to glam rock during its creation. Even The Rolling Stones and Rod Stewart embraced glam trappings in the mid-1970s. "Glam peaks sometime around 1974... Glam seeped into other areas of rock beyond just punk and all the other post-punk genres."
**Decline and Enduring Influence** : Glam and glitter rock largely became "extinct" by 1977, but their influence persisted throughout the 1980s, 1990s, and continues to be seen today. "And while glam and glitter were pretty much extinct by 1977, its influence continued into the 80s, 90s and even today."
**Decline from Charts (1976-1977)** : Glam rock largely disappeared from the music charts by 1976-1977, coinciding with the rise of punk rock and disco. Despite its decline, the theatricality and exaggerated costuming it introduced became a permanent part of the rock canon. "Glam was all but gone from the charts by the time punk rock and disco came along in 1976 and 1977."
**Brief but Global Impact** : Glam rock's "moment in the sun" was short-lived, primarily from 1971 to 1975, and largely a UK phenomenon with only David Bowie and Roxy Music gaining significant traction in North America. However, its DNA of "extremism, flamboyance, and theatricality" has spread globally across rock and pop, continually reappearing in fashion and music trends. "Glam was not around for very long. We can terribly put its moment in the sun from 1971 to 1975."

**Early Roots of Glam - Noel Coward** : Glam rock's roots can be traced back to the 1930s, with Noel Coward, an English singer, actor, composer, director, and playwright, being cited as an early influence. His 1931 song "Mad Dogs in Englishmen" was a witty, ironic part of his cabaret act. Coward also wrote songs like "Mad About the Boy," "The Stately Homes of England," and the opera "Bittersweet," all characterized by a cheeky, stylish, poised, and confident approach that prioritized personal style and showmanship. "Students of glam will point to Noel Coward, the English singer, actor, composer, director and playwright."
**Catalyst for a Simpler Rock** : Around 1970, Mark Bowen became a catalyst for a return to a simpler, more fun, and escapist form of rock, contrasting with the increasing sophistication of progressive and art rock. "A solution emerged around 1970, and the catalyst was Mark Bowen."
**Decriminalization of Homosexuality in UK** : Homosexuality was decriminalized in the UK around three years prior to March 1971, with the passing of the Sexual Offences Act. However, this act stipulated that gay acts had to remain private, and public displays of affection were still illegal. Scotland did not decriminalize until 1980, and Northern Ireland until 1982, with a legal age of 21 for all cases. "homosexuality was finally decriminalized in the UK with the passing of the Sexual Offences Act."
**A Clockwork Orange Film Controversy** : Stanley Kubrick's film *A Clockwork Orange* was highly contentious and banned in the UK due to its explicit depictions of violence and sex, yet its aesthetics influenced David Bowie's Ziggy Stardust costumes. "the film was actually banned in the UK for all its depictions of violence and sex."
**Glam Rock and LGBT Community** : Glam rock was significantly connected to the LGBT community, normalizing androgynous fashion which was further spread by New Romantic acts of the early 1980s. Songs like David Bowie's "Rebel Rebel" and Lou Reed's "Walk on the Wild Side" explicitly addressed themes of sexual ambiguity and transsexuality. "much of Glam was connected to the LGBT community, something that could be heard throughout songs like David Bowie's Rebel Rebel... and Lourid's Walk on the Wild Side."
