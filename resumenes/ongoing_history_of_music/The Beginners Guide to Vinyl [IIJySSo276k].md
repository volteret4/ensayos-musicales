# instrument - Turntable

## curiosities
**Early History and Terminology** : The concept of storing music on a flat, rotating platter originated in the late 19th century with German inventor Emil Berliner's gramophone, which differed from Thomas Edison's phonograph that used a wax cylinder. Today, the terms "phonograph," "gramophone," "turntable," and "record player" are used interchangeably to describe a device consisting of a motor-driven platter that spins at standard speeds, using a stylus, cartridge, and tone arm to convert groove vibrations into electrical signals for amplification and sound output. "The idea of storing music is microscopic bumps in a spiral groove on a flat rotating platter. Goes all the way back to the late 19th century to a German inventor named Emil Berliner."
**Key Components and Function** : A turntable is comprised of a round platter that is spun by a motor at standard speeds. A stylus tracks the grooves on the record, and this stylus is connected to a cartridge at the end of a tone arm. The tiny mechanical wobbles of the stylus are converted into electrical signals by the cartridge, which are then transmitted up the tone arm to an amplifier and finally to speakers, where they are transformed into acoustic energy and audible sound. "A round platter connected to a motor that spins at standard speeds. The grooves on the record are traced by a stylus connected to a cartridge, which is at the end of a tone arm."
**Recommended Manufacturers** : Reputable turntable manufacturers include Rega from the UK, and Audio-Technica and Pioneer, both from Japan. The world's largest turntable producer is the Czech company Sev Litovel, which manufactures 150,000 turntables annually under various brand names, such as Project, with models ranging in price from $200 to over $10,000. "Manufacturers to investigate include Riga out of the UK, AudioTechnica from Japan, and Pioneer, which is also from Japan."
**Most Expensive Turntable** : The AV Design Haus Dereneville VPM 2010-1 is noted as the most expensive turntable observed, weighing over 80 kilos and featuring a starting price of $650,000 US dollars. "The most expensive turntable that I've ever seen is the AV design house Darrenville, VPM 2010-1."
**Drive Systems: Belt Drive** : Belt-drive turntables feature a motor located off to the side, sometimes in a separate housing, connected to the platter by a soft rubber belt. This design aims to isolate the platter from motor vibrations, thereby minimizing the transfer of low-frequency rumbles to the tone arm and improving the listening experience. "With belt drive, the motor that spins the turntable platter is off to the side, sometimes even separate from the main turntable housing."
**Drive Systems: Direct Drive** : Direct-drive turntables connect the motor directly to the platter through the spindle, ensuring the platter rotates at the exact same speed as the motor. Although some vibration transfer can occur, contemporary direct-drive models incorporate significant sound insulation. A key advantage of direct-drive turntables is their high torque, allowing them to reach playing speed (e.g., 33 1/3 RPM) almost instantly, a feature critical for radio stations and for DJs performing scratching and beat mixing. "A direct drive turntable is connected directly to the platter, right up through the spindle, which is that thing that goes through the hole in the record."
**Buying Used Turntables** : When purchasing a used turntable, it is important to inquire if the machine has always been owned by the original purchaser and if it has been used for DJing, as DJ use often indicates significant wear, especially if it's not a robust Technics SL-1200. The website vinylengine.com is a valuable resource, providing a comprehensive database of turntables with pictures, manuals, reviews, and forums to aid in research before making a purchase. "Has the machine you want always been in the possession of the original owner? If so, that's good."

# instrument - Vinyl Record

## curiosities
**Composition and Durability** : Vinyl, or polyvinyl chloride, is a plastic polymer derived from petroleum, initially used in the 1920s for manufacturing sewer pipes. It is a tough material that can be recycled up to seven times and has a lifespan of up to 140 years before degradation, although each recycling cycle introduces impurities. "The word vinyl is short for polyvinyl chloride, which chemist tell us is a plastic polymer made from petroleum that was first used in the 1920s for the manufacturer of sewer pipes."
**Virgin vs. Recycled Vinyl** : Records made from "virgin vinyl" are preferred as recycled vinyl introduces impurities that can negatively impact sonic quality. This issue became prevalent in the late 1970s and early 1980s following the early 1970s oil crisis, when rising petroleum costs led labels to use more recycled, thinner vinyl. This resulted in shallower grooves that stored less sonic information (especially bass) and made records more prone to damage, contributing to the shift towards CDs in the early 1980s. "Try to look for records made with virgin vinyl. That's the term. We don't want impurities because they can result in less than stellar sonic qualities."
**LP Definition and Structure** : An "LP," short for "long playing," is interchangeable with "album" and refers to 12-inch diameter vinyl records that store music in "microgrooves." The continuous spiral groove on a 12-inch LP is approximately 500 meters (1600 feet) long, requiring the stylus to travel about a kilometer to play both sides. An LP includes a "lead-in groove" on the outer circumference for stylus placement and a "dead wax" area—the empty space between the grooves and the center label—which may contain "runoff groove messages" from artists or catalog information. "The terms LP and album are interchangeable. They both refer to vinyl records, 12 inches in diameter, that store the music in what are called micro grooves."
**Weight and Quality** : Reissued vinyl records are often listed with their weight (e.g., 140g, 180g, 200g, 220g), indicating the amount of vinyl used in their production. While some debate its direct impact on audio quality, heavier vinyl (typically above 120-150g) is generally less prone to warping, which ensures the record sits flat on the turntable, allowing the stylus to trace the grooves more easily and reducing wear. "Well, it has to do with the weight of the record, reflective of how much actual vinyl was used to make that disk."
**Remastered Vinyl** : Remastered vinyl refers to recordings where producers and engineers have revisited the original master tapes and utilized the latest technologies to create a new, often enhanced, version of the recording. "It's also a remaster, which means the producers and engineers went back to the original master tapes and started creating this new recording from those raw materials using the latest technologies."
**The Term "Vinyl"** : It is specified that the word "vinyl" does not have a plural form; therefore, one should say "records" when referring to a collection, not "vinyls." "First of all, there is no plural of vinyl. You can say you collect records, but never ever say that you collect vinyl."
**Record Grading System** : A well-established system exists for grading the condition of used records:
    *   **Still Sealed**: The highest theoretical grade, indicating the record is in its original shrink wrap, has never been opened or played, and is in perfect condition (presuming authenticity, as some sellers re-shrink wrap records). "The theoretical top of the line grade is still sealed. That means you have a record that's still in the original shrink wrap."
    *   **Mint**: Denotes a record that has never been opened, never played, and is in perfect, pristine condition, typically after the shrink wrap has been removed to prevent potential warping. "a record like this is rated as mint. Never opened, never played, perfect condition."
    *   **Near Mint**: Describes a record that is almost entirely untouched, showing no signs of wear or abuse, and is pristine apart from not being in its original shrink wrap. "The next grade is near mint. It's almost totally untouched with no signs of wear or abuse."
    *   **Very Good Plus (VG+)**: Indicates a record that has been enjoyed and listened to but was carefully handled by its owner, showing very little visible wear. Such records are typically valued at approximately 50% less than a mint condition record. "This record was loved and listened to, however, the owner was careful with it, meaning that there's very little in the way of visible wear."
    *   **Very Good (VG)**: Applies to a record that has clearly been used but is still functional. Its value is generally around 25% of the same record in mint condition. "Again, this is a record that's obviously been used, but not to the point where it's useless."
    *   **Good, Good Plus, Fair, Poor**: These grades signify lower quality conditions where negotiation is advisable or walking away might be best, unless the record is an extremely rare item like a 78 or a test pressing that holds value despite poor playability. "And then we get into conditions like good, good plus, fair and poor."
**Rarity vs. Demand in Pricing** : The value of a used record is determined solely by demand. A record can be exceptionally rare, but if there's no interest, it holds no monetary worth. Conversely, strong market demand can make even non-pristine or unplayable rare items, such as specific 78s or test pressings, highly valuable.
