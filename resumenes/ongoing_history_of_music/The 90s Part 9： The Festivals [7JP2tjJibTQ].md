# genre - Alt-Rock
## curiosities
**1990s Dominance and Foundation** : The alt-rock nation of the 1990s was instrumental in kickstarting and building the North American festival scene, creating traditions and an industry that previously did not exist culturally. It provided the atmosphere without which festivals like Coachella would not have existed.
**Post-1990s Decline** : By the close of the 1990s, the genre, after 10 years of being the dominant sort of music and culture, was on the wane, with the idea of traveling alternative music festivals burning out. Many artists who pioneered the genre had "grown up, moved on or broken up." The music became fragmented with many one-hit wonders, and new artists often seemed "derivative, too safe, too watered down," leading to a phase described as "the Creed Syndrome."
**Shift in Audience Taste** : Generation X, which had fueled the alt-rock boom, had grown up and moved on. The incoming Generation Y was not interested in "brooding grunge or any of the kind of intense post-punk alt-rock," preferring pop-leaning tastes.
**Future Resurgence** : Despite bottoming out at the end of the 90s, alt-rock's next cycle was already being planted, with new indie artists like The Arctic Monkeys, Arcade Fire, The White Stripes, The Killers, and The Strokes emerging, many of whom bypassed the standard music industry model. The genre was expected to return "for another stretch in the sun" within a few years.

# venue - Coachella
## curiosities
**Origin (1999)** : Coachella, a "monster" festival, is a significant descendant of Lollapalooza and is considered the North American equivalent to Glastonbury. It would not exist without the alt-rock atmosphere of the 1990s. While often perceived as a 21st-century event, it actually began on October 9-10, 1999.
**Location and Schedule** : The festival is held over two weekends every April at the Empire Polo Grounds in Indio, California, attracting attendees from all over the world.
**Inaugural Lineup (1999)** : The first Coachella lineup in 1999 included Beck, Morrissey, Underworld, Rage Against the Machine, Pavement, and Modest Mouse.
**Initial Struggles (1999)** : The first event only drew 10,000 people, despite a strong lineup. Factors contributing to low attendance included the perceived unpleasantness of the drive from Los Angeles into the desert, the recent disaster of Woodstock '99, the lack of on-site camping, and intensely hot weather.
**Financial Losses and Debt** : The promoter lost close to a million dollars on the first Coachella, requiring them to ask bands like Tool and Rage Against the Machine to delay payment.
**Near Demise and Revival** : The inaugural Coachella was so difficult that it nearly "killed the entire concept." There was no event in 2000, but it successfully reappeared in 2001 and has been a continuously growing concern ever since.

# venue - Glastonbury
## curiosities
**Founding and History (1970)** : Glastonbury Festival was first staged in the fall of 1970 by Michael Evis, who was inspired to create the event after seeing Led Zeppelin play outdoors. Evis drew upon an earlier Glastonbury tradition, a classical music and arts event held between 1914 and 1926.
**Original Name and Location** : Michael Evis initially named his festival the Piltdown Festival, though this name only lasted for one year. The festival is held on Worthy Farm, which is a working dairy farm.
**Annual Greenfield Festival** : Known as the largest annual Greenfield Festival in the world, Glastonbury takes place for five days almost every June. It has been held annually since 1981, with "fallow years" occurring approximately every half-decade or so, the idea being to give the land and the people who live in the area a break from the hordes.
**Scale and Attendance** : Glastonbury attracts somewhere in the neighborhood of 175,000 people per event, who attend regardless of weather conditions. The festival is so popular that all tickets sell out in less than an hour, months before any acts are even announced.
**Cultural Significance** : The festival has become a rite of passage for generations of music fans, a place where legends are born, careers are made and occasionally sidelined there, based on a single performance.
**Notable Performers** : The list of acts who have played Glastonbury is extensive, including U2, Radiohead, Foo Fighters, David Bowie, The Cure, Coldplay, Paul McCartney, The Rolling Stones, Neil Young, Bruce Springsteen, and Muse.
**1990s Gold Standard** : By the 1990s, Glastonbury was considered the gold standard against which all other festivals were judged.

# venue - Lollapalooza
## curiosities
**Origin and Inspiration** : The idea for Lollapalooza originated from Perry Farrell of Jane's Addiction, who was inspired by the intense crowd reaction to the Pixies' set at Reading Festival on August 26, 1990. Farrell questioned why such a powerful musical gathering couldn't exist in America.
**Conception as Traveling Festival** : Unlike previous US festivals like Monterey, Woodstock, Altamont, Us Festivals, or Gathering of the Tribes, which were rooted in one place, Lollapalooza was designed as a traveling festival, moving from city to city over several months.
**Initial Purpose as Farewell Tour** : Lollapalooza was originally conceived as a farewell tour for Jane's Addiction, masquerading as a music festival to help it get off the ground.
**Name Origin** : The name "Lollapalooza" was chosen from old Three Stooges clips, derived from a late 19th-century slang word meaning an "extraordinary or unusual thing or event."
**Early Criticisms** : The festival faced significant criticism initially, with skeptics doubting its financial viability, predicting disaster for taking "a bunch of no name, so-called alternative bands" on a two-month road tour.
**First Show (1991)** : The very first Lollapalooza show began outside Phoenix on July 18, 1991. The initial box office performance was modest, making some money but not a lot.
**1991 Lineup** : The inaugural Lollapalooza lineup featured Jane's Addiction, Siouxsie and the Banshees, Living Colour, Nine Inch Nails, Butthole Surfers, Rollins Band, Ice-T & Body Count, and Fishbone.
**Timing of First Tour** : The first Lollapalooza tour wrapped up on August 28, 1991, coincidentally the day before Nirvana released "Smells Like Teen Spirit" and Pearl Jam released their debut album, marking it as "a few months too early" for the impending alt-rock explosion.
**1992 Resurgence** : The second Lollapalooza tour, starting on July 18, 1992, experienced a dramatic change in landscape, becoming a sensation with sold-out shows and immense excitement.
**1992 Main Stage Lineup** : The 1992 main stage featured Lush, Pearl Jam, The Jesus and Mary Chain, Soundgarden, Ice Cube (fresh from N.W.A.), Ministry, and closed with the Red Hot Chili Peppers, who were riding high from their *Blood Sugar Sex Magik* album.
**Introduction of Second Stage (1992)** : 1992 also marked the first year that a second stage was introduced on the tour, featuring bands like Stone Temple Pilots, Cypress Hill, Rage Against the Machine, and Tool at various stops.
**Glory Years (1992-1995)** : The period from 1992 through 1995 is considered Lollapalooza's "glory years," during which it profoundly represented the state of the alternative nation.
**Major Participants (1992-1995)** : During its peak, the festival featured The Smashing Pumpkins, Beastie Boys, Green Day, Sonic Youth, Beck, Tool, Nick Cave, The Verve, Patti Smith, and Moby.
**1996 Headliner Controversy** : The 1996 festival was "okay," but its headliner, Metallica, "kind of hijacked the whole thing" as their inclusion "didn't feel right" for the alternative vibe, despite a lineup that still included Soundgarden, The Ramones, Rancid, and Rage Against the Machine.
**Decline and Hiatus (1997-2003)** : Enthusiasm for the traveling festival waned by its last tour in 1997. An attempt to revive it in 2003 went largely unremembered.
**Permanent Home and Revival (2005)** : Lollapalooza reestablished itself as a major music festival when it found a permanent annual home in Chicago in 2005.

# venue - Monterey Pop
## curiosities
**First Modern Music Festival (1967)** : Monterey Pop, held in San Francisco in the summer of 1967, is considered the first modern music festival, recognizable by today's standards. It led to the creation of other major festivals like Woodstock, Glastonbury, and Roskilde.

# venue - Reading Festival
## curiosities
**Origin and Transition** : The Reading Festival, the older of the two twin festivals, originated as a jazz festival in the 1950s that ran until the 1970s before transitioning into a rock event.
**Embrace of Genres** : It became the first major festival to significantly embrace British punk, British new wave, and British metal.
**Shared Lineup with Leeds** : Along with Leeds Festival, Reading shares a lineup with gigs held on the same days every August. Acts perform at both locations, requiring them to make a 300-kilometer journey between the cities as schedules are juggled.
**Notable Performers** : Over the years, Reading has hosted major acts including Green Day, Oasis, Blur, Muse, The Cure, The Rolling Stones, Pink Floyd, The Who, Black Sabbath, Metallica, Guns N' Roses, and AC/DC.

# venue - Roskilde Festival
## curiosities
**Founding and History (1971)** : Roskilde Festival, held in a Danish town, was started by two high school students in 1971 and has been running annually ever since. It typically occurs right after Glastonbury Festival.
**Scale and Diversity** : In modern times, Roskilde can host up to 130,000 people who come to see 180 different acts, representing genres like rock, folk, electronic, and hip hop.
**The Naked Run** : A unique tradition at Roskilde is the "Naked Run," a race around the campsite where participants wear no clothes. The top male and female runners receive free tickets to the following year's festival, a tradition so popular that qualifier events are now held.
**Notable Performers** : Roskilde's lineups rival Glastonbury's, featuring artists such as U2, Bob Marley, Nirvana, Bob Dylan, Sex Pistols, Radiohead, Red Hot Chili Peppers, Black Sabbath, Prince, and Rage Against the Machine.
**2000 Tragedy and Safety Protocols** : The festival's worst year was 2000, when nine people died and 26 were injured during a Pearl Jam set, in front of approximately 60,000 attendees at the Orange Stage. This tragic accident led to the implementation of new safety protocols at festivals worldwide.

# venue - Warped Tour
## curiosities
**Inception and Concept (1995)** : The Warped Tour began in 1995, taking inspiration from Lollapalooza's traveling festival concept. It crisscrossed North America, occasionally veering into Canada, with a "super efficient self-contained operation" that offered "maximum music for a low, low price."
**Scale and Genre Diversity** : Each stop of the Warped Tour featured dozens of bands. Initially focused on punk, it eventually branched out to include other genres like hip hop.
**Founder** : Kevin Lyman, a California-based event producer, created the Warped Tour.
**Extensive Lineup History** : The list of artists who participated over the years is staggering and so extensive that even the organizers reportedly do not have a clear record of every performer.
**Longevity** : The Warped Tour eventually became the longest-running North American traveling rock show.

# venue - Woodstock '94
## curiosities
**Location and Anniversary** : Woodstock '94 was held in Saugerties, New York, on the 25th anniversary of the original 1969 event, at a site resembling Max Yasgur's farm. Notably, none of the Woodstock festivals have ever actually taken place in Woodstock, New York.
**Attendance and Conditions** : The festival attracted approximately 550,000 people. It was plagued by "terrible organization," overpriced food, and adverse weather conditions including rain, mud, and thunderstorms.
**Career Boost for Alt Acts** : Despite its organizational issues, Woodstock '94 received significant media coverage and provided substantial career boosts for many alternative acts who performed.
**Notable Performers** : Performers included Nine Inch Nails (who performed covered in mud), Red Hot Chili Peppers (with new guitarist Dave Navarro), Violent Femmes, Collective Soul, James, Blind Melon, and The Cranberries. Green Day also performed, on a Sunday, after their album *Dookie* had become a huge hit.
**Mike Dirnt Incident** : During Green Day's set, fans, "having had enough of being abused," began tearing up and throwing turf. In the resulting confusion, Green Day bassist Mike Dirnt was mistakenly tackled by a security guard, smashing his face into a stage monitor, breaking several teeth, and requiring emergency dental surgery.

# venue - Woodstock '99
## curiosities
**Location (1999)** : Woodstock '99 was held five years after Woodstock '94, at an Air Force Base in Rome, New York.
**Disastrous Outcome** : The event was marred by widespread "vandalism, sexual assault, fires, [and] proper damage." It is unfavorably compared to the Altamont Free Concert of the 1960s, described as "Woodstock '99 was to the 90s, where Altamont was to the 1960s, and that is not a good comparison."

# curiosity
**Ancient Music Festivals** : Music festivals are not a modern creation, with people gathering in fields to hear music for centuries. Some original festivals are still active today.
**Global Long-Time Festivals** : Besides the Italian Fiera de la Frequette No La, other long-running festivals exist in Germany, England, India, and Latvia.
**1990s Festival Boom** : The 1990s was the decade where the music festival truly came into its own, with a series of regular events appearing year after year in the same spot, and others moving from place to place. This period marked North America's embrace of the summer festival, a tradition largely driven by the 90s alt-rock nation.
**North American Festival Tradition Lag** : Europeans were accustomed to attending festivals in fields, enduring mud, rain, and heat. In contrast, North Americans were late to adopt this culture, lacking a strong tradition of attending outdoor lifestyle festivals until the 1990s.
**1990s North American Festival Tradition Shift** : While North America had festivals before the 1990s, it lacked a cultural tradition of attending them consistently each summer. This began to change very early in the decade, largely spearheaded by the alt-rock nation, which built the modern festival industry and traditions. The growth of North American festival culture drew heavily on the expertise and experience gained from established European events, particularly from the English.
**1990s Music Industry Boom** : The 1990s saw significant financial prosperity in the music industry, with music sales consistently increasing each year due to what was termed the "Golden Age of Compact Discs." Generation X consumers were heavily invested in buying music and attending shows. The success of Lollapalooza demonstrated the viability of traveling alt-rock festivals, inspiring others to adopt the concept.
**Impact of Princess Diana's Death (1997)** : The death of Princess Diana on August 31, 1997, was described as having "really took the wind out of everybody's sales" in the music scene, contributing to the waning enthusiasm of the late 90s.
**Generation X Shift** : The Generation X demographic, instrumental in bringing alt-rock to prominence, had grown up and moved on by the end of the 1990s, contributing to the genre's decline.
**Generation Y's Musical Tastes** : The new generation entering musical awareness, Generation Y, preferred pop-leaning tastes and was not interested in the "brooding grunge or any of the kind of intense post-punk alt-rock" favored by their predecessors.
**Economic and Cultural Climate Shift (Late 1990s)** : The grimness of the early 1990s had dissipated by the end of the decade, replaced by an improved economy, a major bull market under Democrat Bill Clinton, and the end of the Cold War and first Gulf War, contributing to a more cheerful cultural mood that favored pop music.
**Internet's Impact on Music Industry (1999)** : The internet began to worry the music industry, particularly after June 1999, with the emergence of Napster and the MP3 format. This allowed people to "go online and download whatever song I want from someone I don't know," for free, without paying for CDs, leading to a massive spike in peer-to-peer file sharing by the end of 1999, fundamentally changing the record industry.
**Future Music Distribution Changes** : In the years following 1999, platforms like YouTube would "kill off the traditional music video channel," and iTunes and iPods would further transform music consumption.
**New Alt-Rock Bands (Early 2000s)** : As alt-rock bottomed out in the late 90s, a new crop of bands, often indie artists, emerged who had "no use for the standard music industry model." These included The Arctic Monkeys, Arcade Fire, The White Stripes, The Killers, and The Strokes, who would lead alt-rock back "for another stretch in the sun" within a few years.
