# curiosity

**Library Concept Origin** : The idea of taking all the world's knowledge and storing it in one place originated many centuries ago, leading to the concept of the library.
**Recorded Music Libraries** : Governments, public broadcasters, and companies began collecting recorded audio, applying the library concept to music.
**BBC Physical Media Archive** : Famously holds hundreds of kilometers of shelving for physical media.
**Zero Freitas' Private Record Collection** : A collector in Brazil on a quest to amass all records ever made, possessing at least 8 million records and over 100,000 compact discs.
**Celestial Jukebox Concept (1994)** : Popularized by law professor Paul Goldstein; envisioned as a giant networked computer database holding humanity's music in digital form, available instantly to anyone with an Internet connection. This concept, once considered science fiction, is now a theoretical reality with modern streaming.
**Napster Launch (1999)** : Went online on June 1, 1999, exactly 32 years to the day after _Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band_ was released; made it seem possible to download any song (illegally); allowed users to acquire music "a la carte" when the industry was phasing out singles.
**iTunes Music Store Launch (2003)** : Started with several hundred thousand songs and has since expanded to about 60 million tracks, all available for sale; facilitated the return of the single by selling individual tracks for 99 cents, which dealt a hard blow to the concept of the album.
**Modern Digital Music Libraries (Streaming)** : Streaming platforms draw from digital music libraries containing at least 90 million songs, with new additions daily, accessible anytime, anywhere, on any device, often for a negligible or free price.
**Streaming Payout System (Overall Percentage Share)** : Spotify and other platforms pay artists based on their overall percentage share of total streams, meaning if an artist gets X percent of streams in a month, they receive X percent of the money. This system favors already established and popular artists.
**Low Artist Earnings from Streaming (Spotify Data)** : Spotify's own figures indicate that 97% of all artists on the platform make less than $1,000 USD per year.
**Daniel Ek's 2020 Statement on Artist Viability** : Spotify CEO Daniel Ek stated in 2020 that "Some artists that used to do well in the past may not do well in this future landscape, where you can't record music once every three to four years and think that that's going to be enough."
**Proposed "Penny Per Stream" Payment** : Calls exist for artists to be paid a penny per stream, though this would still not fully compensate for lost physical sales.
**Minimum Stream Duration for Payout (30 Seconds)** : A universal rule across all streaming music services mandates that a song must be listened to for at least 30 seconds to be considered an "official play" and for the artist to receive any payment.
**Metadata as Essential Song Identification** : Data embedded within digital song files (song title, artist, album title, composer, genre, year of release, length, track number, artwork, lyrics, bit rate, sampling rate) is crucial for identifying specific songs and ensuring accurate payouts.
**Lack of Centralized Metadata Database** : There is no single worldwide database containing complete metadata for every song due to the immense volume of music.
**International Standard Recording Code (ISRC)** : Efforts since 1986 to assign a unique number to each audio recording. Its complexity arises from requiring a new number for each different release (e.g., Canadian vs. US release), different formats (album track vs. single), remixes, live recordings, and covers.
**Metadata Challenges – Spelling & Punctuation** : Issues such as the use of "AND" versus an ampersand, apostrophe placement, changing exclamation mark positions, and acronyms (e.g., R80P vs. OLP), along with stylized spellings in hip hop, create significant metadata problems.
**Orphan Songs Phenomenon** : Hundreds of thousands of songs are "orphan songs" because they lack proper metadata, preventing them from earning money. Revenue for these songs often sits in accounts while performing rights collectives attempt to identify the rightful authors.
**CD Player Skip Button (1982-1983)** : The skip button first became widely available with the advent of CD players in late 1982 and early 1983, allowing listeners to easily skip tracks due to the disc's construction and player circuitry. This was the "first dent in the wall for the integrity of the album."
**Attention Spans Shortened by Skipping** : The ease of skipping tracks (from CDs, then MP3s) has progressively shortened listener attention spans. Data shows 24% of streaming listeners skip an unfamiliar song in the first 5 seconds, 29% in 10 seconds, and 35% are gone by the 30-second mark.
**Impact of Skipping on Song Recommendations** : Streaming music algorithms track song skips, causing frequently skipped songs to slide down recommendations and diminish their chances of appearing on crucial playlists.
**Changed Nature of Songwriting (30-Second Rule)** : The 30-second payout rule and short attention spans have altered songwriting, leading to shorter intros, choruses placed upfront, and the addition of many "sugar high hooks" within the first half-minute to retain listeners.
**Decline of the Album Concept (General)** : Streaming continues the assault on the album concept. The internet generation often prioritizes individual songs over albums, with playlists largely replacing albums in the popular music ecosystem. "Deep album cuts" often get lost, questioning the point of recording them.
**Long-Playing Album Introduction (June 1948)** : The LP record first appeared in June 1948, initially too expensive for disposable pop, so it was used for classical, Broadway shows, and jazz.
**Single-Driven Pop Music (Pre-1965)** : Before approximately 1965, popular music (R&B, country, blues, rock and roll) was primarily released and consumed as single songs.
**Shift to Album as Artistic Statement (circa 1965)** : Around 1965, artists like The Beatles, Bob Dylan, and The Beach Boys began creating music as artistic statements with multiple tracks, fostering the album concept.
**FM Radio's Role in Album Popularity (1960s)** : In the 1960s, FM stations, initially "dumping grounds" with low ratings, began playing album tracks instead of just Top 40 singles, which proved popular and made the recorded music industry realize the higher profit margins of albums.
**Album as Industry Currency (Post-1967)** : Following _Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band_ (1967), the album became the primary currency of the recorded music business, with singles serving as enticements to buy the full album.
**Recorded Music Business Value (Early 2021)** : The entire global recorded music business was valued at approximately $21 billion at the beginning of 2021.
**Spotify Market Capitalization (Early 2021)** : Spotify alone had a market cap of $248 billion, roughly 10 times the value of the entire recorded music industry it serves.
**Apple Music's Corporate Backing (Early 2021)** : Apple Music is supported by Apple, a company valued at $2.5 trillion.
**Amazon Music's Corporate Backing** : Amazon Music is a division of Amazon.
**YouTube Music's Corporate Backing** : YouTube Music is owned by Google (Alphabet).
**Streaming Industry Consolidation** : Expected to lead to existing large companies (Spotify, Apple, Amazon, Google) becoming even bigger as CD sales, digital album sales, and digital track purchases continue to decline.
**Vinyl Sales as a Niche Market** : Despite being a bright spot, vinyl sales are considered a niche within the grand scheme of music consumption.
**Pre-Internet vs. Post-Internet Artist Careers (2000 Divide)** : The year 2000 serves as a division. Pre-internet artists benefited from peak industry revenues, busy record stores, and powerful promotion from MTV and MuchMusic, allowing for slow career building and long-term label investment. Post-internet artists struggle to make a name due to oversaturation, competition, and consumer choice.
**Post-Internet Listener Relationship with Music (Song-Focused)** : Many music fans post-2000 are primarily interested in a specific song, not necessarily the artist, often unable to name the artist behind a viral track on platforms like Spotify, Instagram, or TikTok. This makes it difficult for new artists to build sustained careers and leads to more one-hit wonders.
**Data Analytics for Artists (Streaming Era)** : Artists must adapt to the streaming world by utilizing data analytics provided by platforms or specialized companies. This data helps identify trends, optimize touring, manage merchandise sales, and determine the best times to release new music.
**Data-Driven Music Culture** : The immense data from streaming platforms dictates the course of music culture by identifying real-time trends not just by genre, but by length, key, and chord progressions. Music doesn't necessarily have to be "good" if data supports engagement.
**Formulaic Music Creation** : Data analytics encourage committees of musicians, beatmakers, producers, and lyricists to create formulaic songs specifically designed to maximize engagement and revenues, leading to music that increasingly sounds similar, akin to formulaic superhero movies.
**Repetition in Youth Streaming Habits** : The heaviest users of streaming services tend to be young and frequently listen to their favorite songs repeatedly. This documented repetition is analyzed to inform the creation of new music designed to encourage similar behavior, boosting songs up charts and algorithms.
**Genre Prioritization in Promotion (Data-Driven)** : Hip hop, rap, R&B, and pop benefit most from this data-driven "musical moneyball" approach due to their demonstrated streaming success, receiving the majority of label time and money for promotion. Other genres like rock, country, and jazz receive less.
**YouTube as Premier Music Discovery Platform** : YouTube remains the largest source of music discovery globally; artists who are not on YouTube are effectively non-existent for a significant portion of the population.
