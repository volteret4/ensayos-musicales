# curiosity
**Copyright Infringement Lawsuit Craze (2000s-present)** : The 21st century has seen an explosion in copyright infringement cases within the music industry, with accusations often revolving around tracks allegedly copying substantial elements from previously released music. While some similarities can be unintentional due to the finite nature of musical notes and melodies, the 2015 "Blurred Lines" ruling, which established that a "feel" or "vibe" could be copyrighted, opened the floodgates for "ambulance chasing lawyers" seeking to sue for even minor resemblances to older songs. "In the 2000s, the number of such accusations has exploded."
**Non-Copyrightable Musical Elements** : In the realm of music composition, certain fundamental elements cannot be copyrighted. This includes song titles, core chord progressions (as they are considered building blocks of music), and beats and rhythms, though attempts to copyright the latter continue. "Song titles cannot be copyrighted. Core progressions cannot be copyrighted because they form the building blocks of music composition. And beats and rhythms can't be copyrighted for the same reason, although some people continue to try."
**"Feel" or "Vibe" Copyright Precedent (2015)** : A landmark ruling in 2015 established a controversial precedent in copyright law, affirming that the "feel" or "vibe" of a song can be subject to copyright protection. This decision stemmed from the Marvin Gaye estate's successful lawsuit against Robin Thicke and Pharrell Williams over "Blurred Lines," leading to a $5.3 million award for the estate, despite a lack of direct note-for-note or melodic passage copying. "Well, it turns out that you can, and the jury awarded the estate of Marvin Gay $5.3 million."
**Post-"Blurred Lines" Lawsuit Surge (2015-present)** : Following the 2015 "Blurred Lines" settlement, a significant increase in copyright infringement lawsuits has been observed, with lawyers actively seeking out contemporary songs exhibiting even minor similarities to older tracks (20-50 years prior) to pursue settlements or court battles. This trend has created an atmosphere of concern for songwriters, uncertain if their influences could lead to legal action. "Since that case was settled in 2015, ambulance chasing lawyers have come out of the woodwork, looking for examples where contemporary song sounds like it has some similarities, even tiny ones to a song released 20, 30, 40, 50 years ago."
**Album Decline and Playlist Rise (21st Century)** : The music industry has seen a significant shift from album-focused listening to playlist-based consumption in the 21st century. Listeners increasingly prefer curated playlists of individual singles from various artists over cohesive, deliberately sequenced albums, impacting artist strategies and potentially challenging the concept album format. "The decline of the importance of the album and the rise of the playlist."
**Playlist's Influence on Music Creation and Consumption** : Playlists have revolutionized music consumption by offering a modern equivalent to mixtapes, prompting artists to create music specifically designed to gain placement on popular playlists, often releasing multiple singles before an album's full release. This shift, driven by a collective preference for immediate gratification, disrupts traditional album sequencing but also offers lesser-known artists a better chance to break through algorithms with a single hit. "Instead of listening to a whole album featuring a collection of carefully crafted songs sequenced in a deliberate fashion, people are showing a distinct preference for big singles and popular songs from multiple artists on a single playlist."
