# curiosity

**Early Audio Recording Limitations** : The earliest audio recording technologies, such as the Edison cylinder, severely limited the duration of musical pieces to about two minutes. The subsequent development of Emil Berliner's flat, rotating disc spinning at 78 RPM slightly increased capacity, allowing for approximately three minutes of music per song. "An original Edison cylinder could hold maybe two minutes worth of music, therefore any songs committed to the format had to be two minutes or shorter, otherwise you'd run into space."
**Standardization of Song Length** : The physical limitations of early recording technologies, particularly the 78 RPM flat disc allowing roughly three minutes per side, played a crucial role in standardizing the length of popular music songs to around three minutes, a convention that persists even today. "And this, more than anything else, standardized the length of songs in popular music to around three minutes, something that persists even today."
**The Rise of Radio Edits** : With the emergence of albums in the 1960s, songs grew longer than the traditional three-minute radio format. To accommodate these longer album tracks on AM radio, which preferred shorter songs to fit more per hour, "radio edits" were created, often involving significant "butchery" to shorten the song to the desired three-minute length. "The only way to get a great, but long song on an album onto AM radio, which dominated at the time, you had to make that long song shorter. And this gave birth to the first radio edits."
**Definition of Remix** : A remix is an artistic and technological development where a once-finished single is deconstructed into its constituent parts and then rebuilt or altered into something entirely different. It allows for adding, subtracting, altering, and manipulating elements to create a new version while retaining the original song's "DNA." "This gave birth to the remix, an artistic and technological development that took what was once a finished single and turned it into something entirely different."
**Re-orchestration as a Synonym for Remixing** : The term "re-archestration" is presented as another way to describe the process of remixing a song, emphasizing the artistic re-arrangement of its musical components. "In fact, another term for remixing a song is re-archestration."
**Censorship in Radio Edits** : Radio edits often involve "surgery" to carefully remove or obscure "naughty words" or phrases in songs to prevent offense and "destroy the innocence of children," reflecting broadcasting bodies' sensitivity to explicit content. "Broadcasting bodies around the world are very sensitive about songs that contain naughty words that might cause offense and destroy the innocence of children."
**Early 1960s AM Radio Remixing** : The first remixes in the 1960s were specifically tailored for AM radio listeners, whose signals had poor sonic properties, lacking deep bass and crisp highs, resulting in "thin and tinny" music. Record companies would remix singles in the studio, often listening through "junky 5-inch full-range speakers" resembling car dashboards, to ensure the song sounded good on AM radio. "The first remixes were done in the 1960s and they were done with a very specific audience in mind. The AM radio listener."
**Sound Quality of 7-inch Singles** : Many 7-inch releases from the 1960s and 1970s often sound "unnatural and often unbearably bright" when played on a proper stereo. This is a direct consequence of them being mixed to sound good on cheap AM car radios, rather than high-fidelity systems. "That's why a lot of 7-inch releases from the 60s and 70s sound so unnatural and often unbearably bright when you play them on a proper stereo."
**Challenges of 7-inch Vinyl Production for Long Songs** : Fitting long songs (e.g., 7 minutes 56 seconds) onto a 7-inch single required cutting grooves incredibly close together and narrower. This process sacrifices bass storage and exacerbates audio quality degradation as the stylus moves closer to the record's center due to shorter groove paths, though this was often unnoticed on cheap turntables and AM radio. "Step two involved cutting the grooves on this 7-inch record incredibly close together. The grooves had to be made narrower and by doing that, you sacrifice how much bass can be stored in those grooves."
**Jamaican Sound Systems as the Origin of Modern Remixes** : The first "modern remixes" of records emerged in Jamaica in the late 1960s and early 1970s, stemming from the vibrant sound system culture. Operators would request instrumental versions of songs, often with boosted bass or added reverb, from local studios, which were known as "versions." "The first modern remixes of records were found in Jamaica in the late 1960s and early 70s."
**Headley Jones' Amplifier Innovation (1950)** : In 1950, Jamaican inventor Headley Jones built a pioneering amplifier equipped with a three-band equalizer, allowing precise manipulation of low, mid-range, and high frequencies. This invention was crucial for the development of the Jamaican sound system. "Around 1950, a Jamaican inventor named Headley Jones built a new kind of amplifier. It had a three band equalizer, which allowed the operator to emphasize or take away low, mid-rage, and high frequencies."
**Birth of the Jamaican Sound System (1952)** : In 1952, Tom, the Great Sebastian, one of Headley Jones' first amplifier customers, wired a turntable and speakers to the new amp, creating the first Jamaican sound system. These portable DJ rigs became stars, fostering intense competition among operators like Sebastian and Arthur Duke Reed. "He wired up a turn table and speakers to the amp, and voila, the Jamaican sound system was born. This was 1952. And Sebastian became a star."
**Role of Toasters in Sound System Culture** : "Toasters" were integral to Jamaican sound systems, performing "stream of consciousness wraps and rhymes" over the instrumental "versions" of songs. They would exhort dancers to move to the music, with the best toasters contributing to the star status of sound system operators. "The toasters were the people who did stream of consciousness wraps and rhymes over an instrumental bit of the record."
**Real-time Remixing in Jamaican Sound Systems** : Sound system operators would remix songs in real time using two turntables and a microphone, offering unique and surprising experiences to the crowd. This spontaneous creativity meant that some songs were remixed so extensively they became entirely new creations, often titled and claimed by the sound system owner. "Using two turn tables in a microphone, songs were remixed in real time, offering surprise and delight to the crowd."
**Connection Between Jamaican Sound Systems and Hip Hop** : The practices of Jamaican sound system operators, particularly their on-the-fly remixing and instrumental "versions," prefigured the emergence of hip hop in the Bronx in the late 1970s, where DJs similarly created new forms of music and culture through "hack and slice and scratch and wheel and come again" techniques. "The same thing would happen in the late 70s in the Bronx with hip hop. The hack and slice and scratch and wheel and come again with hip hop DJs created not just only a new form of music, but an entirely new culture."
**Disco DJs and the Problem of Short Songs** : In the disco era, DJs aimed to maintain a continuous groove on the dance floor, but commercially released songs were often too short to sustain the desired energy. An early solution was to use two copies of the same song on separate turntables, seamlessly mixing between them to extend the track's duration. "People were heading to clubs in record numbers to dance, and the goal of the DJ was to keep the groove and to keep the party going. The problem was that as soon as you got into the groove of a great song, it was over."
**Tom Molten's Innovation of the Extended Dance Remix** : In the early 1970s, inspired by a DJ's struggle to maintain dance floor momentum, Tom Molten created the first extended dance remix. He meticulously spliced together a long, unbroken version of a song on a Reebok's Real to Real tape machine, adding multiple bars to create five and a half minutes of continuous dance beats. "Tom went home and carefully spliced together a long, unbroken version of a song on our Reebok's Real to Real tape machine, adding eight bars here, and there's 16 bars there."
**The Birth of the 12-inch Single (1974)** : In 1974, Tom Molten inadvertently created the 12-inch single format when his studio ran out of 7-inch blanks for a new mix. He asked an engineer to press it onto a 12-inch blank, with the instruction to play it at 45 RPM instead of the usual 33 RPM, leading to a new standard for dance music. "But sometime in 1974, Tom Molten had a problem. He had a hot new mix that he wanted to debut on the weekend, but the studio he used had run out of seven-inch blanks. So what was he going to do? He asked the engineer, well, could we press this onto a 12-inch blank? And the engineer said, sure, why not? Just remember to play it at 45 instead of the usual 33. And just like that, the 12-inch single was born."
**Advantages of the 12-inch Single** : DJs quickly adopted the 12-inch format because its larger size made it easier to handle, its heavier weight made it more stable, and its wider-spaced grooves allowed for more music, especially bass. The record's extended lip also facilitated new DJ techniques like slip-cuing and scratching. "DJs love the new 12-inch format because they were bigger, they were easier to handle. Because they were heavier, you didn't have to be as careful moving them around. Because the grooves were wider apart, they could store more music, especially bass."
**Beat Mixing as a Remix Technique** : A common remix strategy, especially for 12-inch singles, involved creating a long, beat-heavy intro. This "beat mixing" technique allowed DJs to seamlessly match the tempo with the preceding song, maintaining "floor integrity" and creating an unbroken musical flow where one song transitioned imperceptibly into the next. "One of the most popular ways to remix a song was to create a long beat-heavy intro. This made it easy to match the beats with the previous song."
**Use of Stems in Remixing** : Access to "stems"—the individual musical elements (like drums, bass, vocals, etc.) that layer together to form a song—is crucial for detailed remixing. Stems can be sourced from original multi-track recordings or digitally recreated, allowing remixers to "surgically excis[e] bits of the song and making copies" to extend its length or manipulate parts, akin to adding "breadcrumbs to some hamburger to make the meat go further." "It also helps if you have access to stems of a song. These are the individual musical elements that layered together make up the song."
**The Golden Age of Remixes (1980s)** : The 1980s are described as the "golden age of remixes" due to the widespread adoption of remixing by artists and labels, facilitated by new technologies and a recognition of the format's advantages in reaching new audiences and driving sales. "So many, in fact, that in the 80s, we had the golden age of remixes."
**Survival of the 12-inch Extended Remix Post-Disco** : Despite disco's decline in the early 1980s, the 12-inch extended remix format not only survived but thrived. Record labels and artists recognized its value for reaching dance club audiences and generating multiple sales. "Even though disco had imploded by the beginning of the 80s, the 12 inch extended remix survived."
**Advantages of Dance Remixes for Labels and Artists** : Labels and artists embraced dance remixes for several reasons: they expanded a song's reach to new audiences beyond radio and record stores (e.g., dance clubs); different mixes could appeal to diverse club crowds (e.g., rock remixes); and crucially, issuing multiple remixes encouraged consumers to buy the same song multiple times, increasing profits. "Record labels and artists had a welcome to the advantages of dance remixes, and here's why. Number one, it increased the chance of a song reaching a whole new audience beyond radio and record stores."
**80s Remix Technology: Fairlight and Sinclair** : The 1980s saw advancements in remixing technology with the introduction of sophisticated keyboard devices like the Fairlight and the Sinclair. These instruments enabled operators to digitally store, surgically excise, manipulate, and implant musical material, giving artists unprecedented control over remix creation. "It began with two sophisticated keyboard devices, the Fairlight and the Sinclair. The new keyboards allowed operators to surgically excise a piece of musical material stored digitally, manipulated, and then implanted wherever they chose."
**From Finished Single to Starting Point** : With new technologies and creative input, many bands in the 1980s, including Frankie Goes to Hollywood, New Order, and Pet Shop Boys, began to view the original version of a song not as a finished product, but as a "starting point" for unlimited remix possibilities. "And they, along with dozens of other bands, began to realize that the original version of a song was only a starting point."
**Evolution of Remixing from Unauthorized to Legitimate** : Initially, many DJ-created remixes were "totally unauthorized and, therefore, illegal." However, as the concept gained traction, record labels began to legitimize the practice by enlisting remix experts, including club DJs, record producers, and recording studio engineers, and sometimes the artists themselves, to create official releases. "In the beginning, most of the remixes they did were totally unauthorized and, therefore, illegal. But as the concept caught on, record labels began enlisting these remix experts to create legitimate releases."
**Remixing Process: Access and Creative Freedom** : Remix specialists were given varying degrees of access to master tapes and creative freedom. Some received specific instructions and limited access, while others were given "carte blanche" with master tapes and permission to add and record new parts as they deemed fit, with the primary criterion being a "new and fresh look" at the song. "Sometimes they were given specific instructions and limited access to the master tapes. Other times they had cart wash, complete with the master tapes, and permission to add and record new parts for the song as they saw fit."
**Mark Price (Flood)'s Career Progression** : Mark Price, known as Flood, started in the music industry as a "T-boy," making tea for studio staff. He advanced to an engineer role at Mute Records, working with bands like Depeche Mode, and his esteemed work eventually led Brian Eno to recruit him for U2's "The Joshua Tree." Between album projects, Flood became a sought-after remixer, known for his ability to give singles a "new and fresh look." "One of the best was a guy named Flood. His real name is Mark Price and these days his schedule is so full that people book a service as a year more in advance. Not bad for a guy that started out of the music industry as a T-boy."
**Decline of Rock-Based Remixes During Grunge Era** : With the advent of grunge, the trend of rock bands creating dance-oriented remixes largely receded. While industrial and electronic groups continued to utilize remixes, the practice became less prevalent in the broader rock scene compared to its popularity in the 1980s. "In Grunge came along, the whole idea of rock-based remixes seemed to recede into the background for a bit."
**Modern Digital Remixing Accessibility** : Today, remixing is highly accessible, requiring only a laptop, some software, and potentially downloadable "stems" from the internet. The digital format has led to an explosion of online remixes, with few being pressed to vinyl, offering an immense choice to listeners. "Today, almost anyone can remix a song. All you need is a laptop, a little software, and maybe a couple of stems that you downloaded from the internet."
**Napster Era's Influence on Remixes** : Around the year 2000, during Napster's peak, users actively downloaded a vast array of unique mixes, often seeking "weirder and crazier" versions created by DJs for their nightclubs and shared online, contributing to thousands of one-of-a-kind song versions. "I remember talking to a guy around 2000 when Napster was at its peak. What kind of stuff are you downloading, I asked? I asked him, what kind of mixes he said, and the weirder and the crazier the better."
**Decline of Alt-Rock Remixes and Rise of Mashups** : The alt-rock world has seen a decrease in remixes, a trend attributed to the decline of alt-rock dance clubs after their peak in the late 1990s, suggesting a direct correlation between demand and product. However, mashups have emerged as a prominent form of remixing, characterized by the unexpected mixing of two songs that seemingly "have no business being mixed together," with the most surprising juxtapositions often being the most effective. "I don't think we see as many remixes in the alt-rock world as we used to. And I assume that's because after peaking in the late 90s, the number of alt-rock dance clubs went into decline."
**Streaming Platforms as Remix Hubs** : Current streaming music platforms offer a vast array of remixes and mashups, making them readily accessible to listeners who search for these categories. "What's available now? Just log on to streaming music platforms and enter remixes in mashups. And you'll be there for quite some time."

# instrument - Turntable

## curiosities
**Integral to Jamaican Sound Systems (1952)** : In 1952, Tom, the Great Sebastian, utilized a turntable along with speakers and an amplifier created by Headley Jones to form the foundational Jamaican sound system. This setup enabled DJs to play records and for "toasters" to perform over instrumental versions. "He wired up a turn table and speakers to the amp, and voila, the Jamaican sound system was born."
**Facilitator of Real-time Remixing** : In Jamaican sound systems and later in hip hop, DJs used two turntables and a microphone to perform real-time remixes, creating unique on-the-fly mixes that delighted crowds and allowed for seamless transitions. "Using two turn tables in a microphone, songs were remixed in real time, offering surprise and delight to the crowd."
**Enabler of New DJ Techniques with 12-inch Singles** : The extended lip of 12-inch records, which reached all the way around the circumference of the turntable platter, facilitated the invention of new DJ moves such as slip-cuing and eventually scratching. "And because the lip of the record extended all the way around the circumference of the turntable platter, new moves were invented like slip-cuing and eventually scratching."

**Tom Molten's Use for First Extended Remix** : In the early 1970s, Tom Molten meticulously spliced together a long, unbroken version of a song on a Reebok's Real to Real tape machine to create what is potentially the first-ever extended remix for dance floors. He achieved this by adding eight or sixteen bars to the track. "Tom went home and carefully spliced together a long, unbroken version of a song on our Reebok's Real to Real tape machine, adding eight bars here, and there's 16 bars there."

# instrument - 7-inch single

## curiosities
**Remixing for AM Radio Sound** : During the 1960s and 1970s, many 7-inch singles were specifically remixed in studios to optimize their sound for AM radio, which lacked good sonic properties. Engineers would mix using "junky 5-inch full-range speakers" similar to car speakers, resulting in recordings that often sounded "unnatural and often unbearably bright" on proper stereos. "That's why a lot of 7-inch releases from the 60s and 70s sound so unnatural and often unbearably bright when you play them on a proper stereo."
**Challenges with Long Songs** : To fit lengthy songs, like Meat Loaf's 7-minute 56-second "Paradise by the Dashboard Light," onto a 7-inch single, record grooves had to be cut incredibly close together and made narrower. This process compromised bass storage and audio quality, particularly towards the center of the record, though this was generally unnoticed on the cheap turntables and AM radios for which they were intended. "Step two involved cutting the grooves on this 7-inch record incredibly close together. The grooves had to be made narrower and by doing that, you sacrifice how much bass can be stored in those grooves."
**Initial Format for Dance Mixes** : Initially, all dance mixes, following Tom Molten's innovations, were pressed as 7-inch singles before the 12-inch format was conceived due to a shortage of 7-inch blanks. "At first, all these dance mixes were pressed as seven-inch singles."

# instrument - 12-inch single

## curiosities
**Accidental Birth in 1974** : The 12-inch single was born in 1974 when remixer Tom Molten, facing a shortage of 7-inch blanks for a new mix, asked a studio engineer to press his track onto a 12-inch blank instead. The engineer instructed it be played at 45 RPM, establishing the new format. "And just like that, the 12-inch single was born."
**DJs' Preferred Format** : DJs quickly favored the 12-inch format for its ease of handling due to its size, its stability from its heavier weight, and its capacity to store more music, especially bass, thanks to wider groove spacing. Its extended lip also enabled new DJ techniques like slip-cuing and scratching. "DJs love the new 12-inch format because they were bigger, they were easier to handle. Because they were heavier, you didn't have to be as careful moving them around. Because the grooves were wider apart, they could store more music, especially bass."
**Survival Post-Disco** : Even after the disco genre "imploded" in the early 1980s, the 12-inch extended remix survived as a format. Record labels and artists continued to utilize it, recognizing its advantages in reaching dance club audiences and promoting multiple sales of a single. "Even though disco had imploded by the beginning of the 80s, the 12 inch extended remix survived."

# instrument - Stems

## curiosities
**Definition and Use in Remixing** : "Stems" are defined as the individual musical elements (e.g., drums, vocals, bass) that layer together to create a full song. Access to these stems is crucial for remixing, allowing producers to "surgically excis[e] bits of the song and making copies" to extend its length or manipulate parts, akin to adding "breadcrumbs to some hamburger to make the meat go further." "It also helps if you have access to stems of a song. These are the individual musical elements that layered together make up the song."
**Origin of Stems** : Stems can be obtained from the original multi-track recording of a song or, in later developments, digitally recreated. "You can either get stems from the original multi-track recording or you can digitally recreate them for yourself, but that came later."

# instrument - Fairlight

## curiosities
**Pioneering Digital Keyboard for Remixing (1980s)** : The Fairlight was one of two sophisticated keyboard devices that emerged in the 1980s, revolutionizing remixing. It allowed operators to digitally store, surgically excise, manipulate, and implant musical material, offering unprecedented control for remix artists like Frankie Goes to Hollywood and New Order. "It began with two sophisticated keyboard devices, the Fairlight and the Sinclair. The new keyboards allowed operators to surgically excise a piece of musical material stored digitally, manipulated, and then implanted wherever they chose."

# concert - Jamaican sound system parties

## curiosities
**Birthplace of Modern Remixes** : In the late 1960s and early 1970s, these parties were the genesis of modern remixes, where sound system operators played instrumental "versions" of songs. The goal was to keep everyone dancing amidst intense competition to host the biggest and loudest gigs with the best "toasters" performing live rhymes over the music. "And soon the island was filled with guys who ran sound systems, these giant portable DJ rigs, that were always ready for a party. The goal was to keep everybody dancing."
**Real-Time Remixing** : At these parties, DJs used two turntables and a microphone to perform real-time remixes, surprising and delighting the crowds with on-the-fly creations. The most skilled mixers, coupled with the biggest sound systems and best "toasters," became significant stars. "Using two turn tables in a microphone, songs were remixed in real time, offering surprise and delight to the crowd."

# concert - Disco clubs

## curiosities
**Driving Force for Extended Remixes** : In the disco era, clubs filled with dancers compelled DJs to maintain continuous grooves. The inherent shortness of standard pop songs presented a challenge, leading to innovations like extended remixes and the 12-inch single to keep the party momentum going. "People were heading to clubs in record numbers to dance, and the goal of the DJ was to keep the groove and to keep the party going."
**Audience for New Remix Strategies** : Post-disco, record labels and artists recognized the dance club crowd as a new, significant audience for music. Dance remixes provided a way to tap into this market, with different mixes tailored to appeal to diverse club segments, such as rock remixes for rock crowds. "The dance club crowd was there for the picking."
