## curiosities

# instrument - 78 RPM record

## curiosities
**Standard Format for Decades (Before 1900-1950s)** : Was the primary format for recorded music for more than 50 years, starting sometime before 1900 and adopted as the standard global format for decades.
**Replaced by LP and Single (1950s-1960s)** : Eventually superseded by the 12-inch vinyl LP and the 7-inch single.
**Last Official Releases in US (1960)** : The last 78 RPM records officially released by a record company in America were "Fanny May" by Buster Brown and "Something On Your Mind" by Bobby Marshan, both on Fire Records in June 1960.
**Last Official Release in UK (1960)** : The last 78 RPM record released by HMV Records in the UK was a classic by Ray Charles (catalog number POP 742) in November 1960.
**Extended Use Globally (Until ~1963 and beyond)** : Continued to be released in parts of the world, especially South America (Columbia, Uruguay, Argentina, Brazil) perhaps as late as 1963, with subsequent releases in South Africa, India, and the Philippines, though documentation is scarce.
**Special Promotional Releases (Ongoing)** : Special promotional 78s were produced "just for fun."
**Modern Usage with Historical Speed Settings (1990)** : Turntables sold through the 1960s, 70s, and even 80s often included a speed setting to play 78s. Dave Edmonds released a 10-inch single in 1990 with a side designed to be played at 78 RPM.

# instrument - 8-track

## curiosities
**Invention and Technology (1964)** : Invented in 1964, based on technology used in radio stations for playing songs and commercials. It used a continuous loop of magnetic tape inside a plastic cartridge, allowing fast forwarding but not rewinding.
**First Mobile Music Format (1965)** : It was the first format that allowed people to enjoy their own music while moving, such as in a car. Ford was the first to integrate 8-tracks into vehicles for the 1965 model year.
**Widespread Adoption (1960s-1970s)** : Players and recorders soon became available for home stereos and as portable machines. Record labels released albums in the format, and hundreds of millions of 8-tracks were sold.
**Eclipsed by Cassette and CD (Late 1970s-1980s)** : Eventually superseded first by the cassette and then by the compact disc.
**Phased Out in Retail (1982)** : 8-tracks were phased out of record stores in the US and Canada by the end of 1982.
**Continued Niche Popularity (Late 1980s)** : Remained popular in certain segments, such as with long-haul truckers, and could be found in truck stops until at least the late 1980s. Record and tape clubs also kept the format alive.
**Last Album Release (1988)** : The last album released by a record company on 8-track was Fleetwood Mac's 'Greatest Hits' in November 1988.

# curiosity

**Last TV Commercial for Cigarettes (1970)** : The last TV commercial for cigarettes ran on American TV on December 31, 1970, for Virginia Slims, after which all TV advertising of cigarettes became illegal.
**Diamond Record Achievement (Definition)** : In America, a Diamond record signifies the sale of 10 million physical copies of an album.
**End of Mega-Seller Era (Post-2016)** : The era of mega-selling albums, where artists like Michael Jackson ("Thriller," 65 million copies), AC/DC ("Back in Black," 50 million copies), and Pink Floyd ("Dark Side of the Moon," 45 million copies) sold tens of millions of physical copies, is considered to be over. This is primarily due to declining physical music sales and the rise of streaming, with today's mega-sellers typically reaching around 2.5 million sales in the US.
