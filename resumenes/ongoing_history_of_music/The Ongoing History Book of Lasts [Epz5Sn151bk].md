# artist - Hilly Crystal

## curiosities
**Founder of CBGB (1973)** : Hilly Crystal, an ex-marine, opened the CBGB club in 1973 in the Bowery neighborhood of New York. Despite the area being an economic and social disaster zone at the time, the cheap rent allowed starving artists to live, rehearse, and create. Crystal established the club's musical policy, which fostered an environment where artists, "freaks, and weirdos" could do their own thing, leading to the birth of punk rock. "When an ex-marine named Hilly Crystal opened the place in 1973, it was in a part of town where nobody wanted to go."

**Closure of CBGB (2006)** : Hilly Crystal was forced to close CBGB due to escalating financial pressures. Monthly rent had risen from $19,000 to $35,000, and the club faced a $90,000 lawsuit from a local residence committee. Despite court cases and an attempt to declare the club a historical landmark, the ongoing gentrification of the neighborhood and rising costs made it impossible to continue. "In the end, rent went up to $35,000 a month and Hilly had no choice but to close the place down."

# instrument - 78 RPM record

## curiosities
**Long-Standing Standard Format** : The 78 RPM record was the predominant format for recorded music for over 50 years, appearing before 1900 and becoming the standard global format for decades. It was eventually supplanted by the 12-inch vinyl LP and the 7-inch single. "The 78 RPM record was the heart and soul of recorded music for more than 50 years. It started showing up sometime before 1900, and was eventually adopted as the standard global format for decades before being shoved out of the way by both the 12 inch vinyl LP and the 7 inch single."

**Last Official Releases in US and UK (1960)** : The last 78 RPM records officially released by record companies in America were Buster Brown's "Fanny May" and Bobby Marshan's "Something On Your Mind" in June 1960. In the UK, HMV Records continued releasing 78s until November 1960, with Ray Charles's catalog number POP742 being the apparent last. "That was the last 78 RPM record officially released by our record company."

**Continued Use in Other Regions and Promotional Items** : The 78 RPM format persisted in parts of the world, particularly South America (Columbia, Uruguay, Argentina, Brazil) as late as 1963, and later in South Africa, India, and the Philippines, though documentation is scarce. Special promotional 78s were also produced, such as Dave Edmunds' 1990 10-inch single for "King of Love" which included a 78 RPM version. Many turntables sold through the 1960s, 70s, and even 80s, continued to include a 78 speed setting. "However, the 78 lived on in other parts of the world, especially in South America, Columbia, Uruguay, Argentina, Brazil, perhaps as late as 1963."

# instrument - 8-track

## curiosities
**Invention and Early Adoption (1964-1965)** : The 8-track format was invented in 1964, based on continuous loop magnetic tape technology used in radio stations. It consisted of a continuous loop of magnetic tape housed in a plastic cartridge, allowing for fast forwarding but not rewinding. Ford was the first to integrate 8-track players into their vehicles for the 1965 model year, providing people with the novel ability to enjoy their own music while driving. "The 8-track was invented in 1964 and was based on a technology used in radio stations for playing back songs and commercials and so on. A continuous loop of magnetic tape was placed inside a plastic cartridge."

**Market Penetration and Decline** : Following its introduction, 8-track players and recorders became available for home stereos and as portable units. Record labels rapidly adopted the format, leading to hundreds of millions of 8-tracks being sold. However, it was eventually superseded by the cassette and later the compact disc. While phased out of general record stores in the US and Canada by the end of 1982, 8-tracks remained popular with specific demographics like long-haul truckers, found in truck stops until at least the late 1980s, and maintained by record and tape clubs. "But it was eventually eclipsed first by the cassette and then by the compact disc."

**Last Album Release (1988)** : Fleetwood Mac's "Greatest Hits," released in November 1988, is considered the last album put out by a record company on the 8-track format. "The best answer seems to be Fleetwood Max Greatest Hits, which came out in November of 1988."

# curiosity

**Diamond Record Sales Achievement** : In America, a diamond record signifies the sale of 10 million physical copies of an album. While past mega-sellers like Michael Jackson's "Thriller" (65 million), AC/DC's "Back in Black" (50 million), and Pink Floyd's "Dark Side of the Moon" (45 million) achieved significantly higher numbers, current mega-sellers typically reach around 2.5 million copies. The era of such massive physical sales is believed to be over due to declining physical sales and the rise of music streaming. "In America this means you've sold 10 million copies, 10 million physical copies of your album."

**German Reunification and U2's Flight (October 2, 1990)** : On October 2, 1990, a British Airways flight carrying U2 into Tegal Airport in Berlin became the last commercial flight to enter East Germany before its official reunification with West Germany at midnight. The city was filled with celebrations of a united Germany. The pilot, in a state of excitement, circled Berlin to allow passengers to view landmarks like the Brandenburg Gate and Hansa Studios (where U2 was headed to work on their next album). "On October 2nd, 1990, you two, boarded a British Airways flight into Tegal Airport in Berlin. That was the last commercial flight into East Germany before East Germany ceased to exist."
