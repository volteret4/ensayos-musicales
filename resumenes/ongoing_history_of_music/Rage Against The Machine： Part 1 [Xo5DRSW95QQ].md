## albums
**Rage Against The Machine (1992) - Debut Album** : Released on November 3rd, 1992, this album features a powerful photograph of Thich Kwan Duk, a burning Buddhist monk, on its cover. The liner notes clearly state, "no samples, keyboards or synthesizers were used in the making of this record." The album includes guest vocals from Maynard James Keenan of Tool and Stephen Perkins of Jane's Addiction. Notably, the version of "Bullet in the Head" on the album was recorded before the band had ever performed live. Renowned for its audio quality, "Track 3, take the power back" is specifically recommended for testing speakers and amplifiers. "on November 3rd, 1992, the album with a dying, Fitchquan dock on the cover appeared in stores."

## songs
**Bombtrack (1992)** : This song is the opening track of Rage Against The Machine's 1992 debut record. The instrumental intro for "Bombtrack" was a piece Tom Morello had leftover from his previous band, Lock Up. The main riff of the song was derived from "Alice in My Fantacies" by Funkadelic, a track from their 1974 album "Standing on the Verge of Getting It On." "Bomb track, the first song, and they're amazing, 1992 first record."
**Freedom (1992)** : This track is featured on Rage Against The Machine's self-titled debut album, which was released on November 3rd, 1992. "Rage against the machine with freedom from their self-titled debut record released on November 3rd, 1992."
**Know Your Enemy (1992)** : Appearing on Rage Against The Machine's debut album, "Know Your Enemy" features prominent guest vocals from Maynard James Keenan, the singer of Tool. Additionally, Stephen Perkins, the drummer for Jane's Addiction, also contributed vocals to this song. "Maynard James Keenan of Tool, sings on a song called Know Your Enemy."
**Bullet in the Head (1992)** : A demo version of this song was created during the band's initial rehearsals in the San Fernando Valley, and it was noted for being remarkably true to its final recorded form. The album version of "Bullet in the Head" was recorded before the band had ever performed a single live show. "that's a demo of Bulletin the Head, which of course would end up on Rage's debut record."
**Killing in the Name (1992)** : Recognized as one of the greatest and angriest rock songs of the 1990s from their debut album. Due to its lyrical content, specifically containing 17 instances of the "F-Bomb," the Canadian Broadcast Standards Council's regulations often necessitate radio stations to play a sanitized or truncated version of the song. "Rage with the truncated version of one of the greatest and angriest rock songs of the 1990s."

## curiosities
**Band Naming** : The band adopted the name "Rage Against The Machine" from the title of a planned second album by Zach Dela Roka's previous band, Inside Out, which never materialized. The name was ultimately deemed more fitting and powerful for the new group. "the band had decided that Rage against the machine would be a great name."
**Major Label Deal (Epic Records)** : Despite previous negative experiences with major labels by three-quarters of the band members, Rage Against The Machine signed with Epic Records. This decision was driven by the label's agreement to grant the band complete creative control over their material and recognizing that a major label's global distribution and marketing power was essential for effectively disseminating their political message. "Epic was the major that agreed to all of Rage's demands."
**Initial Performances and Demo Tape** : After forming, the band began by playing at parties and small clubs throughout the Los Angeles area. They self-funded the recording of an indie cassette demo, which they then sold directly at their shows to build a following. "So the new groups started playing parties and small clubs in and around the LA area."
**Support from Tool** : Rage Against The Machine gained significant early exposure by opening for Tool at some of their concerts. Adam Jones of Tool, a friend of Tom Morello, invited Rage to perform, which helped their demo tape attract the attention of talent scouts and led to a bidding war. "Adam invited Rage to open for some of their gigs."
**Liner Notes Thank Yous** : The debut album's liner notes feature an extensive list of acknowledgments, including Beto (Zach Dela Roka's father, an artist who destroyed his paintings during a nervous breakdown) and revolutionary musicians such as Chuck D and Joe Strummer. "Beto is Zack's dad, the artist who went crazy in Burdala's paintings."

## curiosities
**Early Life and Family** : Born in Long Beach, California, Zach Dela Roka experienced a fractured family life, being shuttled between his parents. His father was an artist with extreme artistic and religious views who suffered a nervous breakdown, leading him to destroy all his paintings and demand Zach's help in burning them. His mother was in the process of completing her PhD in anthropology. "He was born in Long Beach, California, and he came from a broken family."
**Awareness of Racism** : As the only Latino child in his class, Dela Roka developed an acute awareness of casual racism, an experience that strongly influenced his determination to speak out against injustice. "as the only Latino kid in his class, he was acutely aware of all kinds of casual racism, and he became determined to speak out."
**Musical Beginnings** : Dela Roka started playing guitar at the age of eight. By his teenage years, he was deeply engrossed in punk music, citing bands like The Clash, Minor Threat, Bad Brains, and Bad Religion as significant influences. "He picked up a guitar at eight, and by the time he was a teenager, Zach was deep into punk, clash, minor threat, bad brains, bad religion, that kind of thing."
**Discovery of Freestyle Rapping** : Tom Morello first encountered Zach Dela Roka at a club where Dela Roka was practicing his freestyle rapping. Impressed by what he heard, Morello initiated a conversation that ultimately led to their collaboration and the formation of Rage Against The Machine. "One night, Tom was out at a club where Zach happened to be working on his freestyle rapping."

# artist - Tom Morello

## member of
Electric Sheep
Lock Up
Rage Against The Machine

## instruments
Guitar

## curiosities
**Early Life and Family** : Born in Harlem, Tom Morello's father was from Kenya and became the first Kenyan ambassador to the UN after Kenya achieved independence from Britain in 1963. His mother met his father while teaching on a military base in Kenya. Following his parents' divorce, Tom and his mother relocated to the suburbs of Chicago. "He was born in Harlem. His dad was from Kenya."
**Political Activism** : Morello was politically engaged from a young age, identifying himself as a "hardcore anarchist." Growing up in an entirely white suburb, he was acutely aware of and deeply affected by the racism he observed around him. "Tom was very political from the beginning. Extremely left wing. In fact, he categorized himself as hardcore anarchist."
**Musical Journey** : Morello's first band, at age 13, was a Led Zeppelin tribute band where he served as the singer. He initially struggled with the guitar and did not begin to study it seriously until he was about 16 years old, indicating he was a "late bloomer" on the instrument. "He really didn't start studying the guitar seriously until he was about 16 years old. He's a real late bloomer."
**Academic Achievement** : In 1982, Morello enrolled at Harvard University, from which he graduated with an honors Bachelor of Arts degree in political science. He managed to achieve decent academic marks despite dedicating up to eight hours at a time to guitar practice. "In 1982, Tom enrolled at Harvard and graduated with an honors BA in political science."
**Post-Graduation Career** : After completing his studies at Harvard, Morello moved to California, where he worked as an aide for Alan Cranston, a Democratic Senator. "Once he graduated, he moved to California to work as an aide to Alan Cranston, a Democratic Senator."

# curiosity

**Thich Kwan Duk - Self-Immolation Photograph (June 11, 1963)** : On June 11, 1963, Thich Kwan Duk, a Buddhist monk, performed an act of self-immolation at a busy intersection in Saigon as a protest against the Vietnamese government's suppression of the Buddhist religion. He sat in a lotus position, meditated, had five gallons of gasoline poured on his head, offered prayers, and then struck a match, maintaining complete serenity as he burned. This profoundly powerful photograph influenced US President John F. Kennedy to withdraw American support from the Vietnamese government of Ngo Dinh Diem, leading to a coup d'état, subsequent governmental instability, and deepening the crisis that contributed to the Vietnam War. This iconic image later appeared on the cover of Rage Against The Machine's debut album in the fall of 1992, almost three decades after the event. "The date of Duk's self-immolation was June 11, 1963."
**Rock Rap Hybrid Style** : This musical fusion, combining elements of rock and rap, was noted for "catching fire" and gaining significant popularity around the period when Rage Against The Machine was forming and achieving recognition for their distinctive sound. "The idea of a rock rap hybrid style was catching fire."
**Canadian Broadcast Standards Council** : This regulatory body is mentioned as generally disapproving of the broadcast of songs that contain a high frequency of profanity, specifically noting that they "tend to frown on the playing of songs that contain 17 different iterations of the F-Bomb," which results in radio-edited versions of tracks like "Killing in the Name" being played. "the Canadian Broadcast Standards Council tends to frown on the playing of songs that contain 17 different iterations of the F-Bomb."
