# label - RCA

## curiosities
**Early Vinyl Experimentation and Patent Lapse (1930s)** : In 1931, RCA experimented with polyvinyl chloride as a superior replacement for the materials used in 78 RPM records. They even produced 12-inch vinyl records, ready for public sale, but the Great Depression prevented their release. Consequently, RCA allowed many of their crucial patents related to this vinyl technology to expire, a decision later regarded as "dumb" as Columbia Records would later use similar principles for the LP. "Then in 1931, RCA was experimenting with vinyl as a replacement for the crappy materials that went into making 78s. RCA even started making vinyl records that were 12 inches in diameter, and were ready to sell them to the general public. But it was the depression. And no one was in the mood for introducing a new format into what seemed at the time to be a dying industry... So not only did RCA give up, but they also led a bunch of their patents expire."
**Reaction to Columbia's LP Unveiling (1948)** : Columbia Records invited RCA executives to a private demonstration of their new LP technology in April 1948, two months before the public press conference. RCA "freaked out" upon realizing they had been severely "scooped" by technology they once possessed but had allowed to slip away due to a "dumb clerical error" (referring to their expired patents). Despite being offered a license for the technology, RCA refused. "Columbia invited RCA executives into their boardroom in April 1948, some two months before that big press conference, to demonstrate their new technology. RCA freaked out because they knew that they'd been scooped and scooped badly, and with technology that they once possessed. But let slip away due to some dumb clerical error. They offered to license the technology to RCA, but they said, no, no thanks."
**Madam X Project and 7-inch Single Development (1949)** : After Columbia's LP announcement, RCA initiated project "Madam X" to develop its own vinyl record format to protect its market dominance. Engineers revisited 1930s archives and found notes from 1901 indicating experiments with a single-song format. As a consumer electronics company, RCA also planned to manufacture dedicated record players for their new records. This led to the introduction of the 7-inch single in February 1949. "Meanwhile, RCA knew that they had to do something. Otherwise, their market dominance in both North America and Europe would be in jeopardy. The one thing they did realize is that vinyl was a much better raw material for records than shellac, so they drew up plans for their own version of the vinyl record. They called the project Madam X."
**Color-Coded Vinyl Records (1949)** : Upon the launch of the 7-inch single, RCA introduced a system of color-coding records by genre. Country music was pressed on green vinyl, serious classical on red, popular classical on midnight blue, children's records on yellow, international songs on sky blue, Rhythm and Blues on "saris" (not red), and everyday pop records on black vinyl. While a "nice idea," this system did not last very long. "RCA had come up with a system of color coding records by genre. Country was green. Serious classical was red. Popular classical was midnight blue. Yellow was reserved for children's records. International songs were sky blue. Rhythm and blues was saris. Not red, saris. And your everyday pop records were on block vinyl. It was a nice idea, but it didn't last very long."
**Format War with Columbia** : Following RCA's refusal to license Columbia's LP technology, a fierce format war erupted. RCA was determined to push its 7-inch, 45 RPM single, though it did release its classical catalog on LPs, which seemed to signal a wavering in the fight. Millions were spent on advertising the advantages of their stackable records. "That led into a brand new format war... It was Columbia versus RCA, each trying to woo other labels and companies and music consumers to adopt their formats."

# label - Columbia Records

## curiosities
**Development of the Micro-Groove Record (LP)** : Columbia Records was responsible for developing the micro-groove record, also known as the Long-Playing record (LP), as a solution to the limitations of 78 RPM records. Led by president Edward Wallerstein and in-house engineer Dr. Peter Goldmark, Columbia refined polyvinyl chloride (Vinylite from Union Carbide) to create records with grooves cut closer together and a smoother surface, resulting in more music per side (20 minutes on a 12-inch disc) and less noise. "It fell to Columbia Records to come up with a solution, which is where we get back to this plastic polyvinyl chloride, which had been refined by Union Carbide of the 1930s under the name vinylite. Led by a guy named Edward Wallerstein, the president of Columbia, and at in-house engineer named Dr. Peter Goldmark, Columbia started working on what they called the micro-groove record."
**Secret LP Development** : The development of the LP was conducted in strict secrecy by Columbia Records, specifically to prevent competitors, especially RCA, from knowing what they were working on. This secrecy allowed Columbia to surprise the industry with their revolutionary format. "This was all very secret, of course. Columbia didn't want any of their competitors, including especially RCA, to know what they were up to."
**Unveiling of the LP (June 18, 1948)** : Columbia Records officially unveiled the 12-inch long-playing album at the Waldorf Astoria Hotel in Park Avenue, New York, on June 18, 1948. Forty members of the press witnessed a dramatic demonstration comparing an 8-foot stack of 78 RPM records to a 15-inch stack of LPs. Edward Wallerstein demonstrated a 4-minute 78 RPM classical recording, abruptly stopping, then played a 22.5-minute uninterrupted version on an LP, stunning reporters. "The big day came on June 18, 1948 at the Waldorf Astoria Hotel in Park Avenue, New York. Forty members of the press entered a room where they saw a big stack of 78 RPM records. It was more than 8 feet high. Next to it was another stack of flat, some things, but it was only 15 inches high. Edward Wallerstein stood up. You're here to witness the unveiling of a revolutionary new technology, he said."
**Format War with RCA** : Columbia's introduction of the LP led to a format war with RCA, who refused to license the technology. Columbia committed to adopting the 12-inch, 33 1/3 RPM format, forcing consumers to choose sides or buy multi-speed players, leading to initial declines in record sales. "All the major record companies loved it and had committed to adopting the format. It except RCA, of course."

# instrument - Phonograph

## curiosities
**Edison's Cylinder-Based System** : The phonograph, invented by Thomas Edison, was the first device capable of recording sound. However, it utilized rotating cylinders, which were eventually superseded by Emil Berliner's flat disk gramophone due to limitations such as shorter lifespan (worn out after about 160 plays) and single-sided capacity. "Thomas Edison may have invented how to record sound with his phonograph, but he used rotating cylinders."
**Mechanical Tracking System** : Edison's phonograph system utilized a mechanical format where the needle tracked in the grooves with an up and down motion. This was less durable and skipped more often than Berliner's side-to-side tracking system for gramophones. Edison eventually abandoned cylinders for his own incompatible disk format in 1913, but was out of the record business by the end of the 1920s. "You see, the needle on an Edison record tracked in the grooves with an up and down motion."

# instrument - Gramophone

## curiosities
**Berliner's Flat Disk Invention (1888)** : The gramophone, invented by Emil Berliner, was the first device to store music on a flat rotating disk. He demonstrated it in 1888 and secured patents (number 564,586) in 1896. This design quickly gained popularity over Edison's phonograph cylinders because it sounded better, disks lasted longer, and offered double the capacity with two sides. "The guy who came up with the idea of storing music on a flat rotating disk was Emil Berliner. He first demonstrated his device, which he called the gramophone, in 1888."
**Superior Side-to-Side Tracking** : Berliner's gramophone records used a superior technology where the stylus moved from side to side in the grooves. This design resulted in fewer skips and increased the record's lifespan compared to Edison's up-and-down tracking system. "Berliner's stylus moved from side to side. This meant that the record skipped last and lasted longer. Gramophone records used superior technology."

# instrument - Spindle

## curiosities
**Special Spindle for Stackable 45s** : RCA's 7-inch, 45 RPM records were designed to be stacked on a record player with a special spindle. This allowed multiple records to be played consecutively, with one dropping into place after another finished, providing up to 50 minutes of uninterrupted music. "By manufacturing a record player with a special spindle so you could stack these new records on top of each other."
**Large Hole Requirement for 45s** : To accommodate the special spindle for stacking and to distribute friction and torque stresses evenly, RCA's 45 RPM records featured a larger hole (1/16-inch wide). A smaller hole would quickly wear out and become out of round, causing the record to wobble, resulting in extra wear and skipping. "Engineers figured that if these new records were going to be dropped down a 16-inch spindle, a bigger hole was needed to distribute the friction and torque stresses more evenly."
**Modern 7-inch Vinyl with Small Hole** : Today, the majority of 7-inch vinyl records are issued with a standard small hole, rather than the large spindle hole of original 45s. This change reflects the fact that record stacking on tall spindles is no longer a common practice, thus eliminating the need for a large hole to absorb torque stresses. "Today though, the majority of seven-inch vinyl issued comes with a standard small hole. Why? Well, because no one stacks records high on tall spindles anymore, so no one needs a big hole to absorb all those torque stresses."

# curiosity

**Vinyl's Resurgence in the 21st Century** : After bottoming out in 2006, vinyl record sales began an unexpected increase. Sales doubled by 2008, doubled again by 2011, and doubled yet again by 2013, indicating a "fascinating resurrection" for a format once thought dead. Despite being a small fraction of the market, it was growing, profitable, and perceived as "cool." "After bottoming out in 2006, a very strange thing happened. Sales of vinyl records started to go up. In 2008, sales had doubled. By 2011, they doubled again, and doubled yet again by 2013. Something weird was going on."
**1999 Perception of Vinyl's Demise** : In December 1999, with digital music and CDs dominating, vinyl was widely considered "ancient history." People were disposing of their collections by selling them to used record stores, leaving them at thrift shops, giving them away, or simply throwing them out. The introduction of iTunes was still over a year away. "CDs ruled, and vinyl was ancient history. And while a few people had heard about MP3s, we were still over a year from Apple introducing the iTunes music store. So vinyl was done. Dead."
**Music Industry Tailspin (Circa 2009)** : Around 2009, the music industry faced a severe downturn. CD sales were consistently dropping, piracy significantly eroded remaining sales, and overall revenue from music sales had shrunk to half of what it was in 2000. This period was characterized by widespread negative growth across most segments of the industry. "The music industry is in a tailspin. CD sales are dropping week after week. Pairacy is eating into the sales that remain, and revenue from the sale of music is shrinking and shrinking and shrinking to the point where they're half what they were in 2000."
**Current Market for Vinyl Records** : In the present day, buying vinyl records is considered "hot and cool again." This trend extends beyond new releases to vintage records found in record shows, specialized stores, and even mainstream retailers like Urban Outfitters. Traditional brick-and-mortar stores, Amazon (the biggest seller globally), and record labels issuing expensive box sets almost universally stock and include high-quality vinyl. Artists now recognize vinyl as an essential format for new album and single releases. "Everyone knows by now that buying vinyl records is hot and cool again. And it's not just new releases... Vinyl is everywhere. Traditional bricks and mortar record stores, once it still exists, stock vinyl again. Amazon is the biggest seller of vinyl in the world. Record labels are issuing expensive box sets that almost always come with high quality vinyl pieces. And it's to the point where almost any artist knows that if they're going to cover the marketplace with a new release, they have to put their album or even their single out on vinyl."
**Early Lack of Record Standardization** : In the early days of disc records, there was a complete absence of industry standards for record sizes and playback directions. Companies produced records in various widths—7-inch, 10-inch, 12-inch, 14-inch, 16-inch, and even 21-inch by NeoPhone in 1904. Additionally, some machines and records were designed to play from the inside out, rather than the more common outside-in. "But even once the idea of disks caught on, there was little in the way of standards. Some companies made records that were seven inches wide. Others issued ten-inch disks. Still others like twelve inches. Fourteen and sixteen-inch disks weren't uncommon. In a 1904, a British company called NeoPhone issued records that were twenty-one inches wide... And get this, some machines and records were designed to play the disk from the inside out, not the outside in."
**Early Record Speed Confusion** : In the initial period of record players, there was no standard playback speed. Emil Berliner's original disks turned at 70 RPM, but other manufacturers' records could range from 50 to 120 RPM. This lack of standardization led to widespread consumer confusion and records that were incompatible with many players. "Berliner's original disks turned at seventy RPM, but other companies went anywhere from fifty to 120 RPM. Naturally, this resulted in a lot of incompatible records and a lot of consumer confusion."
**Emergence of the 78 RPM Standard** : Eventually, the industry converged on 78 RPM as the standard playback speed, determined to offer the best balance of sound quality, optimal capacity per side, and minimal wear. However, even this standard varied slightly based on regional electricity supply: 78.26 revolutions per minute in North America (60 hertz, 120 volts) versus 77.92 revolutions per minute in Europe/UK (50 hertz, 220 or 240 volts), a difference detectable by "sensitive ears." "Eventually, though, everyone came together and decided that seventy-eight RPM would provide the best level of sound, the optimum capacity per side, and the least wear."
**Fragility and Composition of Early 78 RPM Records** : Early 78 RPM records were highly fragile, described as being "worse than glass" or "like delicate China." They were made from a mixture of shellac, cotton, a binding agent, powdered slate or limestone, and a wax lubricant. This composition made them wear out quickly, with obtaining 125 plays considered lucky, and prone to breaking easily. "But they were very fragile. They were made of a weird mix of shellac, cotton, some kind of binding agent, powdered slate or limestone, and a wax lubricant."
**Lack of Dedicated Record Stores in Early 20th Century** : In the early 1900s, dedicated record stores were rare. If one wanted to purchase records, they typically did so from the same place they bought their phonograph, which was usually a local furniture store. Spillers Records in Cardiff, Wales, established in 1894, is an exception as the world's oldest record store and is still operating today. "For the most part, record stores did not exist then. I should point out, however, that the oldest record store in the world is Spillers Records in Cardiff Wales. It was established in eighteen ninety-four, and the place is still open."
**Record Industry Recession (1922) and Great Depression Impact** : After experiencing booming sales post-World War I, the record industry plunged into recession in 1922 with the rise of radio. This decline worsened dramatically with the onset of the Great Depression, causing the industry to shrink by an astonishing 95%, with unit sales plummeting from 104 million in 1927 to under 6 million by 1932. This period saw terrible price wars, with 78s selling for under ten cents, severely impacting artists and songwriters. "But then a new technology called Radio came along, and in nineteen twenty-two, the record industry was thrust into recession... By the time the depression started, the record industry had shrunk by ninety-five percent, ninety-five percent."
**Departure of Edison and Berliner from the Record Business** : By the time of the Great Depression, both Thomas Edison and Emil Berliner had exited the record industry. Edison ultimately abandoned his phonograph system. Berliner, having amassed wealth from his gramophone invention, redirected his efforts to campaigning for milk pasteurization and attempting to build helicopters. "By the way, both Thomas Edison and Emil Berliner were out of the record game by this time. Edison just gave up on his phonograph system, and Berliner took his money and became a campaigner for the pasteurization of milk, and he also tried to build some helicopters."
**Jukeboxes Save the Recorded Music Industry** : One of two major factors that rescued the recorded music industry was the jukebox. By the 1940s, a quarter million jukeboxes were installed across North America in bars, restaurants, nightclubs, and truck stops, generating 13 million in annual sales by requiring regular stocking with new records. "The first was the jukebox, an automated machine found in bars, restaurants, nightclubs, and truck stops that could play a favorite song for a nickel. By the 1940s, there were a quarter million jukeboxes installed across North America, and they all had to be regularly stocked with new records, and that accounted for thirteen million in annual sales."
**Polyvinyl Chloride (Vinyl) Saves the Industry** : The second major factor that saved the recorded music industry was polyvinyl chloride, a century-old chemical compound commonly known as plastic. Chemists and engineers had discovered its durability and resistance to corrosion, making it ideal for applications like sewer pipes, and eventually, for manufacturing superior records. "The other thing that saved the industry was a century-old chemical compound called polyvinyl chloride, which, for lack of a better term, is plastic."
**Shellac Supply Crisis During WWII** : Shellac, a key ingredient in early records, is derived from the secretions of the female lac bug found in the jungles of Malaysia. When Japan occupied the Malay peninsula after 1941 during World War II, the supply of shellac became severely limited. At one point in the mid-1940s, consumers had to trade in an old record to acquire a new one due to the raw material shortage. "Shellac is derived from the secretions of the female lac bug, which is found in the jungles of Malaysia. And the Japanese occupied the melee peninsula after 1941, shellac became in very short supply."
**Limitations of 78 RPM Records Drive Innovation** : Several critical shortcomings of 78 RPM records necessitated the development of improved formats. These included low audio fidelity ("low fi"), limited capacity (maximum of about four minutes per side, requiring classical works like Beethoven's Fifth Symphony to be broken into multiple chunks), rapid wear (lucky to get 125 plays), and extreme fragility (described as "worse than glass"). "Number one, audio quality. 78s were low five. They had to sound better. Number two, capacity... The maximum amount of music per side of a 78 was about four minutes... If you got 125 plays out of a 78 RPM record, you were lucky. The damn things wore out fast. And number four, we talked about this earlier, durability. Seventy-eighths were very, very breakable. They were worse than glass."
**Origin of the Term "Record Album"** : The term "record album" originated from the practice of packaging collections of 78 RPM records. Due to their limited capacity, classical works or longer pieces required 9, 10, 12, or even 15 individual records. These were bound into sleeves resembling photo albums, which then became known as "record albums." Such collections were "hideously expensive," costing $100 or more in the early 20th century. **LP Playback Speed and Grooves Geometry** : Columbia engineers, utilizing information from expired RCA patents and audio technology pioneered by the film industry in the late 1920s, determined the optimal geometry for the Long-Playing record. They settled on a playback speed of 33 and 1/3 revolutions per minute for a 12-inch record, with grooves cut anywhere from 224 to 300 per linear inch. "Columbia engineers working the geometry of the situation decided to go with 33 and 1-third revolutions per minute of a 12-inch record with grooves cut anywhere from 224 to 300 per linear inch."
**Public Confusion during Format War (1949-1954)** : From 1949 through approximately 1954, consumers experienced significant confusion due to the ongoing "format war" between the 12-inch, 33 1/3 RPM LP (supported by most major labels except RCA) and the 7-inch, 45 RPM single (pushed by RCA). Additionally, 78 RPM records remained very popular and widely manufactured. Consumers were annoyed by having to choose sides and potentially buy proprietary hardware, leading to a "tailspin" in record sales regardless of format. "By the end of 1949, consumers were rather confused. There was the 12-inch 33-in-a-third long playing album... RCA was determined to push ahead with their 7-inch 45 RPM single... And 78 RPM records were still very, very popular... Music fans were very annoyed for a while. They didn't like having to take sides... As a result, record sales, regardless of format, went into a tailspin."
**Rationale Behind RCA's 45 RPM 7-inch Design** : RCA designed its 7-inch, 45 RPM record with grooves placed on the outer half of the disc to prevent distortion and wear that typically occurred closer to the center where grooves were more tightly packed. This necessitated a two-and-a-half-inch radius from the center for the label and a tone arm buffer zone, leaving less "real estate" for music. To increase capacity beyond a single song, RCA implemented stackable records on a special spindle, with a slight lip on the outer edge of each record to prevent groove grinding. "Using a little geometry, engineers put the record grooves on the outer half of the record. This presented distortion and wear when you got towards the center when the grooves were packed more closely together... By manufacturing a record player with a special spindle so you could stack these new records on top of each other. When one finished playing, the tone arm would swing back and the next record would drop into place. A slight lip on the outer edge of each record would help keep the delicate grooves from grinding together."
**Myth of 45 RPM Speed Calculation** : A common misconception is that the 45 RPM speed for 7-inch singles was simply derived by subtracting 33 (LP speed) from 78 (old standard speed). This is untrue; the speed was determined through complex "mathematics of capacity versus fidelity" to optimize the format's performance characteristics. "The myth is that RCA just subtracted 33 from 78. Not true. Again, it's all down to the mathematics of capacity versus fidelity."
