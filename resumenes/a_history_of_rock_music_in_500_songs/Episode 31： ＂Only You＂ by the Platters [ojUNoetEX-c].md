## albums

## songs
**Only You (And You Alone) (1955)** : This song was originally written by Book Ram with the intention of giving it to The Ink Spots, but it was set aside. Book Ram initially thought the song was "rubbish," an assessment seemingly confirmed by the initial bad recording by The Platters. Jean Bennett, Ram's assistant, rediscovered the sheet music, and Tony Williams insisted on recording it. A voice crack by Williams on the word "only" during an early take was incorporated, adding to the song's character. It became a massive hit, defining an era as the epitome of 1950s Smooth Balladry, spending 30 weeks on the R&B chart (seven at number one) and 22 weeks on the pop chart (peaking at number five). Federal Records rush-released their "awful" original recording to cash in, leading to a lawsuit with Ram and Mercury, which Federal eventually won the right to release but it didn't chart. "no, it sounded good, use it."
**The Great Pretender (1955)** : This song, also written by Book Ram, was the follow-up to "Only You" and proved to be an even bigger hit, solidifying The Platters' string of successes. "Oh, yes, I'm the great pretender. Retending that I'm doing well. My need is such I pretend too much. I'm only evil, but no one can tell."
**The Magic Touch** : One of a string of hits for The Platters following their initial massive successes, regularly appearing at the top of the pop chart. "The magic touch."
**Twilight Time** : Another hit song for The Platters, contributing to their consistent presence at the top of the pop chart. "twilight time."
**Smoke Gets in Your Eyes** : A hit for The Platters, showcasing their transition into the lucrative adult market by covering standards, and regularly charting high on the pop charts. "smoke gets in your eyes."

## curiosities
**Early Formation as The Flamingos** : The group started under the name The Flamingos, but this was a different group from the more well-known Flamingos. The initial lineup included Cornel Gunter, Gaynell Hodge, Alex Hodge, Joe Jefferson, Richard Berry, and Curtis Williams. None of the five members who sang on their biggest hits were part of this original six-person lineup. "The platters started out as the flamingos."
**Renaming to The Platters** : The group was forced to change its name from "The Flamingos" because another group with the same name achieved a hit record. They decided on "The Platters" because disc jockeys often referred to records as "platters." "So they decided that since records were often called platters by disc juckies, they might as well call themselves that."
**Early Singing Difficulties** : Prior to their breakthrough with Mercury Records, The Platters, as signed by Book Ram, were not proficient singers. They struggled with harmony, often taking turns singing lead and failing to sing in the same key. "At the time, they didn't know how to sing in harmony. They just take turns singing lead, and often not be able to sing in the same case each other."
**Vocal Sound Transformation** : Book Ram aimed to reshape The Platters' vocal sound, drawing inspiration from 1940s groups like The Quintones and The Modernaires who featured a four-men and one-woman lineup. This led to the recruitment of Zola Taylor, whose addition was crucial to achieving Ram's desired vocal blend. "Working with The Quintones had given Ram a taste for the particular vocal blend that comes from having four men and one woman."
**Alex Hodge's Departure Controversy** : Alex Hodge, an original member, left The Platters, officially due to an arrest for marijuana possession. However, Gaynell Hodge claimed that Book Ram and Herb Reed conspired to remove Alex because Alex, the alleged original founder of the group, was too knowledgeable about the music business and was becoming suspicious of Ram's financial dealings. "Gael Hodge now claims that this wasn't the real reason that Alex Hodge was sacked."
**Strategic Signing to Mercury Records** : The Platters secured a deal with Mercury Records through a strategic move by their manager, Book Ram. Ram, who also managed The Penguins (fresh off their hit "Earth Angel"), insisted that Mercury sign The Platters as a condition for signing The Penguins, thereby using The Penguins' success as leverage for his preferred group. "Ram agreed to the deal, but only on the condition that Mercury signed the platters as well."
**Six-Month Practice Period** : Following their signing with Mercury Records and a six-month break after their last session with Federal, The Platters underwent intensive practice. This period transformed them into a "slick, professional group" with a polished sound, leading directly to their breakthrough success. "But they spent that six months practicing, and when they got into the studio, so record for Mercury they suddenly sounded good."
**Comparison to The Ink Spots** : The Platters were often compared to The Ink Spots, not only in their musical style, with The Platters emulating The Ink Spots in their biggest hit, but also in the unfortunate way their group eventually disintegrated due to internal conflicts and legal battles. "The platters were, in many ways, the 1950's equivalent to the ink spots."
**Transition to Adult Market** : Although initially marketed as a rock and roll group, The Platters quickly transitioned into the more financially rewarding adult market. This involved recording albums of popular standards, appealing to a broader audience beyond the rock and roll demographic. "But while they started off marketed as a rock and roll group, they soon transitioned into the more lucrative adult market, recording albums of standards."
**Formation of Five Platters Inc.** : Soon after The Platters signed with Mercury, Book Ram established a corporation named "Five Platters Inc." He controlled this entity, and all the band members were shareholders. The members assigned all rights to the band's name to this corporation, a move that would later become central to decades of legal disputes. "a corporation was set up, five platters ink, which was controlled by Book Ram, and had all the members of the platters as shareholders."
**Male Members' Arrest (1959)** : In 1959, all four male members of The Platters were arrested. Although initially alleged to be customers of sex workers, the charges were actually related to sharing their hotel room with white women, and all charges were later dropped. This incident negatively impacted Tony Williams' budding solo career. "not helped by all four of the male platters being arrested, allegedly as customers of sex workers, but in fact, because they were sharing their hotel room with white women."
**Tony Williams Vocal Overdubs** : After Tony Williams left The Platters in 1961, Mercury Records, convinced he was the most important member, would often take recordings by the new lineup (with Sonny Turner) and overdub new lead vocals from Williams onto them for about a year or two. "Mercury would take completed recordings by the new platters lineup and overdub new lead vocals from Williams."
**Decades of Litigation** : Following the departure of band members, 40 years of litigation ensued between Book Ram's companies and various Platters members over the rights to the band name. Court judgments were inconsistent, making it difficult to ascertain a clear legal victor from available documents. "They followed 40 years of litigation between Ram's companies and various platters members, and the judgements went both ways."
**"Sham Corporation" Ruling (1974)** : A court ruled in 1974 that "Five Platters Inc." was a "sham corporation" whose sole purpose was to illegally deprive band members of their ownership in the band name, and that Book Ram had unfairly benefited from an unbalanced bargaining position. This ruling briefly voided FPI's trademark. "there was a court ruling back in 1974 that the whole purpose of five platters ink had been to illegally deprive the band members of their ownership in the band name, that it was a sham corporation."
**Conflicting Legal Claims** : In later legal cases, "Five Platters Inc." contended that band members primarily left because they were alcoholics who no longer wished to tour, and that they had willingly relinquished their rights to the band name. "five platters ink insisted that the band members had mostly left because they were alchoholics who didn't want to tour anymore, and that they'd given up their rights to the band name of their own free will."
**Proliferation of "Fake" Platters Groups** : Over the years, more than one hundred unauthorized "fake" Platters groups, none containing original members, toured, contributing to confusion and legal battles over the band's identity. This situation was compared to the proliferation of fake Ink Spots groups. "Meanwhile, over a hundred fake platters groups, with no original members, went out on the road at various points."
**Final Name Rights Ruling (2011)** : The complex legal disputes over The Platters' name were finally resolved in 2011. A judge ruled that Herb Reed, the last surviving member of the classic lineup (Book Ram having long died), had the rights to the name, and that "Five Platters Inc." had never owned it. "A judge finally ruled that Herb Read had the rights to the name, and that five platters ink had never owned the name."
**Retirement of Name by FPI** : Just before the 2011 ruling, "Five Platters Inc.", then managed by Jean Bennett, announced its intention to retire the name "The Platters," although this was superseded by the court's decision granting rights to Herb Reed. "Just before that ruling, five platters ink, which was now run by Jean Bennett, announced that they were going to retire the name."

**Transition from Film Music to Management** : Ram initially moved west with aspirations of breaking into the film music business but was unsuccessful. **Seven-Year Contract for The Platters** : Upon signing The Platters, Book Ram secured a lengthy seven-year contract for the group, consisting of Tony Williams, Alex Hodge, David Lynch, and Herb Reed, reflecting his long-term plans for their development. **Intent for Tony Williams' Solo Career** : It appears that Book Ram's long-term plan, dating back to 1956, was to establish Tony Williams as a solo artist while simultaneously maintaining The Platters as a successful group, indicating a calculated management strategy for both entities. "Indeed, it seems they have been Book Ram's plan from the very start to get Williams to be a solo artist while keeping the platters as a hit group."
**Control of Five Platters Inc.** : Book Ram was the controlling force behind "Five Platters Inc.," the corporation he established shortly after The Platters signed to Mercury, which held the rights to the band's name and was instrumental in the subsequent legal battles. # label - Five Platters Inc.

## curiosities
**Controlled by Book Ram** : This corporation was established by Book Ram soon after The Platters signed to Mercury Records, with Ram maintaining control despite band members being shareholders. The purpose was allegedly to manage the band's rights, but it became a source of major legal disputes. "a corporation was set up, five platters ink, which was controlled by Book Ram, and had all the members of the platters as shareholders."
**Assignment of Name Rights by Members** : At the time of its formation, the members of The Platters assigned any rights they had to the band's name to Five Platters Inc., a decision that led to decades of litigation. "The platters, at the time, assigned any rights they had to the platters, the rights they had to the band's name to this corporation."
**"Sham Corporation" Ruling (1974)** : A court ruled in 1974 that Five Platters Inc. was a "sham corporation" designed to unlawfully strip band members of their ownership in the band name, and that Book Ram had unfairly benefited. Subsequently, its trademark in "The Platters" name was declared void. "there was a court ruling back in 1974 that the whole purpose of five platters ink had been to illegally deprive the band members of their ownership in the band name, that it was a sham corporation."
**Contesting Member Departures** : In legal battles, Five Platters Inc. argued that band members mostly left because they were alcoholics unwilling to tour, and that they had willingly relinquished their rights to the band name. "five platters ink insisted that the band members had mostly left because they were alchoholics who didn't want to tour anymore, and that they'd given up their rights to the band name of their own free will."
**Run by Jean Bennett After Ram's Death** : After Book Ram's death, his assistant Jean Bennett took over the operation of Five Platters Inc. "five platters ink, which was now run by Jean Bennett."
**Announced Retirement of Name (Pre-2011 Ruling)** : Before the 2011 court decision, Five Platters Inc., under Jean Bennett's management, announced its intention to retire the name "The Platters." "Just before that ruling, five platters ink, which was now run by Jean Bennett, announced that they were going to retire the name."
**Final Ruling: Never Owned the Name (2011)** : In a definitive 2011 court ruling, it was determined that Five Platters Inc. had never legally owned the rights to The Platters' name, granting them instead to Herb Reed. "A judge finally ruled that Herb Read had the rights to the name, and that five platters ink had never owned the name."

**Movement Between Vocal Groups** : In the era of vocal groups, there was significant fluidity, with members frequently moving between different ensembles. "there was a lot of movement between different vocal groups and the platters were very far from an exception."

"Even so, the demand for vocal groups at the time was so great that they got signed by Ralph Bass."
**Managers Taking Unearned Songwriting Credits** : A common practice among managers in the music industry was to credit themselves on songs they did not write, and sometimes had never even heard. Book Ram was presented as an exception, with no evidence suggesting he took unearned credits for The Platters' songs. "Where other managers got themselves credited on songs that they didn't write, and in some cases had never heard."
**Popularity of Four Men, One Woman Vocal Group Style** : In the 1940s, a vocal group style featuring four men and one woman was popular, significantly influenced by The Modernaires who sang with the Glenn Miller Orchestra. However, this style largely fell out of favor by the 1950s, though Book Ram sought to revive it with The Platters. "This had been a popular group style in the 1940s, thanks to the influence of the modern-airs, the vocal group who sang with the Glemmella Orchestra, but had largely fallen out at favour in the 50s."
