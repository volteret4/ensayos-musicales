## songs
**La Bamba (1958)** : An old folk song from Veracruz, Mexico, exemplary of the Son Haracho style, with its earliest known recording dating to 1939, though its existence may span centuries. Traditionally sung at weddings, its origins are obscure; while some claims suggest an origin in music made by slaves in Mexico, referencing the Ambamba tribe or a 17th-century Bamba Ria uprising, it is more likely that the title refers to the Spanish word "Bambolia" (to sway, swing, or wobble), given that the lyrics are primarily about a dance with nonsense elements. The song's tradition blends Spanish, indigenous, and African styles. Valens was initially hesitant to record it due to concerns about selling out his cultural background and his poor Spanish, but learned the lyrics phonetically from his aunt. Unusually, he recorded the vocal as an overdub, with Bob Keane whispering the lyrics to him. The arrangement differed significantly from earlier versions, making it the first record to successfully merge Latin and rock'n'roll styles into a cohesive whole. It utilizes the "three-cord trick" sequence (one-four-five-four) and became profoundly influential, inspiring countless garage bands and other notable records like "Hang on Sloopy" and "Twist and Shout." It reached number 22 on the charts. "La Bamba was the first record to make that chord sequence popular, and inspired thousands of garage bands."
**Donna (1958)** : This song was named after a girl with whom Valens had a relationship or crush and was completed with assistance from Doug Machia. Initially a demo recorded in Valens' basement, it was later used as the B-side for "La Bamba," with session musicians overdubbing their parts onto the original track. Despite being the B-side, "Donna" became the more successful commercial hit, reaching number two on the charts. "Donna became the bigger hit."
**Come On, Let's Go! (1958)** : Valens' debut record, which featured a foundational one-four-five-one chord progression common in many Latin songs. The recording involved session musicians Red Callender on bass, Earl Palmer on drums, and Ernie Freeman on piano, with guitarists occasionally contributing a tic-tac bass (a six-string bass guitar known for its unique tone, characteristic of many LA records). Many of these musicians later formed parts of The Wrecking Crew. It achieved minor chart success, peaking at number 42. Valens demonstrated his spontaneous performing style by significantly varying the lyrics and melody in live performances compared to the studio version. "Come on, let's go! Was a minor hit, reaching number 42 under heart 100."
**Ooh My Head (1959)** : Credited as a solo composition by Valens, this song is noted for its resemblance to Little Richard's "Ooh! My Soul," suggesting it originated from Valens performing and then creatively altering another artist's material until it became distinct. Led Zeppelin later employed a similar creative process with "Ooh My Head" to produce "Boogie with Stu." Bob Keane, holding the publishing rights for Valens' songs, successfully sued Led Zeppelin for this, despite their attempt to preempt a lawsuit by crediting Valens' mother as a co-writer. Valens performed "Ooh My Head" in the Alan Freed film "Go Johnny Go." "Valence's song seems to have come from precisely this process, of performing someone else's song, and changing it around until it became something different."
**Bony Moronie** : An R&B song originally by Larry Williams, which Richie Valens recorded as an album track.
**Framed** : A song by The Coasters that Richie Valens recorded and included as an album track.

## curiosities
**Early Unpopularity and Appearance** : Richie Valens experienced some unpopularity among other Mexican Americans at his school, partly due to his physical appearance. He was notably light-skinned, which played into existing colorism among Latino youths in the area, and was also described as quite fat. Additionally, his willingness to associate with individuals of other races, including Black and White friends, set him apart from some of his peers. "Some of this was to do with his appearance. He was notably light-skinned from Mexican American, and apparently there was a level of colorism among Latino kids in that area at that time, and he was also quite fat."
**Mortgage Fundraiser Performance** : In January 1958, The Silhouette, Valens' band, organized and played a self-promoted show in a rented hall. The primary purpose of this concert was to raise money specifically to cover Valens' family's mortgage payment for that particular month. "In a hole they'd rented out in order to raise money to pay for Valencia's family's mortgage payment for that month."
**Delayed Audition with Bob Keane** : Valens' initial audition with Bob Keane was postponed because, at the time Machia arrived to take him, Valens was at home minding his siblings. He had to wait for his mother's return before he could leave for the studio. "Valencia was minding the other children at home, and had to wait until his mother got back before he could go to the studio."
**Signed for Energy, Not Skill** : Bob Keane decided to sign Valens to a record deal primarily because he was impressed by Valens' raw energy during his audition, rather than his established musical ability. Following the signing, Keane took on the role of shaping Valens into a new style of performer. "Keane was impressed with Valencia, not because of his ability, but because of his energy."
**Guitar Playing Limitations and Workarounds** : Despite his natural talent and passion for the guitar, Valens was not a technically proficient guitarist when he began recording. He was limited to playing in only a few keys, mainly D or A, and typically used only three or four chords. When a song required the key of D flat, he would manually tune his guitar down a half step as he didn't know the chords for that key. On another occasion, he expressed frustration when trying to guide a bass player for a session, as the low D part he heard in his head was beyond the bass guitar's lowest E note. "Valencia was not a particularly profession guitarist. He had a lot of natural skill and a love of the instrument, but when he first started recording, he could only play in a handful of keys."
**Improvisational Recording Technique** : Due to Valens' spontaneous performing style, most of his completed recordings were laboriously compiled by Bob Keane from numerous takes. Stan Ross, co-owner of Gold Star Studios, vividly described these heavily spliced tapes as appearing as if they had been "through World War XIV." Valens typically entered the studio with only a basic melody idea and a few lyrical fragments, then improvised multiple variations of the song, from which the best segments were edited together to form the final recording. "most of the recordings he completed were pulled together by Bob Keane from multiple takes."
**Lyrical and Musical Spontaneity** : Members of The Silhouette frequently observed Valens' stage performances, where he would begin playing a song, such as Little Richard's "Jenny Jenny," but after only a few lines, he would start improvising entirely new melodies and lyrics that were unique to each performance. This spontaneous approach supports Bob Keane's assertion that Valens typically brought only a four or eight-bar riff and a few lines of lyrics to sessions, improvising the rest for Keane to later shape and structure. "he would start out playing, for example, Jenny Jenny by little Richard, but after a few lines he would start improvising his own new melody and lyrics, which would be different every time."
**"Little Richard of the Valley" Nickname** : Before his career direction was shaped by Bob Keane, Valens was primarily perceived as an R&B singer. This perception earned him the nickname "the Little Richard of the Valley," highlighting his vocal and performance style's similarity to the renowned R&B artist. "he was being referred to as the little Richard of the Valley."
**Appearance in "Go Johnny Go" Film** : Richie Valens featured in the 1959 Alan Freed film "Go Johnny Go," where he performed his song "Ooh My Head" and appeared in several other scenes. However, he was given no spoken lines, as the film's structure stipulated that musical performers were compensated solely as a form of promotion, while anyone delivering dialogue was classified as an actor and required separate payment. The film also showcased other notable artists including Chuck Berry, Eddie Cochran, The Flamingos, and Jackie Wilson. "Valens performed Oomai Head, and also appeared in several scenes of the film, but had no lines as the musical performers were being paid, as the film was considered to be promotion for them, while anyone who had a line was considered an actor and had to be paid."

## curiosities
**Band Name Origin** : The band chose their name, The Silhouette, in homage to "The Silhouettes," a popular hit song by The Rays that had recently been released. "who named themselves after a recent hit of that name by The Rays."
**Unrecorded and Disputed History** : The Silhouette never made any professional recordings. After Richie Valens achieved fame, various members of the band were interviewed, but their accounts often diverged significantly regarding who initially invited Valens into the group, who held leadership within the band, who taught Valens his performance skills, which songs they performed, and who contributed to the material that later brought Valens renown. "These Silhouette never recorded."
**Pioneering Mixed-Race Lineup** : The Silhouette was an unusually large band for its era, featuring a diverse instrumentation including three trumpets, five saxophones, a vibraphone, piano, drums, and multiple singers, alongside Valens on guitar and vocals. Crucially, the group was groundbreaking for its mixed-race composition, including a majority of Mexican Americans, as well as Black, Italian (at a time when Italians were not universally considered fully white by prevailing racial standards), and a Japanese American saxophone player. "They were very unusual for the time in being a mixed race group."
**R&B and Mexican Repertoire** : The band's performance repertoire primarily consisted of R&B songs. However, they would occasionally perform Mexican material, typically when specifically requested for particular events such as weddings. Valens generally did not sing on these Mexican songs due to his limited Spanish proficiency, though he was eventually persuaded to sing "La Bamba." "Their repertoire was apparently largely based around R&B songs, but they would occasionally play Mexican material, usually when requested for a particular event, such as a wedding."
**Local Following and Valens Family Fundraiser** : The Silhouette garnered a significant local following, leading them to organize and promote their own show in January 1958. This specific event was held in a rented hall with the explicit purpose of raising funds to cover Richie Valens' family's mortgage payment for that month. "The silhouettes built up quite a local following. And in January 1958, they played a show that they promoted themselves."
**Initial Recording by Doug Machia** : In January 1958, Doug Machia recorded The Silhouette during a gig using a portable tape recorder. This recording, which is now lost, was later presented to Bob Keane. Although Keane was not impressed with the band as a whole, Valens' performance on the tape caught his attention enough to invite him for an audition. "Machia recorded the silhouettes at the gig, on a portable tape recorder, and took the recording, which is now lost, to Keane."

# curiosity

**Latino Music Culture in Los Angeles** : In Los Angeles, a distinct parallel music culture developed around Latino teenagers, which rarely garnered attention from the broader American public. This subculture was nevertheless a crucial precursor to the emergence of both garage rock and punk as they are known today, with Richie Valens being the first major star to emerge from this vibrant scene. "But there was, in Los Angeles, a whole parallel music culture growing up around Latino teenagers. This subculture only rarely impinged on the consciousness of the wider American public, but without it we would have had no garage rock and no punk as we know them today."
**The Wrecking Crew** : This was an informal collective of highly skilled session musicians who were pivotal in the recording of a vast number of hit records in Los Angeles during the 1960s. Many of the musicians regularly employed by Bob Keane, such as guitarist Renee Hall and drummer Earl Palmer, who worked on Richie Valens' recordings, would later become important members of this influential group. "Many of these musicians would like to go on to be important parts of the wrecking crew. The informal collective of session musicians who played on a huge number of hit records made in LA in the 60s."
**12-Bar Blues Chord Sequence** : A fundamental chord sequence in R&B and rock and roll, the 12-bar blues consists of three chords: the first, fourth, and fifth chords of the major scale. It typically follows a pattern of four bars of the first chord, two bars of the fourth, two more of the first, then one bar each of the fifth and fourth, concluding with two more bars of the first chord. "The 12 bar blues has only three chords in, which are the first, fourth and fifth chords of the major scale."
**One-Four-Five-One Chord Progression** : This is a common three-chord sequence used in much Latin music, arranged as the first, fourth, fifth, and then back to the first chord of the major scale. This progression served as the musical foundation for Richie Valens' first released record, "Come On, Let's Go!". "Again, a lot of Latin music uses those same three chords just arranged in different ways. For example, there's what's known as the one four five one progression."
**Three-Cord Trick (One-Four-Five-Four Progression)** : This specific three-chord sequence, arranged as the first, fourth, fifth, and then fourth chords of the major scale, was popularized by Richie Valens' "La Bamba." While not the first rock'n'roll record to use it (Chuck Berry's "Havana Moon" predated it), "La Bamba" cemented its influence, making it one of the most important chord sequences in rock music and inspiring numerous garage bands and hits like "Hang on Sloopy" and "Twist and Shout." "This one is known as the three-cord trick, and goes one for five for, like this."
**Son Haracho Musical Tradition** : The Son Haracho is a musical style originating from Veracruz, Mexico, of which the folk song "La Bamba" is an example. This tradition is characterized by a blend of Spanish, indigenous, and African musical styles, demonstrating the rich cultural fusion in its composition. "The song Harachio tradition La Bamba is part of is a mixture of Spanish, indigenous, and African styles."
**Bob Keane's View on R&B** : Bob Keane held a belief that only Black people were capable of creating authentic and quality R&B music. This conviction influenced his decision to steer Richie Valens towards a Latin rock sound, despite Valens' initial inclination towards R&B, which he would still record for album tracks. "Keen didn't believe that anyone other than Black people could make good R&B music, and while Valens would record R&B songs as album tracks."
**Bob Keane's Musical Acumen** : Bob Keane, the record company owner who signed Richie Valens, was himself an accomplished musician, noted as a respectable jazz clarinetist. This background lends weight to claims that he actively shaped Valens' songs during recording and editing, especially given Valens' more spontaneous approach to musical structure. "I actually lean towards Keane's claims in this case, because Keane was one of the few record company owners who was himself an accomplished musician, being a fairly respectable jazz climonettist."
