## curiosities
**Early Life and Upbringing** : Mickey Baker had a difficult, impoverished upbringing, with an unknown father and a mother frequently in and out of prison. "Baker had a rough, impoverished upbringing. He didn't know the identity of his father, and his mother was in and out of prison."
**Transition from Jazz to Blues for Financial Gain** : Initially a serious jazz musician playing bebop, Baker shifted his focus to blues music after seeing blues musician Pee Wee Crayton and, more specifically, Crayton's Cadillac. He realized that while blues was technically far easier, it offered significantly more financial reward, stating, "I started bending strings. I was starving to death, and the blues was just a financial thing for me then." "He started out as a serious jazz musician, playing bebop, up until the point he saw the great blues musician, Pee Wee Crater. Or, more precisely, when he saw Crater's Cadillac."
**Sought-After Studio Guitarist** : Mickey Baker became one of the most in-demand studio guitarists in the R&B field, playing lead guitar on notable tracks such as Little Willie John's "Need Your Love So Bad" and contributing to records by Louis Jordan, LeVern Baker, and Ray Charles. "Baker became one of the most sought after studio guitarists in the R&B field, and for example, played the staggering lead guitar, on the jaw of so bad, by Little Willie John."
**Guitar Teacher to Sylvia Van Topoele** : Baker also worked as a guitar teacher, and one of his students was a young woman named Sylvia Van Topoele, who would later become his musical partner in Mickey & Sylvia. "Baker was also a guitar teacher, and one of his students was a young woman named Sylvia Van Topoele."
**Aspiration to Emulate Les Paul and Mary Ford** : Mickey Baker, who started out as a jazz guitarist "very much in the Les Paul mode," had expressed a desire to form his own version of the successful male-female duo Les Paul and Mary Ford. "Mickey Baker, who had started out as a jazz guitarist very much in the Les Paul mode, had wanted to form his own version of Les Paul and Mary Ford."
**Contrasting Personality with Sylvia** : Mickey Baker was described as a rather studious, private man with no interest in stardom or financial success, primarily focused on making music and improving his instrument. This contrasted sharply with Sylvia's razor-sharp business mind, huge ambition, and desire for stardom. "Mickey was a man who had no interest in stardom or financial success. He was a rather studious, private man who just wanted to make music and get better at his instrument, while Sylvia had a razor-sharp business mind, a huge amount of ambition and a desire for stardom."
**Move to Europe Due to Racism** : Mickey Baker moved to Europe because his wife at the time was white, and he no longer wanted to live in the US due to the "particularly virulent form of racism" prevalent at the time. "Mickey was also having problems because his then wife was white, and with the particularly virulent form of racism the US was suffering through at the time, he didn't want to be in the country anymore."
**Academic Pursuits and Composition** : Baker developed a growing interest in the academic side of music, writing "The Complete Course in Jazz Guitar" in 1955, a highly regarded book still available today. In Europe, he returned to jazz, performing with Coleman Hawkins, and studied composition with Iannis Xenakis, writing classical pieces like fugues, a concerto for guitar and orchestra, and "The Blues Suite." "He was also becoming more and more interested in the academic side of music. He had already, in 1955, written a book, The Complete Course in Jazz Guitar, which is still available today and highly regarded."
**Death and Legacy** : Mickey Baker continued performing diverse music across Europe until his death in 2011. He was widely respected for his contributions to blues, jazz, and R&B, and for his influence on the technical proficiency of generations of guitarists. "Baker carried on performing music of all kinds around Europe until his death in 2011. He died massively respected for his contributions to blues, jazz, R&B, and the technical proficiency of generations of guitarists."

## songs

"Originally, they signed with the label called Rainbow Records, a medium-sized indie label in New York, where they put out their first single, I'm so glad."
**Se De Boom Run Dun** : The B-side to their first single, "I'm So Glad," released on Rainbow Records. While also "far from a wonderful song," it contained "the semi-collipse" and "the belights" that would later make them famous. "The B side, though, said a boom-run-dun, while it's also far from a wonderful song, does have the semi-collipse of it and the belights are made them famous."
**Walking in the Rain** : Their first single for Groove Records. It had commercial potential but failed to become a hit because Johnny Ray released a different song with an almost identical title around the same time, leading to customer confusion. "Mickey and Sylvia's first single, Groove, working in the rain, had the potential to be a big hit in the eyes of the record company. But unfortunately for them, Johnny Ray put this out at around the same time."
**Love Is Strange (1956)** : A major hit that reached number 11 on the pop chart and number 1 on the R&B chart. It was co-written by Bo Diddley (lyrics, credited to Ethel Smith) and Jody Williams (music), with Mickey and Sylvia credited as arrangers, presumably for their changes including the famous spoken word section. Bo Diddley later sold his share to Sylvia. The song became a classic, covered by many artists. "Lovey's Strange was a big hit. It went to number 11 in the pop chart, and number 1 on the R&B chart."
**Next Record After Love Is Strange** : Their follow-up record to "Love Is Strange" achieved Top 10 R&B status but only reached number 47 on the pop charts, indicating diminishing returns for their career. "Their next record went top 10 R&B, but only number 47 on the pop charts."
**Dearest** : A "boldly ballad" very much in the same vein as "Love Is Strange," but only reached number 85 on the Hot 100, performing worse than their previous record. Despite its lack of commercial success, it was a favorite of Buddy Holly, who recorded home demos of it. "Even though it was another boldly ballad, very much in the same vein as Lovey's Strange."

## curiosities
**Formation as a Duo** : The duo was formed when Sylvia started taking guitar lessons from Mickey Baker. Different sources attribute the idea to either Joe Robinson (inspired by Jean and Junis) or Mickey Baker (who wanted to emulate Les Paul and Mary Ford). The gimmick was a male-female duo who could both sing and play guitar, focusing on Mickey's string-bending style. "Eventually it was decided that Sylvia should join with her guitar teacher and former duo."
**Onstage Chemistry, Offstage Discord** : Mickey and Sylvia had musical chemistry on stage and recordings, but offstage, they soon began to "great on each other" due to their contrasting personalities and ambitions. "But they worked well as a musical team, even if they were never going to be the best of friends."
**Rainbow Records Signing** : Their first label was Rainbow Records, a medium-sized indie label in New York. Their initial singles, "I'm So Glad" and "Se De Boom Run Dun," did not sell well, but gained some airplay in New York, attracting attention from larger labels. "Originally, they signed with the label called Rainbow Records, a medium-sized indie label in New York, where they put out their first single, I'm so glad."
**Move to Groove Records (RCA Subsidiary)** : Their airplay in New York attracted the attention of Bob Rollant at Groove Records, a subsidiary of RCA established in 1953 as a "fake indie label" to access the R&B market. "But the records did get some airplay in New York, if nowhere else, and that brought them to the attention of Bob Rullant's at Groove Records."
**Recording Team at Groove Records** : At Groove Records, Mickey and Sylvia were paired with saxophonist and arranger King Curtis, whom Mickey already knew from Doc Pomus. Curtis assembled a team of top R&B musicians in New York, many of whom also played on Atlantic sessions. "Valence put them together with the sex-offenist and arranger King Curtis, who Mickey already knew from his work with Doc Pommas, and Curtis put together a team of the best R&B musicians in New York, many of them the same people who would play on most of Atlantic sessions."
**"Love Is Strange" Origin Story** : The song originated from Bo Diddley, who had written and recorded it in May 1956 but not released it. Jody Williams, Bo Diddley's guitarist, played the song for Sylvia, who liked it, leading Mickey and Sylvia to record it and make it a hit. "But at that point, fate intervened in the form of Bo Deadly."
**Post-Hit Decline and RCA Label Closure** : After "Love Is Strange" became a breakthrough hit, RCA controversially closed Groove Records and moved its artists and producer Valence to another subsidiary, Vic Records. This loss of momentum severely affected Mickey and Sylvia's career, leading to diminishing returns on subsequent releases. "But after groove records that had this breakthrough big hit, RCA decided to close the label down and move the acts on the label and their producer Valence to another subsidiary. Vic."
**Diminishing Commercial Returns** : While they released many more records after "Love Is Strange," their sales and chart performance steadily declined. Their next record went Top 10 R&B but only Pop #47, and the one after that, "Dearest," only reached #85 on the Hot 100. "Mickey and Sylvia would release many more records, but they would have diminishing returns."
**Independent Venture: Willow Records** : After being dropped from Vic Records, Mickey and Sylvia temporarily split but later reunited to form their own company, Willow Records, to release their material independently. "After Mickey and Sylvia were dropped along with the rest of the Vic artists. This bled up temporarily, but then got back together to start their own company, Willow Records, to release their own material."
**Production for Ike & Tina Turner** : To return a favor for Ike Turner playing on some of their Willow Records, Mickey and Sylvia agreed to produce a record for Ike & Tina Turner. They suggested "It's Gonna Work Out Fine," a song they had previously recorded for Vic (unreleased), and handled its production and arrangement, with Sylvia playing lead guitar and Mickey providing spoken vocals. "Ag Turner played on some of their records, and to return the favour they agreed to produce a record for Ag and Tina Turner."
**End of Duo and Mickey's Departure** : Their own recordings continued to be unsuccessful, and Mickey faced personal challenges due to his white wife amidst virulent racism in the US, alongside his increasing interest in academic music. These factors led him to move to Europe and pursue jazz and composition, effectively ending the duo's active career. "However, Mickey and Sylvia's recordings under their own name were still having no success, and Mickey was also having problems because his then wife was white, and with the particularly virulent form of racism the US was suffering through at the time, he didn't want to be in the country anymore."

# genre - Blues

## curiosities
**Financial Motivation for Mickey Baker** : For Mickey Baker, blues initially served as a financial opportunity rather than a passion. He transitioned from complex jazz to "bending strings" in blues because it was "far, far easier" and he could make "far more money," as he was "starving to death." "I started bending strings. I was starving to death, and the blues was just a financial thing for me then."
**City Blues vs. Country Blues Attitude** : An informal group of musicians around Atlantic Records, including Mickey Baker, Doc Pomus, Big Joe Turner, and King Curtis, played "sophisticated city blues and R&B." They looked down upon country bluesmen, who are now more widely recognized, as "comparatively unsophisticated musicians," viewing their music as something "anyone could do." "They were playing sophisticated city blues and R&B, and rather looked down on the country bluesmen, who are now much better known, as being comparatively unsophisticated musicians."

# genre - R&B

## curiosities
**Major Label Distribution Problem (1950s)** : Major record labels faced a problem in the 1950s because they had historically ignored R&B records, allowing small indie labels to dominate the genre. This led to an independent network of mostly black-owned shops serving black customers who exclusively sold R&B records and had no interest in major label artists like Doris Day or Frank Sinatra. "For years, none of them had put out R&B records, and the small labels that did put out R&B have been locked out of the distribution networks that the major labels dominated."
**Major Labels' "Fake Indie" Solution** : To rectify the "greatest injustice in the world" (money in the pockets of non-shareholders) and sell to the R&B market, major labels created their own "mini-divisions" or "fake indie labels" (e.g., Groove Records, a subsidiary of RCA). These labels maintained a separate identity to appeal to independent R&B shops and customers who wouldn't buy from the majors directly. "And so, those labels set up their own mini-divisions to sell to those shops. They had different labels because the shops wouldn't buy from the majors, but they were wholly owned subsidiaries. Fake indie labels."

# genre - Hip-hop

## curiosities
**Sylvia Robinson's Pivotal Role (1979)** : Sylvia Robinson (formerly Little Sylvia and Mickey & Sylvia) played a foundational role in the commercialization of hip-hop in 1979. With financing from her husband Joe Robinson's gangster friend Morris Levy, she was instrumental in recognizing and capitalizing on the genre's commercial potential. "Because Sylvia Robinson, who had been little Sylvia, and the woman calling her lover boy, became to hip-hop, what Sam Phillips was to rock and roll."

# label - Groove Records

## curiosities
**RCA Subsidiary Founded (1953)** : Groove Records was established in 1953 as a subsidiary of RCA. It was one of the "fake indie labels" created by major record labels to gain access to the independent R&B distribution networks they had previously been excluded from. "Groove Records was a subsidiary of RCA, set up in 1953."
**Minor Hit with Piano Red (1955)** : Groove Records had a minor hit in 1955 with "Jump Man Jump" by piano player Piano Red, but struggled to achieve significant commercial success afterward until Mickey & Sylvia. "Groove records had had a minor hit in 1955, with the piano player Piano Red and his jump man jump."
**Closure After Breakthrough Hit** : Despite Mickey & Sylvia's "Love Is Strange" becoming a breakthrough hit for the label, RCA controversially decided to close Groove Records. Its acts and producer Valence were transferred to another RCA subsidiary, Vic Records. "But after groove records that had this breakthrough big hit, RCA decided to close the label down and move the acts on the label and their producer Valence to another subsidiary. Vic."

# label - RCA

## curiosities
**Creation of "Fake Indie Labels"** : RCA, like other major labels, created wholly owned subsidiaries like Groove Records (established 1953) as "fake indie labels." This strategy was employed to sell to R&B customers and shops who preferred independent labels and were uninterested in the major labels' mainstream offerings like Doris Day or Frank Sinatra. "And so, those labels set up their own mini-divisions to sell to those shops. They had different labels because the shops wouldn't buy from the majors, but they were wholly owned subsidiaries. Fake indie labels. And Groove was one of them."
**Closure of Groove Records** : Following the breakthrough success of Mickey & Sylvia's "Love Is Strange" on Groove Records, RCA made the decision to close the subsidiary label. The artists and producer were then moved to another RCA subsidiary, Vic Records. "But after groove records that had this breakthrough big hit, RCA decided to close the label down and move the acts on the label and their producer Valence to another subsidiary. Vic."

# label - Vic Records

## curiosities
**Inherited Struggling Talent** : Vic Records was described by producer Valence as having "probably the worst collection of talent in the history of the world" and being "severely in debt" when artists like Mickey & Sylvia were transferred there after Groove Records was closed by RCA. This move led to a loss of career momentum for the transferred artists. "Vic records had, according to Valence, probably the worst collection of talent in the history of the world, and was severely in debt."
**Dropping Artists** : Mickey & Sylvia were among the artists dropped by Vic Records after their commercial success continued to diminish following the closure of Groove Records. "After Mickey and Sylvia were dropped along with the rest of the Vic artists."
