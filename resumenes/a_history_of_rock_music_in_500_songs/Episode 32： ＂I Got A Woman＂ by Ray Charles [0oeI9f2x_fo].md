## songs
**Wondering and Wondering (Early Recordings)** : Ray Charles's very earliest recordings, including "Wondering and Wondering," oddly sounded a lot like his mature style, being almost fully formed in his characteristic sound from the outset. He soon changed his style, however, to be more popular commercially. "His first record, Wondering and Wondering, was almost fully formed mature Ray Charles."
**Mess Around (1953)** : This was Ray Charles's first major venture into the "Atlantic House style" and became his biggest hit to date, making the top five on the R&B charts. The song is credited to Ahmet Ertegun but should more accurately be credited as a traditional song arranged by Ray Charles, Jesse Stone, and Ertegun, who contributed by asking Charles to play in the style of Pete Johnson and then adding words based on traditional floating lyrics to Charles's rendition of Cow Cow Davenport's "Cow Cow Blues" melody. "Messeround, and it should have been me, which Charles as to biggest hits to date, both making the top five on the R&B charts."
**It Should've Been Me (1953)** : This song became one of Charles's biggest hits to date, charting in the top five on the R&B charts. It was recorded with the standard Atlantic musicians and arrangers, similar to other artists like Ruth Brown and LaVern Baker. "Messeround, and it should have been me, which Charles as to biggest hits to date, both making the top five on the R&B charts."
**I Got a Woman (1954)** : This groundbreaking record, widely credited as the first soul record, was released as rock and roll and became Charles's first number one hit on the R&B chart. It was a new fusion of gospel, blues, big band jazz, and early rock and roll, utterly unlike anything recorded before, based on blues lyrics written by his trumpet player Renald Richard and set to a gospel tune by The Southern Tones. The song caused real problems with the gospel community, who viewed it as debasing something holy by using sacred musical styles for secular purposes, but it quickly became a rock and roll standard, covered by Elvis Presley, The Everly Brothers, The Beatles, and The Monkees. "I got a woman went to number one on the R&B chart and quickly entered the stage repertoire of another musician who had church music in his veins."
**This Little Girl of Mine (1955)** : Following the success of "I Got a Woman," Charles continued to rework gospel songs into rock and roll classics, taking the old gospel song "This Little Light of Mine" and transforming it into "This Little Girl of Mine." This demonstrated his formula of fusing sacred and secular music. "As next single, he took the old gospel song, This Little Light of Man. And reworked it into This Little Girl of Man."
**Hallelujah I Love Her So (1956)** : This song is one of Ray Charles's classic hits from a commercially successful period between 1954 and 1956, showcasing his creative vitality during this time. "Classic songs like Hallelujah I Love Her So, Drown in My Own Tears and Lonely Avenue, He hit a dry patch, with such less than stellar efforts as My Bummy and Swany Riffa Rock."

## curiosities
**Birth Name Change** : Ray Charles was born Ray Charles Robinson, but he changed his name when he became a professional musician to avoid confusion with the popular boxer Sugar Ray Robinson, whose peak years coincided with Charles's early career. "His given name was Ray Charles Robinson, but he changed it when he became a professional musician, due to the popularity of the boxer Sugar Ray Robinson, whose peak years were around the same time as Charles's."
**Polyamorous Upbringing** : Charles had an unusual upbringing, being the product of what would now be called a polyamorous relationship, with his father largely absent. He was raised by his mother, whom he called Mama, and his father's wife, whom he called Mother, both women who loved, approved of, and liked each each other. "As far as I can tell from his autobiography, he was the product of what we would now call a polyamorous relationship."
**Blindness and Fierce Independence** : Before reaching adolescence, Charles became totally blind, which profoundly shaped his life and career, fueling a strong sense of independence. He fiercely refused to let his disability define him, often stating his aversion to a dog, a cane, and a guitar, which he saw as common accoutrements for blind men. "That blindness would shape his life, even though, and perhaps because, he had a strong sense of independence."
**Motorbike Riding Despite Blindness** : Demonstrating his extreme independence, Ray Charles not only navigated on his own without a cane or dog but also, at one point, even rode a motorbike by himself. "He not only got about on his own, without a cane or a dog, he also at one point even used to go riding a motorbike by himself."
**Art Tatum's Influence** : From a young age, Charles was fascinated by the piano, and his proficiency intensified, particularly inspired by Art Tatum, a legendary jazz pianist who was also blind. Tatum's musicality and technical skill, for which the computational musicology term "the Tatum" was coined, profoundly influenced Charles's development as a serious student of the instrument and a respectable jazz pianist. "Charles never got quite that good, but he was inspired by Tatum's musicality, and he became a serious student of the instrument, becoming a very respectable jazz pianist."
**Mother's Death and Early Career Start** : When Ray Charles was 15, his mother died, prompting him to leave school and embark on a professional music career, initially touring around Georgia and Florida. "When his mother died, when he was 15, Charles decided to leave school and set up on his own as a musician."
**Early West Coast Attempts** : After initially touring in the South, Charles moved to the West Coast, where he unsuccessfully auditioned to play piano with Lucky Millinder's band and occasionally played jazz with Bumps Blackwell and Dizzy Gillespie. Despite these early jazz associations, playing jazz was not how he would eventually make his name. "He moved to the West Coast, and unsuccessfully auditioned to play piano with Lucky Millen does band, and would occasionally play jazz with Bumps Blackwell and Dizzy Gillespie."
**Nat King Cole and Charles Brown Influence** : In the late 1940s and early 1950s on the West Coast, the dominant style for black musicians was a smooth blues that incorporated crooning, blues, and jazz elements, pioneered by groups like the Nat King Cole Trio and Johnny Moore's Three Blazers featuring Charles Brown. Charles formed his own trio, initially emulating this popular style. "Both of these had very similar styles, featuring a piano player who sang smooth blues with an electric guitarist and a bass player, and sometimes a drummer."
**Formation of The McSon Trio** : Charles formed his own trio, initially named The McSon Trio, with "Son" derived from "Robinson" in his own name and "Muc" from the name of the guitarist. The group quickly changed its name to The Ray Charles Trio as Charles became the obvious star. "Charles formed his own trio, the Muc Sunrio. The Sun came from the Robinson in his own name, the Muc from the guitarist's name."
**Transition to Solo Career** : After a period leading his own trio, Charles grew tired of the responsibilities and went fully solo, opting to travel to gigs on his own and work with local pickup bands instead of maintaining a steady group of musicians, which also allowed him to collaborate with a wider variety of artists. "Charles soon tired of running his own trio though, and went fully solo, traveling to gigs on his own, and working with local pickup bands, rather than having his own steady musicians."
**Lifelong Collaboration with Quincy Jones** : Charles was introduced to Quincy Jones by Bumps Blackwell, initiating a significant and long-lasting collaboration that would span much of the rest of his career, working together in various capacities. "Around this time, Charles was introduced by Bumps Blackwell to Quincy Jones, with whom he would go on to collaborate in various ways from much of the rest of his career."
**Pianist and Musical Director for Lowell Fulson** : For a short period, Ray Charles served as the pianist and musical director for Lowell Fulson, a pioneer of the smooth West Coast blues sound. Although Charles didn't perform on many of Fulson's sessions, his influence can be inferred from the style exemplified by Fulson's biggest record, "Reconsider Baby." "Foulson was one of the pioneers of the smooth West Coast blues sound, and Charles became his pianist and musical director for a short time."
**Key Role in Guitar Slim's "The Things That I Used to Do"** : Ray Charles played a crucial role in the creation of "The Things That I Used to Do," Guitar Slim's most famous record and a blues classic. Charles produced, arranged, and played piano on the track, making it sound far more like his own contemporary work than Slim's other material, despite retaining Slim's recognizable guitar sound. "Charles produced, arranged, and played piano, and the result sounds far more like the work that Charles was doing at the time than it does guitar slimms other work, though it still has slimmed recognizable guitar sound."
**Shift to Unique Style at Atlantic Records** : After experiencing minor successes at Swingtime, Charles moved to Atlantic Records, recognizing that he needed to develop a style uniquely his own rather than sounding like Nat King Cole or Charles Brown, a realization that was crucial for him to achieve first-rank musician status. "While several of the swing time recordings were minor successes, people kept telling him how much he sounded like Nat Cole or Charles Brown, but he realised that it was unlikely that anyone was telling Nat Cole or Charles Brown how much they sounded like Ray Charles, and that he would never be in the first rank of musicians unless he got a style that was uniquely his."
**Singing in His Own Voice** : After recording several records with Atlantic's standard session musicians and arrangers, Charles gained enough confidence in the studio to shed his earlier influences and sing purely as himself, distinguishing his generic R&B music from anyone else's sound. "By this point, he had gained enough confidence in the studio that he was able to sing like himself, not like Charles Brown or Nat Cole or anyone else."
**Gospel as an Inseparable Influence** : Charles realized that the "church" was an integral part of his voice that he could not remove, even if he wished to, becoming natural in his singing by embracing this influence. This realization led to his breakout success by basing a song around a gospel tune, introducing gospel techniques into pop music. "As Charles would later say, the church was something which couldn't be taken out of my voice, even if I had wanted to take it out."
**Count Basie Influenced Band Formation** : Charles formed his own touring band, strongly influenced by the Count Basie band, which, like Lionel Hampton's, was a significant influence on early R&B with its combination of jump band and classy jazz. His band was a slimmed-down version due to touring practicalities, featuring three sax players, piano, bass, drums, two trumpets, and a trombone. "Charles had now formed his own band, which was strongly influenced by Count Basie."
**Addition of The Raelettes** : To complement his band, Charles added a girl group called The Raelettes, composed mostly of former members of another girl band. "And he added a girl group called the Ray Lets, who were mostly former members of a girl band."
**Gospel Community's Criticism of "I Got a Woman"** : The song "I Got a Woman" caused significant problems for Charles within the gospel community, as secular musicians who used musical styles created for worshipping God and turned them to secular purposes were seen as "going over to the side of the devil," a criticism loudly applied to Charles despite his never having been a gospel singer. "Gospel singers who went over to making secular music were considered by their original fans to be going over to the side of the devil."
**Creative Wandering and Commercial Volatility** : Ray Charles did not adhere to a single musical style, and his wandering musical mind made him arguably the most vital creative force in American music for several years. However, this artistic freedom also meant his career swung wildly between periods of significant commercial success and relative failure. "Ray Charles had hit on a formula that any other musician would have happily milked for decades. But Ray Charles wasn't a musician who would just stick to one style of music."
**Future Revolution in Rock and Soul** : After a period of varied commercial success, Ray Charles was poised in 1959 to once again revolutionize both rock and roll and the music he invented, which would come to be known as Soul. "And when we next look at Ray Charles in 1959, we'll see him once again revolutionise both rock and roll and the music he invented. The music that we now call Soul."

**Vocal Style Avoiding Malisma** : Sinatra is cited as one of the great voices of the first part of the 20th century who, almost without exception, would hit the note "dead arm" with one note per syllable, eschewing ornamentation and frills, which was considered good singing at the time. "If you listen to any of the great voices of the first part of the 20th century, Sinatra, Bing Crosby, Tony Bennett, they will almost without exception hit the note dead arm, one note per syllable."

**Malismal Aversion in Early Pop** : In the pop songwriting of the 1930s, 40s, and 50s, particularly for Broadway musicals and crooner repertoire, the use of malisma was strongly disfavored and considered anathema, contrasting sharply with its later acceptance and prominence. # curiosity

**Definition and Historical Perception of Malisma** : Malisma is a technical musical term referring to singing multiple notes to the same syllable of lyric, a practice that has existed since people started singing, such as in "The Star-Spangled Banner." However, among ASCAP-registered pop songwriters of the mid-20th century, malisma was considered "anathema" and "pure laziness," with a strict preference for one note per syllable. "Malisma is a technical musical term, but it has a simple meaning. It's when you sing multiple notes to the same syllable of lyric."
**Malismal Standard in Modern Singing Competitions** : In contemporary singing competitions like "The Voice," "American Idol," or "The X-Factor," a singer's command of malisma – their ability to ornament notes and sing multiple notes per syllable – is the primary way they attempt to impress judges and fit today's standards of good or impressive singing. This represents a significant shift from earlier vocal norms. "The more they can ornament the notes, the more they fit today's standards of good singing."
**Computational Musicology Term "the Tatum"** : The term "the Tatum" exists in computational musicology, defined as the smallest time interval between successive notes in a rhythmic phrase, coined in recognition of jazz pianist Art Tatum's extraordinary proficiency and speed. "Tatum was such a proficient pianist, that there is a term in computational musicology, the Tatum, meaning the smallest time interval between successive notes in a rhythmic phrase."
