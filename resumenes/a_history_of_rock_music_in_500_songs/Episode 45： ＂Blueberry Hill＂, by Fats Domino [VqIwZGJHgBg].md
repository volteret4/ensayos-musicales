## albums
**Rock and Rollin' with Fat Domino (Early 1956)** : This was Fats Domino's inaugural album released by Imperial Records, marking a significant milestone as it was his first album after six years of exclusively recording singles for the label. The release was notable because albums were rarely issued for R&B and blues artists in the 1950s, whose sales were typically directed at jukebox manufacturers. This album was composed almost entirely of his previously released material, primarily hit singles from the years preceding the rock and roll boom. "With him and Blues artists hardly ever put out albums in the fifties."
**Fat Domino Rock and Rollin (April 1956)** : Released only three months after his first album, this follow-up consisted mainly of outtakes and unreleased tracks dating back to 1953. Despite its content, it achieved remarkable commercial success, selling 20,000 copies in its first week upon release in April 1956, an extraordinary accomplishment for an album by a black artist aimed at a teenage audience at that time, especially one featuring only a single hit. "That doesn't sound a lot now but for an album aimed at a teenage audience by a black artist in 1956 and featuring only one hit single that was quite an extraordinary achievement."
## songs
**Ain't That a Shame (Undated, prior to 1956)** : This song served as Fats Domino's breakthrough rock and roll hit, propelling his career and prompting Imperial Records to release his first album. It featured a characteristic bass line common in New Orleans R&B records, which was notably similar to the one later used in "Blueberry Hill." "When we left Domano he had just had his breakout rock and roll hit with Ain't That a Shame."
**My Blue Heaven (1956)** : Representing Domino's initial effort to appeal to country and older pop audiences, this song was a cover of an old standard originally a hit in 1927 for The Crew and Jane Austen. Domino's rendition, which offered a mild R&B flavor, became a top 20 pop hit and was released as a double-sided single with "I'm in the Mood for Love." "His first attempt at doing so became a top 20 pop hit."
**I'm in the Mood for Love (1956)** : This classic standard was featured on one side of a double-sided single, paired with "My Blue Heaven," as part of Fats Domino's strategy to broaden his appeal to older pop and country listeners. It was among several older songs he recorded during this period. "His single releases for the next 18 months or so would include on them such standards as I'm in the mood for love."
**As Time Goes By (1956 or 1957)** : Included on one of Domino's singles, this old standard was part of a formula he employed for approximately 18 months, where one side of each single featured an original song and the other a standard from two to four decades prior. "His single releases for the next 18 months or so would include on them such standards as I'm in the mood for love, as time goes by and when my dreamboat comes home."
**When My Dreamboat Comes Home (1956 or 1957)** : This classic song appeared on Fats Domino's singles as part of his strategy over an 18-month period to combine his original material with older standards. This approach aimed to attract a diverse audience, including those from country and older pop demographics. "His single releases for the next 18 months or so would include on them such standards as I'm in the mood for love, as time goes by and when my dreamboat comes home."
**Honey Child (1956)** : An original composition, "Honey Child" was initially slated to be Fats Domino's next hit and was featured in the exploitation film "Shake, Rattle and Rock." It was recorded during the same studio session where "Blueberry Hill" was attempted. "The song that was meant to be the hit was Honey Child, a new original, which Domino got to feature in an exploitation film called Shake, Rattle and Rock."
**Blueberry Hill (1956)** : This song, originally written in 1940 by Vincent Rose, Larry Stock, and Al Lewis, became Fats Domino's biggest hit, climbing to number two on the pop charts. Despite producer Dave Bartholomew's initial reluctance and attempt to prevent its release, engineer Bunny Robin expertly pieced together a complete version from disparate half-finished takes, aided by drummer Earl Palmer's precise timing. "The song became Domino's biggest hit, rising to number two in the pop charts."
**I'm Ready, I'm Willing, and I'm Able (Undated)** : Co-written by Fats Domino and Al Lewis, this song exemplifies the collaborative efforts that followed Lewis's career resurgence, spurred by the success of Domino's "Blueberry Hill." It is an original composition that reflects the continued development of Domino's music. "Louis made a comeback as an R&B songwriter, co-writing songs for Domino himself."
**Blue Monday (Undated, prior to 1956 remake)** : Originally penned by Dave Bartholomew for Smiley Lewis, Fats Domino's remake of "Blue Monday" became his next major hit after "Blueberry Hill," achieving nearly the same level of success. The song notably featured a distinctive bass line motif frequently used by Bartholomew in his arrangements. "Domino's remake of that song would become his next hit after Blueberry Hill, and almost his biggest success."
## curiosities
**Pre-Rock Star Success** : Fats Domino distinguished himself as the singular star from the pre-rock and roll era of R&B who not only endured but thrived and achieved greater prominence within the emerging rock and roll landscape. He was, alongside Elvis Presley, the most successful artist among the first wave of rock and roll pioneers. "He was the one star from the pre-rock days of Arunbe to last and thrive and even become bigger in the rock and roll era."
**Dominant Black Rock & Roll Star (1956)** : By late 1956, after many white rockabilly acts had faded from the US charts, Fats Domino, alongside Little Richard and Chuck Berry, stood as one of the few black R&B stars consistently delivering rock and roll hits. At this time, the default image for a rock and roll star was a black man with a piano, and Domino was by far the biggest record seller in this category. "But in late 1956 that default image was a black man with a piano and the black man with a piano who was selling the most records by far was Fat Stomano."
**Albums for R&B Artists - Rarity in the 1950s** : In the 1950s, it was highly unusual for R&B and blues artists to release albums, as their records were primarily sold to jukebox manufacturers rather than directly to consumers purchasing LPs. Imperial Records' decision to issue Fats Domino's first album after six years of singles indicated a significant shift in his audience towards white buyers with disposable income. "With him and Blues artists hardly ever put out albums in the fifties."
**Audience Shift to White Consumers** : The introduction of Fats Domino's first album marked a pivotal change in his audience, signifying that he was increasingly selling records to white individuals who had the financial means to purchase LPs. This contrasted sharply with his traditional black audience, who largely continued to buy 78s due to economic constraints. "He was selling to white people with money."
**Racial Context of 1956** : Fats Domino's remarkable commercial success in 1956 occurred against a backdrop of intense racial tension and the burgeoning Civil Rights Movement. Events like the murder of Emmett Till, Rosa Parks' defiance, and escalating white supremacist violence highlighted the societal conflict, making rock and roll's appeal to white teenagers listening to black musicians a revolutionary force. "This, as much as anything musical, is the context you need to understand why rock and roll was seen as so revolutionary in 1956 in particular."
**Concert Riots and Racial Blame** : Throughout 1956, Fats Domino's concerts were frequently marred by riots, which contemporary news reports often attributed to his "pulsating jungle rhythms"—a overtly racist explanation. Despite Domino's efforts to cross over to country and older pop audiences with mellowed standards, the violence persisted, driven by racial prejudices against integrated audiences and music. "The riots which broke out at his shows throughout that year were blamed in contemporary news reports on his pulsating jungle rhythms and there's not even an attempt made to hide the racism in statements like that."
**Medal of Arts Recipient** : Fats Domino was honored with the Medal of Arts by two different U.S. Presidents. He initially received the medal from Bill Clinton, and later a replacement from George W. Bush after his first medal was lost during the devastation of Hurricane Katrina. "You get the Medal of Arts awarded to you by two presidents."
**Hurricane Katrina Tribute Album** : After Hurricane Katrina destroyed his New Orleans home, Fats Domino became the recipient of widespread support from the music community, with acclaimed artists including Elton John, Dr. John, Paul McCartney, and Robert Plant uniting to record a tribute album specifically to raise funds for its rebuilding. "When your home is destroyed in a hurricane, everyone from Elton John to Dr. John, from Paul McCartney to Robert Plant, will come together to record a tribute album to help raise funds to rebuild it."
**Remarkably Non-Rock and Roll Life** : Despite his status as a rock and roll pioneer, Fats Domino led a life that largely deviated from the typical image of rock star excess, save for some drinking and infidelity in the 1950s. He remained married to the same woman for 61 years, rarely traveled beyond his New Orleans home due to a dislike of other foods, and was known for being shy, polite, and genial, avoiding major public scandals. "Other than drinking a bit too much, and sleeping around a little in the 50s, Domino led a remarkably non-rock and roll life."
**Longevity and Career Span** : Fats Domino's career was exceptionally long-lasting, spanning from 1949 until his death in 2017 at the age of 89, encompassing multiple profound revolutions in popular music. His top 30 R&B chart hits alone extended from 1949 through 1964, demonstrating his remarkable ability to remain relevant and successful across changing musical eras, outlasting many of his contemporaries. "His top 30 Arv and B chart hit, spanned from 1949 through to 1964, a career that covers multiple revolutions in music."
**Unacknowledged Legacy** : Despite being, alongside Elvis, the biggest rock and roll star of the 1950s and a profound influence on New Orleans music and artists like Mick Jagger and Leonard Cohen, Fats Domino remains curiously underappreciated in popular consciousness compared to lesser-known figures. This oversight is suggested to stem from racism, either by ignoring him as a black man or because his quiet, polite demeanor did not conform to white prejudices about black artists and their music. "Yet he is curiously unacknowledged in the popular consciousness, while much lesser stars loom larger."

# artist - Dave Bartholomew
## member of
Fats Domino's band (producer, co-writer)
## curiosities
**Producer and Co-Writer for Fats Domino** : Dave Bartholomew served as a producer and co-writer for Fats Domino, contributing original compositions to Domino's singles. He also brought "Treceo rhythms," described as having a "jelly roll, molten, Spanish tinge," to his arrangements, which became a signature element of New Orleans R&B. "One side of each of his singles would be written by Domino or his producer Dave Bartholomew."
**Attempt to Block "Blueberry Hill"** : Initially unenthusiastic about recording "Blueberry Hill," Dave Bartholomew famously declared "we got nothing" at the end of the session and later tried to prevent its release, telling Imperial Records owner Lou Chudd that it would "ruin Domino's career forever." He later admitted this was a "huge mistake," recognizing the song's massive success and stating that his arrangement for it would be his lasting legacy. "When I'm dead and gone a million times, they'll still be playing, that the dad the dad did that."
**Signature Bass Line Motif** : Dave Bartholomew frequently incorporated a distinctive bass line motif into his arrangements, which became a staple of New Orleans R&B. He used this bass line in "Ain't That a Shame" and also in "Blue Monday," a song he wrote for Smiley Lewis. "It seems to have been taken up by Bartholomew as a signature motif."
**Death (June 2022)** : Dave Bartholomew, the long-time collaborator and producer for Fats Domino, passed away in June of the current year (relative to the podcast's original broadcast), at the remarkable age of 100. He left behind one of the finest legacies in the histories of rock and roll, rhythm and blues, and New Orleans music. "His collaborator Dave Bartholomew died in June this year, aged 100."

# genre - R&B
## curiosities
**Albums Rarity in the 1950s** : In the 1950s, albums were an uncommon format for R&B artists, whose record sales primarily went to jukebox manufacturers rather than direct consumer purchases of LPs. This trend shifted with artists like Fats Domino, whose album releases signaled a new audience demographic. "With him and Blues artists hardly ever put out albums in the fifties."
**Dominance in Late 1956 Rock and Roll** : By late 1956, black R&B stars such as Little Richard, Chuck Berry, and Fats Domino, along with Elvis Presley, constituted the primary rock and roll artists who were still achieving significant chart success in the US, following the decline of white rockabilly acts. "If we're talking about actual rock and roll hits rising to decent positions in the charts, by late 1956 you're looking at acts like Little Richard, Chuck Berry and Fat Stomano with only Elvis left of the rock-a-billy's."

# genre - Rock and roll
## curiosities
**First Wave Decline (1956)** : By late 1956, the initial wave of white rockabilly acts that had gained prominence in 1955 and 1956 had largely fizzled out on the US charts, with artists like Carl Perkins, Gene Vincent, and Bill Haley no longer having major domestic hits. "The wave of white rock-a-billy acts that had hit throughout 1955 and 56 had all fizzled."
**Image Shift to Black Man with Piano (Late 1956)** : In late 1956, the default image for a rock and roll star was a black man with a piano, primarily due to the immense record sales and popularity of artists like Fats Domino, a notable contrast to the later "white man with guitar" image. "But in late 1956 that default image was a black man with a piano and the black man with a piano who was selling the most records by far was Fat Stomano."
**Revolutionary Context (1956)** : Rock and roll was seen as profoundly revolutionary in 1956, largely because it facilitated racial integration through music, with white teenagers actively listening to and imitating black musicians at a critical juncture in the civil rights struggle. "This, as much as anything musical, is the context you need to understand why rock and roll was seen as so revolutionary in 1956 in particular."
**Worldwide Chart Success (1956)** : In 1956, Fats Domino's "Blueberry Hill" achieved the distinction of being the biggest rock and roll hit worldwide, surpassing the global chart positions of even Elvis Presley's prominent songs like "Hound Dog" and "Heartbreak Hotel." "Worldwide, Blueberry Hill was the biggest rock and roll hit of 1956, outdoing even Elvis's Hound Dog and Hard Break Hotel in Worldwide Chart positions."

# concert - Roanoke, Virginia
## curiosities
**Segregation and Riot (May 1956)** : A Fats Domino concert in Roanoke in May 1956 featured strict racial segregation, with white attendees in the balcony and black attendees on the main floor. When white teenagers descended to dance with black people, a white individual in the balcony initiated a riot by throwing a whiskey bottle, which then escalated into street violence. The New York Times controversially blamed the black audience, leading the venue owner to ban mixed-race audiences thereafter. "In Ruanok, Virginia, for example, in May, Domino was playing to a segregated crowd. Whites in the balcony, black people on the floor."

# concert - Houston
## curiosities
**Segregation and Dance Prohibition Incident (Undated)** : At a Fats Domino concert in Houston, white audience members from the balcony moved to join black dancers on the floor. A black policeman, attempting to avert a race-mixing riot, ordered everyone to sit down and stop dancing. However, a white police officer overruled him, declaring that only white people were permitted to dance. Domino refused to continue playing if black people were not also allowed to dance, and while the show avoided violence, a dozen people were arrested for threatening the police. "In Houston, Domino played another show where white people were in the balcony and black people were on the dance floor below."

# instrument - Bass line
## curiosities
**New Orleans R&B Staple** : A distinctive bass line style, characterized by "jelly roll, molten, Spanish tinge" and Treceo rhythms, was a common feature on New Orleans R&B records and became a staple of the genre. It was famously utilized by Fats Domino in "Ain't That a Shame" and "Blueberry Hill," and adopted by Dave Bartholomew as a signature motif in arrangements for artists like Smiley Lewis. "The bass line here is not one that was used on any of the previous versions, but it was common on New Orleans R&B records."
**Copyrightability Dispute** : A specific bass line, initially developed by Jimmy Yancey and popularized by Meade Lux Lewis, became the subject of a copyright infringement lawsuit between Lewis and Sunni Thompson. The court ultimately ruled that such a simple musical idea could not, by itself, be copyrighted, and noted Lewis's prior public release of the composition without registration. "One was that the bass line was such a simple idea that it couldn't by itself be copyrightable."

# curiosity
**"White Man with Guitar" Default Image (Mid-1957)** : Although the default image for a rock and roll star in late 1956 was a black man with a piano, a subsequent "second wave of white rock and rollers" rapidly emerged. This new wave fundamentally altered the music landscape, leading to the "white man with guitar" becoming the standard image for a rock and roll star by mid-1957. "by the time we get to mid-1957 we'll be in a period where white man with guitar is the default image for a rock and roll star."
**1950s Record-Buying Demographics** : Despite the prevalent popular image of the 1950s being defined by teenage culture and "ponytail bobby socks," a substantial portion of the record-buying public comprised older individuals who preferred gentler music over rock. This demographic's influence was evident in Doris Day's "Que Sera, Sera" outperforming all rock and roll hits in worldwide chart positions in 1956. "However much our popular image of the 1950s is based on ponytail bobby socks, the fact remains that the sizable proportion of the record-bying public were older and less inclined to rock than to gently sway."
**Civil Rights Struggle Context (1955-1956)** : The mid-1950s represented a pivotal and volatile period in the civil rights struggle, marked by significant events such as the murder of Emmett Till in August 1955, Rosa Parks' refusal to yield her seat in December 1955, and escalating white supremacist terrorism, including the firebombing of churches and the homes of civil rights leaders like Dr. Martin Luther King. This social ferment provided a crucial backdrop for understanding the revolutionary nature of rock and roll at the time. "The murder of Emmett Till, supposedly for whistling at a white woman, had been in August 1955."
**Dave Bartholomew's Death** : Dave Bartholomew, the renowned collaborator and producer for Fats Domino, passed away in June of the year the transcript was recorded (aged 100), leaving behind a monumental legacy that shaped the histories of rock and roll, rhythm and blues, and New Orleans music.
