## albums
**Bob Dylan (1962) - Debut Album** : Dylan's first album, recorded quickly in a couple of days, contained only two original songs. Producer John Hammond found Dylan difficult to work with due to his microphone technique and refusal to do second takes for many songs. The album was not a commercial success and was known within Columbia Records as "Hammond's folly," but it did not lose money due to its swift recording. Dylan and Hammond later spoke poorly of it, though it is regarded by some as one of the best outputs from the Greenwich Village folk scene. "Hammond's track record was good enough that he was allowed to get on with it and put out an album."
## songs
**Let Me Die In My Footsteps** : This was Dylan's first truly successful song, a composition about nuclear war. When performing it, he would introduce it as a Weavers' song, despite it being his own creation. "His first truly successful song, a song about nuclear war, let me die in my footsteps. He would introduce as a weaver's song."
**Talking New York** : One of two original songs on Dylan's debut album, this was a comedy talking blues. It humorously described his early experiences in New York, closely emulating the style of Woody Guthrie's talking blues songs. "One, talking New York, was a comedy talking blues about his early time in New York, very much in the style of woody gut-free's talking blues songs."
**Song to Woody (1962)** : The second original song on Dylan's debut album, this track was a rewrite of his earlier song written for Bonnie, which itself was based on Woody Guthrie's "1913 Massacre." It served as a poignant tribute to Woody Guthrie, Dylan's most significant musical influence, marking his acknowledgment of moving on from that influence while still expressing gratitude. "The song is a touching one, Dylan paying tribute to his single biggest influence."
**Song for Bonnie** : This early song, only preserved as a lyric, was written by Dylan for his girlfriend, Bonnie (Jahanara Romney). It was set to the same tune as Woody Guthrie's "1913 Massacre" and featured lyrics like, "Hey, hey, Bonnie, I'm singing to you now. The song I'm singing is the best I know how." "one which we only have as a lyric was written to a girlfriend, and was set to the same tune we just heard. Guthrie is 1913 Massacre."
## curiosities
**Birth and Early Life** : Born Robert Zimmerman in the Minnesota Iron Range, an area experiencing economic decline after World War II. He was born in Duluth but moved to the smaller town of Hibbing when very young. "Rob Dylan was born Robert Zimmerman in the Minnesota Iron Range, an area of the US that was just beginning a slow descent into poverty, as the country suddenly needed a lot less metal after the end of World War II."
**Early Musical Fascination** : As a child, he was initially captivated by Johnny Ray's music, particularly Ray's hits from the early 1950s. This was followed by an obsession with Hank Williams' songwriting and a fandom for country singer Hank Snow. "He was first captivated by Johnny Ray."
**Ambition to Join Little Richard's Band** : Dylan taught himself rudimentary piano in the style of Little Richard. His ambition, as noted in his high school yearbook, was to become a member of Little Richard's touring band. "his ambition, as quoted in his high school yearbook, was to join Little Richard's band."
**Witnessing Buddy Holly's Last Tour** : He attended the penultimate date of Buddy Holly, Richie Valens, and The Big Bopper's ill-fated tour, later claiming to have seen a halo over Holly's head during the performance. "He later claimed to have seen a halo over Holly's head during the performance."
**Brief Stint with Bobby Vee** : Dylan's first indirect brush with fame came when he joined Bobby Vee's band for a short period, playing piano under the stage name Elston Gunnn. He was reportedly not very good at piano, only able to play in C. While in the band, he sometimes pretended to be Bobby Vee. "Dylan joined for his band for a short while, under the stage name Elstungun, playing the piano, though he was apparently not very good."
**Reinvention at University** : Robert Zimmerman transformed into Bob Dylan upon entering university in Minneapolis. He adopted a new name, possibly inspired by Marshall Dylan from the TV series "Gunsmoke" or the Welsh poet Dylan Thomas, ceased his studies to pursue music and women, and embraced a new musical style. "it wasn't until he went to university in Minneapolis, the Robert Zimmerman became Bob Dylan, and changed everything about his life."
**Odetta Epiphany** : Dylan experienced a musical epiphany in a record store listening booth after hearing an album by folk singer Odetta, likely her first album of traditional songs. This led him to sell his electric guitar, buy an acoustic, and learn Odetta's and Lead Belly's repertoires. "The way he tells it he had an epiphany and a record-shop listening booth listening to an album by the folk singer Odetta."
**Discovery of Woody Guthrie** : At Link Casna's apartment, Dylan encountered a collection of recordings by Woody Guthrie, which immediately profoundly impacted him, alongside hearing the "Spirituals to Swing" album. "It was a collection of recordings by Woody Guthrie."
**Guthrie Persona Adoption** : Upon hearing Woody Guthrie's music, Dylan visibly reacted by deciding to emulate Guthrie completely, adopting his playing style, singing style, and even his accent. "He decided that he had to become Muddy Guthrie, and took on Guthrie's playing and singing style, even his accent."
**Influence of Ramblin' Jack Elliott** : After learning that Ramblin' Jack Elliott was already renowned for imitating Woody Guthrie, Dylan modulated his approach, incorporating aspects of Elliott's persona into his own, intending to be the "next Woody Guthrie" by deeply inhabiting his persona and repertoire. "Dylan was shocked that there was someone out there doing the same thing, but then he just took on aspects of Elliott's persona as well as Guthrie's."
**Meeting Woody Guthrie (1961)** : In January 1961, Dylan moved to New York with the goal of meeting Woody Guthrie, who was severely ill with Huntington's disease and hospitalized. Dylan visited Guthrie regularly, playing his own songs for him and occasionally joining family visits. These months of interaction deeply affected Dylan. "In January 1961, he decided he was going to go to New York, and while he was there, he was going to go and meet Woody Guthrie in person."
**Early Greenwich Village Gigs** : Dylan's first performances in Greenwich Village were on the "basket house" circuit, where musicians played for donations. He initially performed on bills curated by Fred Neil. "Gullens' first gigs were on this circuit, playing on bills put together by Fred Neal, a musician who was at this point mostly playing blues songs, but who within a few years would write some of the great classics of the 60s singer-songwriter genre."
**Political Affiliation in Folk Scene** : Dylan maintained a presence in both sides of the folk movement's political factions through his connection with Woody Guthrie and his friends. He published lyrics in magazines like Broadside and Sing Out, which were associated with the communist side of the folk movement. "Dylan had a foot in both camps through his association with Guthrie and Guthrie's friends."
**Mentor Relationship with Dave Van Ronk** : Dylan actively sought out Dave Van Ronk, a leading figure in the blues and ballads folk group, to imitate. He frequently stayed at Van Ronk's home and was briefly managed by Van Ronk's wife, learning extensively from him for about six crucial months. Despite a later parting of ways, they remained friendly, and Dylan consistently acknowledged Van Ronk's mentorship. "Dylan consciously sought him out as the person to imitate on the scene, and he was soon regularly sleeping on Van Runk's couch, and being managed for a brief time by Van Runk's wife."
**Early Performing Talent** : Initially, Dylan's talent was more recognized for his performing ability rather than his songwriting. Audiences were captivated by his personal charisma, humor, and particularly his astonishing singing, which combined influences from R&B records and Guthrie's phrasing. "One comparison you see time and again when people talk about his early performances, his Charlie Chaplin, and his singing."
**Breakthrough Catalysts** : His breakthrough was facilitated by multiple connections: Carla Rotolo, Alan Lomax's assistant and his girlfriend Susie Rotolo's sister, recorded Dylan and wrote to John Hammond. Simultaneously, John Hammond Jr., a friend of Dylan, mentioned him to his father. Additionally, folk critic Robert Shelton of "The New York Times" provided a rave review for Dylan's performance at Gerde's Folk City. "The Ratollo's played a part in Dylan's big breakthrough."
**Robert Johnson Influence** : John Hammond gave Dylan a copy of Robert Johnson's "King of the Delta Blues Singers" before its public release. While Dave Van Ronk found Johnson's musical originality lacking, Dylan was deeply affected by Johnson's sparse, economical performance style and ability with words, seeing Johnson as a relatable figure rather than the mythical "crossroads devil" figure. Dylan later tried to incorporate Johnson's style into his songwriting. "What got to Dylan was Johnson's performance style, but also his ability with words."

**Outlaw Songs (various)** : Guthrie wrote songs that took the forms of old folk and country songs, reworking lyrics to create pieces about contemporary events, including famous outlaws seen as "Robin Hood type figures." "There were songs about famous outlaws, V-Caster's Robin Hood type figures."
**Talking Blues (various)** : Woody Guthrie was known for writing "talking blues" with comedy lyrics. These were a significant influence on Bob Dylan, whose early original "Talking New York" was written in this style. "The were talking blues with comedy lyrics."
**Dust Bowl Ballads (various)** : Famous songs written by Guthrie about the devastating Dust storms of the 1930s in the American West, depicting the destruction and hardship. One notable example recounted the first storm on April 14, 1935. "The were the famous Dust Bowl ballads about the Dust storms that had caused so much destruction and hardship in the west."
**This Land Is Your Land** : A radical song by Guthrie, intended as a critique of private property, emphasizing that land should be accessible to all. It has been adopted as an anthem by diverse groups, some of whom may not align with Guthrie's original political intent. "A radical song about how private property is immoral and unnatural, which has been taken up as an anthem by people who would despise everything that God three stood for."
## curiosities
**Pivotal in American Folk Music** : Woody Guthrie was a pivotal figure in the development of American folk music in the 1930s, particularly noted for politicizing the genre. "Guthrie is someone we've only mentioned in passing so far, but he was pivotal in the development of American folk music in the 1930s, and in particular he was important in the politicisation of that music."
**Early Career as Country Singer** : He began his career as a country singer, drawing inspiration from 1920s greats like The Carter Family and Jimmie Rodgers. "Guthrie had started out as a country singer, singing songs inspired by the Carter family, Jimmy Rogers, and other 1920s greats."
**Journey to California (1936)** : After years of odd jobs in Oklahoma and Texas, Guthrie traveled to California in 1936 in search of work, where he connected with his cousin, Jack Guthrie. "But then in 1936 he travelled out to California in search of work."
**The Oki and Woody Show** : Woody and his cousin Jack Guthrie performed together with a group called the Beverly Hillbillies and had their own radio show called "The Oki and Woody Show," which was not successful. "They performed with a group called the Beverly Hill Belize and got their own radio show, the Oki and Woody show, but it wasn't successful"
**The Woody and Lefty Act** : After Jack Guthrie quit, Woody continued performing with Maxine Christman, known as Lefty Lou from Old Mizzou. Their duo became "hugely popular" until Lefty quit due to failing health, which indirectly benefited Guthrie's career. "The Woody and Lefty act became hugely popular, but Lefty eventually also quit due to her health failing."
**Radicalization and Newspaper Work** : After Lefty Lou left, the left-wing owner of their radio station, who also ran a left-wing newspaper, hired Guthrie to write columns about the plight of Oki migrants. Spending time with these impoverished and discriminated people radicalized Guthrie. "Guthrie went and spent time with those people, and his shock at the poverty they were living in and the discrimination they were suffering seems to have radicalised him."
**Communist Sympathies** : Guthrie associated with members of the Communist Party but apparently never formally joined, being more interested in supporting "victims against the bullies" than Marxist theory. He initially adopted the communist stance on World War II as a capitalist fight until Russia joined, then became an anti-fascist. "He started hanging around with members of the Communist Party, though he apparently never joined."
**Move to New York** : Losing his newspaper job due to his employer's stronger anti-fascist views, Guthrie moved to New York and joined a group of left-wing intellectuals and folk singers, including Alan Lomax, Pete Seeger, and Lead Belly, at Armanac House in Greenwich Village. "Guthrie lost his newspaper job, and he decided to move across the country to New York, where he hooked up with a group of left-wing intellectuals and folk singers"
**Most Important Folk Singer** : Guthrie was considered the most important of the left-wing folk singers centered in Armanac House, influencing groups like The Armanac Singers and The Weavers. "And Guthrie had been the most most important of all of them."
**Huntington's Disease** : By January 1961, Guthrie was severely ill with Huntington's disease, a genetic disorder that also claimed his mother. The disease caused dementia, spasmodic movements, and loss of body control. He had been in a psychiatric hospital since 1956, with Sunday visits to family. "Guthrie was, by this time, severely ill. He had Huntington's disease, a truly awful genetic disorder that had also killed his mother."
**Mentorship of Ramblin' Jack Elliott** : Guthrie was a mentor and "apprentice master" to Ramblin' Jack Elliott, who lived with him and learned everything from him, enabling Elliott to become a significant figure in the British folk and skiffle scenes. "Elliott at Napaas, the son of a surgeon, had become Ramblin Jack the singing in Cowboy, and had been an apprentice of Guthrie, living with him and learning everything from him"

# artist - Harry Smith
## genres
American Folk Music (compiler)
Blues (compiler)
Country (compiler)
Gospel (compiler)
## labels
Folkways Records
## albums
**Anthology of American Folk Music (1952) - Groundbreaking Compilation** : Released in 1952 by Harry Smith and Folkways Records owner Moses Asch, this six-album set was considered "the most important bootleg ever." Smith, a record collector, experimental filmmaker, and occultist, compiled it from his vast collection of old 78 records, mostly commercial issues from the late 1920s and early 1930s. The compilation spanned genres from hard blues to country and gospel, suggesting a unique American Roots music tradition. It became the "core repertoire for every American folk musician of the 50s" and crystallized the folk revival movement. The labels from which the original 78s were sourced did not protest the release, as there was no market for back catalog at the time. "Harry Smith's anthology of American folk music... became the core repertoire for every American folk musician of the 50s, the seed around which the whole movement crystallised."
## curiosities
**Record Collector and Occultist** : Harry Smith was a record collector and experimental filmmaker, known for his vast collection of old 78 records. He was also a follower of the occultist Alistair Crowley, and his "Anthology of American Folk Music" was at least partly conceived as a magical work, with esoteric symbolism in its album covers. "Harry Smith was a record collector, experimental filmmaker, and follower of the occultist Alistair Crowley."

# artist - Robert Johnson
## genres
Blues
Polka (enjoyed playing)
## labels
Columbia Records (later reissue)
## albums
**King of the Delta Blues Singers (1961) - Reissue Album** : This reissue of old blues records by Robert Johnson was given to Bob Dylan by John Hammond before its public release. Its release transformed Robert Johnson into a "systemic figure" among white blues fans in the 1960s. The album was critical in establishing Johnson's posthumous reputation, as he was largely forgotten by most blues musicians and listeners prior to its release. "Hammond also gave Dylan an album to take home and listen to, a record which hadn't come out yet, a v-issue of some old blues records called King of the Delta Blues Singers by Robert James."
## curiosities
**Mythology vs. Reality** : Johnson is widely perceived as the most important and influential blues musician of his generation, influencing blues and R&B, but this is a popular myth. He was influential on white musicians in the 1960s, but not on black musicians in the 1930s, 40s, and 50s. His lifetime best-selling record sold only around 5,000 copies. "There's a popular perception that Johnson was the most important blues musician of his generation, and that he was hugely influential on the development of blues and album B, and it simply falls."
**Limited Commercial Success** : In his lifetime, Robert Johnson's best-selling record sold approximately 5,000 copies, a very modest number that does not align with his later legendary status. "In his lifetime, his best selling record sold around 5,000 copies"
**Forgotten Before Reissue** : For the 23 years between his death and the release of "King of the Delta Blues Singers," most blues musicians and listeners would not have recognized Robert Johnson's name, possibly confusing him with the much more famous Lonnie Johnson. "if you'd asked most blues musicians or listeners about Robert Johnson in the 23 years between his death and the King of the Delta Blues singers album coming out. Most of them would have looked at you blankly, or maybe asked if you meant Lonnie Johnson"
**Missed Spirituals to Swing Concerts** : John Hammond wanted Johnson to perform at the 1938 "Spirituals to Swing" concerts but discovered Johnson had died shortly before. Big Bill Broonzy played in his place. "Hammond had wanted to get Johnson to perform at the spirituals to swing concerts, only to find that Johnson had died only a short time earlier."
**Remarkable Musical Mind** : Despite the myths, Johnson was a truly great musician, a versatile and skilled guitarist and arranger with a broader musical palette than his recorded legacy suggests. "While the myth of Robert Johnson has almost no connection to the real man, his music demonstrated a remarkable musical mind."
**Diverse Musical Tastes** : Ramblin' Johnny Shines, who traveled with Johnson, reported that Johnson enjoyed playing a variety of songs including "Yes Sir That's My Baby," Jimmie Rodgers songs, and polkas, even calling him a "polka-hound man." "Ramblin Johnny Shines, who travelled with Johnson for a time, described him as particularly enjoying playing songs like Yes Sir That's My Baby, and Jimmy Rogers songs, and Polkas, calling Johnson a polka-hound man."
**Sparse Lyrical Style** : Johnson possessed a sparse, economical lyrical style that deeply resonated with Bob Dylan on a primal level. "Johnson had a very sparse, economical, living style, which connected with Dylan on a primordial level."
**Crossroads Myth Origin** : The popular myth of Johnson selling his soul to the devil at a crossroads for guitar skills was originally associated with a different Mississippi bluesman, Tommy Johnson, and there's no contemporary evidence it was believed about Robert Johnson. "Though that's a myth that was originally told about a different Mississippi blues man, called Tommy Johnson, and there's no evidence that anyone thought that of him at the time."
**Dylan's Realistic Perception** : In his autobiography "Chronicles," Bob Dylan described Johnson as a "bothersome kid who played harmonica," later learning guitar from records rather than live performers, a portrayal closer to reality than the prevailing legend. Dylan was delighted to later learn Johnson's guitar teacher was named Hank Zimmerman. "Dylan's version of Johnson is closer to the reality, as far as we know it, than the Johnson of legend is, and Dylan seems to have been delighted when he found out much later that the name of the musician who taught Johnson to play guitar was Hank Zimmerman."

# curiosity

**The 1960s Beginning** : The year 1962 is identified as the true beginning of the 1960s, setting the stage for the emergence of influential musicians of the decade. "1962 is the year when the 60s really started"
**Nobel Prize for Literature in Music** : Bob Dylan is recognized as the only songwriter ever to win the Nobel Prize for Literature, highlighting his profound impact on the literary and musical worlds. "the only songwriter ever to win the Nobel Prize for Literature"
**The American Folk Music Tradition** : This tradition was largely overlooked in early discussions of rock and roll, with its major figures only significantly entering the rock and roll sphere with Bob Dylan's advent. "We've barely looked at the American folk music tradition and largely ignored the musicians who were major figures in it, because those figures only really enter into rock and roll in a real way, starting with Dylan."
**Greenwich Village as Bohemian Center** : In the early 1960s, Greenwich Village in New York was a vibrant bohemian hub, attracting artists, intellectuals, and radicals like Allen Ginsberg, Moondog, and Tiny Tim, and serving as the focal point for the second great folk revival. "When not visiting Guthrie, Dylan was spending his time in Greenwich Village, the Bohemian Centre of New York."
**Armanac House** : Located in Greenwich Village, Armanac House was a central gathering place for a group of left-wing intellectuals and folk singers, including Alan Lomax, Pete Seeger, and Lead Belly. This environment was crucial to the formation of The Armanac Singers and later The Weavers. "It was this environment, centered in Armanac House in Greenwich Village, that spawned the Armanac singers and later the weavers"
**Huntington's Disease** : A severe genetic disorder that afflicted Woody Guthrie and his mother, causing dementia, spasmodic movements, and loss of bodily control. Guthrie was hospitalized for it from 1956. "He had Huntington's disease, a truly awful genetic disorder that had also killed his mother."
**The Second Great Folk Revival (1952)** : This revival began in 1952 with the release of Harry Smith's "Anthology of American Folk Music." It revitalized interest in uniquely American roots music and became a core repertoire for 1950s folk musicians. "It was also the centre of what was becoming the second great folk revival. That revival had been started in 1952, when the most important bootleg ever was released, Harry Smith's anthology of American folk music."
**Record Industry Boom and Depression (1920s-30s)** : The late 1920s and early 1930s saw a temporary boom in the record industry before the onset of the Depression, during which many of the 78 records compiled in Harry Smith's anthology were commercially issued. "all of them things that had been issued commercially in the late 1920s and early 30s, when the record industry had been in a temporary boom before the Depression."
**Market for Back Catalogue (Pre-1950s)** : In the era prior to the 1950s, there was not really a market for back catalog records, which is why major labels did not protest Harry Smith's reissuing of their older recordings on "Anthology of American Folk Music." "But at this time, there was not really such a thing as a market for back catalogue, and none of the record labels involved seemed to have protested."
**Basket Houses of Greenwich Village** : These were coffee shops in Greenwich Village where folk musicians performed without charge for entry or artist payment. Instead, performers passed a basket for audience donations. Initially, folk musicians were booked to clear out poetry audiences, but later gained their own following. "Those folkies had started playing at coffee shops in Greenwich Village, places that were known as basket houses, because they didn't charge for entry or pay the artists, but the performers could pass a basket and split whatever the audience decided to donate."
**Poetry as Main Entertainment** : In early Greenwich Village coffee shops, poetry was the primary entertainment, with folk musicians initially serving as interstitial acts to clear the room for new customers, before gaining popularity. "The main entertainment for those coffee shops was poetry, and the audience for poetry would mostly buy a single coffee and make it last all night."
**Greenwich Village Folk Scene Factions** : By the time Bob Dylan arrived, the folk scene in Greenwich Village was segmented into multiple factions: Stalinists (around Pete Seeger), Zionists (singing "Hava Nagila"), bluegrass players, and groups singing old folk ballads and blues (which attracted Dylan). "By the time Dylan hit the scene, there were quite a few very good musicians in the village, and the folk scene had grown to the point that there were multiple factions."
**Philosophical Disagreement in Folk Scene** : A core disagreement existed between Pete Seeger's "Stalinist" camp, which prioritized a song's social good over its musical quality, and the blues and ballad singers, who believed music was paramount and any social benefit a byproduct. "Seagher's camp though that the quality of a song was secondary to the social good it could do, while the blues and ballad singers held at the important thing with the music, and any political or social good was a nice byproduct."
**The Folklore Centre** : A record and bookshop in Greenwich Village owned by Izzy Young, serving as a crucial hub where folk musicians, poets, and scene participants gathered to buy materials, exchange fanzines, and discover new artists. **Broadside Magazine** : A magazine dedicated to new, topical songs, where Bob Dylan began publishing some of his lyrics. It was associated with the communist side of the folk movement. "Dylan started publishing some of his song lyrics in Broadside, a magazine for new, topical songs"

**Robert Johnson's Crossroads Myth** : A popular myth states that Robert Johnson sold his soul to the devil at a crossroads in exchange for guitar-playing ability. This myth, however, was originally attributed to another bluesman, Tommy Johnson, and has no contemporary evidence linking it to Robert Johnson. "The myth commonly told about him is that he sold his soul at a crossroads to the devil, in return for the ability to play the guitar."
