## songs
**Be-Bop-A-Lula (1956)** : This song, initially released as the B-side to "Woman Love," became Gene Vincent's most memorable hit of the 1950s. Its title has multiple origin stories, including being inspired by the 1920s vaudeville song "Don't Ring Lulu" (per Donald Graves), a "Little Lulu" comic book (per Tex Davis, who is credited as co-writer but widely believed not to have contributed to the writing), or most likely Helen Humes' song "Be-Baba-Leba," later re-recorded by Lionel Hampton as "Hey Baba Reba." Lyrically, it's described as "absolute nonsense" and "gibberish," but its sound was pivotal, being the "precise midpoint" between Elvis Presley's "Heartbreak Hotel" and Little Richard's "Tutti Frutti," and is said to epitomize 1956. During its recording, drummer Dickie Harrell famously let out a scream mid-song so his mother would know he was on the record, a take that was ultimately used. "Be-Bop-A-Lula is by any standards, a quite astonishing record."
**Woman Love (1956)** : This track was originally chosen as the A-side for Gene Vincent's debut single, with initial confidence that it would be a hit. However, most radio stations refused to play it, citing that the lyrics were either obscene or that the record itself was. Ken Nelson, the producer, had to review the lyric sheet and was satisfied that Vincent could have been singing "hooking," alleviating initial concerns about explicit content. "Most radio stations refused to play Woman Love at all, saying that even if the Levit's weren't obscene, and plenty of people were convinced that they were, the record itself still was."
**Race with the Devil (1956)** : This song was the follow-up single to "Be-Bop-A-Lula" and is considered one of the "all-time great rockabilly records." Despite its musical quality, its lyrics, which described a hot-rod race with the actual devil, were deemed "un-broadcastable" by radio stations, similar to "Woman Love." Unlike its predecessor, it had no major hit hidden on its B-side to salvage its commercial performance, reaching only the lower echelons of the Top 100 in the USA but becoming a big hit in the UK. "Race with the devil... was one of the all-time great rockabilly records, but the lyrics about a hot-rod race with the actual devil were, like woman love, considered un-vulcastable..."
**Blue Jean Bop (1956)** : Released after "Race with the Devil," this single performed moderately better. It managed to reach the lower reaches of the Top 50 in the USA and successfully broke into the Top 20 in the UK, a significant improvement over its predecessor's chart performance in the US. "Blue Jean Bop did a little better, reaching the lower reaches of the top 50, rather than the lower reaches of the top 100 as race with the devil had, and making the top 20 in the UK."
**Lotta Lovin' (1957)** : Written by the obscure songwriter Bernie Spetwell, whose publishing rights were bought by Texas oilman Tom Flieger, this song was quickly recorded at Capitol's new studio in the Capitol Tower in Hollywood. With "Wear My Wings" as its B-side, "Lotta Lovin'" climbed to number 13 on the pop chart and number 7 on the R&B chart, offering a brief period of revitalization for Gene Vincent's career. "Lotta Lovin' went to number 13 on the pop chart, and number 7 on the R&B chart, and it looked like it would revitalise Jean's career, but it was not to be."
**Wear My Wings (1957)** : This ballad served as the B-side to Gene Vincent's "Lotta Lovin'" single. It was co-written by Warren Kosouto, who would later become known as Bobby Darin, and Don Kirshner.
**Opalazy River** : This was one of several "old standards" suggested by Ken Nelson for Gene Vincent to record. While Vincent personally enjoyed such songs, they were considered ill-suited for the rockabilly treatment and the raw performance style of the original Blue Caps lineup.
**Ain't She Sweet** : Another "old standard" proposed by Ken Nelson for Gene Vincent's recordings. Like other similar suggestions, it was deemed unsuitable for the group's rockabilly style, despite Vincent's personal appreciation for the song.
**Those Wedding Bells Are Breaking Up That Old Gang of Mine** : This song was among the older standards that Ken Nelson suggested Gene Vincent record. It was considered a poor fit for the rockabilly genre and the rough-and-ready performances the Blue Caps were known for.

## curiosities
**Sailor Career and Motorcycle Accident (1955)** : Gene Vincent, born Vincent Eugene Craddock, had been content with his career as a sailor until 1955. After reenlisting and receiving a $900 bonus, he purchased a Triumph racing motorbike. This bike was involved in an accident that severely damaged his left leg. The official story attributed the crash to a woman driving a Chrysler, while another account suggested Vincent crashed trying to ride under a naval base security barrier while intoxicated. The accident left his leg shattered, causing chronic osteomyelitis, a permanent open sore, and frequent bone fractures, leading to lifelong pain. He adamantly refused amputation, opting for over a year in a cast followed by a metal brace. "Whatever the truth, his left leg was smashed up, and looked for a long time like it was going to be amputated, but he refused to allow this."
**Elvis Presley Inspiration (1955)** : Following his discharge from the naval hospital in September 1955, Gene Vincent attended a country music show headlined by Hank Snow, which also featured the Louvin Brothers. However, it was witnessing a young Elvis Presley perform lower down the bill that profoundly changed Vincent's life, inspiring him to pursue a career in rock and roll. "The story seems to be the same for almost every one of the early rockabilly artists... he immediately decides that he wants to do that, that Elvis is doing something that's like what the young man has already started doing, but he's proved that you can do it on stage for people."
**Name Creation (1956)** : Born Vincent Eugene Craddock, his stage name, "Gene Vincent," was coined by Capitol Records' Ken Nelson. Nelson ingeniously created the concise stage name by combining elements from Vincent's four given names. "A name Nelson created from Jean's four names."
**Elvis Presley Comparison and Encounter** : Many contemporaries commented on the sonic and stylistic similarities between Gene Vincent's "Be-Bop-A-Lula" and Elvis Presley's music. Anecdotes circulated, including Elvis's mother mistakenly liking "Be-Bop-A-Lula" as if it were her son's, and Elvis himself allegedly being confused. Despite these resemblances, Vincent's voice was described as thinner, tenser, and twitchier compared to Elvis's consistently playful, exuberant, and controlled delivery at the time. When Vincent eventually met Elvis, he offered an apology for any perceived copying, but Elvis reassured him that he understood and that it was simply "just how Gene sang." "While Elvis is fully in control at all times, playful and exuberant, Gene Vincent is tense and twitchy."
**Las Vegas Residency Incident (1956)** : Gene Vincent and The Blue Caps secured a lucrative Las Vegas residency, earning $3,000 a week, a "staggering sum" for 1956. However, when manager Tex Davis relayed the casino owner's demand (backed by the mafia) for Vincent to tone down his act, Vincent reacted defiantly. He went on stage, performed only one song, then spotted Davis in the crowd, gave him "the finger," and walked off, leaving his band to finish the show without him. This act of insubordination led to the residency's abrupt cancellation and Davis terminating his management contract. "Instead, Jean went on stage, sang one song, found Tex Davis in the crowd, caught his eye, flipped him off, and walked off stage, leaving the band to do the rest of the show without him."
**Erratic Behavior and Career Decline** : Already known for being impetuous before his accident, Gene Vincent's chronic pain from his leg injury, combined with the pressures of rock and roll stardom and heavy drinking, exacerbated his erratic behavior. This included breaking contracts, his outburst in Las Vegas, and pulling a gun on band members on multiple occasions. This increasingly unstable conduct, coupled with Capitol Records' and Ken Nelson's misunderstanding of rock and roll, ultimately led to Vincent becoming a "forgotten figure" in the United States. "Vincent's increasingly erratic behaviour including pulling a gun on band members on multiple occasions, and Capital and Ken Nelson's lack of understanding of rock and roll music meant that he quickly became a forgotten figure in the US."
**UK Image Reinvention (1950s)** : Gene Vincent had a profound impact in the United Kingdom, largely thanks to TV producer Jack Good, who was instrumental in introducing rock and roll to British television. Good suggested Vincent abandon his conventional stage wear in favor of black leather and encouraged him to accentuate the postural problems caused by his disability. Vincent's appearances on shows like "Boy Meets Girls," dressed in black leather, hunched over and seemingly in pain, defined the visual archetype of a rock and roll star for British teenagers of the 1950s, particularly at a time when few American rock and roll acts visited or received TV exposure in the UK. "Vincent's appearances on Boy Meets Girls, dressed in black leather, hunched over, in pain because of his leg, defined for British teenagers of the 1950s what a rock and roll it was meant to look like."

## curiosities
**Rock and Roll Star Definition (1954-1955)** : Elvis Presley's performances in 1954 and 1955 were transformative, permanently defining the archetype of a "rock and roll star." His shows inspired countless future rockabilly artists, including Gene Vincent, by demonstrating that the blend of country and blues music they were already exploring could be successfully presented on stage. "This is it at every single show Elvis played in 1954 and 1955, there was a future rockabilly star in the audience, and by playing those shows Elvis permanently defined what we mean when we say rock and roll star."
**Vocal Comparison with Gene Vincent** : Many people noted the sonic and stylistic similarities between Elvis Presley's music and Gene Vincent's "Be-Bop-A-Lula." However, critics observed distinct differences in their vocal styles: Elvis was consistently "fully in control, playful and exuberant," whereas Vincent's performance was "tenser and twitchier" with a thinner voice. "While Elvis is fully in control at all times, playful and exuberant, Gene Vincent is tense and twitchy."
**Later Vocal Style Evolution** : Although Elvis Presley's early singing was distinct from Gene Vincent's, his later material would increasingly incorporate the "hiccups and gasps" that Vincent used. At the time of "Be-Bop-A-Lula's" release, the differences in their vocal techniques would have been more immediately apparent to listeners. "This is something that's less easy to see in retrospect, because Elvis's singing would get steadily more full of the kind of hiccups and gasps that Vincent used, as his material got steadily worse, but at the time it would have been obvious."

## curiosities
**Band Formation and Name Origin (1956)** : The Blue Caps were formed by Gene Vincent as his backing band before his Nashville recording session. Their name was derived from the blue hats they wore on stage, which were reportedly inspired by the golf caps frequently worn by President Eisenhower. This inspiration was humorously noted as "not the most rebellious" for a group that became a significant influence on youthful rebellion. "They took the name Blue Caps from the hats they all wore on stage, which were allegedly inspired by the golf caps that President Eisenhower used to wear while playing golf."
**Mixed Musical Ability** : The Blue Caps were characterized as a "mixed ability group." While some members were described as "rudimentary musicians at best," the rockabilly genre was generally accommodating of "enthusiastic amateurs." Lead guitarist Cliff Gallop was a notable exception, considered one of the most technically accomplished guitarists in rockabilly. "In truth, the blue caps were probably best described as a mixed ability group."
**Unstable Lineup (1956-1958)** : The band was plagued by an unstable lineup, with an astonishing 20 different individuals serving as members within its first two and a half years of existence. Gene Vincent struggled to maintain a consistent group for more than a few weeks or months at a time. "In the first two and a half years of the blue caps existence, 20 different people were members of the band."
**Cliff Gallop's Departure and Post-Band Life** : Cliff Gallop, the lead guitarist, left The Blue Caps because he felt he was "too old to be touring in a rock and roll band." Ken Nelson offered him a steady gig as a session guitarist to avoid travel, but Gallop declined, instead taking a job as a high school janitor and maintenance man, only playing occasional gigs for extra money. He became embarrassed when contacted by fans, refused to sign autographs, and his widow ensured his obituaries omitted his time with Gene Vincent after his death in 1989. "Gallop was actually offered a regular gig as a session guitarist by Ken Nelson, which would have meant that he didn't have to travel, but he turned it down and got a job as a high school janitor and maintenance man, just playing the occasional extra gig for pin money."

# label - Capitol Records

## curiosities
**Founding and Early Focus (Early 1940s)** : Capitol Records was established in the early 1940s by songwriter Johnny Mercer, who wrote many standards for artists like Frank Sinatra, Bing Crosby, and Tony Bennett. The label initially served as a home for these traditional artists, and aside from a small country music division, it largely lacked engagement with the emerging new forms of music such as rock and roll. "Karl had been founded in the early 1940s by the songwriter Johnny Mercer... Capital at the time was the home of crew members like Frank Sinatra and Nat King Cole..."
**Acquisition by EMI (1955)** : In 1955, Capitol Records was acquired by the large conglomerate EMI. This acquisition marked a turning point for the label, leading to changes in its direction and a new push towards embracing contemporary music styles. "However, in 1955 capital had been bought up by the big conglomerate EMI and things were changing at the label."
**Ken Nelson's Role and Reputation** : Ken Nelson served as the head of country music for Capitol Records. He held a "very mixed reputation" among music lovers: praised for his "impeccable taste in artists," demonstrated by signing Buck Owens and The Louvin Brothers, but criticized for his tendency to impose styles on artists that didn't suit them, and a general misunderstanding of rockabilly. "Ben Nelson was the head of country music for capital records and is someone who has a very mixed reputation among lovers of both country music and rock-a-billy..."
**Search for an "Elvis Equivalent" (1955)** : Despite Ken Nelson's lack of understanding of rockabilly, he recognized Capitol Records' need to find its own answer to Elvis Presley. He initiated a search for country singers who could emulate Elvis, which eventually led to him calling in Gene Vincent for a session after receiving a tape from Tex Davis. "Nelson didn't really understand rock-a-billy at all, but he knew that capital needed its own equivalent of Elvis Presley, so he put out a call for people to recommend him country singers who could sound a bit like Elvis..."
**New Hollywood Studio (1957)** : Capitol Records opened a new recording studio located in the Capitol Tower in Hollywood. This facility became the site where Gene Vincent recorded his song "Lotta Lovin'." "Lotta Lovein was quickly recorded at Jean's first session at Capital's new studio at the Capital Tower in Hollywood."
**Lack of Rock and Roll Understanding** : Capitol Records, particularly through the decisions of Ken Nelson, displayed a notable lack of understanding regarding rock and roll music. This was evidenced by their suggestions for Gene Vincent to record unsuitable "old standards," which contributed to his struggle to gain lasting success in the US. "Capital and Ken Nelson's lack of understanding of rock and roll music meant that he quickly became a forgotten figure in the US."

# genre - Rock and Roll

## curiosities
**Elvis Presley's Defining Role (1954-1955)** : Elvis Presley's live performances between 1954 and 1955 played a crucial role in shaping the identity of rock and roll. His stage presence and musical style at these shows permanently defined what it meant to be a "rock and roll star" and significantly influenced subsequent artists. "by playing those shows Elvis permanently defined what we mean when we say rock and roll star."
**Introduction to British TV (1950s)** : Jack Good is credited as the individual most responsible for bringing rock and roll music to British television audiences. He produced groundbreaking shows like "6-5 Special" for the BBC and later "Oh Boy" and "Boy Meets Girls" for ITV, showcasing the new genre. "Jack Good was the person who, more than anyone else, had brought rock and roll to British TV."
**Gene Vincent's Definitive UK Image (1950s)** : On British television, particularly through appearances on "Boy Meets Girls," Gene Vincent's black leather attire and hunched posture (exacerbated by his disability) created a powerful and iconic image. This look, influenced by producer Jack Good, defined for British teenagers of the 1950s what a rock and roll star was meant to look like, especially when few American stars were visiting the UK. # genre - R&B

## curiosities
**Relaxed Culture of Musical Borrowing (1950s)** : During the era when "Money Honey" and "Be-Bop-A-Lula" were released, the R&B music culture was sufficiently "relaxed about borrowings." This meant that melodic similarities between songs were generally not a cause for legal action, as exemplified by Jesse Stone not suing Gene Vincent despite the near-identical melody of their songs. "Luckily for Vincent at this time the culture in R&B was relaxed enough about bowings that Jesse Stone seems not to have even considered suing."
**"Bbapp" as a Colloquial Umbrella Term (1956)** : In 1956, "Bbapp" was colloquially used as a broad term for "noisy music I don't understand that the young people like," particularly music made by black artists. This expansive usage covered R&B, rock and roll, and even rockabilly, distinguishing it from its specific jazz definition. "So it covered Bbapp itself, but it was also used for rhythm and blues, rock and roll, even rockabilly."

# genre - Bebop

## curiosities
**Original Definition (Mid-1940s)** : The term "Bebop," as it originated in New York in the mid-1940s, specifically referred to a style of jazz music that prioritized "harmonic complexity, instrumental virtuosity, and individual self-expression." This genre was famously performed by musicians such as Charlie Parker, Oscar Peterson, Dexter Gordon, and Dizzy Gillespie, effectively defining jazz in the post-war era. "The term Bbapp, as it was originally used, was used in the same way we use it now, for a type of jazz music that originated in New York in the mid-1940s, which prized harmonic complexity, instrumental virtuosity, and individual self-expression."
**Colloquial Usage in 1956** : By 1956, the term "Bbapp" had acquired a distinct, colloquial meaning for most people, divergent from its precise jazz definition. It generally meant "that noisy music I don't understand that the young people like," often associated with black artists. This broader usage encompassed rhythm and blues, rock and roll, and rockabilly, illustrating a rapid cultural shift in terminology. "Colloquially, Bbapp meant, that noisy music I don't understand that the young people like, and most of the people making it a black."
**"Be-Bop-A-Lula" Naming Convention** : Despite having minimal musical connection to jazz bebop, the name "Be-Bop-A-Lula" still fit the colloquial understanding of "bop" in 1956. At that time, "bop" was a catch-all term for youth-oriented, "noisy music," making the title culturally relevant even without direct jazz influence. "So even though Bbappalula had about as much to do with Bbapp as it did with Stravinsky, the name still fit."

**The "Bbapp" Term Evolution** : The term "Bbapp" underwent a significant transformation in cultural meaning. Originally, it accurately described a specific jazz genre that emerged in New York in the mid-1940s, characterized by "harmonic complexity, instrumental virtuosity, and individual self-expression," as played by artists like Charlie Parker. However, by 1956, its colloquial use broadened dramatically to encompass "noisy music I don't understand that the young people like," often associated with black artists, and included R&B, rock and roll, and rockabilly. This evolution highlights how rapidly cultural context can change and be lost. "And that's something we should probably at least talk about briefly here, because it shows just how much culture changes and how fast we lose context for things that seemed obvious at the time."
**Owen Bradley's Nashville Studio** : The recording studio in Nashville where Gene Vincent held his initial session was owned and operated by Owen Bradley. This studio was a significant location in the burgeoning music scene, having recently been used to record early tracks for Buddy Holly, a singer from Texas, indicating its role in fostering emerging talent. "The session was at a studio run by Owen Bradley, who had just recently recorded some early tracks by a singer from Texas named Buddy Holly."
**Donald Graves and "Be-Bop-A-Lula" Songwriting Dispute** : Donald Graves, a fellow patient with Gene Vincent at a naval hospital, was widely regarded as a co-writer of "Be-Bop-A-Lula." While Tex Davis is also credited as a co-writer, it is generally acknowledged that he had no actual involvement in the song's creation. Most sources indicate that Davis paid Graves $25 for his half of the songwriting rights, suggesting Graves' legitimate claim. "Donald Graves, a fellow patient in the naval hospital, who was widely considered to have co-written the song with Jean. Always claimed the song was inspired by the 1920s Vordville song, Don't Ring Lulu."
**Sheriff Tex Davis - DJ, Promoter, Manager** : William Dushet, a DJ from Connecticut, adopted the more country-sounding name "Sheriff Tex Davis." He was a DJ and show promoter responsible for the gig where Elvis Presley performed, inspiring Gene Vincent. Davis subsequently signed Vincent to a management contract and sent his demo tapes to Ken Nelson at Capitol Records. He later terminated his management of Vincent following Vincent's defiant behavior during a Las Vegas residency. "Davis was a DJ and show promoter, and he was the one who had promoted the gig that Elvis had appeared at."
**Jack Good's Pioneering Role in British TV Rock and Roll** : Jack Good was an immensely influential TV producer who, more than anyone else, was responsible for bringing rock and roll to British television. He produced the BBC's "6-5 Special" and later ITV's groundbreaking rock and roll shows "Oh Boy" and "Boy Meets Girls." Good also significantly shaped Gene Vincent's iconic black leather image for British audiences. "Jack Good was the person who, more than anyone else, had brought rock and roll to British TV."
