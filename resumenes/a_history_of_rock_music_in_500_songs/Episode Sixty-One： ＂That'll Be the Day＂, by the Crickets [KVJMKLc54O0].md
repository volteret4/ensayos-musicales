**Blue Days Black Knight (1956) - Decca Single** : A song written by Ben Hall, a friend of Buddy's from Lubbock, which was recorded at Holly's first Decca session and eventually chosen as his single over "Midnight Shift." "A song written by Ben Hall, a friend of buddies from Lubbock."
**Midnight Shift (1956) - Unreleased Decca Song** : The fourth song recorded at Buddy Holly's first Decca session, intended as an entry in the "Annie series" and written by Luke McDaniel. "And the fourth song that buddy recorded at this first Decker session, Midnight Shift, was intended to be another in the Annie series."
**That'll Be the Day (1956) - Original Decca Version** : This initial recording was inspired by a line from the John Wayne film "The Searchers," which Holly and Joe Allison had seen multiple times. Owen Bradley, the producer, described it as "the worst song I've ever heard" due to Holly singing it in a high voice, following ill-advised counsel from Webb Pierce. This version was ultimately unreleased by Decca. "One of the songs that he recorded during that time, but which wasn't released, was one that Owen Bradley described as the worst song I've ever heard."
**Words of Love (1957) - First Solo Single** : Buddy Holly's first solo single, which was musically based on "Love Is Strange" by Mickey and Sylvia, a personal favorite of Holly's. Although not a hit for Holly himself, a cover version by The Diamonds reached number 13 on the charts even before Holly's original was released. "That song was based on Love Is Strange by Mickey and Sylvia, a favourite of Holly's."

## curiosities
**Early Musical Background** : Holly grew up in Lubbock, Texas, a town notable for producing many famous musicians. His elder brothers performed as a country duo, modeling their style after the Louvin Brothers, influencing Holly's early exposure to music. "Holly grew up with music. His elder brothers performed as a country duo in much the same style as the Louvain Brothers."
**Decision to Pursue Rock and Roll (1955)** : Buddy Holly decided he wanted to be a rock and roll star after seeing Elvis live in 1955. The promoter of Elvis's show, a friend of Buddy and Bob, added them to the bill, and Holly became friendly enough with Elvis to hang out with him regularly when Elvis passed through town in those early career years. "But it wasn't until he saw Elvis live in 1955 that Buddy Holly knew he didn't want to do anything, or that they become a rock and roll star."
**Purchase of Fender Stratocaster (1955)** : Believing in Buddy's talent, bass player Larry Wellborn lent him $1000 in 1955—a substantial sum at the time—which enabled Holly to purchase his first Fender Stratocaster, an amplifier, and a stage suit, marking a significant investment in his rock and roll career. "Larry Wellborne, believed in Buddy's talent and lent him $1000, a massive amount of money in 1955, so he could buy himself a friend of Stratocaster, an amp, and a stage suit."
**The Stratocaster's "Gear Shift" (1955)** : Holly's friend Joe B. Mauldin humorously described the Fender Stratocaster as the first instrument he'd ever seen with a "gear shift," referring to its innovative tremolo arm, which had only been introduced that year. "Holly's friend Joe B. Mouldin said of the Strat that it was the first instrument he'd ever seen with a gear shift."
**Musical Style Transition with Sonny Curtis** : After Elvis's emergence, Buddy Holly and Sonny Curtis immediately shifted their musical style; Holly, despite owning an electric guitar, would borrow Curtis's Martin acoustic to try and emulate Elvis's singing and playing, while Curtis would borrow Holly's Strat to perform Scotty Moore's guitar licks. "Curtis would later talk about how as soon as Elvis came along, he and Buddy immediately switched their musical style."
**Rivalry with Roy Orbison** : Buddy Holly was slowly becoming the most popular rock and roll singer in his part of Texas, but he faced an ongoing rivalry with Roy Orbison, who, despite being from 100 miles away in Wink, was considered Holly's only serious local competition for the title of best rock and roller. "though he had an ongoing rivalry with Roy Orbison, who was from a hundred miles away in Wink, but was the only serious competition around for the best local rock and roller."
**Failed Louisiana Hayride Attempt (1956)** : Elvis had assured Buddy Holly that the Louisiana Hayride would feature him based on his recommendation. However, when Holly and Sonny Curtis drove to Shreveport, the radio station informed them that Elvis did not control their bookings, forcing them to return to Texas without performing. "Elvis had told him that the Louisiana Hayride would definitely have him on at Elvis's recommendation, but when he and Sonny Curtis drove to Shreveport, the radio station told him that it wasn't up to Elvis who got on their show and who didn't."
**Decca Records Misspells Name (1956)** : When signing his paperwork for Decca Records, Charles Holley (H-O-L-L-E-Y) discovered his name was misspelled as H-O-L-L-Y. Told they needed the document back quickly, he signed it, and from then on, he was known as Buddy Holly, without the 'e'. "When the paperwork for it came through for Buddy to sign, he discovered that they'd miss spelled his name."
**Decca's Disillusionment (1956)** : Throughout 1956, Decca Records grew increasingly disillusioned with Buddy Holly's lack of commercial success, despite numerous recordings. Paul Cohen, the executive who signed Holly, was known to tell friends that Buddy Holly was "the biggest no-talent I have ever worked with." "Decker became ever more disillusioned with Holly, while Paul Cohen, the executive at Decker who had signed Holly, at one point was telling his friends, Buddy Holly is the biggest no-talent I have ever worked with."
**End of 1956 Career Stagnation** : As 1956 drew to a close, Buddy Holly found himself in a difficult position: without his long-time guitarist Sonny Curtis, signed to a record company that did not know how to promote him, and failing to realize his musical ambitions despite constant recording. "So as 1956 drew to a close, Buddy Holly was without his long-time guitarist, signed to a record company that didn't know what to do with him, and failing to realise his musical ambitions."
**Decca Contract Prevents Re-recording (1957)** : Following the expiration of his Decca contract, Buddy Holly was prohibited from re-recording any songs he had recorded for the label in 1956 for a period of five years, a clause that significantly complicated his future recording plans. "Buddy's contract with Decker said that even though they'd only released two singles by him and hadn't bothered to release any of the other songs he'd recorded during the year he was signed to them, he couldn't re-record anything he'd recorded for them for another five years."

## curiosities
**Band Naming - Rejecting "The Beetles"** : Buddy Holly and Jerry Allison, inspired by New Orleans R&B groups like The Spiders, initially considered naming their group "The Beetles" but decided it was "too creepy" because people would want to squish them, ultimately settling on "The Crickets." "At first they considered The Beatles, but decided that that was too creepy. People would want to squish them. So they settled on The Crickets."
**Contract for "That'll Be the Day" (1957)** : To avoid issues with Buddy Holly's previous Decca contract, the contract for the release of "That'll Be the Day" on Brunswick was sent to Jerry Allison, intentionally omitting Buddy Holly's name in case record executive Bob Thiele reported it to his superiors. "And the contract was sent to Jerry Allison, not Buddy Holly. His name wasn't mentioned at first, in case Thiel decided to mention it to his buses, and the whole thing was blown."
**Joe B. Mauldin's Audition and Joining** : The band's permanent lineup was finalized with Joe B. Mauldin on bass after he sat in on a gig with Buddy Holly, Jerry Allison, and Nikki Sullivan, impressing them with his playing. Mauldin had previously played with The Four Teens. "Joe B had sat in on a gig with the other three, and they'd been impressed with his bass playing."
**Joe B. Mauldin's Mother's Song** : One of the songs recorded by The Crickets was originally written by Joe B. Mauldin's mother, though she refused to take credit for it, adhering to her fundamentalist Southern Baptist beliefs that rock and roll was "the devil's music." "She was a fundamentalist Southern Baptist, and Rockin' Roll was the devil's music."
**Prolific Recording Pace (Early 1957)** : The Crickets were constantly in Norman Petty's studio, recording a "ludicrous amount of material" and piling up songs much faster than they could be released. This output included dozens upon dozens of songs in the early months of 1957 that continued to be released for years afterward. "And they were recording a ludicrous amount of material."
**Extensive Session Work (Early 1957)** : In addition to recording their own material, The Crickets actively engaged in session work for other artists, playing on records by Jim Robinson, Jack Huddl, Hal Goodson, and Fred Crawford, among others, demonstrating their busy schedule and versatility. "They were also doing session work, playing on records by Jim Robinson, Jack Huddl, Hal Goodson, Fred Crawford and more."
**Inconsistent Songwriting Credits** : Despite Buddy Holly and Jerry Allison, sometimes with Joe B. Mauldin, being the primary writers, songwriting credits often varied, with names occasionally omitted, Holly sometimes credited by his middle name "Hardin," and Norman Petty almost always adding his name. "sometimes while other name would get missed off the credits, sometimes Holly would be credited by his middle name Hardin instead of his surname, and almost always Norman Petty would end up with his name on the songwriting credits."
**"Peggy Sue" Origin Story** : Buddy Holly originally intended to name a song after his niece, Cindy Lou. However, Jerry Allison suggested that the song would be better if it was about his girlfriend, leading to the eventual title "Peggy Sue." "Jamie, on the other hand, thought the song would be better if he was about his girlfriend."

## curiosities
**Early Overdubbing Experiments (Late 1940s)** : Norman Petty was a former musician who, in the late 1940s, conducted "crude experiments in overdubbing," albeit in a less sophisticated manner than pioneers like Les Paul and Mary Ford. "Petty was a former musician who had performed crude experiments in overdubbing in the late 40s, copying Les Paul and Mary Ford, though in a much less sophisticated manner."
**Minor Hit with "Mood Indigo"** : Petty achieved a minor hit with his version of Duke Ellington's "Mood Indigo," demonstrating some early success in his musical career before transitioning to studio work. "One of his singles, a version of Duke Ellington's mood indigo, had actually been a minor hit."
**Recording Studio Business Model** : Petty established a recording studio business where he charged bands $60 to record two songs. However, if he deemed the songs commercially viable, he would waive the fee in exchange for publishing rights and a co-writing credit. "He'd gone into the recording studio business, and charged bands $60 to record two songs in his studio, or if he thought the songs had commercial potential, he'd waive the charge if they gave him the publishing and a co-writing credit."
**Interest in Rockabilly and Echo (1950s)** : Petty's interest in rockabilly was sparked after recording Roy Orbison and The Teen Kings' first single, "Ooby Dooby." He became fascinated by the possibilities of echo after hearing Sam Phillips' remake of the song, which inspired him to build his own echo chamber. "When he heard Sam Phillips' remake of the song, he became intrigued by the possibilities that Echo offered and started to build his own Echo Chamber."
**Echo Chamber Construction** : Norman Petty successfully built his own echo chamber, completing it with the assistance of Buddy Holly, Holly's father, and Holly's brother, demonstrating a collaborative effort in developing his studio's technical capabilities. "started to build his own Echo Chamber, something that would eventually be completed with the help of Buddy Holly and Buddy's father and brother."
**Producing a Number 1 Hit** : Petty recorded another rockabilly group, Buddy Knox and the Rhythm Orchids, on their song "Party Doll," which went to number 1, solidifying his reputation as a successful rockabilly producer. "Petty recorded another rockabilly group, Buddy Knox and the Vithermorkids, on a song, Petty style that went to number 1."
**Initial Impression of Holly and Curtis** : Petty was impressed by Buddy Holly during their demo sessions, but he was even more impressed by Sonny Curtis, who was still with Buddy at that time, highlighting Curtis's strong musical presence. "Petty was impressed by Holly, though he was more impressed by Sonny Curtis, who was still with Buddy for those demo sessions."
**Devising a Contract Solution for Holly (1957)** : Faced with Buddy Holly's Decca contract prohibiting him from re-recording 1956 songs for five years, Norman Petty secretly recorded a conversation with Paul Cohen to understand the specifics. He then proposed releasing Holly's new recordings under a group name, The Crickets, to circumvent the legal issue. "So Norman Petty, who had been secretly recording the conversation, suggested a way around the problem."
**Assuming "That'll Be the Day" was a Demo (1957)** : When Norman Petty shopped the recordings for "That'll Be the Day" to different labels, he initially assumed they were merely demos, but Bob Thiele of Brunswick Records believed the tracks were already polished enough for immediate release. "Norman Petty had assumed that what they'd recorded so far was just going to be a demo, but Thiel said that no, he thought what they had was fine as it was, and put this out."
**Frequent Songwriting Credit Inclusion** : Norman Petty almost always ended up with his name on the songwriting credits for songs written by Buddy Holly, Jerry Allison, and sometimes Joe B. Mauldin, despite their primary creative input. "almost always Norman Petty would end up with his name on the songwriting credits."

# instrument - Fender Stratocaster

## curiosities
**Recent Innovation (1955)** : The Fender Stratocaster was a recent innovation in 1955, noted for its tremolo arm, which Buddy Holly's friend Joe B. Mauldin humorously referred to as a "gear shift." "Holly's friend Joe B. Mouldin said of the Strat that it was the first instrument he'd ever seen with a gear shift. He was referring there to the tremolo arm on the guitar, a recent innovation that had only been brought in that year."
**Purchase by Buddy Holly (1955)** : Buddy Holly was able to purchase his own Fender Stratocaster in 1955, along with an amp and stage suit, thanks to a $1000 loan from bass player Larry Wellborn, marking a significant step in his musical career. "Lent him $1000, a massive amount of money in 1955, so he could buy himself a friend of Stratocaster, an amp, and a stage suit."

# curiosity

**The Annie Songs Phenomenon (1954)** : The release of Hank Ballard and the Midnighters' "Work With Me Annie" in 1954, a song that was considered sexually explicit for its era, triggered a widespread trend of "answer records." This series included The Midnighters' own "Annie Had a Baby," Etta James' famous "The Wallflower," and numerous other songs like "Annie Met Henry" and "Annie Kicked the Bucket." "That song had started up a whole series of answer records."
