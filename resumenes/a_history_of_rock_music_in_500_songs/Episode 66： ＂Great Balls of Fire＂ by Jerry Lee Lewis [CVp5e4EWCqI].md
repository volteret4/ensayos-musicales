## songs
**Whole Lotta Shakin' (1957)** : This was Jerry Lee Lewis's first really major success, propelling him to the top of the world as the most promising artist in rock and roll music. With Elvis Presley about to be drafted, Lewis intended to take over his mantle as the biggest star. "Holot Shaken was such a massive hit that on the basis of that one record, Jerry Lee was invited to perform his next single in a film called Jamboree."
**Great Balls of Fire (1957)** : This song was immediately recognized as a hit by Lewis and Sam Phillips upon hearing Otis Blackwell's demo. Phillips was concerned about how to improve upon Blackwell's nearly perfect demo. It was tailored specifically for Lewis's established persona, refining the "Whole Lotta Shakin'" formula with a more dynamic, stop-start opening. A film version was quickly cut for "Jamboree," but several days were spent perfecting the hit record, focusing on the intro. Lewis expressed theological concerns about the song sounding "satanic," leading to an angry discussion with Phillips, who eventually persuaded him to continue recording. Released with Hank Williams' "You Win Again" as the B-side, it was an immediate success, selling over a million copies in the first ten days and becoming a classic that defines 1950s Rock and Roll. It established Lewis as one of the genre's greatest stars and has been covered by artists from Dolly Parton to Aerosmith. "It's one of the records that defines 1950s Rock and Roll music, and it firmly established Jerry Lee Lewis as one of the greatest stars of Rock and Roll, if not the greatest."
**You Win Again (1957)** : This Hank Williams ballad was released as the B-side to "Great Balls of Fire." "When Great Balls of Fire came out with a cover of Hank Williams' ballad you win again on the B side. It was an immediate success."
**Big Legged Woman** : This old blues number was among the backlog of recordings made by Jack Clement and Sam Phillips, intended for release for decades to come. "Everything from Hank Williams' covers to the old blues number Big Legged Woman."
**Breathless (1958)** : Following the success of "Great Balls of Fire," Sun Records released this as the next single, another song written by Otis Blackwell. The band for this recording included Jimmy Van Eaten on drums, Billy Lee Riley on guitar, and Lewis's cousin J.W. Brown on bass. It was stylistically similar to "Great Balls of Fire" but perhaps not quite as strong. "Breathless was very much in the same style as Great Balls of Fire, if perhaps not quite so good."
**High School Confidential (1958)** : This was the title song for a film Lewis appeared in, a "terribly shlucky attempted drama" about teenage marijuana use, widely considered one of the worst films ever made. Despite the film's poor quality, the theme music was noted as "pretty good." It was released on May 19, 1958, and immediately began rising up the charts. "The theme music, though, was pretty good."
**Another Place Another Time (1968)** : Released in the late 1960s, this song marked Jerry Lee Lewis's comeback, reaching number four on the country charts. It initiated a successful run of country hits for Lewis that lasted for nearly a decade. "Another place another time went to number four on the country charts, and started a run of country hits that lasted for the best part of a decade."

## curiosities
**Elvis's Successor** : With Elvis Presley about to be drafted into the army, Jerry Lee Lewis saw an opportunity to take over the role of the biggest rock and roll star, intending to become the new "king of rock and roll." "With Elvis about to be drafted into the army, the role of biggest rock and roll star was wide open, and Lewis intended to take over Elvis's mantle."
**Theological Discussion with Sam Phillips** : While recording "Great Balls of Fire," Jerry Lee Lewis became concerned that the song sounded "satanic," aligning with contemporary criticisms of rock and roll as "the devil's music." This led to an angry, rambling theological discussion with Sam Phillips, which was recorded. The conversation revealed Lewis's deep belief in a physical hell and his conviction that his music would lead him there, showcasing his tortured mindset and making him difficult to work with. Sam Phillips, demonstrating remarkable patience, eventually calmed Lewis down and convinced him to continue recording. "He truly believed in the existence of a physical hell, and that he was destined to go there because of his music."
**"Breathless" Promotional Scheme** : Judd Phillips, Sam Phillips' brother, devised a unique promotional campaign for "Breathless" in conjunction with Dick Clark, who hosted "The Dick Clark Show" (also known as "Dick Clark's Saturday Night Beach Nut Show," sponsored by Beach Nut Chewing Gum). Fans could send Sun Records five Beach Nut wrappers and 50 cents for postage and packing to receive a signed copy of the new record. This led to extraordinary results, with some areas of the country running out of Beach Nut Gum, and over 38,000 singles being distributed. Sun Records had to purchase an autograph stamp and hire temporary staff to manage the demand, splitting profits with Dick Clark. "The result was extraordinary."
**Disputes on the Alan Freed Tour** : Jerry Lee Lewis embarked on a legendary tour with Chuck Berry, Buddy Holly, Larry Williams, The Chantels, and other acts. The tour became renowned not for its music, but for personal disputes between Lewis and Berry. One incident involved Lewis's father, Elmo, who was known for using racial slurs and threatening behavior. After a parking dispute between Lewis and Berry, Elmo chased Berry three blocks with a knife, shouting racial threats, though the next day they were friendly. Another point of contention was Berry's belief that he should headline over Lewis, prompting Lewis to perform increasingly outrageous stunts to upstage Berry, including an apocryphal story of setting his piano on fire during "Great Balls of Fire." "The tour later became legendary, not so much for the music, though that was great, but for the personal disputes between Lewis and Berry."
**Respect for Chuck Berry** : Despite their frequent clashes, Jerry Lee Lewis showed unusual respect for Chuck Berry, partly due to his admiration for Berry's songwriting, going so far as to call Berry "the Hank Williams of Rock and Roll"—the highest praise imaginable from Lewis. Both Jerry Lee and his father, Elmo, were careful to prevent news of their feuds from reaching Lewis's mother, who strongly believed Chuck Berry was "the greatest rock and roller in the world," surpassing even Elvis and her own son. "Partly, that's because of Lewis's admiration for Berry's songwriting. He's called Berry the Hank Williams of Rock and Roll before now, and for someone who had met as Williams as much as Lewis does, that's about the highest imaginable praise."
**Scandalous Marriage and UK Tour Downfall (1958)** : On May 21, 1958, during what was meant to be a triumphant UK tour, Jerry Lee Lewis's career collapsed when the press discovered he had married his 13-year-old cousin, Myra Gale Brown. This revelation, made public upon their arrival in the UK, immediately halted the tour, which was meant to last six weeks but ended after just three days. Lewis's actions, involving a 20-something man marrying a 13-year-old in his own family, were unequivocally deemed "child sexual abuse" and "simply wrong." While Lewis and those around him did not view it as wrong within the context of the Southern US culture at the time, where young marriages to older men were common, this cultural context does not excuse his behavior. His own bass player and Myra's father, J.W. Brown, was so enraged he attempted to murder Lewis with a shotgun, only being deterred by Sam Phillips. "And that is when everything came crashing down, because it was when he and his entourage landed in the UK and the press saw the 13-year-old girl with him and asked who she was that it became public knowledge he had married his 13-year-old cousin, Maver."
**"The Return of Jerry Lee" Single (1958)** : In an attempt by Sun Records to both capitalize on and defuse the controversy surrounding Jerry Lee Lewis's scandalous marriage, Jack Clement produced a novelty record. This single, a joke initially for Sun staff, featured DJ George Klein interviewing Lewis about the controversy, with Lewis's answers spliced from his existing records. Sam Phillips, despite its origins as a joke, insisted on releasing it. However, the record, which included "leering jokes about Myra's physical attractiveness," failed to defuse the situation and was criticized for its exploitation of a child. "Unsurprisingly, that did not have the effect that was hoped, and did not defuse the situation one Iota."
**Career Rebuilding** : After the scandal destroyed his career and led to the end of Sam Phillips' reign at Sun Records, Jerry Lee Lewis spent several years struggling to produce hits. He gradually rebuilt his audience by playing small venues and winning over crowds one at a time, paving the way for a comeback in the late 1960s. "Jerry Lee Lewis meanwhile spent several years trying and failing to have a hit, but slowly rebuilding his live audiences, playing small venues and winning back his audience one crowd at a time."
**Divorce from Myra Gale Brown** : Around the time of his late 1960s comeback, Myra Gale Brown divorced Jerry Lee Lewis, citing physical and emotional abuse. Myra is now known as Myra Williams, has been happily married for 36 years, and works as a real estate agent. "Mire divorced Jerry Lee around that time, citing physical and emotional abuse."
**Later Marriages and Controversies** : Following his divorce from Myra, Jerry Lee Lewis married four more times. His fourth and fifth wives died under mysterious circumstances; the fourth drowned shortly before their divorce was finalized, and the fifth's death remains unclear, with suspicions raised that Lewis may have been involved. In the late 1970s, Lewis, known as "The Killer," shot his bass player in the chest, claiming it was an accident. He was also arrested outside Graceland while drunk and armed, yelling for Elvis Presley to emerge and settle who was the "real king." Lewis is currently 84 years old, married to his seventh wife (who is Myra's brother's ex-wife), still performs, and, according to recent interviews, remains concerned about his eternal fate. "The man known as the killer did once shoot his bass player in the chest in the late 70s."

**Post-Sun Career** : Unlike Johnny Cash, Karl Perkins did not achieve greater success after leaving Sun Records for Columbia. ## curiosities
**Credit Discrepancies** : Karl Perkins expressed frustration with Sun Records' crediting practices, noting that Jerry Lee Lewis's records were always credited to "Jerry Lee Lewis and his pumping piano," while Perkins' own records never mentioned "Karl's guitar." Sam Phillips eventually promised to credit Perkins as "the Rocking guitar man," but it was too late to retain him. "Jerry Lee's records were credited to Jerry Lee Lewis and his pumping piano. Why did Karl's records never say anything about Karl's guitar?"
**Departure of Key Artists (1957)** : On November 1, 1957, both Karl Perkins and Johnny Cash made an agreement with Columbia Records to leave Sun Records once their current contracts expired. This was due to frustrations with Sam Phillips' focus on Jerry Lee Lewis (Perkins) and production decisions by Jack Clement (Cash). "Patrons and Johnny Cash, both made an agreement with Columbia Records, on November 1, 1957, that when their current contracts with Sun expired, they'd start recording for the new label."
**Marketing Collaboration with Dick Clark (1958)** : Sun Records collaborated with Dick Clark on a major promotional campaign for Jerry Lee Lewis's single "Breathless." This involved offering signed copies of the record in exchange for five Beach Nut chewing gum wrappers and 50 cents for postage and packing. The campaign was highly successful, leading to gum shortages in some areas and the distribution of over 38,000 singles. Sun Records purchased an autograph stamp and hired temporary staff to manage the demand, splitting profits with Dick Clark. "Judd Phillips, Sam's brother, came up with a great promotional scheme."
**Sam Phillips' Reign Ends (1958)** : Following the scandal surrounding Jerry Lee Lewis's marriage and the subsequent collapse of his career, combined with the departure of other major acts like Johnny Cash and Karl Perkins, Sam Phillips' brief but significant reign as "the most important record producer and company owner in the USA was over." Phillips continued to run Sun Records for a few more years before selling it. "With Jerry Lee Lewis's career destroyed, and with all his other major acts having left him, Phillips's brief reign as the most important record producer and company owner in the USA was over."
**Acquisition by Shelby Singleton (Late 1950s/Early 1960s)** : After Sam Phillips' reign as a major producer ended, he sold Sun Records to Shelby Singleton. Singleton was credited with effectively exploiting Sun's extensive back catalog. "He carried on running son records for a few years, and eventually sold it to Shelby Singleton."
**Extensive Back Catalog** : During its four-year rockabilly heyday, Sam Phillips and Jack Clement recorded thousands of unreleased songs by artists such as Jerry Lee Lewis, Johnny Cash, Roy Orbison, Karl Perkins, Conway Twitty, Charlie Rich, and Billy Lee Riley. These tracks remained in Sun's vaults for over a decade until Shelby Singleton acquired the company. Singleton then ensured that almost all of this material was released, especially with the help of British reissue label Charlie, which employed Martin Hawkins and Colin Escott to systematically release Sun's rockabilly and blues archives. "In their four-year Rocket Billy Hayday, Sam Phillips and Jack Clements had recorded literally thousands of unreleased songs by Jerry Lee Lewis, Johnny Cash, Roy Orbison, Karl Perkins, Conway Twitty, Charlie Rich, Billy Lee Riley, and more."
**Impact on Music History** : As more of Sun Records' back catalog was released, it became evident that Sam Phillips had discovered something "very, very special" at the label. Throughout the 1950s, this small studio in Memphis consistently produced "staggering recordings on a daily basis," solidifying Phillips' reputation as one of the most important figures in music history by the time of his death in 2003. "By the time Sam Phillips died in 2003, aged 80, he was widely regarded as one of the most important people in the history of music."

# genre - Rock and Roll

## curiosities
**Initial Decline in Popularity (1958)** : Around 1958, rock and roll began its first decline in popularity, marking a point where musical fashions would frequently change, and new artists would regularly replace the most popular acts of the preceding period. This initial decline was notably "traumatic" for the first rock and roll era, characterized by deaths, shifts in fashion, and significant scandals involving figures like Jerry Lee Lewis and Chuck Berry. **Traditional Histories' Framing of Decline** : Traditional histories of rock and roll often frame the decline around 1958 as a result of "preorient attitudes of a few foods" who sought to destroy rock and rollers' careers due to perceived threats to traditional ways of life. However, this narrative is challenged, asserting that the downfall of artists like Jerry Lee Lewis was not a conspiracy, but a consequence of public anger over "unquestionably just wrong" actions, with rock journalism minimizing these truths. "If you read almost anything about rock and roll history, what you see when it gets to 1958 is, and here rock and roll nearly died because of the preorient attitudes of a few foods, who were out to destroy the careers of these new exciting rock and rollers because they hated the threat they posed to their traditional way of life."
**Establishment Opposition** : There was indeed significant establishment opposition to rock and roll music. However, this opposition is not presented as the primary cause for the downfall of artists like Jerry Lee Lewis, whose scandal was attributed to understandable public anger over his actions rather than a "conspiracy of blue-nose foods." "Yes, there was a great deal of establishment opposition to rock and roll music, but what happened to Jerry Lee Lewis wasn't some conspiracy of blue-nose foods."

# genre - Rockabilly

## curiosities
**Sun Records' Heyday** : Rockabilly experienced a four-year heyday at Sun Records, during which Sam Phillips and Jack Clement recorded thousands of songs by numerous artists. These recordings became a crucial part of Sun's extensive back catalog, later systematically reissued by labels like Charlie. "In their four-year Rocket Billy Hayday, Sam Phillips and Jack Clements had recorded literally thousands of unreleased songs by Jerry Lee Lewis, Johnny Cash, Roy Orbison, Karl Perkins, Conway Twitty, Charlie Rich, Billy Lee Riley, and more."

# concert - Jamboree

## curiosities
**Exploitation Film (1957)** : "Jamboree" was one of many exploitation films produced in the late 1950s, starring popular DJs like Dick Clark (who starred in this one, rather than Alan Freed who appeared in most others). These films featured interchangeable, simplistic plots, often about teenagers putting on a dance. Their primary appeal was not the plot but the live performances by rock and roll musicians, including Jerry Lee Lewis, Karl Perkins, Fat Stumino, Slim Whitman, and Count Basie with Joe Williams. The film also contains the only known footage of Lewis Lyman and the Teen Courts. "This was one of the many exploitation films that were being put out starring popular DJs."

# concert - Alan Freed Tour

## curiosities
**Legendary for Disputes** : This tour, featuring Jerry Lee Lewis, Chuck Berry, Buddy Holly, Larry Williams, The Chantels, and eleven other acts, became legendary not primarily for the music, which was excellent, but for the intense personal disputes between Jerry Lee Lewis and Chuck Berry. These included a knife-waving confrontation involving Lewis's father, Elmo, and ongoing rivalry over headlining status, which pushed Lewis to perform increasingly outrageous onstage stunts. "The tour later became legendary, not so much for the music, though that was great, but for the personal disputes between Lewis and Berry."

# concert - Jerry Lee Lewis UK Tour (1958)

## curiosities
**Cancelled Due to Scandal** : Jerry Lee Lewis's UK tour, intended as a triumphant six-week engagement to solidify his status as a top rock and roll star, was abruptly cancelled after only three days. The tour ended when the British press discovered Lewis had married his 13-year-old cousin, Myra Gale Brown, making his scandalous private life public and leading to immediate public outcry. "The British tour, which was meant to last six weeks, ended up lasting only three days."

# instrument - Piano

## curiosities
**"Pumping Piano" Signature** : Jerry Lee Lewis's distinctive piano style was so integral to his sound that his records were often credited to "Jerry Lee Lewis and his pumping piano," a practice that caused some resentment among other Sun artists like Karl Perkins. "Jerry Lee's records were credited to Jerry Lee Lewis and his pumping piano. Why did Karl's records never say anything about Karl's guitar?"
**Apocryphal Fire Stunt** : A legendary but likely apocryphal story from the Alan Freed tour describes Jerry Lee Lewis setting his piano on fire at the climax of "Great Balls of Fire" and then challenging Chuck Berry to follow that. While probably not true, this anecdote vividly captures the intense rivalry and showmanship between the two artists. "The legend has it that at one show he went so far as to set his piano on fire at the climax of Great Bulls of Fire, and then Warcraft Stage, challenging Berry to follow that."

# curiosity

**Exploitation Films** : During the rise of rock and roll, many low-budget exploitation films were produced, often starring popular DJs like Dick Clark or Alan Freed. These movies typically featured simplistic, interchangeable plots, such as teenagers trying to organize a dance. Their primary draw was the inclusion of performances by popular rock and roll musicians, rather than their cinematic quality, making Elvis's films appear as "masterpieces" in comparison. "This was one of the many exploitation films that were being put out starring popular DJs."
**Professional Songwriting** : Otis Blackwell is highlighted as an example of a professional songwriter in New York, notably one of the few black individuals to successfully secure work in this capacity for major publishing companies. He wrote numerous hits for various artists, demonstrating the industry's structure for creating popular music. "He was a New York-based songwriter, one of a relatively small number of black people, who managed to get work as a professional songwriter for one of the big publishing companies."
**Decline of First Rock and Roll Era (Late 1950s)** : The late 1950s marked the first decline in rock and roll's popularity, a traumatic period for the genre. This era saw a shift in musical fashions every few years, with new artists replacing previous stars. The decline was characterized by a combination of deaths (e.g., Buddy Holly, Ritchie Valens, The Big Bopper), evolving musical trends, and significant scandals involving major figures like Jerry Lee Lewis and Chuck Berry, which severely impacted their careers. "We're hitting a point in the history of rock and roll where, for the first time, rock and roll begins its decline in popularity."
**Cultural Context of Young Marriages in the Southern US** : In the Southern United States during the 1950s, it was considered normal for very young girls, sometimes as young as 12 or 13, to marry, often to older men. This cultural context, where such practices were widely accepted by some communities, informed Jerry Lee Lewis's belief that his marriage to his 13-year-old cousin, Myra Gale Brown, was not "particularly wrong," despite being universally condemned as child abuse from a contemporary perspective. Lewis himself had married at 16, his younger sister at 12, and marrying cousins was common within his extended family. "In the culture of the southern US at the time, it was normal for very young girls to be married, often to older men."
**Rock Journalism's Minimization of Abuse** : Traditional rock and roll histories have been criticized for minimizing or excusing the child sexual abuse committed by figures like Jerry Lee Lewis. These narratives often frame the downfall of such artists as a result of "blue-nosed prudes" or a "conspiracy" against rock and roll, rather than acknowledging public anger over genuinely "unquestionably just wrong" actions. This tendency highlights a problematic aspect of rock journalism's culture. "And the fact that this has been minimized by rock and roll histories says a lot about the culture around rock journalism, none of it good."
