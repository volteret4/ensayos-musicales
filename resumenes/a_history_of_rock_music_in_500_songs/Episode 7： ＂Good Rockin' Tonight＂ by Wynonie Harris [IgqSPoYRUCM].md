## curiosities
**Forgotten Figure in Black Music** : Lucky Millinder, though largely overlooked today, was one of the most significant figures in black music during the 1940s, known for his compelling personality and influential role. "Lucky Melinda is a largely forgotten figure now, but he was one of the most important figures in black music in the 1940s."
**Origin of "Lucky" Nickname** : A popular story suggests that Al Capone, after losing $10,000 in dice, was offered good luck by Millinder rubbing the dice. Capone subsequently won $50,000 and called him "Lucky" ever since. However, it's more probable that "Lucky" was a shortening of his birth name, Lucius, with the anecdote reflecting the kind of people Millinder associated with. "One story about how he got his name is that Al Capone was down $10,000 playing dice. Melinda offered to rub the dice for luck, and Capone ended the night $50,000 up and called him lucky from then on."
**Non-Musician Talent Recognizer** : Millinder did not play instruments, read music, or sing frequently, but he possessed an exceptional ability to identify and cultivate musical talent in others. "What Melinda could definitely do was recognize talent."
**Early Collaborations and Hires** : Millinder worked with Bill Doggett before Doggett joined The Ink Spots' backing band. For his first hit, Millinder hired trumpet player Dizzy Gillespie, who had recently been fired from Cab Calloway's band after stabbing Calloway in the leg. "He'd worked with Bill Doggett before Doggett went off to join the ink spots backing band, and the trumpet player on his first hit was Dizzy Gillespie, who Melinda had hired after Gillespie had been sacked from CapCalloway's band after stabbing Calloway in the leg."
**Female Singers in the 1940s** : At the beginning of the 1940s, Rosetta Tharpe was his female singer, providing occasional lead vocals. He also later worked with Ruth Brown. "He had rose at a tharp as his female singer at the beginning of the 40s, and Ruth Brown, who we'll talk about later."
**Leader of Mills Blue Rhythm Band** : Millinder started his career as the leader of the Mills Blue Rhythm Band, which served as the house band at the Cotton Club, before moving on to lead his own ensemble. "Later on, he'd started out as the leader of the mill's Blue Rhythm band, the house band in the cotton club."
**Residency at The Savoy** : Millinder's own band secured a main residency at The Savoy, a prominent venue, and also undertook occasional tours on the "Chitlin' Circuit." "Before moving on to lead, asked his own band, one of the main bands in residence at the Savoy, along with occasionally touring the Chitlin circuit."
**Transition to Jump Band Style** : Throughout the 1940s, Millinder progressively shifted his band's sound from the popular swing music of the 1930s to the more contemporary jump band style, which was gaining widespread appeal. "Slowly, during the 1940s, Melinda transitioned his band from the kind of swing music that had been popular in the 30s to the jump band style that was becoming more popular."
**Pioneer of Rhythm and Blues** : Millinder's band is widely considered a strong candidate for being the first rhythm and blues band, as they uniquely combined boogie, jump, and jive sounds with a powerful blues feeling, specifically designed to make people dance. "And if you want to point to one band that you can call the first rhythm in Blue's band, you probably want to look at Melinda's band, who more than any other band of the era were able to combine all the boogie, jump, and give sounds with a strong blues feeling and get people dancing."
**Replacement of Wynonie Harris (1940s)** : After Wynonie Harris left the band to pursue a solo career, Lucky Millinder replaced him with Henry Glover, a trumpet player and singer. "Harris went solo, and Lucky Millinda replaced him with a trumpet player and singer called Henry Glover."

## songs
**Who Threw the Whiskey in the Well (May 1944)** : Recorded in May 1944, immediately after the musicians' union strike, this song features a strong backbeat, a key element of emerging rock and roll. Its release was delayed for almost a year due to wartime rationing of shellac and the prioritization of vinylite for life jackets, limiting record production. "That was recorded in May 1944, almost straight after the end of the musician's union strike, but it wasn't released straight away."
**Around the Clock Blues (Late 1940s)** : This marked Wynonie Harris's first solo record and is recognized as one of the most influential records ever made, serving as a significant milestone in the development of Rock and Roll. The backing band on this recording was led by session drummer Johnny Otis. "His first record as a solo artist was around the clock blues, one of the most influential records ever made."
**Dig This Boogie (Late 1940s)** : This single, recorded around the late 1940s, is notable for featuring Herman "Sonny" Blount, who would later become famous as Sun Ra, on piano. It was released during Harris's period of recording for various small labels. "And just as a brief diversion, we'll take a listen to one of the singles he made around this time, Dig This Boogie."
**Good Rockin' Tonight (Late 1940s)** : Wynonie Harris's cover version of Roy Brown's song, this rendition became a world-changing hit in the R&B market, significantly outselling Brown's original. It features distinctive "hoi hoi" vocals, borrowed from "The Honeydripper," and an instrumental introduction based on "When the Saints Go Marching In," along with an added backbeat, leading many to consider it the first rock and roll record. "Not Roy Brown's original is the one that people call the first rock and roll record."
**All She Wants to Do Is Rock (Late 1940s)** : Following the immense success of "Good Rockin' Tonight," Harris recorded this sequel song, which contributed to the proliferation of "rock"-titled records that characterized the R&B scene in the late 1940s. "Harris himself recorded a sequel, all she wants to do is rock."
## curiosities
**Early Nickname "Mr. Blues"** : Wynonie Harris was already known as "Mr. Blues" when Lucky Millinder first encountered him performing in Chicago and subsequently invited him to join his band. "Harris was already known as Mr Blues, when Melinda first saw him playing in Chicago and invited him to join the band."
**Vocal Influences** : Harris was primarily recognized as a blues shouter, drawing inspiration from artists such as Big Joe Turner and Jimmy Rushing. However, he also possessed the versatility to perform in a more subtle style, akin to the jive singing of Cab Calloway or Louis Jordan. "He was primarily a blues shelter, inspired by people like Big Joe Turner and Jimmy Rushing, but he could also perform in a subtler style, close to the give singing of a cap-caller way or Louis Jordan."
**Joined Millinder's Band Residency** : Upon joining Lucky Millinder's band, Harris began performing with them during their residency at the renowned Savoy venue. "Harris joined the Melinda band and started performing with them in their residency at the Savoy."
**Underpaid for Hit Song** : Despite "Who Threw the Whiskey in the Well" becoming a massive hit, Harris received only a flat session fee of $37.50, while Lucky Millinder collected the royalties. This perceived injustice fueled Harris's belief that his vocal contributions were solely responsible for the record's success and that he could earn more independently. "It became a massive hit, and the only money Harris saw from it was a flat $37.50 session fee."
**Remarkable Stage Presence** : Wynonie Harris possessed an "astonishing stage presence," with many later commentators suggesting that Elvis Presley's iconic performances, including his hip thrusts and leg shaking, were merely a "watered down version" of what Harris had already been doing. "Lots of people who spoke about Elvis Presley in later years said that his performances, hip thrusts and leg shaking at all were just a watered down version of what Wainoni Harris had been doing."
**Highly Confident Personality** : Harris was renowned for his extreme self-confidence and self-belief, which propelled his success on stage but often led to strained personal relationships. This confidence contributed to his conviction that he was a major star deserving greater recognition and financial reward. "In case you hadn't realised yet, Wainoni Harris was never known as the most self-effacing of people, and that confidence gave him a huge amount of success on stage, but it didn't win him many friends in his personal life."
**Departure from Millinder's Band** : Driven by his confidence and the belief that he was undercompensated for "Who Threw the Whiskey in the Well," Harris decided to leave Lucky Millinder's band and embark on a solo career. "Harris went solo, and Lucky Millinda replaced him with a trumpet player and singer called Henry Glover."
**Financial Success and Extravagance** : Harris boasted of earning $1,500 a week by specifically targeting women with disposable income ("meat on their bones" and "long green stuff in their pocketbook"), rather than younger, less affluent audiences. He spent his earnings lavishly, once buying two Cadillacs and hiring two chauffeurs after receiving a $10,000 royalty check. "I star in Georgia, Texas, Alabama, Tennessee, and Missouri, and get those who have money to buy stronger stuff and my records to play while they drink it."
**Decline in Career and Death** : Harris's hit-making career largely ended by 1953, and he virtually ceased performing by the early 1960s, finding that contemporary bands struggled to play his boogie style. Accounts of his final performances describe him as a "sad and pitiful figure." He died in 1969, reportedly due to complications from excessive drinking. "He died in 1969, after more or less drinking himself to death."

## songs
**Good Rockin' Tonight (Late 1940s)** : Roy Brown wrote this song, confident it would resonate with wider audiences due to its consistent success in his own live shows. He initially offered it to Wynonie Harris, who declined. After relocating to New Orleans, Brown presented it to Cecil Gant, who, deeply impressed, made a 2:30 AM call to his record company head, securing Brown a recording deal. This original version became a minor hit, peaking at number 13 on the R&B chart and later re-entering at number 11. "In particular, one called Good Rock in Tonight, he thought might be popular with other audiences, since it always went down so well in his own shows."
**Musical Structure of "Good Rockin' Tonight" (Original)** : Roy Brown's original recording of "Good Rockin' Tonight" is characterized by holding the first chord and repeating the same riff for the majority of the verse and chorus. Its chord changes are closer to passing chords, resulting in a "one chord feel" that emphasizes groove over complex harmonic progression, diverging from a conventional 12-bar blues structure. "Roy Brown's original, on the other hand, just holds the first chord and keeps playing the same riff for almost the entire verse and chorus."
**"Good Rockin' Tonight" Name-Checks** : The song openly displays its musical influences by name-checking popular characters from contemporary A&B songs, including Sweet Lorraine, Sue City Sue, Sweet Georgia Brown, and Caldonia. "In fact, it wears its influences on its sleeve so much that its name checks, sweet Lorraine, Sue City Sue, Sweet Georgia Brown and Caldonia."
**Rockin' at Midnight (Late 1940s)** : This song served as Roy Brown's own sequel to "Good Rockin' Tonight," contributing to the prevalent "rock craze" of the late 1940s, where many records featured "rock" in their titles. "There was Roy Brown's own sequel, rockin' at midnight."
## curiosities
**Fan of Wynonie Harris** : Roy Brown was a significant admirer of Wynonie Harris and performed as a blues singer, often in a similar style to Harris. "Roy Brown was a big fan of Harris and was a blues singer himself. In something like the same style."
**Early Vocal Style and Influences** : Initially, Brown's vocal style was distinct, leading him to be hired as a "black singer who sounds white," which was unusual given his frequent use of melisma, a characteristic typically associated with black singing. His primary vocal influences were crooners like Bing Crosby, rather than traditional blues artists. "Brown's main vocal influences when he started were people like Bing Fosby and other crewners, rather than blues music."
**Shift to Rhythm and Blues** : Brown soon developed a profound appreciation for rhythm and blues music, subsequently beginning to compose his own songs within this genre. "However, he soon became very fond of drum blues and started writing songs in the style himself."
**Offer to Wynonie Harris (Rejected)** : Brown composed "Good Rockin' Tonight," believing in its popular potential. He offered the song to Wynonie Harris when Harris was performing in town, but Harris expressed no interest at that time. "Brown suggested the song to him, and Harris wasn't interested."
**Move from Galveston to New Orleans** : Roy Brown relocated back to New Orleans from Galveston, Texas, where he had been performing, due to unspecified personal reasons involving "a girl, and a club owner." "But after Brown moved back to New Orleans from Galveston, Texas, where he'd been performing, there was a girl, and a club owner, and these things happen and sometimes you have to move."
**Difficulty with Record Companies** : Roy Brown developed a reputation for being "difficult" after he sought assistance from the Musicians' Union to recover money owed to him by his record company. This led to a period in the 1950s where he struggled to achieve hit records. "Roy Brown got a reputation for being difficult. After he went to the musician's union to try to get some of the money the record company owed him."
**Post-Music Career and Death (1981)** : After enjoying success in the late 1940s and very early 1950s, Brown spent decades unable to secure a hit. He eventually began selling encyclopedias door-to-door, using the unique marketing tactic of offering autographed photos of himself in black neighborhoods. He continued to release occasional R&B records but earned more from his encyclopedia sales before his death in 1981. "He eventually started selling in Cyclopidia's door to door, with the unique gimmick that when he was in black neighborhoods, he could offer the people whose doors he was knocking on, a nautical raft photo of himself."

## curiosities
**Highly Successful with "I Wonder"** : Cecil Gant was enjoying considerable success with "I Wonder," his biggest hit, which was a ballad, when Roy Brown approached him seeking to have "Good Rockin' Tonight" recorded. "Gant was writing high off his biggest hit, I Wonder, which was a ballad."
**Dual Musical Style** : While Gant's A-side releases were typically ballads, his B-sides consistently featured boogie rockers, a style that perfectly aligned with Roy Brown's "Good Rockin' Tonight." "Everything Gant recorded was improvised in one take, and he could never remember what it was he'd just done and could never repeat a song."
**Securing Roy Brown's Deal** : Despite his improvisational nature, Gant was so impressed by Roy Brown's singing and his song "Good Rockin' Tonight" that he called the head of his record company at 2:30 AM. After hearing Brown sing the song twice over the phone, the executive instructed Gant to give Brown $50 and ensure he remained in sight, leading to Brown's recording deal. "But he was so impressed by Brown singing, as well as his song, that he phoned the head of his record company at 2.30am and got Brown to sing down the phone."

# genre - Rhythm and Blues
## curiosities
**Combination of Styles** : Rhythm and Blues emerged as a genre from the successful combination of boogie, jump, and jive sounds, infused with a strong blues feeling, specifically crafted to encourage dancing. Lucky Millinder's band is considered a strong candidate for being the first true R&B band due to their effective integration of these elements. "And if you want to point to one band that you can call the first rhythm in Blue's band, you probably want to look at Melinda's band, who more than any other band of the era were able to combine all the boogie, jump, and give sounds with a strong blues feeling and get people dancing."
**Early Popularity and "Rock Craze" (1948-1951)** : Between 1948 and 1951, rhythm and blues records were heavily influenced by a "rock craze," characterized by songs featuring "rock" in their titles, a boogie-woogie baseline, a backbeat, and lyrics focused on rocking and rolling. This period is identified as the actual beginning of the rock and roll music fad within the R&B scene, six years before it captured the attention of mainstream white audiences. "From 1948 through about 1951, if you listened to rhythm and blues records at all, you couldn't escape this new rock craze."

# genre - Rock and Roll
## curiosities
**Backbeat and Boogie Bass Combination** : The defining elements that truly constitute rock and roll are the combination of a backbeat and a boogie bass, a pairing that became increasingly prevalent in popular music during the mid-1940s. "One of the things we talked about right back in episode two was that the combination of the backbeat and the boogie bass is what really makes rock and roll, and we're now getting to the point that that combination was turning up more and more."
**Milestones in Development** : Records such as Wynonie Harris's "Around the Clock Blues" are recognized as significant milestones in the gradual development of rock and roll, even if they aren't considered the singularly most important records by Harris. "On the clock, in fact, is one of the milestones in the development of Rock and Roll."
**"First Rock and Roll Record" Debate** : While Jackie Brenston's "Rocket 88" (1951) is often cited as the "canonical first rock and roll record," Wynonie Harris's version of "Good Rockin' Tonight" is presented as an equally strong candidate, particularly due to its prominent backbeat. The concept of a single "first" rock and roll record is generally viewed as contentious, with the genre's emergence being a more diffuse process. "Not Roy Brown's original is the one that people call the first rock and roll record."
**Cohesive Movement by 1948** : Rock and roll solidified into a "cohesive unified whole" and a recognizable, popular musical movement around 1948. This era was marked by records featuring a boogie-woogie baseline, a backbeat, and lyrics about rocking and rolling, predating its widespread notice by the white mainstream audience by six years. "1948 is when rock and roll first became a cohesive unified whole, something that was recognizable and popular, a proper movement in music rather than odd individuals making their own separate music."
**Early Instrumentation** : First-wave rock and roll was primarily instrumented by piano and horn sections, with guitars playing a lesser role. The electric guitar only began to gain significant importance in the early 1950s, following its merger with Hillbilly Boogie. "First wave rock and roll is a music that's based on the piano and horn sections, rather than guitars, and it wouldn't be until it merged with Hilboly Boogie in the early 50s that the electric guitar started to be an important instrument in it."
**"Rocky Era" Beginning** : The period characterized by songs like "Good Rockin' Tonight" and the subsequent "rock craze" in R&B is identified as the true beginning of the "Rocky era" in music history. "but we're now finally into the Rocky era."

# genre - Blues

**Strong Feeling in Jump Bands** : A strong blues feeling was a crucial component successfully integrated with boogie, jump, and jive sounds by Lucky Millinder's band, which is considered an early rhythm and blues group, to create music specifically for dancing. # genre - Pop
## curiosities
**Three-Minute Pop Song Format** : The music industry provided an avenue for non-musician shapers to transform raw talent, such as a "spotty teenager with a guitar," into a star, even if their stardom lasted only for the duration of a "three minute pop song." "Men who have the power to take some spotty teenager with a guitar and turn him into a god, at least for the course of a three minute pop song."

# genre - Ballad
## curiosities
**Cecil Gant's A-sides** : Ballads were a characteristic feature of Cecil Gant's A-side record releases, contrasting with his boogie rocker B-sides. His major hit, "I Wonder," was a prominent example of his ballad style. "But while Gant A-sides were ballad, his B-sides were Boogie Rockers."

# genre - Boogie Rockers
## curiosities
**Cecil Gant's B-sides** : Boogie Rockers were the typical style for Cecil Gant's B-side record releases, setting them apart from his ballad A-sides, and were stylistically very similar to Roy Brown's "Good Rockin' Tonight." "his B-sides were Boogie Rockers, and very much in the style of Brown's song."

# genre - Hillbilly Boogie
## curiosities
**Merger with Early Rock and Roll** : Hillbilly Boogie merged with the nascent first-wave rock and roll in the early 1950s, a pivotal development that led to the electric guitar becoming a vitally important instrument within the rock genre. "it wouldn't be until it merged with Hilboly Boogie in the early 50s that the electric guitar started to be an important instrument in it."

# label - Deluxe Records
## curiosities
**Signed Roy Brown for "Good Rockin' Tonight"** : Deluxe Records signed Roy Brown after Cecil Gant facilitated a late-night phone audition with the label head. This led to Brown recording "Good Rockin' Tonight," which subsequently became a minor hit. "And so Roy Brown ended up recording his song on Deluxe Records and having a minor hit with it."

# concert - Cotton Club
## curiosities
**House Band for Mills Blue Rhythm Band** : The Cotton Club was the venue where Lucky Millinder's Mills Blue Rhythm Band served as the resident house band during Millinder's early career. "he'd started out as the leader of the mill's Blue Rhythm band, the house band in the cotton club."

# concert - Savoy
## curiosities
**Main Residence for Millinder's Band** : The Savoy was a significant venue where Lucky Millinder's band held one of the main residencies, occasionally supplemented by tours on the Chitlin' Circuit. Wynonie Harris joined Millinder's band and performed during their residency there. "one of the main bands in residence at the Savoy, along with occasionally touring the Chitlin circuit."

# concert - Chitlin' Circuit
## curiosities
**Tour Stops for Black Artists** : The Chitlin' Circuit was a derogatory but commonly used term referring to a network of clubs and theaters that served as regular tour destinations for nearly all major black artists of the era. Lucky Millinder's band, among others, occasionally toured this circuit. "the rather derogatory name for the clubs and theatres that were regular tour stops for almost all major black artists at the time."

# instrument - Trombone
## curiosities
**Glenn Miller's Instrument** : Glenn Miller was a competent trombone player, although the success and appeal of his band were not primarily attributed to his individual virtuosity on the instrument. "Glenn Miller was a competent enough trombone player, but no one listens to the Glenn Miller band, and thinks, wow, one of those four trombone players is fantastic."

# instrument - Guitar
## curiosities
**Secondary in Early Rock and Roll** : In the initial wave of rock and roll, the guitar was not a primary instrument; the music was largely based on piano and horn sections. The electric guitar only gained significant importance in the early 1950s after the genre merged with Hillbilly Boogie. "First wave rock and roll is a music that's based on the piano and horn sections, rather than guitars, and it wouldn't be until it merged with Hilboly Boogie in the early 50s that the electric guitar started to be an important instrument in it."

# curiosity
**"Phanomancers" in the Music Industry** : Some individuals in the music industry, despite not being traditional musicians (playing instruments or singing), significantly shape music by producing, managing, or acting as DJs or journalists. They are responsible for getting great records made by molding and shaping the work of others, effectively creating their own creative work through the efforts of others. "There are people in the music industry who don't themselves play an instrument or sing or any of the normal musician things, but who manage to get great records made, records which are their creative work, by moulding and shaping the work of others."
**Individuals Who Shaped Music Without Being Musicians** : Brian Eno, Sam Phillips, Joe Meek, Phil Spector, Malcolm McLaren, and Simon Napier Bell are cited as examples of individuals who significantly influenced music without being traditional musicians. They had a vision and a flair for self-publicity, guiding musicians to turn their ideas into reality. "Brianino, Sam Phillips, Joe Meek, Phil Specter, Malcolm McLaren, Simon Napier Bell."
**Influence of Non-Playing Band Leaders** : Many non-playing band leaders, though sometimes singing rarely, exerted immense influence by conceiving a specific sound, assembling the right musicians to achieve it, and fostering a productive work environment. Their talent lay in management or editorial roles, not instrumental performance or songwriting. "It was having an idea for a sound and getting together the right people who could make that sound and creating a work environment in which they could make that sound well."
**Shellac Rationing and Vinylite Prioritization (WWII Era)** : The release of records in the mid-1940s was significantly impacted by wartime rationing. Shellac, derived from insect secretions from Vietnam (occupied by Japan) and India (facing its own problems), was scarce. While vinylite, a promising new product, could be used for records, it was prioritized for life jackets during the war, causing considerable delays in record releases. "Records at that time were released on discs made out of shellac, which is a resin made from insect secretions. Unfortunately, the insects in question were native to Vietnam, which was occupied by Japan and to India, which was going through its own problems at the time. So shellac was strictly rationed."
**Music for Dancing vs. Harmonic Interest** : There exists a fundamental tension in music between compositions that prioritize rhythm for dancing and those that emphasize melody and harmonic complexity. Generally, music with fewer chord changes is easier to dance to, while music focused on harmonic interest provides an opportunity for more intricate melodies. "The more chord something has, in general, the harder it is to dance to. And there will always, always, be attention between music that's all about the rhythm, and which is there for you to dance to, and music which is all about the melody line, and which treats harmonic interest as an excuse to write more interesting melodies."
**Origin of "Hoi Hoi" Vocals** : The distinctive "hoi hoi" vocals introduced by Wynonie Harris in his cover of "Good Rockin' Tonight" were borrowed from "The Honeydripper," described as another massively important R&B record. "That's a line which comes from the honeydrepper, another massively important R&B record."
**"Rock Craze" (1948-1951)** : From 1948 to approximately 1951, the rhythm and blues market was engulfed in a "rock craze," characterized by numerous records featuring "rock" in their titles, a boogie-woogie baseline, a backbeat, and lyrics explicitly about rocking and rolling. This period is considered the true initiation of the rock and roll fad within the R&B genre, predating its entry into the white mainstream by six years. "From 1948 through about 1951, if you listened to rhythm and blues records at all, you couldn't escape this new rock craze."
**Music Industry's Treatment of Originals** : The music business is often unfriendly towards "originals," particularly black originals, leading to situations where pioneering artists might struggle or fail to achieve lasting success despite their significant contributions. "The music business is never friendly towards originals, especially black originals."
