## albums
**Elvis' Gold Records Volume 2 (1959)** : This compilation album, along with other repackaged single-only recordings, was issued by RCA during Elvis's two years in the army to keep him in the spotlight despite him not recording new material. It contained hits like "My Wish Came True," "Don't," and "I Need Your Love Tonight." "Along with those hits, they repackaged several single-only recordings into new albums, and managed to keep Elvis in the spotlight, despite him not recording any new material."
**Elvis's Back (1960)** : This was Elvis's first album of new material since his Christmas album two and a half years prior, recorded two weeks and one day after his discharge from the army. It is considered "quite possibly the most consistent studio album Elvis ever made," with the second 1960 session yielding the most impressive material. The album went to number two on the charts and sold half a million copies. "Elvis's Back is quite possibly the most consistent studio album Elvis ever made, and that second 1960 session is where the most impressive material on the album was recorded."
**G.I. Blues (1960) - Soundtrack** : The soundtrack to Elvis's first post-Army film, which he disliked, was a massive commercial success. It went to number one on the charts and stayed there for 11 weeks, selling a million copies, an "absurd number" for albums at the time. This commercial success ironically cast the die for his future film career, pushing him towards more light comedies. "the soundtrack to GI Blues went to number one and stayed there for 11 weeks, and sold a million copies, an absurd number at a time when albums generally sold very little."
**His Hand in Mine (1960) - Gospel Album** : This was Elvis's first full-length gospel record and is considered "probably the purest expression of Elvis's own musical instincts yet," as he had always wanted to be a singer in a gospel quartet. He sang gospel songs with the Jordanaires exactly as he wanted to, but the album only made number 13 on the charts. "His handyman was probably the purest expression of Elvis's own musical instincts yet."
**Blue Hawaii (1961) - Soundtrack** : The soundtrack for this "mindless film" full of "sea, sand and bikinis" and "dreadful songs" (like "Aloha Oe") massively outsold Elvis's studio album released the same year. This demonstrated a repeating pattern where commercially successful, lighter film soundtracks eclipsed his more artistically fulfilling studio work. "Their studio album was massively outsold by the soundtrack album for Blue Hawaii, a mindless film that was full of sea, sand and bikinis, and which featured dreadful songs like Otto Eat's."

## curiosities
**Post-Army Transformation** : Elvis's two years in the army profoundly changed him, marked by his mother's death, separation from his familiar circle, and meeting Priscilla Beaulieu, who would later become his wife. He returned to a music world where rockabilly had disappeared from the charts. "Elvis' two years spent in the army had changed him profoundly."
**Strategic Recording Stockpile** : While in the army, Elvis continued to have hits because his manager, Colonel Parker, had orchestrated recording sessions in 1958 to create a small stockpile of material. This allowed RCA to issue singles over the two years Elvis was away, keeping him in the spotlight without new recordings. "He'd recorded and off in 1958, for R.C.E. to have a tiny stockpile of recordings they could issue as singles over the intervening two years."
**Colonel Parker's Contract Leverage Strategy** : Colonel Parker's strategy was to ensure Elvis recorded only the minimum material stipulated by his RCA contracts, and "not one song more." This was to provide leverage in future contract negotiations, preventing a surplus of unreleased material from diminishing his bargaining power. "His strategy then, and from then on, was to record precisely as much material for R.C.E. as the contracts stipulated they were entitled to, and not one song more."
**Graceland Post-Army Return** : Elvis had only a couple of weeks to settle into Graceland, the home he bought for his mother but barely inhabited before his draft. Upon his return, it was to be shared with his father and his father's new, much younger girlfriend, whom Elvis "definitely did not approve." "He had a couple of weeks to settle in at Graceland, the home he had bought for his mother."
**Segregated Cinema Visit** : During his post-army two-week leave, Elvis visited a cinema and an ice-skating show, choosing to attend the performance for "Black people, rather than the one for whites," indicating his stance against segregation. He also made a brief, impromptu appearance conducting the orchestra at that show. "He went to the performance for Black people, rather than the one for whites, as when this was still segregated."
**Grave Visit** : Importantly, during his two-week post-army leave, Elvis visited his mother's grave for the first time, a deeply personal moment for him. "And most importantly to him, he visited the grave of his mother for the first time."
**Nashville A-Team Collaboration (1960)** : For his first post-army recording session, Elvis worked with many of the Nashville A-Team musicians, who were crucial to the Nashville Sound and had played on his last pre-army session in 1958. To deter fans, they were told it was a session for country singer Jim Reeves. "Many of the Nashville A team, the musicians who were responsible for making those records with Atkins, or the other main producer of the sound, are in Bradley, had played on Elvis's last session before he went into the army, and they were at this session."
**Bill Black's Departure (1960)** : Bill Black, Elvis's original bass player who often pushed for more recognition, was not included in Elvis's first post-army recording session. By this time, Black was a star in his own right with his Bill Black Combo and never recorded with Elvis again. "Bill Black was not included at all. Black had always been the one who had tried to push for Moore recognition, and he was now a star in his own right, with his Bill Black combo."
**Initial Session Atmosphere (1960)** : The first hour of Elvis's initial post-army recording session was leisurely, spent ordering hamburgers, listening to demos, and Elvis and Bobby Moore showing each other karate moves, before they got down to recording. "The first hour of our show was spent ordering in hamburgers, listening to demos, and Elvis and Bobby Moore showing each other karate moves."
**Vocal Improvement in Army (1960)** : Elvis's voice "improved dramatically" during his time in the army. He practiced extensively with his friend Charlie Hodge, adding "a full octave to his vocal range" and becoming eager to display his ability to tackle different kinds of material. "Elvis's voice had improved dramatically during his time in the army. He had been practicing a lot with his new friend Charlie Hodge, and had added a full octave to his vocal range."
**First Post-Army TV Appearance (1960)** : Elvis made what would be his only TV appearance for eight years on "The Frank Sinatra Timex Show, Welcome Home Elvis," arranged by Colonel Parker. Sinatra, who had previously been critical and would continue to be insulting towards Elvis, hosted the special. "By that time, Elvis had already made what would turn out to be his only TV appearance for 8 years."
**Train Trip Fan Reception (1960)** : Scotty Moore vividly recalled the train trip to Florida for the TV show, noting that at "every single crossroads the train tracks went past, there were people lined up to cheer on the train," comparing it only to the funeral journeys of Abraham Lincoln and Franklin D. Roosevelt. "Scotty Moore would always remember, saying that at every single crossroads the train tracks went past, there were people lined up to cheer on the train."
**Elvis's "Little Pills" (1960)** : During the train journey to Florida, Scotty Moore also remembered that Elvis offered him some "little pills" he had been taking in the army to stay awake and alert, hinting at early drug use. "Elvis had offered him some of the little pills he'd been taking in the army to keep him awake and alert."
**Colonel's Socializing Ban (1960)** : During rehearsals for the TV show in Miami, Colonel Parker explicitly forbade Scotty Moore, DJ Fontana, and the Jordanaires from socializing with Elvis, despite their friendly relationship on the train journey. "Scotty, DJ and the Jordan Ayers were forbidden from socialising with Elvis, by order of the Colonel."
**Family-Friendly Image Attempt (1960)** : On the Sinatra TV special, Elvis appeared in a dinner jacket, clearly attempting to project a new, "family friendly entertainer" image. He initially looked "deeply uncomfortable" grooming his way through "Fame and Fortune." "dressed in a dinner jacket and clearly attempting to prove he was now a family friendly entertainer, he looks deeply uncomfortable at first."
**Sinatra Duet & Postmodern Performance (1960)** : During a duet on the TV special, Sinatra sang "Love Me Tender" while Elvis sang Sinatra's "Witchcraft." By this point, Elvis was completely comfortable and "wipes the floor with Sinatra." Elvis's performance is described as "postmodernist" (knowingly mocking himself while embracing the cliche), contrasting with Sinatra's "modernist" contempt for the material. "While Elvis is postmodernist, since he is even as he's also knowingly mocking himself."
**One Overnight Session (1960)** : During one overnight recording session, Elvis and his band recorded an astonishing 12 songs, covering an "almost inconceivable" stylistic range that encompassed "every style of American popular song as of 1960." "During that one overnight session, Elvis and the band recorded 12 songs, covering a stylistic range that's almost inconceivable."
**Creative Success Post-RCA Signing (1960)** : The 18 tracks (three singles and a 12-track album) Elvis recorded between his initial post-army sessions were considered "every bit as artistically successful" as his Sun recordings, and represent "the best creative work he had done since signing to RCA." "it's very easy to make the case that they were every bit as artistically successful, and this was certainly the best creative work he had done since signing to RCA."
**Film Songwriting Issues (1960)** : For his first post-army film, "G.I. Blues," Elvis was annoyed that he wasn't recording any Leiber and Stoller songs, discovering that Colonel Parker had made them "persona non grata." Instead, he recorded songs by "journeyman songwriters with little taste for Ruck and Roll," which he disliked. "Elvis seems not to have been aware that the Colonel had made them persona non grata."
**Context for Film Songs** : It is argued that Elvis's film songs should be criticized carefully, as they were intended to drive a plot forward within a film, not to be listened to as standalone singles or albums. "These aren't songs that were intended to be listened to as singles or albums, but they were intended to drive a plot forward and to exist in the context of a film."
**"G.I. Blues" Performance Decline (1960)** : Bones Howe, who worked on the "G.I. Blues" sessions, observed that Elvis seemed to have "lost something" compared to his pre-army work, "now trained, and often failing, to find his way into a performance which pre-army he would have been able to do naturally." This was attributed to Elvis's lack of desire for the film and its songs, rather than his army service. "He was now trained, and often failing, to find his way into a performance which pre-army he would have been able to do naturally."
**Contractual Obligation for "G.I. Blues"** : Despite Elvis telling Colonel Parker that at least half the songs for the "G.I. Blues" soundtrack should be scrapped, the Colonel insisted Elvis was "locked into them by contract" and had to do his best, which he did, but "his heart wasn't in it." "Elvis told the Colonel that at least half the songs for the film soundtrack had to be scrapped, but the Colonel told him he was locked into them by contract, and he just had to do the best he could with them."
**Serious Film Attempts (1960-1961)** : Elvis was placated by promises that his next two films, "Flaming Star" and "Wild in the Country," would be "proper films" like those he made before the army. Both were intended to be serious dramas, even more so than "Jailhouse Rock" and "King Creole." "These next two films were made back to back. Flaming Star was a western, with a rather heavy handed message about racism."
**"Flaming Star" Role & Director (1960)** : In "Flaming Star," a western with a heavy-handed message about racism, Elvis starred as a mixed-race man. The role was originally intended for his acting idol, Marlon Brando, and the film was directed by Don Siegel, who would later direct "Dirty Harry." "Elvis's role was originally intended for Mildred and Brando, his acting idol, and he only sang one song in the film, other than the title song which played over the credits."
**"Wild in the Country" Character (1961)** : In this film, Elvis played a troubled young man who undergoes psychological counseling before going to college to become a writer, featuring only a small number of songs. "Wild in the Country, which featured only a very small number of songs, and had Elvis playing a troubled young man who has to get caught or did psychological counselling."
**Post-"G.I. Blues" Career Path (1962-1968)** : The unexpected commercial success of "G.I. Blues" cemented Elvis's film career path. Colonel Parker decided Elvis would no longer appear in dramatic roles, instead committing him to a routine of three interchangeable, light comedies a year, often set in exotic locales with Elvis as a surfer or race car driver, a "treadmill" he couldn't escape until 1968. "From that point on the die was cast for Elvis. The Colonel wasn't going to let him appear in any more dramatic roles."
**Irrelevance in the 60s (1962-1968)** : From 1962 to 1968, Elvis mostly "sat out the 60s," becoming irrelevant to the new generation of musicians. John Lennon's sentiment that "Elvis died when he went into the army" captured this perception. "While the music world was changing all around him, he was in a relevance to the new generation of musicians, who mostly agreed with John Lennon that Elvis died when he went into the army."
**Post-Treadmill Film Attempts (After 1968)** : After 1968, Elvis did make a handful of films where he again tried to be an actor, but after two decades of lightweight films about beaches and bikinis, "no one noticed." "After 68 he did make a handful of films in which again he tried to be an actor, but after 20 or so lightweight films about beaches and bikinis, no one noticed."

**Copyright on "O Sole Mio"** : The song "O Sole Mio," composed in 1897, was long considered to be in the public domain, but a later Italian court ruling determined it will remain in copyright until 2042. This required Freddie Bienstock to commission new lyrics for Elvis Presley's "It's Now or Never," which was based on a version of the tune. "Though in fact, a later Italian court ruling means that even though it was composed in 1897, it will remain in copyright until 2042."
**Definition of Postmodernism (Umberto Eco)** : Umberto Eco, in his book about writing "The Name of the Rose," explains postmodernism as embracing and acknowledging a cliche, allowing for the discussion of real emotions that modernism, by rejecting cliche, cannot. For example, a postmodernist might write, "as Barbara Cartland might say, I love you Mabli," in contrast to a modernist's avoidance of such sentiments. "This, crucially, means that the postmodernist is, once again, able to talk about real emotions, which the modernist, in Ecos' view, can't."
**Postmodernism in Music Performance** : By Umberto Eco's definition, Frank Sinatra's performance, showing contempt for the material, is "modernist," while Elvis Presley's performance, knowingly mocking himself even while embracing a cliche, is "postmodernist." "By this definition, Sinatra's performance is modernist. He's just showing contempt for the material. While Elvis is postmodernist, since he is even as he's also knowingly mocking himself."
