## albums
**The Tommy Steele's Story (Film Soundtrack)** : This film, based on Steele's life, had its 12 songs written by Steele, Lionel Bart, and Mike Pratt in just one week to meet a deadline. "a film based on his life for which he, Bart and Pratt wrote all 12 of the songs in a week to meet the deadline."

## songs
**Knock on Wood** : As a young teenager, Thomas Hicks would mime to a record of this song by Danny Kay as part of a routine he performed in pubs. "he would mind to a record by Danny Kay, Knock on Wood."
**Call Elijah** : This Hank Williams song became Thomas Hicks' new party piece after he fell in love with Hank Williams' music on a cruise ship. He would sing it with accompaniment from anyone he could persuade to play guitar, eventually learning to play it himself from a crewmate. "singing Call Elijah, with accompaniment from anyone he could persuade to play guitar for him."
**Rock with the Cave Man (1956)** : Co-written by Hicks, Lionel Bart, and Mike Pratt, this song was performed by Hicks when he jumped on stage during a Viper's Skiffle Group break at The Two Eyes, impressing PR man John Kennedy. The single went to number 13 on the UK charts after he was signed to Decca Records. "sang a song that he, Bart and Pratt had written called Rock with the Cave Man."
**Elevator Rock** : This song, described as "A working store on the edge of town, but Dave Lee chose going up and down," did not chart well after "Rock with the Cave Man." "The next record, elevator rock, didn't do so well however."
**Singing The Blues (1956)** : A cover version of a Guy Mitchell hit (which itself was a cover of Marty Robbins), Steele's version was recorded to be released before Mitchell's hit the charts, featuring an identical arrangement but sung with an "incredibly manored Elvis impression." Despite being released shortly after Mitchell's, a minor distribution problem for Mitchell's label allowed Steele's version to briefly take the top spot for one week, making him the first British rock and roll singer to reach number one in the UK charts. "Steele's version has an identical arrangement and sound to Mitchell's, except that Steele sings it in an incredibly manored Elvis impression."

## curiosities
**Early Life and Aspirations** : Thomas Hicks came from a dirt-poor background, experienced the death of three siblings at young ages, and suffered from ill health himself, which limited his formal education despite being a voracious reader. From an early age, he wanted to be a performer, developing a routine of miming to Danny Kay records in pubs. "Hicks had come from an absolutely dirt poor background."
**Merchant Navy Career (age 15)** : At age 15, Thomas Hicks joined the merchant navy, working as wait staff on a cruise ship that made regular trips to America. This provided him exposure to American music, notably Hank Williams. "at age 15 he had joined the merchant navy."
**False Claim of Buddy Holly Encounter (1955)** : Tommy Steele claimed in his autobiography and other sources that he saw Buddy Holly sing "Peggy Sue" on a Grand Ole Opry tour in Norfolk, Virginia, in 1955, which inspired his turn to rock and roll. However, this story is inaccurate; Buddy Holly didn't tour until May 1956 (three months after Hicks left cruise ships), only toured in Oklahoma (a landlocked state), and "Peggy Sue" was not written until 1957. "So, whatever it was that introduced young Tommy Hicks to the wonders of rock and roll, it wasn't seeing Buddy Holly sing Peggy Sue in North of Virginia in 1955."
**False Claim of Gymnastics Instructor Role** : Early in his career, Thomas Hicks claimed to have worked his way up to being a gymnastics instructor on cruise ships, a claim that was denied to the press by the Shipping Federation. "he claimed he'd worked his way up on the cruise ships to being a gymnastics instructor, something that the shipping Federation denied to the press."
**Renamed Tommy Steele** : Thomas Hicks was renamed Tommy Steele by his managers, John Kennedy and Larry Parns, as a nod to his paternal grandfather, Thomas Steele Hicks. The spelling of Steele (STIL or STIGL) varies by source. "Hicks was renamed Tommy Steele, in a nod to his paternal grandfather, Thomas Steele Hicks."
**Staged High-Society Rock and Roll Show** : To generate national attention, John Kennedy rented a large house, hired showgirls, models, and sex workers to attend a party dressed elegantly and using fake posh accents and double-barrelled names. He then invited the press, claiming it was the "first high-society rock and roll show" featuring debutantes. This publicity stunt successfully got Steele national attention. "He then called the press and said it was the first high-society rock and roll show and that the girls were all deputants."
**Audition in Men's Toilets** : Eager to sign him, Decca Records producer Hugh Mendel did not check for available studios, leading to Tommy Steele auditioning for his record contract in the men's toilets. "Britain's first homegrown rock idol auditioned for his record contract in the Jents toilets."
**Influence on David Bowie** : A nine-year-old David Jones (later David Bowie) was inspired to become a rock and roll star after seeing Tommy Steele perform on a tour following the success of "Singing The Blues." Steele's "overly manored, musical inspired, cocknified impression of an American singing style" is seen as a root for Bowie's own London take on Elvis. "On Steele's subsequent tour, a nine-year-old kid called David Jones, who would later change his name to Bowie, went to see him and came away inspired to become a rock and roll star."
**Brief Number One Hit (1956)** : Tommy Steele became the first British rock and roll singer to reach number one in the UK charts with his cover of "Singing The Blues" in late 1956. This was due to a one-week distribution problem for Guy Mitchell's competing version, allowing Steele's version to briefly top the charts before Mitchell's returned to number one. "Tommy Steele had become the first British rock and roll singer to get to number one in the UK charts."
**Transition to Light Entertainment** : Within a year of his rock and roll success, Tommy Steele moved into light entertainment, starring in films like "The Tommy Steele's Story" and then stage musicals, effectively giving up rock and roll altogether. He eventually became a "national treasure." "Very soon Tommy Steele moved on into light entertainment."
**Later Career and Longevity** : Steele worked on stage with Gene Kelly and on screen with Fred Astaire, wrote several books, pursued a minor artistic career as a sculptor, and toured constantly in pantomimes and musicals. At age 82, he continued to tour yearly, performing as Scrooge in a stage musical version of "A Christmas Carol." "At age 82 he still tours every year, performing a scrooge in a stage musical version of a Christmas Carol."
**Enduring Popularity (2009)** : Despite moving away from rock and roll, his 1950s hits remained popular enough in the UK for a compilation of them to reach number 22 on the charts in 2009. "His 1950s hit remained popular enough in the UK, there's a compilation of them, what's in number 22 in the chart in 2009."
**Pioneer of British Rock and Roll Industry** : The transcript asserts that "without him there would be no British rock and roll industry as we know it, and the rest of this history would be very different." "without him there would be no British rock and roll industry as we know it."

## songs
**Don't You Rock Me Daddy-O (1956)** : Wally Whiten substantially rewrote an old C-shanty called "Sailor-Way Ladies," which had been popularized by Uncle Dave Macon, to create this song. Macon copyrighted Whiten's song and rushed out his own version, but the Viper's version also charted at a respectable number 10, produced by George Martin on Pye Records. "the Viper's put out their own version too, and The Two chased each other up the charts."

## curiosities
**First Drug Reference in British Pop Music** : The group's full name, The Viper's Skiffle Group, was the first drug reference in British pop music, taken from the autobiography of American jazz clarinetist Mez Mezrow, who was known as a dope dealer. "Mez" became slang for marijuana, and "Viper" for dope smokers. Despite the name, none of the founding members (Whiten, Booker, Van Den Bosch) smoked dope; they just thought it sounded cool. "The name of the Viper's Skiffle Group was actually the first drug reference in British pop music."
**Playing at The Two Eyes Cafe** : The group started performing at a cafe called The Two Eyes, building a reputation to the point that Lonnie Donegan began using their material. "They started performing at a cafe called The Two Eyes, two as in the Numeral, I as in the letter, and started to build up something of a reputation."
**Occasional Collaboration with Thomas Hicks** : Thomas Hicks, a merchant seaman who was away at sea much of the time, occasionally joined the Viper's on stage. He was primarily a country and western fan and didn't care much for Skiffle, but played it because guitars in Britain in 1955-56 meant playing Skiffle. "Occasionally they would be joined on stage by a younger Quentin's name Thomas Hicks."
**Backup Band for Cliff Richard** : Former members of the Viper's Skiffle Group later formed the backing band for Cliff Richard, another singer who was a regular at The Two Eyes. "whose backing band was made up of former members of the Vipers Skiffle Loop."

# artist - Buddy Holly
## songs
**Peggy Sue (1957)** : Tommy Steele inaccurately claimed to have seen Buddy Holly perform this song in Norfolk, Virginia, in 1955, sparking his interest in rock and roll. However, Holly didn't meet Peggy Sue Gehrman (the song's namesake) until 1956, and the song itself wasn't written until 1957. "the song doesn't seem to have been written until 1957."

## curiosities
**Inaccurate Inspiration for Tommy Steele** : Tommy Steele claimed to have been introduced to rock and roll by seeing Buddy Holly sing "Peggy Sue" on a Grand Ole Opry tour in Norfolk, Virginia, in 1955. This account is contradicted by various timelines, including those from the Buddy Holly Centre in Lubbock, Texas, which indicate Holly did not tour outside Lubbock until May 1956, never toured Virginia, and "Peggy Sue" was written in 1957. "So, whatever it was that introduced young Tommy Hicks to the wonders of rock and roll, it wasn't seeing Buddy Holly sing Peggy Sue in North of Virginia in 1955."
**Limited Touring History (Pre-1957)** : According to the Buddy Holly Centre in Lubbock, Texas, Buddy Holly did not perform outside Lubbock until a Grand Ole Opry tour in May 1956, and only performed outside West Texas one other time before 1957. "Holly didn't perform outside Lubbock until that tour, and that's the only time he did perform outside West Texas until 1957."
**Meeting Peggy Sue Gehrman (1956)** : Buddy Holly did not meet Peggy Sue Gehrman, the woman who inspired the song "Peggy Sue," until 1956. "Buddy Holly didn't meet Peggy Sue Gehrman, the woman who gave the song its name, until 1956."

# genre - British Rock and Roll
## curiosities
**Lack of Cultural Roots** : The first wave of British rock and roll was fundamentally an imitation of American music, lacking the deep cultural and social issue ties that characterized its American counterpart. "it was essentially a music that had no roots in the culture."
**Clean-Cut and Mainstream from Inception** : Unlike American rock and roll, early British rock and roll started as a clean-cut, mainstream show business product, with little black influence or sexuality, aimed directly at teenagers, rather than emerging from an underclass or minority group. "British rock and roll started out at the point that American rock and roll was only just starting to get to, a clean-cut version of the music, with little black influence or sexuality left in it, designed from the outset to be a part of mainstream show business aimed at teenagers."
**Rapid National Exposure** : Due to Britain's national media structure (unlike America's local media), an artist could become known throughout the country with the right media exposure, sometimes before their second gig. "In Britain, someone could become known throughout the country before they played their second gig, if they got the right media exposure."

**Subject of Tommy Steele's False Claim** : Tommy Steele claimed to have seen Buddy Holly perform on a Grand Ole Opry tour in Norfolk, Virginia, in 1955, an event that purportedly inspired him to pursue rock and roll. # curiosity
**Britain's Homogenous Culture (1950s)** : In the 1950s, Britain was ethnically and culturally very homogenous, largely due to minimal immigration and a national media that contrasted sharply with America's more diverse local media landscape. This environment shaped the early, clean-cut nature of British rock and roll. "Britain in the 1950s was a very different place to the one it is today, or to America. It was ethnically extremely homogenous, as the waves of immigration that have so improved the country had only just started."
**The Two Eyes Cafe - Hub for Early British Rock and Roll** : The Two Eyes cafe served as a significant venue for early British rock and roll and skiffle acts, where The Viper's Skiffle Group built their reputation, Thomas Hicks performed, and future stars like Cliff Richard were regulars. "They started performing at a cafe called The Two Eyes, two as in the Numeral, I as in the letter, and started to build up something of a reputation."

The musical was later rewritten for a film version, removing the satire and turning it into a straight rags-to-riches story for Cliff Richard.
