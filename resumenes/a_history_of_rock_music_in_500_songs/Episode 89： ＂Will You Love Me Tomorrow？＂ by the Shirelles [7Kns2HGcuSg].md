## songs
**I Met Him On A Sunday** : This song was made up by the girls themselves and performed at a school talent contest. It became the first song they recorded for Florence Greenberg's new label, TRA Records, with Stan Green producing. It had minor local success and was picked up by Decca Records, eventually reaching number 50 on the chart before the label dropped them. "They performed at the show singing a song they'd made up themselves and met him on a Sunday and went down a storm with the kids at the school."
**Tonight the Night (Year unknown, late 1950s/early 1960s)** : Co-written by Shirley Owens and Luther Dixon, this was the first record Dixon worked on for The Shirelles and the group's first Top 40 hit, reaching number 39. Initially intended as a B-side to a song with a lead vocal by Doris, its unexpected success established Shirley firmly as the group's lead singer. A competing version by The Shiffens also made the Hot 100 chart. "The first record he worked on for the Cherelles was also the group's first top 40 hit, with Shirley Owens, Dixon wrote Tonight the Night."
**Will You Love Me Tomorrow (Year unknown, early 1960s)** : Written by Gerry Goffin and Carole King, this song was chosen by Don Kirshner as the follow-up to "Tonight the Night." It featured lyrics by Goffin that brought a new depth to pop music, discussing female adolescent emotion and a woman's worry about being a one-night stand. Carole King wrote the string arrangement overnight, drawing inspiration from The Drifters' "There Goes My Baby," despite never having written for an orchestra before. The group initially disliked the song, finding it sounded like a country song, but Luther Dixon insisted on its release, leading to it becoming a number one hit. "The group didn't like the song, thinking it sounded like a country song, but Luther Dixon insisted, and the result went to number one."
**Boys (Year unknown, early 1960s)** : This Luther Dixon song was released as the B-side to the number one hit "Will You Love Me Tomorrow." It also became a well-known track. "The B side to that single, a Luther Dixon song called Boys, would also become a well-known track in the song."
**Baby It's You (Year unknown, early 1960s)** : Originally titled "Al Cherishu" and written by Burt Bacharach with Mac David, Luther Dixon found the original lyrics "a bit too sickly." He revised them to be about someone who loves her boyfriend despite friends' negative opinions and retitled the song. For the recording, Dixon used Bacharach's original demo and overdubbed Shirley Owens' voice, making Shirley the only member of The Shirelles to sing on the track. The song returned the group to the Top Ten. "Dixon liked the song, but thought the lyrics were a bit too sickly. He changed the lyrics around, making them instead about someone who still loves her boyfriend, despite her friends telling her how bad he is, and retitling it, baby it's you."
**Soldier Boy (Year unknown, early 1960s)** : Written by Luther Dixon and Florence Greenberg, this song became The Shirelles' second number one hit. Unfortunately, it marked their last chart-topping success. "the follow-up, soldier boy, written by Dixon and Greenberg, became their second number one."

## curiosities
**Formation and Original Name - The Parakeets** : Shirley Owens, Doris Coley, Addie Harris, and Beverly Lee met at school in Pasey, New Jersey, about 16 miles from New York, and formed a group called The Parakeets. This name, Spanish for "little birds," was in line with the trend of early do-wop groups often being named after birds. "Shirley Owens, Doris Coley, Adi Harris and Beverly Lee met at school and formed a group called the Parqueos which is apparently Spanish for little birds."
**Reluctance to Professional Singing** : The group that became the most successful early girl group never wanted to be professional singers and had to be coerced at every stage. Their first public performance was a punishment for singing in gym class instead of paying attention, leading their teacher to make them perform in a school talent contest. "Oddly the group that became the most successful of the early girl groups and the one that more than any other set the template for all those that would follow never wanted to become professional singers and almost had to be forced against their will at every stage."
**Discovery by Mary Jane Greenberg** : After performing "I Met Him On A Sunday" at their school talent contest, they impressed Mary Jane Greenberg, a fellow student. Mary Jane insisted that the girls meet her mother, Florence Greenberg, who would later become their manager and record label owner, despite the girls' attempts to avoid her initially. "In particular one of the girls there, Mary Jane Greenberg, insisted that the girls come and meet her mother, Florence."
**Renaming to The Shirelles** : After signing with Florence Greenberg's TRA Records, The Parakeets were renamed The Shirelles. The new name was derived from Shirley Owens, the lead singer, with an "-elles" ending to evoke other popular groups like The Chantels. "The Perqueos were also renamed to the Chirrells after Shirley Owens but with the Al ending to be reminiscent of the Shang Tells and that was the name that would be known back from that point on."
**Decca Records Contract and Drop** : After "I Met Him On A Sunday" found minor local success, Decca Records bought The Shirelles' contract from Florence Greenberg. Although the song reached number 50 on the charts, their subsequent two singles for Decca were unsuccessful, leading the label to drop them. "They managed to get it to number 50 on the chart but the two singles they recorded for Deca after that didn't have any success and the label dropped them."
**Influence of Luther Dixon** : After Florence Greenberg started Scepter Records and re-signed The Shirelles, the label's A&R director Wally Roker introduced her to Luther Dixon. Dixon's involvement as a staff writer and producer, and his eventual relationship with Greenberg, dramatically changed the group's fortunes, leading to their biggest hits, including their first Top 40 and number one singles. "but then a change in SEPTRA's staffing changed everything not just for the Chirrells but for the world of music."
**Maternity Leave and Replacement Singers** : As the group members started families, one or another member often had to be replaced for road shows while on maternity leave. During these times, session singer Dionne Warwick, who Burt Bacharach was producing for Scepter, filled in for them. "It didn't help that the girls were starting families, and at various times, one or other member had to be replaced on the road while they were on maternity leave."
**Royalty Dispute and Lawsuits** : The Shirelles discovered that Florence Greenberg had lied to them about their royalties. They had been told their earnings were being placed into a trust for them until they turned twenty-one, but no such trust existed, and Greenberg had been keeping their money. This led them to file lawsuits against Scepter Records, but remaining signed to the label, they were unable to record for anyone else, which effectively destroyed their career. "They've been told that their royalties were being put into a trust for them for when they turned twenty-one, but they discovered that no such trust existed, and Greenberg had just been keeping their money."
**Post-Scepter Career and Multiple Lineups** : Despite the legal troubles that effectively ended their hit-making career, The Shirelles continued to perform in various lineups, with members coming and going, until the early 1980s. At that point, the original members went their separate ways, but each started their own version of The Shirelles. "They remained together in one line up of another, with members coming in and going, until the early eighties, when they all went their separate ways, though they all started their own line-ups of Chirrells."
**Rights to the Name** : Today, Shirley Owens performs under her married name, Shirley Alston Reeves, with her group "Shirley Alston Reeves and her Shirelles." Beverly Lee, another original member, owns the rights to perform as "The Shirelles" with no modifiers. "These days, Shirley Toys under her married name, as Shirley Alston Reeves and her Chirrells, while Beverly Lee owns the rights to Toys the Chirrells with no modifiers."
**Deaths of Members** : Addie Harris passed away in 1982, and Doris Coley died in 2000. "Adi Harris died in 1982, and Doris Kohley in 2000."
**Pioneering Girl Group Influence** : The Shirelles were historically mistreated by their record company and largely ignored by rock historians due to their success falling within the 1958-1964 period, which is often dismissed as uninteresting. However, their success was pivotal, setting the template for the booming girl group phenomenon of the 1960s and profoundly influencing groups like The Supremes, The Marvelettes, The Crystals, and The Ronettes. "They made some of the most important records of the sixties, and it was their success that led to the great booming girl groups of the next few years."

## curiosities
**Child Prodigy and Relative Pitch** : From a very early age, Carole King displayed exceptional musical talent. As a child, her father would use her talent as a party trick: she would turn to the wall while he played notes on the piano, and she would call out which ones he was playing. While her father claimed she had perfect pitch, she actually possessed relative pitch, meaning she could identify all other notes once given a starting note, which her father always provided as middle C. "In her autobiography, she talks about how, when she was a child, her dad would have her, as a party trick, turn to the wall while he played notes on the piano, and she called out which one he was playing."
**Early Performances** : By the age of eight, King had appeared in a few shows, including the nationally broadcast "Ted Mack Amateur Hour," where she performed in a duo with a friend. "By the age of 8 she had appeared in a couple of shows, including Ted Max Amateur Hour, which was a nationally broadcast show, performing in a duo with a friend."
**Inspiration at Alan Freed Show (1955)** : At 13, a date with Jules Zwick took King to an Alan Freed show at the Paramount in Brooklyn, where she saw R&B stars like LaVern Baker, B.B. King, Mickey Baker, and The Moonglows. This show was her introduction to a world of music far removed from the "white bread pop" of her upbringing. It was not primarily seeing the performers on stage that inspired her, but rather an accidental backstage experience. "Zwick took her to an Alan Fried show at the Paramount in Brooklyn, where she saw Le Verne Baker, BB King, Mickey Baker, The Moon Glows, and several other R&B stars of the period."
**Backstage Epiphany** : At the Alan Freed show, after trying to get autographs backstage, King and her date were accidentally let in through the stage door. Seeing the performers casually hanging out backstage solidified her ambition. It wasn't to be a performer herself, but to be "part of that world, someone that those performers knew and respected." "She knew then, not that she wanted to be a performer herself, but that she wanted to be part of that world, someone that those performers knew and respected."
**Stage School and Al Pacino** : King briefly attended a stage school where one of her classmates was Al Pacino. She soon left, however, deciding she wasn't suited for the non-musical aspects of the curriculum, and returned to a regular high school. "She started attending a stage school, where one of her classmates was Al Pacino, but after a short while she left, deciding that she wasn't cut out for the non-musical aspects of the school, and went back to a normal high school where she formed her first group, The Co-Sounds, along with Swick."
**Start in Songwriting** : King started writing songs after hearing Neil Sedaka, then with The Linktones, sing "While I Dream," a song he co-wrote with Howard Greenfield. Sedaka's group, The Tokens, got a record deal, prompting King to believe she could do the same. She discovered a talent for melodies but felt less confident in her lyrical abilities. "King figured that if he could do that, so could she. She started writing songs, and found she was good at melodies, but not particularly great at lyrics, but she still thought she was good enough to do something."
**Meeting Alan Freed for Advice** : King approached Alan Freed to play him her songs. Freed listened politely and advised her on how to become a professional songwriter for the R&B market, suggesting she find record label addresses in the phone book and explaining publishing contracts. He specifically mentioned Atlantic Records. "She decided that she was going to go and see Alan Friede and play him some of her songs. Friede listened to her politely, and explained to her how, at the time, one went about becoming a professional songwriter for the R&B market."
**First Meetings with Record Labels** : King first visited Atlantic Records, where Jerry Wexler and Ahmet Ertegun listened to her and encouraged her to return with more songs, which was not a rejection but also not the instant acceptance she hoped for. Her second stop was ABC-Paramount, where she met Ennio Costa. "The record label he mentioned to her specifically was Atlantic Records, so she tried that one first."
**Signing a Recording Contract with ABC-Paramount** : At ABC-Paramount, Ennio Costa not only offered King a publishing contract but also, to her surprise, a recording contract as a solo artist. She was disappointed he wasn't interested in signing her group, The Co-Sounds, but still signed immediately. Her initial solo singles, primarily her own compositions, did not achieve significant sales, partly due to her perceived weakness as a lyricist. "She was flabbergasted to find that rather than just sign her to a publishing contract, he asked her to sign a recording contract as well."
**College and Meeting Jerry Landis (Paul Simon)** : At 16, King, who had skipped multiple school years, enrolled in college while attempting to build her music career. There, she met Jerry Landis, who had a minor hit with "Hey School Girl" as one half of the duo Tom and Jerry. King and Landis collaborated on recording demos for other songwriters, sometimes reusing The Co-Sounds name, though they never wrote songs together. "So while she was trying to have a music career, she also went off to college aged 16. She had skipped multiple years in school, where she met someone else, who had had a minor hit."
**Meeting and Marriage to Gerry Goffin** : While in college, King met Jerry Goffin, who would become her first husband. She was initially attracted to his appearance, as he resembled an ideal boyfriend drawing she had clipped from a magazine. Goffin initially disliked rock and roll, viewing its lyrics as vacuous, but became eager to write with King when he learned she sold songs. By age 17, they were married and expecting their first child, supplementing Goffin's day job income with occasional $25 songwriting advances. "Someone else she met while at college was the man who was to rick on her first husband, another Jerry, Jerry Goughin."
**Discovery by Aldon Music** : King met Neil Sedaka at 1650 Broadway while trying to sell songs. Sedaka introduced her to Al Nevins and Don Kirshner, the owners of Aldon Music. # curiosity

**Undocumented Rock and Roll Period (1958-1964)** : The period of rock and roll history between 1958 and 1964 is notably under-documented, with existing literature often focusing heavily on major stars of the early era (like Elvis or Little Richard) or the mid-to-late 1960s beat boom. This era, despite producing many influential and enduring hits, is often dismissively summarized by the rise of "white bread" pop figures. "But the period from 1958 through 1964 is generally summed up by and there were some white red nun entities like Fabian and Frankie Avalon."
**Florence Greenberg's Entry into Music Business** : Florence Greenberg, a suburban housewife in her mid-40s with no prior music business experience, decided to start her own record label. Her husband's friendship with Freddie Bienstock (who selected songs for Elvis Presley) and her son's interest in music exposed her to the Brill Building atmosphere, convincing her to launch TRA Records. "Florence Greenberg was a board suburban housewife who until her mid-40s had concentrated on being a homemaker for her husband who was an executive at a potato chip firm and for her two children."
**Stan Green / Stan Greenberg** : Stan Greenberg, Florence Greenberg's "musical son," produced and arranged "I Met Him On A Sunday" for The Shirelles under the name Stan Green. "they recorded the song they'd written with Greenberg's musical Sun Stan producing an arranging under the name Stan Green."
**Luther Dixon's Diverse Musical Career** : Luther Dixon began his career as a singer in a minor group called The Bodies, recording singles like "I Stole Your Heart." He quickly transitioned into songwriting, co-writing hits such as Pat Boone's "Why Baby Why?" (1957) with Larry Harrison, Bobby Darin's "All Away Home" with Otis Blackwell, and The Crests' "16 Candles" with Alison Kent. He later became a pivotal staff writer and producer for Scepter Records, significantly impacting The Shirelles' success. "Dixon had started out as a singer, joining a minor group called The Bodies who had recorded singles like I Stole Your Heart."
**Luther Dixon's Publishing Rights Deal** : Florence Greenberg, upon hiring Luther Dixon as a staff writer and producer for Scepter, granted him a cut of the publishing rights for his songs. This arrangement was almost unheard of at the time, demonstrating his value to the label. "Greenberg took him on as a staff writer and producer and gave him a cut of the publishing rights for his songs, almost unheard of at that time."
**Luther Dixon and Florence Greenberg's Relationship** : Luther Dixon's initial meeting with Florence Greenberg was brief but led to his employment at Scepter Records as a staff writer and producer, and he also became her lover. Their relationship, both professional and personal, ended, with Dixon subsequently moving to Capitol Records. "Soon Dixon was working for Greenberg at SEPTRA and was also her lover."
**Ennio Costa's Diverse Career** : Ennio Costa, head of A&R at ABC-Paramount, signed Carole King to her first recording contract. Beyond his executive role, Costa was a musician who released a cover of Bill Justis's "Raunchy" under the name "Mister Guitar Hubbard." He also had a respected career as a session guitarist and later became known for arranging and conducting for Frank Sinatra. "Costa would later go on to arrange and conduct for Frank Sinatra, and he also had a respectable career as a session guitarist, but Camel didn't know any of this when she went into his office and played through her songs for him."
**Don Kirshner's Music Publishing Origins** : Don Kirshner began as a songwriter, notably co-writing "I Want Elvis for Christmas" with Bobby Darin. As Darin moved into performance, Kirshner transitioned to music publishing, finding early success with Vanderbilt Music by bringing Lewis, a 1930s/40s hit songwriter, out of retirement and pairing him with Sylvester Bradford to write new hits like "Tears On My Pillow." "Kirschner is someone we've met briefly before. **The Brill Building Sound Location** : The "Brill Building Sound" is strongly associated with the actual Brill Building at 1619 Broadway, but many of the companies responsible for the sound, most notably Aldon Music, were actually based at 1650 Broadway. "Even though they weren't based in the actual Brill Building, which was at 1619 Broadway, but in 1650 Broadway, the companies in that second building were so associated with the Brill Building Sound that you'll find almost every history of music messaged reputes them and places them there."
