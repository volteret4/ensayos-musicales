## albums
**If I Didn't Care - Studio Hit** : This song marked the first time The Ink Spots used their "Top and Bottom" formula in the studio, becoming a massive hit for the group. The formula involved an acoustic guitar intro, Bill Kenny singing the main verse in his high tenor, Hoppy Jones speaking the entire song lyrics in his deep bass voice, and then Kenny singing a final lead line. "They first did it in the studio, with their massive hit, if I didn't care."

## songs
**Swing High Swing Low (1937)** : This recording represents the early style of The Ink Spots after Bill Kenny joined, but before they developed their signature "Top and Bottom" sound. At this stage, they were still performing up-tempo numbers, primarily mirroring the sound of The Mills Brothers, with occasional spoken lines from Hoppy Jones. "As you can hear in the 1937 recording of Swing High Swing Low."
**That's When Your Heartaches Begin (1941)** : This record is a prime example of The Ink Spots' "Top and Bottom" formula, which became their hit-making style. The song starts with a familiar acoustic guitar figure, followed by Bill Kenny singing the entire song in his high tenor, with others singing backing vocals. Hoppy Jones then repeats the whole song, speaking it in his deep bass voice, before Kenny sings a final lead line. "But the one we're going to look at is the 1941 record, that's when your heart takes begin."
**We Three, My Echo, My Shadow and Me** : This song features the identical acoustic guitar intro and "Top and Bottom" vocal formula that became The Ink Spots' trademark, contributing to its chart success. It demonstrated how consistently the band applied their winning formula across different tracks. "Here's the intro to We3, my echo, my shadow and me."
**I Don't Want to Set the World on Fire** : This song is a notable example of The Ink Spots' "Top and Bottom" formula, which led it to become a Top 10 hit and even reached number 1 on the pop charts. Its intro is characteristic of their highly successful and often-repeated style. "Now here's the intro to I don't want to set the world on fire."
**Each His Own** : A song that followed The Ink Spots' established "Top and Bottom" formula, it achieved significant chart success, reaching the Top 10. The consistent application of this sound contributed to its popularity with the public. "And here's the intro to each his own."
**Whispering Grass** : This song also utilized the well-known "Top and Bottom" formula, featuring the distinctive acoustic guitar intro and vocal arrangement, helping it climb into the Top 10 on the pop charts. "And to whispering grass."

## curiosities
**Formation from Coffee Pot Groups** : The Ink Spots formed from musicians who had started their careers in "coffee pot groups," which were comprised of poor Black teenagers performing on street corners, using everyday items like kazoos and containers to imitate orchestral sounds. Original members Charlie Fouquay, Jerry Daniels, Deek Watson, and Happy Jones emerged from this background. "The ink spots formed out of people who'd started their careers in these groups."
**Early Names and Beginnings** : Before settling on The Ink Spots, members performed in various combinations under different names, including "Charlie and Jerry" (Fouquay and Daniels) and "King Jack and Jester" (or "King Jack and Jester's"). In their early years, they secured a video show on a local station as a fill-in for The Four Mills Brothers, who heavily influenced their initial sound. "Before they settled on calling themselves King Jack and Jester, or sometimes King Jack and Jester's."
**Name Origin Controversy** : Deek Watson claimed he suggested the name "The Ink Spots" after seeing drops of ink fall from a booking agent's pen. However, his bandmates, particularly Charlie and Happy, reacted negatively, interpreting it as another one of Watson's attempts to emphasize their race, comparing it to "Black dots" or "old black joes." "To me, it seemed like inspiration. That's it, I shouted. How about calling us the ink spots?"
**"Minstrily Behaviours" Criticism** : In their early years, Deek Watson, performing under the name Dice Vastus in his coffee pot group, was criticized in early reviews of The Ink Spots for "eye rolling, hand waving, and other minstrily behaviours." This often drew disapproval from Black reviewers who felt it perpetuated negative stereotypes. "Many early reviews of the ink spots criticised him for eye rolling, hand waving, and other minstrily behaviours, which many black reviewers at the time considered brought black people into this repute."
**Jerry Daniels' Illness and Departure** : Jerry Daniels, who served as the band's main jive singer, became seriously ill from the stress of their performing schedule and was eventually hospitalized. His inability to continue touring led to a temporary reduction of the group to three members and necessitated a change in their lineup and sound. "Jerry Damules, their main jive singer, was getting seriously ill from the stress of the band's performing schedule, and eventually ended up hospitalised."
**Bill Kenny's Impact and "Top and Bottom" Formula** : In 1936, 21-year-old high-tenor singer Bill Kenny joined the band after winning an amateur contest at the Savoy Ballroom, which was co-owned by The Ink Spots' manager, Mo Gale. Kenny's extraordinary vocals became the defining characteristic of their records, and he is credited with conceiving the "Top and Bottom" idea, transforming the band from an up-tempo jive act into a balladeering group. "Bill Kenny, a 21-year-old high-tener singer, won an amateur night contest at the Savoy Ballroom."
**"Top and Bottom" Formula Development** : Bill Kenny, an inspired arranger, refined Hoppy Jones' occasional spoken vocals into a hit formula. This arrangement started with an acoustic guitar figure, followed by Kenny's high-tenor lead vocal, then Hoppy Jones speaking the entire lyrics in his deep bass voice, and finally Kenny singing the last line. This formula was first used in the studio on "If I Didn't Care" and was consistently applied to subsequent hits. "He refined the idea of Hoppy's spoken vocals, and came up with a hit formula, which they would use over and over again."
**Chart Dominance with Formula** : The Ink Spots' "Top and Bottom" formula proved incredibly successful, selling tens of millions of records from approximately 1939 to the late 1940s. Many of their songs, including "I Don't Want to Set the World on Fire," reached the Top 10, with some even hitting number 1 on the pop charts, not just the R&B charts. "This was a formula that absolutely, undoubtedly worked. And when I say number 1 or top 10, I don't mean on the R&B charts. I mean number 1 on the pop charts."
**Mo Gale's Management and Finances** : Mo Gale, The Ink Spots' manager and co-owner of the Savoy Ballroom, managed other bands like Ella Fitzgerald and Lucky Millinder, enabling him to organize package tours and find easy replacements for ill or departing members. However, Gale notoriously took 60% of the band's earnings, leaving 40% to be split four ways among the members, leading to future conflicts. "Gale took 60 percent, while the eggspots got the other 40 between them, split four ways."
**Lineup Changes and Wartime Draft** : The Ink Spots experienced a series of lineup changes due to personality conflicts, legal disputes, and deaths. In 1942, Charlie Fouquet was drafted into the army, leading to three years of replacements covering his part and marking the beginning of significant shifts in the band's personnel. "Four years after their career took off, in 1942 Charlie Fouquet was drafted, and that began a whole series of line-up shifts."
**Leadership Conflict and Hoppy Jones' Role** : By 1944, deep personality conflicts emerged between Deek Watson, who saw himself as the leader for founding and naming the band, and Bill Kenny, who believed he was the leader due to his lead vocals and arranging skills. Hoppy Jones, having worked with Watson for years and holding an assured place with his spoken parts, acted as the peacemaker between the two. "Deke Watson and Bill Kenny never got on very well."
**Hoppy Jones' Illness and Death** : Hoppy Jones, crucial to the "Top and Bottom" sound, became increasingly ill, missing more and more dates due to a series of brain hemorrhages. Tragically, after Deek Watson quit the band, Jones collapsed on stage and died, a pivotal moment that dissolved the original partnership and led to further legal battles. "But Hoppy Jones was growing ill and started missing more and more dates because of what turned out to be a series of brain hemorrhages."
**Deke Watson's Departure and First Legal Battle** : Allegedly after Mo Gale gave Bill Kenny a pay rise while not extending it to Watson or Jones, Deke Watson quit the band to form his own version of The Ink Spots. This move, combined with Hoppy Jones' death, ignited a complex four-way legal battle involving Watson, Kenny, Charlie Fouquet (still in the army), and Mo Gale. "Deke Watson quit the band as a result of this, and went off to form his own ink spots."
**First Court Ruling on Band Name** : The initial legal battle primarily favored Bill Kenny, ruling that no club could book an act called The Ink Spots unless Kenny was a member. However, Deke Watson and Charlie Fouquet retained a financial interest in the band, Mo Gale remained manager, and Fouquet was paid a regular salary during his army service. "The courts ruled that no club could book an act called the ink spots, which didn't have Bill Kenny in it."
**Post-War Lineup and Subsequent Departures** : After Charlie Fouquet returned from the war, a new lineup featuring Bill Kenny, his brother Herb Kenny, Fouquet, and Billy Bowen toured and recorded for several years. However, in 1952, Fouquet and Bill Kenny decided to part ways, leading to further disputes over the band's name. "Fouquet came back, and for a few years a new lineup of Bill Kenny and his brother Herb, Fouquet and Billy Bowen, Tordon recorded."
**Charlie Fouquet's "New Ink Spots"** : After parting with Bill Kenny in 1952, Charlie Fouquet began touring as "Charlie Fouquet's New Ink Spots," despite an earlier agreement that only Kenny's band could legally use the name. He later broke this agreement by performing simply as "The Ink Spots" and even briefly reunited with Deke Watson in his version of the group. "Fookeway would tour as Charlie Fookeway's new ink spots, except that Fookeway soon ended up breaking this agreement and just touring and recording as the ink spots."
**Live Recording with Fouquet and Watson** : One recording exists of a version of The Ink Spots featuring original members Charlie Fouquet and Deke Watson, alongside Jimmy Holmes and Harold Jackson, performing live at the Apollo. This brief reunion occurred before Watson was once again removed from Fouquet's band. "There's one recording of that version of the band, Jimmy Holmes, Charlie Fookeway, Deke Watson and Harold Jackson, live at the Apollo, before Watson was kicked out again."
**Bill Kenny's Post-Split Recordings** : Following the split with Charlie Fouquet, Bill Kenny continued to make records under The Ink Spots name. These recordings maintained the classic Ink Spots sound, but notably lacked the deep bass spoken vocals of Hoppy Jones, who had passed away years prior. "Meanwhile, Bill Kenny was still making records as the ink spots, which still sounded like the old ink spots, minus Hoppy's bass vocal."
**Second Court Ruling and Name Dissolution** : Faced with two competing versions of The Ink Spots, a second court case was initiated. Unexpectedly, the judge ruled that because The Ink Spots were a partnership and not a corporation, the partnership had legally dissolved upon Hoppy Jones' death a decade earlier. This meant that no one, neither surviving original members nor Bill Kenny, had a legitimate claim to the name, effectively making it public domain. "Instead, the ruling was one that no one had expected, and that no one wanted. You see, it turns out that the ink spots weren't a corporation. They were a partnership."
**Proliferation of "The Ink Spots" Groups** : As a direct result of the court ruling that dissolved legitimate ownership of the name, anyone could form a group and call themselves "The Ink Spots." This led to a proliferation of touring and recording groups, with up to 40 different "Ink Spots" acts performing at one point, often with tenuous or no direct connection to the original band. "At one point, there were up to 40 different ink spots groups touring, and many of them were recording too."
**Last Original Member and Final Performance** : Huey Long, who served as a temporary replacement for Charlie Fouquet for nine months in 1945, was the last actual Ink Spots member, dying at the age of 106 in 2009. The last known gig for any iteration of The Ink Spots that could be traced was in 2013, marking the long and complex end of their performing career. "The last actual ink spots member, Huey Long, who had been one of the temporary replacements for Charlie Fouquet in 1945 for nine months, died aged 106 in 2009."
**Influence on Elvis Presley** : The Ink Spots' song "That's When Your Heartaches Begin" was a favorite of a young Elvis Presley. In 1953, shortly after the band's final split, Presley chose to record his own version of the song as a gift for his mother when he walked into a Memphis recording studio to make his first single. "He wanted to make a record of himself singing as a gift for his mother, and he chose one of his favourite songs. That's when your heartakes begin, as one of the two tracks he would record."

**Do Wop, Don't Mean a Thing** : This performance by The Mills Brothers showcases their unique vocal style, where they imitate various brass instruments like trumpet, tuba, and trombone using only their voices. The recording features no instruments other than a single acoustic guitar, highlighting their skill in vocal mimicry. "Oh, we don't mean a thing if you don't. There is no instrument on there. ## curiosities
**Origin in a Talent Contest** : The Mills Brothers began their unique vocal journey after a talent contest where John Mills lost his kazoo. Improvising, he cupped his hands and imitated a trumpet, leading the brothers to decide to specialize in imitating brass instruments with their voices. "John Mills had lost the kazoo he was going to play. He cupped his hands in front of his mouth and imitated a trumpet."
**Pioneers of Vocal Instrumentation** : The Mills Brothers became renowned for their ability to perfectly imitate brass instruments like trumpet, tuba, and trombone using only their voices, often listening to instrumental musicians to copy their voicings. This technique set a standard for vocal groups and influenced many who followed. "They're imitating trumpet, a tuba, and a trombone with their voices, and they'd listen to instrumental musicians and copy their voicings."
**Massive Early Success (1930-1939)** : From 1930 through 1939, The Mills Brothers were by far the biggest Black act in the United States. They made multiple appearances on Bing Crosby's radio show, starred in films, and toured the world, establishing themselves as a premier entertainment act. "From 1930 through 1939, they were far and away the biggest black act in the US."
**World War II Disruption and Decline** : Their international touring schedule led to their eventual downfall when, during a 1939 UK tour, the outbreak of World War II trapped them. Massive transport disruptions and the war itself meant it took them two years to return to the US, by which time their widespread popularity had faded. "It was the touring the world that caused their eventual downfall. They went to play the UK in 1939, and discovered that with World War II eminent, the only ship away from the UK they could get at the end of their tour was one that went to Australia."
**Longevity of Career** : Despite a decline in popularity after WWII, The Mills Brothers continued to have occasional hits into the late 1960s and performed together into the late 1980s. The last surviving Mills Brother, performing with one of his sons, carried on the family band until his death in 1999, showcasing remarkable career longevity. "Their career was far from over. They carried on having occasional hits into the late 60s. And carried on performing together into the late 80s."

## curiosities
**Early Stage Name (Dice Vastus)** : Before joining The Ink Spots, Deke Watson worked under the stage name Dice Vastus when performing with his "coffee part group," The Percolating Poppies. This name was associated with his early performance style. "When he was in the percolating puppies, Watson used to work under the name for Dice Vastus."
**Claim to Ink Spots Name** : Watson attempted to claim credit for numerous good ideas within The Ink Spots, including the band's name and the "Top and Bottom" formula, though historical consensus often attributes these to others. He also made outrageous claims, such as the original lineup performing at King Edward VIII's coronation. "Watson in his book does seem to take credit for absolutely every good idea anyone involved in the band had, and for other things, which had nothing to do with them."
**Leadership Conflict with Bill Kenny** : Watson believed himself to be the leader of The Ink Spots because he had originally assembled the band and been its onstage leader before Bill Kenny joined. This belief led to significant personality conflicts and power struggles within the group. "Watson thought of himself as the leader, on the grounds that he was the one who'd put the band together, named it, and been the onstage leader until Kenny came along."
**Quitting The Ink Spots (1944)** : In 1944, Deke Watson quit The Ink Spots, reportedly due to manager Mo Gale giving Bill Kenny a pay raise that Watson and Hoppy Jones did not receive. Following his departure, Watson attempted to form his own version of The Ink Spots, leading to legal disputes. "Deke Watson quit the band as a result of this, and went off to form his own ink spots."
**Legal Setbacks and Financial Interest** : Deke Watson was largely considered the "real loser" in the first major legal battle over The Ink Spots' name. While he retained some financial interest in the name, he was explicitly prohibited from touring with The Ink Spots or rejoining the band he had co-founded. "The only real loser was Deke Watson. He continued to get some money for his share of the ink spots name, although I've seen some claims that Bill Kenny bought him out totally."

**Savoy Ballroom Amateur Night Winner (1936)** : At 21 years old, Bill Kenny won an amateur night contest at the prestigious Savoy Ballroom. His exceptional high-tenor voice caught the attention of Mo Gale, The Ink Spots' manager and co-owner of the Savoy, leading to his recruitment into the band. "In 1936, Bill Kenny, a 21-year-old high-tener singer, won an amateur night contest at the Savoy Ballroom."
**Defining Voice and Arranger for The Ink Spots** : Kenny's extraordinary high-tenor vocals became the defining characteristic of The Ink Spots' records from the moment he joined. He was also an inspired arranger, credited with developing the "Top and Bottom" formula that fundamentally changed the band's style and sound. "Kenny was, by any standards, an extraordinary singer, and his vocals would become the defining characteristic of the ink spots records from that point on."
**Leadership Conflict with Deke Watson** : Bill Kenny saw himself as the leader of The Ink Spots due to his role as the lead singer and arranger, which clashed with Deke Watson's belief that he was the leader for founding the band. This internal conflict contributed significantly to the band's eventual breakup. "Meanwhile Kenny thought of himself as the leader, on account of being the lead singer and arranger."
**Pay Rise and Band Dissolution** : Manager Mo Gale allegedly gave Bill Kenny a pay raise that was not extended to Deke Watson or Hoppy Jones, which directly contributed to Watson quitting the band in 1944. Kenny continued with Hoppy Jones for a month until Jones' death, further altering the band's structure. "Meanwhile, Mo Gale allegedly gave Bill Kenny a pay rise, but not Watson or Jones."
**Victory in First Legal Battle** : In the initial legal dispute over The Ink Spots' name, Bill Kenny largely prevailed. The courts ruled that only acts featuring Bill Kenny could be booked under The Ink Spots name, solidifying his control over the band's identity at that time. "In the end, Bill Kenny more or less won. The courts ruled that no club could book an act called the ink spots, which didn't have Bill Kenny in it."
**Continued Recordings Post-Split** : Even after Charlie Fouquet departed in 1952 and amidst ongoing disputes, Bill Kenny continued to make records under The Ink Spots name. These recordings intentionally retained the classic Ink Spots sound, though they lacked Hoppy Jones' bass vocal. "Meanwhile, Bill Kenny was still making records as the ink spots, which still sounded like the old ink spots, minus Hoppy's bass vocal."

## curiosities
**Formation by Deke Watson** : After losing the initial legal battle over The Ink Spots' name, Deke Watson formed The Brown Dots, proclaiming it a new vocal group based on a "completely new idea." This idea, ironically, closely mirrored The Ink Spots' signature "Top and Bottom" formula, including the guitar intro, high-tenor lead, spoken lyrics, and tenor finish. "Deke Watson, meanwhile, had been performing with his own ink spots before the lawsuits. But once they were settled and not in his favour, he said he was going to form a new vocal group based on a completely new idea."
**Short-Lived Success and Breakup** : While "I Love You for Sentimental Reasons" proved to be a successful and influential record for The Brown Dots, the band's existence was short-lived. The other band members eventually left due to difficulties in getting along with Deke Watson, forming their own successful group, The Four Tunes, without him. "It looked like Deke Watson had found himself a second great band to be with, but then the other band members realised that it was hard to get on with Deke Watson and left to form their own band without him."

# curiosity

**Coffee Pot Groups - Early Street Music** : "Coffee pot groups" were informal musical ensembles formed by poor Black teenagers who performed on street corners in the late 1930s and early 1940s. They aimed to replicate the lush sounds of radio records using improvised instruments, such as ukuleles, guitars, banjos for strings, and kazoos blown into different-sized coffee pots or tea pots for horn sections. "Coffee part groups were groups of poor black teenagers who performed on street corners and tried to reproduce the sounds of the lush records they heard on the radio."
**Improvised Instrumentation of Coffee Pot Groups** : These groups ingeniously replaced traditional orchestral instruments with common items; kazoos served as horns, which were made to sound more impressive by blowing them into resonant containers like tea pots, small coffee pots, and large coffee pots to mimic different horn sections. Other instrumental parts were replaced with simple mouth noises. "You could get a pretty fair approximation of a horn section by having a tea part, a small coffee part, and a large coffee part, and having three of your band members play kazoo's into them."
**Collection Method for Coffee Pot Groups** : The large coffee pot used for kazoo amplification also served a practical purpose after performances, being passed around the crowd to collect money. Deek Watson of The Percolating Poppies noted the necessity of monitoring the person collecting the money to prevent theft. "The large coffee part, you could also pass around to the crowd afterward to collect the money in."
**Vocal Imitation of Instruments** : The practice of imitating instruments with voices, notably pioneered by The Mills Brothers, became a significant technique for vocal groups. This involved closely listening to instrumental musicians and copying their voicings, a skill that greatly influenced subsequent vocal harmony acts. "They're imitating trumpet, a tuba, and a trombone with their voices, and they'd listen to instrumental musicians and copy their voicings."
**Managerial Dominance of Mo Gale** : Mo Gale, manager for The Ink Spots and co-owner of the Savoy Ballroom, also managed other prominent acts like Ella Fitzgerald and Lucky Millinder's band. This allowed him to organize joint package tours and easily source replacement musicians when his acts faced illness or personnel changes. "Mo Gale, the ink spots manager, was the co-owner of the Savoy. And Charles Buchanan, the club's manager, knew his boss's band wanted a new singer and suggested Kenny."
**Impact of Hoppy Jones' Death on Band Legality** : The death of Hoppy Jones 10 years prior to a second court case concerning The Ink Spots' name was ruled to have dissolved the band's legal partnership. This unexpected ruling meant that the name "The Ink Spots" was no longer legitimately owned by anyone, leading to its widespread use by numerous unauthorized groups. "The judge ruled that when Hoppy Jones had died 10 years earlier, that partnership had been dissolved."
**Progenitor to Rock Music** : The Ink Spots are considered precursors to rock music, not only for their legal infighting and credit disputes but also for inspiring a new wave of vocal groups. Their unique harmonic style and "Top and Bottom" formula indirectly shaped the emerging sounds of rhythm and blues and early rock and roll. "Over the next few weeks, we'll hear how, along with the jump band sound that was coming to dominate rhythm and blues, a new wave of ink spots inspired vocal groups ended up shaping the new music."
