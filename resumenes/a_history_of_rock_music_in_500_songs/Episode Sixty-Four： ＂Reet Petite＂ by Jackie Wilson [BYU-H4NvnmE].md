## curiosities
**Inspiration for Music Icons** : Jackie Wilson was a massively important performer who inspired Elvis Presley, Michael Jackson, and James Brown, providing a key link between 1950s R&B and 1960s Soul. "Someone who inspired Elvis, Michael Jackson, and James Brown, and who provides one of the key links between 50s R and B and 60s Soul."
**Early Life as a Boxer** : Wilson started out as a boxer in his teens before deciding to pursue singing instead, regularly performing at talent contests around Detroit. "Wilson started out as a boxer in his teens, but he quickly decided to move into singing instead."
**Talent Contest Alliance** : He was part of a loose association of musicians, including his cousin Levi Stubbs (later of The Royals), and Little Willie John, who would pre-arrange wins at talent shows. "They would all perform on the same talent shows and would agree among themselves who was going to win beforehand."
**King Records Rejection and Later Recording** : Talent scout Johnny Otis saw Wilson perform and tried to get him signed to King Records, but they initially only signed The Royals, recording a song Otis had written for Wilson with The Royals instead. He would later record with Billy Ward and his Dominoes for King Records. "King decided they only wanted to sign the royals, though they would sign Little Willie John a couple of years later."
**Danny Boy - Vocal Virtuosity** : Wilson's favorite song to sing in talent contests was "Danny Boy," which remained in his set list throughout his life, used to showcase his vocal virtuosity by ornamenting the melody to an almost unrecognizable extent. "Wilson's favourite song to sing in talent contests was Danny Boy, which would remain in his set list until late in his life, and he would use that song as a way to show off his vocal virtuosity, ornamenting it to the point that the melody would become almost unrecognizable."
**Mother's Plea to Billy Ward** : One story of Wilson joining Billy Ward and his Dominoes suggests his mother begged Ward to take him, fearing for his life due to his involvement in street trouble. "Ward used to tell, which is that one reason Wilson was picked for the group is that his mother begged Ward, saying that she was scared for the life of her son as he was getting into trouble on the streets."
**Stabbing Incident** : Prior to joining Billy Ward and his Dominoes, Wilson had been stabbed in the chest by a sex worker, providing a reason for his mother's concerns for his safety. "Wilson had recently been stabbed in the chest by a sex worker."
**Stage Name Change** : Billy Ward changed Wilson's stage name from "Jack" or "Sonny" to "Jackie," as Ward felt "Sonny" "smacked of Uncle Tombery," a change Wilson initially disliked but later accepted, though he remained "Jack" or "Sonny" to close acquaintances. "Ward thought that being called Sunig smacked of Uncle Tombery, had decided that from this point on, Wilson's stage name was going to be Jackie."
**Boxing-Inspired Dance Style** : Wilson developed a unique, riveting dance style on stage, incorporating slides, sidesteps, and ducks learned from his boxing background, which allowed him to move from unexpected angles and dominate the stage. "He had a unique way of moving all his own, which he had learned in the boxing ring, where he'd learned to slide, sidestep, and duck away from other fighters, and to come at them from unexpected angles."
**Discipline from Billy Ward** : Billy Ward became a father figure to Wilson, teaching him breath control and instilling discipline for the first time in his life, which helped him mature as a performer. "Ward taught in breath control and became something of a father figure, giving him some discipline for the first time in his life."
**Las Vegas Residency Salary** : While performing with The Dominoes in a two-year Las Vegas residency supporting Louis Prima and Keely Smith, the group earned $5,000 a week, but Wilson received a mere $90 a week as a salary. "Wilson was on a salary of only $90 a week."
**Elvis Presley's Admiration** : In 1956, Elvis Presley, during his own less successful first trip to Las Vegas, watched Wilson perform Elvis's hit "Don't Be Cruel" better than Elvis himself, leading Elvis to rave about Wilson on the famous Million Dollar Quartet tapes. "In the famous million dollar quartet tapes, you can hear Elvis raving about Wilson to Johnny Cash, Karl Perkins, and Jerry Lee Lewis."
**Attempt to Join The Four Ames (Four Tops)** : After leaving Billy Ward and his Dominoes, Wilson attempted to join a vocal group with his cousin Levi Stubbs and distant relative Lawrence Payton, but his voice didn't blend well, sounding too similar to Stubbs. "Unfortunately, they found that Jackie's voice didn't blend well with the group."
**Mario Lanza's Influence** : Wilson became obsessed with Italian American pop singer Mario Lanza, studying his performances and emulating his pseudo-operatic diction and projection, particularly after watching Lanza's 1951 film "The Great Caruso," while simultaneously acknowledging the commercial necessity of R&B as a black singer in Detroit. "Wilson studied Lanza's performances and he tried to emulate Lanza's diction and projection, but at the same time he was at heart an Arvind B performer and he also knew that as a black singer in Detroit in the early 50s, Arvind B was what he needed to do to make money."
**Al Green's Management and Death** : Local mobster Al Green took on Wilson's management, securing a contract with Bob Thiel at Decca Records by promising to also sign LaVern Baker, but Green died suddenly the day before Wilson's contract was meant to be signed. "Al Green died suddenly."
**Nat Tarnapol's Rise to Power** : After Al Green's death, office boy Nat Tarnapol audaciously took over Wilson's management, getting him signed to Brunswick (Decca's rock and roll subsidiary), and later used this leverage to become a co-owner of Brunswick. "More by Chutzpah the Nathan Else, Natt Tarnapoll, an office boy who had been employed by Green, managed to take over Wilson's management, just by saying that he was in charge now."
**Laryngitis During Recording "Reet Petite"** : During the recording preparations for "Reet Petite," Wilson had laryngitis but insisted on singing the song a full octave higher than arranger Dick Jacobs suggested, despite initial skepticism from Jacobs and Bob Thiel. "Wilson couldn't demonstrate what he meant during the preparations for the session, as he had long in jatis, but he kept insisting that he should sing it a full octave higher than Jacobs initially suggested."
**Dentures Flying Off On Stage** : During a show where he was Dina Washington's support act, Wilson rolled his 'R' on the title of "Reet Petite" as he did on the record, causing his two front dentures to fly off, after which he never sang the song live again. "A one show where he was Dina Washington's support act, he rolled his R on the title of the song like he did on the record, and his two front dentures went flying off."
**Fallout with Tarnapol** : Wilson's hit factory songwriters, Barry Gordy Jr., Billy Davis, and Gwen Gordy, fell out with manager Nat Tarnapol after discovering he was putting his own copyrighted songs on the B-sides of Wilson's records to collect royalties, refusing their demand to write both sides. "After they figured out that Tarnopol was putting songs to which he owned the copyright on the b-sides of all Wilson's records, so he could get royalties from the sales."
**Career Dip and Revival** : Wilson's career experienced a dip in the early 1960s with the British Invasion but saw a significant revival in 1967. "Although his career dipped in the early 60s with the British invasion. He then had a revival in 1967."
**On-Stage Collapse in 1975** : In 1975, while performing "Lonely Teardrops" and singing the line "my heart is crying," Wilson clutched his chest and collapsed on stage; initially mistaken for part of his act, he did not recover. "But then, in 1975, in the middle of performing lonely teardrops, right after singing the line, my heart is crying, he clutched his chest and collapsed."
**Life-Saving Intervention** : Cornel Gunter of The Coasters gave Jackie Wilson mouth-to-mouth resuscitation after his collapse, possibly saving his life, although some later questioned whether this was a good idea in retrospect. "Cornel Gunter of the coasters gave him mouth to mouth and possibly saved his life."
**Coma and Vegetative State** : Following his collapse, Wilson entered a coma from which he never fully recovered, remaining institutionalized in a persistent vegetative state for eight and a half years until his death. "For the next eight and a half years, Wilson was institutionalised."
**Music Industry Support for Medical Bills** : The music business rallied to help pay for Wilson's treatment, with reports of anonymous donations from Elvis Presley (unverified) and confirmed contributions from The Detroit Spinners ($5,000), Al Green (singer, $10,000), Stevie Wonder ($5,000), Gladys Knight ($2,500), Michael Jackson ($10,000), and Patti LaBelle ($1,200). "At first, the music business rallied round and helped pay for his treatment."
**James Brown's Bounced Check** : James Brown initially sent a $1,000 check for Wilson's medical bills that bounced, but he paid the actual money after Wilson's common-law wife threatened to expose the incident to Jet Magazine. "James Brown sent a $1,000 check which bounced, but he cuffed up the actual money when Jackie's common-law wife said she was going to tell Jet Magazine about the bouncing check."
**Nat Tarnapol's Deception Regarding Insurance** : Nat Tarnapol and Brunswick Records provided no financial help for Wilson's medical bills and actively harmed his case by lying to Blue Cross Blue Shield, claiming Wilson had no earnings from them in the year prior to his collapse, thus preventing his medical bills from being covered by insurance. "They lied to Blue Cross Blue Shield, claiming that Wilson hadn't had any earnings from them in the year prior to his collapse, when he'd been in the studio and was owed regular union rates for recording sessions."
**Conspiracy Theories Around Collapse** : Hypotheses for Wilson's collapse included induced hypertension from drinking salt water to sweat, but several people close to him believed he was poisoned by Nat Tarnapol, as Wilson was due to testify against Tarnapol in a grand jury ten days after the incident. "But several people close to Wilson believed that his collapse was somehow caused by Nat Turner-Paul, having him poisoned."
**Unmarked Grave and Headstone** : Wilson was initially buried in an unmarked grave after his death in 1984, but three years later, funds were raised for a headstone that reads, "No more lonely teardrops." "He was buried in an unmarked grave, but three years later, funds were raised for a headstone, which reads, no more lonely teardrops."

## curiosities
**Creator of a Genre** : Barry Gordy Jr. is one of the very few people in the world who created an entire genre of music, primarily as a record company owner rather than a musician. "There are very few people in the world who can be said to have created an entire genre of music, and even fewer who were primarily record company owners, rather than musicians, but Barry Gordy Jr. was one of them."
**Early Songwriting Career** : Gordy began his music industry career as a songwriter, focusing on his early songwriting efforts before becoming a record executive. "When he first got into the music industry, it was as a songwriter, and today we're going to look at his early songwriting career."
**Significance in Music History** : He is considered one of the most important people in 20th-century music, as important to the music of the 1960s as Sam Phillips was to the 1950s, if not more so. "He was as important to the music of the 60s as Sam Phillips was to the 50s, if not more important."
**Childhood Piano Instruction** : Born the seventh of eight children to a poor Detroit family, Gordy was taught piano rudiments by an uncle who tried to teach him scales, arpeggios, and music reading, but Gordy, easily bored, soon discovered how to play simple triad chords by playing the first three notes of an arpeggio together. "An uncle, who tried to get him to learn to play in the proper manner, learning scales and arpeggios and how to read music."
**Boogie Woogie Piano Discovery** : Young Barry Gordy Jr. quickly figured out how to play the C, F, and G chords, enabling him to start playing Boogie Woogie Piano by ear. "Once young Barry Gordy Jr. figured out how to play the chords C, F and G, he was able to start playing Boogie Woogie Piano by E."
**Musical Influences** : From an early age, Gordy was a fan of vocal group performances, particularly touching songs about loneliness, loving "Paper Doll" by The Mills Brothers and "We Three" by The Ink Spots, and his favorite Boogie record was Hazel Scott's "Boogie Woogie." "He loved Paper Doll by the mills put this. And we three by the ink spots."
**Boxing vs. Music Career Decision** : Gordy paralleled Jackie Wilson's early career dilemma, initially unsure whether to become a musician or a boxer like his hero Joe Lewis, until he observed concert posters showing band leaders working every night and looking young, while boxers worked sporadically and looked old, leading him to choose music. "He knew what he was going to do, and it wasn't boxing."
**Korean War Service** : His nascent music career was put on hold when he was drafted to fight in the Korean War. "His attempt at a music career was soon put on the back burner when he was drafted to fight in the Korean War."
**Post-Military Struggles** : After military service, he held various short-term jobs and was seen by his family as a "bum" and a dreamer who failed to realize his aspirations of becoming a successful songwriter and millionaire. "He never held down a steady job, and he was a dreamer, who saw himself as becoming a successful songwriter and a millionaire, but had never quite managed to make anything of his dreams."
**Collaboration with Billy Davis and Sisters** : Gordy began collaborating on songs with Billy Davis and his sister Gwen, who was dating Davis, agreeing to credit any individually written songs to the group and pool their earnings, with their primary goal being to write songs for Jackie Wilson. "Anything any of them wrote on their own would also get credited to them as a group, and they'd pool whatever they got, and they were going to write songs for Jackie Wilson."
**Conflict with Nat Tarnapol** : Gordy and Billy Davis insisted they should write both sides of Jackie Wilson's singles to get a fair share of royalties, especially as they were effectively producing the sessions without credit or pay, leading to a disagreement with manager Nat Tarnapol, who felt Wilson didn't need them. "Gordy and Davis insisted that they should get to write the songs on both sides of the singles so that they could get a fair share of the money, especially as they were effectively producing the sessions without either a credit or royalties."
**Founding Anna Records** : After the fallout with Nat Tarnapol, Barry Gordy's sisters, Gwen and Anna Gordy, along with Billy Davis, went on to found their own record label, Anna Records. "Davis and Gwen Gordy went off to found their own record label, along with Gwen and Barry's sister Anna. Anna Records, as it was called, didn't have the most propitious start, with its first single being a Davis and Gwen Gordy song, Hope and Pray, performed by The Voice Masters."
**Mentoring Smokey Robinson** : After leaving Jackie Wilson's production team, Barry Gordy decided to groom another young artist for stardom, seeing great potential in William "Smokey" Robinson and his group, The Miracles. "Barry, meanwhile, decided to groom another young artist for Stardom. He saw a lot of possibilities in a young man called William Robinson, who everyone referred to as Smokey, and his group The Miracles."
