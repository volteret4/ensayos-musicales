## songs
**Johnny B. Good (1958)** : This song is described as the definitive 50s rock and roll song and was the only rock and roll track included on the Voyager probes' golden record, sent into space by NASA in the 1970s. Its introduction is one of the most well-known guitar parts in history, influencing many subsequent artists. The lyrics, initially intended as a message to Johnny Johnson, evolved to be about an idealized version of Berry himself, with the original version referring to a "coloured boy." "Johnny Be Good is so much the epitome of American post-war culture that when NASA sent a record into space on the Voyager probes in the 70s, it was the only rock and roll song included in the selection of audio."
**School Day (1957)** : Released prior to "Oh Baby Doll," "School Day" was Chuck Berry's breakout hit, successfully introducing him to the broader white teenage market that was beginning to embrace rock and roll music. "he'd just released School Day, which had been his breakout hit into the broader white teenage market that had started to listen to rock and roll."
**Oh Baby Doll (1957)** : Released as a single after "School Day," this track was a comparative flop for Berry, reaching number 12 on the R&B chart but only managing number 57 on the pop charts. Its lukewarm performance came before a string of successful releases. "His next single, Oh Baby Doll, was a comparative flop. Hit reach number 12 in the R&B chart, but only number 57 on the pop charts."
**Rock and Roll Music (1957)** : This record marked the beginning of a three-single run that cemented Berry's status as rock and roll's premier mythologiser. Having previously sung about "these rhythm and blues" in May 1956, Berry explicitly used the music's new name in this song, propelling him back into the top 10. "Just let me hear some of that rock and roll music. That put him back in the top 10 and everything seemed to be going wonderfully for him."
**Real in a Rockin' (1958)** : One of seven songs cut during a studio session, this track was a slightly reworked version of Winony Harris's old song "Round the Clock Blues." Berry's version notably removed some of the more sexually explicit lyrical content, though this often reappeared in live performances, and emphasized its similarity to "Rock Around the Clock." The copyright for the song was solely in Chuck Berry's name, despite its origins. "One of them, Real in a Rockin, was a slightly working of the old Winony Harris song, Round the Clock Blues."
**Sweet Little Sixteen (1958)** : Recorded at the same session as "Real in a Rockin'," this song captured the experience of being a rock and roll fan, moving beyond just dancing to music to describe fandom as a major part of life. While it contains elements of the "teen girl fan filtered through the male gaze," its strength lies in verses where the girl becomes the viewpoint character, expressing excitement for shows and her collections. The song famously name-checks "American Bandstand" and its location, Philadelphia. "Sweet Little Sixteen was one of the first songs about the experience of being a rock and roll fan."
**30 Days (Pre-1959 Classics)** : This song is identified as one of Chuck Berry's classics that, despite its quality, completely failed to make an impact on the pop charts. "Classics like 30 days... had totally failed to hit the pop charts at all."
**Too Much Monkey Business (Pre-1959 Classics)** : Regarded as one of Chuck Berry's classic compositions, "Too Much Monkey Business" surprisingly did not achieve any position on the pop charts. "too much monkey business... had totally failed to hit the pop charts at all."
**Brown-Eyed Handsome Man (Pre-1959 Classics)** : Despite being considered a classic, this Chuck Berry song did not register on the pop charts, highlighting the inconsistency in his early chart performance. "brown-eyed handsome man... had totally failed to hit the pop charts at all."
**You Can't Catch Me (Pre-1959 Classics)** : This is another classic Chuck Berry track mentioned that, for reasons of chart inconsistency, failed to enter the pop charts during its initial release. "and you can't catch me, had totally failed to hit the pop charts at all."

"There were some good songs, things like Camel, Little Queenie, and I've got to find my baby, but even those weren't Barry at his absolute peak."
**I've Got To Find My Baby (Post-1958)** : From the period following "Johnny B. "There were some good songs, things like Camel, Little Queenie, and I've got to find my baby, but even those weren't Barry at his absolute peak."

"And the majority of the material he put out during that time was stuff like Anthony Boy and Too Pooped to Pop, which very few of even Barry's most ardent fans will tell you a worth listening to."
**Memphis, Tennessee (Post-1958)** : Described as potentially the best song Chuck Berry ever wrote, "Memphis, Tennessee" inexplicably failed to chart. Its theme of a man seeking contact with his six-year-old daughter after a marital split resonated more with adults than the teenage audience Berry typically attracted. "While it's a travesty that that record didn't chart, in retrospect it's easy to see why it didn't."
**Nadine (1964)** : Released in 1964, immediately after Chuck Berry's release from prison, "Nadine" reached number 23 on the charts, becoming one of three significant hits he had that year. It marked a temporary commercial resurgence for Berry. "Nadine made number 23."
**You Never Can Tell (1964)** : This 1964 single became a number 14 hit, contributing to Chuck Berry's commercial comeback after his prison sentence and representing one of his biggest successes since summer 1958. "You never can tell, made number 14."

## curiosities
**Voyager Probes' Golden Record Inclusion** : Chuck Berry's "Johnny B. Good" was the sole rock and roll song selected for inclusion on the golden record sent into space aboard NASA's Voyager probes in the 1970s. The selection, which also featured classical pieces, world music, and greetings, was initially criticized for containing any rock and roll, but the record is now the furthest human-made object from Earth. "Johnny Be Good is so much the epitome of American post-war culture that when NASA sent a record into space on the Voyager probes in the 70s, it was the only rock and roll song included in the selection of audio."
**Steve Martin's "Send More Chuck Berry" Joke** : On Saturday Night Live the week the Voyager probe was launched, comedian Steve Martin famously joked about a message from aliens stating, "Send more Chuck Berry." This anecdote highlights the immense cultural significance and recognition of "Johnny Be Good" at the time. "On Saturday Night Live, the week the probe was launched, Steve Martin joked that there been a message from aliens. Send more Chuck Berry."
**Inconsistent Chart Performance Before Arrest** : Chuck Berry's career trajectory was not a continuous upward curve; after his breakout hit "School Day," his next single "Oh Baby Doll" was a comparative flop. Even before his arrest in 1959, his singles had an inconsistent chart history, with only five of his first eleven singles making the top 10 on the pop charts, and classics like "30 Days" and "Brown-Eyed Handsome Man" failing to chart at all. "Barry's career didn't go on a completely upward curve after that point. His next single, Oh Baby Doll, was a comparative flop."
**Jerry Lee Lewis's Piano Style Influence** : The popularity of Jerry Lee Lewis and his "ripping" piano technique, where fingers slide across keys quickly, significantly influenced the piano style on Chuck Berry's records. Leonard Chess insisted on Berry's regular player, Johnny Johnson, adopting this technique for new recordings despite Johnson's initial dislike for it. "This is because of the popularity of Jerry Lee Lewis, who had risen to fame since Berry's last session. Lewis tended to use a simple technique called ripping when playing the piano."
**Guitar Intro Innovation for "Johnny B. Good"** : While the basic riff for the intro to "Johnny B. Good" was derived from earlier blues and jazz records, such as Carl Hogan's playing on Louis Jordan's "Ain't That Just Like A Woman," Chuck Berry made a crucial innovation. He played the solo line on two strings instead of one, creating a thicker sound with parallel harmonies, a technique he learned from T-Bone Walker. This subtle change transmuted a common musical phrase into a signature part instantly identifiable with him. "The crucial change Barry makes there is that most of the time he's playing the solo line on two strings instead of one, creating a thicker sound with parallel harmonies, rather than just the simple melody line."
**Lyrics Origin for "Johnny B. Good"** : Chuck Berry initially conceived the lyric for "Johnny B. Good" as a personal message to his pianist, Johnny Johnson, urging him to "stop drinking so much." However, this quickly evolved into a song about Berry himself, or an idealized version of him, which was even more explicit in the original lyric where he sang "a coloured boy" instead of "a country boy." "Barry always talked about how the original idea for the lyric was as a message to Johnny Johnson saying, Johnny Be Good, stop drinking so much."
**Connection to "Good Avenue" in "Johnny B. Good"** : The title "Johnny B. Good" subtly refers to Chuck Berry's childhood home, which was located at 2520 Good Avenue. The spelling of "Goode" in the song's title, with an 'E' at the end, mirrors the street name, linking the narrative to Berry's personal history. "Barry is spelled G-O-O-D-E with an E on the end, and Barry's childhood home was at 2520 Good Avenue with an E."
**"Go Johnny Go" Film Appearance (1959)** : Chuck Berry co-starred as himself with Alan Fried in the 1959 film "Go Johnny Go," which took its title from Berry's hit song. The movie proved to be a final moment of rock and roll cultural relevance for many involved, as several featured artists faced professional decline or untimely deaths shortly after its release. "That film came out in 1959 and had Barry as Fried's co-star, appearing with Fried as himself in almost every scene."
**Reconciliation with Jerry Lee Lewis After Boston Incident** : Despite prior tension and fighting throughout their 1958 co-headlining tour, the shared adversity faced during a controversial Boston concert, where police intervened, brought Chuck Berry and Jerry Lee Lewis together. Following this event, they became close friends and spoke highly of each other. "Oddly, going through this adversity seems to have brought Barry and Jerry Lee Lewis together. While they'd been fighting each other for almost the entire tour, after this point they became quite close friends and would speak only about each other."
**Mann Act Conviction and Racial Injustice** : In December 1959, Chuck Berry was arrested for having sexual relations with a 14-year-old sex worker he had met in Texas and then fired from his St. Louis nightclub. He was charged and convicted under the Mann Act, also known as the White Slave Traffic Act, a law historically created to punish black men. While the prosecution was racially motivated and the judge made racist remarks, leading to a reduced sentence, Berry did serve time in prison from 1961. "Like Lewis, Barry has been more or less forgiven by the consensus narrative of rock history. There is slightly more justification for doing so in Barry's case than in Lewis's, because the man act, the law under which he was charged and convicted, was a law that was created specifically to punish black men."
**Class Action Lawsuit for Videotaping Women** : Years after his Mann Act conviction, Chuck Berry famously settled a class action suit brought against him by numerous women who alleged he had videotaped them on the toilet without their permission. This incident further contributed to the pattern of his problematic behavior towards women. "He would later famously settle a class action suit brought against him by a large number of women he had videotaped on the toilet without their permission."
**Myth of Career Wreckage by Arrest** : The popular belief that Chuck Berry's career was completely ruined by his 1959 arrest is a myth. While "Johnny B. Good" was his last top 10 hit for several years, his chart performance had already been inconsistent, with many classics failing to chart. His decline was more attributable to shifting public tastes and the quality of his material in the late 50s and early 60s, rather than solely his legal troubles. "There's a myth that Barry's career was completely wrecked by his arrest. This simply isn't true."
**Reasons for Late 50s/Early 60s Chart Decline** : Chuck Berry's reduced chart success in the late 1950s and early 1960s was due to two main factors: a natural shift in public taste, which few singles artists from the 50s could survive, and his own admission that the material he recorded after "Johnny B. Good" was not his best. Many of these later songs, such as "Anthony Boy" and "Too Pooped to Pop," are largely forgotten. "The first is that public taste had moved on as it does every few years. There are very few singles artists, and all artists in the 50s were singles artists, who can survive a major change in the public's taste."
**Post-Prison Commercial Resurgence (1964)** : Serving 18 months in jail unexpectedly revitalized Chuck Berry's commercial prospects. Upon his release in 1964, his first three singles—"Nadine," "You Never Can Tell," and "No Particular Place To Go"—were all bigger hits than he had experienced since 1958, suggesting a potential return to the top. "As odd as it is to say, Barry's 18 months in jail may have done him some good as a commercial prospect."
**Studio Work Decline After 1964** : Following a brief commercial resurgence in 1964, Chuck Berry's studio recordings largely reverted to mediocrity, marked by only occasional flashes of his earlier brilliance. This period saw a general decline in the quality of his new music. "But it was a false dawn. Barry's studio works slid back into mediocrity with occasional flashes of his old brilliance."
**Retirement from New Music and Touring Style** : In the late 1970s, Chuck Berry essentially ceased making new music. For the next nearly 40 years, he toured the world exclusively with his guitar, relying on local pickup bands provided by promoters and often not informing them of the next song, confident that everyone knew his extensive repertoire. "In the late 70s, Barry essentially retired from making new music, choosing instead to spend the best part of 40 years touring the world with just his guitar, playing with whatever local pickup band the promoter could scrape together."
**"Extremely Bitter Man" Personality** : Chuck Berry was widely known, according to various accounts, to possess an extremely bitter personality throughout his later years. "He was, by all accounts, an extremely better man."
**Death at Age 90 (2017)** : Chuck Berry passed away in 2017 at the age of 90. His obituaries primarily focused on his immense contributions to music, rather than his personal controversies and crimes against women. "He died, aged 90, in 2017, and the obituary is concentrated on his music, rather than his crimes against women."
**John Lennon's Rock and Roll Epithet** : John Lennon famously stated, "if you tried to give rock and roll another name, it would be Chook Barry." This quote underscores Berry's foundational and indispensable role in shaping the genre, for both its positive and negative aspects. "John Lennon once said, if you tried to give rock and roll another name, it would be Chook Barry, and for both better and worse, that's probably true."

## curiosities
**Reluctance to Adopt "Ripping" Style** : Johnny Johnson, Chuck Berry's regular pianist, initially disliked the "ripping" piano technique popularized by Jerry Lee Lewis, considering it "all flash and no technique." Despite his reluctance, Leonard Chess insisted he adopt the style for Berry's new records, even demonstrating it himself. # artist - Dick Clark

# artist - Alan Fried

## concerts
Go Johnny Go (1959 Film)
1958 Chuck Berry and Jerry Lee Lewis Tour (MC)

## curiosities
**Co-star in "Go Johnny Go" Film (1959)** : Alan Fried appeared as himself and co-starred with Chuck Berry in the 1959 film "Go Johnny Go." The film marked a late moment of cultural relevance for Fried before his professional decline. "That film came out in 1959 and had Barry as Fried's co-star, appearing with Fried as himself in almost every scene."
**Career Downturn and Industry Changes** : Fried's career was already in decline as rock and roll evolved, shifting from predominantly black musicians to white artists and being increasingly dominated by major labels producing "pre-packaged teen idols." Fried lacked the connections with major labels and the understanding of this manufactured pop, unlike his rapport with R&B records from smaller labels like Chess. "Rock and roll was moving from being a music made largely by black musicians to one dominated by white people, and to make matters worse, the major labels had finally got a handle on it, and started churning out dozens of pre-packaged teen idols, most of them called Bobby."
**Boston Concert Incident and Downfall** : During the 1958 Chuck Berry and Jerry Lee Lewis tour, Alan Fried's career began its precipitous decline at a Boston concert. After police forced the audience to sit down for dancing, Fried defiantly told the audience, "it looks like the Boston police don't want you to have a good time." This led to police falsely blaming the show for numerous crimes and contributed to Fried's eventual firing. "So Fried got back on stage and said, it looks like the Boston police don't want you to have a good time."
**Forced Resignation from Radio Station** : Following the Boston incident and existing difficulties working with him, Fried's radio station, now profitable, used a "morals clause" in his contract to fire him. He was permitted to frame it as a resignation, but it marked a significant loss of prominence. "Now that this had happened, they took the opportunity to use the Mubbles' clause in Fried's contract to fire him, although he was allowed to present it as a resignation instead of a firing."
**Payola Scandal Scapegoat** : Within a few years of losing his prominent radio job, Alan Fried became the industry's designated fall guy for the widespread practice of payola. His involvement included receiving writing credits for songs like "Mabeline" and "Sincerely" as a form of payment. "He would, within a couple of years, become the designated industry fall guy for the practice of Paola."
**Post-Scandal Career and Death** : After the payola scandal broke, Fried lost his job at WABC by the end of 1959 and spent his remaining years moving between progressively smaller radio stations, never regaining his previous prominence. He died in 1965 at the age of 43 due to illnesses caused by alcoholism. "He died in 1965, a vilness is caused by his alcoholism. He was only 43."

# genre - Rock and Roll

## curiosities
**Voyager Probes Golden Record Inclusion** : Chuck Berry's "Johnny B. Good" was the sole rock and roll song included in the selection of audio sent into space on the Voyager probes in the 1970s. This choice was initially criticized but highlights the song's iconic status as an epitome of American post-war culture. "it was the only rock and roll song included in the selection of audio, which also included pieces by Bach, Beethoven, Mozart and Stravinsky, and performances by Louis Armstrong and Blind Willie Johnson, along with folk songs, spoken greetings from world leaders, and so on."
**Transition from Black to White Domination** : In the late 1950s, rock and roll underwent a significant shift, moving from being a music largely created by black musicians to one increasingly dominated by white artists. This change coincided with major labels gaining a foothold in the genre and manufacturing "pre-packaged teen idols." "Rock and roll was moving from being a music made largely by black musicians to one dominated by white people."

# genre - Rhythm and Blues

## curiosities
**Evolution to Rock and Roll** : The term "rhythm and blues" was the precursor to "rock and roll" as the music's new name. Chuck Berry, initially singing about "these rhythm and blues," later explicitly used "rock and roll music" in his songs, marking a significant linguistic and cultural transition for the genre. "Having May 1956, Berry had sung about these rhythm and blues. This time he was going to use the music's new name and he was singing. Just let me hear some of that rock and roll music."

# genre - Jazz

## curiosities
**Early Riff Source** : The distinctive guitar riff that Chuck Berry adapted for the intro to "Johnny B. Good" has roots tracing back to early jazz recordings. The first known recording of this riff dates to 1918 in "Blue In The Blues" by Wilbur Sweatman's Jazz Orchestra. "The first recording we have of that riff dates back to 1918, and a recording by Wilbur Sweatman's Jazz Orchestra."

# label - Chess Records

## curiosities
**Influence on Chuck Berry's Piano Style** : Leonard Chess, of Chess Records, played a pivotal role in shaping the piano style on Chuck Berry's records. Convinced of the effectiveness of Jerry Lee Lewis's "ripping" technique, he insisted that Berry's pianist, Johnny Johnson, adopt this flashier style, even demonstrating it himself during rehearsals. "Chess insisted to the extent that when they were rehearsing the tracks, Chess would work over and rip his hand down the keys himself to show Johnson what he wanted."
**Competition with Major Labels through Payola** : Chess Records was one of the smaller labels that utilized payola—the practice of paying DJs to play their records—as a means to level the playing field against the major labels. These smaller labels saw payola as a way to get their records heard, countering the inherent advantage of larger companies. "the small labels that built rock and roll, labels like Sun or Chess or Atlantic, all saw it as a way that they could equalise things a little bit."

# concert - 1958 Chuck Berry and Jerry Lee Lewis Tour

## curiosities
**Boston Police Intervention and Rock and Roll Ban** : During the Boston leg of the 1958 tour, police became upset with the dancing audience during Jerry Lee Lewis's set and forced Alan Fried to make them sit down. In a later show headlined by Chuck Berry, police refused to dim the lights, prompting Fried to publicly challenge them. This led to police falsely attributing numerous crimes to the concert and Boston's decision to ban rock and roll concerts altogether, resulting in the cancellation of many remaining tour dates. "The early show, which had been headline by Lewis, had had the audience dancing, and the police were not at all impressed with this. They'd forced Alan Fried to make the audience sit down."

# concert - American Bandstand

## curiosities
**Growing Importance in Rock and Roll Culture** : American Bandstand rapidly became the most important rock and roll TV show of its era, broadcast from Philadelphia, PA. Its significance was so pronounced that Chuck Berry modified the lyrics of "Sweet Little Sixteen" to name-check "on Bandstand and in Philadelphia PA," reflecting its central role in youth culture. "American bandstand, which was fast becoming the most important rock and roll TV show around."

# instrument - Piano

## curiosities
**"Ripping" Technique and its Influence** : The "ripping" piano technique, involving rapidly sliding fingers across the keys, was popularized by Jerry Lee Lewis and became influential in rock and roll. Leonard Chess, inspired by Lewis's success, insisted that Chuck Berry's pianist, Johnny Johnson, adopt this style for Berry's recordings, despite Johnson's initial disdain for its lack of traditional technique. "Lewis tended to use a simple technique called ripping when playing the piano, in which you just slide your fingers across the keys as fast as possible."

# instrument - Guitar

## curiosities
**Chuck Berry's Two-String Innovation** : Chuck Berry's iconic guitar intro to "Johnny B. Good" transformed a common riff by playing the solo line on two strings instead of one. This technique, learned from blues guitarist T-Bone Walker, created a thicker sound with parallel harmonies, making the previously generic riff uniquely identifiable with Berry. "The crucial change Barry makes there is that most of the time he's playing the solo line on two strings instead of one, creating a thicker sound with parallel harmonies, rather than just the simple melody line."
**Tracing the "Johnny B. Good" Riff** : The foundational guitar riff for "Johnny B. Good" has a long history, with its earliest known recording dating back to Wilbur Sweatman's Jazz Orchestra's "Blue In The Blues" in 1918. It also appeared in recordings by Blind Lemon Jefferson ("Got The Blues," 1926), Blind Blake ("Too Tight," 1926), Andy Kirk, and Count Basie before Carl Hogan played it as the intro to Louis Jordan's "Ain't That Just Like A Woman." "The first recording we have of that riff dates back to 1918, and a recording by Wilbur Sweatman's Jazz Orchestra."

# curiosity

**The Mann Act (White Slave Traffic Act)** : The Mann Act, officially titled the White Slave Traffic Act, was a law specifically created to punish black men. Chuck Berry was charged and convicted under this act in December 1959 for having sexual relations with a 14-year-old sex worker, highlighting a racial injustice in its application, as other rock and roll artists were not similarly prosecuted despite similar behaviors. "Indeed, its official title was the White Slave Traffic Act."
**Payola Scandal in Music Industry** : Payola was a pervasive practice in the mid-20th century music industry, where DJs and TV presenters were paid to play specific records. Methods included adding DJs' names to writing credits (as with Alan Fried on "Mabeline" and "Sincerely"), promoters paying cash for airplay, or hiring DJs to announce shows. While small labels used it to compete with major labels, the latter campaigned against it, leading to scandals and figures like Alan Fried becoming scapegoats. "This is something we've mentioned before. The Mubbles would pay the EJs to play their records. Sometimes it was in the form of adding their name to the writing credits, as was the case for Fried with records like Mabeline and Sincerely."
