## albums
**Just Jesse Belvin (1959)** : This was Jesse Belvin's first album, recorded for RCA, a major label he signed with to pursue a career as a smooth ballad singer, similar to Nat King Cole. The album's lead single was "Guess Who," which earned Grammy nominations for Best R&B Performance and Best Male Vocal Performance. Belvin recorded this album as part of a strategic career move managed by his wife, Joanne. "He was going to be a huge star, and he actually got to record an album, Just Jesse Belvin."
**Mr. Easy (1960)** : Jesse Belvin's second album, which was recorded just before his death and ended up being a posthumous release. This album was intended to further consolidate his shift towards pop balladry in the style of Nat King Cole, moving him towards mainstream pop stardom. Its release after his passing cemented its place as a poignant part of his legacy. "That album was the one that was going to consolidate Belvin's turn towards pop balladry in the Nat King co-style, but it would end up being a posthumous release."

## songs
**All That Wine Is Gone** : Jesse Belvin's first recording with the vocal group Three Dots and a Dash, where he joined as lead singer. This track was an answer record to the popular song "Drinking Wine Spodiodi," showcasing Belvin's early involvement in the music industry. "Belvin's first recording with the group was on all that wine is gone, and answer record to drinking wine Spodiodi."
**Baby Don't Go** : Jesse Belvin's first solo single, released after he signed to Specialty Records. This single was not especially successful, prompting him to form the duo Jesse and Marvin with songwriter Marvin Phillips to seek greater commercial traction. "His first solo single, Baby Don't Go, was not especially successful, so he teamed up with the songwriter Marvin Phillips in a duo called Jesse and Marvin."
**Dream Girl** : A hit song by the duo Jesse and Marvin, which included Jesse Belvin and Marvin Phillips. The track achieved significant commercial success, reaching number two on the R&B chart, and seemed to signal a massive career ahead for the duo before Belvin was drafted into the Armed Forces. "Dream Girl went to number two on the R&B chart, and it looked like Jesse and Marvin were about to have a massive career."
**Earth Angel** : A song co-written by Jesse Belvin that became a hit while he was serving in the Armed Forces. Like many of his compositions, he initially didn't receive credit for it, but notably, he went to court over this specific track and successfully secured some royalties. "It was while he was in the Armed Forces that Earth Angel became a hit, a song he co-wrote..."
**Kokomo (Cover)** : A cover version recorded by Marvin and Johnny, a group that Jesse Belvin rejoined temporarily as one of the "Johnnys" for some follow-up singles. This track, however, did not manage to outsell either the original version or Perry Como's popular rendition, despite Belvin's involvement. "However, on some of the follow-ups, Jesse Belvin returned as one of the Johnny's, notably on a cover version of Kokomo, which didn't match to out-sell either the original or Perry Komo's version."
**Good Night My Love (1956)** : One of the most well-known songs of the 1950s, Jesse Belvin's version reached number 7 on the R&B charts. Its impact extended beyond chart success when DJ Alan Freed adopted it as the outro music for his radio show, making it familiar to a generation and establishing it as a standard covered by diverse artists like James Brown and Gloria Estefan. The song is credited to George Matola and John Mariscalco, with a prevalent rumor that Belvin composed the bridge and sold his contribution to Mariscalco for $400. "Belvin's version of the song went to number 7 in the Ardenby charts, but its impact went beyond its immediate chart success."
**Senorita** : A Latin pop track recorded by Jesse Belvin as a solo artist. This song exemplified the astonishing stylistic range of his post-"Good Night My Love" singles, showcasing his versatility beyond the doo-wop genre and his exploration of diverse musical styles. "The stylistic range of these records is quite astonishing, from Latin pop like Senorita..."
**My Satellite** : A do-wop novelty song credited to Jesse Belvin and The Spacewriters, featuring Alex Hodge of The Platters on backing vocals. Its melody shared similarities with "Hound Dog" and it was strategically released to capitalize on the "space craze" that commenced with the launch of the Russian satellite, Sputnik. "My Satellite, a song whose melody owes something to Houndog, credited to Jesse Belvin and the spacewriters, and released to cash in on the space craze that had started with the launch of the Russian Satellite's spot neck."
**Guess Who (1959)** : The first single from Jesse Belvin's album *Just Jesse Belvin*, written by his wife, Joanne Belvin, based on a love letter she had penned to him. The song was a commercial success, hitting number 33 on the Pop Chart and number 7 on the R&B charts, and earned prestigious Grammy nominations for Best R&B Performance and Best Male Vocal Performance. "That song made the top 40, hitting number 33 on the puck chart, and managed to reach number 7 on the R&B charts."

## curiosities
**Prolific Career and Aliases** : Jesse Belvin was an exceptionally prolific songwriter, performer, and group leader who released an astonishing number of records under so many different names that it is challenging to ascertain the full extent of his career. Despite his widespread influence and immense potential for stardom, this complexity contributed to him being an often-overlooked figure in music history. "He put out a truly astonishing number of records as a songwriter, performer and group leader, under so many different names that it's impossible to figure out the true extent of his career."
**Misinformation and Historical Obscurity** : Jesse Belvin's career is plagued by inaccuracies and a general lack of information, often relegating him to a footnote in the biographies of other artists. For instance, the false claim that Barry White played piano on "Good Night My Love" persisted despite White being only eleven at the time of the song's release. "But that kind of misinformation is all over everything to do with Jesse Belvin."
**Influence and Esteem Among Peers** : In the 1950s R&B scene, particularly on the West Coast, Jesse Belvin was considered an unparalleled figure among musicians, possessing immense potential to be a bigger star than anyone. Stevie Wonder cited him as his favorite singer, and Etta James vocally advocated for his inclusion in the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame, considering his exclusion a travesty. Sam Cooke explicitly modeled his career after Belvin's. "But in 1950s R&B among musicians, especially those on the West Coast, there was no bigger name than Jesse Belvin."
**Samuel Brown's Tutelage** : Like many R&B and Jazz greats, Jesse Belvin attended Jefferson High School and studied music under Samuel Brown. Brown, a significant but often uncredited figure, also taught other notable musicians like Big Jay McNeely and is recognized as an "unsung hero of rock and roll music." "Belvin had attended Jefferson High School and studied music under the great teacher Samuel Brown, who is one of the great on-song heroes of rock and roll music."
**Drafted into Armed Forces** : Jesse Belvin's burgeoning career was set back when he was drafted into the Armed Forces shortly after the success of "Dream Girl" with Jesse and Marvin. While serving, a song he co-wrote, "Earth Angel," became a hit, though he initially received no credit for his contribution. "But shortly afterwards, Belvin was drafted."
**Cavalier Attitude Towards Songwriting Credit** : Jesse Belvin frequently sold his songwriting rights for as little as $100, often resulting in him not receiving label credit for his compositions. This "cavalier attitude" is exemplified by the alleged sale of the bridge he wrote for "Good Night My Love" to John Mariscalco for $400, reflecting his focus on immediate cash over long-term royalties. "Most of the time, he would also be writing the songs, but he didn't get label credits on most of them, because he would just sell all his rights to the songs for $100."
**Abandoning The Cliques' Success** : After forming The Cliques with Eugene Church, their song "The Girl in My Dreams" reached number 45 on the Pop Chart, a clear commercial success. However, Belvin made the decision to abandon the group name and pursue a solo career rather than capitalize on or follow up on this minor hit. "And so of course, at this point, Belvin ditched the Cliques name, rather than follow up on the minor hit, and started making records as a solo artist instead."
**Joanne Belvin's Management and Songwriting** : Jesse Belvin's wife, Joanne, became his manager and songwriting partner, taking firm control of his career in 1958. Her influence led to his signing with RCA and a strategic shift towards a smooth ballad singing style, aiming for mainstream stardom in the vein of Nat King Cole. She notably wrote the hit song "Guess Who" based on a love letter to him. "But his wife also became his songwriting partner and his manager, and in 1958 she seemed to finally take control of his career for him."
**Tragic Death in Car Crash (February 6, 1960)** : Jesse Belvin died instantly in a car crash on February 6, 1960, shortly after performing at a concert in Little Rock, Arkansas. His wife, Joanne, who he reportedly shielded from the worst of the impact, was seriously injured and succumbed to her injuries a few days later. The circumstances of their deaths are shrouded in rumor and lack of official investigation due to institutional racism at the time. "On February the 6th, 1960, there was a concert in Little Rock, Arkansas at the Robinson Auditorium."
**Uninvestigated Death and Sabotage Rumors** : Following the fatal car crash that killed Jesse and Joanne Belvin, no thorough investigation was conducted due to institutional racism prevalent in the Deep South at the time. Rumors quickly circulated, including newspaper reports of slashed tires on Belvin's car, unconfirmed death threats against Belvin, and car troubles experienced by Jackie Wilson and other musicians on the same night, leading to speculation of sabotage or racist murder. "So when Jesse and Joanne Belvin died, they could have been the victims of a racist murder, or they could just have been horribly unlucky."
**Children Raised by Grandmother** : Jesse and Joanne Belvin left behind two children under the age of five, who were subsequently raised by Jesse's mother. Their oldest son, Jesse Belvin Jr., later became a singer himself, performing his father's material and dedicating his life to uncovering the truth behind his parents' mysterious deaths, though he never found definitive answers. "When they died, they left behind two children under the age of five who were brought up by Jesse's mother."

## curiosities
**Student of Samuel Brown** : Big Jay McNeely, a prominent rhythm and blues saxophone player, was a student of the influential music teacher Samuel Brown at Jefferson High School. This connection highlights Brown's significant, though often unsung, impact on rock and roll music through the many talented artists he taught. "One of the other people that Brown had taught was the great rhythm and blues saxophone player, Big Jane McNally."
**Saxophone Honker** : McNeely was known as one of the "old-time great saxophone honkers," a style largely inspired by Illinois Jacquet. He became the lead tenor saxophone player for Johnny Otis's band at the Barrel House Club, and his playing featured prominently on records such as Otis's "Barrel House Stomp." "McNally was one of the old-time great saxophone hunkers, inspired mostly by Illinois's Ikeh't, and he had become the lead tenor saxophone player with Johnny Otis's band at the Barrel House Club, and played on records like Otis's Barrel House Stump."
**Forming Three Dots and a Dash** : After gaining experience with Johnny Otis, Big Jay McNeely embarked on a solo career and formed his own vocal group, Three Dots and a Dash. He personally invited Jesse Belvin to join this group as the lead singer, marking one of Belvin's early and formative recording experiences. "As with many of the musicians Otis worked with, McNally soon went on to a solo career of his own, and he formed a vocal group, Three Dots and a Dash."

## songs
**Dragnet Blues** : A novelty "cash-in" record made by Frankie Ervin, which resulted in a lawsuit from the creators of the popular TV show *Dragnet*. "He'd made Dragnet Blues, which had resulted in a lawsuit from the makers of the TV show Dragnet..."
**Johnny Ace's Last Letter** : A version of this song by Frankie Ervin, which was rush-released by Johnny Moore's Three Blazers after Johnny Ace's death. "He'd also done his Johnny Ace infreshing on Johnny Aces last letter, a single that had been rush released by the Blazers after Johnny Aces death."
**You Cheated** : Although Frankie Ervin reportedly "hated the song" and disliked doo-wop in general, he recorded the lead vocals for this track, which was ultimately credited to The Shields. The record became a massive hit, reaching number 12 on the pop charts, and famously outsold the original white group's version. "Irving hated the song. He didn't like Doop generally, and he thought the song was a particularly bad example of the genre."
**Nature Boy** : The follow-up single by The Shields, for which original members, including Frankie Ervin, reunited. They were joined by members of The Turks. Although Ervin claimed it sold a million copies, it was less successful than "You Cheated," and the record label, Dot Records, reportedly grew tired of Ervin's demands for credit and royalties. "That, according to Irving, also sold a million copies, but it was nothing like a successful as you cheated."

## curiosities
**Reputation for Novelty Cash-in Records** : While a member of Charles Brown's group, Frankie Ervin developed a reputation for performing novelty "cash-in" records. This included tracks like "Dragnet Blues," which led to a lawsuit, and his version of "Johnny Ace's Last Letter," demonstrating his knack for capitalizing on popular trends and events. "While with Brown's group, he developed a reputation for being able to perform novelty cash in records."
**Dislike for Doo-wop and "You Cheated"** : Despite his involvement with the hit doo-wop track "You Cheated," Frankie Ervin generally disliked the doo-wop genre and found "You Cheated" to be a particularly bad example of it. He only agreed to record it for career reasons, as he was actively seeking a solo career at the time. "Irving hated the song. He didn't like Doop generally, and he thought the song was a particularly bad example of the genre."
**Frustration Over Lack of Credit for The Shields** : Frankie Ervin was annoyed when "You Cheated" was released under the group name The Shields rather than as a solo record by him, despite him performing the lead vocals. The label's decision was reportedly a strategic move to avoid promoting a solo singer who might later demand money and royalties. "Frankie Irving was naturally annoyed, that he wasn't given the label credit for the record."

## curiosities
**Accidental Million-Selling Group** : The Shields originated as a scratch vocal group assembled by Jesse Belvin for Frankie Ervin's recording of "You Cheated." The song unexpectedly became a massive hit, credited to The Shields by Dot Records, effectively creating another million-selling group that Belvin was inadvertently involved with. "But while Belvin had accidentally put together yet another million-selling group, he had also moved on to bigger things."
**Outselling the Original by a Black Group** : In an unusual turn for the music industry at the time, The Shields' sound-alike version of "You Cheated," performed by a black group, managed to outsell the original recording by the white group, The Slades. This was a notable reversal of the typical industry trend, where covers by black artists rarely surpassed white originals commercially. "This time, a sound-like record by a black group managed to outsell the original by a white group."
**Label's Avoidance of Solo Artist Demands** : The recording of "You Cheated" was an independent production leased to Dot Records. The label deliberately credited the song to a generic group name, The Shields, rather than Frankie Ervin as a solo artist. This decision was reportedly made to avoid promoting a solo singer who might later demand credits, royalties, or more money, especially given that Ervin was Black. "Somewhere along the line someone decided that it was better to have a generic group name, rather than promote it as by a solo singer, who might get ideas about wanting money."
**Touring Lineup vs. Recording Lineup** : Due to original recording members (Jesse Belvin, Johnny "Guitar" Watson, Tommy "Buster" Williams, Mel Williams) being under contract to other labels, Frankie Ervin toured and made TV appearances as "The Shields" backed by random singers assembled by the record label. Only for the follow-up, "Nature Boy," did some original members reunite, highlighting the ad-hoc nature of the group's performance lineup. "Irving started touring and making TV appearances as The Shields, backed by some random singers the record label had pulled together."
**Changing Lineups and Belvin's Departure** : For The Shields' third record, only Jesse Belvin remained from the original recording lineup, indicating the transient nature of the group. Subsequent recordings by The Shields did not feature Belvin at all, as he had moved on to bigger career opportunities with RCA, signaling the end of his direct involvement. "So The Third Shields record only featured Belvin out of The Original lineup, and subsequent recordings didn't even feature him."

# concert - Little Rock, Arkansas Concert (February 6, 1960)

## curiosities
**First Rock and Roll Show of 1960** : This concert, held at the Robinson Auditorium in Little Rock, Arkansas, on February 6, 1960, was notable as the first rock and roll show of the year. Headlined by Jackie Wilson, it featured an all-black lineup, including Jesse Belvin, who was a rising star on the bill, marking a significant event in the early rock and roll calendar. "Bill Vas, the first rock-and-roll show of 1960, the headliner was Jackie Wilson, a friend of Belvin's."
**Potentially First Integrated Audience in Little Rock** : According to some reports, the concert featured an integrated audience, with some sources claiming it was the first ever in Little Rock. This was a profoundly significant social event given the city's recent history of strong opposition to integration, including the National Guard being called in just two years prior to protect black children integrating a school. "In fact, some reports go so far as to say it was the first integrated audience ever in Little Rock."
**Racial Abuse and Car Problems** : The concert faced racial abuse shouted by members of the audience, a common experience for musicians of color at the time. After the show, several musicians, including headliner Jackie Wilson and potentially Jesse Belvin, experienced suspicious car problems en route to Dallas, contributing to later suspicions surrounding the Belvins' fatal crash. "And so, apparently, there was some racial abuse shouted by members of the audience, but it was nothing that the musicians hadn't dealt with before."

# curiosity

**Samuel Brown - Unsung Hero** : Samuel Brown was a respected music teacher at Jefferson High School who taught many significant R&B and Jazz greats, including Jesse Belvin and Big Jay McNeely. He is described as one of the "great unsung heroes of rock and roll music" due to his profound influence on these artists, shaping their musical foundations. "Samuel Brown, who is one of the great on-song heroes of rock and roll music."
**Ralph Goldberg - Vocal Group Expert** : Ralph Goldberg is identified as the preeminent expert on 1940s and 1950s black vocal group music. He held Jesse Belvin in high regard, referring to him as "the genre's most riveted stylist," a testament to Belvin's unique artistic contribution and influential style within vocal group traditions. "Ralph Goldberg, who is by far the preeminent expert on forties and fifties black vocal group music, refers to Belvin as the genre's most riveted stylist."
**John Mariscalco - Disputed Songwriter** : John Mariscalco is credited as a co-writer of "Good Night My Love" and is described as an actual songwriter for artists like Little Richard and Harry Nilsson. However, he faced accusations of undeserved songwriting credits, notably for "Birthaloo" by Johnny Fair, where it was claimed he bought the rights for $50. In the case of "Good Night My Love," he allegedly paid Jesse Belvin $400 for the bridge and took the credit. "John Mariscalco, who is credited as the co-writer of Good Night My Love, is still alive."
**Charles Shackleford - Dangerous Driver** : Charles Shackleford was Jesse Belvin's driver during the fatal tour, having previously been fired by Ray Charles for dangerous driving. According to Charles, Shackleford frequently stayed awake watching late-night shows, leading to sleep deprivation and driving that was "dangerously enough to scare Ray Charles, who was fearless enough that he used to ride motorbikes despite being totally blind."
**Little Rock, Arkansas - History of Segregation** : Little Rock, Arkansas, had a strong history of racial segregation, with white residents actively opposing integration. Two years before the 1960 concert featuring Jesse Belvin, the National Guard had to be deployed to protect black children during the integration of the city's first school, highlighting the deeply entrenched racial tensions. "Little Rock was not a place where the white people were fans of integration."
**Misinformation Around Barry White's Early Career** : A persistent but false claim suggested that Barry White played piano on Jesse Belvin's song "Good Night My Love." This was plausible on the surface as they lived in the same neighborhood and attended the same school, but White was only eleven years old when the song was released. White himself confirmed in an interview that he did not play on such classics before puberty, dispelling the widespread misinformation. "For example, in researching this episode, I found over and again that people claimed that Barry White played piano on the song we're looking at today. Good night my love."
**Car Trouble Incidents on Feb 6, 1960** : On the night of Jesse Belvin's fatal car crash (February 6, 1960), there were multiple reports of car problems among musicians traveling from the Little Rock concert to Dallas. Jackie Wilson experienced car trouble, and unconfirmed sources suggest at least one other musician on the bill also did, fueling speculation that the Belvins' crash might have been due to sabotage. "Jackie Wilson had some car problems on the way, and got to the stop in Dallas later than he was expecting to."
**Hospital's Refusal to Treat Joanne Belvin** : After the car crash that killed Jesse Belvin, his seriously injured wife, Joanne, was reportedly refused treatment at a hospital in Hope, Arkansas, until payment was guaranteed. According to Etta James, who heard the story from Jackie Wilson, Joanne remained untreated until Wilson drove from Dallas to Hope with the necessary cash, tragically highlighting the racial discrimination in healthcare at the time. "The survivors were rushed to the hospital, where, according to Weta James, who heard the story from Jackie Wilson, they refused to treat Joe Annen Belvin, until they knew that they would get money."
