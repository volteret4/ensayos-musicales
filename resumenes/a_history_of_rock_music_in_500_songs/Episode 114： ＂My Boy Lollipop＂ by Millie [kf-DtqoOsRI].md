# artist - Millie Small

## genres

British Pop Music

## labels
Fontana
Island Records (as distributor)

## members
Owen Gray (duo partner)

## albums
**Millie's Early Records** : Her early records were selling well enough for Chris Blackwell that in 1963 he convinced her mother to bring her to the UK with the promise of making her a star. These records predated her major international hits. "Her early records had been selling well enough for him, that in 1963 he had decided to call Milly's mother and promise her that if a daughter came over to the UK, he would be able to make her into a star."

## songs
**My Boy Lollipop (1964)** : Released in early 1964, this song became a massive international hit, reaching number two on the charts in both the UK and the US, launching Millie into stardom. Chris Blackwell chose this song, remembering it as a sound system favorite, and commissioned Ernest Ranglin to arrange it. The backing was performed by Jimmy Powell and the Five Dimensions, with a harmonica solo replacing the original saxophone solo to align with British chart trends; Millie consistently claimed Rod Stewart played this harmonica part. "My Boy Lollipop came out in early 1964 and became a massive hit, reaching number two on the chart both in the UK and the US, and Millie was now a star."
**Don't You Know (1963)** : Millie's first British single, released in late 1963, was described as standard British pop music of the era, lacking distinctiveness. Consequently, it did not achieve chart success. "Milly's first British single, don't you know, was released in late 1963, and was standard British pop music of the time, with little to distinguish it."
**Sweet William** : This song was Millie's next single after "My Boy Lollipop," but it only reached number 30 on the charts. Its limited success indicated the brief first wave of interest in Ska among the white public was subsiding. "Her next single though, Sweet William, only made number 30, has the brief first wave of interest in Scar among the White Public subsided."
**Bloodshot Eyes (1965)** : Millie recorded a remake of Wynonie Harris's "Bloodshot Eyes" in late 1965, intentionally omitting the original's references to intimate partner violence. This version managed to reach number 48 on the UK chart, but did not revive her earlier success. "the last thing that came near was a remake of Bloodshot Eyes, without the intimate partner violence references, which made number 48 on the UK chart at the end of 1965."
**I Canteen Attair as I'm Blue** : Millie was teamed with the rock group The Spencer Davis Group to record a cover version of this "Gang Gang song" in an attempt to replicate her earlier success. Despite their collaboration, this effort did not revive her fortunes. "she was also teamed with the rock group Blackwell had discovered, and who would soon become big stars themselves with versions of songs by Edwards, on a cover version of I Canteen Attair as I'm Blue, the Gang Gang song."
**He and I Kappa (early 1970s)** : This track represents Millie Small's final recordings in the early 1970s. It was created in response to the racism instigated by the right-wing politician Enoch Powell. "her final recordings coming in the early 1970s when she recorded the track He and I Kappa in response to the racism stirred up by the right wing politician, Enoch Powell."
**Sitting Cry** : Millie recorded this song with Owen Gray, as part of Coxsone Dodd's attempt to create a Shirley and Lee sound-alike duo. The song uniquely combined the vocal style of Shirley and Lee with the melody of "The Twist." "pairing her with the more established singer, Owen Gray, to record songs like Sitting Cry, a song which combined the vocal sound of Shirley and Lee, with the melody of The Twist."
**We'll Meet** : After Owen Gray left to pursue a solo career, Millie was teamed with Roy Panton, and their collaboration on "We'll Meet" achieved significant success, reaching number one in Jamaica. "Millie was instead teamed with another performer, Roy Panton, and Will Meet by Roy and Millie went to number one in Jamaica."

## curiosities
**Early Life and Discovery (1960s)** : Millie Small started performing at the age of 12, winning Kingston's biggest talent contest, the Vere Johns' Opportunity Hour. At 14, in 1962, her voice, noted for its spooky resemblance to Shirley Goodman of Shirley and Lee, attracted the attention of producer Coxsone Dodd, leading to her first recordings. "Millie Small had started performing at the age of 12, when she won the Vier jumps opportunity hour, the single biggest talent contest in Kingston."
**First International Ska Star** : Millie Small became Ska's first international star following the success of "My Boy Lollipop" in 1964. Her career marked the first significant impact of a Jamaican artist on the music charts in both Britain and America. "a 14-year-old girl who would become Scar's first international star."
**UK Television Appearances (1964)** : Following the success of "My Boy Lollipop," Millie gained significant media attention, including her own UK TV special. She also made an appearance on "Around the Beatles," a special produced by Jack Good and starring The Beatles. "She got her own UK TV special, as well as appearing on Around the Beatles, a special starring the Beatles and produced by Jack Good."
**Romantic Link to Peter Asher (1964)** : Millie Small was romantically associated with Peter Asher, who was one half of the popular British pop duo Peter and Gordon, during the height of her international fame. "She was romantically linked to Peter Asher of Peter and Gordon."
**Decline in Success and Poverty (1960s-2000s)** : After the initial wave of Ska's popularity subsided, Millie struggled to replicate her early success despite numerous attempts and collaborations. She subsequently spent much of the following decades in poverty, never receiving significant financial benefits from her massive chart achievements. "Millie spent much of the next few decades in poverty."
**Jamaican Honors (Later Life)** : Despite her financial struggles, Millie Small was recognized and honored multiple times by the people of Jamaica for her pioneering musical achievements, including being made a Commander in the Order of Distinction. "She was given multiple honors by the people of Jamaica, including being made a commander in the order of distinction, but never really got any financial benefit from her enormous child success."
**Later Life and Death (1970s-2019)** : Millie Small ceased performing after the early 1970s and maintained a private life, giving only one brief interview in 2016. Despite talk of a comeback in the early 1980s, she never returned to performing. She passed away last year at the age of 72. "She died last year, aged 72."

## songs

**Feel So Good** : This song showcased a more developed New Orleans style from the duo, incorporating some of the Caribbean influence prevalent in many records from the New Orleans area. "But they'd soon developed a more new Orleans style, with records like Feel So Good, showing some of the Caribbean influence that many records from the area had."

## curiosities
**New Orleans R&B Duo (1950s)** : Shirley and Lee were a New Orleans R&B duo who achieved a string of hits in the early and mid-1950s. Their sound was characterized by pairing Leonard Lee's baritone voice with Shirley Goodman's soprano. "they were a new Orleans R&B duo who had a string of hits in the early and mid-50s, recorded at Kassimon Metassa's studio, pairing Leonard Lee's baritone voice with Shirley Goodman's Saffano."
**Recording Studio (1950s)** : The duo recorded their hits in the early and mid-1950s at Cosimo Matassa's studio, a prominent recording facility for New Orleans R&B. "recorded at Kassimon Metassa's studio."
**Minor US Chart Success & Imitators** : Despite their popularity among sound system listeners in Jamaica, Shirley and Lee only achieved minor chart success in the United States. However, their distinctive sound spawned numerous imitators, including Jean and Eunice and Mickey and Sylvia. "Shirley and Lee only had minor charts success in the US, but spawned a host of emitators, including Jean and Eunice and Mickey and Sylvia."
**Jamaican Sound System Popularity** : Like much New Orleans R&B, Shirley and Lee's music was hugely popular among Jamaican sound system audiences. This popularity influenced Coxsone Dodd's decision to seek out a singer with a similar voice to Shirley Goodman, leading to his discovery of Millie Small. "Like much new Orleans R&B, Shirley and Lee were hugely popular among the sound system listeners."

# artist - Higgs & Wilson

## songs
**Come on Manioe** : This R&B track is considered the first milestone in the development of Ska. Produced by Edward Seaga, it incorporated a Mento rhythm by having the guitarist stress the off-beats, creating a uniquely Jamaican sound despite an otherwise straightforward New Orleans-style R&B composition with a Trisio bass line. "Siaga had the guitarist on Manioe by Higgs & Wilson do this, on a track that was otherwise a straightforward new early in style R&B song with a Trisio bass line."

**Early Career as R&B Radio DJ** : Duke Reid began his music career as one of the few R&B radio DJs in Jamaica, hosting a show whose theme song was Tab Smith's "My Mother's Eyes." "Reed had actually started out in music as an R&B radio DJ, one of the few in Jamaica."
**Gangster and Sound System Operator** : A former policeman, Duke Reid transitioned into a gangster and became a prominent sound system operator, founding the Trojan sound system. He was notorious for intimidating rivals with guns, escalating the soundman rivalries beyond friendly competition. "But when the former policeman turned gangster Duke Reed, started up his Trojan sound system, intimidating rivals with guns soon became par for the course."
**Trojan Sound System** : Duke Reid ran the Trojan sound system, one of the two largest sound systems in Kingston. His system was genuinely popular due to his good musical instincts, in addition to his use of intimidation to maintain power. "Reed's Trojan was one of the two biggest sound systems in Kingston."
**Acquisition of "Gator" (1960s)** : After Coxsone Dodd maintained exclusivity on "Gator" by Willis Gatortail Jackson for seven years, Duke Reid eventually tracked down the song and played it at a dance, openly signaling to Dodd that he had been "beaten." "Eventually, Word got out that Duke Reed had tracked the song down and were played at a dance. Dodd went along and was allowed in unmolecsted, Reed wanted Dodd to know he'd been beaten."

# genre - Ska

## curiosities
**Birth and Influence** : Ska emerged as a unique form of music that would profoundly influence the music of the 1970s, 80s, and beyond. Initially, in Britain and America, it was often perceived as novelty music. "We're going to look at the birth of Scar, and at the first Scar record to break big outside of Jamaica."
**Milestones in Development (Late 1950s)** : The development of Ska in the late 1950s is marked by three pivotal records. These include Higgs & Wilson's "Come on Manioe" (incorporating Mento rhythm), Coxsone Dodd's "Easy Snapping" (considered the first true Ska record), and Prince Buster's "O'Carolina" (integrating Rasta Nyabinghi rhythms). "there are three records which are generally considered to be milestones in its development."
**Origin of the Term "Ska"** : One believable theory suggests the term "Ska" originated from Coxsone Dodd's instruction to Ernest Ranglin, the arranger for "Easy Snapping," to emphasize the off-beats by saying, "play it, Scar, Scar, Scar." "There are a few different stories about where the term Scar came from, but one of the more believable is that it came from Dodd directing Ernest Wranglin... saying, play it, Scar, Scar, Scar."
**Initial View as R&B Substitute** : Early producers like Duke Reid and Coxsone Dodd initially viewed the music that would become Ska as primarily a substitute for American R&B, rather than a distinct new Jamaican genre. "But Duke Reed and Coxand Dodd at this point still saw the music they were making as a substitute for American R&B."
**Integration of Rasta Rhythms** : Prince Buster, a black nationalist and Rastafari adherent, notably incorporated the sacred rhythms of the Rasta Nyabinghi tradition into Jamaican R&B for the first time with his single "O'Carolina," marking a significant step in Ska's development. "incorporating the rhythms of Rasta's sacred music into Jamaican R&B for the first time."
**Popularity in Britain (1960s)** : Ska gained significant popularity in Britain, leading to local Ska productions by Jamaican musicians living there and charting success. Its appeal was widespread enough that even The Beatles incorporated a Ska break into "I Call Your Name" in March 1964. "SCAR was becoming the in sound in Britain, to the point that in March 1964... The Beatles made a brief tour into SCAR, in the instrumental break too, I call your name."
**First British Chart Hits (1960s)** : The first Ska record to chart in Britain was Ezrako and the Launchers' cover of "King of Kings," which reached the lower Top 40. Soon after, The Miguel Five's Ska remake of "Mockingbird Hill" made the Top 10, solidifying Ska's presence. "The first SCAR record to hit the charts in Britain was a cover of a Jimmy Cliff song, King of Kings... That made the lower reaches of the top 40, and soon after came Mockingbird Hill... That made the top 10."
**Called "Bluebeat" in Britain** : In Britain, the genre of Ska became so popular that "Bluebeat," the name of a prominent label releasing Jamaican music, became a common colloquial term used to describe the entire genre. "Bluebeat became a common term for SCAR, used to describe the whole genre."
**First Wave of Interest Subsiding (Mid-1960s)** : After an initial surge of popularity, particularly among the white public, the first wave of interest in Ska began to subside around the mid-1960s, as reflected in the lower chart performance of Millie Small's subsequent singles like "Sweet William." "the brief first wave of interest in Scar among the White Public subsided."

# curiosity

**Jamaican Music Culture (1950s-1960s) - Poverty & Communal Experience** : The Jamaican music scene in the 1950s and early 1960s developed under severe conditions of absolute poverty. Unlike richer countries, most people in Jamaica lacked access to personal record players or radios, making music a necessarily communal experience centered around public gatherings. "The thing that needs to be understood about the Jamaican music culture of the 50s and early 60s is that it developed in conditions of absolute poverty."
**Sound Systems - Origins and Function** : Sound systems emerged in Kingston, Jamaica, as big public, open-air setups run by DJs who played records for dancing. They initially started in shops to attract customers but quickly grew into standalone popular events, becoming the focal point of music consumption due to widespread lack of personal radios/record players. "Making music, or at least the music in Kingston, the biggest city in Jamaica, was organised around sound systems, big public open-air systems run by the DJs playing records for dancing."
**Music Divide - Colonial vs. Local Tastes (1950s)** : In 1950s Jamaica, still part of the British Empire, a stark contrast existed between the music favored by the white British colonial class (heard on radio) and the music preferred by the rest of the Jamaican population (played on sound systems), which predominantly consisted of Mento and imported black American R&B. "there was an extraordinary gap between the music the white British colonial class liked, and the music that the rest of the population liked."
**Limited Recording Facilities in Jamaica (Early Period)** : In the early years, Jamaica possessed very few recording facilities. Any records produced were typically made in small runs, primarily to be sold as souvenirs to tourists, rather than for widespread local distribution. "There were very few recording facilities in Jamaica, and to the extent that records were made at all there, they were mostly done in very small runs to sell to tourists."
**Record Supply by Merchant Seamen** : Due to the scarcity of records in Jamaica, soundmen relied on merchant seamen, who would often be commissioned to acquire new and exciting records from abroad. These seamen were typically compensated with rum and marijuana for their services. "They found their way into Jamaica through Merchant Seaman, who would often be tasked by soundmen with getting hold of new and exciting records, and paid with Roma Marijuana."
**The Role of the Soundman (DJ)** : Soundmen were the DJs who operated the sound systems, acting as performers who hyped crowds, invented dance steps, and used basic technology like added bass or echo to modify sounds. Their reputation hinged on finding exclusive, obscure records that audiences loved. "The soundman was the term used for the DJs who ran these sound systems, and they were performers as much as they were people who played records."
**Soundman Secrecy and Label Scratching** : To maintain their competitive edge and exclusivity, soundmen would scratch out all identifying information on record labels and replace it with new titles. This practice prevented rivals from easily obtaining copies of their unique selections. "As soon as a soundman got hold of a record, he'd scratch out all the identifying copy on the label, and replace it with a new title, so that none of his rivals could get hold of their own copies."
**Escalation of Soundman Rivalry** : Soundman rivalry initially involved friendly competition over system size and crowd draw, but escalated significantly when Duke Reid, a former policeman turned gangster, introduced intimidation tactics, including the use of guns. "But when the former policeman turned gangster Duke Reed, started up his Trojan sound system, intimidating rivals with guns soon became par for the course."
**Sound Systems as Identity Markers** : Over time, allegiance to a particular sound system became a powerful marker of identity in Jamaican society, similar to supporting sports teams or political parties. This loyalty often led to serious fights between supporters of rival systems. "People started to see their favourite sound systems in the same way they see sports teams or political parties, as marks of identity that were worth getting into serious fights over."
**American vs. Jamaican Musical Tastes (1960s Divergence)** : In the early 1960s, a significant divergence occurred between American and Jamaican musical tastes. While Americans favored new artists like Elvis, Jerry Lee Lewis, and Bobby Darin, Jamaicans continued to prefer R&B and jump band artists such as Louis Jordan, Fats Domino, and Jean and Eunice. "American musical tastes were starting to change, and Jamaican ones weren't. Jamaican audiences wanted Louis Jordan, Fat Storner and Jean and Eunice, but the Americans wanted Elvis and Gerrilly Lewis and Bobby Daring."
**Need for Local Jamaican Records** : The reliance on imported American records presented two problems: public availability for rivals and diverging musical tastes. This necessitated the creation of local "Jamaican R&B" to meet the unique demands of the island's audiences. "The solution eventually became obvious. They needed Jamaican R&B."
**Kingston Talent Contests and Disc-Cutting Services** : Kingston hosted weekly talent contests offering a five-pound prize, which winners often used to record a single copy of their song at disc-cutting services. These unique records could then be sold to soundmen, ensuring exclusivity. "Every week there was a big talent contest in Kingston... Many of the winners would then go to a disc-cutting service... They could then sell that record to one of the soundmen, who would be sure that nobody else would have a copy of it."
**Local Records as "Test Audience" for Big Sound Systems** : Initially, local Jamaican records were sold to smaller sound systems due to perceived inferiority compared to American imports. However, their success with these smaller systems eventually led bigger operators to use them as "test audiences," buying successful local records to incorporate into their own sets. "the bigger systems would start using the small ones as a test audience. If a local record went down well at a small system, one of the big operators would get in touch with the soundmen of that system, and buy the record from him."
**Jamaican Patois and Translation Challenges** : Jamaican Patois, a Creole language, is mutually intelligible with but distinct from standard English. Quoting Patois speakers presents challenges for accurate and respectful representation, with options including translation, reading directly in one's own accent (risking patronization), or attempting an approximation (risking racism). "Buster spoke Jamaican Patoir, a Creole language that is mutually intelligible with, but different from standard English."
**Jamaican Independence (1962)** : The year 1962 marked a significant turning point for Jamaican music, as it was the year Jamaica gained independence from the British Empire, taking control of its own destiny. "1962 was a turning point in Jamaican music in a variety of ways. Most obviously, it was the year that Jamaica became independent from the British Empire."
**Caribbean Immigration to UK (1948-1961)** : Following World War II, starting in 1948, the British government encouraged a massive wave of immigration from its Caribbean colonies to help rebuild the country. This led to a substantial increase in the black population in the UK, from about 20,000 in 1950 to nearly 200,000 by 1961, creating a demand for Caribbean music. "starting in 1948, there had been a massive wave of immigration from other parts of what was then still the British Empire, as the government encouraged people to come here to help rebuild the country after the war."
**Blanche Blackwell - Chris Blackwell's Mother** : Blanche Blackwell, Chris Blackwell's mother, was a notable socialite in Jamaica. Her life and numerous affairs, including with Errol Flynn and Ian Fleming, led to her being the basis for characters like Noel Coward's scandalous play character and Fleming's Pussy Galore and Honeychild Ryder. "His mother, Blanche, lived in Jamaica, where she was amused to many men."

He would write at his Jamaican villa, Golden Eye, and based characters like Pussy Galore and Honeychild Ryder on Blanche. After Fleming's death, his wife forbade Blanche from renting the villa, which was later bought by Bob Marley and then by Chris Blackwell. According to several sources, Fleming based the characters of Pussy Galore and Honeychild Ryder on Blanche."

"He'd been given a job as a go-for on the first James Bond film, Dr No, thanks to his family connections, and had also had a cameo role in the film."

Levy changed the songwriting credit to feature a mother quaintants of his, Johnny Roberts, and then passed the song to Guy Tano Vastola, another mobster."

"response to the racism stirred up by the right wing politician, Enoch Powell."
