# genre - Rock and Roll

## curiosities
**Defining Characteristics** : Early rock and roll is fundamentally defined by the presence of a backbeat, which gives a straight, simple, and exciting pulse, stressing the second and fourth beats in a bar. It draws heavily from Boogie Woogie piano parts (especially Pete Johnson's style, which influenced Chuck Berry and Jerry Lee Lewis), blues shouting vocals, a simple verse-chorus structure and floating lyrics patterned after hookum songs, and saxophone influences from rhythm and blues and swing music. "That's what rock and roll was in its early stages. A blues shelter, singing over a boogie inspired piano part with a backbeat on the snare drum, a structure and lyrics patterned after the hook and song and horns coming out of swing music."
**Evolution of Rhythm** : While blues and jazz musicians used the backbeat rhythm since the 1920s, it truly made rock and roll. The interaction between shuffle beats and backbeats, often rubbing up against each other, was a significant driver of early rock and roll. The pronounced backbeat, as heard in songs like "Shake, Rattle and Roll," provided a strong forward momentum. "It's a simpler rhythm, but a more exciting one, and that's the rhythm that made rock and roll."
**Influence from Western Music** : The cliche that rock and roll comes from R&B mixed with country music is considered "slightly wrong." While it has much influence from rhythm and blues, it has a significant, distinct influence from Western music, more so than traditional country music. "Rock doesn't have much influence from country music, but it has a lot of influence from western music."

# genre - Swing

## curiosities
**The Shuffle Rhythm** : Swing music is primarily characterized by the shuffle rhythm, where the first beat in a bar is stressed (one and two and three and four). This rhythm was the basis of almost all popular music before the backbeat. While strong and popular, it is described as a "little bit polite, a little bit tame" compared to the excitement of a backbeat. "The shuffle rhythm was the swing rhythm."
**Decline and Transformation (Late 1944)** : By the end of 1944, Swing was effectively dead as the dominant popular music form, largely due to a confluence of events including WWII, the ASCAP Boycott, and the Musicians' Union Strike. Swing musicians either transitioned into bebop or began playing new forms of music like Rhythm and Blues, which were inspired by swing but also by boogie and blues. "The end result of all this was that, at the end of 1944, Swing was effectively dead, as was the tradition of instrumentless being the stars in American music."

# genre - Blues

## curiosities
**Backbeat Origins** : Players in blues music had been using the backbeat rhythm, often done on drums, since the 1920s, long before it became popular in the 1950s. This demonstrates the rhythm's deep roots in African American music forms. "Players in blues and jazz music had been using that rhythm often done since the 1920s."
**Floating Lyrics Tradition** : A significant blues tradition involved "floating lyrics," where songs were not conventional compositions with fixed lyrics but rather a melodic structure over which a singer would improvise. Singers would pull lyrics from a set of pre-existing, commonly known phrases that were known to work, allowing for flexibility and extended performances, as seen in many of Big Joe Turner's songs. "When blues singers improvised, they tend to pull out lyrics from a set of pre-existing phrases that they knew worked."
**Folk Art Form Status** : Like country music, blues was essentially a folk art form to start with. This informal origin meant that authorship claims for songs could be difficult due to the tradition of floating lyrics and the common practice of assembling songs from pre-existing elements. "rock and roll, like blues and country before it, was essentially a folk art form to start with."

# genre - Boogie Woogie

## curiosities
**Piano Playing Style** : Boogie Woogie is a distinct style of piano playing that became popular in the 1930s. It is characterized by the left hand playing a strong, repeating bass line, often referred to as "eight to the bar," while the right hand plays decorative melodic passages over it. This bass line and melody combination became the cornerstone of rock piano playing, as well as influencing country music. "The left hand would play that, while the right hand would play decorative melodic stuff over it."
**Revival and Mainstream Popularity (1938)** : By 1938, Boogie Woogie had somewhat fallen out of fashion after a brief popularity a decade earlier. However, the "Spirituals to Swing" concerts organized by John Hammond, featuring artists like Pete Johnson and Albert Ammons, dramatically changed this. Suddenly, Boogie Woogie became "the biggest thing in music," leading every big band to adapt it to their style, and its influence appeared in numerous popular songs of the early 1940s. "That show changed that, and suddenly Boogie Woogie was the biggest thing in music."

# genre - Hookum Song

## curiosities
**Origins and Evolution** : The hookum song is a type of music with a long history, tracing its origins through Vaudeville back to minstrel songs. Initially used for comedy performances, a significant subgenre later emerged focusing on songs about sex, often using euphemistic or sometimes very explicit language, as exemplified by songs like "Fishing Pole Blues" or "Need a Little Sugar in My Bowl." "It was originally for comedy performances more than anything else, but later a whole subjummer of them started being just songs about sex."
**Simple Structure** : Hookum songs adhered to a fairly straightforward formula, avoiding the complicated structures of Tin Pan Alley songs of the time. They typically featured a simple alternating pattern of a verse (with different lyrics each time) and a chorus (which remained constant). Both the verse and chorus were usually 12-bar blues, sometimes an 8-bar blues for the verse, and this simple pattern influenced rock and roll songs throughout the 1950s. "There was a simple pattern of a verse which had different lyrics every time and a chorus which was always the same and the two would alternate."

# label - ASCAP

## curiosities
**Dominant Songwriter/Publisher Organization** : The American Society of Composers and Publishers was, and still is, an organization representing most of the important songwriters and music publishers in the USA. They collected royalties for live performances and radio plays for their members, focusing on "respectable Tin Pan Alley composers." In the early 1940s, they controlled a repertoire of over a quarter-million songs, including most popular songs played by big bands. "The American Society of Composers and Publishers was, and still is, an organisation that represented most of the most important songwriters and music publishers in the USA."
**1941 Boycott** : For ten months in 1941, ASCAP banned all US radio stations from playing any of its songs due to a royalty dispute. This boycott, intended to be catastrophic for radio, instead provided an enormous boost to rival organization BMI and the genres it represented, such as blues, country, and boogie. "for ten months in 1941, they banned all the radio stations in the USA from playing any of their songs over a royalty dispute."

# label - BMI

## curiosities
**ASCAP Rival and "Low-Class Music" Advocate** : BMI (Broadcast Music, Inc.) was set up as a rival to ASCAP a couple of years before 1941. Unlike ASCAP, BMI dealt exclusively with "low-class music," including blues, country songs, gospel songs, hillbilly music, and boogie, which ASCAP did not deem important. This focus proved advantageous during the ASCAP Boycott. "BMI dealt with only the low-class music, the blues, and country songs, and gospel songs, and hillbilly music, and boogie."
**Beneficiary of ASCAP Boycott** : During the ten-month ASCAP Boycott in 1941, BMI's repertoire of "low-class" music became immensely important because it was almost the only music, alongside public domain songs, that radio stations were allowed to play. This gave a massive audience to blues, hillbilly, and boogie performers and effectively served as free publicity for these previously marginalized genres. "Except that now, all that music became very important, because that was all you could play on the radio."

# instrument - Drums

## curiosities
**Backbeat vs. Shuffle** : Drums are crucial in differentiating the shuffle rhythm from the backbeat. In swing, drums typically emphasize the first beat in a shuffle, creating a "t-t-t-t-t-t-t-t-it" sound. In contrast, the backbeat, which made rock and roll, stresses the second and fourth beats ("BOOM, BAP, BOOM, BAP!"), providing a simpler yet more exciting pulse, a style used by blues and jazz drummers since the 1920s. "The backbeat is, to all intents and purposes, the thing that differentiates early rock and roll from the music that preceded it."
**Lionel Hampton's Afterbeat** : Lionel Hampton described his early drumming style as playing with a "heavy afterbeat," which he considered the "rock and roll beat" that became popular later. He aimed for people to dance, clap their hands on two and four, and have a good time to his drumming, demonstrating an early, unrecorded application of the backbeat. "I had a different style on drums, I was already playing with a heavy afterbeat, getting that rock and roll beat that wouldn't even get popular until the 1950s."

**Early Limitations on Vocal Performance** : In the early 20th century, microphones were either unknown or unreliable, and amplification wasn't very powerful. This forced singers to belt out their vocals as loud as they could to be heard over musicians. Even after their introduction, their limitations meant that "blues shout-outs" like Big Joe Turner were necessary to compete with the increasing volume of big bands. "In the early 20th century, microphones were unknown at first, and singers had to be able to be heard over the musicians simply by their force of their voices."

**Backbeat Rhythm Definition** : The backbeat is a straight, simple, and exciting pulse that stresses the second and fourth beats in a bar (BOOM, BAP, BOOM, BAP!). It is the fundamental rhythmic element that differentiates early rock and roll from preceding music like swing, which typically used a shuffle rhythm. While blues and jazz musicians had been using this rhythm since the 1920s, it only gained widespread popularity in the 1950s. "It's a simpler rhythm, but a more exciting one, and that's the rhythm that made rock and roll."
**Shuffle Rhythm Definition** : The shuffle rhythm, also known as swing rhythm, is characterized by stressing the first beat in a bar (One and two and three and four). It creates a "t-t-t-t-t-t-t-t-it" sound and was the typical beat in swing music and a lot of very early rock and roll records. It was the basis of almost all popular music before the backbeat. "The shuffle rhythm was the swing rhythm."
**Musicians' Union Strike of 1942-44** : This was the biggest entertainment strike in US history, lasting two years. It banned instrumentalists from recording for major record labels due to a royalty dispute, though live performances and singers (backed by vocal groups) were unaffected. This strike profoundly impacted the music industry by stockpiling old big band music, boosting minor labels and their blues/hillbilly artists, preventing the recording of bebop's birth, and ushering in the era of singers as stars. "what is still the biggest entertainment strike in US history, the Musicians' Union Strike of 1942-44."
**Impact of WWII on Big Bands** : The Second World War reaching America caused significant problems for the Big Band industry. Men who typically played in or danced to these bands were being drafted, contributing to the decline of the big band era. "The second world war reached America, and that caused a lot of problems for the Big Band industry."
**Shift from Instrumentalists to Singers as Stars** : Following the Musicians' Union Strike of 1942-44, the tradition of instrumentalists being the stars in American music ended. Instead of trombone players like Glenn Miller or clarinetists like Benny Goodman, the stars became singers like Frank Sinatra and Joe Turner, reshaping the landscape of popular music. "From that time on, the stars would stop being trombone players like Glen Miller, or clarinetists like Benny Goodman, or piano players like Pete Johnson. Instead, they were singers, like Frank Sinatra, and like Joe Turner."
**Pre-History of Rock and Roll Live Performance Importance** : Much of the pre-history of rock and roll was created by musicians who were primarily live performers and only secondarily, if at all, recording artists. Live performances, like those by Turner and Johnson, could extend for an hour with improvisation and captured an energy that recordings often failed to preserve. "But those live performances mattered."
