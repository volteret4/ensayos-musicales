## curiosities
**High School Music Training** : Martha Reeves was a soloist in her high school choir, and she credited her high school music teacher, Abraham Silver, for her real musical training. She spoke in her autobiography about singing with other people who had been taught by the same teacher in the park, including future members of The Supremes, The Miracles, and The Undisputed Truth. "her real training came from her high school music teacher, Abraham Silver."
**Motown Secretary Job** : After being seen performing solo as Martha Lavelle by Mickey Stevenson, Martha was told to audition at Motown. Due to a misunderstanding of the audition process (Stevenson expected a call, Martha just showed up), she ended up answering phones in his office, calling herself the A&R Secretary. She worked for three weeks unpaid before being put on salary as Stevenson's secretary, allowing her to get her "foot in the door" at Motown. "She started answering the phone, calling herself the A&R Secretary, taking messages and sorting up problems."
**$5 Session Bonus** : While working as Mickey Stevenson's secretary at Motown, Martha would help out on recording sessions, as many staff did. She would add backing vocals, handclaps, or foot stomps, earning a $5 bonus for each session. "adding backing vocals, handclaps or footstumps for a $5 per session bonus."
**Lifelong Crush on Mike Love** : Martha Reeves performed on the same bill as The Beach Boys in Detroit and was particularly impressed by them. She developed a lifelong crush on Mike Love. "Martha was particularly impressed by the Beach Boys, who performed on the same bill as them in Detroit, and she developed a lifelong crush on Mike Love."
**Rock and Roll Hall of Fame Induction** : Martha Reeves was inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 1995 as part of Martha and the Van Dellers, along with Rosalind Ashford, Betty Kelly, Annette Beard, and Lois Reeves. This made them only the second all-female group to receive this honor. "Martha, Rosalind, Betty, Annette and Lois were all also inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 1995, becoming only the second all-female group to be inducted."

# artist - The Delphiys

## members

Martha Reeves

## labels

## curiosities
**Formation and Early Dynamics** : Martha Reeves joined The Delphiys with three other singers: Gloria Williams (or Williamson), Annette Beard, and Rosalind Ashford. Despite personality clashes, their voices blended well, and they worked effectively on stage. Gloria Williams was initially the leader and lead singer. "The group found out early on that they didn't particularly get on with each other as people, their personalities were all too different, but their voices blended well and they worked well on stage."
**Talent Contest Rivalries** : The group performed at amateur nights and talent contests, developing rivalries with The Primes (who became The Temptations) and The Primettes (who became The Supremes). These three groups often took turns winning the contests. "developed a rivalry with two other groups. The Primes, who would later go on to be the Temptations and the Primette, who would name themselves after the Primes, but later became the Supremes."
**Lineup Fluctuations and Local Success** : The Delphiys experienced lineup changes with members coming and going, though the accounts of who was in the group when are often disputed based on credit claims rather than strict truth. The group never achieved more than local success. "No matter who was in the group though, they never had more than local success."
**Motown Audition** : When Motown's usual female backing group, The Andantes, were unavailable, Martha Reeves called the rest of The Delphiys (who may have been split up) to fill in. They auditioned for Barry Gordy, who was happy with them and signed them to Motown, though he wanted a new name due to potential contract issues with Checkmate Records. "They had to audition for Barry Gordy, but Gordy was perfectly happy with them and signed them to Motown."

## curiosities
**Name Origin** : After being signed to Motown, Barry Gordy wanted a new name for The Delphiys. They first went by The Dominettes. Martha Reeves then suggested "Martha and the Van Dellers," combining Van Dyke Street (where she lived) and Della Reese (her favorite singer). Rosalind Ashford disputes Martha's role in naming, stating the group was initially just "The Van Dellers" with Gloria as lead. "Martha suggested a name based on the street she lived on, Van Dyke Street, and Delerie's Martha's favourite singer, who had hits like, what do you know? The group became Martha and the Van Dellers."
**Backing Vocalists for Marvin Gaye** : The Van Dellers became Marvin Gaye's backing vocalists, both in the studio and for shows like the Motortown Revue. This gave them early exposure and allowed them to perform with many other artists. "Van Dellers were now also Marvin Gaye's backing vocalists, and it shows like the Muggeda Town review shows, as well as performing their own set, the Van Dellers would sing with Gaye as well."
**Shift in Group Leadership** : The session for "I'll Have to Let Him Go" marked a turning point where Martha Reeves definitively took over as the leader of the group, leading to Gloria Williams' departure. This shift in power dynamics might have been influenced by Martha booking the group for sessions as Mickey Stevenson's secretary. "this seems to be the moment that Martha Reeves took over the group."
**Gloria Williams' Departure** : Gloria Williams left the group around the time "I'll Have to Let Him Go" was released, after Martha Reeves became the definitive leader. Reasons suggested include Gloria's disapproval of Martha's leadership or her desire to keep a stable job with the city rather than risk it for a full-time singing career. "That was released under the Martha and the Van Dulles name, and around this point, Gloria left the group."
**Annette Beard's Departure** : Annette Beard left the group after two Holland-Dozier-Holland records ("Heat Wave" and "Quicksand") and two subsequent singles failed to break the top 40. She was replaced by Betty Kelly. "a net left, being replaced by Betty Kelly."
**Post-Motown Split and Touring Groups** : When Motown moved from Detroit to LA in the early 1970s, Martha Reeves chose not to relocate, leading to the group's split. Currently, there are two touring versions: "Martha Reeves and the Van Dellers" (Martha and her sisters, including Lois Reeves) and "The Original Van Dellers" (Rosalind Ashford and Annette Beard). "When Motown moved from Detroit to LA in the early 70s, Martha was one of those who decided not to make the move with the label, and the groups split up"
**Classic Lineup Members Still Alive** : Martha and the Van Dellers are noted as one of the very few 1960s hitmaking groups where all members of their classic lineup (Martha, Rosalind, Betty, Annette, and Lois) are still alive and performing, despite Gloria Williams' death in 2000. "Martha and the Van Della's are one of the very few 60s hitmaking groups, where all the members of their classic lineup are still alive and performing."
**Rock and Roll Hall of Fame Induction** : The classic lineup members Martha Reeves, Rosalind Ashford, Betty Kelly, Annette Beard, and Lois Reeves were inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 1995, marking them as only the second all-female group to receive this honor. "Martha, Rosalind, Betty, Annette and Lois were all also inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 1995, becoming only the second all-female group to be inducted."
**Greatest Motown Girl Group** : The Van Dellers are recognized as one of the greatest Motown acts and girl groups, with their biggest hits standing up against any contemporary Motown recordings, still embodying "the Sound of Young America" nearly 60 years later. "The Van Della's were one of the greatest of the Motown acts, and one of the greatest of the girl groups, and their biggest hits stand up against anything that any of the other Motown acts were doing at the time."

## songs
**I'll Have to Let Him Go (Year unknown)** : This song was originally being produced by Mickey Stevenson for Mary Wells. During a surprise union inspection, Mary Wells was absent, so Stevenson grabbed his secretary, Martha Reeves, to sing lead for the musicians. Martha performed with full effort, impressing Stevenson, who decided to give the song to her. It was then recorded with new vocals by Martha and the Van Dellers (with Gloria on lead for the backing track), and its release marked Martha's definitive leadership in the group. "That was released under the Martha and the Van Dulles name, and around this point, Gloria left the group."
**Come and Get These Memories (Year unknown)** : Released five months after "I'll Have to Let Him Go," this song is widely considered the beginning of the "Motown sound" and the start of Motown's true golden age, so much so that Brian and Eddie Holland's autobiography is named after it. It was primarily written by Lamont Dozier, who was inspired by country singers like Loretta Lynn, and features a lyrical style using physical objects as metaphors for emotional states. The song reached number 29 on the pop chart and number 6 on the R&B chart, establishing Martha and the Van Dellers as stars. "Come and get these memories, made number 29 on the pop chart, and number 6 on the Warren B chart."
**Heat Wave (Year unknown)** : The follow-up to "Come and Get These Memories," this Holland-Dozier-Holland song, again stemming from an idea by Lamont Dozier, is described as possibly Motown's most exciting record to that point. While "Come and Get These Memories" established the Motown sound, "Heat Wave" specifically established the Martha and the Van Dellers' sound and style, which Holland-Dozier-Holland would apply to many up-tempo productions. Its energetic rhythm, featuring drums accenting the backbeat and tambourine on every 4/4 beat, is credited as a precursor to Northern Soul, disco, high energy, electro-pop, the 80s hit factory sound, and much dance music. The song highlights Martha Reeves' powerful, blues and gospel-influenced voice, making it a top 10 hit. "Heatwave made the top 10, as did the follow-up, a heatwave sounder like called Quick Sound"
**Quicksand (Year unknown)** : This song was the follow-up to "Heat Wave" and had a similar sound. It also made the top 10, but the two subsequent Holland-Dozier-Holland records for the group failed to break the top 40. "Heatwave made the top 10, as did the follow-up, a heatwave sounder like called Quick Sound"
**Dancing in the Street (Year unknown)** : Written by Mickey Stevenson, Marvin Gaye, and Ivy Joe Hunter, this song became the group's most remembered record and their cultural and commercial high point, reaching number two on the charts. Marvin Gaye played drums, and Ivy Joe Hunter provided prominent percussion using either a steel chain or hitting a tire. Martha Reeves' "astonishing" vocal, with an "edge," was reportedly from a second take, as she sounded annoyed after nailing the first take, only to find a recording problem. Two years after its release, in the wake of civil rights protests, the song gained notoriety when it was misinterpreted as an incitement to riot, an idea later referenced by The Rolling Stones in "Street Fighting Man." "That went to number two in the chart, and will be the group's cultural and commercial high point."
**Wild One (Year unknown)** : Released after "Dancing in the Street," this song was so similar in sound that claims circulated it used an alternate backing track from the earlier hit, though this is stated to be incorrect. "The record after that, Wild One, was so much of a dancing in the street's sound alike that I've seen claims that the backing track is an alternate take of the earlier song. It isn't, but it sounds like it could be."
**Nowhere to Run (Year unknown)** : Following "Wild One," the group reunited with Holland-Dozier-Holland, who provided them with this "yet another great track." "the record after that, so the me-united with Holland Dozier Holland, who provided them with yet another great track, No Where To Run."
**Jimmy Mack (Year unknown)** : One of a string of classic hits released by the group in the years following "Nowhere to Run." "For the next few years, the group would release a string of classic hits, like Jimmy Mac and and honey child"
**Honey Chile (Year unknown)** : Another in the series of classic hits released by the group. "For the next few years, the group would release a string of classic hits, like Jimmy Mac and and honey child"

# artist - Holland, Dozier and Holland

## genres
Pop
Soul
R&B
Northern Soul

## curiosities
**Motown Domination** : The team of Holland, Dozier and Holland were pivotal in Motown's domination of the American charts throughout the rest of the decade, following the release of their defining "Motown sound" record for Martha and the Van Dellers. They transitioned from minor players to the most important team for the label's commercial success. "it began a domination of the American charts that would last the rest of the decade, a domination caused in large part by the team of Holland, Dozier and Holland."
**Division of Labor** : Within the Holland, Dozier and Holland team, Brian Holland and Lamont Dozier typically took charge of the musical aspects, including riffs, hooks, melodies, chord changes, and studio work with drummers, keyboardists, and bass players. Eddie Holland primarily concentrated on crafting lyrics, focusing on meaning and "repeat formation." "Holland Dozier and Holland, really Brian Holland and the Montdozier, who took charge of the musical side of things with Eddie concentrating on the lyrics, would make their own."
**Autobiography Title** : The autobiography of Brian and Eddie Holland is named after "Come and Get These Memories," despite the team having co-written many bigger and more obvious hits. This highlights the significance of that song as the beginning of the Motown sound. "Brian and Eddie Holland's autobiography is named after this, rather than after any of the bigger and more obvious hits they would like to co-write."

## songs
**Locking Up My Heart (Year unknown)** : This was the first single released with a Holland-Dozier-Holland writing credit, written and produced for The Marvelettes after Motown requested a new single for their album "The Marvelous Marvelettes." The song was a comparative flop for the group and contributed to their "downward slump." "The three of them wrote and produced Locking Up My Heart, the first single to be released with the writing credit, Holland Dozier Holland."

# artist - Eddie Holland

## member of
Holland, Dozier and Holland

## curiosities
**Early Singing Career & Jackie Wilson Comparison** : Years before Motown's start, Eddie Holland worked with Barry Gordy as a singer. He was known for a voice similar to Jackie Wilson's, and Gordy initially hired him as a demo singer for songs intended for Wilson, later attempting to mold him into a "Wilson clone" with his first single, "You." "He was a singer who was known for having a similar sounding voice to that of Jackie Wilson, and Gordy had taken him on, first as a sound-like demo singer, recording songs written for Wilson, so Wilson could hear how they would sound in his voice, and later trying to mold him into a Wilson clone, starting with Holland's first single, You."
**Dislike of Live Performance** : While Eddie Holland loved singing, he strongly disliked the experience of performing on stage. Despite this, he continued performing, believing it was important not to quit a job until a better opportunity arose. "Holland quickly found that he didn't enjoy performing on stage. He loved singing, but he didn't like the actual experience of being on stage."
**Advocacy for Brian Holland** : In his early time with Barry Gordy, Eddie frequently tried to convince Gordy to take his younger brother Brian seriously, despite Brian being in his early teens and Gordy's skepticism about his talent. "Eddie spent much of his early time working with Gordy, trying to get Gordy to take his little brother seriously."
**Switch to Songwriting for Royalties** : After his minor hit "Jamie" failed to earn him royalties (due to unearned production costs), but his brother Brian showed a royalty statement for thousands of dollars from songwriting, Eddie realized he was in the wrong job. He disliked performing and was earning nothing, so he took nine months off to teach himself songwriting, only performing the bare contractual minimum. "Eddie decided that he was in the wrong job. He didn't like performing anyway, and his brother was making serious money while he was working away earning nothing."
**Songwriting Technique - Repeat Formation** : Eddie Holland developed a songwriting technique he called "repeat formation" after studying Smokey Robinson's writing. He aimed for lyrics where every word mattered, without meaningless filler. To ensure the song's idea was conveyed even if a line was misheard, he would paraphrase the same piece of information two or three times within the lyrics. "So he came up with a technique he referred to as repeat formation, where he would give the same piece of information two or three times, paraphrasing it."

## songs
**You (Year unknown)** : This was Eddie Holland's first single, released as Barry Gordy attempted to mold him into a Jackie Wilson clone due to their similar-sounding voices. "starting with Holland's first single, You."
**Jamie (Year unknown)** : A song co-written by Barrett Strong and Mickey Stevenson, originally recorded by Strong. When Strong left Motown, the label reused the backing track, and Eddie Holland recorded new vocals over it, resulting in a minor hit that reached the top 30. Despite its chart success, Eddie received no royalties because he hadn't yet earned out the production costs from previous flop singles. "That made the top 30, which must have been galling at the time for Strong, who'd quit in part because he couldn't get a hit."

"Gordy listened to Brian and took him under his wing, pairing him with Jenny Bradford to add music to Bradford's lyrics, and also teaching him to engineer."

# artist - Lamont Dozier

## member of
Holland, Dozier and Holland
The Romeo's

## labels

## curiosities
**Early Musical Connections** : Lamont Dozier grew up surrounded by musical people. In Hutchins Jr. High School, he was a couple of years younger than Motown's first star, Marv Johnson, knew Frieda Payne, and was classmates with Otis Williams (later of The Temptations). "At Hutchins, Jr. High School, he was a couple of years below Marv Johnson, the first Motown star."
**Inspired by Aretha Franklin** : As a junior high student, Dozier was profoundly inspired by his classmate, 14-year-old Aretha Franklin, after hearing her sing and play piano at church. This experience caused him to "radically revise his ideas of what it was possible for him to do" and pursue his own musical aspirations. "That 14-year-old Aretha Franklin, and as you can imagine, being classmates with someone who could perform like that caused the Motown Dozier to radically revise his ideas of what it was possible for him to do."
**Attempted Negotiation with Atlantic Records** : At 16, after his group The Romeo's had a successful third single, "Fine Fine Fine," distributed by Atlantic, the label sought a follow-up. Dozier, believing he had negotiating power, demanded an album instead of just a single. Atlantic Records executive Jerry Wexler responded by releasing them from their contract, prematurely ending The Romeo's career. "But Lamont Dozier, at 16, thought that he had some kind of negotiating power, and wrote back saying they weren't interested in just doing a single, they wanted to do an album."
**Odd Jobs at Anna Records** : While singing with The Voice Masters, Lamont Dozier also worked for Anna Records, a Gordy family label, doing odd jobs such as cleaning floors. "And he'd continued to sing with them, as well as working for Anna Records doing odd jobs like cleaning the floors."
**Cease and Desist for "Pop-I" Song** : Dozier's first solo record under the name Lamont Anthony, released on Anna Records, was based on the comic character Popeye. As the song gained traction, King Features Syndicate, owners of Popeye, issued a cease and desist order. Dozier then re-recorded the vocal, changing the lyrics to "Benny the Skinny Man" instead of "Pop-I the Sailor Man," but the song subsequently flopped without the original hook. "Unfortunately, just as that record was starting to take off, King Feature's syndicate, The Owners of Pop-I, sent a cease and desist order, Dozier went back into the studio and recut the vocal, this time singing about Benny the Skinny Man instead of Pop-I the Sailor Man."
**Musical Telepathy with Brian Holland** : After joining Motown, Lamont Dozier and Brian Holland were introduced and quickly found an instant musical connection, discovering they had "musical telepathy." This led to their collaboration on "Forever" for The Marvelettes and formed the basis of their highly successful songwriting and production partnership. "The two instantly clicked musically. They discovered that they almost had a musical telepathy, and Holland got Freddie Garman, his lyricist partner at the time, to finish up the lyrics for the song, while he and Dozier came up with more ideas."
**Skill for Riffs, Hooks, and Rhythms** : Lamont Dozier possessed a great aptitude for creating both lyrical and musical riffs and hooks, as well as developing rhythmic ideas for songs. "Dozier had a great aptitude for coming up with riffs and hooks, both Liverpool and Musical, and rhythmic ideas, while Brian Holland could come up with great melodies and interesting chord changes, though both could do both."

# label - Motown

## curiosities
**Beginning of Domination** : Following the release of "Come and Get These Memories" by Martha and the Van Dellers, Motown began a period of chart domination in America that lasted the rest of the decade, largely attributed to the songwriting and production of Holland, Dozier and Holland. This era is considered Motown's "true golden age." "it began a domination of the American charts that would last the rest of the decade, a domination caused in large part by the team of Holland, Dozier and Holland."
**Multitracking System Innovation** : Mickey Stevenson instituted a new multitracking system at Motown, moving away from recording all musicians, lead singers, and backing vocalists simultaneously. This allowed backing tracks to be recorded separately by The Funk Brothers, with vocals overdubbed later, improving efficiency and flexibility. It also enabled reusing backing tracks for different artists (e.g., The Miracles' hits for Marvelettes covers) and replacing singers if unsuitable. "Now, backing tracks would be recorded by the Funt Brothers in the studio, whenever a writer produced her had something for them to record, and then the singer would come in later and overdubbed their vocals when it was convenient to do that."
**Union Rule Circumvention** : The Musician's Union had a rule requiring a vocalist to be present if musicians were cutting a vocal track. Motown, like other labels, often broke this rule. A surprise union inspection during a Mary Wells session led Mickey Stevenson to have his secretary, Martha Reeves, sing the lead to avoid penalties, ultimately leading to Martha taking over the song. "Motown, like most labels, were perfectly happy to break the union rules on occasion, but there was always the possibility of a surprise union inspection"
**Move to Los Angeles** : In the early 1970s, Motown Records relocated its operations from Detroit to Los Angeles. This move prompted some artists, like Martha Reeves, to decide not to make the transition with the label, leading to the dissolution of groups like Martha and the Van Dellers. "When Motown moved from Detroit to LA in the early 70s, Martha was one of those who decided not to make the move with the label"
**"The Sound of Young America"** : Motown's output, particularly its major hits from groups like Martha and the Van Dellers, is epitomized by the phrase "The Sound of Young America." Even decades later, these records continue to capture the essence of this sound. "When you hear them now, even almost 60 years later, you're still hearing the sound they were in at the birth of. The sound of Young America."

# curiosity

**High School Music Teacher Abraham Silver** : Abraham Silver was a high school music teacher in Detroit who trained many future Motown artists, including Martha Reeves, Mary Wilson, Florence Ballard (The Supremes), Bobby Rogers, Claudette Robinson (The Miracles), and Little Joe Harris (The Undisputed Truth). "her real training came from her high school music teacher, Abraham Silver."
**Musician's Union Vocalist Rule** : Historically, the Musician's Union had a rule that required the vocalist to be present during a recording session if musicians were cutting a track intended for vocals. This rule, though seemingly "ridiculous today," had "good reasons for it at the time" and was sometimes circumvented by labels like Motown. "The union had a rule that if musicians were cutting a track that was intended to have a vocal, the vocalist must be present at the session."
