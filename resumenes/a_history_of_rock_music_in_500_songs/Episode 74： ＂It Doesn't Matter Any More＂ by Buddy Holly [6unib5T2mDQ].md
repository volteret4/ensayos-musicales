## songs
**That'll Be the Day (1957)** : Buddy Holly's first hit single, released in May 1957. It initially struggled on the charts but gained traction after being picked up by Black radio stations, whose DJs mistakenly thought The Crickets were a Black doo-wop group. The song eventually reached number one. "That'll Be the Day eventually went to number one."
**Words of Love (Year not specified)** : Released as a Buddy Holly solo single, recorded by the same personnel as The Crickets' records. "And they'd also released Words of Love as a Buddy Holly solo single."
**Everyday (1957)** : Recorded on May 27, 1957, two days after "That'll Be the Day" was released, but before it charted. "Two days after That'll Be the Day was released, on the 27th of May 1957, they recorded every day and not fade away."
**Not Fade Away (1957)** : Recorded on May 27, 1957, two days after "That'll Be the Day" was released, but before it charted. "Two days after That'll Be the Day was released, on the 27th of May 1957, they recorded every day and not fade away."
**Tell Me How (1957)** : Recorded on July 1, 1957, before "That'll Be the Day" and "Words of Love" had charted. "And then and the 1st of July they recorded Tell Me How, Oh Boy, Listen to Me, I'm going to love you too."
**Oh Boy (1957)** : Recorded on July 1, 1957, before "That'll Be the Day" had charted. It was originally written by Sonny West and became a major hit for The Crickets, their last top ten, recorded at a faster tempo than West's version. "And then and the 1st of July they recorded Tell Me How, Oh Boy, Listen to Me, I'm going to love you too."
**Listen to Me (1957)** : Recorded on July 1, 1957, before "That'll Be the Day" had charted. "And then and the 1st of July they recorded Tell Me How, Oh Boy, Listen to Me, I'm going to love you too."
**I'm Gonna Love You Too (1957)** : Recorded on July 1, 1957, before "That'll Be the Day" had charted. Nikki Sullivan later received co-writing royalties for this song as part of his financial settlement with Norman Petty. "And then and the 1st of July they recorded Tell Me How, Oh Boy, Listen to Me, I'm going to love you too."
**Valley of Tears (Year not specified)** : A cover version of a Fats Domino song, recorded on July 1, 1957, before The Crickets had a single hit. "And cover versions of Fat Stomachnose Valley of Tears and Little Richards Ready Teddy."
**Ready Teddy (Year not specified)** : A cover version of a Little Richard song, recorded on July 1, 1957, before The Crickets had a single hit. "And cover versions of Fat Stomachnose Valley of Tears and Little Richards Ready Teddy."
**Peggy Sue (1957)** : This song, originally titled "Cindy Lu" after Holly's niece, was re-recorded on July 2, 1957, with lyrics changed to honor Jerry Allison's girlfriend, Peggy Sue Garrison. Its unique sound involved Allison playing a fast paviddle on snare drums outside the main studio and producer Norman Petty using an echo chamber with a flicking switch, making it sound like two drummers. Buddy Holly also had Nikki Sullivan flick a pickup switch on his guitar during the fast solo for tonal changes. It eventually reached number three on the charts. "Peggy Sue hid number three."
**Think It Over (1958)** : Holly's last top 30 single released during his lifetime, in May 1958. "His last top 30 single during his lifetime, Think It Over, was released in May 1958."
**Heartbeat (Year not specified)** : Holly's most recent record before his death, which only reached number 82 on the charts, indicating a decline in his commercial success. "And his most recent record, Heartbeat, had only reached number 82."
**It's So Easy (Year not specified)** : This song was the first recording that Tommy Allsup played on, with Buddy Holly playing rhythm guitar and Allsup taking the lead role. It did not perform well on the charts, failing to reach the top 100. The Crickets mimed to this song on American Bandstand, which was their last performance together. "It's so easy, didn't even reach the top 100."
**Reminiscing (Year not specified)** : An experimental recording where King Curtis played saxophone, indicating Holly's efforts to try different variations on his musical formula. "like getting King Curtain to play saxophone and reminiscing."
**Early in the Morning (Year not specified)** : A Bobby Darin song that Buddy Holly covered, recorded with other musicians and dispensing with The Crickets entirely, another attempt to vary his sound. "on one occasion dispensing with the crickets entirely, and having Buddy caught a Bobby DARREN song early in the morning with other musicians."
**Joli Blanc (1958)** : An old Cajun song previously a hit for Moon Mulligan, recorded by Waylon Jennings in September 1958 at his first session, produced by Buddy Holly. This version featured Holly on guitar and King Curtis on saxophone, representing an interesting blend of Cajun music with R&B. "This version had Holly on guitar and King Curtain's on saxophone, and is a really interesting attempt at blending Cajun music with R&B."
**True Love Ways (1958)** : One of three songs Buddy Holly intended to record with the Dick Jacobs Orchestra in October 1958, pioneering the use of a full string section for a rock and roll singer. "recording three songs, his own true love ways, a song called Moon Dreams written by Patti and one called Raining in My Heart written by Feliz and Woodlow's Bryant."
**Moon Dreams (1958)** : A song written by Norman Petty, intended for recording with the Dick Jacobs Orchestra in October 1958. "recording three songs, his own true love ways, a song called Moon Dreams written by Patti and one called Raining in My Heart written by Feliz and Woodlow's Bryant."
**Raining in My Heart (1958)** : Written by Felice and Boudleaux Bryant, who wrote many hits for The Everly Brothers, and intended for recording with the Dick Jacobs Orchestra in October 1958. "one called Raining in My Heart written by Feliz and Woodlow's Bryant, who'd written many hits for his friends, the Evli Brothers."
**It Doesn't Matter Anymore (1958)** : A song given to Buddy Holly by Paul Anka. Holly decided to record it at the last minute during his October 1958 session with the Dick Jacobs Orchestra. Dick Jacobs quickly arranged it, basing it around pizzicato violins due to time constraints. After Holly's death in January 1959, the song became his first posthumous hit, rising to number 13 on the US charts and number one in many other countries. "At the last minute though, he decided to record a fourth song, which had been met him for him by Paul Anker, the same kid whose Diana had been so overtating to him the year before."
**Rave On (Year not specified)** : Another song by Sonny West that Buddy Holly later covered. "Holly would later also cover another of West's songs, Raven."

## curiosities
**Early Career Stagnation (1957)** : Despite an astonishing outpouring of songs, including "Everyday," "Not Fade Away," and covers of Fats Domino and Little Richard, Holly and The Crickets recorded extensively before having a single hit with "That'll Be the Day" or "Words of Love." "Remember, this was all before they'd had a single hit."
**Affair and Group Tensions (Year not specified)** : Holly was secretly having an affair with a married woman, which caused tension as his best friend and bandmate, Jerry Allison, was also in love with her. This personal conflict, along with Nikki Sullivan's growing unhappiness, contributed to internal group problems. "Holly was having an affair with a married woman at the time, and Jerry Allison, who was Holly's best friend as well as his bandmate, was also in love with her, though not in a relationship with her, and so Holly had to keep his affair hidden from his best friend."
**Anxiety Over Chart Success (1957)** : During a fishing holiday in Colorado, Holly was constantly worried about the lack of success of "That'll Be the Day" and obsessed over Paul Anka's number one hit "Diana," feeling his own records were just as good. "But he spent much of the trip worried about the lack of success of that'll be the day, and obsessing over a new record by a new singer, Paul Hanke, that had gone to number one."
**Racial Ambiguity in Early Success (1957)** : "That'll Be the Day" gained traction after Black radio stations began playing it, mistaking The Crickets for an earlier Black doo-wop group also called The Crickets. This led to the band being booked on an otherwise all-Black tour without the promoters realizing they were white. "People at Black radio stations had assumed that this new group called the Crickets was the same one."
**Harlem Apollo Performance (1957)** : When The Crickets performed at the notoriously hostile Harlem Apollo, they had to reconfigure their sets because the audience disliked Holly's original material, with the exception of "That'll Be the Day," but responded well to their R&B cover versions like Bo Diddley. They worked hard to win over the audience. "the group had to reconfigure their sets, as the audiences didn't like any of Holly's original material, except that'll be the day."
**Backstage Revelations at Brooklyn Paramount (Year not specified)** : During an engagement at the Brooklyn Paramount on an Alan Fried show, Nikki Sullivan, while trying to quit smoking and in a bad mood, revealed Holly's affair to Jerry Allison, leading to a fist fight within the group. "Nikki Sullivan, who was in an awful mood because he was trying to quit smoking, revealed the truth about Holly's affair to Alison, and the group got in a fist fight."
**Financial Exploitation by Norman Petty (Year not specified)** : Despite promises of an equal share of income, producer and manager Norman Petty began to manipulate the group's finances, handling all the money himself and only giving the members pocket money. He eventually reconfigured the live act's financial split, giving Holly 50% and Mauldin/Allison 25% each, while keeping the full amount himself. "Petty was handling all the money and only giving the group pocket money, rather than giving them their actual shares of the money they'd earned."
**Marriage to Maria Elena Santiago (Year not specified)** : Holly met Maria Elena Santiago, a Puerto Rican receptionist at their publisher in the Brill Building, and proposed to her on their very first date. Her aunt, with whom she lived and who headed the publishing company's Latin American division, approved the marriage after verifying Holly's character. "Buddy proposed on the very first date."
**Maria Elena's Influence and Conflict with Petty (Year not specified)** : Maria Elena, being older and from the music industry, encouraged Holly to get a proper accounting of his money, straining his relationship with Norman Petty, who expressed misogynistic and racist sentiments towards her. "Maria Elena was someone who worked in the music industry, and was a little bit older, and she started saying things to Buddy, like, you need to get a proper accounting of the money that sold you, and you should be getting paid."
**Creative Explosion in Greenwich Village (1958)** : After firing Norman Petty and being cut off financially, Holly moved to Greenwich Village with Maria Elena and experienced a massive creative surge, writing dozens of acoustic demos and planning ambitious projects including a Latin music album, gospel duets with Mahalia Jackson, and soul duets with Ray Charles. He also enrolled in Lee Strasberg's Actor's Studio to study method acting. "He was not only writing songs himself, but he was busily planning to make an album of Latin music, and he was making preparations for two more projects he'd like to do. An album of duets on gospel songs with Mahalia Jackson, and an album of sold duets with Ray Charles."
**Petty's Contract Deception (1958)** : Norman Petty revealed that Holly had actually been recording for Petty's company, not Brunswick or Coral, meaning all royalties went to Petty, and the Crickets' supposed bank account was empty. This financial revelation occurred while Maria Elena was pregnant, forcing Holly to undertake the "Winter Dance Party" tour. "By the terms of buddy's contract, he hadn't really been recording for Buns Recall Coral, so they didn't tell him a penny."
**The Winter Dance Party Tour Conditions (1959)** : The tour in Wisconsin, Iowa, and Minnesota through January and February 1959 was plagued by miserable conditions, including zigzagging routes with 400-mile distances between gigs, musicians sleeping on buses, and using eight different buses in 11 days due to breakdowns. Heating failures in temperatures as low as minus 40 degrees Fahrenheit and three feet of snow caused illness (flu for Bopper and Valens) and frostbite (Carl Bunch). "The musicians had to sleep on the tour bus, or buses."
**Robert Zimmerman's Early Exposure (1959)** : A young man named Robert Zimmerman, later known as Bob Dylan, saw one of the Winter Dance Party shows in Duluth, Minnesota, where Holly, Dion, and Valens were taking turns playing drums due to their drummer's frostbite. "The shows were still good, at least according to a young man named Robert Zimmerman, who saw the first drummers show in Duluth, Minnesota."
**Plane Crash Circumstances (1959)** : After a show in Clear Lake, Iowa, Holly chartered a plane to Fargo, North Dakota, to get laundry done and sleep in a real bed. Tommy Allsup and Waylon Jennings gave up their seats to the Big Bopper and Richie Valens, who were suffering the most from the tour conditions. The pilot, despite being good in daylight, had a poor instrument flight rating, and the plane had an unusual altimeter that functioned inversely to typical models. The plane plummeted nose first less than five minutes after takeoff, killing everyone instantly. "The plane took off, and less than five minutes after takeoff, it plummeted straight down, nose first, into the ground top speed, killing everyone on board instantly."
**Maria Elena's Tragic Discovery and Miscarriage (1959)** : Maria Elena learned of Buddy Holly's death from the television, not the police, which later prompted changes in how news of celebrity deaths was handled. She was so distraught that she miscarried two days later and has never been able to visit his grave. "Maria Elena found out about Bodies Death, not from the police, but from the TV."
**Posthumous Financial Success for Petty (1959-1965)** : After Holly's death, Norman Petty profited immensely from a stockpile of unreleased Buddy Holly recordings. He also secured an agreement with Holly's estate to overdub new backing tracks onto Holly's acoustic demos, transforming them into full-band rock and roll songs, which resulted in posthumous hit singles until 1965 outside the US, making both Petty and Holly's estate wealthy. "Suddenly, his stockpile of unreleased Buddy Holly recordings was a gold mine, and not only that, he ended up coming to an agreement with Holly's estate that he could take all those demos Holly had recorded, and overdub new backing tracks on them, turning them into full-blown rock-and-roll songs."
**Hologram Tour (Current)** : Buddy Holly's image is currently used in hologram tours, where an actor's image is projected onto glass using the Pepper's Ghost trick, accompanied by a live band playing along to his records, continuing to generate income for his estate. "You can now go and see a hologram tour, in which an image of a look not very alike actor is projected on glass, using the old Victorian stage trick Peppers Ghost, while a live band plays along to the records."

## curiosities
**Studio Personnel and Recording Strategy (Year not specified)** : The Crickets' records were made by Buddy Holly (vocals, lead guitar), Nikki Sullivan (rhythm guitar), Jerry Allison (drums), Joe Mauldin (bass), and producer Norman Petty (with his wife sometimes on keyboards). Straight-ahead rock and roll tracks would have backing vocals overdubbed by The Picks and be released as Crickets records, while more experimental tracks with only Holly's vocal would be solo releases. "The more straight ahead Rock and Roll records would have backing vocals overdubbed on them, usually by a vocal group called The Picks, and would be released as Cricket's records."
**The Name Split Strategy (Year not specified)** : The distinction between "Buddy Holly" solo records and "The Crickets" group records was a deliberate strategy by their management to encourage DJs to play two different records, creating the impression of distinct artists. "the whole idea of the split was that DJs would play two records instead of one, if they appeared to be by different artists."
**Jerry Allison's Drumming Technique on "Peggy Sue" (1957)** : For "Peggy Sue," drummer Jerry Allison played a fast "paviddle" on his snare drum continuously. Due to the loudness, he had to play in a separate, outside room from the main studio. Producer Norman Petty added an echo chamber effect, flicking the switch on and off to create the illusion of two drummers. "Allison started playing a fast paviddle on his snare drum. Holly decided that they were going to change the tempo of the song, and have Allison play that part all the way through."
**Nikki Sullivan's Side-lined Role (1957)** : Nikki Sullivan's rhythm guitar was often deemed unnecessary in The Crickets' songs, and on "Peggy Sue," he ended up not playing guitar at all. Instead, he acted as Holly's "foot pedal," kneeling beside him to flick a pickup switch on Holly's guitar to change its tone for the solo, and then back again. "Nikki Sullivan was already starting to regret joining the cricket, because the rarely wasn't room for his rhythm guitar and most of the songs they were playing."
**Internal Group Tensions (Year not specified)** : Beyond Holly's affair, tensions existed between Jerry Allison and Nikki Sullivan. A holiday to Colorado with Holly's brother Larry was intended to defuse the situation but did not alleviate the stress. "Allison and Sullivan were starting to have problems with each other, too."
**The Apollo Controversy (1957)** : The Crickets were among the few white acts to play the Harlem Apollo, though not the first (Bobby Darin and possibly Buddy Rich preceded them). They faced a notoriously hostile audience and had to adapt their setlist to include more R&B covers to win over the crowd, as Holly's original material (except "That'll Be the Day") was not well received. "when it reached the Harlem Apollo, which had notoriously hostile audiences, the group had to reconfigure their sets, as the audiences didn't like any of Holly's original material, except that'll be the day."
**Nikki Sullivan's Departure (Year not specified)** : Nikki Sullivan quit The Crickets after only 90 days of touring and a total of 27 songs recorded during Holly's lifetime, due to feeling side-lined and unhappy with the group's internal dynamics and financial arrangements. He attempted a solo career with "It's All Over" on Dott Records but was unsuccessful, later working in electronics and benefiting from the Buddy Holly nostalgia industry. "Sullivan was becoming more and more sideline in the group, and when they returned to Lubbock at the end of the tour, drawing which he'd ended up breaking down and crying, he decided he was going to quit the group."
**Sullivan's Financial Settlement (Year not specified)** : Nikki Sullivan engaged in endless battles with Norman Petty over the group's accounts and his promised equal share of income. He eventually settled for just $1,000, 10% of the recording royalties for "That'll Be the Day," and co-writing royalties for "I'm Gonna Love You Too." His church, which Petty had promised 10% of the Crickets' income, received nothing. "Sullivan got into endless battles with Petty, overseeing the group's accounts, which Petty wouldn't show him, and eventually settled for getting just $1,000, 10% of the recording royalties just for the single That'll Be the Day, and co-writing royalties on one song, I'm going to love you too."
**Norman Petty's Manipulation Post-Split (1958)** : After Holly's plan to confront Petty and form their own company, Petty manipulated Jerry Allison and Joe Mauldin by telling them they were "small town Texas boys" incapable of living in New York, that they didn't need Holly, and that Maria Elena was controlling him. He also threatened to withhold all their money, leading Mauldin and Allison to side with Petty against Holly. "Pettie started manipulating them. He told them they were small town Texas boys who would never be able to live in the big city."
**Continuation After Holly's Death (1959-2015)** : After Buddy Holly's death, the remaining Crickets picked themselves up and continued as a band. Sonny Curtis joined on guitar, and they went through a succession of Holly sound-alike singers. Joe Mauldin remained a member until his death in 2015. "Meanwhile, the remaining crickets picked themselves up and carried on."

"Holly, Dion and Valens would all take it in turns playing the drums, as all of them were adequate drummers."

# artist - Norman Petty

## genres

## curiosities
**Producer and Manager (Year not specified)** : Norman Petty served as the producer for Buddy Holly and The Crickets, and later became their manager. His wife sometimes contributed by adding keyboards to their recordings. "producer Norman Petty and his wife, sometimes adding keyboards."
**Financial Exploitation (Year not specified)** : Petty handled all the money for The Crickets, giving them only pocket money and withholding their actual shares. He later manipulated the financial split to give Buddy Holly 50% and Joe Mauldin/Jerry Allison 25% each, while still controlling all funds. He also made false promises of church donations. "Petty was handling all the money and only giving the group pocket money, rather than giving them their actual shares of the money they'd earned."
**Misogyny and Manipulation (Year not specified)** : Petty expressed extreme misogynistic slurs against Buddy Holly's new wife, Maria Elena Santiago, and manipulated Jerry Allison and Joe Mauldin against Holly by claiming Maria Elena was controlling him and that they couldn't survive in New York without Petty. "Petty used some extremely misogynistic slurs I'm not going to repeat here against his new wife."
**Contract Deception (Year not specified)** : Petty secretly structured Buddy Holly's recording contract so that Holly was recording for Petty's company, which then sold the masters to other labels like Brunswick and Coral, allowing Petty to collect all royalties and leave The Crickets' bank account empty. "By the terms of buddy's contract, he hadn't really been recording for Buns Recall Coral, so they didn't tell him a penny. He'd been recording for Petty's company, who then sold the masters onto the other labels and would get all the royalties."
**Posthumous Riches (1959-1984)** : After Buddy Holly's death, Norman Petty became very rich by exploiting his stockpile of unreleased Holly recordings and by securing an agreement with Holly's estate to overdub new backing tracks onto Holly's acoustic demos, transforming them into full-band songs. "Suddenly, his stockpile of unreleased Buddy Holly recordings was a gold mine, and not only that, he ended up coming to an agreement with Holly's estate that he could take all those demos Holly had recorded, and overdub new backing tracks on them, turning them into full-blown rock-and-roll songs."
**Death and Unreleased Project (1984)** : Norman Petty died in 1984. His final project was an unreleased attempt to update Buddy Holly's biggest hits using synthesizers. "Norm and Petty died in 1984. His last project was a still unreleased updating of Buddy's biggest hits with synthesizers."

# concert - Winter Dance Party

## curiosities
**Tragic Tour Conditions (1959)** : The "Winter Dance Party" tour, spanning Wisconsin, Iowa, and Minnesota in early 1959, was marked by miserable logistical and environmental conditions, including a zigzagging route with gigs up to 400 miles apart, constant bus breakdowns (eight buses in 11 days), non-functioning heating in sub-zero temperatures (minus 40 Fahrenheit) with three feet of snow, leading to illness (flu for Big Bopper, Valens) and frostbite (Carl Bunch). "The Winter Dance Party toured Wisconsin, Iowa and Minnesota through the end of January and the beginning of February 1959, and the conditions were miserable for everyone concerned."
**Reduced Lineup and Financial Woes (1959)** : The tour had a surprisingly small lineup of only five acts (Frankie Sado, Dion and The Belmonts, The Big Bopper, Richie Valens, Buddy Holly), and was run on a shoestring budget. After the fatal plane crash, the remaining performers were forced to continue the 13 remaining dates without the headliners and were not paid their full wages due to the bill being unadvertised. "This one had only five. There was an opening act called Frankie Sado, and then Dion and the Belmont, who had had a few minor hits, and had just recorded, but not yet released."

# concert - Harlem Apollo

## curiosities
**White Act Performances (Pre-1957)** : While The Crickets performed at the Harlem Apollo, they were not the first white act to do so; Bobby Darin had played there before them, and jazz drummer Buddy Rich possibly as well. "Some have said that the Crickets were the first white act to play the Apollo. That's not the case. Bobby Daring had played there before them, and I think so had the jazz drummer Buddy Rich and maybe one or two others."
**Challenging Audiences (1957)** : The Harlem Apollo was known for its notoriously hostile audiences, requiring The Crickets to reconfigure their setlists to include more R&B cover versions to win over the crowd, as Holly's original material (except "That'll Be the Day") was not well received. "when it reached the Harlem Apollo, which had notoriously hostile audiences, the group had to reconfigure their sets, as the audiences didn't like any of Holly's original material, except that'll be the day."

"More than any other musician of his generation, his death has entered Ruck and Roll mythology."
