## albums
**Meet the Searchers (1963) - Debut Album** : This album featured "Sweets From My Sweet" and a selection of songs mixing standard Merseybeat repertoire with more obscure tracks. It included covers like "Money," "Dedu Run," "Twist and Shout," "Stand By Me," The Everly Brothers' "Since She Broke My Heart," P.J. Proby's "Ain't Gonna Kiss You," Dillon Di's "Farmer John," and "Love Potion Number 9." "Farmer John" later became a garage rock standard largely due to The Searchers' version. "Love Potion Number 9" was not released as a single in the UK but became their biggest hit in the US. "Their first album, Meet the Searchers, featured sweets from my suite, along with the selection of songs that mixed the standard repertoire of every mezzy beat band."
**It's the Searchers (1964) - Second Album** : This album included "Needles and Pins." By the time of its release, Tony Jackson was the only group member not to have a solo lead vocal, highlighting his diminishing role. "By the third album, it's the searches, which included Needles and Pins. Jackson was the only group member not to get a solo lead vocal."
**Re-recordings Album (1972) - Old Hits Collection** : The band released an album of re-recordings of their old hits between 1966 and 1979. "Their one album between 1966 and 1979 was a collection of re-recordings of their old hit, in 1972."
**Sire Records Albums (1979) - New Wave Pop Period** : After being signed to a two-album deal by Seymour Stein of Sire Records, The Searchers produced records that fit the late 70s New Wave Pop landscape while retaining their signature sound. These albums had critical success but failed commercially, leading Sire to drop them. "Apparently, during those sessions, Curtis, who had given up music and become a civil servant, would regularly phone the studio threatening to burn it down if he wasn't involved."

## songs
**Sweet Nothings (1962, released later) - Brenda Lee Cover** : A live version of this Brenda Lee song was recorded during their performances at the Star Club in Hamburg in 1962 and later released as a single after the group achieved success. "Recordings were made of their performances there, and their live version of Brenda Lees Sweet Nothings later got released as a single after they became successful."
**Sweets For My Sweet (1963) - US Top 20 Cover, UK #1** : This cover of a song by Doc Pomus and Mort Shuman, originally a US Top 20 hit for The Drifters, became The Searchers' first single. Tony Jackson sang lead with Chris Curtis on high falsetto backing vocals. The group transformed the original Latin Cha-Cha rhythm into a straightforward, thumping rocker, and misheard lyrics led to "your first sweet kiss" instead of "your tasty kiss." It went to number one in the UK and was endorsed by John Lennon. "That went to number 1, helped by an endorsement from John Lennon, who said it was the best record to come out of Liverpool, and launched the searches into the very top tier of Liverpool groups."
**Sugar and Spice (1963) - Tony Hatch Composition, UK #2** : This song was Tony Hatch's attempt to replicate the success of "Sweets For My Sweet," presented under the pseudonym "Fred Nightingale." The group disliked the song, which was a blatant ripoff of their first hit with a nearly identical arrangement. It peaked at number two in the charts, despite the group's later anger upon discovering Hatch's deception. "The song in question, Sugar and Spice, was a blatant ripoff of sweets from my suite, and recorded in a near identical arrangement."
**Needles and Pins (1964) - International Breakthrough, UK #1, US #16** : This song, originally recorded by Jackie DeShannon, became The Searchers' international breakthrough and established a new musical style. Chris Curtis picked it up after hearing Cliff Bennett and the Rebel Rouses cover it in Hamburg. The Searchers tightened the song, altered its dynamics, and emphasized the riff by playing it simultaneously on two electric guitars with added reverb, and in the key of A. This inadvertently invented Jangle Pop and Folk Rock, influencing bands like The Byrds, Love, Big Star, Tom Petty, R.E.M., The Smiths, and The Bangles. "While the Beatles were, overall, obviously the more influential group by a long way, Needles and Pins has a reasonable claim to be the most influential single track from the Mersey Beat era."
**Don't Throw Your Love Away (1964) - Orlons Cover, UK #1, US Top 20** : This cover of an Orlons B-side, featuring Mike Pender on lead vocals and Chris Curtis on harmonies, continued their run of success, reaching number one in the UK and the US Top 20. "The follow-up, don't throw your love away, a cover of a B side by the Orlands, again featuring Pendron Leed vocals and Curtis on harmonies, also made number one in the UK, and the US top 20, giving them a third number one out of four singles."
**Someday We're Gonna Love Again (1964) - Barb Lewis Cover, UK #11** : A cover of a Barb Lewis song, this single only reached number 11, leading journalists to question if The Searchers were losing their touch. "The next single, someday we're going to love again, a cover of a Barb the Lewis song, only made number 11, and caused journalists to worry if the searchers lost their touch."
**Love Potion Number 9 (1965) - US Hit** : While not released as a single in the UK initially, this Leiber and Stoller song, originally written for The Clovers, became The Searchers' biggest US hit. Tony Jackson later re-recorded it as a solo artist hoping for UK chart success. "A song that Leibh and Stuller had written for the Clovers, which was not released as a single in the UK, but later became their biggest hit in the US."
**What Have They Done To The Rain (1965) - Malvina Reynolds Cover, UK Top 20** : This song, written by socialist activist Malvina Reynolds to oppose nuclear weapons testing, was learned by Chris Curtis from a Joan Baez album. It was The Searchers' first folk song released as a single and, despite its departure from their normal style and controversial subject matter, achieved a Top 20 position. "Even though it wasn't as big a success as some of their other hits, given how utterly different it was from their normal style and how controversial the subject was, getting it into the top 20 at all seems quite an achievement."
**Good Bye My Love (1965) - Last UK Top 10 Hit** : This single marked The Searchers' final Top 10 hit in the UK. "The next single, Good By My Love, was their last Top 10 hit, and the next few singles only made the Top 40, even when the Rolling Stones gave them take it or leave it."
**Take It Or Leave It (1966) - Rolling Stones Composition** : This song was given to The Searchers by The Rolling Stones. It was one of several singles that only made the Top 40 after their last Top 10 hit. "The next single, Good By My Love, was their last Top 10 hit, and the next few singles only made the Top 40, even when the Rolling Stones gave them take it or leave it."
**Have You Ever Loved Somebody (1966) - Hollies Composition** : This song was given to The Searchers by The Hollies, who had originally written it as an Everly Brothers-style album track. However, Chris Curtis produced a rival version for Paul and Barry Ryan, which competed with The Searchers' release. Neither single made the Top 40, and The Searchers never had another hit single. "Unfortunately for the searches, Chris Curtis had also heard the song, decided it was a likely hit, and had produced a rival version for Paul and Barry Ryan, which got pushed out to compete with it."
**When You Walk In The Room (1964) - Jackie DeShannon Cover, UK #3** : This song, written solely by Jackie DeShannon and originally a B-side, was rearranged by The Searchers. They emphasized the riff from DeShannon's original, playing it on real 12-string guitars with added compression, and featured a joint lead vocal by Mike Pender and Frank Allen. It reached number 3 on the charts. "Their version featured a joint lead vocal by Pender and Allen."

## curiosities
**Early Origins Dispute** : The story of The Searchers' early origins is highly disputed, with the two surviving original members, John McNally and Mike Pender, having legal disputes and not speaking for 36 years. "As a result, literally everything about the group's history has become a battlefield in their ongoing arguments."
**Skiffle Group vs. Country/Rock and Roll Group** : Mike Pender vehemently denies that he or the group were ever involved in Skiffle, insisting he disliked the genre and that if John McNally had a Skiffle group, it was before Pender joined. Pender claims he and McNally co-formed a group that was "definitely absolutely not in any way a Skiffle group" and was not led by McNally. "According to Pender, he never liked Skiffle, never played Skiffle and if McNally had a Skiffle group it must have been before I met him."
**Name Origin Dispute - "The Searchers"** : While everyone agrees the name came from the John Wayne film *The Searchers* (which also inspired Buddy Holly's "That'll Be the Day"), Pender claims it was his idea, while McNally attributes it to an early singer named Big Run. "Pender claims that it was his idea while McNally says that the name was coined by a singer named Big Run, who sang with the band for a bit before disappearing into obscurity."
**Johnny Sandin's Departure** : Billy Beck, performing as Johnny Sandin, was the lead singer for two years before quitting to join The VMO4, a group managed by Brian Epstein. This left The Searchers without a frontman, leading them to split lead vocals. "The group performed as Johnny Sandin in the searches for two years, before Sandin quit the group to join the VMO4, a group that was managed by Brian Epstein."
**Chris Curtis as Frontman and Second Lead Singer** : After Johnny Sandin's departure, Chris Curtis moved his drum kit to the front of the stage, played standing up, and became the group's frontman and second lead singer, a distinctive move for the band. "In a move which made them stand out, Chris Curtis moved his drum kit to the front line, started playing standing up, and became the group's frontman and second lead singer."
**Clean Living Image vs. Members' Vices** : The Searchers were known as the most clean-living Liverpool band, all devout Catholics attending mass weekly and generally avoiding vices common among rock stars. However, Tony Jackson was a heavy drinker who became aggressive when drunk, and Chris Curtis was known for his eccentricity and possible mental illness. "The searches were the most clean living of the Liverpool bands. They were all devout Catholics who would go to mass every Sunday without fail, and seemed to have never indulged in most of the vices that pretty much every other rock star indulged in."
**Internal Conflicts and Factions (Early Days)** : Cracks appeared early in the group. Curtis and Jackson didn't get along, and Jackson drifted apart from Pender, leading to a dynamic of "three plus one outsider" by their first recording sessions, with Jackson isolated. "By the time of their first recording sessions, they appeared to most people to be a group of three plus one outsider, with Jackson not getting on well with any of the others."
**Musical Taste Differences as Creative Drive** : A split in musical taste defined much of their creativity: Pender and McNally favored softer country and rockabilly (Everly Brothers, Buddy Holly), while Jackson preferred harder, stomping music. Chris Curtis, however, primarily shaped the group's repertoire, seeking out obscure R&B B-sides and folk music. "There was also a split in the band's musical taste, but that would be the split that would drive much of their creativity."
**Strong Harmony Vocals** : The Searchers were considered the best harmony group in Liverpool outside of The Beatles, and the only other one with multiple strong lead vocalists, with their harmonies inspired more by The Everly Brothers than by soul groups. "Other than the Beatles, the searchers were the best harmony group in Liverpool, and were the only other one to have multiple strong lead vocalists."
**Brian Epstein's Missed Management Opportunity** : The Searchers struggled to gain attention from the London music industry partly due to an unfortunate incident where Brian Epstein came to see them, but Tony Jackson was drunk, fell off the stage, and Epstein decided against managing them. "The day that Brian Epstein turned up to see them, with the thoughts of maybe managing them. Tony Jackson was drunk and fell off the stage, and Epstein decided that he would be going to give them a miss."
**A&R Club Demo Session** : Lacking talent scout interest, the band recorded a full, short demo album at the A&R Club, where they regularly played, sending it to A&R people. This session captured their "stompiest and hardest driving" sound, which was more powerful than their later studio recordings. "That demo session produced a full, short album, which shows them at their stompiest and hardest driving."
**Pye Records Contract and Tony Hatch** : Tony Hatch, a successful songwriter and producer, was impressed by The Searchers' demo and offered to sign them. Their manager agreed on the condition that Hatch also sign The Undertakers, which he did. "Hatch heard the group's demo and was impressed and offered to sign them. The search has managed at the time agreed on one condition that Hatch also signed another band he managed, the undertakers."
**Connection to The Undertakers and Jackie Lomax** : As part of their Pye Records deal, The Searchers' manager insisted that Tony Hatch also sign The Undertakers. Although The Undertakers released several flop singles, their lead singer, Jackie Lomax, later released the solo single "Sour Milk Sea," written for him by George Harrison. "The biggest mark that the undertakers would make on music would come many years later, when their lead singer Jackie Lomax would release a solo single, SourMLC, which George Harrison wrote for him."
**John Lennon's Endorsement of "Sweets For My Sweet"** : John Lennon publicly declared "Sweets For My Sweet" the "best record to come out of Liverpool," significantly boosting The Searchers' standing among local groups. "That went to number 1, helped by an endorsement from John Lennon, who said it was the best record to come out of Liverpool, and launched the searches into the very top tier of Liverpool groups."
**"Farmer John" Becoming a Garage Rock Standard** : The Searchers' cover of Dillon Di's "Farmer John" is credited with making it a garage rock standard, as it was not widely known before their version. "Farmer John by Dillon Dui, which hadn't yet become a garage rock standard, and indeed seems to have become so largely because of the searches version."
**Controversial Lyric in "Love Potion Number 9"** : The lyric for "Love Potion Number 9" contains a word for Romany people that many regard as a slur. "The lyric contains a word for Romany people, which many of those people regard as a slur."
**Tony Hatch's Pseudonym for "Sugar and Spice"** : Tony Hatch used the pseudonym "Fred Nightingale" when presenting "Sugar and Spice" to the group, a common practice for producers at the time to hide their authorship from labels. The group was later angry upon discovering his deception. "In this case, he brought the group a song that he claimed to be missing by one Fred Nightingale, and which he thought would be perfect for them."
**Jackie DeShannon's Uncredited Co-Authorship of "Needles and Pins"** : Jackie DeShannon insists she co-wrote "Needles and Pins" with Sonny Bono and Jack Nitzsche, but her name was left off the credit, likely due to their alleged abusive and misogynistic nature. "Bano and Nitchie are the credited writers on Needles and pins, but Jackie Dishanan insists that she co-wrote the song with them, but her name was left off the credit."
**Chris Curtis's Bass Pedal Squeak on "Needles and Pins" Single** : The original single mix of "Needles and Pins" is marred by an "incredibly squeaky bass pedal" on Chris Curtis's drum kit. While barely audible on early 60s equipment, modern digital copies make it overpower the music, leading to modified versions on compilations. "The mix used for the original single version of the song, which is what I'm exerting here, is marred by an incredibly squeaky bass pedal on Chris Curtis's drum kit."
**Invention of Jangle Pop and Folk Rock with "Needles and Pins"** : The Searchers' arrangement of "Needles and Pins," by emphasizing the riff with two electric guitars played simultaneously with reverb (creating an electric 12-string sound), inadvertently invented the genres of Jangle Pop and Folk Rock. "That record is the root of Django Pop and Folk Rock."
**Tony Jackson's Departure and Disputes** : Tony Jackson, who regarded himself as the lead singer, felt sidelined as Mike Pender took over lead vocals on hits. Musical differences (Jackson preferring harder R&B while the group moved melodic) and personality conflicts, including a fistfight with Pender on stage, led to his departure. Jackson claimed he was pushed out so Chris Curtis could bring in his boyfriend (Frank Allen), which Allen denied. "The combination of these musical differences and his feelings about having his place usurped, meant that Jackson was increasingly getting annoyed at the other three band members."
**Chris Curtis's Sexuality Speculation** : Tony Jackson publicly alleged that Chris Curtis was gay and wanted to bring his boyfriend, Frank Allen, into the band. While Allen denied a relationship, Curtis was widely described as gay or bi by contemporaries but never came out publicly, possibly due to strong religious views. There were suggestions Jackson blackmailed the band about Curtis's sexuality for more lead vocals. "There's some suggestion, indeed, that one reason Jackson ended up out of the band was that he blackmailed the band, saying that he would publicly out-curtis if he didn't get more lead vocals."
**Chris Curtis's Erratic Behavior and Departure** : The other band members grew annoyed with Chris Curtis's song choices and increasingly erratic behavior. During an Australian tour, they flushed his supply of "uppers and downers" down the toilet, leading to his departure from the group upon returning to the UK. "Eventually, on an Australian tour, they took his supply of uppers and downers, which he had been using as much to self-medicators for enjoyment as far as I can tell, and flushed them down the toilet."
**Chris Curtis's Post-Searchers Career - Roundabout and Deep Purple Connection** : After leaving The Searchers, Chris Curtis released one solo single, "Aggravation," featuring Jimmy Page, John Paul Jones, and Joe Moretti, but it failed to chart. He then formed a band called Roundabout with a rotating lineup concept. However, John Lord and Richie Blackmore, who were part of Roundabout, decided to form Deep Purple after Curtis's departure. "The guitarist and keyboard player quickly decided that it would be more convenient for them if Curtis was the one to hop off, and without Curtis, John Mord and Richie Blackmore went on to form Deep Purple."
**Sire Records Deal and Revival Attempt** : In 1979, Seymour Stein of Sire Records, inspired by The Ramones covering "Needles and Pins," signed The Searchers to a two-album deal. These albums were critically well-received for fitting into the New Wave Pop landscape but were commercial failures, leading Sire to drop the band. "Apparently, during those sessions, Curtis, who had given up music and become a civil servant, would regularly phone the studio threatening to burn it down if he wasn't involved."
**Financial Struggles and Internal Strife (1980s)** : By 1985, The Searchers were at a breaking point due to lack of new material and intense animosity between Mike Pender and John McNally, the only two Liverpool-based members. They were so poor they shared one car for gigs, leading to unpleasant long drives. "The group was so poor that McNally and Pender had one car between the two of them."
**Mike Pender's Departure and Formation of "Mike Pender's Searchers"** : Mike Pender, feeling he was the band's star and resented splitting money three ways (drummers were salaried), decided to tour independently. An agreement was reached for him to tour as "Mike Pender's Searchers," provided all promotional material used equal-sized lettering. "Pender decided that he could make more money by touring at his own, but still doing essentially the same show, with hired backing musicians."
**Legal Disputes Over Band Name and Touring Rights** : Following Mike Pender's departure, extensive litigation ensued between "Mike Pender's Searchers" and the remaining "The Searchers" (with John McNally and Frank Allen). These disputes over billing and naming rights were highly contentious, nearly leading to Pender's imprisonment and threatening both groups with bankruptcy due to undercutting. "A big chunk of the autobiographies of both Pender and Alan are taken up with the ensuing litigation, as there were suits and counter suits over a matter of billing which on the outside look incredibly trivial, but which of course mattered greatly to everyone involved."
**Longevity of Rival Bands** : Mike Pender continued to tour, and the McNally and Allen lineup performed regularly until 2019, ceasing due to McNally's ill health. Pender's show was noted as being better, sticking to a guitar-based sound, while McNally and Allen's lineup relied on a "cheesy sounding synthesiser." "McNally and Alan's lineup of the group relied rather too heavily on a rather cheesy sounding synthesiser for my taste, while Pender stuck closer to a straight guitar-based drum sound, but both kept audiences very happy for decades."
**Mike Pender's MBE (2020)** : Mike Pender was appointed a Member of the Order of the British Empire (MBE) in 2020 for his services to the music industry. "Mike Pender was made an MBE in 2020, as a reward for his services to the music industry."
**Posthumous Reconciliation and Enduring Animosity** : Tony Jackson and Chris Curtis both died in the 2000s, with Pender and Allen exchanging pleasantries at their funerals. However, John McNally and Mike Pender never reconciled, refusing to speak even after McNally's band retired, with the remaining Searchers' website still prominently asserting ownership of the band name. "McNally and Pender wouldn't even say hello to each other, and even though McNally and Alan's band has retired, there's still a prominent notice on their website that they own the name of the surges, and nobody else is allowed to use it."
**Enduring Influence of Combined Guitar Sound** : The jangly 12-string electric guitar sound, originally created by Mike Pender and John McNally playing in unison, proved to be greater than its individual parts and is a lasting legacy. "But every time you hear a jangly 12-string electric guitar, you're hearing a sound that was originally created by Mike Pender and John McNally playing in unison, a sound that proved to be greater than any of its constituent parts."
