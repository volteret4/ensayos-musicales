## albums
**The Return of The Marvelettes (1970) - Wanda Young Solo Album** : This album, released under The Marvelettes' name, was effectively a solo project by Wonder Young, produced by Smokey Robinson. Catherine Anderson refused to appear on the cover photograph, stating that if she wasn't good enough to sing on the record, she wouldn't appear on the cover either. "This was a solo album produced by Smokey Robinson, but they did try to get Catherine to appear on the cover photograph."

## songs
**Please Mr. Postman (1961)** : This song became Motown's first number one on the pop charts and its second million-seller, crucially saving the label from potential bankruptcy after earlier follow-ups to "Shop Around" had sold poorly. It was the first record from Motown to distinctly feature "the Motown Sound" and established the label as "the sound of Young America." "Please Mr. Postman became the second million seller for Motown, and its first number one on the pop charts."
**Twistin' Postman (1962)** : Released as The Marvelettes' second single, this song was designed to capitalize on the success of "Please Mr. Postman" and the widespread popularity of the "twist craze" at the time. It reached the top 40 on the charts. "Twist in Postman went top 40, but it didn't do anything like as well as please Mr Postman."
**Playboy (1962)** : Originally conceived by Gladys Horton, this song was rewritten by Brian Holland, Robert Bateman, and Mickey Stephenson, propelling the group back into the top 10 on the pop chart (number 7) and number 4 on the R&B chart. It marked a significant return to chart success after "Twistin' Postman" didn't perform as well. "This time it was Gladys, who came up with a song called Playboy, which Brian Holland, Robert Baigman and Mickey Stephenson rewrote, and which made number 7 on the pop chart and number 4 on the R&B chart."
**Don't Mess With Bill (1966)** : This single, their first of 1966, marked a resurgence in the group's pop chart success, becoming their first top ten pop hit since "Playboy" in early 1962. Smokey Robinson took over their production and began using Wonder Young as the primary lead vocalist for this and subsequent tracks. "Don't mess with Bill, their first single of 1966, became their first top ten pop hit since Playboy in early 1962."
**The Hunter Gets Captured by the Game (1967)** : Written by Smokey Robinson specifically for Wonder Young, this track continued the trend of Wonder serving as the sole lead vocalist, with Motown's in-house backing vocal group, The Andantes, providing harmonies. This production method was attributed to the group's constant touring schedule. "Robinson also wrote the marvelous The Hunter Gets captured by the game for the group. Or at least he wrote it for Wander."
**Destination Anywhere (1968)** : This record was released during a period when The Marvelettes continued to have R&B hits but were struggling to break into the Top 100 on the pop charts. The group was facing internal challenges, including Wonder Young's substance abuse and mental health issues. "The group continued having R&B hits, but while they kept releasing great records like Destination anywhere, they were by now barely scraping the heart 100 on the pop charts."

## curiosities
**Origin as The Delvid Matt** : Gladys Horton, at just 14 years old, formed an earlier group named The Delvid Matt in Inkster, Michigan. This group released one single before disbanding, but Gladys remained determined to pursue music. "When Horton was only 14, she had formed a group called The Delvid Matt, who made one single, and the Delvid Matt had split up."
**Formation as The Cassin Yet** : Gladys Horton formed a new group for a high school talent contest, initially jokingly naming themselves The Cassin Yet. The name, a self-deprecating reference to their perceived vocal abilities ("because they can't sing yet"), was used for their performance, which placed fourth but secured them a Motown audition. "The group she formed originally jokingly named themselves The Cassin Yet, because they can't sing yet, and that was the name under which they performed at the talent contest."
**Auditioning for Motown** : After placing fourth in a talent contest offering a Motown audition as a prize, The Cassin Yet impressed the label but were told they needed to present original material to be signed. This led to the creation of "Please Mr. Postman," which later secured their contract. "The group came forth, but one of their teachers, Shirley Sharply, knew the person from Motown who was arranging the auditions, and persuaded them to offer auditions to the top five, rather than just to the winners."
**Name Change to The Marvelettes** : The group's name, The Cassin Yet, was deemed unsuitable, and Barry Gordy supposedly renamed them The Marvelettes after hearing them sing and exclaiming, "those girls are marvels." This new name marked their official birth as a Motown act. "The name they were eventually given supposedly came after Barry Gordy heard them sing it and said, those girls are marvels."
**Georgia Dobbins' Father's Refusal to Sign Contract** : Georgia Dobbins, a key contributor to "Please Mr. Postman," was forced to quit the group when her father refused to counter-sign her record contract because she was under 21 and he believed the entertainment industry was "sinful." Her mother was too ill to sign, and this led to Georgia giving up singing entirely until 1978. "Her father hadn't known she was singing, until she brought the record contract home for him to counter sign."
**Juanita's Departure Due to American Bandstand Mockery** : Juanita (Winnie Cowatt) left the group after a humiliating experience on "American Bandstand." She mistakenly said Detroit was a suburb of Inkster instead of the other way around, leading to mockery that caused her to have a breakdown. "She felt so bad about this slip-up and the way she was mocked for it, so she had a breakdown and ended up leaving the group."
**Motown's Policy on Group Members and Touring** : After "Please Mr. Postman" became a hit, Motown initially suggested sending "any five girls" on the road as The Marvelettes, a proposition the actual members rejected. The label later replaced a pregnant Wonder Young on tour with Florence Ballard from The Supremes, ensuring that touring contracts for the group specified "five Marvelettes." "Not only that, but at one point when Wonder had been pregnant, Motown had replaced him on the road with Florence Ballard from the Supremes."
**Rejection of "Where Did Our Love Go"** : The Marvelettes missed the opportunity for a major hit when Gladys Horton disliked the song "Where Did Our Love Go," which was written for them by Holland, Dozier, and Holland. She found the song "too simplistic," leading Motown to offer it instead to The Supremes, who were then known internally as "the no-hit Supremes." "Holland, Doge and Holland had written a song for them, but Gladys didn't like it."
**Georgiana Tillman's Illness and Early Death** : Georgiana Tillman, who had been diagnosed with sickle cell anemia as a child, became increasingly ill from the strain of constant touring. She was later diagnosed with lupus, an autoimmune disease that was often a death sentence at the time, leading her to retire from music and work as a secretary for Motown. She died in 1980 at the age of 36. "Eventually it was realised that she had lupus, an autoimmune disease which is now largely treatable if not curable, but at the time was often a death sentence."
**Dual Lead Vocal Attempt as The Darnells** : Motown experimented with changing the group's dynamic by releasing a single under the name "The Darnells," featuring Gladys Horton and Wonder Young on dual lead vocals, backed by Motown's in-house group, The Andantes. This endeavor, however, proved unsuccessful. "The record was put out under the name The Darnells, but was unsuccessful."
**Shift to Wonder Young as Sole Lead Vocalist** : After a period of less successful follow-ups, Smokey Robinson took over the group's production and decided to make Wonder Young the primary lead vocalist, replacing Gladys Horton, who had sung lead on their earlier hits. This change coincided with a second commercial wind for the group as a trio. "Smokey Robinson took over their production and decided to start using Wander as the lead vocalist, rather than Gladys, who had sung lead on their hits up to that point."
**Impact of Touring on Recording Practices** : By the mid-1960s, while records were still released as by The Marvelettes, Smokey Robinson often only utilized Wonder Young for lead vocals, with The Andantes providing backing. This practice was justified by the group's constant touring schedule, making it easier to record without the full lineup. "By this point, while the records were getting released as by The Marvellettes, Robinson was only using Wander for lead vocals, and having the Andante's single or backing vocals."
**Erosion of Group Cohesion** : The departure of Juanita and Georgiana significantly impacted the group's internal dynamics, as they had been the "glue" that held the initial members together. Catherine Anderson later noted that the remaining members, who hadn't known each other prior, transitioned from being friends to merely "acquaintances and co-workers." "Juanita and Georgiana had been the glue holding the group together. They'd been the ones who had been friends with all the others."
**Gladys Horton's Departure and Group Priority** : Gladys Horton's decision to leave the group in 1967 lowered The Marvelettes' priority within Motown. Although Wonder Young was the lead singer by then, the group was still informally considered "Gladys' group" by Motown staff due to her initial leadership role. "This made the group an even lower priority for Motown. While Wander was by now the undisputed lead singer, within Motown there were thought of as Gladys' group, as she'd been the leader in the beginning."
**Wanda Young's Personal Tragedies and Mental Health Decline** : Wonder Young experienced a series of personal crises, including cocaine and alcohol abuse, the shooting death of her younger sister, and having her drink spiked during a European tour. These events severely impacted her mental health, leading to lasting problems for over 50 years and rendering her unable to perform regularly for decades. "She was never the same again, and has had mental health problems for the last 50 years."
**Unannounced Group Split** : The Marvelettes informally dissolved without any official announcement; they simply stopped receiving bookings for tours and went their separate ways. Motown, owning the group name, subsequently sold it off, allowing other groups to tour under The Marvelettes' name without any original members. "The groups split up though nothing was announced, they just didn't get booked and anymore tours, and went their separate ways."
**Motor City Records Reunion Album** : In the late 1980s, British DJ Ian Levine, who owned Motor City Records, persuaded Gladys Horton and Wonder Young to come out of retirement to record a new Marvelettes album. This reunion effort featured two new backing vocalists, Echo Johnson and Jean McLean, and included remakes of old hits and new songs. "He managed to persuade Gladys and Wanda out of retirement to make a new Marvillette's album with two new backing vocalists, Echo Johnson and Jean McLean."
**Lack of Recognition at Motown Anniversaries** : Despite being the first group to achieve a number one hit for Motown, none of the original Marvelettes members were invited to participate in any of the label's 25th, 40th, 50th, or 60th-anniversary celebrations or associated TV specials, which honored the label's history. "None of the members of the first group to hit number one on the label were invited to be part of any of them."

# artist - The Primettes

## members

Florence Ballard

## labels
Motown

## curiosities
**Motown's First Girl Group** : The Primettes were the first girl group signed to Motown, preceding The Marvelettes. They were later renamed The Supremes but struggled to achieve success for several years after releasing only two singles. "Motown had already signed one girl group, a group called The Primates, who had been renamed and who had so far released two singles."
**Controversial Signing Reason** : The Primettes' signing was controversial among Motown staff, who widely believed they were brought on more due to Barry Gordy's attraction to member Diane Ross than for their talent. Florence Ballard, however, was well-regarded for her helpful and friendly demeanor. "But the Supremes, as they were renamed, wouldn't become successful for several years, and were generally regarded as a joke among the Motown staff, who thought, not entirely without reason, that they had been signed more because Barry Gordy was attracted to Diane Ross, one of the members of the group, than because of any talent they had."

**Florence Ballard's Popularity and Support** : Florence Ballard, a member of The Supremes, was highly popular among Motown staff, known for her helpful and friendly nature. She assisted Gladys Horton in crafting her lead vocal performance for "Please Mr. Postman," showcasing inter-group collaboration despite The Supremes' then-unsuccessful status. "One of the girls, though, Florence Ballard, was very popular at Motown, and was generally regarded as being helpful and friendly. She worked with Gladys on her lead vocal part, and helped her craft her performance."
**Touring as The Marvelettes Substitute** : At one point, when Wonder Young of The Marvelettes was pregnant, Florence Ballard replaced her on tour to fulfill a contractual obligation for five Marvelettes. This highlights the fluidity of Motown's touring lineups and the good relations between Florence and the Marvelettes. "At one point when Wonder had been pregnant, Motown had replaced him on the road with Florence Ballard from the Supremes."

## songs
**Playboy (1962) - Original Song Idea** : Gladys Horton initially conceived the song "Playboy," which was later rewritten by Brian Holland, Robert Bateman, and Mickey Stephenson, ultimately becoming a top 10 pop and R&B hit for The Marvelettes. "This time it was Gladys, who came up with a song called Playboy, which Brian Holland, Robert Baigman and Mickey Stephenson rewrote, and which made number 7 on the pop chart and number 4 on the R&B chart."

## curiosities
**Early Group Formation** : At the age of 14, Gladys Horton formed her first group, The Delvid Matt, in Inkster, Michigan. Despite their brief existence and single release, her ambition led her to form The Cassin Yet for a high school talent contest, which eventually secured an audition with Motown. "When Horton was only 14, she had formed a group called The Delvid Matt, who made one single, and the Delvid Matt had split up."
**Leadership and Musical Direction** : Gladys Horton was the original leader of The Marvelettes and sang lead on their early hits, including "Please Mr. Postman" and "Playboy." She also served as the group's musical director and choreographer, responsible for set lists and dance moves. "Gladys was a combination musical director and choreographer, working out the groups set lists and dance moves."
**Departure from The Marvelettes** : Gladys Horton decided to leave The Marvelettes in 1967. Despite Wonder Young becoming the lead vocalist, Motown staff still viewed the group as "Gladys' group" due to her foundational leadership, and her departure lowered the group's priority. "And then in 1967 Gladys decided to leave the group. This made the group an even lower priority for Motown."
**Post-Motown Reunion Recording** : In the late 1980s, British DJ Ian Levine persuaded Gladys Horton, along with Wonder Young, to come out of retirement to record a new Marvelettes album for Motor City Records, featuring remakes of old hits and new material. "He managed to persuade Gladys and Wanda out of retirement to make a new Marvillette's album with two new backing vocalists, Echo Johnson and Jean McLean."
**Touring as "Gladys Horton of The Marvelettes"** : After leaving Motown, Gladys Horton toured on the oldies circuit, performing her old hits. Due to Motown's ownership and sale of the group name, she was legally required to bill herself as "Gladys Horton of The Marvelettes." "Wanda was still too well to perform regularly, but Gladys went out on tour on the oldies' circuit, singing her old hit as Gladys Horton of the Marvillette, as none of the group owned the original name."
**Exclusion from Motown Anniversaries** : Despite her pivotal role as the original leader and lead singer of Motown's first group to hit number one, Gladys Horton was never invited to any of Motown's 25th, 40th, 50th, or 60th-anniversary celebrations or TV specials. "None of the members of the first group to hit number one on the label were invited to be part of any of them."

# artist - Georgia Dobbins

## songs
**Please Mr. Postman (1961) - Lyrical Contributor** : Georgia Dobbins played a crucial role in shaping "Please Mr. Postman" from an initial blues idea by William Garrett. She wrote most of the lyrics and recast the song into a less bluesy style, contributing significantly to its eventual chart-topping success. "A friend of Georgia, William Garrett, had started a blues song about a postman, and Georgia worked on his idea, writing most of the lyrics, and recasting it as something less bluesy."

## curiosities
**Discovery by Gladys Horton** : Georgia Dobbins initially caught Gladys Horton's eye through her performances in high school talent contests in Inkster, Michigan. Despite having graduated, Gladys persuaded Georgia to join her group for a talent contest entry, laying the groundwork for The Marvelettes. "One who caught her eye was a young girl who would appear in the high school talent contests, named Georgia Dubbons."
**Forced Departure from The Marvelettes** : Georgia Dobbins was forced to quit The Marvelettes before they signed their record contract because her father, who believed the entertainment industry was sinful, refused to counter-sign the contract as she was under 21. Her mother was too ill to sign, leading Georgia to a deep depression where she gave up singing entirely until 1978. "Her father believed the entertainment industry to be sinful, and wouldn't sign. She was so depressed that she gave up singing altogether, and by her own account didn't sing a note until 1978."

**Refusal for Album Cover Appearance** : When Motown sought to feature her on the cover photograph for Wonder Young's 1970 solo album "The Return of The Marvelettes," Catherine Anderson refused. "She told her label that if she wasn't good enough to sing on the record, she wasn't good enough to appear on the cover either, and so the cover, like the record, only featured Wanda of the original Marvillette."
**Attempt to Regain Group Name and Death** : Catherine Anderson (Jean Caffin) was actively pursuing a lawsuit under the Truth in Music Act to regain ownership of The Marvelettes' name, which Motown had sold off, when she died of a stroke in 2011. This legal action highlighted the struggle of original members to control their legacy. "Jean Caffin were in the process of suing to regain the name under the Truth in Music Act, when she died of a stroke in 2011."
**Exclusion from Motown Anniversaries** : Despite her contributions, Catherine Anderson was not invited to any of Motown's major anniversary celebrations, a consistent oversight for the original members of the label's first number one group. "None of the members of the first group to hit number one on the label were invited to be part of any of them."

**Replacement of Georgia Dobbins** : Wonder Young joined The Marvelettes as the replacement for Georgia Dobbins, who had to leave the group due to her father's refusal to sign her contract. She became an integral part of the lineup from this point forward. "By the time they came back to Motown with the beginnings of a song, Georgia had been replaced by Wonder Young"
**Pregnancy and Touring Replacement** : During her time with The Marvelettes, Wonder Young became pregnant, leading Motown to temporarily replace her on tour with Florence Ballard of The Supremes. This ensured the group could fulfill their contracts requiring five members on the road. "At one point when Wonder had been pregnant, Motown had replaced him on the road with Florence Ballard from the Supremes."
**Ascension to Lead Vocalist** : After a period of declining pop chart success, Smokey Robinson took over the production of The Marvelettes and made the strategic decision to utilize Wonder Young as the primary lead vocalist, replacing Gladys Horton. This shift led to a "second commercial wind" for the group. "Smokey Robinson took over their production and decided to start using Wander as the lead vocalist, rather than Gladys, who had sung lead on their hits up to that point."
**Substance Abuse and Personal Tragedies** : Wonder Young faced severe personal struggles including cocaine and alcohol abuse, which caused her to start acting strangely. Further trauma included her younger sister being shot dead (mistakenly for her older sister) and her own drink being spiked during a European tour. "Anne Wanda was having problems. She'd been doing too much cocaine and drinking too much, and was starting to act strangely."
**Enduring Mental Health Problems** : The series of personal tragedies and abuses left Wonder Young with profound and lasting mental health problems that persisted for 50 years. This significantly impacted her ability to perform regularly for decades. "She was never the same again, and has had mental health problems for the last 50 years."
**Reunion Recording and Later Performance** : In the late 1980s, Ian Levine convinced a still-unwell Wonder Young to participate in a Marvelettes reunion album for Motor City Records alongside Gladys Horton. "He changed that slogan to create, sell, and collect."

**"Please Mr. Postman" Original Conception** : The initial idea for "Please Mr. Postman" originated as a blues song about a postman, conceived by William Garrett. Georgia Dobbins later transformed it, writing most of the lyrics and making it "less bluesy." "A friend of Georgia, William Garrett, had started a blues song about a postman, and Georgia worked on his idea, writing most of the lyrics, and recasting it as something less bluesy."

# label - Motown

## curiosities
**First Big Hit with "Money"** : Motown (under the Tamla family of labels) achieved its first significant success with Barrett Strong's "Money," though this record did not yet embody the distinct "Motown Sound" that would define the label. "When we left at the Tamler Motown family of labels, a couple of months back, they'd finally had their first big hit with Barrett Strong's Money"
**"Motown Sound" and "Sound of Young America"** : "Please Mr. Postman" was the first number one hit from Motown that distinctly featured "the Motown Sound" and simultaneously established the label as "the sound of Young America," solidifying its musical identity. "So today we're going to look at the first number one to come out of Motown, a record that definitely did have the Motown Sound, and which established the label as the sound of Young America."
**Financial Crisis and "Please Mr. Postman"** : Following the million-selling "Shop Around," Motown's subsequent releases performed poorly, leading to a severe financial crisis. Distributors were not paying for records, pushing the label close to bankruptcy; "Please Mr. Postman" was the critical hit that saved Motown by ensuring they got paid for past successes. "And please Mr. Postman was the record that saved Motown. I've talked before about how a hit record could put a small label out of business."
**Evolving Slogan: "Create, Sell, and Collect"** : Barry Gordy's original Motown slogan, "create, make, and sell," was changed to "create, sell, and collect." This revision reflected his realization that simply selling records was insufficient if the label wasn't getting paid by distributors, highlighting the crucial business aspect of the music industry. "He changed that slogan to create, sell, and collect."
**Policy for Hit Follow-Ups** : Motown adopted a strict policy: after an artist had a hit, the same producers and songwriters were assigned to create new material that sounded similar to the original hit. Artists were only permitted to diverge from this formula once their current hit-making strategy ceased to be successful. "This would be the policy from this point on with Motown. If someone had a hit, the same producers and songwriters would be a sign to come up with something that sounded like the hit, and the artist would only go in a different direction once they stopped having hits with their original formula."
**Ownership of Group Names** : Motown, rather than the artists, owned the names of its groups, a practice that allowed the label to sell off names like "The Marvelettes" after groups disbanded. This meant original members had to tour under modified names. "Motein, rather than the women, had owned the group name and had sold it off."
**Exclusion of First #1 Artists from Anniversaries** : Despite The Marvelettes being the first group to score a number one hit for Motown, none of its original members were invited to participate in any of the label's 25th, 40th, 50th, or 60th-anniversary celebrations or TV specials. "None of the members of the first group to hit number one on the label were invited to be part of any of them."

# label - Motor City Records

## curiosities
**Ian Levine's Motown Artist Revival Label** : Motor City Records was a British label owned and operated by DJ Ian Levine. Its mission was to release new recordings by artists who had previously recorded for Motown in the 1960s, successfully attracting over a hundred former Motown artists. "Levine is a British DJ who at the time owned and ran Motor City Records, which put out new recordings by people who had released records on Motown in the 60s."

# label - Chess Records

## curiosities
**Potential Motown Footnote** : It is suggested that Motown might have been reduced to a mere "footnote" in the history of Chess Records if "Please Mr. Postman" had not become a hit, highlighting the precarious early financial situation of the label. "If it hadn't come along, it's possible that Motown would just be a footnote in histories of chess records."
**Distribution for Harvey Fuqua's Brother-in-Law** : Chess Records distributed a small number of records for a Detroit label owned by Harvey Fuqua's brother-in-law, a label that eventually went bankrupt despite producing co-written hits for Jackie Wilson. "Chess also distributed a handful of records from a small Detroit label owned by Harvey Foucault's brother-in-law, who co-wrote several hits for Jackie Wilson before that label went bankrupt."

# concert - American Bandstand

## curiosities
**Juanita's Geographical Slip-Up** : During an appearance on American Bandstand, Juanita (Winnie Cowatt) of The Marvelettes mistakenly stated that Detroit was a suburb of Inkster, instead of the correct reverse. # concert - Motown Review (1962)

## curiosities
**All-Star Lineup** : The 1962 Motown Review was The Marvelettes' first major national tour and featured an incredibly impressive roster of Motown acts, including The Miracles, The Temptations, Stevie Wonder, Marvin Gaye, Martha and the Vandellas, and The Supremes, showcasing the depth of Motown's talent. "A tour with a lineup that seems preposterously good these days. All of Motown's major acts and several acts that weren't yet major but soon would be were on the same bill."
**Experience with Segregation in the South** : Having grown up in Michigan, The Marvelettes and other Motown artists on the 1962 Motown Review tour were unprepared for the stark realities of segregation in the South, encountering restrictions like being unable to use public toilets or eat in white-only restaurants. "The girls had grown up in Michigan, and while they had an intellectual understanding that the South was different, they were unprepared for the realities of segregation, of not being able to use public toilets or eating the same restaurants that white people did."
**Bus Targeted by Gunfire** : In several Southern locations, the Motown Review tour bus, filled with Black artists, was mistaken for a Freedom Riders' bus, leading to people taking actual shots at it. This highlights the dangerous atmosphere of the Civil Rights era. "In several places in the South, the site of a lot of black people on a bus, for the freedom riders to mind, and people actually took part shots at the bus."
**Toll on Georgiana Tillman's Health** : The intense and challenging conditions of two years of touring with the Motown Review took an immense toll on Georgiana Tillman's health, exacerbating her pre-existing sickle cell anemia and contributing to her later diagnosis of lupus. "A couple of years living like that took an immense toll on George Anna's health, and she started suffering from unexplained fatigue."

# instrument - Drums

## curiosities
**Two Drummers on "Please Mr. Postman"** : The recording of "Please Mr. Postman" uniquely featured two drummers: Benny Benjamin, Motown's usual session drummer, and a young Marvin Gaye, who was then doing odd jobs around the studio. "the backing musicians included the percussion player, Eddie Bungo Brown, and two drummers. The normal session drummer are most of the Motown recordings, Benny Benjamin, and a young man who had been a member of the last lineup of the Moon Glows before Harvey Fouqua had moved over to working for the Gordy family labels, and who was now doing whatever he could around the studio, named Marvin Gaye."

# curiosity

**Motown Sound Definition** : The early hit "Money" by Barrett Strong did not embody what would later be known as the Motown Sound, sounding more like a Ray Charles record. "Please Mr. Postman" was the first number one from Motown that definitely had this distinct sound, establishing the label as "the sound of Young America." "But while money is a great record, it's not a record with what would later become known as the Motown Sound. It sounds far more like a Rage Hull's record than the records that would later make Motown's name."
**High School Talent Contest Prize** : A high school talent contest in Inkster, Michigan, offered an audition at Motown as its first prize, attracting young musicians like Gladys Horton and her group. Motown, though still small, was gaining recognition after the success of "Shop Around." "This one had, as its first praise, the chance of an audition at Motown."
**Financial Risks of Early Hits for Small Labels** : Small labels with a big hit faced significant financial risk because they had to pay for records to be pressed and distributed, but distributors would often delay payment for months or not pay at all. A second big hit was crucial leverage to compel distributors to pay for the first, threatening to withhold future releases. "The only leverage a small label with a big hit had was a second big hit. If they had another record the distributors wanted from them, then they could tell the distributors they wouldn't get it until they paid up."
**The Twist Craze Influence** : The Marvelettes' second single, "Twistin' Postman," was explicitly designed to capitalize not only on the success of their first hit but also on the widespread popularity of the "twist craze" at the time. "In this case, the Marvelettes' second single was designed not only to capitalize on their original hits, but on the popularity of the twist craze, and so they released Twist in Postman."
**Detroit vs. Inkster Social Dynamics** : The Marvelettes, hailing from Inkster, Michigan, felt looked down upon by people from Detroit, who perceived them as "hicks from the sticks." This self-consciousness contributed to the departure of member Juanita (Winnie Cowatt) after she made a geographical error on "American Bandstand" and faced mockery. "It felt all along that they were looked down on a bit by the people from Detroit, who thought of them as hicks from the sticks because they came from Inkster."
**Group Dynamics: From Friends to Acquaintances** : Catherine Anderson observed that the Marvelettes, initially five friends, evolved into more of an acquaintance/co-worker relationship as their lineup shrank to a trio. Each member had assigned responsibilities, but the personal bond diminished over time. "But as Catherine would later put it, when there had been five of them they'd been friends. Now there were somewhere between acquaintances and co-workers."
**The Truth in Music Act** : Catherine Anderson (Jean Caffin) was in the process of suing to regain ownership of The Marvelettes' name under the Truth in Music Act when she died in 2011. This act presumably addresses issues of legacy acts touring without original members. "Jean Caffin were in the process of suing to regain the name under the Truth in Music Act, when she died of a stroke in 2011."
**Freedom Riders and Tour Bus Attacks** : During the 1962 Motown Review, the tour bus, filled with Black artists from the North, was mistakenly associated with Freedom Riders (activists protesting segregation by sitting in "whites only" seats). This led to people taking actual shots at the bus in several Southern locations. "In several places in the South, the site of a lot of black people on a bus, for the freedom riders to mind, and people actually took part shots at the bus."
**Lupus as a Death Sentence** : Georgiana Tillman was diagnosed with lupus, an autoimmune disease that significantly worsened due to touring. At the time, lupus was "often a death sentence," highlighting the severe and often fatal medical challenges faced by individuals diagnosed with it then, contrasted with its current treatability. "Eventually it was realised that she had lupus, an autoimmune disease which is now largely treatable if not curable, but at the time was often a death sentence."
