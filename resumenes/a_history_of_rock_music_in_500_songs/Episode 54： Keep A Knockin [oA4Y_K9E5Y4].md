## songs
**Long Tall Sally (1956)** : This song marked Little Richard's career peak and was among his early hits. During his 1962 British comeback tour, he dramatically launched into "Long Tall Sally" after a minute of darkness, signaling his return to rock and roll. "he had just had a hit with Long Tull Sally and was at the peak of his career."
**Rip It Up (1956) / Ready Teddy (1956)** : These were Little Richard's immediate follow-up to "Long Tall Sally," released as a double-sided hit with both songs credited to John Mariscalco and Bumps Blackwell. Both went to number one on the R&B charts, though they were closer to typical songs of the era with lyrics about partying, unlike his truly unique earlier singles. "Rip it up, backed with Ready Teddy. These both went to number one on the R&B charts..."
**The Girl Can't Help It (1956)** : Little Richard was chosen over Fats Domino to record the theme tune for this movie, a testament to his growing popularity and success. "he had become big enough that he was chosen over Fats Domino to record the theme tune to the Girl Can't Help It..."
**She's Got It (1956)** : This song is mentioned as one of Little Richard's hits released during his prolific period in 1956-57. "We've discussed the girl can't help it and she's got it in the episode on 20 Flight Rock..."
**Jenny Jenny (1957)** : Released during his hit-making streak in 1956 and 1957, this song became one of Little Richard's classics, often appearing as an A or B-side that achieved lasting popularity. "there was also Jenny Jenny, send me some love in and possibly the greatest of them all, Lucille."
**Send Me Some Love In (1957)** : This song was one of many hits Little Richard released during 1956 and 1957, a period where he frequently put out singles with both sides becoming classics. "there was also Jenny Jenny, send me some love in and possibly the greatest of them all, Lucille."
**Lucille (1957)** : Released during Little Richard's successful run in 1956-57, this song is noted as "possibly the greatest of them all" among his hits from that period. "possibly the greatest of them all, Lucille."
**Keep a-Knockin' (1957)** : Recorded in early 1957 with his backing band, The Upsetters, this song has deep roots, tracing back to "A Bunch of Blues" (1915) and "My Book It's Got A Whole in It," and was heavily influenced by Louis Jordan's version, even including a verse about drinking gin in some takes. The final recording was a "Frankenstein's monster," crafted from a mere 57 seconds of usable material by copy-pasting segments to double its length, resulting in repeated verses and saxophone solos. "So by the time Little Richard came to record Keeper Knockin, in very early 1957, he had a host of different versions he could draw on for inspiration, but what we ended up with is something that's uniquely Little Richard, something that was all together, wilder."
**Oh My Soul (1957)** : This song, described as one of Little Richard's "very best records," was recorded during the same productive session in early 1957 that produced "Keep a-Knockin'." "At the same session, he also recorded another of his very best records. Oh my soul."
**Good Golly Miss Molly (1958)** : Released by Specialty Records from a backlog of recordings after Little Richard's conversion and temporary hiatus from rock and roll, this song became a significant hit. It was considered "as good as anything he'd done in his 50s heyday" and proved that even unreleased material could achieve great success. "Some of those, like Goodgolly Miss Molly, were as good as anything here that were recorded and rightly became big hits."
**Lord, Lord, King of the World (circa 1958-1962)** : After settling lawsuits with Specialty Records and fully committing to his religious conversion, Little Richard exclusively recorded gospel songs, including this one. This marked a period of his career where he focused solely on religious music. "He started recording again, but only gospel songs. Lord, Lord, King of the world, Hall of tide, Lord, Lord, Lord, Lord, Heaven and Land, Lord, Christ, Lord, Lord, Lord, Lord, Lord, Lord, Lord, Lord!"
**I'm In Love Again (1962)** : Before his pivotal 1962 UK tour, Little Richard was "tempted back into the studio" to record this Fats Domino song in his old style with his original group, The Upsetters. The single, featuring Richard's recognizable vocal, was released in December 1962 under the name "the world famous Upsetters." "When he joined his old group to record fat stomonos I'm in love again for a single that didn't get released until December 1962."
**Well All Right (circa 1963)** : This song, written by Sam Cooke, was the only rock and roll music Little Richard recorded immediately after returning to the US from Hamburg in 1962, representing a cautious step back into secular music. "At first he still wouldn't record any rock and roll music other than one song that Sam Cook wrote for him, well all right..."
**Parmalaja by Maloo (circa 1963)** : Intended as Little Richard's comeback single after his return to rock and roll, this song was recorded with backing from Don and Duey, who had performed with him in the UK. Despite its quality, the single "didn't do anything in the charts," signaling a decline in his recording career. "He went back into the studio, backed by a group including Don and Dui who had been performing with him in the UK and recorded what was meant to be his comeback single Parmalaja by Maloo Unfortunately, great as it was, that single didn't do anything in the charts, and Richard spent the rest of the 60s making record after record that failed to chart."
**Tutti Frutti (1955)** : During his wild 1962 UK tour performances, Little Richard famously faked his own death on stage, collapsing off the piano and lying still, only to suddenly jump up and launch into "Tutti Frutti," creating a dramatic effect and engaging the audience. "Just to create attention in the audience for when he suddenly jumped up and started singing Tuti Fruity."

## curiosities
**Inspiration for James Brown** : Little Richard's musical style and performance were a direct inspiration for James Brown, influencing the direction of soul and funk music. "he was the inspiration for James Brown."
**Conflict and Identity Struggles** : Little Richard was deeply conflicted throughout his life, grappling with his attraction to both men and women, his love for rock and roll versus his profound religious beliefs, his enjoyment of indulgent food, drink, and drugs, and an uncertainty about his own gender identity. "Richard had always been deeply conflicted about, well, about everything really."
**Seventh-day Adventist Faith** : Little Richard was a deeply religious believer in the Seventh-day Adventist Church, which held strict views condemning same-sex attraction, trans identities, and secular music as "the work of the devil," advocating for a vegetarian and kosher diet, and prohibiting all drugs, including caffeine. "He was also deeply, deeply religious, and a believer in the seventh day Adventist Church, which believed that same sex attraction, trans identities, and secular music would the work of the devil and that one should keep a vegetarian and kosher diet and avoid old drugs even caffeine."
**Religious Conversion Experience (October 1957)** : While on a tour of Australia in October 1957, Little Richard experienced a profound religious awakening after seeing a plane engine glowing red, convincing him of angels, and then witnessing Russia's Sputnik satellite pass low over Australia like a "fireball," which he interpreted as a divine sign to abandon his sinful life for religion. To prove his sincerity, he threw all his rings into the ocean and abruptly canceled the remaining ten days of the tour to return to the US. "He decided this was a sign, and he was being told by God he needed to give up his life of sin and devote himself to religion."
**Contractual Dispute with Specialty Records** : According to John Mariscalco, Little Richard's conversion in 1957 might have been partially motivated by a desire to break his contract with Specialty Records, which only paid him half a cent per record and allowed termination only upon his death or an "act of God"; Richard allegedly aimed to claim his religious awakening as an "act of God" to escape the unfavorable terms. "Richard's contract with specialty was only paying him half a cent per record sold, which he considered far too low, and the wording of the contract only let him end it on either his own death or an act of God."
**Post-Conversion Lifestyle Changes** : After his conversion, Little Richard drastically altered his lifestyle: he became an evangelist, studied for the ministry, married a woman (though they later divorced due to sexual incompatibility), ceased drinking alcohol, taking drugs, and even consuming coffee, and adopted a strict vegetarian diet cooked solely in vegetable oil. "He changed his whole lifestyle. He married a woman, although they were later divorced as, among other things, they weren't sexually compatible."
**Seminary Expulsion and Arrest** : His internal conflict between sexuality and religion continued to torment him, leading to him dropping out of the seminary after propositioning another male student and later being arrested in a public toilet. "He dropped out of the seminary after propositioning another male student, and he was arrested in a public toilet."
**Comeback Spurred by Sam Cooke (1962)** : During his October 1962 UK tour, which he intended to be gospel-focused, Little Richard was confronted by an audience expecting rock and roll. His competitive nature was ignited by Sam Cooke's "incendiary performance" of "Twistin' the Night Away," serving as the "final straw" that propelled Richard back to performing rock and roll. "Cook was at the height of his success at this point and he was an astonishing live performer and so when he went out on stage and closed the first half including an insendiary performance of twist in the night away that left the audience applauding through the intermission Richard knew he had to up his game."
**Dramatic Stage Persona and Antics** : Little Richard's 1962 British tour was marked by his increasingly wild and frantic stage performances, reflecting five years of pent-up rock and roll energy. He frequently threw off clothes into the audience, dressed in just a bathrobe on his knees, jumped on the piano, and famously faked his own death mid-song before springing up to sing "Tutti Frutti," all designed to captivate and energize the audience. "Many shows he'd pull off most of his clothes and throw them into the audience and he got dressed in just a bathrobe on his knees."
**Mentoring The Beatles in Hamburg (1962)** : After his successful UK tour, Little Richard spent two months in Hamburg hanging out and performing in clubs with The Beatles, a local Mersey's side band. During this time, he personally taught their bass player, Paul McCartney, how to make his distinctive "woo" sounds when singing. "He then went to Hamburg with that group and spent two months hanging out with them and performing it in the same kinds of clubs and teaching their bass player how he made his wool sounds when singing."
**Recording Career Decline in the 1960s** : His five-year absence from rock and roll music severely damaged Little Richard's career as a recording artist, leading to a string of chart failures throughout the rest of the 1960s, despite some recordings being as good as his 1950s heyday material. "his 5 years away from rock and roll music had killed his career as a recording artist."
**Enduring Live Performer (1960s-2010s)** : While his recording career faltered, Little Richard remained a formidable live performer, touring for 50 years and playing his classic hits from 1955-1957. These decades of performing were interspersed with periodic bouts of remorse where he would temporarily abandon rock and roll for evangelism, only to be drawn back by "the lure of material success and audience response." "They hadn't though, killed him as a live performer, and he would spend the next 50 years touring, playing the hits he had recorded, during that classic period from 1955 through 1957..."
**Final Performance in Golden Wheelchair (2013)** : Little Richard retired from live performing a few years ago due to disabilities exacerbated by decades of strenuous stage shows. His last public performance was in 2013 in Las Vegas, where, ever the showman, he appeared in a wheelchair that was custom-made to look like a "golden throne." "His last public performance was in 2013, in Las Vegas, and he was in a wheelchair, but, because he's little Richard, the wheelchair was made to look like a golden throne."

# artist - Buddy Bolden

## instruments

## curiosities
**Pioneering Jazz Trumpeter** : Buddy Bolden is remembered as a great jazz trumpeter, with claims that he originally played the jazz standard "My Book It's Got A Whole in It" around the turn of the 20th century, potentially dating the melody to 1906. However, no recordings of him performing the song survive. "There are claims that it was originally played by the great jazz trumpeter Buddy Bolden around the turn of the 20th century."
**Hospitalized with Schizophrenia** : From 1907 onwards, Buddy Bolden was admitted to a psychiatric hospital due to schizophrenia, which helps establish the latest possible date (1906) for his purported playing of "My Book It's Got A Whole in It" before his institutionalization. "As from 1907 on, Bolden was in a psychiatric hospital with schizophrenia, but the 1915 date for A Bunch of Blues is the earliest definite date we have for the melody."

"It usually involved simple songs with a verse, Corvus structure, and with lyrics that were an extended comedy metaphor, usually some form of innuendo about sex, with titles like Meatballs and Banana in your Fruit Basket."

# curiosity

**My Book It's Got A Whole in It - Jazz Standard Origin** : This "old jazz standard" is claimed to have been originally performed by the acclaimed jazz trumpeter Buddy Bolden around the turn of the 20th century, suggesting its melody could date back to around 1906 at the latest. It later became a popular song among country singers and was recorded by a diverse array of artists including Hank Williams, Louis Armstrong, Jimmy Page, Robert Plant, Willie Nelson, and Wynton Marsalis. "My book It's Got A Whole in It, which is an old jazz standard."
**Keep a-Knockin' Melody Evolution** : The melody for "Keep a-Knockin'" boasts a deep and intricate history, potentially stemming from the old jazz standard "My Book It's Got A Whole in It" (possibly played by Buddy Bolden around 1906) and definitely from J. Paul Weyer and Alph Kelly's "A Bunch of Blues" (1915), which W.C. Handy's band recorded in 1917. This melody then evolved through various blues and country interpretations, including Tampa Red's "You Can't Come In" (1928) and Louis Jordan's version, eventually influencing Little Richard's iconic recording. "Keepin' knocking had a long, long history."
**Keep a-Knockin' Recording Production Technique (1957)** : Little Richard's 1957 recording of "Keep a-Knockin'" initially yielded only about 57 seconds of usable material. To achieve a suitable length, the engineers ingeniously copied and pasted the entire section from the beginning of Richard's vocal to the end of the saxophone solo, effectively doubling the song's duration and creating repeated verses and sax solos. "To get it to a usable length, they copied and pasted the whole song, from the start of Richard's vocal through to the end of the saxophone solo, and almost doubled the length of the song."
**Pioneering Black Record Producers** : Mayo Williams, who produced Bert Mayes' 1928 recording of "Keep a-Knockin'," is highlighted as one of the first black record producers, marking a significant early contribution to the music industry. "Mayes and his producer Mayo Williams, one of the first black record producers..."
**Sputnik's Impact on Little Richard's Conversion (1957)** : Russia's launch of its first satellite, Sputnik, in October 1957, played a direct role in Little Richard's religious conversion. While on tour in Australia, he claimed to see Sputnik pass low overhead "like a fireball in the sky" while performing, reinforcing his belief that it was a divine sign from God. "He became even more worried a couple of days later when Russia launched their first satellite, Sputnik, and it passed low over Australia."
