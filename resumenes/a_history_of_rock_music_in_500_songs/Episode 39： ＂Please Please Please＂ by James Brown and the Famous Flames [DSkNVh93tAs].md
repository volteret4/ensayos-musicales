## albums
**So Long (1961) - Rerecording of Early Talent Show Song** : James Brown rerecorded "So Long" in 1961, a song he had performed and won a talent show with in 1944. This rerecording might give an idea of what Brown sounded like during the period when he was trying to emulate Little Richard, as his early recordings for tiny labels were mostly cover versions of R&B hits. "which he later re-recorded in 1961, and that version might give you some idea of what Brown sounded like at the period when he was trying to be Little Richard."

## songs
**So Long (1944)** : This song was performed by an 11-year-old James Brown in his first talent show in 1944, which he won. At the time, it was best known in a version by The Charioteers, and it would later become Ruth Brown's first big hit. "His first one was in 1944, when he was 11, and he performed so long."
**Caldonia (Later Recording)** : James Brown sang "Caldonia" by Louis Jordan when performing with small groups around town in his early career, having decided he wanted to be like Jordan. Years later, he recorded it as a tribute to his idol. "As soon as he started performing with small groups around town, he started singing Jordan's songs, especially Caldonia, which years later he would record as a tribute to his idol."
**Please Please Please (1956)** : This song, written by James Brown and Johnny Terry, was based on something Little Richard had scribbled on a napkin, which Brown saw potential in as a title. It fit a specific genre of R&B ballad, influenced by songs like Flow Example Richards' "Directly from My Heart to You," Fay Adams' "Shake a Hand," and The Orioles' version of Big Joe Williams' "Baby Please Don't Go," from which Brown borrowed the backing vocal arrangement. The emotional intensity of Brown's singing on this track was particularly effective, resonating with a type of woman who felt sympathy for him. The song reached number six on the R&B chart and reportedly sold between one and three million copies, though record companies often inflated sales figures at the time. "Bass was absolutely convinced that please, please, please would be a hit and championed the flames in the face of opposition from his boss, Sid Nathan."
**Johnny on Chon (Year Unspecified)** : This record was released by James Brown and his new group of Flames during a three-year period after the success of "Please Please Please" when all their records flopped. It attempted to capitalize on the trend of name-checking "Annie" from Hank Ballard and the Midnighters' "Work With Me Annie," suggesting a belief among R&B singers that this would lead to a hit. Despite lyrics about having a rock and roll party, the single failed commercially. "Records like Johnny on Chon tried to jump on various bandwagens."
**Try Me (1958)** : Inspired by Jerry Butler's "For Your Precious Love," this slow-burn ballad was initially rejected by Sid Nathan at Federal Records, who threatened to drop Brown. Convinced it would be a hit, Brown recorded a demo with his own money and took it directly to radio stations, where it quickly became the most requested song. After Federal received orders for 25,000 copies, Nathan reluctantly agreed to properly record the song in a studio at the label's expense. "Try Me" became an even bigger hit than "Please Please Please," reaching number one on the R&B charts and number 48 on the pop charts. "Primee became an even bigger hit than please please please had and went to number one and the R&B charts and number 48 on the pop charts."

## curiosities
**Hard Childhood and Abandonment** : James Brown experienced a very difficult childhood; his mother left his father when James was four, reportedly after his father pulled a gun on her, leaving James feeling abandoned. A few years later, his father, unable to care for him, left James with a relative he called an aunt, who ran a brothel, which was not an ideal environment for raising a child. He later recalled not having shop-bought underwear until his teens. "His mother left his father when James was four."
**Early Talent Show Victory** : At the age of 11, James Brown won his first talent show in 1944 by performing "So Long," a song then best known by The Charioteers and later a hit for Ruth Brown. This early success highlighted his ambition and musical inclination from a young age. "His first one was in 1944, when he was 11, and he performed so long."
**Musical Influences - Jazz, Gospel, Louis Jordan** : Young James Brown loved music, particularly jazz and gospel, and eagerly learned from various sources. He disliked the blues his father played, but still learned from blues legend Tampa Red. His music always retained a gospel flavor. He especially loved Count Basie and tried to teach himself "One O'Clock Jump" on the piano. Above all, he idolized Louis Jordan, listening to his records and watching him in film shorts, and aimed to emulate Jordan, singing his songs like "Caldonia" in his early performances. "He loved music, especially jazz and gospel, and he was eager to learn anything he could about it."
**Juvenile Detention and Meeting Bobby Bird** : At age 16, James Brown was sent to a juvenile detention center for 8 to 16 years after being caught stealing a car, a consequence of his earlier thefts which began after stealing clothes. This inadvertently led to a significant turning point in his life: meeting Bobby Bird during a baseball game, who was impressed by Brown's singing talent and reputation as "Music Box" within the center, ultimately helping him secure an early release after three years. "At the age of 16, Brown was sent to a juvenile detention centre, on a sentence of 8 to 16 years, and this inadvertently led to the biggest piece of look in his life, when he met the man who would be his mentor and principal creative partner for the next 20 years."
**Parole Restrictions** : Following his early release from juvenile detention, James Brown spent the next 10 years, throughout his early musical career, on parole. This meant he was constantly in danger of being recalled to prison at any time, adding significant pressure and risk to his life, especially in 1940s Georgia where racial abuse by white guards was prevalent. "And for the next 10 years, throughout his early musical career, Brown would be on parole, and in danger of being recalled to prison at any time."
**Little Richard Imitation and "Ghosting" Gigs** : Early in his career, James Brown was heavily influenced by Little Richard, essentially acting as a tribute act due to their shared Georgia roots, Louis Jordan influence, and church music inspiration. At one point, Brown and The Flames were hired by Little Richard's agent, Clint Brantley, to "ghost" gigs that Richard had been booked for but could no longer play due to his rising popularity. Brown would be introduced as "Little Richard," performing a mix of Richard's material, his own originals, and The Flames' R&B songs. "Every night, Brown would go on stage to the introduction, please welcome the hardest working man in show business today, Little Richard, and when he finished ghosting for Little Richard, he liked the introduction enough that he would keep it for himself, changing it only to his own name, rather than Richard's."
**"Hardest Working Man in Show Business" Introduction** : James Brown adopted the iconic introduction "please welcome the hardest working man in show business today" for himself after using it while "ghosting" gigs for Little Richard. He kept the phrase, simply replacing Little Richard's name with his own, and made it a staple of his performances, embodying the intense work ethic he imposed on himself and his band. "Every night, Brown would go on stage to the introduction, please welcome the hardest working man in show business today, Little Richard, and when he finished ghosting for Little Richard, he liked the introduction enough that he would keep it for himself, changing it only to his own name, rather than Richard's."
**Ralph Bass's Faith in "Please Please Please"** : Ralph Bass, the A&R man at Federal Records, was uniquely convinced that "Please Please Please" would be a hit, championing The Famous Flames despite strong opposition from his boss, Sid Nathan, who dismissed the song as merely Brown screaming one word repeatedly. Brown's autobiography even claims Bass was temporarily fired for releasing the record against Nathan's will, only to be rehired after its success, illustrating the emotional truth of Bass's solitary belief in the band. "Bass was absolutely convinced that please, please, please would be a hit and championed the flames in the face of opposition from his boss, Sid Nathan."
**Elaborate Live Routine for "Please Please Please"** : "Please Please Please" became a staple of The Famous Flames' live repertoire, featuring an elaborate routine. James Brown would drop to his knees, feigning sobbing, and other band members, inspired by the caped wrestler Gorgeous George, would drape him in a cape and try to lead him off stage. Brown would dramatically resist, pulling away to continue singing, while his bandmates tried to remove him, sometimes escalating to him climbing into the rafters and dropping into their arms. "Brown would drop to his knees, sobbing, and the other band members would drape him in a cape, something that was inspired by a caped wrestler, gorgeous George, and try to lead him off stage, concerned for him."
**Band Quits Over Credit and Pay** : After the success of "Please Please Please," which reached number six on the R&B chart, the record was credited to "James Brown and the Famous Flames," rather than just "The Famous Flames" as a group. When this billing extended to stage shows, the rest of the band, feeling slighted, collectively quit. Bobby Bird and Johnny Terry eventually rejoined, but Brown had to rebuild the group with new members. "But it was credited to James Brown and the famous flames, not just to the famous flames as a group. When they started to be built that way on stage shows too, the rest of the band decided that enough was enough, and quit on mass."
**Absolute Discipline and Fines for Band Members** : As the undisputed leader after his first band dissolved, James Brown implemented a strict policy of absolute discipline, earning him the title "the hardest working man in show business." He fined band members on stage for any mistakes, such as unpolished shoes, missed dance steps, or wrong notes. Brown would point at a musician and flash five fingers to the rhythm, each flash representing a $5 fine, which audiences mistook for part of the routine but the musicians understood as a monetary penalty. "Brown took on the title, the hardest working man in show business, but his band members had to work equally hard if not harder."
**Overcoming Physical Appearance Stereotypes** : James Brown faced challenges in his career due to his physical appearance, which did not align with the prevailing preferences for black male entertainers in the 1940s and 50s. While he defended his unique vocal style ("I grunt in tune"), his very dark skin, "extremely African features," and short, stocky build contrasted with the admired image of light-skinned, non-stereotypical, tall, and thin black men. This led him and his group to believe they "just had to work harder than everyone else" to succeed. "Brown was very dark, had extremely African features and was short and stocky."
**Sid Nathan's Initial Rejection of "Try Me"** : Sid Nathan, James Brown's boss at Federal Records, initially refused to release "Try Me," dismissing it and threatening to drop Brown from the label. Brown, convinced of the song's potential, self-financed a demo and successfully promoted it to radio stations, making it highly requested. Only after Federal received orders for 25,000 copies did Nathan relent, offering Brown "one more chance" and eventually agreeing to finance a proper studio recording. "Brown told the story, he wrote his new song and took it to Sid Nathan at Federal, who said that he wasn't going to waste his money putting out anything like that, and that in fact he was dropping Brown from the label."
**First Apollo Theatre Residency** : James Brown and his new lineup of The Famous Flames performed their first residency at the Apollo Theatre, a highly prestigious and demanding venue on the Chitlin Circuit. This run, which featured Little Richard's old group The Upsetters and was headlined by blues star Little Willie John, saw Brown's group impress audiences more than any other act, quickly earning them co-headline status, much to Little Willie John's annoyance. This residency marked the beginning of the Apollo becoming synonymous with Brown. "That was the first time James Brown ever played at the Apollo a venue which in later years would become synonymous with him"
**Reunion with Mother After 20 Years** : After finishing his Apollo residency, James Brown had a poignant reunion with his mother, whom he had not seen since he was four years old. She appeared at his room in the Hotel Theresa in Washington, surprising him completely. Brown, seeing she had lost all her teeth, immediately offered to pay to fix her mouth. This meeting marked their first embrace and kiss in over two decades, signifying an emotional reconciliation after a lifetime of feeling abandoned. "We hugged and then I kissed my mother for the first time in more than 20 years."

## curiosities
**Early Name Changes** : Bobby Bird's gospel group, originally called The Gospel Starlighters, transitioned into an R&B group named The Avons. They soon renamed themselves again to The Flames, and later adopted the name The Famous Flames, which they retained. This series of name changes, along with the existence of The Hollywood Flames featuring a different Bobby Bird, caused considerable confusion. "They soon renamed themselves again to The Flames, and later to The Famous Flames, the name they would stick with from then on, and a name which would cause a lot of confusion, as we've already talked about The Hollywood Flames, who featured a different Bobby Bird."
**Patterning Style on Other Groups** : The Famous Flames increasingly modeled their sound and performance on two specific groups: Billy Ward and the Dominoes, whose records with Clyde McPhatter as lead singer were instrumental in establishing vocal group R&B, and Hank Ballard and the Midnighters, known for the then-unusual blatant sexuality in their work with Annie. They prioritized energy and raw emotional expression over the smooth, sophisticated styles of groups like The Platters or The Penguins. "The famous flames were patterning themselves more and more on two groups."
**Initial Quit After "Please Please Please" Success** : Following the success of "Please Please Please," which was credited to "James Brown and the Famous Flames" rather than solely to the group, the band members felt slighted. This led to the entire group, except for James Brown himself, quitting en masse, unhappy with the billing and perceived lack of credit. Bobby Bird and Johnny Terry eventually rejoined, but the other original members never returned, forcing Brown to assemble a new lineup. "When they started to be built that way on stage shows too, the rest of the band decided that enough was enough, and quit on mass."
**Strict Discipline and Fines Imposed by James Brown** : James Brown enforced absolute discipline on his band members, who were expected to work as hard, if not harder, than him, the "hardest working man in show business." He would issue fines on stage for any imperfections—unpolished shoes, missed dance steps, or wrong notes—by pointing at a musician and flashing five fingers, with each flash signifying a $5 fine. Audiences perceived this as part of the dance routine, but the musicians understood the financial penalty. "Any band member whose shoes weren't or who missed a dance step or hit a wrong note on stage would be found."
**Second Group Dissolution Before Apollo Residency** : Just before a significant residency at the Apollo Theatre, the most prestigious and demanding venue on the Chitlin Circuit, James Brown's group experienced another breakup. Although Johnny Terry remained and Bobby Bird rejoined after a few years' absence, Brown was worried about how his newly reformed group would perform at such a high-stakes engagement. "But once again, Brown lost his group and this time just before a big residency at the Apollo, the most prestigious and also the most demanding venue on the Chipplin circuit."

# concert - Apollo Theatre

## curiosities
**Demanding Chitlin Circuit Venue** : The Apollo Theatre in Harlem was a highly prestigious yet extremely demanding venue on the Chitlin Circuit, particularly known for its "Amateurs Night." Audiences there were notoriously critical, coming "prepared with baskets full of rotten fruit and eggs to throw at the stage," but would also show fierce loyalty to acts that exceeded expectations. "The worst of these audiences was at the Amitainite at the Apollo Theatre in Harlem."
**James Brown's First Residency** : James Brown's first residency at the Apollo Theatre marked a significant turning point in his career. Despite initial worries about his new group and headliners like Little Willie John, Brown's performance was so compelling that his group was promoted to co-headline status, laying the groundwork for the venue to become synonymous with him in later years. "That was the first time James Brown ever played at the Apollo a venue which in later years would become synonymous with him"

# concert - Chitlin Circuit

## curiosities
**Network for Black Artists** : The Chitlin Circuit was an informal network of clubs and theatres spanning the USA, catering almost exclusively to black audiences. With few exceptions, nearly all black acts, even those not primarily aiming for white audiences like Harry Belafonte or Nat King Cole, performed on this circuit. "The Chitlin circuit was an informal network of clubs and theatres that stretched across the USA, catering almost exclusively to black audiences."
**Demanding Audiences** : Audiences on the Chitlin Circuit were known for being hard to impress, expecting excellent value for their entertainment dollar and showing swift disapproval for anything less than the best. While they were quick to criticize, they were also fiercely loyal to artists who consistently delivered outstanding performances, a loyalty James Brown aimed to inspire by ensuring his group worked harder than any other. "As with poor audiences everywhere, the audiences wanted value for their entertainment dollar and were not prepared to tolerate anything less than the best."

# curiosity

**Archetypal Musical Genius Type** : There is a recurring type of musician, almost always male, regarded as a musical genius who is singularly focused on his music and money. Such individuals typically have employees rather than friends, treating them appallingly while taking all the credit for their staggering work. Despite this, the work produced sounds uniquely like the genius, and employees' subsequent independent efforts rarely match the original quality, indicating the 1% difference the genius adds. These figures are grudgingly respected by their employees and loved by their audience, embodying an "outsized portion of the most influential music ever made." "And James Brown is almost the archetypal example of this kind of musician."
**Colorism in the Black Community (1940s Georgia)** : In 1940s Georgia, and generally during that era, there was a societal preference within the black community for black men who were light-skinned, had features that did not fit traditional stereotypes of black people, and were tall and thin. This cultural preference meant that artists like James Brown, who was very dark, had "extremely African features," and was short and stocky, had to "work harder than everyone else" to achieve success. "While I am absolutely not the person to talk about colorism in the black community, there is a general consensus that in that time and place, black people were more likely to admire a black man if he was light-skinned, had features that didn't fit the stereotype of black people and was tall and thin."
