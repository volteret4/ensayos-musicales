## songs
**Every Hour (1951)** : This was Richard's first single for RCA Records, a very close imitation of Billy Wright's style. Richard was solely a singer at this point, not yet playing an instrument. "It was so close to right style, in fact, that right soon recorded his own knock-off of Richard's song, Every evening."
**Wonderin' (early 1955)** : An early version of this song was one of two tracks on the demo tape Richard sent to Specialty Records. It was deemed "okay" during his first Specialty recording session. "Wondering which we heard before, came out okay."
**I'm Just a Lonely, Lonely Guy (1955)** : This song was written by Dorothy LaBostrie and recorded by Little Richard during his initial, uninspired session for Specialty Records. It was considered "okay" and one of the usable materials from the session. "They also caught a decent version of Directly From My Heart To You, the song which Richard had previously recorded with Johnny Otis."
**Directly From My Heart To You (1953/1955)** : A version of this song was recorded during Richard's last session for Peacock Records, handled by Johnny Otis, but it remained unreleased at the time. He later recorded a decent version of it during his first Specialty Records session. "They also caught a decent version of Directly From My Heart To You, the song which Richard had previously recorded with Johnny Otis."
**Little Richard's Rhapsody (1953)** : This song was recorded during Richard's final session for Peacock Records, when Don Roby gave him to Johnny Otis to handle. The four songs from this session, including this one, went unreleased at the time. "Nobody was very happy with the recordings, and Richard was dropped by Peacock."
**Tutti Frutti (1955)** : This song began as a sexually explicit R&B joke song with unbroadcastable lyrics ("A Wopper Bopper loom up a good god damn, Judy Fruity good booty, if it don't fit don't force it, you can grease it make it easy"). Bumps Blackwell realized its hit potential and had Dorothy LaBostrie rewrite the lyrics to be broadcastable and about a girl named Sue. Richard played piano on the track, which was cut in three takes, and it became a massive hit, reaching number 21 on the pop charts. "Tutti frutti was indeed a massive hit."
**Long Tall Sally (1956)** : For his second hit, Richard and Bumps Blackwell aimed for a louder, faster, and more raucous sound to make it difficult for cover artists like Pat Boone. The basis came from a few lines of lyric written by teenage Nona Johnson. Blackwell enriched the song with lyrics implying sex of a non-heteronormative variety, possibly with a sex worker, but kept it on the broadcastable side. "The basis for Lung Tull Sally came from a scrap of lyric written by a teenage girl."

## curiosities
**True Original in Rock and Roll** : Little Richard is considered a true original in rock and roll, with nobody before him sounding like he did and nobody later quite capturing his sound, despite attempts by artists like Captain Beefheart and Paul McCartney. "Nobody before him sounded like he did, and while many later tried, everyone from Captain Befart to Paul McCartney, nobody ever quite sounded like him later."
**Complex Sexuality and Gender Identity** : Little Richard's sexuality and gender identity are described as complex, as he defined himself as gay at various times but also had relationships with women. His autobiography contains passages where he wished he had been born a woman, suggesting that had he been born later, he might have identified as a bisexual trans woman. "He has at various times defined himself as gay, but he has also had relationships with women, and from reading his autobiography, there are so many passages where he talks about wishing he had been born a woman, that it may well be, that had he been born 50 years later, he would have defined himself as a bisexual trans woman, rather than a gay man."
**Fundamentalist Christian Beliefs** : Little Richard is a very religious man, holding a specific variety of fundamentalist Christianity that condemns any sexuality or gender identity other than monogamous cis heterosexual as evil and sinful. He deeply believes this and has often tried to live his life by these beliefs. "Little Richard is also a very, very religious man, and a believer in a specific variety of fundamentalist Christianity that believes that any kind of sexuality or gender identity, other than monogamous cis heterosexual, is evil and sinful and the work of the devil."
**Swinging Between Secular and Divine** : Richard's life has been characterized by wild swings between embodying the "sex and drugs and rock and roll" lifestyle and renouncing it to embrace his fundamentalist Christian beliefs, where he claims homosexuality, bisexuality, and being trans are works of the literal devil. As of 2017, he had once again renounced rock and roll and same-sex relationships. "He has swung wildly between being almost the literal embodiment of the phrase sex and drugs and rock and roll, and being a creature who claims that homosexuality, bisexuality, and being trans are all works of the literal devil, several times he's gone from one to the other."
**Childhood and Disability** : Born Richard Penniman, he was the third of twelve children and known for causing trouble, including practical jokes with his own feces. As a child, he was terribly bullied due to physical disabilities: one leg significantly shorter than the other, a disproportionately large head, and different sized eyes. He also faced homophobic abuse from a young age due to his "vaguely mincing" gait. "He was also terribly bullied as a child, because he was disabled."
**Early Performance Career (Age 14)** : At the age of fourteen, Richard left school to pursue a performing career. He began by touring with a snake oil salesman named Doc Hudson, singing the non-religious song "Caldonia" by Louis Jordan. "At the age of fourteen he decided to leave school and become a performer."
**Adopting the Pompadour and Name** : While touring with Bee Brown and his Orchestra, Richard grew his hair into the huge pompadour that would become his trademark. It was also during this period that he acquired the name Little Richard. "It was while he was touring with that band, that he grew his hair into the huge pumpador that would later become a trademark, and he also got the name Little Richard."
**Female Impersonation** : Richard's performances as a female impersonator began when he had to cover for a sick woman in one of the touring shows, quickly donning her costume. He soon found himself performing in shows predominantly featuring drag performers for largely gay crowds. "It was while he was performing in these shows that he met the first of his two biggest influences."
**Influences from Billy Wright** : Billy Wright, a blues star from 1949-1951, greatly influenced Richard. Wright was a female impersonator with a pompadour and was one of the first blues singers to use gospel-inspired vocalizations ("panorasms"). Richard imitated Wright's style, including using "pancake 31" make-up on stage, a practice he continued throughout his career. "Richard became something of a bellywright wannabe and started incorporating parts of right style into his performances."
**Learning Piano from Escarito** : Richard was solely a singer until he met Escarito, another gay, black R&B singer, who also played piano. Richard consistently stated that Escarito taught him to play the piano, claiming his own piano style was a copy of Escarito's and that Escarito was a better player. "Richard soon learned to play piano, and he was seen by Johnny Otis, who was impressed."
**Impressed Johnny Otis** : Johnny Otis was highly impressed by Richard's live performance, describing him as "outrageous," "good looking and very effeminate," with a big pompadour. Otis likened his singing to Dinah Washington and was particularly amused when Richard, after proclaiming himself "King of the Blues," added "and the Queen too." "Otis said, I see this outrageous person, good looking and very effeminate, with a big pumpador."
**Abuse and Injury from Don Roby** : Don Roby, owner of Peacock Records, was reportedly disrespectful and treated his artists poorly. Richard openly criticized Roby, who retaliated by beating Richard so severely that it caused a hernia, which hurt for years and required an operation. "Roby responded, by beating Richard up so badly that he got a hernia, which hurt for years and necessitated an operation."
**Legal Troubles and Exile from Macon** : Around the time he was dropped by Peacock Records, Richard was arrested for "lewd conduct," which amounted to consensual voyeurism. This incident forced him to move away from his hometown of Macon, Georgia. "He was also, around the same time, made to move away from Macon, Georgia, where he lived, after being arrested for lured conduct."
**Career Low Point (1953)** : Following his arrest and being dropped by two record labels (RCA and Peacock), the split of his band The Tempo Toppers, and the recent death of his father, Richard was at a very low point in his career, unable to live in his hometown. "Things seemed pretty low."
**Persistent Demo Submissions to Specialty Records** : Lloyd Price suggested Richard send a demo tape to Specialty Records. The tape lay unheard for months until Richard's constant pestering finally prompted Bumps Blackwell, A&R at Specialty, to listen to it. "The tape lay on-listened at specialty for months, and it was only because of Richard's constant pestering for them to listen to it, that Bump's Blackwell, who was then in charge of A&R at specialty, eventually got round to listening to it."
**Contract Buyout for $600** : Specialty Records, after hearing Richard's demo, made an agreement with Don Roby of Peacock Records to buy out Richard's contract for $600. It was implied that Roby was so eager to be rid of Richard that he might have paid Specialty to take him. "Blackwell and Roup made an agreement with Don Roby to buy out his contract for $600."
**Uninspired First Specialty Session** : Richard's initial recording session for Specialty Records at Cosimo Matassa's Studio, using Fats Domino's session musicians, was largely unsuccessful. Bumps Blackwell noted Richard's lack of energy in the studio compared to his live performances. "Blackwell had heard that Richard was an astounding live act, but he was just doing nothing in the studio."
**Discovery of "Tutti Frutti" at the Dew Drop Inn** : During a break from an unproductive Specialty Records session, Little Richard went to the Dew Drop Inn. There, he performed "Tutti Frutti" on the piano for an audience, instantly impressing Bumps Blackwell with its hit potential despite its sexually explicit original lyrics. "The dude drop in had a piano, and it had an audience that Little Richard could show off in front of."
**Lyrical Embarrassment for "Tutti Frutti"** : When Dorothy LaBostrie was tasked with rewriting the explicit lyrics of "Tutti Frutti" to be broadcastable, Richard, despite his usual flamboyance, became embarrassed to sing the song in front of the "innocent looking young woman." He eventually agreed to sing it, but only if he was turned to face the wall. "Richard, not normally a man overly known for his modesty, became embarrassed at singing his song to the young woman."
**"Tutti Frutti" Publishing Deal** : The publishing rights for "Tutti Frutti" were owned by a company belonging to Art Rupe, the owner of Specialty Records. This company licensed the song to Specialty for half the normal mechanical licensing rate (one cent per record instead of two cents), a common practice with independent labels that Richard later resented. "This sort of cross collateralisation was common with independent labels at the time, but it still wrankled to Richard when he figured it out."
**Hiring an Imposter for Low-Class Gigs** : As Richard became a huge star, he had concert bookings in second-rate clubs that conflicted with his need to be in Hollywood to promote his new record. To avoid a reputation for missing gigs, he hired an imposter, James Brown, to perform as "Little Richard" at these lower-tier venues. "So while Richard went off to promote his record, another young singer from Georgia, with a pompadour and a gospel feel, was being introduced with the phrase, Ladies and Gentlemen, the hardest working man in show business today, little Richard."
**Worry About "Devil Music"** : As Richard's fame grew, so did his ego, but he also started to express concern about whether he should be creating what he perceived as "devil music," hinting at the future conflict between his religious beliefs and rock and roll career. "But Richard was also becoming a big star himself, and he was getting an ego to go along with it, and he was starting to worry whether he should be making this devil music at all."

## curiosities
**Major Blues Star and Influence** : Billy Wright was a significant blues star between 1949 and 1951, known for being one of the first blues singers to incorporate gospel-inspired "panorasms" into his vocals. He served as one of Little Richard's two biggest influences, with Richard becoming a "wannabe" and incorporating parts of Wright's style. "Billy Wright, like Richard, had been a female impersonator for a while."
**Female Impersonator and Image** : Like Little Richard, Billy Wright had been a female impersonator for a period and sported a pompadour haircut. He was also known for using "pancake 31" make-up on stage, a practice Richard adopted and continued throughout his career. "Like Richard, he had a pumpador haircut, and he was a fairly major blues star in the period from 1949 through 1951, being one of the first blues singers to sing with gospel-inspired panorasms."

## curiosities
**Little Richard's Piano Teacher and Clone** : Escarito, born Stephen Quincy Reader (also known as Escaucreader), was a gay, black R&B singer, known for his massive pompadour, mustache, and flamboyant, loud screaming vocal style. Little Richard consistently credited Escarito with teaching him to play the piano, claiming his own piano style was a copy of Escarito's and that Escarito was a superior player. Escarito later made records as a "Little Richard clone." "Richard has said for years that Escarito was the person who taught him how to play the piano, and that not only was his piano playing style a copy of Escarito's, Escarito was better."
**Flamboyant Image** : Escarito was known for an even larger pompadour and more flamboyant persona than Little Richard, screaming even louder when he sang. "Escarito looked like a caricature of Little Richard."

## curiosities
**Clean-Cut Cover Artist** : Pat Boone was known for his "good clean-cut wholesome Christian white boy" image. He often covered Little Richard's songs, changing the lyrics to remove any perceived immorality. "A good Christian boy like Pat Boone couldn't be heard thinking about such immorality."

# label - Peacock Records

## curiosities
**Little Richard's Second Label** : Peacock Records signed Little Richard and his band, The Tempo Toppers, after Johnny Otis recommended him to Don Roby, the label owner. Their initial recordings in early 1953, issued as two singles, were unsuccessful. Richard later recorded one more session for Peacock, but was ultimately dropped due to creative and personal conflicts with Roby. "Otis recommended Richard to Don Roby of Peacock Records, and Roby signed Richard and his band The Tempo Toppers."

# label - Specialty Records

## curiosities
**Discovery of Little Richard** : Specialty Records signed Little Richard after A&R head Bumps Blackwell, looking for a gospel-sounding R&B singer to compete with Ray Charles, heard Richard's demo tape. They bought Richard's contract from Peacock Records for $600. "Blackwell and Roup made an agreement with Don Roby to buy out his contract for $600."
**Half-Rate Mechanical Licensing** : Specialty Records, owned by Art Rupe, licensed songs like "Tutti Frutti" from a publishing company also owned by Art Rupe. This cross-collateralization meant Specialty only paid half the normal mechanical licensing rate (one cent per record instead of two cents), a common but frustrating practice for artists like Little Richard. "That company licensed the song to specialty records, for half the normal mechanical licensing rate."

# curiosity

**Snake Oil Salesmen and Entertainers** : In the US, snake oil salesmen traveled from town to town selling placebo remedies (mixtures of turpentine, tallow, camphor, and capsaicin) for serious illnesses. They often employed entertainers to draw crowds. A young Richard Penniman (Little Richard) started his performing career touring with one such salesman, Doc Hudson, singing non-religious songs. "Snake Oil Salesman would travel from town to town, sewing their placebo, and they would have entertainers performing with them, in order to draw crowds."
**White DJs Playing Black Music** : In the mid-20th century, many white DJs across America, such as Zenas Sears, became successful by playing black music and adopting approximations of African American vernacular English. These DJs often held significant influence and connections within the music industry. "Zena Ceeas, who was one of the many white DJs, all across America, who were starting to become successful by playing black music and speaking in approximations of African American vernacular English."
**Unacknowledged Songwriting Influence - Dorothy LaBostrie** : Dorothy LaBostrie was a songwriter for Specialty Records who, despite being trained, wrote all her lyrics to the same melody, specifically that of Dinah Washington's "Bloatop Blues." She was instrumental in rewriting the explicit lyrics of "Tutti Frutti" into a broadcastable form on short notice. "All her lyrics were written to the melody of the same song, Dynna Washington's bloatop blues."
**Joe Lubin's "Tutti Frutti" Credit** : Songwriter Joe Lubin received a third of the publishing credit for "Tutti Frutti" for making minimal lyrical changes to Pat Boone's cover version. These changes mainly involved altering one line to remove implied immorality and making minor word changes to form full sentences. "Getting a third of a song like Tutti frutti, for that little work, sounds like a pretty good deal, at least for Lubin, if not for Richard."
**Nona Johnson and "Long Tall Sally"** : Nona Johnson was a teenage girl who wrote a single verse of lyrics that formed the basis of Little Richard's song "Long Tall Sally." She walked many miles to New Orleans to show her lyrics to DJ Honey Child, hoping to earn money for her sick "Aunt Mary." She resided in Bogalusa, Louisiana. "The noterist Johnson had written a single verse of lyric on a scrap of paper and had walked many miles to New Orleans to show the lyric to a DJ named Honey Child."
