## albums
**Cold Shot (Late 1960s)** : This album by "the new Johnny Otis show" was signed by Kent Records, a small blues label owned by the Bihari Brothers, at Frank Zappa's persuasion. It featured a core band of only three people: Otis on piano and drums, Delmar "Mighty Mouth" Evans on vocals, and Otis's 15-year-old son, Sugar, playing all guitar and bass parts. The album marked Otis's first R&B hit since "Willie and the Hand Jive" over a decade earlier. "The record brought Otis his first R&B hit since William the Han Jive, more than a decade earlier, country girl."
**For Adults Only (Late 1960s)** : Recorded by the same trio (Otis, Evans, and Sugar) as "Cold Shot," this album was released under the name Snatch and the Poon Tangs. The cover, designed by Otis, was a "spot on imitation of the style of Robert Crum." Despite its obscene humor, the album had a serious sociological purpose: Otis wanted to preserve black folklore, including songs like "The Signifying Monkey," which had been performed for years but typically stripped of their sexual insults in other recordings by artists like Chuck Berry and Willie Dixon. "Around the same time, that trio also recorded another album called For Adults Only, under the name Snatch and the Poon Tangs, and with the covered run by Otis in a spot on imitation of the style of Robert Crum."
**Monterey Jazz Festival (1970) - Live Album** : A live performance by Johnny Otis's band at the Monterey Jazz Festival in 1970 was released as a live album. This performance featured many veteran musicians, including Esther Phillips, Big Joe Turner, Eddie "Cleanhead" Vincent, Roy Milton, Peewee Crayton, Ivory Joe Hunter, and Roy Brown. Footage from this show, specifically the band performing "Willie and the Hand Jive," was later featured in Clint Eastwood's film "Play Misty for Me," showcasing Otis's belief in the commonality and combined power of different strands of black American music. "That performance was released as a live album, and Clint Eastwood featured footage of that show. The band performing William the Han Jive, in his classic film Play Misty for Me, it was probably the greatest example of Otis's belief that all the important strands of black American music shared a commonality and could work in combination with each other."

## songs
**Lonely Lonely Night (1957)** : Johnny Otis produced and co-wrote this song for Little Julian Herrera and the Tigers. It became a local hit and Otis believed it was the first big record to feature a Chicano singer. Released on Dig Records, a label Otis owned, the song propelled Herrera to hero status among the Chicano population in Los Angeles. "In 1957 he produced and co-wrote Lonely Lonely Night for little Julian however and the Tigers which became a local hit and which he thought at the time was the first big record to feature Chicano singer."
**Ma, He's Making Eyes at Me (1957)** : This song, a vaudeville tune dating back to 1921, was recorded by the Johnny Otis band with vocals by Marie Adams and The Three Tons of Joy. In their rendition, it was transformed to sound more like Ruth Brown, especially with Marie Adams's distinctive pronunciation of "Mama." While it failed to chart in the US, it became a massive hit in the UK, reaching number two on the pop chart. Its success led to plans for a Johnny Otis Show tour of the UK in 1958, though these plans ultimately fell through due to union restrictions. "In the US, that did nothing at all on the charts. But for some reason it took off massively in the UK, and went to number two on the pop chart over here."
**Ma (1958)** : Released as a follow-up to "Ma, He's Making Eyes at Me," this song featured vocals by Marie Adams and Johnny Otis. Otis later expressed his dislike for many of his Capital recordings, including this one, feeling he was being pushed too far into commercial rock and roll. The song was described as an "endless litany" of recent rock and roll hits with little originality. Despite its poor quality, it reached the top 20 in the UK, primarily due to the momentum from the previous single's success. "The song is just an endless litany of the tackles of recentish rock and roll hit, with little to recommend it. It made the top 20 in the UK, mostly on the strength of people having bought the previous single."
**Willie and the Hand Jive (1958)** : Inspired by the "Hand Jive" dance popularized on the British TV show "6-5 Special" and demonstrated by The Three Tons of Joy, Johnny Otis wrote this song. He incorporated the Bo Diddley rhythm, although he consistently claimed to have heard the rhythm from convicts on a chain gang before Bo Diddley recorded it. Despite Otis's intention to capitalize on his UK success and the dance's popularity, the song did not achieve commercial success in the UK. However, it became Otis's only Top 10 pop hit in the US, reaching number 9 on the pop charts and number 1 on the R&B charts, his first R&B Top 10 in six years. "But while William the Han Jive didn't have any success in the UK, in the US it became Otis's only Top 10 pop hit, and his first R&B Top 10 hit as a performer in six years, reaching number 9 on the pop charts, and number 1 on the R&B charts."
**Willie did the Chacha (Post-1958)** : A follow-up to "Willie and the Hand Jive," this song was an attempt by Otis to replicate his previous hit's success while on a major label. However, the "Chacha's success remained elusive," and none of Otis's subsequent releases charted higher than number 52 on the pop charts. "Of course, with him now being on a major label, he had to do follow-ups to his big hit, like Willie did the Chacha."
**Country Girl (Late 1960s)** : This song was the first R&B hit for Johnny Otis since "Willie and the Hand Jive," appearing on his "Cold Shot" album. The album was recorded with his son Sugar and Delmar "Mighty Mouth" Evans. "The record brought Otis his first R&B hit since William the Han Jive, more than a decade earlier, country girl."

## curiosities
**Early Career and Influence Shift** : Johnny Otis was a recurring figure in early rock music, influential as a producer, arranger, performer, and talent scout. His significant impact on mainstream rock and roll largely ended with the rise of "rocker Billy" in the mid-1950s, after which he was less involved in the genre's commercial forefront. "But the days of his influence on rock and roll music, more or less came to an end with the rise of the rock abilities in the mid-50s."
**First Solo Rock and Roll Hit** : Coincidentally, as his production and discovery run was ending, Johnny Otis achieved his first and only rock and roll hit under his own name, "Ma, He's Making Eyes at Me," thanks to its success on the British TV show "6-5 Special." "He had a rock and roll hit under his own name for the first and only time."
**Dig Records Closure (Post-1957)** : Johnny Otis's label, Dig Records, closed down shortly after the scandal involving its artist, Little Julian Herrera. Herrera, known to Otis as a Chicano singer and a hero in the LA Chicano community, was revealed to be Ron Gregory, of Jewish and Hungarian American descent from Massachusetts, wanted on rape charges, effectively ending both Herrera's career and the label. "Pretty much the end of Little Julian Herrera's career and indeed shortly after that dig records itself closed down and Otis had no record contract"
**Signing with Capital Records (1957)** : After Dig Records closed and Otis was without a contract, comedian Mickey Katz, who was signed to Capital, a major label, saw Otis perform live. Recognizing Otis's talent as a band leader, Katz urged his label to sign him. This led to Otis securing guaranteed major label distribution for his records for the first time in many years. "Cats was on capital, one of the biggest labels in the country and so for the first time in many years Otis had guaranteed major label distribution for his records."
**Mass Release of Singles (October 1957)** : Capital Records took the unusual step of releasing four Johnny Otis singles simultaneously in October 1957. Each single featured a different vocalist from Otis's large roster of performers, one of which, featuring Marie Adams and The Three Tons of Joy, would significantly impact Otis's career. "In October 1957, capital took the unusual step of releasing four Johnny Otis singles at the same time."
**Dislike of Capital Recordings** : Johnny Otis later stated he disliked most of his recordings for Capital Records, feeling that the label pushed him too far into a commercial rock and roll direction. He considered himself spiritually closer to jazz and R&B legends like Count Basie, Lionel Hampton, and Louis Jordan, rather than rock figures such as Elvis Presley or Buddy Holly. "Johnny Otis meant, when he said later that he didn't like most of his capital recordings, because he was being pushed too far in a commercial rock and roll direction, while he saw himself as far closer in spirit to Count Basie, Lionel Hampton, or Louis Jordan, than to Elvis Presley or Buddy Holly."
**Planned UK Tour Failure (1958)** : Following the massive success of "Ma, He's Making Eyes at Me" in the UK, plans were made for a Johnny Otis Show tour of the country in 1958. However, these plans fell through due to the mutual protectionist agreement between the American Federation of Musicians and the British Musicians Union, which required a "like for like swap" of musicians for tours. Otis's band could not find an appropriate British counterpart for the exchange. "Those plans failed, because of something I've not mentioned in this podcast before, but which radically shaped British music culture, and to a lesser extent American music culture for decades."
**"Hand Jive" Misinterpretation and Bans** : Despite "Willie and the Hand Jive" becoming a major hit in the US, several radio stations banned the song. This was due to the mistaken assumption that the "Hand Jive" dance, which The Three Tons of Joy demonstrated on Otis's TV shows and stage performances, was a reference to masturbation. Otis continued to address these questions about the song's perceived lascivious meaning as late as the 1990s. "This was despite several radio stations banning it, as they assumed the Han Jive was a reference to masturbation."
**Civil Rights Activism (1960s)** : In the 1960s, Johnny Otis became increasingly involved in the Civil Rights Movement. He wrote a weekly newspaper column condemning racism, assisted his friend Mervyn Dymally (who became the first black person elected to state-wide office in the USA since Reconstruction), and worked alongside Malcolm X, facing death threats and having crosses burned on his lawn. Despite being white, his wife was black, which heightened the personal stakes of his activism. "He was also becoming increasingly involved in the civil rights movement, writing a weekly newspaper column, Decraing Racism, helping his friend Mervyn Damley, who became the joint first black person elected to state-wide office in the USA since the reconstruction."
**Reduced Musical Activity (1960s)** : As a direct consequence of his deep involvement in the Civil Rights Movement, Johnny Otis recorded and toured only infrequently throughout the 1960s. At one point, his income as a musician became so low that his wife became the family's primary breadwinner, and Otis assumed the role of a stay-at-home father. "The result was that Otis recorded and toured only infrequently during the 60s, and at one point was making so little as a musician that his wife became the main breadwinner of the family, while he was a stay-at-home father."
**Literary Works - Listen to the Lambs (1965)** : Following the Watts riot in 1965, Johnny Otis authored "Listen to the Lambs," a multifaceted work combining political essay, autobiography, and eyewitness accounts of the riots. In this book, he argued radically for economic equality as the immediate priority for the black community, believing social integration was impossible in the short term due to white racism. He asserted that economic and political control over their lives would benefit both black and white Americans. "Otis wrote the book Listen to the Lams, a combination political essay, autobiography, and mixture of eyewitness accounts of the riots that made a radical case that the first priority for the black community in which he lived. Wasn't so much social integration, which he believed impossible in the short term, due to white racism, as economic equality."
**Critique of Systemic Injustice in Listen to the Lambs** : Otis's book, "Listen to the Lambs," expressed fierce anger at systemic racism, colonialism, Anglo-centric beauty standards, police brutality, the Vietnam War, and the inequalities suppressing black people. He emphasized the stark disparity in survival rates for black babies compared to white babies, attributing it to a "white supremacist way of life" rather than biological factors. The book dedicates over 30 pages to witness accounts of the riots, including descriptions of white police officers casually shooting black teenagers. "The book is fierce in its anger at systemic racism, at colonialism, at Anglo-centric beauty standards that made black people hate their own bodies and faces, at police brutality, at the war in Vietnam, and at the systemic inequalities keeping black people down."
**Literary Works - Upside-Down Head (Early 1990s)** : In the early 1990s, Johnny Otis wrote a follow-up to "Listen to the Lambs" titled "Upside-Down Head." This second book was inspired by the Los Angeles riots that occurred after the Rodney King beating, continuing his critical examination of racial injustice in America. "Otis wrote a follow-up, upside-down head, in the early 90s, inspired by the LA riots that followed the Rodney King beating."
**Collaboration with Frank Zappa (Late 1960s)** : In the late 1960s, Frank Zappa, a significant admirer who even copied Otis's trademark beard, contacted Johnny Otis. They collaborated casually, with Otis primarily connecting Zappa with musicians he knew, such as Don "Sugarcane" Harris, and conducting The Mothers of Invention in the studio for songs like "The One," where Zappa sought Otis's help to achieve a "greasy 50s sound." During recording, Otis notoriously clapped his hands in bassist Roy Estrada's face. "Otis also conducted the mothers of invention in the studio and a few songs, while Zapper was in the control room, helping him get the greasy 50s sound he wanted, on songs like the one."
**Son Sugar Otis's Early Career** : Johnny Otis's teenage son, Sugar, started playing with his father's band at age 11, often using false moustaches and sunglasses to perform in venues serving alcohol. Sugar played bass on Frank Zappa's "Son of Mr. Green Genes" from the "Hot Rats" album and later played all guitar and bass parts on Johnny Otis's "Cold Shot" album at just 15 years old. "Sugar was only 15 at the time, but had been playing with his father's band since he was 11, often wearing false moustaches and sunglasses to play in venues serving alcohol."
**Obscenity Accusations** : Johnny Otis humorously recalled receiving accusations of obscenity for earlier songs like "All With Me Henry" and "Willie and the Hand Jive." However, he noted that no one seemed bothered by the explicit sexual insults present in the recordings of Snatch and the Poon Tangs, despite his intent to preserve black folklore through that project. "Otis would in later years laugh that he'd received accusations of obscenity, for all with me Henry and for William the Han Jive, but nobody had seen bothered in the slightest by the records of Snatch and the Poon Tangs with their constant sexual insults."
**Later Career and Diverse Activities (Post-1970)** : For several decades after 1970, Johnny Otis continued to tour with his old collaborators, including Marie Adams who rejoined in 1972. Beyond music, he hosted his own radio show dedicated to black musical history and interviewed old musicians, wrote more books (including a cookbook and art collection), ran an organic apple juice company and food store, painted blues artists in a style inspired by African art and Picasso, and served as pastor of a broad-minded Pentecostal church that affirmed LGBT individuals and included Buddhist and Jewish congregants. "For the next few decades, Otis combined touring with as many of his old collaborators as possible. Marie Adams, for example, rejoined the band in 1972, with having his own radio show in which he told people about black musical history and interviewed as many old musicians as he could."
**Blues Spectrum Records (1970s)** : In the 1970s, Johnny Otis founded and operated Blues Spectrum Records. This label released late-career recordings by notable artists such as Charles Brown, Big Joe Turner, and Louis Jordan, some of which were their very last recordings. "He ran blues spectrum records in the 70s, which put out late career recordings by people like Charles Brown, Big Joe Turner and Louis Jordan. Some of them their last ever recordings"
**Lecturer at Berkeley** : Johnny Otis lectured on the history of black music at the University of California, Berkeley. "and he lectured in the history of black music at Berkeley."
**Death and Legacy (2012)** : Johnny Otis passed away in 2012 at the age of 90, having led an exceptionally accomplished life and significantly supported many other talents. He died three days before Etta James, an artist he was immensely proud of discovering, and she consequently overshadowed him in obituaries, which he would have approved of. "Johnny Otis died in 2012, aged 90, having achieved more than most of us could hope to achieve if we lived five times that long, and having helped many, many more people to make the most of their talents."

## curiosities
**Obscure British Band** : The Bell-Cats were an extremely obscure British group that never made a record. Their only lasting trace on "post-stability" (post-Beatles era) is their popularization of the "Hand Jive" dance, documented in three photos taken by Ken Russell on a single night. They were one of many British bands attempting to emulate the sound of Bill Haley and the Comets. "The dance that was originally popularised by a British group that was so obscure that they never made a record, and the only trace they left on post-stability was this dance in three photos."
**The Cat's Whisker Residency** : The Bell-Cats held a regular gig at a London coffee house called the Cat's Whisker. They were so popular that audiences were often dangerously packed "like sardines," leading the police to eventually shut down the venue due to overcrowding. The owner subsequently reopened it as the first Angus Steakhouse. "Their regular gig was at a coffee house called the Cat's Whisker, where they were popular enough that the audience were packed in like sardines."
**Originators of the Hand Jive Dance** : Due to the extreme overcrowding at their performances at the Cat's Whisker, audiences were unable to dance properly. This constraint led to the development and spread of a new dance among customers that only involved moving one's hands—the "Hand Jive." "In those bell-cats performances, the audiences were packed so tightly that they couldn't dance properly, and so when you danced developed among the customers and spread, a dance where you only moved your hands. The Han Jive."
