## albums
**Rhythm Is My Business (1962)** : An album by Ella Fitzgerald that featured Bill Doggett's arrangements and prominently highlighted his Hammond organ playing. ## songs
**Shout Sister Shout** : A song co-composed by Bill Doggett and Lucky Millinder during Doggett's tenure as pianist and de facto musical director for Millinder's band. It later gave its title to a biography of Sister Rosetta Tharpe, who performed with the band. "he co-composed with Melinda, the song that later gave its title to a biography of Tharp, Shout Sister Shout."
**When the Lights Go On Again All Over the World** : This was the biggest hit for Lucky Millinder's band, and Bill Doggett was in the band when it was recorded. It is notable for featuring one of the first recordings of a young trumpeter named Dizzy Gillespie. "Doggert was also in the band when they had their biggest hit, a song called When the Lights Go On Again all over the world."
**B. Baba Leba** : A song recorded by the Bill Doggett Octet, his own band, featuring Helen Humes on vocals. This song is believed to have inspired Gene Vincent to write a similarly named song a few years later. "They were the ones who recorded B. Baba Leba with Helen Hume's on vocals."
**Saturday Night Fish Fry** : Bill Doggett is often credited with rewriting this song, transforming it from its original "innermost vicious" version into one of Louis Jordan's biggest hits during Doggett's time as his musical director, though Jordan is credited on the record. "Dogget is often credited as the person who rewrote Saturday Night Fish Fry into one of Jordan's biggest hits, from its innermost vicious original version, though Jordan is credited on the record."
**Tambor Ritz a Boogey** : A song recorded by Louis Jordan's band that featured both Bill Doggett on piano and Wild Bill Davis on the Hammond organ. This collaboration allowed Doggett to hear the Hammond organ up close, further fueling his fascination with the instrument. "which had Dogget on piano, and Davis on the Hammond organ."
**Honky Tonk Part 1 and Part 2 (1956)** : Bill Doggett's biggest hit, an instrumental born from an accidental jam during a dance in Lima, Ohio. Guitarist Billy Butler started noodling a 12-bar blues pattern, the audience started dancing, and the band improvised along, with Clifford Scott taking the lead on honking saxophone. The song was over five minutes long, requiring it to be released as a double-sided 78 RPM record (Parts 1 and 2), a format disliked by jukebox operators but agreed to by King Records' Sid Nathan due to its undeniable popularity. It was recorded in a single take, selling 5,000 copies the first week, then 12,500 the next, and eventually 100,000 copies a week. It reached number one on the R&B chart for 13 weeks, becoming the biggest-selling R&B song of 1956, and remarkably also peaked at number two on the pop chart. This song became a staple for cover bands and influenced many artists, including Dwayne Eddy, whose entire style seems to have stemmed from it, and was covered by acts like The Ventures, Buddy Holly, The Beach Boys, and James Brown. "Honky Tongue Parts 1 and 2 came about almost by accident."
**Honky Tonk Vocal version** : One of several follow-up recordings made by Bill Doggett after the success of "Honky Tonk Part 1 and Part 2," demonstrating his tendency to repeat the successful formula with variations. "Over the decade after Honky Tonk, Parts 1 and 2, he recorded tracks like Honky Tonk Vocal version..."
**Hippie Dippy** : A track recorded by Bill Doggett in the decade following "Honky Tonk Part 1 and Part 2," part of a series of releases that echoed his biggest hit, showing a pattern of repeating his successful formula. "Over the decade after Honky Tonk, Parts 1 and 2, he recorded tracks like ... Hippie Dippy..."
**Blip Blop** : A track recorded by Bill Doggett in the decade following "Honky Tonk Part 1 and Part 2," part of a series of releases that echoed his biggest hit, showing a pattern of repeating his successful formula. "Over the decade after Honky Tonk, Parts 1 and 2, he recorded tracks like ... Yucky Dog..."
**Honky Tonk Buss & Over** : A track recorded by Bill Doggett in the decade following "Honky Tonk Part 1 and Part 2," part of a series of releases that echoed his biggest hit, showing a pattern of repeating his successful formula. "Over the decade after Honky Tonk, Parts 1 and 2, he recorded tracks like ... Honky Tonk Buss & Over."

This collaboration was part of Brown's effort to acknowledge artists who had been on King Records when Brown first started with the label in the 1950s. "For Doggett, Brown produced and wrote Hanky Tonk Popcorn."

## curiosities
**Early Band Leader** : In his late teens, after moving to New York from Philadelphia (where he was born in 1916), Bill Doggett formed his own band, where he played the piano. "Doggert was born in 1916 in Philadelphia, and he moved to New York in his late teens and formed his own band, for which he was the piano player."
**De Facto Musical Director for Lucky Millinder** : Bill Doggett served as the unofficial musical director for Lucky Millinder's band during his tenure as pianist. Millinder, unable to play an instrument, relied on musicians like Doggett to create arrangements. "Doggert was, from what I can tell, the de facto musical director for Melinda's band in this period."
**Pivotal in Genre Transition (Swing to R&B)** : Bill Doggett was an important part of Lucky Millinder's band, which was pivotal in the transition of popular music from swing to R&B. He helped set up the band's style, making it suitable for future R&B vocalists. "The Melinda band were pivotal in the move from swing music to R and B, and Doggert was an important part in that move."
**Reputation Untarnished After Millinder Departure** : The reasons for Bill Doggett's departure from Lucky Millinder's band are unknown, but it didn't negatively impact his career. He remained managed by Mo Gale, who then placed him with The Ink Spots. "it can't have been anything that put a stain on his reputation, because Doggert remained with Melinda's manager, Mo Gillespie."
**Recognized as De Facto Ink Spots Member** : Although primarily a side-man and arranger for The Ink Spots for two years, Bill Doggett was so integral to the group's sound and success during their classic lineup that he is now included in lists of proper members by band historians. "While he was a side-man, rather than a full member of the group, he was important enough to them that he now gets counted in lists of proper members put together by historians of the band."
**Extensive Work as Arranger/Pianist** : After leaving The Ink Spots, Bill Doggett became Ella Fitzgerald's full-time arranger and pianist for a couple of years, then moved to Illinois Jacquet's band in the same role, demonstrating his high demand and versatile skills. "Dogget moved over full-time to become Ella's Aranger and Peanest."
**Louis Jordan's Musical Director** : Bill Doggett became the sole pianist and effectively the musical director for Louis Jordan's band, one of the biggest R&B groups of the era, after initially alternating duties with Wild Bill Davis. "Dogget once again basically became the musical director for one of the biggest bands in the R&B business."
**Shift from Piano to Hammond Organ Trio** : Inspired by Wild Bill Davis's pioneering work, Bill Doggett left Louis Jordan's band to form his own organ trio, adopting the Hammond organ as his primary instrument and recording blues tracks in a similar style to Davis. "When Dogget left Jordan's band, he decided to form an organ trio, just like Davis's."
**Desire for Originality with Honking Saxophone** : After a couple of years playing in an organ trio and being compared to Wild Bill Davis, Bill Doggett decided to differentiate his sound. He brought in a "honking saxophone player" to take the lead instrumental role, with Doggett focusing on rhythmic support, leading to his unique sound. "And so, to vary the style, he decided to take on a hunking saxophone player to be the group's lead instrumentalist, while Dogget would concentrate on providing a rhythmic pad."
**"Honky Tonk" - Accidental Creation** : Bill Doggett's biggest hit, "Honky Tonk," originated entirely by accident at a dance in Lima, Ohio. It started as guitarist Billy Butler's unplanned noodling on his bass strings between songs, which captivated the audience and led the band to improvise around the riff. "Honky Tongue Parts 1 and 2 came about almost by accident."
**Audience Demand for "Honky Tonk"** : After the spontaneous creation of "Honky Tonk," an audience member requested "that hot new song," prompting the band to recreate it from memory. The crowd's enthusiastic response led them to play the improvised tune ten times by the end of the night. "But then, a couple of songs later, someone in the audience came up and asked them if they could play that hot new song they'd been playing before, again, not realizing it had just been a spur of the moment jam."
**King Records Dropping Doggett Over Royalty Dispute** : Bill Doggett's career as a charting artist largely ceased after 1960 when he asked King Records' Sid Nathan for a higher royalty rate, given the millions his recordings had generated for the label. King Records responded by dropping him from their roster. "Doggett's spent most of the rest of his life, touring the oldies circuit."
**Continued Touring Success** : Despite being dropped by King Records, Bill Doggett remained a prolific working musician, touring the oldies circuit and performing hundreds of shows a year until his death in 1996 at age 80. "Doggett's spent most of the rest of his life, touring the oldies circuit."
**Lifelong Dedication to "Honky Tonk"** : Bill Doggett consistently played "Honky Tonk" at every single one of his shows throughout his life, understanding its importance to his audience and his identity as an artist. "I just wouldn't be Bill Doggett if I didn't play Hanky Tonk. That's what the people pay to hear, so that's what they get."

**Band Acquisition Strategy** : Lucky Millinder had a unique method of forming his orchestra: he would buy out and assume leadership of existing bands, which would then become the Lucky Millinder Orchestra. "The way Melinda worked was that he bought out and took over the leadership of, existing bands, which then became the Lucky Melinda Orchestra."
**Savoy Residency Successor** : The incarnation of Lucky Millinder's Orchestra, initially formed by Bill Doggett, secured a residency at The Savoy nightclub after Chick Webb's band stopped performing there, under the management of Mo Gale. "This incarnation of the Lucky Melinda Orchestra, the one that was put together by Doggert before Melinda took the band over, is the one that got a residency at the Savoy, after Chick Webb's band stopped playing there."
**Non-Instrumentalist Leader** : Lucky Millinder served as a frontman and occasional singer for his orchestra but could not play any instrument himself. He relied heavily on his musicians, particularly Bill Doggett, to work out arrangements. "Melinda was a frontman and occasional singer, but he couldn't play an instrument, and was reliant on the musicians in his band to work the arrangements out for him."
**Film Appearance - Paradise in Harlem (1938)** : Lucky Millinder appeared in the 1938 all-Black film "Paradise in Harlem" with his band, featuring Bill Doggett on piano. "he appeared with Melinda in the 1938 All Black Film Paradise in Harlem, playing on this song."
**Talent for Sacking Musicians** : Lucky Millinder had a habit of dismissing musicians from his band before they fully realized their potential, as exemplified by his quick sacking of a young Dizzy Gillespie. "Gillespie was quickly sacked by Melinda, who had a habit of getting rid of musicians before they reached their full potential."

**Lineup Changes During Bill Doggett's Tenure** : During Bill Doggett's two years with the group, he was part of the classic lineup and witnessed key personnel changes, including Charlie Fouquet being drafted and Deek Watson being fired. **Collaborations with Ella Fitzgerald** : When The Ink Spots were not touring, their members, including Bill Doggett, would perform with Ella Fitzgerald, who was also managed by Mo Gale. "Davis and Dogget between them gave the Hammond organ its prominence in the world of jazz, R&B, and Soul Music."

# genre - Rock and Roll

## curiosities
**Evolution from Instrumental Music** : While primarily a vocal music, rock and roll was not a complete break from the past, drawing heavily from earlier instrumental traditions. The existence of instrumental groups like The Ventures (US) and The Shadows (UK) with hit records was crucial for the development of rock and roll guitar bands. "While rock and roll was primarily a vocal music, it wasn't a completely clean break with the past, and for most of the decades from the 1920s, through to the early 50s, if you wanted music for dancing, you would want instrumental groups."
**Early Instrumental Hits** : Even into the 1960s, rock and roll would continue to feature purely instrumental hits, demonstrating the genre's continued connection to its instrumental predecessors. "But while into the 1960s we'll still be seeing rock and roll hits that were purely instrumental."
**Uncomfortable Fit for Some Artists** : Not all musicians promoted as "rock and roll" acts felt comfortable in the genre. Bill Doggett, for instance, who was 40 years old when "Honky Tonk" became a hit, considered himself a jazz organ player, not a "Little Richard type." "But Doggett was being promoted as a Ruck and Roll act, and playing bills with other Ruck and Roll stars, and he didn't really feel comfortable in the Ruck and Roll world."

# genre - Instrumental Music

## curiosities
**Dominance for Dancing (1920s-1950s)** : For much of the period from the 1920s through the early 1950s, instrumental groups were the preferred choice for dance music, with big bands led by instrumentalists rather than vocalists. "for most of the decades from the 1920s, through to the early 50s, if you wanted music for dancing, you would want instrumental groups."
**Shift to Vocal Dominance** : The music industry transitioned from being dominated by instrumental music to being dominated by singers primarily out of necessity, following the musician's union strikes of the 1940s. "It was only with the musician's union strikes of the 1940s, which we've talked about before, that more food necessity than anything else. The music industry moved from being dominated by instrumental music to being dominated by singers."
**Continued Relevance in Rock and Roll** : Despite the shift towards vocal music, purely instrumental rock and roll hits continued to appear into the 1960s, and instrumental groups like The Ventures and The Shadows were fundamental to the existence of rock and roll guitar bands. "But while into the 1960s we'll still be seeing rock and roll hits that were purely instrumental. Indeed we probably wouldn't have rock and roll guitar bands at all, without instrumental groups like the Ventures in the US, or the Shadows in the UK, who had hits with pure instrumental records."
