## curiosities
**Discovery and Nurturing by Sam Phillips** : Sam Phillips is credited not only with discovering Elvis Presley but also with nurturing his artistic ability, to the extent that "Elvis the artist was as much the creation of Sam Phillips as he was of Elvis Presley himself." "It was entirely likely that without Sam Phillips Elvis would never have been anything more than a truck driver with a passable voice."
**Impact of Success on Sun Records** : Elvis's success created a significant problem for Sun Records, an independent label, due to cash flow issues. The demand for hundreds of thousands of copies meant paying pressing plants upfront while distributors paid months later, if at all. "Something is more deadly to the indie label than a hit."
**Evolution of Band Lineup** : By his last single for Sun, Elvis's core band solidified with the addition of drummer DJ Fontana, evolving the trio of Elvis, Scotty, and Bill into "Elvis Presley and the Blue Moon Boys." Session drummer Johnny Bernero played on that specific single, but Fontana became a permanent fixture. "The trio of Elvis Scottie and Bill had now become a singer in his backup band, Elvis Presley and the Blue Moon Boys."
**Tom Parker's Interest in Elvis** : Tom Parker became interested in managing Elvis after witnessing the large, wild crowds he drew, despite not understanding Elvis's music appeal but recognizing the crowd's potential to spend money. "When Tom Parker saw one of those crowds, he knew he had to have Elvis."
**Parental Influence on Management Deal** : Tom Parker secured Elvis's management by working on his family, particularly his parents, who were big fans of Hank Snow. Snow convinced them of Parker's benefits, leading them to be "practically salivating" to sign Elvis with Parker. "Elvis's parents were big Hank snow fans and after being told by their idol how much the Colonel had helped him they were practically salivating to get to Elvis signed with him."
**Elvis's Personality and Motivation** : Carl Perkins described Elvis as "the most introverted person ever to enter a recording studio." Elvis, young and naive, primarily wanted to earn enough money to support his ailing parents, whose father had a bad back and mother was constantly sick and tired. "He just wanted to make some money to look after his parents."
**Contract Sale to RCA Records (1955)** : In 1955, Elvis's contract was sold by Sun Records to RCA Records for $35,000. This sum was the highest amount ever paid for a recording artist's contract at that time. Sam Phillips initially proposed this figure as a bluff, never expecting Parker to raise the money. "But in 1955 that would be the highest sum of money ever paid for a recording artist's contract."
**Immediate Reissue by RCA** : After signing Elvis, RCA Records quickly reissued his Sun singles, "I forgot to remember to forget" and "Mystery Train," while waiting for his first recording session with the new label. "RCA quickly reissued, I forgot to remember to forget, and mystery train, while they were waiting for Elvis's first recording session for his new label."
**Likely Encounters with Junior Parker** : Elvis almost certainly saw Junior Parker perform live, possibly during Parker's time with The Beale Streeters. BB King later recalled the teenage Elvis being among the very few white people attending their shows. "Indeed, Elvis almost certainly saw Junior Parkour live when he was playing with the Beatles Streeters."
**Artistic and Financial Overlap** : The choice of "Mystery Train" for Elvis was a rare instance where financial incentives (Sam Phillips was a credited songwriter) perfectly aligned with artistic preference, as it was a song Elvis clearly liked and continued to perform live in the 1970s. "So this was one of those rare cases where the financial and artistic incentives perfectly overlapped."
**Material Selection Philosophy** : The pattern established in his early career indicated that whenever Elvis chose his own material and followed his instincts, the music was good. Conversely, when music was chosen for him by others, even sympathetic figures like Sam Phillips, the music suffered. "Whenever Elvis got to choose his own material and follow his own instincts, the results would be good music."

"And we know that he was dishonorably discharged from the US Army as a psychopath, and that the webroom was around his hometown decades later that Parker was responsible for the murder."

His roles included running a "dancing chicken show" (using a hot plate to make chickens lift their legs), fortune-telling, and coaxing the "geek" back to the show with whiskey. "Parker had started out as a carny, working in travelling shows, doing everything from running a dancing chicken show in which he put a hot plate under a chicken's feet, so it would keep lifting his legs up and look like it was dancing, to telling fortunes, to being the person whose job it was, to tempt the geek to come back to the show with a bottle of whiskey when he became too sick and by his job."
**Colonel Tom Parker - Carnival Lessons on Audience Exploitation** : His time in carnivals led Parker to conclude that audiences existed to be exploited, and that nothing mattered to them "other than the promise of sexuality." "It had led him to the conclusion that audiences were there to be ripped off and that absolutely nothing mattered to them, other than the promise of sexuality."

"Parker soon used his music industry connections to get an honorary Kernel's commission from Louisiana Governor Jimmy Davis, himself a former country musician."
**Colonel Tom Parker - Template for Grasping Managers** : Tom Parker is considered the archetype for "the worst, most grasping managers in the music business," influencing figures like Alan Klein, Peter Grant, and Don Arden.
