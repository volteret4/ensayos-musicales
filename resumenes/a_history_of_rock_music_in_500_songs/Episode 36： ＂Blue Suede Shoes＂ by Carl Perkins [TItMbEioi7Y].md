## songs
**Movie Mag (1946)** : Written by Carl Perkins at the age of 14, this song vividly portrays his family's extreme poverty, describing a journey to the cinema by mule. In the specific time and place where Perkins grew up, riding a mule was considered more socially respectable than using a car, which would likely be rusted and broken down. Sam Phillips found the song worth recording and released it as Perkins's first single on the Flip label. "He started up that song he had written when he was 14, Movie Mag."
**Turn Around (Unknown Year)** : This ballad, composed by Carl Perkins, was released as the B-side to "Movie Mag" on the Flip label. "Phillips put out Movie Mag, backed with the ballad Carl had written, turn around."
**Gongs-Gongs-Gongs (Unknown Year)** : This improvised song served as the A-side for Carl Perkins's first single released on Sun Records proper. It followed the established formula of pairing an uptempo A-side with a ballad B-side. The song was based on the old phrase "it must be jelly, because jam don't shake like that," and its improvised nature meant every take was different. Although not a major hit, it sold well enough for Sam Phillips to arrange a tour for Perkins alongside Elvis Presley and Johnny Cash. "The A side, Gong-Gong-Gong, was one of Carl's improvised songs."
**Blue Suede Shoes (1955)** : The idea for this landmark song originated from Johnny Cash's anecdote about a serviceman referring to his shoes, and was solidified when Perkins overheard an audience member exclaim, "Don't step on my suede!" Perkins wrote the song one night, playing his electric guitar unplugged to avoid disturbing his wife. He recorded it on December 19, 1955, and famously misspelled "suede" as "SWAD-A-D-E" because he did not know the correct spelling. The song became a massive cross-over hit, reaching number two on both Pop and Country charts and number five on the R&B chart. Perkins's version is rhythmically sophisticated, featuring a stop-time introduction that transitions from 6/4 time into 4/4, creating a unique tension and release dynamic. "Perkins found himself one night getting out of bed, playing his electric guitar unplugged, so as not to disturb his wife, and writing a song he called Blue Suede shoes."
**Honey Don't (1955)** : Released as the B-side to Carl Perkins's smash hit single "Blue Suede Shoes," this song also received considerable airplay. "Blue Sway Chuse was an obvious hit, but the B side, Honeydones, got more than a little airplane as well."
**Only You (Unknown Year)** : Carl Perkins was inspired to add this song to his setlist after discussing his mutual affection for it with Elvis Presley during a tour. Perkins's performance of the song, however, upstaged Presley, who had planned to perform it himself that same night, leading to an unspoken agreement that they would not perform on the same bill again. "Perkins and Fesley had been talking about their mutual love for the song Only You, and I think Spide Perkins to add the song to his own set list."

## curiosities
**Childhood Poverty and Guitar Origin** : Carl Perkins endured extreme poverty during his childhood, growing up in a family of sharecroppers. When he expressed a desire for a guitar, his parents were unable to afford one, leading his father to ingeniously craft his first instrument from a cigar box and a broom handle. "his father made him one from a cigar box in a broom handle."
**Unique Guitar Style Development** : Due to his family's destitution, Carl Perkins could not afford new guitar strings when they broke and resorted to tying them back together. To avoid the pain caused by the knots, he innovated a distinctive guitar playing style involving bending strings to achieve different notes, rather than employing conventional fretting techniques. "he ended up developing a unique guitar style, bending strings to get different notes rather than fretting them normally, to avoid the knots in the strings which hurt his fingers."
**Early Musical Influences** : Carl Perkins was a fervent admirer of Bill Monroe, acknowledged as the originator of Bluegrass music, appreciating his high, driving, and harmony-rich songs of longing. This preference contrasted with his brother Jay's taste for Ernest Tubb's more subdued honky-tonk sound. "Carl was a massive fan of the inventor of Bluegrass, Bill Mundo, who sang high, driving, harmony-filled songs of longing."
**Sun Records Audition Initial Rejection** : Upon arriving at Sun Records for an audition, Carl Perkins and his band were initially dismissed by Marion Keisker, an employee, who stated the label wasn't holding auditions and had no need for new singers, especially not another act resembling Elvis Presley. Their opportunity for a quick listen only arose from a fortuitous encounter with Sam Phillips as they were leaving. "Marion Kaiske told them that they weren't auditioning right then, and that they didn't need any new singers."
**Sam Phillips' Audition Condition** : After being rejected on his first two attempts, Carl Perkins played "Movie Mag" for Sam Phillips, who listened with keen interest. Phillips then offered the band a chance to record if they returned with more songs in that vein, but he specifically told Carl that his brother Jay's lead vocals were not suitable, making it clear that the group would effectively become "Carl Perkins and his band." "He told Carl that there was no place for any lead vocals by his brother Jay."
**Pre-Fame Financial Struggles** : Despite having released records and performing on stage, Carl Perkins earned so little from his music that he was compelled to work a side job on a farm to generate enough income to buy Christmas presents for his children. This situation often astonished those around him, who recognized him from his performances and records and assumed he was financially successful. "He was earning so little money from his music that he was having to do farm work as a side job in order to make enough money to buy his kids Christmas presents."
**Confusion Over 7-inch Vinyl Single** : After his complimentary copies of "Blue Suede Shoes" arrived damaged, Carl Perkins went to a local shop to purchase a replacement. He was dismayed to find a small, seven-inch plastic record with a large center hole, a stark contrast to the ten-inch shellac records he considered "real." Distraught, he cried, believing his record had been ruined, before eventually understanding that these new vinyl singles were the popular format desired by younger audiences. "He was horrified at what he saw. Instead of a proper record, a big ten-inch thing with a tiny little hole in the middle made out of shell-ac."
**Rivalry with Elvis Presley** : Carl Perkins and Elvis Presley maintained a friendship despite an incident on tour where Perkins "upstaged" Presley by performing "Only You," a song Elvis had also planned to perform that night. This event led to the two artists never sharing a stage again. However, Elvis did offer fashion advice, taking Carl shopping to help him develop a more sophisticated stage appearance. "The two remained friends, but would never perform on the same bill again."
**Career Derailed by Car Accident** : Carl Perkins' career suffered a tragic setback when he was involved in a serious car accident en route to perform on the "Perry Como Show" on television. The collision with a truck resulted in the death of the truck driver and the hospitalization of both Carl and his brother Jay, causing a critical loss of career momentum. "On his way to perform on the Perricomo show on TV, Perkins' car hit a truck."
**Royalty Disputes with Sam Phillips** : Sam Phillips presented Carl Perkins with a Cadillac, claiming it was a reward for "Blue Suede Shoes" becoming the first Sun Records million-seller. However, Perkins later discovered the car's cost was deducted from his own royalties. This incident initiated a lifelong series of royalty disputes between the two men, with Perkins consistently believing he was denied his rightful earnings. "Perkins was less impressed when he found out that the Cadillac wasn't a gift, but had been paid for out of Perkins' royalties, and that eventually started a lifelong series of royalty disputes between the two men."
**Single-Song Defining Career** : Following the immense success of "Blue Suede Shoes," Carl Perkins never achieved another hit as a performer, and his career largely became defined by that one song. Despite this, he continued to create records that would later inspire globally significant musicians and become classics. "Perkins would never have another hit as a performer, and his career would be defined by that one song."

## curiosities
**Early Bar Performances in Tennessee** : The band refined their skills by playing in rough bars across Tennessee, necessitating the development of dance music. These venues were characterized by patrons who would often buy two bottles of beer at a time – one for drinking and one for smashing over someone else's head. "The Perkins Brothers spent their next several years honing their craft, playing some of the ruffus bars in Tennessee."
**Adapting to Bar Culture** : They developed a dance-oriented musical style rooted in honky-tonk, incorporating elements from bluegrass, western swing, and blues. This versatile approach allowed them to keep intoxicated crowds dancing with their limited instrumentation of two guitars, a double bass, and a drum kit. They began performing in these bars even before they were legally old enough to enter them. "So they very quickly developed a style that was rooted in honky-tongue music, but which was totally oriented around getting people dancing."
**Improvised Songwriting** : Carl and Jay Perkins would alternate lead vocals, and when they exhausted their prepared repertoire, Carl would spontaneously compose new songs around standard chord changes. He possessed the ability to improvise both words and music on the spot, often remembering successful choruses or lines, which gradually transformed these improvisations into structured and permanent parts of their setlist. "when they ran out of songs to perform, Carl would improvise new ones around standard chord changes."

## curiosities
**Move to RCA and Impact on Sun Records** : Elvis Presley's transfer from Sun Records to RCA provided Sam Phillips with a substantial sum of money, securing Sun Records' financial stability for the first time. This newfound security allowed Phillips to fully focus on developing the rockabilly genre with other artists. "While Elvis Presley had moved from Sun to RCA, that didn't mean that Sam Phillips had given up on recording rock and roll music, far from it."
**Early Rockabilly Pioneer** : Elvis Presley is considered "arguably the first rockabilly artist," and the characteristic elements of his earliest recordings—such as slapback echo, electric guitar, and slapback bass—served as the foundational template for the entire rockabilly sound. "while Elvis was arguably the first rockabilly artist, it wasn't until Elvis had left Sun that the floodgates opened, and Sam Phillips started producing the records to define the genre as a genre"
**Perkins Brothers' Reaction to Elvis** : When Carl Perkins and his brothers attended an Elvis Presley live performance, Jay and Clayton Perkins reacted negatively, inferring from Elvis's attention to his appearance that he "must be gay" and therefore "worthy of nothing but contempt." Carl, however, found inspiration in the performance. "Jay and Clayton took a dislike to Elvis, deciding that because he paid any attention to his appearance, he must be gay, and therefore, in their opinion, worthy of nothing but contempt"
**Fashion Mentor to Carl Perkins** : Following an on-stage disagreement during a tour, Elvis Presley took Carl Perkins "clothes shopping" and imparted advice on "how to dress in a more sophisticated manner on stage," influencing Perkins's stage persona. "Elvis did, however, take Carl out clothes shopping and show him how to dress in a more sophisticated manner on stage."
**Simultaneous TV Performances** : Elvis Presley performed his cover of "Blue Suede Shoes" on "Stage Show" on one television network at the same time Carl Perkins was making his debut TV appearance, also performing "Blue Suede Shoes," on the "Ozark Jubilee" on another network. "Thresley was on stage show on another network, performing his cover version of it."

# genre - Rockabilly

## curiosities
**Birth in Sun Studios** : The distinctive rockabilly sound was "in essence, created in Sun Studios." This genre truly began to define itself after Elvis Presley's departure from Sun Records, when Sam Phillips intensified his efforts to produce records that encapsulated this specific style, moving beyond it being solely identified with a single artist. "The rockabilly sound was, in essence, created in Sun Studios"
**Purist Definition** : For purists, rockabilly holds a "very, very specific meaning" regarding its sonic characteristics. It mandates the presence of slapback echo on vocals, an electric guitar, and slapback bass, essentially mirroring the core elements of Elvis Presley's earliest recordings. While piano or drums could be added (to accommodate artists like Jerry Lee Lewis), the sound strictly prohibits horns, strings, or backing vocals to maintain its primitive quality. Furthermore, steel guitar or fiddle are excluded to prevent it from crossing over into country music territory. "It had to have slapback echo on the vocals. It had to have an electrically guitar and slapback bass."

# genre - Country

## curiosities
**Distinction from Rockabilly** : In the definition of the rockabilly sound, instruments like the steel guitar and fiddle were deliberately excluded. Their inclusion would have "tip it over into country," clearly demarcating stylistic boundaries between the nascent rockabilly and established country genres. "And no steel guitar or fiddle either, that would tip it over into country."

# label - Sun Records

## curiosities
**Post-Elvis Financial Security and Genre Shift** : The substantial payment received from RCA for Elvis Presley's contract provided Sun Records with financial stability for the first time. This financial security allowed founder Sam Phillips to pivot the label's focus almost entirely from blues to rockabilly, a move that enabled him to significantly shape his legacy through the development of this new genre. "With the amount of money that RCA had paid for Elvis's contract, Sun Records was, for the first time, on a completely secure footing, and now Phillips could really begin work on making the music that would come to define his legacy."
**Sam Phillips' Discovery of New Talent** : Following Elvis Presley's departure, Sam Phillips and his dedicated staff actively discovered a pool of "young, exciting musicians." These new artists proved crucial in cementing Sun Records' enduring place in music history, demonstrating the label's importance beyond its initial biggest star. "After Elvis stopped recording for Sun, Sam Phillips and his small staff discovered enough young, exciting musicians that Sun Records was a sure-de-place in music history, even though its biggest artist was gone."

# label - Flip

## curiosities
**Audition Label for Sun Records** : Flip was a new record label established by Sam Phillips as a cost-cutting measure while he faced financial difficulties. Its purpose was to release records, like Carl Perkins's first single "Movie Mag," that did not pay union scale. Distributed only locally around Tennessee, these releases served as "audition records," allowing Phillips to gauge an artist's local performance without incurring full royalty payments, expensive shipping costs, or large print runs. "Rather, he put them out on a new label, Flip, that didn't pay union scale."

# curiosity

**Memphis 706 Union Avenue** : This specific address in Memphis housed Sun Records, the legendary studio where many pioneering rockabilly and early rock and roll artists recorded, playing a pivotal role in the development of these genres. "drove down to Memphis to 706 Union Avenue."
**Marion Keisker** : An employee at Sun Records, Marion Keisker was initially responsible for turning away Carl Perkins and his band when they sought an audition. She informed them that the studio was not auditioning new artists and explicitly stated they did not need another singer who sounded like Elvis Presley. "Marion Kaiske told them that they weren't auditioning right then, and that they didn't need any new singers."
**Sam Phillips' Audition Style** : During Carl Perkins's audition, Sam Phillips initially dismissed the first two songs played. However, he showed keen interest when Perkins performed "Movie Mag," eventually offering a recording opportunity if they returned with more songs in that style. Phillips also made a unilateral decision to exclude Jay Perkins's lead vocals from future recordings, effectively transforming "The Perkins Brothers Band" into "Carl Perkins and his band." "When they got to the end, he said that if they came back with a few more songs that sounded like that, they might just be worth recording."
**7-inch Vinyl Singles Revolution** : The introduction of seven-inch plastic records featuring a large center hole marked a revolutionary shift from the traditional ten-inch shellac records. Initially causing distress and confusion for artists like Carl Perkins, these "new vinyl records" rapidly gained popularity as "what all the kids wanted," signifying a major transformation within the music industry. "Instead of a proper record, a big ten-inch thing with a tiny little hole in the middle made out of shell-ac. He was confronted with something only seven inches across, made of some kind of plastic, and with a big hole in the middle."
**C.V. Wright** : C.V. Wright was a Black serviceman whose casual reference to his "service issue shoes as blue swed shoes" was overheard by Johnny Cash. This phrase was subsequently relayed by Cash to Carl Perkins, serving as the direct inspiration for the immensely popular song "Blue Suede Shoes." "Cash remembered a fellow serviceman, a black man named CV Wright, had referred to his service issue shoes as blue swed shoes."
**1956 Rock and Roll Defining Moment** : The year 1956 is highlighted as a critical juncture for rock and roll. This period saw the simultaneous chart success of Carl Perkins's "Blue Suede Shoes" and Elvis Presley's "Heartbreak Hotel," both cross-over hits that, together, played a pivotal role in defining the future trajectory of the genre. "sums up that point in 1956, when two contrabies from Tennessee were chasing each other up the chart, and defining the future of rock and roll."
