# artist - Solomon Linda

## members
Solomon Linda (leader, writer, lead singer)

## genres
Isekathamia
Zulu music

## labels
Gallo Records

## songs
**In Boube (1939) - Origin of "The Lion Sleeps Tonight"** : This song was written by Solomon Linda and recorded in the group's second session. It means "Lion" and was about hunting lions that fed on his family's cattle during his youth. While there's a dispute over whether it's entirely original or based on a traditional Zulu song, Linda is definitely known for creating the distinctive falsetto melody. It became massively popular, so much so that the master copy disintegrated from repeated pressing. It sold 100,000 copies on Gallo Records. "In Boube, which means Lion, and was about hunting the lions that would feed on his family's cattle when he was growing up."
**Anono Du Gondas (Year not given)** : This was one of many other records made by Linda and The Evening Birds, though "In Boube" remained their biggest hit. "Linda and the evening birds would make many more records, like Anno Du Gondas."

## curiosities
**Zulu Identity in Racist South Africa** : Solomon Linda was a Zulu, which meant he was largely without rights in the racist regime of South Africa during the 1930s and 1940s. "Linda was a Zulu, and thus in the racist regime of South Africa was largely without rights."
**Most Important South African Performer** : In the 1930s and 1940s, Solomon Linda was probably the single most important performer in South Africa, leading the highly popular vocal group The Evening Birds. "Linda was, in the 30s and 40s, probably the single most important performer in South Africa."
**Day Job at Gallo Records** : Linda secured a day job packing records for Gallo Records, the only record label in South Africa which also owned the only recording studio in Sub-Saharan Africa. This connection enabled him to get The Evening Birds signed. "It was because Solomon Linda got a day job packing records for Gallo records, the only record label in South Africa, which owned the only recording studio in Sub-Saharan Africa."
**Exploited for "In Boube" Success** : Despite writing "In Boube," which sold 100,000 copies and gave its name to an entire music genre, Solomon Linda only earned ten shillings for it. This was estimated by the South African government to be a quarter of a week's poverty-level wages for a black family at the time. "It sold 100,000 copies on Gallo records, and earned Solomon Linda, its writer and lead singer, Ten Shillings."
**Death in Poverty** : Solomon Linda died in 1962 with only 100 rand (equivalent to 50 British pounds then) in his bank account, and was buried in an unmarked grave. His song had become an international number one hit record a little over a year before his death, yet he saw none of the millions it earned for others. "When Linda died in 1962, he had a hundred grand, equivalent then to 50 British pounds, in his bank account."
**Posthumous Royalty Battles for Family** : Linda's family received only a couple of thousand dollars a year in royalties from the song for decades, while American copyright owners earned an estimated $15 million. Legal action in 2006 led to an out-of-court settlement with Disney and Aberlin Music, providing Linda's daughters with a lump sum and royalties until 2017, though most of the money went to legal bills. "The surviving sisters were told that the copyright in Inbube should have reverted to them in the 80s, and that they had a very good case under South African law to get a proper share of the rights to both Wimmerway and The Lion sleeps tonight."

# artist - The Evening Birds

## members
Solomon Linda (leader)
Extra bass singers

## genres
Isekathamia

## labels
Gallo Records

## curiosities
**Most Popular Isekathamia Group (1933-1948)** : The Evening Birds, led by Solomon Linda, quickly became the central group in the Isekathamia scene and were the most popular group between 1933 and their split in 1948. "The evening birds quickly became the centre of this scene, and between 1933 and 1948, when they split, they were the most popular group around."
**Innovations in Style and Sound** : The group introduced innovations such as dressing in matching straight suits, a departure from the baggy trousers of older groups. They also incorporated extra bass singers, creating a much thicker bass part which in some ways prefigured the sound of doo-wop that emerged in America two decades later. "The evening birds at Linders' instigation had a much thicker bass part, and in some ways prefigured the sound of dewwap that would take over in America 20 years later."
**Revolving Lineup and Rivalry** : Similar to many doo-wop groups, The Evening Birds experienced a revolving lineup with members joining and leaving to form or join other groups like The Crocodiles and The Dundee Wandering Singers. There was also a competing group named The Evening Birds with a singer who sounded like Linda, leading to a long-running feud. "As with many of the dewwap groups they saw resembled, they had a revolving line up with members coming in going, and joining other groups like the crocodiles and the dundee wandering singers."
**Secured Record Deal through Linda's Job** : The group's recording opportunity with Gallo Records, the only label in South Africa, came about because Solomon Linda had a day job packing records at their factory. This connection enabled him to get the group signed for recording sessions. "But it wasn't this popularity that got the evening birds recorded. It was because Solomon Linda got a day job packing records for Gallo records, the only record label in South Africa."

He had attended Harvard as a classmate of John F. Kennedy."
**Activism and Friendships** : Seeger was friends with both Woody Guthrie and Lead Belly in the 1940s and dedicated his later career to the same kind of left-wing activism that Guthrie had pursued. "He had been friends with both Woody Guthrie and Lead Belly in the 40s, and he dedicated his later career to the same kind of left-wing activism the Guthrie had taken part in."
**Refusal to Testify to HUAC** : Called before the House on American Activities Committee as a past Communist Party member, Seeger took the risky option of refusing to testify on First Amendment grounds. He asserted that his political activities, voting history, and party membership were his private business, spending much of the next decade under threat of prison. "But Seeger took the riskier option of simply refusing on first amendment grounds. He said, quite rightly, that his political activities, voting history, and party membership were nobody's business except his, and he wasn't going to testify about them in front of Congress."
**Blacklisting as Solo Artist** : As a result of his refusal to testify, Seeger, along with The Weavers, was blacklisted from radio and TV as a solo artist. "As a result, the Weevers were blacklisted from Radio and TV, as was Seeger as a solo artist."
**Quit The Weavers Over Tobacco Commercial** : Driven by his strong principles against tobacco and advertising, Seeger quit The Weavers when they accepted an offer to do a commercial for tobacco. Despite his disapproval, he honored a prior commitment and appeared in the commercial. "But Seeger didn't approve of tobacco or advertising, and quit the group because of it. Though, because he'd made a commitment to the group, he did appear on the commercial, not wanting to break his word."
**Efforts to Compensate Solomon Linda** : Upon discovering that Solomon Linda, not tradition, was the author of "In Boube," Seeger insisted that all future money he would have made from "Wimoweh" go to Linda and sent Linda a check for a thousand dollars for money already earned. However, his assumption that his colleagues would honor the agreement proved false, and Linda received nothing from them. "As it turned out, he didn't. When he discovered that Solomon Linda had written the song, that it wasn't just a traditional song, he insisted that all future money he would have made from it go to Linda, and sent Linda a check for a thousand dollars for the money he'd already earned."
**Frugal Life and Philanthropy** : Seeger was known for not caring much about money, donating the vast majority of his earnings to worthy causes and living frugally. "But Seeger was someone who didn't care much about money at all. He donated the vast majority of his money to worthy causes, and lived frugally."
**Second Payment to Linda's Daughters** : Just before Ryan Mulan's article exposed the disparity in royalties, Seeger received a $6,000 payment when a commercial used "Wimoweh." Realizing he had been receiving royalties for "Wimoweh" all along, despite his earlier instructions, he calculated his total earnings from the song, which amounted to $12,000, and sent a check for that sum to Linda's daughters. "Pete T. Gennotis to $6,000 payment, which came to him when a commercial used Wimmerway, rather than The Lion sleeps tonight."

# genre - Isekathamia

## curiosities
**Origins in Forced Displacement** : This music form was created when rural black people in South Africa were forcibly displaced in the late 19th century and compelled to find work in cities. "it's a music created when rural black people were forcibly displaced in the late 19th century and forced to find work in the city."
**Fusion of Musical Elements** : Isekathamia combines elements of traditional Zulu music with two Western influences: religious music heard from church missions, and American minstrel songs, particularly those performed by troupes led by Orfius Macadoo that toured South Africa in the late 19th century. "Those people combined elements of traditional Zulu music with two more Western elements. The first was the religious music that they heard from church missions, and the second was American minstrel songs."
**A Cappella Performance and Formalized Structure** : This style of music was typically performed a cappella, though sometimes a single instrument might be added. It developed a relatively formalized structure with specific parts for tenor, soprano, alto, and bass, adhering to strict four-part harmony common in European choral music. The soprano and alto parts were sung in falsetto by men. "This new style of music was usually performed at Capella, though sometimes there might be a single instrument added, and it gained a relatively formalised structure."
**Western Chord Progressions and Zulu Language Adaptation** : Isekathamia music usually centered around the common one, four, and five chords found in most Western popular music. The Zulu language lyrics were often distorted to fit Western meters, although the music maintained a more freeform quality compared to much of the Western music of that era. "It would usually be based around the same one four and five chords, that most Western popular music was based on, and the Zulu language would often be distorted to fit western metres, though the music was still more freeform than most of the Western music of the time."
**Early Recording Era** : This musical style began to be recorded around 1930, with examples like "Umtetto, Wheel and Act" by Coluza's Double Quartet and "Jim Ticata Kanjami" by The Gleesingers. "This music started to be recorded in around 1930, and you can get an idea of the stylistic range from two examples."

# genre - In Boube

## curiosities
**Genre Named After a Song** : The genre of In Boube music was directly named after Solomon Linda's massively popular song, "In Boube." This mirrored how late 1950s American vocal groups were called doo-wop groups. "It gave its name to a whole genre of music."
**Lasting Legacy** : More than 80 years after its origin, South African groups like Ladysmith Black Mambazo are still recognized and referred to as In Boube groups, demonstrating the enduring impact of the genre's namesake. "South African groups like Ladiesmith Black Mambazo are, more than 80 years later, still known as In Boube groups."

# label - Gallo Records

## curiosities
**Dominant South African Record Label** : At the time Solomon Linda was active, Gallo Records was the sole record label in South Africa and owned the only recording studio in Sub-Saharan Africa, giving it a monopolistic position in the region's music industry. "Gallo records, the only record label in South Africa, which owned the only recording studio in Sub-Saharan Africa."
**Mismanagement of "In Boube" Copyright** : After Folkways copyrighted "Wimoweh" in the USA, Gallo realized its mistake in not registering "Ember Bay" (In Boube) under Solomon Linda's name there. They were later pressured into a hugely disadvantageous agreement with Folkways, effectively surrendering global copyright to the song in exchange for a modest one-off fee and rights in only a few African territories. "Gallo had, without realizing it, given up their legal rights to the song throughout the world."

# label - Folkways

## curiosities
**Folk Music Promotion and Corporate Affiliation** : Folkways was a record company specifically established to promote folk music, but it operated as part of a much larger conglomerate, the Richmond Organization. "Folkways, a company that was set up to promote Folk Music, but was part of a much bigger conglomerate, the Richmond organisation."
**Initial Copyright of "Wimoweh"** : When The Weavers decided to record "Wimoweh," Folkways contacted Gallo Records and was mistakenly informed that "Ember Bay" was a traditional song. Folkways then copyrighted "Ember Bay" as "Wimoweh" in the USA under the collective pseudonym Paul Campbell, preempting Gallo's later attempt to copyright it under Solomon Linda's name. "Folkways copyrighted Ember Bay as Wimmer Way, in the name Paul Campbell, a collective pseudonym that the Weevers used for their arrangements of traditional songs."
**Acquisition of Global Copyright** : Folkways, seeking global rights, secured an agreement with Gallo Records that proved highly disadvantageous to Gallo. Folkways paid a modest one-off fee and allowed Gallo rights in a few African territories, while Folkways gained the copyright everywhere else. "Because they agreed that they would pay Gallow a modest one-off fee, and let Gallow have the rights to the song in a few territories in Africa, and in return Folkways would get the copyright everywhere else."

# label - Richmond Organization

## curiosities
**Conglomerate Owner of Folkways** : The Richmond Organization was a much larger conglomerate that owned Folkways, the company responsible for promoting folk music and signing The Weavers. "Folkways... was part of a much bigger conglomerate, the Richmond organisation."
**Publisher of "Wimoweh" and "The Lion Sleeps Tonight"** : The Richmond Organization acted as the publisher for "Wimoweh." Later, in an amicable agreement, they received the publishing money for "The Lion Sleeps Tonight" while the credited songwriters (Weiss, Hugo, Luigi) retained songwriting royalties. "But a Richmond organisation would get the publishing money."
**Legal Battle Over Copyright Renewal** : In 1989, when George Weiss sought to renew the copyright for "The Lion Sleeps Tonight" and exclude the Richmond Organization from publishing, they took him to court. They argued that the song was based on "Wimoweh," which they owned. Although the court sided with Weiss, it acknowledged a point regarding the song's similarity to "Wimoweh," leading to a small payment to Solomon Linda's family. "As soon as the Richmond Organization heard about this, they took Weiss to court, saying that he couldn't take the publishing rights away from them, because the song was based on Wimaway, which they owned."

# curiosity

**Orfius Macadoo - Minstrel and Gospel Performer** : Orfius Macadoo was a black performer who led a troupe of minstrel and gospel performers, touring South Africa extensively in the late 19th century. His performances significantly influenced the development of Isekathamia music among forcibly displaced rural black communities. "a black performer named Orfius Macadoo, who led a trooper of minstrel and gospel performers who toured South Africa a lot in the late 19th century."
**Copyright Laws in 1950s USA** : In the 1950s, the USA had not yet signed the Berne Convention, the international agreement on copyright laws. This meant that US copyright was independent, and the first entity to register a song in the USA held the legal rights there, regardless of its copyright status in other countries like South Africa. "Now, in the 1950s, the USA was not yet a signatory to the Burn Convention, the international agreements on copyright laws, and so it made no difference that in South Africa, the song had been copyrighted under Lindner's name."
**McCarthy Era Blacklisting Impact** : During the McCarthy era, Pete Seeger and Lee Hayes of The Weavers were identified as former Communist Party members and summoned before the House on American Activities Committee. Their refusal to testify led to The Weavers and Seeger as a solo artist being blacklisted from radio and television, causing their song "Wimoweh" to drop from the charts and their catalogue to be deleted. "Both Pete Seeger and Lee Hayes had been named as past Communist Party members, and were called before the House on American Activities Committee to testify."

**Ryan Mulan's Investigation (2000)** : Ryan Mulan, a South African journalist based in America, dedicated his work to expunging his "ancestral guilt" as a relative of key apartheid figures Daniel Mulan and Magnus Mulan. In 2000, he exposed that Solomon Linda's daughters received only a couple of thousand dollars annually from the song, while its American copyright owners had generated an estimated $15 million. "In 2000, Ryan Mulan, a South African journalist based in America... found out that while Solomon Lenders' family had been getting some money, it amounted at most to a couple of thousand dollars a year, shared between Lenders daughters."
**Solomon Linda's Daughter Dies in Poverty (2001)** : In 2001, one of Solomon Linda's four daughters tragically died of AIDS, a disease that would have been treatable if she had been able to afford the necessary anti-retroviral medication, underscoring the severe poverty the family endured despite the immense success of their father's song. "Those daughters were living in such poverty that in 2001, one of the four died of AIDS, a disease which would have been completely treatable if she'd been able to afford the anti-retroviral medication to treat it."
**Disney's Out-of-Court Settlement (2006)** : Following legal advice that they had a strong copyright infringement case against Disney (due to the film's use of the song and Disney's assets in South Africa), Linda's surviving sisters entered an out-of-court settlement with Disney in 2006. This agreement secured a lump sum payment into a trust and royalties on "The Lion Sleeps Tonight" for the daughters until 2017, even after the South African copyright expired. "So in 2006, Disney came to an out-of-court settlement with Linda's family, and they appeared to have pressured Aberlin music to do the same thing."
**Remake of The Lion King (2017)** : In 2017, the year the settlement with Linda's family expired, Disney announced a photo-realistic CGI remake of "The Lion King." This new version also featured "The Lion Sleeps Tonight" and went on to become the most successful animated film of all time. "In 2017, the year the agreement expired, Disney announced they were making a photo-realistic CGI remake of The Lion King."
**Evolution of US Copyright Law** : American copyright law has undergone several changes. For songs written before 1978 and subsequent "Mickey Mouse copyright extensions," the rule was an initial 28-year copyright term, renewable by the writer for a second 28-year term. Currently, songs published in America remain in copyright until 70 years after the writer's death. "American copyright law has had several changes over the years, and nothing of what I'm saying applies now, but for songs written before 1978... the rule used to be that a song would be in copyright for 28 years."
**"He's So Fine" Authorial Dispute** : The Chiffons' hit song "He's So Fine," which was produced by The Tokens, later became the subject of its own prominent authorial dispute case, illustrating the complexities and controversies often found in music copyright. "That song would of course generate its own famous authorial dispute case in later years."
