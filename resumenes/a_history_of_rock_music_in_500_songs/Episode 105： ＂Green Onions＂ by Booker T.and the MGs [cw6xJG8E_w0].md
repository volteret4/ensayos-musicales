# artist - Jim Stewart

## member of
Western swing band

## labels
Satellite Records
Stax Records

## curiosities
**Early Life and Influences** : Jim Stewart was a part-time fiddle player deeply influenced by Bob Wills' western swing music. Despite his musical passion, he worked a day job at a bank as music wasn't providing a stable income. "Stewart was in a western swing band and was hugely influenced by Bbock Will's, but he wasn't making any real money from music."
**Witnessing Elvis's Rise** : Stewart had been part of a house band at a venue where Elvis Presley played in his early career. He observed Elvis's rapid ascent from an obscure local artist to a global superstar, which fueled his attraction to the music industry. "One of the gigs he'd had was in the house band at a venue where Elvis sometimes played in his early years, and he'd seen how Elvis had gone from an obscure local boy all the way to the biggest star in the world."
**Barber's Business Advice** : A tip from his barber, Irwin Ellis, about the lucrative nature of owning song publishing rights, rather than just selling records, significantly influenced Stewart's approach to the music business. This financial insight appealed to him given his background in banking. "Alice explained to Stuart that he was still getting checks every few months, because he owned the publishing for this song that someone else had written and wrote to him."
**Founding Satellite Records** : Stewart pooled savings with a singing DJ and a rhythm guitarist, raising $1,000 to release a record. They named their label Satellite Records, a name nobody liked but was chosen due to Sputnik's recent launch. "They came up with the name satellite records for their label. Nobody liked it, but they couldn't think of anything better, and satellites were in the news with the recent launch of Sputnik."
**Wife's Uncle's Garage Studio** : The initial recordings for Satellite Records, including Fred Byler's "Blue Roses," took place in Jim's wife's uncle's garage using Irwin Ellis's tape recorder. This makeshift studio ceased to be available when Ellis moved and the uncle needed his garage back. "they hired Alice in his tape recorder to record it in Jim's wife's uncle's garage."
**Restarting Satellite Records** : After initial failures and setbacks, Stewart convinced his sister Estelle Axton to buy out his business partners. With Axton's financial backing from remortgaging her house, and Chips Moman's studio expertise, Satellite Records was re-launched in a barber's storage space. "Stuart Mice persuaded his sister, Estelle Axton, to buy out his business partners."
**Stax Records Name Origin** : When Satellite Records faced a trademark conflict with another label of the same name, Jim Stewart, who disliked the original name anyway, decided to rename the label. The new name, Stax Records, was created using the first two letters of his surname (Stewart) and his sister Estelle Axton's surname. "Jim Stewart had never liked satellite as a name anyway, and so they quickly reissued the record with a new label, named after the first letters of Jim Stewart and Asterl Axton's surnames. Stax Records was born."
**"What'd I Say" Damascene Conversion** : Jim Stewart experienced a profound shift in his musical direction after hearing Ray Charles's "What'd I Say," produced by Jerry Wexler. This record solidified his commitment to an R&B style for his company. "He'd recently had a sort of damacine conversion after hearing that record, and was now fully committed to his company's new R&B style."
**Conflict with Chips Moman** : Jim Stewart was involved in a dispute with Chips Moman, who claimed he had been promised a third of the company but Stewart refused. This disagreement led to Moman's departure from Stax Records. "he also claims that he had been promised a third of the company, but Jim Stewart changed his mind and refused to cut him in."
**Reluctant Release of Otis Redding's Debut** : Jim Stewart was initially unimpressed by Otis Redding's first recording session, describing the A-side as "a fun but unoriginal little Richard sound alike." He only agreed to release the record as a favor to promoter Joe Galkin, not anticipating the B-side would become a hit. "Jim Stewart wasn't particularly impressed with the results, but he owed Galkin a favour, so he released the record."

## songs
**Blue Roses (Year Unknown)** : This was the only song Jim Stewart ever wrote. It was sung by Fred Byler and was the first release on Stewart's Satellite Records label, recorded in Jim's wife's uncle's garage. "Stuart wrote a song, the only song he'd ever write."

## curiosities
**Tiny Record Label Owner** : Irwin Ellis, Jim Stewart's barber, was also a country fiddle player and owned a minuscule record label called Irwin Records. The label was so small that its first release, by Ellis himself, is believed to be entirely lost. "Irwin Records was a tiny label. It was so tiny that its first release, by Ellis himself, seems not to exist anywhere."
**Music Business Mentor** : Ellis, despite his own limited success, was instrumental in educating Jim Stewart about the economics of the music industry. He explained that the real money came from owning publishing rights rather than just selling records, influencing Stewart's business model for Satellite/Stax. "He told Stuart that you didn't make money from the records themselves. Small labels didn't sell much, but that he was making some good money from their songs."

# artist - Estelle Axton

## labels
Satellite Records
Stax Records

## curiosities
**Business Acumen and Music Passion** : Estelle Axton, Jim Stewart's sister, was naturally business-minded and had a keen interest in music. She would buy records from wholesalers for 76 cents and sell them to her colleagues for a dollar, demonstrating her entrepreneurial spirit. "Estelle was a naturally business-minded person, who also had a year learning to do something involving music, and had been doing things in little ways."
**Remortgaging for Equipment** : Estelle Axton, against her husband's better judgment, remortgaged their house to secure the funds needed to purchase recording equipment, which was then set up in a barber's storage space to restart Satellite Records. "Estelle persuaded her husband against his better judgement, to remogge their house, and she used the money to buy recording equipment."
**Stax Records Name Origin** : Estelle Axton's surname was combined with Jim Stewart's surname to create the name Stax Records when Satellite Records was renamed. "Stax Records was born."
**Record Shop Owner and Market Researcher** : Estelle Axton transformed the lobby and concession stand of the old cinema that became the Stax studio into a record shop, which she personally managed. She allowed customers to listen to music all day without buying and implemented a loyalty card scheme, allowing her to understand the buying habits of black teenagers and inform the studio's production choices. "A stalaxe and turned that into a record shop which she ran herself, with Cropper often helping out behind the counter."
**Boosting "Last Night"** : Despite Jim Stewart and Jerry Wexler's belief that The Mar-Keys' instrumental "Last Night" would be a flop, Estelle Axton championed its potential. She secured a test pressing for a DJ, and seeing the public's demand, eventually pressured Stewart to release it. "But Asterl Axton believed in its potential, and also believed in her son, who Stewart had little time for."
**Conflict with Chips Moman** : Estelle Axton had disagreements with Chips Moman, which was one of the reasons cited for Moman's eventual departure from Stax Records. "He'd not been getting on with the Stella Axton"

## curiosities
**Early Career and Mentorship** : As a teenage guitarist, Chips Moman was discovered by Sun Records artist Warren Smith while playing at a Memphis drugstore. Smith offered him a job, kickstarting Moman's career in music. "Chip's was a teenage guitarist who had been playing a friend's guitar at a drugstore in Memphis, just hanging around after work, when Warren Smith worked him."
**California Studio Experience** : Moman moved to California with Johnny and Dorsie Bennett, working as a session player at Gold Star Studios. This experience provided him with valuable studio techniques that he later applied back in Memphis. "He went with the Bennett to California, where he was a session player for a time, though I've never been able to find a list of any of the records he played on, just people saying he played at Gold Star Studios."
**Return to Memphis and Studio Expertise** : An accident led Moman to return to Memphis, where he applied his Californian studio techniques to produce Don Willis's "But in High School Baby." His eagerness and knowledge made him a key figure in the revitalized Satellite Records. "He'd played guitar on the Don Woolest session, and he'd essentially produced it, applying some of the techniques he'd learned in Californian studios."
**Departure from Stax** : Chips Moman left Stax Records following disagreements with Estelle Axton and claims that Jim Stewart reneged on a promise to give him a third of the company. After leaving, he moved to Nashville briefly before founding American Sound Studio in another part of Memphis. "Everyone has a different story about what happened, but the upshot was that Momen left the company, went to Nashville for a while, and then founded his own studio, America, in another part of Memphis."
**Musical Contributions After Stax** : Post-Stax, Moman became a prolific songwriter and producer, creating a string of soul, country, and pop classics. "Momen would become responsible for writing and producing a whole string of Soul, Country, and Mark Classics"

## songs
**Last Night (1961)** : Chips Moman received songwriting credit for this instrumental hit by The Mar-Keys because he suggested combining the separately written keyboard and horn lines. He also produced the several sessions where the song was recorded, often with different lineups. "Chip's moment had suggested combining the keyboard and horn lines, and so he got songwriting credit as well."
**Burnt Biscuit (Year Unknown)** : Moman wrote this instrumental track, which was the first single released on Stax's subsidiary label, Bolt. Released under the name The Triumphs, after Moman's sports car, he believed it had the potential to be as big as "Last Night," though it wasn't a hit. "Chips Momen wrote, burnt biscuit, by a group consisting of Momen, Rufus Thomas's son Mavelle, Louis Steinberg, and Howard Grames."

# artist - Charles Axton

## member of
The Royal Spades
The Mar-Keys

## instruments
Tenor sax

## curiosities
**Controversial Nickname** : Charles Axton, Estelle Axton's son and Jim Stewart's nephew, was known by a nickname that, while harmless in 1950s US, later became an offensive racist slur in the UK against people of Pakistani descent. For historical accuracy, the transcript refers to him as "Charles." "Normally, I refer to people by the name they chose to go by, but in his case he was known by a nickname which was harmless in that time and place, but later became an extremely offensive racist slur in the UK, used against people of Pakistani descent."
**Joining The Royal Spades** : Charles Axton, despite only having played tenor saxophone for a couple of weeks, managed to join The Royal Spades by leveraging his family's ownership of a record label. He also suggested expanding the guitar-based drum band with a horn section. "They'd laughed when he told them that he'd only been playing a couple of weeks, but once he explained that his mother and uncle owned a record label, he was in the group"
**Studio Refurbishment** : Charles Axton contributed to the conversion of the old cinema on East Maclamore Avenue into the new Stax recording studio. He, along with Steve Cropper, assisted with the refurbishment efforts. "Cropper and Charles Axton helped with the refurbishing of the cinema into a recording studio."
**Songwriting Credit for "Last Night"** : Charles Axton received songwriting credit for The Mar-Keys' hit "Last Night" primarily because his family owned the record label, even though his actual contribution to the song was limited to playing on the record. "And Charles Axton didn't contribute anything to the song other than playing on the record, but because his family owned the record label, he got credit as well."
**Leadership Conflict and Tour Behavior** : Charles Axton believed himself to be a co-leader of The Mar-Keys, leading to conflicts with Steve Cropper. During the tour to promote "Last Night," Charles and the other teenage band members, many away from home for the first time, behaved like "wild animals," contributing to Cropper's decision to quit the tour. "Kropper and Charles Axton both believed themselves to be the band's leader, and that the other should obey them."

**Studio and Record Shop Contributions** : Cropper assisted in refurbishing the cinema into the Stax recording studio and worked in Estelle Axton's record shop, often helping behind the counter. This dual role made him readily available for studio sessions. "Cropper and Charles Axton helped with the refurbishing of the cinema into a recording studio."
**"Last Night" Authorship Theory** : Steve Cropper attributed the success of The Mar-Keys' instrumental "Last Night" to its danceability, specifically its suitability for the twist craze. "Steve Krupper always argued that it was because you could dance the twist to it, and so it wrote the twist craze"
**Quitting The Mar-Keys Tour** : Cropper left The Mar-Keys' tour to promote "Last Night" due to resentment over the other teenage band members' unruly behavior and a leadership conflict between himself and Charles Axton. He returned to Memphis to work in the record shop and play on Stax sessions. "Kropper soon got tired of the tour, quit the group, and came back to Memphis."
**Producer Role Post-Moman** : After Chips Moman's departure from Stax, Steve Cropper shared the production duties with Jim Stewart. Cropper had been diligently learning how to make records and was well-equipped to take on this responsibility. "Jim Stewart and Steve Cropper split the production duties that Momen had had between them."
**Songwriting Credits** : Steve Cropper co-wrote several influential soul classics, including "In the Midnight Hour," "Dock of the Bay," and "Knock on Wood," showcasing his significant talent as a songwriter for Stax. "even the man who co-wrote in the midnight hour, Doc of the Bay, and Knock on Ward, had his off days."
**Unsuccessful Country Production** : Steve Cropper produced "The Three Dogwoods" by Nick Charles, a novelty country song told from the perspective of the tree used for Jesus's crucifixion. This record was a commercial failure, demonstrating that even prolific songwriters like Cropper had "off days." "That was Covid and by Cropper, which shows that even the man who co-wrote in the midnight hour, Doc of the Bay, and Knock on Ward, had his off days."
**Origins of "Green Onions"** : Cropper remembered a distinct musical lick he and Booker T. Jones had heard on the radio, but couldn't identify the song. This lick became the foundation for "Green Onions," which they improvised into a 12-bar instrumental during an unplanned session. "Cropper remembered a little musical lick he'd heard on the radio one day when he'd been driving with Booker."
**Reasons for Duck Dunn Replacing Louis Steinberg** : Cropper stated that Louis Steinberg was replaced by Duck Dunn in Booker T and the M.G.'s because Dunn's "hard-eared style" better suited their music. He also mentioned that Dunn was his best friend and he wanted to play more with him, implicitly downplaying Steinberg's claim of racial motivation. "Krupper has always said, he was because Duck Dunn had a hard-eared style that fit their music better than Steinberg's Lucifer Hill, but also that Dunn had been his best friend for years and he wanted to play more with him."

Jim Stewart had to direct the string players to perform a "head arrangement," giving them simplified instructions like "just play doughnuts" (meaning semi-breves or whole notes). "Stewart had to make the string players play from a head arrangement, something string players normally never do, and ended up giving them directions like just play doughnuts"

**Name Change from The Royal Spades** : The group, originally known as The Royal Spades, was renamed The Mar-Keys by Estelle Axton, who found their original name "inappropriate." The new name was a combination of "marquee" (as in the noble title) and "keys" (as in keyboards). "Upon our marquee, as in the noble title, and keys as in keyboards, as Estelle Axton thought, entirely correctly, that their original name was inappropriate."
**Developing a Distinctive Sound** : The Mar-Keys were credited with helping Satellite Records develop the distinctive sound the label was seeking. "It was the marquees, who finally gave satellite the distinctive sound they were looking for"
**Touring Resentment** : The Mar-Keys' tour to promote "Last Night" caused significant resentment among the black session players who had contributed to the record. These players felt the instrumental was primarily their work but that "a bunch of white boys were" receiving all the recognition. "which brought resentment from the Black Session players, some of whom claimed that during the session it hadn't even been intended as a marquee's record, and who have annoyed that even though the record was primarily their work, they weren't getting the recognition, and a bunch of white boys were."
**Recording Second Single Without Them** : While The Mar-Keys were on tour, Stax studio musicians recorded the group's second single without their involvement. The band members only discovered its release when they saw it in the record shop. "The second marquee's single was recorded by the studio musicians while the group were out on tour. The first they even knew about it was when they saw it in the shop."

## songs
**Last Night (1961)** : This instrumental became a big hit for Satellite Records, reaching number three on the pop chart and number two on the R&B chart. Its authorship was debated, with credit given to session sax players Caples and Newman (horn part), Smoochie Smith (keyboard part), Chips Moman (combining ideas), and Charles Axton (family ownership). The first section of the original tape was erased, requiring a meticulous splice from another take. "The record became a big hit, and there were a couple of hypotheses as to why."

**Publishing Rights Business Model** : Irwin Ellis taught Jim Stewart that the real money in the music business for small labels came not from selling records (which were just "bits of plastic" that cost money to produce), but from owning the publishing rights to songs. This meant receiving royalties every time a song was played on the radio, or more significantly, if a big star covered it, without any additional outlay. **Decline of Indoor Cinemas** : In the late 1950s, many indoor cinemas were closing down due to the combined impact of television and the rise of drive-in theaters. Additionally, white flight to the suburbs meant fewer people with disposable income lived within walking distance of inner-city cinemas, leaving many buildings abandoned and available cheaply. "A lot of cinemas were closing down in the late 50s, due to the combination of television and the drive-in, making indoor cinemas less appealing, and because white flak to the suburbs went that people with money no longer lived in walking distance of cinemas the way they used to."
