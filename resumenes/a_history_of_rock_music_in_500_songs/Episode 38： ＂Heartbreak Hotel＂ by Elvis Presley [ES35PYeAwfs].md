## albums
**First Album (1956)** : Recorded after the "Heartbreak Hotel" sessions, consisting mostly of R&B and Rockabilly covers. The material was almost entirely Elvis's own choice, resulting in "fine performances and perfect for album tracks." "Almost all of these were Elvis's own choice of material, and so while his versions of Blue Swade's shoes, or Tuti Fruity, didn't match the quality of the originals, they were fine performances and perfect for album tracks."

## songs
**I Forgot to Remember to Forget (1956)** : Reissued by RCA Records at the beginning of 1956 as his last Sun single, backed with "Mystery Train." With RCA's backing, the single performed significantly better than its original Sun release, hitting number one on the country charts. "With RCA's backing, the single did far better than it had on Sun, hitting number one on the country charts at the beginning of 1956."
**Mystery Train (1956)** : Released as the B-side to "I Forgot to Remember to Forget" when RCA Records reissued the single.
**Heartbreak Hotel (1956)** : Chosen by Elvis for his first RCA session, this song was initially conceived by Tommy Durden (inspired by a newspaper story about a man, possibly Alvin Crowley, who died by suicide or was shot during a robbery), with Mae Axton contributing the iconic "heartbreak hotel" phrase. Glenn Reeves helped finish it and recorded the demo but refused credit. Elvis instantly loved the song, which reminded him of Roy Brown's "Hard-Luck Blues," and Mae Axton insisted it would be his first million-seller. Despite criticisms about its reverb sound compared to Sun Records' slapback, it became a smash hit, reaching number one on the Pop and Country charts and the top five in R&B, marking his transformation into "the King of Rock and Roll." "He instantly fell in love with the song, which reminded the young blues lover of Roy Brown's hard-look blues."
**I Got a Woman (1956) - Ray Charles Cover** : A quick version of this Ray Charles song was recorded at the very beginning of the "Heartbreak Hotel" session. It had been a staple of Elvis's live act since its release. "To start the session off, they recorded a quick version of Agata Woman, the Ray Charles song, which had been a staple of Elvis's live act since it had been released."
**Money Honey (1956)** : A cover version that was a staple of Elvis's stage show. It was recorded in a three-hour session following "Heartbreak Hotel" and marked the very first time backing vocalists were used on an Elvis recording. "After recording Heartbreak Hotel, they took a break before spending another three hour session recording another arm and beak hover that was a staple of Elvis's stage show, Money Honey."
**I'm Counting on You (1956)** : One of two ballads recorded to conclude the initial RCA sessions. It was described as "rather mediocre." "And counting on you was rather mediocre."
**I Was The One (1956)** : Recorded at the end of the initial RCA sessions, this ballad became Elvis's "personal favorite track" from those recording dates. "I was the one ended up being Elvis's personal favorite track from the sessions."
**Blue Suede Shoes (1956)** : Elvis's cover version of this song was chosen by him for his first album and was described as a "fine performance" suitable for an album track, even if it didn't match the quality of the original. "while his versions of Blue Swade's shoes, or Tuti Fruity, didn't match the quality of the originals, they were fine performances and perfect for album tracks."
**Tutti Frutti (1956)** : Elvis's cover version of this song was chosen by him for his first album and was described as a "fine performance" suitable for an album track, even if it didn't match the quality of the original. "while his versions of Blue Swade's shoes, or Tuti Fruity, didn't match the quality of the originals, they were fine performances and perfect for album tracks."
**I Want You, I Need You, I Love You (1956)** : This song, a suggestion from Steve Sholes for Elvis's fourth RCA session, proved disastrous as Elvis had no feel for it and struggled to learn it, leading to a session where no usable take was recorded. Steve Sholes had to cobble together a take from two different performances. Despite the recording difficulties, it reached number one on the country chart and number three on the pop charts. "But the session went terribly, because Elvis had no feel for the song at all."
**My Baby Left Me (1956)** : This was the B-side of the single "I Want You, I Need You, I Love You," and was a leftover track from earlier album sessions. It was a cover version of an Arthur Crudup song, similar to Elvis's first single, and offered DJ Fontana his first notable opportunity to showcase his talent. "It was the B side of that single that was really worth listening to."
**Hound Dog (1956)** : A cover version of Big Mama Thornton's song, chosen by Elvis for what would become his biggest double-sided hit, though it bore almost no relation to Thornton's original. It took 31 takes to record and, combined with "Don't Be Cruel," spent 11 weeks at the top of the charts, reaching number 2 before swapping with "Don't Be Cruel" to hit number 1. "These days, their cover version of Big Mama Thunton's Hound Dog is the better known of the two sides they cut that day, but while that's an excellent track, and one that bears almost no relation to Thunton's original."
**Don't Be Cruel (1956)** : The A-side of Elvis's biggest double-sided hit, definitely chosen by Elvis, and written by Otis Blackwell (credited to Blackwell and Presley due to Colonel Parker's insistence). This song showed a new "lightness of touch" in Elvis's performance, as he was finally in control of the studio sound. It took 28 takes, but resulted in a "light, fluffy sound" where Elvis sounded playful. It was the song that convinced several detractors, including Sam Phillips, of Elvis's ability to make good records away from Sun. It reached number 1 on the charts, then number 2, and along with "Hound Dog," spent 11 weeks at the top. "Elvis's version showed a lightness of touch that had been absent on his earlier RCA records."

## curiosities
**King of Rock and Roll Designation** : Elvis Presley transitioned from a minor regional country star to "the King of Rock and Roll" and a "cultural phenomenon" upon signing with RCA Records and releasing his first major label single, "Heartbreak Hotel." "He didn't become the Elvis wheel now, until he started recording for RCA."
**John Lennon's Quote on Influence** : John Lennon famously stated, "before Elvis there was nothing," reflecting the profound cultural impact Elvis Presley had, eclipsing all music prior to him. "lead John Lennon to say before Elvis there was nothing."
**Unprecedented RCA Signing Fee (1955)** : Colonel Tom Parker secured Elvis's signing to RCA Records for a sum "far greater than anything anyone had paid for a single before," after Sam Phillips made a "ludicrous demand" to release him from Sun Records. This deal heavily funded the entire rockabilly genre. "Elvis's manager, Colonel Tom Parker, had managed to get Elvis signed to RCA records for a sum of money far greater than anything anyone had paid for a single before."
**21st Birthday Session (1956)** : Elvis entered the studio on January 10th, 1956, two days after his 21st birthday, under immense pressure to record something extraordinary for RCA. "When Elvis went into the studio on January 10th, 1956, two days after his 21st birthday, the pressure was on him to record something very special indeed."
**Parental Dedication and Songwriting Credit** : Mae Axton was deeply moved by Elvis's desire to earn money to bring his parents to live in Florida, promising to write him a song. She later stated she cut him in on the songwriting credit for "Heartbreak Hotel" for this specific reason, though others attributed it to Colonel Parker's insistence. "She said later, that just went through my heart, because I looked down there, and there were all these other kids, different show members for that night, all the guys looking for cute little girls. But his priority was doing something for his mother and daddy."
**Phrasing and Demos** : While Elvis often found it easier to record songs after hearing a demo and would sometimes copy phrasing exactly in the 1960s, he made "dozens of tiny choices" and was "thinking hard about the choices he was making" when recording "Heartbreak Hotel," not merely copying Glenn Reeves' demo. "There are dozens of tiny choices Elvis makes throughout the song, which differ from those made by Reeves, and it's clear that Elvis was thinking hard about the choices he was making."
**Initial Career Ambition: Gospel Quartet** : Elvis's earliest ambition, even before meeting Sam Phillips, was to be a member of a gospel quartet, as he loved to sing harmony. This foundational aspiration influenced his increasing use of backing vocalists throughout his career. "Elvis's first ambition, the aspiration he had right at the beginning of his career, was to be a member of a gospel quartet."
**Insistence on The Jordanaires** : After discovering that Steve Sholes had used only Gordon Stoker of The Jordanaires along with members of The Speer Family for backing vocals in initial RCA sessions as a cost-cutting measure, Elvis insisted that all members of The Jordanaires be employed for all his future sessions. "When Elvis found out at a later session that this had happened as a cost-cutting measure, he insisted that all the Jordanaires be employed at his future sessions."
**Studio Perfectionism vs. Session Efficiency** : Elvis was a perfectionist in the studio, often insisting on re-takes even when producer Steve Sholes felt a version was releasable. This, combined with being accustomed to Sun Studios' laid-back atmosphere, resulted in only five usable tracks from three sessions in two days, far fewer than the typical four songs per session. "Several times they'd recorded a take that Scholes had felt would be good enough to release, but Elvis had insisted he could do it better."
**First Album Material Selection** : For his first album, Elvis mostly recorded R&B and Rockabilly covers, and almost all of this material was chosen by him, contrasting with later sessions where material was imposed. "Almost all of these were Elvis's own choice of material."
**TV Appearances and Controversy (1956)** : Elvis's rapid ascent through TV shows (Stage Show, Milton Berle, Steve Allen, Ed Sullivan) fueled the success of "Heartbreak Hotel." His appearances generated significant controversy, from his hip gyrations leading to him being branded a "Marvel Menace," to being forced to sing standing still, and eventually being filmed only from the waist up. "Each show caused more controversy than the last, as first Elvis's hip-gyrrations got him branded a Marvel Menace, then he was first to sing while standing still, and then only filmed from the waist up."
**Colonel Parker's TV Strategy** : Colonel Tom Parker decided to limit Elvis's TV appearances, reasoning that if people could see him for free on TV, they wouldn't pay to see him live or in films. This led to a strategic shift towards a film career. "If people could see him without paying, why would they pay to see him?"
**Disastrous Fourth RCA Session (1956)** : Due to being too busy promoting "Heartbreak Hotel" and lacking prepared material, Elvis's fourth RCA session was largely a disaster. He struggled with Steve Sholes' suggested song, "I Want You, I Need You, I Love You," resulting in no usable take despite a whole session dedicated to it. "Elvis had been so busy promoting heartbreak hotel that he hadn't had any chance to prepare material, and so he just went with Steve Scholl's suggestion of, I want you, I need you, I love you."
**Control over Material Impact** : It became evident that Elvis produced "great things" when given control of the studio and material he connected with, but was "much less successful" when performing what others thought he should do. "By this point, it was very clear that if Elvis was given control of the studio and singing material he connected with, he would produce great things."
**Initial Film Career Expectations (1956)** : When signing for his first film, originally "Doreen O'Brothers" (later "Love Me Tender"), Elvis expected a straight acting role with no songs, aspiring to emulate parallel careers like Frank Sinatra or acting idols Marlon Brando and James Dean. "He wanted to follow the path of people like Frank Sinatra, who had parallel careers in the cinema and in music, and he also hoped that he could emulate his acting idols, Marlon Vando and James Dean."
**Film Company Interference in Music** : For his first film, Elvis was annoyed that songs were added to the script, and his regular band (Scotty Moore, Bill Black, DJ Fontana) was not allowed to play on the soundtrack. They were replaced by Hollywood session musicians whom the film company believed could "sound hillbilly enough." "he found out to his annoyance that he wasn't allowed to use Scotty Bill and DJ on the soundtrack, because the film company didn't think they could sound hillbilly enough."

# artist - Mae Axton

## member of
Acuff-Rose Publishing (songwriter)

## genres
Country

## songs
**Heartbreak Hotel (1956)** : As a songwriter, she was crucial in finishing "Heartbreak Hotel," conceiving the phrase "heartbreak hotel" itself after Tommy Durden presented initial lyrics. She played the finished song to Elvis, insisting it would be his first million-seller, and later claimed she gave him a third of the songwriting credit to help him buy a house for his parents in Florida. "She thought a lot about the phrase, and eventually came to the conclusion that what you'd find at the end of a lonely street was a heartbreak hotel."
**Early Collaborations with Dub Dickerson** : Began her songwriting career by collaborating with Dub Dickerson, including the song "Her pamphlet was peeping through the back door." "She eventually wrote over 200 songs, starting with her early collaborations with Dub Dickerson."

## curiosities
**Unexpected Songwriting Debut** : An English teacher and freelance journalist, Mae Axton initially knew nothing about "Hilboly music." Her songwriting career began unexpectedly when Mini Pearl falsely introduced her as a songwriter to Fred Rose of Acuff-Rose Publishing, leading to her being asked to write a novelty song for Dub Dickerson. "And so all of a sudden, May Axton was a songwriter, and she eventually wrote over 200 songs."
**Publicist for Hank Snow** : For a period, Mae Axton served as the personal publicist for country singer Hank Snow. "For a time, she was Hank Snow's personal publicist."
**Collaboration with Colonel Tom Parker** : Mae Axton frequently worked with Colonel Tom Parker to promote shows when they came through Florida, where she resided. "she would often work with Colonel Parker on promoting shows when they came through Flavida, where she lived."
**Inspired by Elvis's Family Devotion** : After interviewing Elvis in Florida, Mae Axton was deeply struck by his earnest desire to earn enough money to bring his parents to live there, inspiring her to promise to write him a song. "She said later, that just went through my heart, because I looked down there, and there were all these other kids, different show members for that night, all the guys looking for cute little girls. But his priority was doing something for his mother and daddy."

# artist - Chet Atkins

## member of

## instruments

## curiosities
**Presence at "Heartbreak Hotel" Session** : Though Steve Sholes was the credited producer, Chet Atkins was nominally in charge of running the "Heartbreak Hotel" session for Elvis Presley. He played an acoustic rhythm guitar part, which was considered an underutilization of his talent as "probably the greatest country guitarist of his generation." "But by all accounts Atkins was nominally in charge of actually running the session, and certainly there would be no other reason for having Atkins there."
**Satisfaction with Makeshift Echo** : Chet Atkins was satisfied with the makeshift echo chamber created for the "Heartbreak Hotel" session, which involved setting up a speaker and feeding in sound from microphones at the other end of the room. "creating a makeshift echo chamber which satisfied Chet Atkins."
**Minimal Production Input** : According to Scotty Moore, Chet Atkins' only production suggestion during the "Heartbreak Hotel" session was to "just keep doing what they'd been doing." "According to Scotty Moore, his only suggestion was that they just keep doing what they'd been doing."
**Architect of Nashville Sound** : Chet Atkins is one of the "principal architects" of what became known as the Nashville Sound or Countrypolitan style of country music. "Atkins and Kramer are two of the principal architects of what became known as the Nashville Sound, or Country Politan."

# artist - Frank Sinatra

# genre - Nashville Sound / Countrypolitan

## curiosities
**Architects and Characteristics** : Chet Atkins and Floyd Kramer are identified as the "principal architects" of the Nashville Sound (also known as Countrypolitan). This style of country music moved away from its traditional roots, leaning towards a "pre-rock pop sound" characterized by vocal groups and strings, with a notable absence of traditional country instrumentation such as fiddles, mandolins, banjos, and steel guitars. It aimed to smooth out rough edges and make the music more "manoured." "Atkins and Kramer are two of the principal architects of what became known as the Nashville Sound, or Country Politan."

# label - Sun Records

## curiosities
**Tiny Regional Operation** : Sun Records was described as a "tiny regional operation" before the Elvis Presley deal with RCA. "Sun Records, as we've seen, was a tiny regional operation."
**Financial Success from Elvis Deal** : After Elvis Presley signed with RCA Records, the "ludicrous demand" sum paid by RCA allowed Sun Records to make substantial cash, which in turn enabled the label to generate "massive hits" for artists like Carl Perkins and Johnny Cash after Elvis's departure. "It was able to generate massive hits for Karl Perkins and Johnny Cash after Elvis left, but that's only because of the cash the label was able to make from the Elvis deal."
**"Slapback Echo" Secrecy** : Sam Phillips, the owner of Sun Records, possessed a secret "slapback echo sound" that was unique to his studio and could not be replicated by RCA engineers during Elvis's first major label sessions. "Sam Phillips' slapback echo sound, which was a sound whose secret nobody but Phillips knew."
**Studio Atmosphere and Arrangement Style** : Sun Studios were known for their "laid back feel" where owner Sam Phillips "never clock watching." Their recording arrangements were typically simple, aiming for spontaneity, usually featuring only three instruments with a single solo. "The whole point of those records was to capture a kind of spontaneity, and you can't do much to play with the dynamics of an arrangement when there were only three instruments there."

**One of the Biggest Labels** : RCA Records was noted as "one of the biggest labels in the world" at the time of Elvis Presley's signing. "RCA, on the other hand, was one of the biggest labels in the world."
**Reissue of Sun Single (1956)** : The very first action RCA Records took after signing Elvis Presley was to reissue his last Sun single, "I Forgot to Remember to Forget," which then hit number one on the country charts with their backing. "The first thing RCA did was to reissue his last Sun single. I forgot to remember to forget."

"The session to record Heartbreak Hotel started with the engineers trying and failing to get a replica of Sam Phillips' slapback echo sound."

# curiosity

**Colonel Tom Parker's Business Acumen** : Colonel Tom Parker, Elvis Presley's manager, negotiated an unprecedented sum for Elvis's signing to RCA Records, forcing Sam Phillips of Sun Records to make a "ludicrous demand" which Parker accepted. This deal was so significant that it essentially funded the entire genre of rockabilly and allowed Sun Records to thrive after Elvis's departure. "Elvis's manager, Colonel Tom Parker, had managed to get Elvis signed to RCA records for a sum of money far greater than anything anyone had paid for a single before."
**Sam Phillips' Secret Slapback Echo** : Sam Phillips, the owner of Sun Records, possessed a unique "slapback echo sound" that was proprietary to his studio. Engineers at RCA Records attempted to replicate this sound during Elvis Presley's "Heartbreak Hotel" session but failed, resorting to a makeshift echo chamber. "Sam Phillips' slapback echo sound, which was a sound whose secret nobody but Phillips knew."
**Fred Rose and Acuff-Rose Publishing** : Fred Rose was the co-owner of Acuff-Rose Publishing, described as "the biggest publishing company in country music." He unknowingly launched Mae Axton's songwriting career by asking her to write a novelty song for Dub Dickerson after Mini Pearl falsely introduced her as a songwriter. "Pearl introduced her to Fred Rose, the co-owner of Acre of Rose Publishing, the biggest publishing company in country music."
**Alvin Crowley and "Heartbreak Hotel" Inspiration** : Later research suggests that the newspaper story inspiring Tommy Durden's "Heartbreak Hotel" was likely about an armed robber named Alvin Crowley, who was shot dead, rather than a suicide. Crowley had previously made news with a partial autobiography containing lines about loneliness and purpose, possibly influencing the song's themes. "Later research has suggested that rather than a suicide, the story Durden had read was probably about an armed robber, Alvin Crowley, who had been shot dead in the course of committing a robbery."
**Backing Vocals in Rock and Roll** : Backing vocals, often seen as "spoiling Elvis's artistry" and deprecated in rock and roll for the past 50 years, were in fact "the essence of it" for Elvis Presley. His lifelong ambition was to sing in a gospel quartet, driving his increasing use of harmony singers. "But the backing vocals weren't an irritating addition to Elvis's artistry, rather they were the essence of it, and if you're going to listen to Elvis at all and have any understanding of what he was trying to do, you need to understand that before anything else."
**Hollywood Session Musicians for Film Soundtracks** : For Elvis Presley's first film, "Doreen O'Brothers" (later "Love Me Tender"), his regular band (Scotty Moore, Bill Black, DJ Fontana) was replaced by Hollywood session musicians. The film company believed these musicians could "do a better job of sounding hillbilly" than the country musicians. "They were replaced with Hollywood session musicians, who could do a better job of sounding hillbilly than those country musicians could."
