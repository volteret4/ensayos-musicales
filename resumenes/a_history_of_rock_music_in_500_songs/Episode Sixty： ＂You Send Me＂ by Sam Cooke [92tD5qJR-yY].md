## albums
**The Dale Cook Sessions (1957)** : Recorded secular music under the pseudonym "Dale Cook" in New Orleans, at Cosimo Matassa's studio, with the same musicians responsible for many rock and roll hits. This was a strategic decision to prevent damaging his gospel career while pursuing pop stardom, using the plausible deniability that "Dale" could be his brother. "So the decision was made to put the single out under the name Dale Cook and maintain a small amount of plausible denierability."

## songs
**If I Didn't Care** : A song by The Ink Spots that Sam Cooke was heard singing to a girl, leading to him being invited to join the newly formed gospel group, the Highway QC's. "Cook joined a newly formed gospel group who had heard him singing the Inkspot song, if I didn't care, to a girl."
**Jesus Gave Me Water (1951)** : This song was a highlight of the Highway QC's set and the Soul Stirrers insisted on recording it during Sam Cooke's first session with them in March 1951, despite Art Rupe's initial objections given The Pilgrim Travelers had recently had a hit with it. The resulting recording was deemed "absolutely majestic" by Rupe and became the group's next single. "But the group insisted, and the result was absolutely majestic."
**Peace in the Valley (1951)** : Written by Thomas Dorsey 14 years prior, this song was released as the B-side to "Jesus Gave Me Water" in 1951, marking its first release on record by any prominent artist. "That song had been written by Thomas Dorsey, 14 years earlier, but this was the first time it had been released on a record, at least by anyone of any prominence."
**Wonderful (Pre-1957)** : An original gospel song that Sam Cooke had recorded with The Soul Stirrers. It was later reworked into the secular song "Lovable" for his first solo secular recording session under the pseudonym "Dale Cook." "As his first secular recording, they decided to record a new version of a gospel song that Cook had recorded with the soul-sturvers. Wonderful."
**Lovable (1957)** : A secularized version of the gospel song "Wonderful," released under the pseudonym Dale Cook as Sam Cooke's first solo secular recording. DJs often introduced it as by "Dale Sam Cook," revealing Cooke's distinctive voice despite the alias. "One quick view out later, and that song became, instead, lovable."
**Mean Old World (1957)** : A song that Sam Cooke recorded with The Soul Stirrers, noted for having only "a minimal amount of religious content," which could easily be refigured into a secular song, highlighting his gradual shift towards pop. "Though it's noticeable that songs like Mean Old World could easily be refigured into being secular songs and have only a minimal amount of religious content."
**Summertime (1957)** : One of four songs chosen by Art Rupe for Sam Cooke's first major secular recording session, alongside "You Send Me." Although the expectation was for "Summertime" to be the hit, "You Send Me" ultimately achieved massive success. "When the single came out everyone thought that summertime would be the hit, but you sent me quickly found itself all over the airwaves and became massive."
**You Send Me (1957)** : A song written by Sam Cooke, initially met with skepticism by guitarist Clifton White who thought it repetitive. It was one of four songs selected for his secular recording session, and despite Art Rupe's objections to the white female backing vocalists, it quickly became a massive hit, reaching number one on both the R&B and Pop charts. "You Send Me, was just him repeating the same thing over and over again."
**You Were Made For Me (1957)** : An original song written by Sam Cooke, chosen as one of the four tracks for his debut secular recording session. "another song Sam had written called You Were Made For Me"
**I'll Come Running Back to You (1957)** : A demo recorded by Sam Cooke, which Art Rupe later took after their legal dispute. Rupe had Rene Hall and the Lee Gotch Singers overdub the track to make it sound as much like "You Send Me" as possible, in an attempt to leverage Cooke's success for Specialty Records. "Roup took a demo Sam had recorded. I'll come running back to you, and got many hall and the Lee got singers. The very people whose work on You Send Me and Sumitain, he despised so much. To record overdubbs, to make it sound as much like You Send Me as possible."

## curiosities
**Early Life and Religious Upbringing** : Sam Cooke was one of seven children born to Reverend Charles Cook and Annie May. His father, a Reverend, trained all five performing children as singers from a very early age, forming the group The Singing Children who regularly performed in Chicago-area churches. "Young Sam was taught religion by his father, but he was also taught that there was no prohibition in the Bible against worldly success."
**Father's Maxims for Success** : Reverend Cook instilled two core principles in Sam that would significantly shape his life: the importance of striving for excellence in everything ("if a thing's worth doing, it's worth doing properly") and a ruthless pursuit of personal advancement ("you do whatever is necessary to give yourself the best possible life and don't worry about who you step on to do it"). "And the second was that you do whatever is necessary to give yourself the best possible life and don't worry about who you step on to do it."
**Behavior Towards Women** : Cook was known for his "far less admirable" behavior towards women, getting married in 1953 but having at least one child with another woman whom he abandoned. He consistently engaged in infidelity while touring, leading to multiple pregnancies with different women he refused to acknowledge or support. "But Cook treated women the same way he treated the groups he was in. Use them for as long as they've got something you want, and then immediately cast them aside once it became inconvenient."
**Mentorship and Betrayal within Gospel** : While a member of the Highway QC's, the group was mentored by R.B. Robinson of The Soul Stirrers, who helped them secure better gigs. Unknown to the QC's, Robinson was actually training Sam Cooke to replace R.H. Harris as the lead singer of The Soul Stirrers, causing heartbreak for the QC's when Cooke left. "Robinson would train them and help them get better gigs, and the QCs became convinced they were headed for the big time."
**Solo Contract and Royalty Disputes** : Sam Cooke's solo contract with Specialty Records only offered him one-third of the royalties that The Soul Stirrers had received as a group, prompting him to push for a greater share. This perceived "greed" angered label head Art Rupe, who believed artists should not receive more than one cent per record sold. "Cooke discovered that his solo contract only paid him a third of the royalties that the soul-sturers had been getting as a group, he started pushing for a great to share of the money."
**Keen Records Formation** : Sam Cooke and Bumps Blackwell took "You Send Me," "Summertime," and other recordings to a new label called Keen Records, which released them as its very first release after Blackwell's departure from Specialty Records. "But Cooke and Blackwell were free to take you send me summertime and the rest, to a new label that wanted them for its first release. Keen."
**Central Avenue Music Scene Involvement** : While waiting for Keen Records to get set up, Sam Cooke became deeply involved in the Central Avenue music scene in Los Angeles, regularly interacting with figures like Gaynell Hodge, Jesse Belvin, Dutti Williams, Guggi Renne, and John Dolphin within the LA R&B community. "Sam made himself firmly a part of the central avenue music scene, hanging around with Gaynell Hodge, Jesse Belvin, Dutti Williams, Guggi Renne, John Dolphin, and everyone else who was part of the LAR and B community."
**Ed Sullivan Show Debacle (1957)** : Sam Cooke experienced a "catastrophic" appearance on the Ed Sullivan Show, where he was accidentally cut off halfway through a song, despite his version of "You Send Me" being a massive hit. "You Send Me...went to number one on both the R and B and the pop charts, despite a catastrophic appearance on the Ed Sullivan show which accidentally caught him off halfway through a song."
**Royalty Evasion Tactic** : To prevent Art Rupe, who still held his songwriting contract, from collecting royalties on "You Send Me," Sam Cooke falsely attributed the song's authorship to his brother, LC Cook. This led to legal action initiated by Rupe against Cooke. "Sam was still signed to Roup's company as a songwriter, and so he'd put You Send Me in the name of his brother, LC, so Roup wouldn't get any royalties."
**Seeds of Downfall (1957)** : By the close of 1957, despite being at the peak of his commercial success, Sam Cooke's relentless pursuit of career control and personal gain at the expense of others had laid the groundwork for his future downfall, causing pain to those who had supported him, including his gospel groups and many women. "He was upsetting all the right people with his desire to have control of his own career, but he was also hurting a lot of other people along the way."
**Controversial Application of Father's Maxims** : Cooke rigidly applied his father's advice about self-interest to all aspects of his life, demonstrating a disregard for the feelings and well-being of others, yet his immense talent and charm often led even those he hurt to defend him. "He was taking his father's maxims about only looking after yourself, and applying them to every aspect of life, with no regard to who it hurt."

## curiosities
**Founding and Early Style (1926)** : The Soul Stirrers originated in 1926, performing in the "Jubilee Style," a manner of singing spiritual music common among black singers before Thomas Dorsey's revolution in gospel. "The Soul Sturlers had started out in 1926 as a group in what was called The Jubilee Style."
**Transition to New Gospel Style** : Following Thomas Dorsey's innovation in gospel music, The Soul Stirrers successfully adapted their style, becoming one of the first and most acclaimed gospel quartets in this new format. "The Soul Sturlers switched styles and became one of the first and best gospel quartets in the new style."
**Live Performance Popularity (1955)** : The Soul Stirrers were notably more popular as a live act than as a recording act, a characteristic evident in a 1955 live recording produced by Bumps Blackwell. "The soul-sturvers were more popular as a live-act than as a recording act, and hearing the live recording of them that Bumps Blackwell produced in 1955, it's easy to see why."
**Financial Performance as a Gospel Group** : In 1956, the group's record sales were in the "low tens of thousands," which was considered a reasonable amount for a gospel group but insufficient to generate significant wealth for its members. "The soul-sturvers were only selling in the low tens of thousands. A reasonable amount for a gospel group but hardly the kinds of numbers that would make anyone rich."

## curiosities
**Discovery of Sam Cooke** : The group discovered Sam Cooke singing The Ink Spots' song "If I Didn't Care" to a girl, leading them to invite him to join. "Cook joined a newly formed gospel group who had heard him singing the Inkspot song, if I didn't care, to a girl."
**Sam Cooke's Stardom within the Group** : Even though the other members were decent singers, Sam Cooke was "absolutely, unquestionably, the star" of the Highway QC's, with member Kredel Copeland noting that Sam's captivating voice was their primary claim to fame. "Our claim to fame was that Sam's voice was so captivating we didn't have to do anything else."
**Desire for Pop Direction** : The group struggled financially, prompting discussions about shifting towards a pop direction instead of exclusively performing gospel songs, a sentiment shared by Sam Cooke who was already singing secular music in his free time. "The group didn't make a huge amount of money, and they kept talking about going in a pop direction rather than just singing gospel songs"
**Rivalry with The Soul Stirrers** : The Highway QC's considered themselves disciples of The Soul Stirrers but also believed the older group was jealous of them. When the QC's attended Soul Stirrers shows, they were often ignored and only allowed to perform one song if the audience strongly insisted. "When the highway QCs turned up at soul-stirvers shows, though, the soul-stirvers would act as if they didn't know them, and would only invite them onto the stage if the audience absolutely insisted, and would then limit their performance to a single song."
**Mentorship and Betrayal** : R.B. Robinson of The Soul Stirrers mentored the Highway QC's, helping them secure better gigs and convincing them they were "headed for the big time." However, his true intention was to train Sam Cooke to replace R.H. Harris in The Soul Stirrers, leaving the remaining QC's heartbroken when Cooke departed. "But it turned out that behind the scenes that had been trouble in the soul-stirvers."
**Influence on Young Singers** : To the younger generation of singers in Chicago, such as Lou Rawls, the Highway QC's were seen as "the group to beat" and a significant aspiration. "On the other hand, to all the younger kids around Chicago, the highway QCs were clearly the group to beat, and people like a young singer named Lou Rolls looked up to them as something to aspire to."

# artist - Art Rupe

## labels
Specialty Records

## curiosities
**Initial Disapproval of Sam Cooke** : As head of Specialty Records, Rupe was displeased when The Soul Stirrers brought in Sam Cooke as their new singer without informing him, and initially considered Cooke's performances to be inferior to R.H. Harris. "Art group, the head of specialty records, was not at all impressed that the group had got a new singer without telling him."
**Musical Vision and Commercial Acumen** : Rupe, despite loving gospel music, recognized the greater commercial potential in other black music styles after Lloyd Price's "Lordy Miss Claude" became Specialty's biggest hit, prompting him to shift the label's focus towards more secular genres. "Even though Rooop loved gospel music, he realised when Lordy Miss Claude became the biggest hit specialty he had ever had to that point, that maybe he should refocus the label away from gospel and towards more secular styles of music."
**Specific Recording Criteria** : Rupe had strict ideas about commercial records, including a precise length requirement of "exactly two minutes and twenty seconds," which influenced song choices for Sam Cooke's secular sessions. "Rube had very particular ideas about what made for a commercial record. For example, that a record had to be exactly two minutes and twenty seconds long"
**Racial and Gender Objections to Backing Vocals** : Rupe was "horrified" by the use of white female backing vocalists (Lee Gotch Singers) on Sam Cooke's "You Send Me," viewing it as an attempt to make black music sound "white" and explicitly objecting to the presence of women. "Rube was horrified that bumps and bathees had put white backing vocalists behind Cooke's vocals. They were, in Rube's view, trying to make Sam Cooke sound like Billy Ward and his dominoes at best, and like a symphony orchestra at worst."
**Falling Out with Bumps Blackwell (Little Richard's "Rip It Up")** : Rupe had a major conflict with producer Bumps Blackwell over Little Richard's "Rip It Up." Blackwell, on a plane to the session, changed the arrangement from piano triplets (which Rupe approved) to a drum pattern, leading Rupe to almost fire him, only relenting when the song became a massive hit. "Rube and black well had already had one massive falling out overripped up by little Richard. When they agreed to record that, black well had worked out an arrangement beforehand the Rube was happy with."
**Conflict over Sam Cooke's Royalties** : Rupe believed Sam Cooke's demand for a higher royalty share (more than one cent per record) was "greed" and disliked artists pushing for more money. "Rube didn't like that kind of greed from his artists. Why should he pay the artist more than one cent per record sold?"
**Settlement with Bumps Blackwell** : Rupe and Bumps Blackwell eventually reached an agreement: Blackwell would leave Specialty, taking Sam Cooke and his existing recordings, while Rupe would retain publishing rights for any songs Sam wrote and an option on future Cooke recordings. "Black well would leave specialty and take Sam Cooke, and Cooke's existing recordings with him, since he was so convinced they were going to be a hit."
**Legal Action Against Sam Cooke** : After Sam Cooke listed his brother LC as the songwriter for "You Send Me" to avoid paying royalties to Rupe, Rupe initiated legal action against him. "Roup started legal action against him, and meanwhile he took a demo Sam had recorded. I'll come running back to you"
**Retaliation against Sam Cooke** : In retaliation for Cooke's success on Keen, Rupe took Sam's demo "I'll Come Running Back to You" and had Rene Hall and the Lee Gotch Singers overdub it to mimic the sound of "You Send Me," then released it on Specialty. "Roup started legal action against him, and meanwhile he took a demo Sam had recorded. I'll come running back to you, and got many hall and the Lee got singers. The very people whose work on You Send Me and Sumitain, he despised so much. To record overdubbs, to make it sound as much like You Send Me as possible."
**Forced Release of Little Richard Material** : Specialty Records was forced to "rush release" a Little Richard song, which they were trying to "drip-feed" to the public, as a direct response to Bumps Blackwell's counter-release of an unreleased Little Richard track on Keen. "Specialty had to rush release Little Richard's version to make sure it became the hit. A blow for them, given that they were trying to drip-feed the public what few Little Richard recordings they had left."

# artist - Bumps Blackwell

## labels
Specialty Records

## curiosities
**Advocate for Sam Cooke's Secular Career** : Bumps Blackwell was instrumental in pushing Sam Cooke to become a secular solo performer in 1956, recognizing Cooke's "very, very special talent" beyond his gospel work with The Soul Stirrers. "Blackwell knew then that Cook was a very, very special talent and he and the rest of the people at specialty became more and more insistent as 1956 went on that Sam Cook should become a secular solo performer rather than performing in a gospel group."
**Disguising Sam Cooke's Secular Debut** : Blackwell helped orchestrate the release of Sam Cooke's first secular recordings under the pseudonym "Dale Cook" to avoid jeopardizing Cooke's gospel career. "So the decision was made to put the single out under the name Dale Cook and maintain a small amount of plausible denierability."
**Producer of "You Send Me" Session** : Blackwell supervised Sam Cooke's "You Send Me" recording session in Los Angeles, which featured New Orleans veteran musicians. "Blackwell supervised it, and while the session was in LA, almost everyone there was a veteran New Orleans player."
**Conflict with Art Rupe over "Rip It Up"** : Blackwell had a significant dispute with Specialty head Art Rupe over Little Richard's "Rip It Up" when he changed the song's arrangement to a drum pattern, contrary to Rupe's approved piano triplet arrangement, nearly costing him his job. "When they agreed to record that, black well had worked out an arrangement beforehand the Rube was happy with. One that was based around piano triplets."
**Termination from Specialty and Deal with Rupe** : Rupe fired Blackwell from Specialty Records due to his repeated insubordination regarding recording arrangements and vocal choices. However, they reached a deal where Blackwell could leave and take Sam Cooke and his recordings with him to a new label. "Rube decided it was the last straw. Black well had to go."
**Retaliation Against Art Rupe** : In response to Rupe's attempt to copy "You Send Me" with "I'll Come Running Back to You," Blackwell orchestrated the rush-release of an unreleased Little Richard song on Keen Records, featuring Don and Dewey and The Valiants, forcing Specialty to also rush their own Little Richard release. "In retaliation for that being released, Bump's Blackwell took a song that he'd recorded months earlier with Little Richard, but which still hadn't been released, and got the specialty duo Don and Dui to provide instrumental backing for a vocal group called The Valiant, and put his house on Keen."

# artist - Ray Charles

## genres
Rhythm and Blues

# genre - Gospel

## curiosities
**New Form in Chicago (1940s)** : In the 1940s, Chicago became a hub for the creation of a new form of gospel music, spearheaded by figures like Thomas Dorsey, which significantly influenced young musicians such as Sam Cooke. "Chicago which...was the city where a whole new form of gospel music was being created at that point, spearheaded by Thomas Dorsey."
**Jubilee Style** : Before Thomas Dorsey revolutionized gospel, black singers of spiritual music often performed in a style known as "Jubilee Style," which groups like The Soul Stirrers initially employed. "The style the black singers of spiritual music sang in the period before Thomas Dorsey revolutionised gospel music."
**Quartet Terminology** : In both Black Gospel and White Gospel (euphemistically called Southern Gospel), the term "quartet" refers to groups that can comprise five, six, or even more members, not strictly four. "in both Black Gospel and in the White Gospel music euphemistically called Southern Gospel, the term quartet is used for groups which might have five, six, or even more people in them."
**Professionalization Movement** : R.H. Harris, along with Abraham Battle and Charlie Bridges, led a movement to professionalize the gospel quartet scene, which included establishing the National Quartet Convention to guide groups on appropriate conduct and appearance. "he was the joint leader of a movement to professionalise the gospel quartet movement."
**Mid-50s Secularization Trend** : In the mid-1950s, there was a prevalent trend where gospel-inspired performers achieved massive secular hits by simply changing a few words in gospel songs, most notably replacing "Jesus" with "baby" or similar terms. "People were having a hit by taking gospel songs and changing the word Jesus to baby or near as dammit."

"Together, they founded the National Quartet Convention, which existed to try to take all the young gospel quartets who were springing up all over the place, and most of whom had casual attitudes to their music, and their onstage appearance, and teach them how to comport themselves in a manner that the organization's leaders considered appropriate for a gospel singer."

"Sam Cook travelled to New Orleans to record in Cosimo Matassa's studio with the same musicians who had been responsible for so many rock and roll hits."
**Secularization Trend in Mid-50s Music** : In the mid-1950s, a notable trend emerged where artists achieved massive popular hits by taking existing gospel songs and minimally changing key religious words, often replacing "Jesus" with "baby" or similar secular terms, as exemplified by Ray Charles. "In the mid-50s things seem to be the other way. People were having a hit by taking gospel songs and changing the word Jesus to baby or near as dammit."
