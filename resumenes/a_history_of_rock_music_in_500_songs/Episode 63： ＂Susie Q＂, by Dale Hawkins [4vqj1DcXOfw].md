## albums
**[Suzy Q Demo Album] (1956)** : Hawkins entered the studio in 1956 with engineer M.L. Kilgore and guitarist James Burton to record a demo of "Suzy Q." This early recording already contained most elements of the final hit, with the main differences being a saxophone solo and James Burton's guitar part not yet fully developed. "Listening to that, it's clear that they already had all the elements of the finished record nearly in place."
**[Untitled Swamp Rock Album] (Late 1960s)** : Following Creedence Clearwater Revival's hit cover of "Suzy Q" in 1968, Hawkins returned to the studio for a brief second recording career attempt. He worked with James Burton on guitar, Jo-El Sonnier on bass, and blues musicians Taj Mahal and Ry Cooder to create an album filled with the Swamp Rock style he originated in the 1950s. "One of the album full of the Swamp Rock style he had created in the 50s."
**Wildcat Tamer (1998)** : This album marked Dale Hawkins' first recording in 30 years, released after he overcame amphetamine addiction and earned his master's degree. It garnered some of the best reviews of his career. "That got some of the best reviews of his career."
**[Untitled Album] (Late 2000s)** : Nearly a decade after the release of "Wildcat Tamer," Hawkins released another album. However, by this time, he had been diagnosed with colon cancer, which ultimately led to his death in 2010. "but his next album took nearly a decade to come out, and by that time he had been diagnosed with the colon cancer that eventually killed him in 2010."

## songs
**See You Soon, Baboon (1956)** : Dale Hawkins was persuaded by Stan Lewis to write this "answer record" to Bobby Charles's "Later Alligator." Checker Records released Hawkins' demo, but made three key changes: adding a Tarzan-style yodeling call, misspelling his first name as "Del," and removing his songwriting credit, assigning it to Stan Lewis and Delna Roadwater. "Lewis sent that off to checker, who released Hawkins's demo, although they did make three small changes."
**Suzy Q (1957)** : This song achieved significant commercial success, reaching number 7 on the R&B chart and number 27 on the pop charts. It was recorded in four takes by engineer Bob Sullivan, with Hawkins choosing an early take that captured an audible quick volume adjustment on his vocal. The final version featured James Burton's biting guitar riff and Ronnie Lewis's cowbell-infused drum part, inspired by AJ Tomanello's demo performance. "The song went to number 7 on the R&B chart, and number 27 on the pop charts."
**La-Dud-a-Da (Late 1950s/Early 1960s)** : This song was the closest Dale Hawkins came to a follow-up hit after the success of "Suzy Q." The guitarist on this track was Jo-El Sonnier, who later joined James Burton in Los Angeles as a session player, becoming part of The Wrecking Crew. "That guitarist was another young player, Joaz Bonn, who would soon follow James Burton to L.A., and to the pool of session players that became known as The Racking Crew, though Osborne would switch his guitar for bass."
**My Babe (Cover)** : Dale Hawkins recorded a cover version of Little Walter's song "My Babe." Moebue Cannon played guitar on this track, showcasing Hawkins' continued ability to work with talented guitarists after James Burton's departure. "Moebue Cannon, who played on Hawkins' cover version of Little Waltz's My Babe."

## curiosities
**Self-Identified Bluesman** : Dale Hawkins consistently thought of himself as a bluesman, rather than a rockabilly, a description he disliked. While living in Shreveport, Louisiana, he resided in a shack in the black part of town, possessing only a folding camp bed, a record player, and thousands of 78 RPM blues records. "When he was living in Shreveport, Louisiana, he lived in a shack in the black part of town, and inside the shack there was only a folding camp bed, a record player, and thousands of 78 RPM blues records."
**Country Music Family Background** : Hawkins came from a country music family; his father, Dalmar Hawkins Sr, was a country musician and bass player, possibly an original member of the Sons of the Pioneers, and his younger brother Jerry was a rockabilly who released singles in the 1950s. Despite this, Dale felt he lacked the country element necessary for the rockabilly label. "His father, Dalmar Horkin's senior, was a country musician who was, according to some sources, one of the original members of the sons of the pioneers, the group that launched the Come Here."
**Early Connection to Hank Williams** : Hawkins' early musical collaborator, Sonny Jones, was a local guitarist whose sister was the widow of Hank Williams, providing Jones with valuable contacts in the local music industry that helped Hawkins form his first band. "Jones had a lot of contacts in the local music industry and how Hawkins pulled together the first line-up of his band when he was 19."
**Louisiana Hayride Parking Scheme** : Hawkins' initial job at the Louisiana Hayride was not as a performer but working in the car park. He and his brother charged drivers 50 cents for parking, but they failed to disclose that the car park was actually free to the public. "What they didn't tell the drivers was that the car park was actually free to the public."
**Record Shop Expertise and Friendships** : While beginning his music career, Hawkins worked at Stan's Record Shop, where he became deeply familiar with nearly every blues and R&B record available. This job also led to a lifelong friendship with customers like B.B. King. "It was through doing this job that Hawkins became friendly with customers like Bibi King, who would remain a lifelong friend."
**Name Change to "Del Hawkins"** : During the release of "See You Soon, Baboon," Chess Records' Leonard Chess misheard "Dale Hawkins" over the phone, leading to his first name being misspelled as "Del" on the record. This spelling then became his professional name from that point forward. "Leonard Chess misheard Del Hawkins over the phone, and the record came out as by Del Hawkins, which would be his name from that point on."
**"Suzy Q" Songwriting Credit Discrepancy** : Hawkins claimed that not only were the songwriting credits for "Suzy Q" wrongly attributed (to Stan Lewis and Delna Roadwater), but he also did not receive any royalties until 1985. Crucially, co-writers like James Burton received no credit. "But according to Hawkins, not only did the credit get split with the wrong people, but he didn't receive any of the royalties to which he was entitled until as late as 1985."
**"Suzy Q" Musical Synthesis** : The song "Suzy Q" was a blend of various musical sources; its overall sound was influenced by Muddy Waters' "Baby Please Don't Go," its melody by The Clovers, and its guitar riff by Howlin' Wolf, suggesting it evolved from jam sessions despite conflicting authorship claims. "The most obvious influence for its overall sound. Both the melody and the way the melody interacts with the guitar riff. Is Baby Please Don't Go by Marty Waters."
**Pioneering Electric Bass Use in Rock and Roll** : For the released version of "Suzy Q," Hawkins consciously chose an electric bass, played by Sonny Tramble (the only person in Shreveport to own one), to emulate the sound of electric blues records, differentiating it from the double bass typically associated with country and rockabilly music. "This was a deliberate choice by Hawkins, who wanted to imitate the sound of electric blues records, rather than using the double bass, which he associated with country music."
**Presaging 1960s White Rock Acts** : Dale Hawkins is argued to be a pivotal figure, possibly the first white rock and roller to be directly inspired by black blues musicians like Muddy Waters and Howlin' Wolf. His song "Suzy Q," with its electric bass, Chess-style guitar riffs, and country-inflected vocals, is considered a precursor to 1960s white rock acts like The Rolling Stones and The Animals. "One can make an argument that Dale Hawkins was the first white Wuck and Ruler to be inspired by people like Waters and Wolf, and for Susie Q as the record, more than any other, that presaged the white rock acts of the 60s."
**Chess Records' Delayed Release of "Suzy Q"** : Chess Records inexplicably delayed the release of "Suzy Q" for 10 months after receiving the master. Hawkins, frustrated, sent a copy to Jerry Wexler at Atlantic Records, who expressed interest, which then prompted Chess to recognize its commercial potential and finally release it on Checker. "What's surprising is how reluctant chests were to release the single."
**The Dale Hawkins Show TV Series** : Following his initial string of hits, Hawkins leveraged his connection with Dick Clark of "American Bandstand" to secure his own television program, "The Dale Hawkins Show." This show ran for 18 months and followed a format similar to "American Bandstand." "he used his connection with Dick Clark, the TV presenter whose American bandstand had helped to break Suzy Kew on the national market to get his own TV show, The Dale Hawkins Show, which ran for 18 months and was a similar format to the bandstand."
**Credited Record Production** : After his TV show, Hawkins transitioned into record production for Stan Lewis's new labels. He is credited as the producer for the hit song "Do It Again Just a Little Bit Slower" by John & Robin, though claims of producing Bruce Channel's "Hey Baby" and John Fred and His Playboy Band's "Judy in Disguise (With Glasses)" are uncredited. "On the other hand, he is the credited producer on the big hit. Do it again just a little bit slower by John and Robin."
**Head of A&R at RCA Records** : After his late 1960s recording comeback was unsuccessful, Hawkins moved to RCA Records to become the head of A&R for their West Coast rock department, a position reportedly recommended to him by Jo-El Sonnier. "When that wasn't a success, he moved on to RCA Records to become head of A&R for their West Coast rock department, a job he was apparently put forward for by Joaz Boran."
**Amphetamine Addiction and Recovery** : Hawkins battled an amphetamine addiction throughout much of the 1970s, having started using speed in the 1950s. He successfully got clean in the early 1980s, subsequently performing on the Rockabilly Revival circuit. "But after a successful few years, he spent much of the 70s suffering from an emphetamine addiction, having started taking speed back in the 50s."
**Academic Achievement** : In the early 1980s, after getting clean, Hawkins achieved a significant personal milestone by earning his master's degree, an accomplishment he was as proud of as any hit record, especially considering he was a high school dropout. "as well as finally getting his master's degree, which for a high school dropout was a major achievement and something to be as proud of as any hit."
**Paradoxical Musical Legacy** : Dale Hawkins is considered a paradoxical figure: he foresaw the future of rock and roll where white artists adapted black blues ideas, yet his own biggest hit, "Suzy Q," was meticulously crafted from various musical fragments, despite his reputation for raw, instinctive rock and roll. "He was in many ways a paradoxical figure."

## curiosities
**Precedent for White Artists** : Checker, a subsidiary of Chess Records, set a precedent for releasing music by white artists when it put out Bobby Charles's single "Later Alligator." This made Stan Lewis believe Checker might be receptive to another record by a white act, specifically Dale Hawkins. "their subsidiary checker had recently released a single by a white artist, a song by Bobby Charles called Later Alligator"

# label - Atlantic Records

## curiosities
**Interest in "Suzy Q" Release** : Atlantic Records, through its executive Jerry Wexler, showed keen interest in releasing Dale Hawkins' "Suzy Q." This interest was crucial as Chess Records had delayed the single's release for ten months, and Wexler's ultimatum spurred Chess to finally put the record out. "Wixler got excited and told Leonard Chess that if Chess weren't going to put out the single, Atlantic would release it instead."

# label - Stan Lewis

## curiosities
**Music Entrepreneur and Producer** : Stan Lewis was a prominent figure in the music industry, owning Stan's Record Shop, acting as a talent scout for Chess Records, and serving as one of the biggest record distributors across the region. He also worked as a record producer, achieving one of his biggest successes with Lowell Fulson's "Reconsider Baby." "Lewis was, among other things, both a talent scout for chess records and one of the biggest customers of the label."
**Debut as Record Producer** : Stan Lewis's first foray into record production resulted in one of the biggest blues hits of all time, Lowell Fulson's "Reconsider Baby," which was released on Checker, a Chess subsidiary. "The first record he ever produced was one of the biggest blues hits of all time, Lowell Falson's Reconsider Baby, which was released on the chess subsidiary Checker."
**Claimed Inspiration for "Suzy Q" Name** : Stan Lewis claimed that the song "Suzy Q" was named after his daughter, Susan. He asserted that he had suggested to Dale Hawkins that they write a song with that name, believing it would appeal to Leonard Chess, who also had a daughter named Susan. "He claimed he had mentioned this to Hawkins and suggested that the two of them write a song together with the name in it, because it would appeal to Chess."

# instrument - Electric Bass

## curiosities
**Evolution from Rockabilly Sound** : The demo version of "Suzy Q" initially featured a double bass played in the slapback style, a defining characteristic of the rockabilly genre. However, for the final recording, Dale Hawkins deliberately chose to use an electric bass, a move intended to emulate the sound of electric blues records and differentiate his music from traditional country-associated double bass. "The demo version of Suzy Q had, like almost all rock and roll records of the time, featured a double bass played in the slapback style, and as we talked about, back in the episodes on Bill Haley several months back, slapback bass is one of the defining features of the rockabilly genre."
**Willie Dixon's Acoustic Electric Bass Sound** : Willie Dixon, who played bass on all Chess blues records, achieved a sound remarkably similar to an electric bass without actually using one. He accomplished this by placing a microphone inside the bottom of his double bass's tailpiece, demonstrating an innovative approach to bass recording. "Willie Dixon, who played bass on all the chess blues records, actually didn't play an electric bass. Rather, he got a sound similar to an electric bass, by actually placing the microphone inside the bottom of the bass's tailpiece."

# instrument - Cowbell

## curiosities
**"Suzy Q" Signature Drum Element** : The drum part for Dale Hawkins' "Suzy Q" featured a distinctive and ear-catching cowbell. This element originated during a soundcheck when Hawkins heard AJ Tomanello playing his drum and cowbell simultaneously, and the part was subsequently copied by Ronnie Lewis for the released version of the song. "That drum part was played by Stan Lewis's 15-year-old brother, Ronnie, on the new version, but he's closely copying the part that AJ Tomanello played on the demo."

# curiosity

**Late Rockabilly Era and Definition** : By 1957, the true rockabilly era was drawing to a close, with major figures having already emerged from Sun Studios. While country-influenced white rock and rollers like Buddy Holly and the Everly Brothers appeared, rockabilly aficionados often excluded them if their sound didn't sufficiently resemble the Sun Studios style. "We're pretty much at the end of the true rockabilly era already."
**"Suzy Q" Dance History** : The "Suzy Q" was a dance step from the late 1930s and 1940s, known for involving crossing one's feet, swiveling, and stepping to one side. It was incorporated into the more complex Lindy Hop and was notably promoted by a song performed by jazz pianist Lillian Hardin. "That dance was never a formalized dance, and one of its major characteristics was that it would incorporate dance moves from any other dance around."
