"In particular, one of the bands he was in had its own regular 15-minute show on a local radio station and their show was on next to a show presented by the Blues singer Howlin Wolf."

**Accidental Amplifier Distortion in Philadelphia** : During a show in Philadelphia on the way to Nashville, Paul Burlison dropped his amplifier, which loosened one of the internal vacuum tubes, producing a distorted sound he had never heard before. "At that show, Paul Burlison dropped his amplifier, loosening one of the vacuum tubes inside."

"The distorted sound it gave was like nothing he'd ever heard, and while he replaced the tube, he started loosening it, every time he wanted to get that sound."
**Post-Trio Career as Electrician** : After the Rock and Roll Trio split up, Paul Burlison gave up music and returned to his profession as an electrician. "Paul Burlison, the last member of the trio, gave up music once the trio split up and became an electrician again."

"He then got back into performing and started a new lineup of the rock and roll trio, featuring Johnny Black, who had briefly replaced Dorsey in the group, and Tony Austin, the drummer who had joined with them on many tour dates after they got a recording contract."
**Death in 2003** : Paul Burlison continued to play rockabilly music until his death in 2003 at the age of 74. "He kept playing Rocky Billy until he died in 2003, aged 74."

**You're Undecided (1953)** : This was their first single, recorded in 1953 for the tiny Vom Records label in Booneville, Mississippi. A new version of the song later appeared as the B-side of their first Coral single. "In 1953 that they recorded their first single, You're Undecided, for a tiny label called Vom Records in Booneville, Mississippi."
**Vocabelli Boogey (1957)** : The band wrote this song around 1953 but did not record it until 1957. It has been claimed as the first use of the word "Vocabelli," with Dorsey's son Billy and Johnny's son Rocky cited as the inspiration for the name, though the more likely origin is a portmanteau of "rock" and "hillbilly." "Around this time, they also wrote a song called Vockebelli Boogey which they didn't get to record until 1957. That has been claimed as the first use of the word Vockebelli and Billy Burnett Dorsey's son says they coined the word based on his name and that of Johnny's son, Rucky."
**Shattered Dreams (Unreleased until later)** : This was the only track the band recorded with a 32-piece orchestra during their initial New York session for Coral Records, but it was not released until many years later due to their dissatisfaction with the orchestral sound. "They did record one track with the orchestra, shattered dreams, although that song didn't get released until many years later."
**Tear It Up (1956)** : Dorsey Burnette quickly wrote this up-tempo song in a hallway during their New York recording session, and it subsequently became the A-side of their first Coral single. "And so Dorsey went out into the hallway and quickly wrote a song called Territop, which became the A side of their first cover single, with the B side being a new version of your own decided."
**Honey Hush (1956)** : This song was the A-side of the single recorded at their Nashville session. It was originally written and recorded by Big Joe Turner three years earlier, and while some critics noted its misogynistic lyrics, the Rock and Roll Trio's cover is considered by many to be the best and most exciting version, inspiring subsequent covers by artists like Chuck Berry, Paul McCartney, Elvis Costello, and Jerry Lee Lewis. "The A-side, Horie Hosh, was eventually written and recorded by Big Joe Turner three years earlier."
**Train Kept A Rollin' (1956)** : The B-side of their Nashville-recorded single, this track became a classic despite the single not being a commercial success. It was radically rearranged from Tiny Bradshaw's version (itself adapted from Elma Morse's "Cow Cow Boogie") to feature an octave-double distorted guitar riff, apparently invented by Dorsey Burnette but played by Burlison and Martin. This version introduced a deliberately distorted guitar sound that profoundly influenced British guitar players and became a standard in rock music. "But the song that really mattered to people was the B-side, a track called Train kept a rolling."

## curiosities
**Early Country Style** : Despite changing their name to "The Rock and Roll Trio" a year after forming in 1952, the band was initially a country band, with early set lists including songs like Hank Snow's "I'm Moving On." "While they were called the Rock and Roll trio they were still basically a country band and their early set lists included songs like Hank Snow's and Moving On."
**Grand Ole Opry Incident** : The band drove to Nashville to ask Hank Snow to help them get on the Grand Ole Opry, but Snow refused, stating it took him 20 years to earn his spot. This refusal led to Johnny Burnette's expletive-laden response and the band dropping all Hank Snow songs from their live performances. "They wanted to get on the Grand Ole Opry and so they drove up, found Snow who was going to be on that night's show and asked him if he could get them onto the show."
**Goodwin Institute Jamboree Development** : The band regularly played the Saturday Night Jamboree at the Goodwin Institute Auditorium, a country music show in Memphis. The backstage area of this venue was crucial, as musicians would jam, share new riffs, and exchange ideas, which was formative for the Rock and Roll Trio and many other rockabilly acts. "They also regularly played the Saturday night Jamboree at the Goodwin Institute Auditorium and regular country music show that was occasionally broadcast on the same station that Burlesons Old Bands had performed on KWEM."
**Breakthrough on Ted Mack Amateur Hour** : Their big break came in early 1956 when they appeared on the Ted Mack Amateur Hour, winning three times in a row, a television series comparable to later talent shows like "The X Factor" or "American Idol." "Their big break came in early 1956 when they appeared on the Ted Mac Amateur Hour and won three times in a row."
**Signing with Coral Records** : After winning the Ted Mack Amateur Hour for the third time, the band was signed to Coral Records, a subsidiary of Decca Records, which was one of the biggest major labels in the USA at the time. "they got signed to cover records, a subsidiary of Decker Records, one of the biggest major labels in the USA at that time."
**Disastrous New York Recording Session** : Their first recording session for Coral in New York was unproductive, as they were met by a 32-piece orchestra that had no understanding of rock and roll music. They eventually sent all the orchestral musicians home, keeping only the drummer, to achieve their desired sound. "Their initial session for cover was in New York, and when they got there, they were surprised to find a 32 piece orchestra waiting for them, none of whom had any more clue about playing Ruck and Roll music than the Ruck and Roll trio had about playing orchestral pieces."
**Demand for Nashville Recordings** : Unhappy with their New York recordings and the lack of understanding from orchestral musicians, the band refused to record there again and insisted on making records in Nashville. "But now, the Ruck and Rolls-Veele weren't happy at all about their recordings. They didn't want to make any more records in New York, with a bunch of orchestral musicians who didn't know anything about their music."
**Accidental Sound Discovery in Nashville** : During their Nashville sessions at Owen Bradley's studio, they achieved a unique and superior sound by accident. Paul Burlison's amplifier, damaged from being dropped, produced a deliberately recorded distorted guitar sound, a pioneering moment in music history. "In fact, they got something all together different, and better than that sound, and it happened entirely by accident."
**Augmentation by Nashville A-Team** : For their Nashville sessions, the trio was augmented by studio musicians from the Nashville A-Team, including Bob Moore on bass (as Dorsey preferred playing rhythm guitar in the studio), Buddy Harmon on drums, and Grady Martin on an additional electric guitar. "At these sessions, the trio were augmented by a few studio musicians from the Nashville A team, the musicians who made most of the country hits of the time."
**Distinctive Guitar Style of Burlison** : Paul Burlison's guitar style and the deliberate distortion effect he applied were absolutely distinctive and unlike anything heard from session guitarist Grady Martin at that point, underscoring Burlison's unique contribution to their sound. "Certainly, Burlison's guitar style is absolutely distinctive, and the effect he puts on his guitar is absolutely unlike anything else that you hear from Brady Martin at this point."
**Name Credit Dispute Leading to Split** : Coral Records decided to release their recordings as "by Johnny Burnette and the Rock and Roll Trio," infuriating the other members who considered themselves a band, not just Johnny's backing musicians. This dispute led to Dorsey Burnette temporarily quitting before their film appearance and eventually to the group's permanent split when later tracks were released solely under "Johnny Burnette." "While they had always performed as the rock and roll trio, Coral Records decided to release their recordings as by Johnny Burnett and the rock and roll trio, and the other two members were understandably furious."
**Appearance in "Rock, Rock, Rock" Film** : The band was scheduled to appear in "Rock, Rock, Rock," a cheap exploitation film starring Alan Freed, but Dorsey Burnette quit days before the shoot, leading to Johnny Black temporarily replacing him. "They were due to appear in rock, rock, rock, a cheap exploitation film starring Alan Fried."

# artist - Grady Martin

## instruments
Electric guitar
Fuzz tone

## curiosities
**Nashville A-Team Session Guitarist** : Grady Martin was a session guitarist from the Nashville A-Team who added another electric guitar to complement Paul Burlison's on the Rock and Roll Trio's Nashville sessions. "session guitarist Grady Martin added another electric guitar to complement Burlison's."
**Distinct from Burlison's Style** : At the time of the Rock and Roll Trio sessions, Grady Martin's guitar style and effects were distinctly different from Paul Burlison's, highlighting Burlison's unique approach to distorted guitar. "the effect he puts on his guitar is absolutely unlike anything else that you hear from Brady Martin at this point."
**Introduced Fuzz Tone to Country Music** : Martin later introduced the fuzz tone to country music through his playing on records like Marty Robbins' "Don't Worry," approximately five years after the Rock and Roll Trio's sessions. "Martin did, later, introduce the Fuzz tone to country music, with his playing on records like Marty Robinson's Don't Worry."
**Likely Inspired by Paul Burlison's Distortion** : The most probable explanation for Grady Martin's later adoption of fuzz tone is that he was inspired by Paul Burlison's intentional use of distorted guitar during the Rock and Roll Trio's Nashville sessions. "the most likely explanation is that Martin was inspired to add Fuzz to his guitar by Paul Burlison."

# instrument - Amplifier

## curiosities
**Accidental Source of Distortion** : While playing a show in Philadelphia, Paul Burlison accidentally dropped his amplifier, which loosened an internal vacuum tube and unexpectedly produced a distorted sound unlike anything he had heard before. "At that show, Paul Burlison dropped his amplifier, loosening one of the vacuum tubes inside."
**Deliberate Manipulation for Desired Sound** : After discovering the accidental distortion, Burlison started deliberately loosening the vacuum tube in his amplifier whenever he wanted to reproduce that unique sound, leading to its intentional use in recordings. "The distorted sound it gave was like nothing he'd ever heard, and while he replaced the tube, he started loosening it, every time he wanted to get that sound."

# instrument - Distorted Guitar

## curiosities
**Possibly First Deliberately Recorded Instance** : The Rock and Roll Trio's Nashville sessions, spurred by Paul Burlison's amplifier accident, are considered possibly the first time a distorted guitar was deliberately chosen and recorded in music history. "and for possibly the first time ever, deliberately recorded a distorted guitar."
**Distinction from "Rocket 88" Distortion** : Unlike the distorted guitar heard on "Rocket 88," where the distortion was an unavoidable accident on recording day, Burlison intentionally chose to use the result of his amplifier accident when he could have easily had it in perfect working order, marking a conscious artistic decision. "But the difference there was that the accident happened on the day of the recording, with no time to fix it. This was Burlison choosing to use the result of the accident, at a point where he could easily have had the amplifier in perfect working order had he wanted to."
