## songs
**Silent Night (Traditional)** : Jerry Lee Lewis almost instantly figured out how to play the beginning of this song on the piano when he was four years old, after feeling the urge to play his rich uncle's piano. "He'd almost instantly figured out how to play the beginning of Silent Night."
**Waiting For A Train (Jimmie Rodgers)** : This was a favorite song of Jerry Lee Lewis, who admired Jimmie Rodgers greatly and adapted his style to the piano. "Jerry Lee would play his waiting for a train."
**Drinking Wine Spodio D (Sticks McGhee)** : This was Jerry Lee Lewis's favorite song to play, a record by Sticks McGhee that some credit as the first rock and roll record ever. "His favorite song to play though was Drinking Wine Spodio D, the sticks McGee record that some credit as the first rock and roll record ever."
**My God Is Real (Traditional)** : Jerry Lee Lewis was kicked out of the Southwestern Bible Institute for playing this hymn with a "Boogie feel," which was deemed to be inciting lust among other students. "Unfortunately, he was kicked out, after playing the hymn My God Is Real with a Boogey feel, which, according to the people in charge, was inciting lust among the other students."
**Crazy Arms (1956)** : This song was recorded by Jerry Lee Lewis almost as a joke during his first solo session for Sun Records, having also played it on his audition tape. It was a bit of a disappointment to him because his father, Elmo Lewis, who had given up his own piano playing ambition, wasn't impressed, finding it bittersweet to see his son achieve what he couldn't. The conventional narrative credits Ralph Mooney with writing it in 1949, with a demo by Windstewet in 1954, and the first release by Marilyn Kay before Ray Price's version became the biggest country hit of 1956, changing honky-tonk music with its four-notes-in-a-bar bass line. "Crazy Arms would always be a bit of a disappointment to Jerry Lee, not because it wasn't a massive hit."
**Honey Hush (1953) (Johnny Burnette version)** : Jerry Lee Lewis considered cutting this song by Big Joe Turner, which had just been cut by Johnny Burnette, in between tour dates for his second single, but it didn't seem suitable for the record that would make him a star. "He'd play old Jean Orchery songs and Big Joe Turner's honey hush, which had just been cut by Johnny Benette."
**Middle B. Me (Jack Clement)** : This sensible country song was written by Cowboy Jack Clement, who initially wanted it to be the A-side of Jerry Lee Lewis's second single, with "Whole Lotta Shakin' Going On" relegated to the B-side. The song was inspired by Clement's personal experience of a breakup, imagining himself as a "turd" winking up at his ex from a toilet bowl, an idea he "cleaned up a little" for the song. "That was going to be the A side, of course, but they'd let Jerry Lee cut this shaking thing for the B side if he wanted."
**Whole Lotta Shakin' Going On (1957)** : This song became Jerry Lee Lewis's big hit, having been a highlight of his live show. Its authorship is disputed, with both Dave Curly Williams and Roy Hall (credited as Sonny Day David) claiming to have written it; Big Maybelle originally recorded it with an arrangement by Quincy Jones, and Roy Hall also recorded his own version on Decca. Jerry Lee's inspiration came from Johnny Little John, who performed a faster, lead-vocal version with a spoken section at a gig, which Jerry Lee then adopted for his own performances. Initially, Sam Phillips and Jack Clement doubted the song, thinking it too vulgar for radio and too similar to Elvis, preferring "Middle B. Me" as the A-side. However, after several takes during the recording session, which involved Clement and Phillips contributing ideas, lowering the bass in the mix, and adding slapback echo to the piano, the spontaneous-sounding result convinced everyone it had to be the A-side. "When Jerry Lee took his version of the song into Sun, convinced that this was going to be his big hit and neither Sam Phillips nor Jack Clement believed in it."

## curiosities
**Early Obsession with Music** : Jerry Lee Lewis, from Faraday, Louisiana, had an unparalleled obsession with music, driving him to create and giving him vast knowledge across genres, often playing piano for eight hours a day. "Lewis was the one who had the greatest obsession with his music, the greatest drive to create and the greatest sheer knowledge of music in all different genres."
**Al Jolson Incident** : On a date at the cinema, Lewis heard an Al Jolson song playing before the film, immediately left, cycled home, worked out the song on piano, then cycled back to rejoin his date, claiming he was gone so long because he picked up popcorn. "Lewis immediately got out of his seat, told his girlfriend he needed to use the toilet, cycled home, worked out how to play the song on the piano, cycled back and rejoined his date for the film."
**Child Prodigy** : At four years old, Jerry Lee felt an urge to play the piano at his rich uncle Lee Calhoun's house and almost instantly figured out "Silent Night." His parents, doting on him after the tragic death of his older brother, recognized his talent and nurtured it; his father even remortgaged his farm to buy Jerry Lee a piano and later wired their house for electricity so he could listen to the radio. "His parents, who always doated on him and tried to give him everything he wanted after the tragically young death of his older brother, realized that they might have a child prodigy on their hands."
**Pentecostal Upbringing and Rebellion** : Raised in a strict Pentecostal family where cinema was a sin, young Jerry Lee would often cause trouble, once dragging his double-first cousin, Jimmy Swaggart, to a movie, causing Swaggart to run out crying, convinced he was damned, while Jerry Lee stayed to watch the cowboys. "As a kid, Jerry Lee was always the one who would get his relatives into trouble."
**Musical Influences from Guitarists** : Despite being from Louisiana, a hub of great piano playing, Jerry Lee Lewis's main musical influences were guitarists, with Jimmie Rodgers being his favorite musician. "most of his musical influences were guitarists."
**Duality of Religion and Secular Life** : Jerry Lee Lewis was deeply religious and studied at the Southwestern Bible Institute to become a priest, but was expelled for playing a hymn with a boogie feel, which administrators deemed "inciting lust." This tension between religious and secular worlds would persist throughout his life, with him being "firmly on the side of the devil" by the time he signed with Sun Records at age 21. "This tension between religion and the secular world would recur throughout Lewis's life, but by the time he signed to Sun Records, aged 21, he was firmly on the side of the devil."
**Early Life Controversies - Marriages** : Before his fame, Jerry Lee Lewis had already been married twice, failing to divorce his first wife before marrying the second, and was contemplating leaving his second wife as well. "He'd already got married twice, and hadn't actually got around to divorcing his first wife before marrying the second."
**Sewing Machine Salesman** : Prior to his music career taking off, Jerry Lee Lewis made a living as a sewing machine salesman, employing a deceptive tactic of telling women they had won a machine in a contest to con them into buying one on credit. "He'd been making a living as a sewing machine-salesman, conning women into signing up to buy one on credit, by telling them they'd won the machine in a contest."
**Jealousy of Johnny Little John** : Jerry Lee Lewis toured with blind musician Paul Whitehead and bassist Johnny Little John, noting Little John's Elvis-like style with jealousy. "Little John had something of the style of Elvis, and Jerry Lee was jealous of him."
**Rejection by RCA and Grand Ole Opry** : Before signing with Sun Records, Jerry Lee Lewis tried RCA Records, the label Elvis was on, and the Grand Ole Opry, but was told he needed to play guitar, not piano, to get a chance. "They told him he needed to play a guitar."
**"The Great I Am" Nickname** : Jerry Lee Lewis was known to refer to himself blasphemously as "The Great I Am," reflecting his immense self-belief in his destiny to be a great star. "a man who had been known to refer to himself blasterously as The Great I Am."
**Auditioning for Sun Records** : Jerry Lee and his father drove to Memphis, staying in their first hotel with running water after selling hundreds of eggs from their hen house to afford it. When Sam Phillips was out, Jerry Lee refused to leave Sun Studio until someone listened to him, eventually convincing Cowboy Jack Clement to hear him play for three or four hours, making his piano sound "better than Chet Atkins on guitar." "It was now or never, and Jerry Lee was just going to stay there until someone listened to him play the piano."
**"Crazy Arms" - Father's Disappointment** : Jerry Lee Lewis believed his father, Elmo Lewis, wasn't impressed by "Crazy Arms" because Elmo had always wanted to be a musician but gave up playing piano when young Jerry Lee cried upon seeing his father play a song he himself was struggling with, fearing he would demoralize his son. "Jerry Lee believes to this day that the reason his dad wasn't hugely impressed by Jerry Lee's first record was just that, that seeing his son achieve an ambition he'd given up on himself was at best bittersweet."
**"Whole Lotta Shakin' Going On" - Inspiration and Adoption** : Jerry Lee Lewis adapted "Whole Lotta Shakin' Going On" from Johnny Little John's live performance, which was faster and included a spoken section encouraging the audience to "wiggle around just a little bit." Lewis later admitted to doing it "just like Johnny Duhit" and did not feel guilty, especially as it made women in the audience dance. "I had done it just like Johnny Duhit, maybe I should have felt guilty about that."
**"Whole Lotta Shakin' Going On" - Recording Anecdotes** : While Cowboy Jack Clement claimed the song was recorded in a single take with only three minutes of tape left, the reality involved several takes, with both Clement and Phillips contributing ideas, changing instrumentation, lowering the bass in the mix, and adding slapback echo to the piano, ultimately achieving a spontaneous sound. "There are different stories about the recording of whole lot of shaking going on."
**Early Touring with Cash and Perkins** : Jerry Lee Lewis toured with Johnny Cash, Carl Perkins, and Wanda Jackson, bringing only whiskey, comic books, and cherry bombs in addition to their clothes and instruments. He quickly rose from the third-billed act to the headliner because "nobody could follow him," leading to resentment from Perkins and Cash, who saw him as Sam Phillips's new golden boy and plotted to leave Sun. "The only things Jerry Lee and his band brought with them on the tour, other than their clothes and instruments, were whiskey, comic books and cherry bombs."
**Niagara Falls Detour** : During a tour, Jerry Lee insisted on a detour to Niagara Falls, got out of the car, stood for 30 seconds, declared, "Jerry Lee Lewis has seen the Niagara Falls, now let's go home boys," and got back in. "Jerry Lee Lewis has seen the Niagara Falls, now let's go home boys."
**Judd Phillips' Vision** : Sam Phillips' brother, Judd Phillips, who handled much of Sun's promotion, recognized Jerry Lee Lewis's star potential from his appearance and performance, particularly how his slicked-back hair would fall into "wild blonde shocks," and knew he needed to be on TV, specifically the Ed Sullivan or Steve Allen Show. "Judd did a lot of the promotion work for Son and he saw something in Jerry Lee."
**Steve Allen Show Booking** : Judd Phillips took Jerry Lee to New York for a 15-minute meeting with Steve Allen's manager and NBC's head of talent, arriving without usual booking materials while Jerry Lee read a Superman comic and blew bubble gum. Judd persuaded them to let Jerry Lee play, emphasizing that records couldn't capture his performance; his playing so impressed them that they offered $500 to keep him from other shows and booked him for an appearance in a month. "They booked him in for an appearance on the Steve Allen show in a month's time."
**Steve Allen Show Performance** : During his appearance, Jerry Lee Lewis famously screamed "shake," kicked his piano stool across the stage, which was then thrown back by Steve Allen, the host known for his contempt for rock and roll. This moment launched Jerry Lee Lewis into stardom. "That was the moment when Jerry Lee Lewis became a star."

**Clarence "Pinetop" Smith's Influence** : The spoken section in Jerry Lee Lewis's "Whole Lotta Shakin' Going On," encouraging the audience to "wiggle around just a little bit," was likely inspired by a similar passage in Clarence "Pinetop" Smith's song "Pinetop's Boogie Woogie."
