## songs
**Blue Yodel number six (Jimmie Rodgers Cover)** : Wanda Jackson performed this song by Jimmie Rodgers during her audition for a local TV show talent section when she was in her early teens, with her friends accompanying her to the station.
"she played Jimmy Rogers' Blue Yodel number six for the DJ who ran the show."
**First Single (Decca Records, Early Teens)** : This song was a duet with Billy Gray, a featured singer from Hank Thompson's band. Thompson, who ran the session, forced Jackson to sing it against her objections, as she preferred to debut as a solo artist rather than making her name from a duet. Despite her initial unhappiness, it became a top 10 country single by the time she started her senior year in high school.
"Her first single was a duet with another featured singer from Thompson's band Billy Gray. Thompson who was running the session basically forced Jackson to sing it against her objections."
**Without Your Love (Songwriter, 1950s)** : Wanda Jackson wrote this song for Bobby Lord, a singer with whom she appeared on the radio show Ozark Jubilee. The song achieved significant success, going to the top 10 on the country chart.
"That song had gone to the top 10 in the country chart."
**I Gotta Know (1956)** : This song was written by Thelma Blackmon, a friend's mother who had previously written songs for Jackson. Wanda decided it would be perfect to restart her career after Elvis Presley convinced her to move into Rockabilly. Recorded at Capitol Tower with session musicians like Jelly Sanders (fiddle), Joe Mephys (guitar), and Ralph Mooney (steel guitar), it became a big country hit, reaching number 15 on the country charts. The song is notable for its genre mixing, with the choruses being distinctly country and the verses falling into the rock and roll genre, a common technique for female performers transitioning into rock and roll. It featured Elvis-style hiccups on the title line, which came close to parody but contributed to its success.
"Agata No was both country and rock and roll. And the co-verses are definitely country. The verse is a family in the rock and roll genre."
**Hot Dog! That Made Him Mad (1956)** : This song had been Wanda Jackson's favorite in her stage act for years, and she would perform it with a tremendous growl on the title line, which consistently excited audiences. However, during the studio recording, she horrified producer Ken Nelson by drinking a large amount of milk, unaware of its detrimental effect on a singer's throat. As a result, she couldn't achieve the signature growl, and while the record was still good, it failed to become the massive success it otherwise would have been.
"Unfortunately, she horrified Ken Nelson in the studio by taking a big drink of milk while all the session musicians were on a coffee break. She hadn't realised what milk does to a singer's throat and when they came to record the song, she couldn't get her voice to do the growl that had always worked on stage."
**Don't Wanna (1950s)** : This Calypso song, written by Budlow Bryant, was presented to Wanda Jackson by producer Ken Nelson in an attempt to capitalize on the Calypso craze. It was considered a poor choice, not even Bryant's best work, and was further marred by Nelson's suggestion to add a racist accent, which Jackson disliked even at the time. It went "down like a lead balloon" on the charts.
"Probably the worst example was a terrible attempt to capitalise on the current Colipso craze, a song called Dono Wanna which was written by Budlow Bryant, one of the great songletters of the 50s but which wouldn't have been his best effort even before it was given a racist accent at Nelson's suggestion and which Jackson finished it doing even at the time, let alone 60 years later."
**Cool Love (1950s)** : Co-written by Wanda Jackson with her friend Vicki Contriemann, this song was possibly too closely modeled after Elvis Presley's recent hits, even down to the backing vocals. It featured a "great Buccoins guitar solo" (likely a reference to a Buck Owens-style guitar solo) and Jackson was clearly engaged with the material, making it a fun record. Despite this, it did not achieve chart success.
"Much better was Cool Love, which Jackson co-wrote herself with her friend Vicki Contriemann i pet and uh That one is possibly too closely muddled after Elvis's recent hits, right down to the backing vocals, but it features a great Buccoins guitar solo, it's fun, and Jackson is clearly engaged with the material."

## curiosities
**Early Life and Migration** : Born in Oklahoma, Wanda Jackson and her family migrated to California during the tail end of the Great Depression, as "stragglers in the Great Migration" of "Oak Ease." They lived in Greenfield, a few miles from Bakersfield, where her father worked. This put her in the same musical and social environment as future Bakersfield Sound musicians, before they moved back to Oklahoma a few years later.
"Jackson was born in Oklahoma but as this was the tail end of the Great Depression she and her family migrated to California when she was small as stragglers in the Great Migration that permanently changed California."
**Exposure to Western Music (Ages 5-10)** : Due to her parents' inability to afford babysitters, Wanda Jackson frequently accompanied them to dances on weekends, where she saw "almost every great western band of the 40s." This early exposure shaped her musical tastes and aspirations, leading her to admire artists like Spade Cooley and The Maddox Brothers and Rose.
"between the ages of 5 and 10 she seems to have seen almost every great western band of the 40s."
**Childhood Musical Aspirations** : Before even starting school, Wanda Jackson was deeply influenced by Rose Maddox's stage presence and decided she wanted to be a "girl singer just like Rose Maddox." Her father supported this ambition, buying her a guitar from the Sears Robuck catalogue when she was six and teaching her chords. They would play together nightly, with her mother writing down chords for songs they learned from radio and records.
"She decided before she was even in school that she wanted to be a girl singer as she put it just like Rose Maddox."
**Self-Taught Yodeling** : Wanda Jackson taught herself to yodel by copying Alton Britt's record "Chime Bells," a skill that was essential for country and western singers at the time, dating back to Jimmie Rodgers.
"The record she copied to learn to yodel was Chime Bells by Alton Britt, Country Music's"
**Early TV Show and Mentorship (14 years old)** : At 14, after auditioning for a local TV show, Hank Thompson discovered Wanda Jackson. Initially unaware of her age due to her mature voice, he invited her to perform with his band. Although she had never played with a band and felt she wasn't good initially, Thompson mentored her, teaching her stage performance and eventually inviting her to his local TV show, which led to her getting her own show in Oklahoma.
"He hadn't realised she was only 14 because her voice made her sound so much older."
**Rejection by Capitol Records (Underage)** : While Wanda Jackson was still in high school, Hank Thompson recorded demos of her singing and presented them to Ken Nelson at Capitol Records. Nelson liked her voice but declined to sign her due to her being under 18, citing legal complications and the fact that she hadn't finished school.
"Nelson liked her voice but when he found out she was under 18 he decided to pass on recording her just due to the legal complications and the fact that she'd not yet finished school."
**Grand Ole Opry Scandal (Uncovered Shoulders)** : During her first performance at the Grand Ole Opry, Wanda Jackson was told she could not go on stage in her chosen dress because her shoulders were uncovered, which was deemed "obscene" by the establishment. At the time, she was one of the only country singers trying to present a glamorous image rather than a "farm girl" look. When she complied by wearing a jacket, she was further humiliated by comedy acts who mocked her throughout her set, confirming the country establishment's resistance to her style.
"She was told she couldn't go on stage in the dress she was wearing because her shoulders were uncovered and that was obscene."
**Discovery of Elvis Presley and Influence** : After leaving school and becoming a full-time musician, Jackson's father, her manager, secured a booking agent named Bob Neal in Memphis. Neal arranged for her to tour with a new singer he managed, Elvis Presley, and Johnny Cash. Backstage at the first show, she heard the "terrible screaming" from Elvis's fans and immediately recognized he was not an ordinary country singer. They started dating, and Elvis gave her his ring, which she still possesses. He had a profound influence on her, teaching her to strum an acoustic guitar with a single strum, a more rock and roll style, and convincing her to pursue rockabilly music.
"The screaming was of course at Elvis and immediately wondered you that he was not any ordinary country singer."
**Father's Disapproval of Elvis** : Wanda Jackson's father, who managed her, was unimpressed with Elvis Presley's stage performance, criticizing his energetic movements and vocal style. Despite his reservations, Elvis profoundly influenced Wanda's musical direction.
"Her father was not impressed with Elvis' performance saying that boy's got to get his show in order, he's all over the stage messing around and he's got to stop slurping his words too."
**Producer Missteps** : After the relative failure of "Hot Dog! That Made Him Mad," producer Ken Nelson struggled to find suitable material for Wanda Jackson at Capitol Records. While her own rockabilly tracks often lacked the power of her live shows, Nelson's choices were particularly poor, leading to records that failed commercially, such as the Calypso-influenced "Don't Wanna."
"Nelson floundered around for quite a while, trying to find something else that could work for Jackson."
**Japan Success** : Despite facing difficulties with chart success in the United States, Wanda Jackson achieved her biggest success overseas in Japan. She became a significant rock and roll star there, an early example of an artist finding fame abroad before achieving it in their home country.
"she was big in Japan."

## curiosities
**Honky Tonk Singer and Rockabilly Precursor** : Hank Thompson was a honky tonk singer who performed a "paired down version" of Bob Wills' western swing style. His music incorporated the rhythms and instrumentation of western swing but emphasized vocals and non-instrumental solos. Thompson was one of several artists whose music served as a precursor to rockabilly. He was most successful with mid-tempo ballads, such as "The Wild Side of Life."
"She was a hunky-tunk singer who performed a paired down version of the Bob Will's style of western swing."
**Mentor to Wanda Jackson** : Thompson discovered Wanda Jackson when she was 14 during a TV audition. Impressed by her voice, he invited her to perform with his band, even though she was inexperienced. He couldn't take her on tour due to her schooling but mentored her on stage whenever he played in Oklahoma, teaching her how to perform with other musicians. He also invited her to appear on his local TV show, which eventually led to her own TV show. Thompson later facilitated her signing with Capitol Records by informing Ken Nelson that she was no longer underage.
"Thompson had faith in her. He couldn't take her on the road because she was still so young she had to go to school, but every time he played Oklahoma he'd invite her to do a few numbers with his band, mentoring her and teaching her on stage how to perform with other musicians."

# genre - Rockabilly

## curiosities
**Origins in California Western Swing** : Rockabilly music originated from the western swing created by Dust Bowl migrants in California during the 1940s. This music, influenced by blues and boogie woogie, then made its way eastward to Tennessee, where it "transmuted itself into rockability." Jack Guthrie's "Oki Boogie" is cited as a clear precursor.
"It was the western swing that was being made by Dust Bowl migrants in California in the 1940s that when it made its way Eastwoods to Tennessee, transmuted itself into rockability."
**Female Performers and Genre Mixing** : It was common for female performers, particularly those transitioning into rock and roll from other genres, to mix styles within a single song. This often involved having a verse in one style and a chorus in another (as Wanda Jackson did with "I Gotta Know") or other combinations (like Kay Starr's "Rock and Roll Waltz"). This ambiguity allowed established stars to hedge their bets, portraying the music as either a parody or a mockery of new styles, in case rock and roll proved to be a temporary fad.
"But this kind of genre mixing was very common particularly with women and particularly with women who had previously had careers outside rock and roll and were trying to transition into it."

# curiosity

**The Oakies Migration (1930s)** : The Great Depression, coupled with an "almost apocalyptic scale" environmental disaster caused by poor farming practices across the middle of America, led to the migration of three and a half million people from the Great Plains. A large number moved to California, where they became known as "Oak Ease" regardless of their actual state of origin. This group formed a "despised underclass," which, as historical patterns suggest, often leads to the creation of innovative music. Their music in California was distinct from eastern country music, heavily incorporating blues and boogie woogie.
"As entire states became almost uninhabitable, three and a half million people moved from the Great Plains to elsewhere in the US and a large number of them moved to California where no matter what state they actually came from they became known as Oak Ease."
**Bakersfield Sound Genesis (1950s)** : Bakersfield, California, became a significant musical hub in the 1950s, particularly for musicians like Buck Owens, Merle Haggard, and Wynn Stewart. Most of these artists were from Dust Bowl migrant families ("Oak Ease") and developed a "tough form of honky tongue country in western" that blended influences from Hillbilly Boogie and western swing. Wanda Jackson spent her formative years in the same musical and social environment.
"Bakersfield would become in the 1950s the place where musicians like Book Owens, Merl Haggard and Wins Stewart...developed a tough form of honky tongue country in western that was influenced by Hillbilly Boogie and western swing."
**Review Style Tours** : A type of tour common in music, where one main act introduces and performs with several featured guests on stage, rather than multiple acts each doing their own separate set. Johnny Otis, James Brown, and Hank Thompson's show are cited as examples of this style.
"There were basically two types of tours that happened. Package tours featuring multiple different acts doing their own things and reviews where one main act would introduce several featured guests to join them on stage."
**Ken Nelson's Material Selection Issues** : Producer Ken Nelson, known for his work with Wanda Jackson and Gene Vincent at Capitol Records, had a recurring issue where material chosen by the artists themselves (or covers they picked) would be successful, but songs he brought in would often fail commercially. This pattern was observed with both Jackson and Vincent.
"Just like with Vincent, whenever Wanda brought in her own material or material she picked to cover by other people, it worked fine but when Nelson brought in something it would go down like a lead balloon."
