## albums
**Surfin' USA (March 1963)** : The group's second album, released only four months after their debut. It featured the immensely successful title song, which reached number two on the charts. Brian Wilson assumed production duties for this album, driven by his dissatisfaction with Nick Vene's production on the first record. "Surfing USA was the title song of the group's second album, released in March 1963 only four months after their first."
**Surfer Girl (September 1963)** : This album marked a significant evolution in The Beach Boys' sound. It expanded from the rudimentary vocal arrangements of their first two albums to incorporate more of The Four Freshmen's influence, a shift most strikingly evident on the track "In My Room". This was the first album on which Alan Jardine appeared, contributing to the group's vocal harmonies. "the surfer girl album, the first one on which Jardine appears. Seized Brian expanding from the rather rudimentary vocal arrangements of the first two albums, to something that incorporates a lot more of the influence of the four freshmen."
**Little Deuce Coupe (October 1963)** : Released a mere month after "Surfer Girl", this album was conceived entirely around car-related themes. Due to a scarcity of new material, the album was filled out with four songs recycled from earlier releases, including two tracks from the "Surfer Girl" album. David Marks expressed frustration that his song, "Custom Car Show," was not deemed suitable for inclusion. "That album was made up entirely of songs about cars, and the group was so short of material that the album ended up being filled out with four songs from earlier albums, including two from the surfer girl album, really, only the previous month."
**Shutdown Volume 2 (March 1964)** : This album, released in March 1964, featured the song "Don't Worry Baby", which had been recorded in January of the same year. The track was originally conceived and intended for The Ronettes. "It had been recorded in January, and had already been released on their shutdown volume 2 album in March."
**All Summer Long (1964)** : This was one of four albums released by The Beach Boys in 1964, alongside "Shutdown Volume 2", a live album, and a Christmas album, showcasing the group's prolific output during this period. "In 1964, the group once again released four albums, shut down volume 2, all some along, a live album and a Christmas album."
**The Beach Boys Today (Early 1965)** : Most of the recording work for this album was completed in 1964, with its official release scheduled for early 1965. During these sessions, Brian Wilson's increasing ambition was evident, both in the emotional depth of the music and the complexity of his arrangements. He progressively incorporated session musicians to augment the group's sound. "they also did most of the work on yet another album, The Beach Boys Today, which would be released in early 1965."

## songs
**Surf and Safari (1962)** : The Beach Boys' first national hit, which was instrumental in securing their contract with Capitol Records. This surf song was paired with the car-themed "409" as its B-side. "released their first national hit, Surf and Safari, backed with 409."
**409 (1962)** : Released as the B-side to "Surf and Safari," this car song contributed to the group's initial success and helped them secure a national record deal. "released their first national hit, Surf and Safari, backed with 409."
**Ten Little Indians (1962)** : Following "Surf and Safari," Capitol Records made an "incomprehensible decision" to release this album track as a single. Co-written by Brian Wilson and Gary Usher, it was a surf rock interpretation of the nursery rhyme, featuring "Kimo Sabay" chants in the backing vocals. It only managed to reach number 49 on the charts and is widely considered a weak follow-up, possibly released at the instigation of manager Murray Wilson to diminish Gary Usher's influence. "That track, a surf rock version of The Nursery Rhyme, with the group chanting, Kimo Sabay in the backing vocals, made only number 49 on the charts."
**Shut Down (1963)** : This car song, co-written by Brian Wilson and Roger Christian (who assisted with car-related lyrics), served as a top 30 B-side. It is particularly notable for featuring one of the very few instrumental contributions from lead singer Mike Love, a two-note saxophone solo that, despite some fan mockery, fit the record effectively. "Shutdown is most notable for being one of the very small number of Beach Boys records to feature an instrumental contribution from Mike Love, the group's lead singer."
**Surfin' USA (1963)** : This song became a massive hit, peaking at number two on the charts and later being named Billboard's biggest hit of 1963. Brian Wilson drew inspiration from Chuck Berry's "Sweet Little Sixteen" for its melody and the concept of listing place names. With help from his girlfriend's brother and Mike Love, he transformed it into a song about surfing locations. Due to its close resemblance to Berry's track, Chuck Berry received the sole songwriting credit, resulting in a substantial financial benefit for him as "Surfin' USA" continues to be played on oldies radio, in commercials, and films. "Surfing USA will all be planning out a room. Surfing USA became a huge hit, reaching number two on the chart, and later being named by Billboard as the biggest hit of 1963."
**In My Room (1963)** : This track represented a major advancement in the group's sound and the thematic scope of their songwriting, particularly in its poignant juxtaposition of lyrical theme and musical arrangement. The lyrics, penned by Gary Usher but inspired by Brian Wilson's personal experiences, explore themes of solitude. Musically, it progresses from Brian singing alone to a complex, layered vocal arrangement where Carl and Dennis, then Mike and Alan, join in, symbolizing familial support amidst the feeling of being alone. "In My Room is a major, major step forward in the group's sound, in the themes that would appear in their songwriting for the next few years, and in the juxtaposition of the lyrical theme and the musical arrangement."
**The Warmth of the Sun (1964)** : A composition of remarkable harmonic sophistication, written by Brian Wilson with lyrics by Mike Love around the time of the Kennedy assassination (though the precise timing is debated by the two). It begins with a standard Doo-wop progression (C, A minor) but quickly veers into an unexpected E flat chord, an innovative move for teen pop. It then returns to a D minor, holding it longer than usual, before a normal G, and finally a G augmented chord that ties into the earlier unexpected change, showcasing a harmonic complexity akin to modern jazz. "The warmth of the sun is quite staggeringly harmonically sophisticated."
**Fun, Fun, Fun (January 1, 1964)** : Recorded on January 1, 1964, this song fused two of Brian Wilson's key influences. It features Mike Love's witty lyrics and an opening intro directly borrowed from Chuck Berry's "Johnny B. Good". While maintaining a Chuck Berry feel overall, the verse adopts a distinct harmonic direction, utilizing a 1-4-1-5 progression reminiscent of The Ronettes' "Be My Baby," though applied to a different effect. It became the group's fourth top 10 hit, reaching number five on the charts, despite intense competition from The Beatles. "Fun, fun, fun, have lyrics by Mike Love, saw of his wittiest, and start out with an intro taken straight from Johnny B. Good."
**I Get Around (April 1964)** : This song became The Beach Boys' first number one hit, with recording commencing in April 1964. Brian Wilson began incorporating additional session musicians to augment the core band, expanding the instrumental palette. The basic track featured Carl Wilson (lead and rhythm guitar), Alan Jardine (bass), Dennis Wilson (drums), and Brian Wilson (keyboards), enhanced by two six-string bass players (Glenn Campbell, May Pullman), two saxophones, and percussion (Hal Blaine). Unusually, the primary chordal support was provided by a harpsichord, an instrument Brian would frequently use in subsequent years. The lyrics, penned by Mike Love but reflecting Brian Wilson's sentiments, hinted at a desire for change and new experiences ("I'm getting both driving up and down the same old strip, I've got to find a new place where the kids are hip"). It achieved Top 10 status in the UK and became a particular favorite within London's mod subculture. "I get around is a worthy classic, but the B side, don't worry baby, is there anything even better?"
**Don't Worry Baby (January 1964 / Released March 1964)** : Recorded in January 1964, this song was first released on the "Shutdown Volume 2" album in March, and then famously as the B-side to "I Get Around". Originally intended for The Ronettes, it was directly inspired by "Be My Baby," a record that deeply impressed Brian Wilson, causing him to pull over upon hearing its drum intro for the first time. Wilson meticulously absorbed its nuances, reworking its basic rhythm, chord sequence, and melodic fragments into a distinct song. Phil Spector ultimately turned it down for The Ronettes. The lyrics, written by Roger Christian, ostensibly describe a car race but cleverly subvert the teen tragedy genre by focusing on the protagonist's profound anxiety and insecurity, rather than a crash. The girlfriend's role as a supportive, "supernaturally good woman" who understands his fears speaks to male insecurity and the inability to live up to traditional masculine roles, providing an emotional counterpoint to the bravado of the A-side, "I Get Around." It charted independently, reaching number 24. "Don't worry baby, released as I get around B side. Made the charts in its own light, peaking at number 24."

## curiosities
**Four Albums a Year (1963-1964)** : During both 1963 and 1964, The Beach Boys maintained an exceptionally demanding release schedule, putting out four albums annually. This prolific output, coupled with intensive touring, ultimately contributed to the immense pressure on Brian Wilson. "We're going to look at a period in which they released four albums a year, had three line-up changes and saw their first number one and after song which, despite being a B side, regularly makes lists of the best singles of all time."
**Three Line-up Changes (1963-1964)** : The group underwent significant personnel shifts between 1963 and 1964, experiencing three line-up changes. These included Alan Jardine's return, David Marks' departure, and Brian Wilson's temporary cessation of touring, which necessitated Glenn Campbell stepping in. "We're going to look at a period in which they released four albums a year, had three line-up changes and saw their first number one and after song which, despite being a B side, regularly makes lists of the best singles of all time."
**Murray Wilson's Interference (Late 1962 - April 1964)** : Murray Wilson, the father of Brian, Carl, and Dennis Wilson, and the group's manager, was known for actively interfering in the band's career. He reportedly instigated the release of the weak single "Ten Little Indians" to reduce the influence of co-writer Gary Usher, as Murray preferred to keep the business within the family. His persistent interference, particularly during the recording session for "I Get Around" when he repeatedly criticized the bass line, ultimately led Brian Wilson to fire him as manager in April 1964, marking a turning point for the band's autonomy. "Some have suggested in fact that the record was released at the instigation of Moby Wilson, who was both Brian Carl and Dennis Wilson's father and the group's manager, as a way of weakening also's influence with the group, as Moby didn't want outsiders interfering in what he saw as a family business."
**Brian Wilson Quits Touring (April 1963)** : Overwhelmed by the strenuous combination of recording multiple albums per year and an intensive touring schedule, which sometimes involved four shows in a single day, Brian Wilson decided to stop touring with the group in April 1963, shortly after the release of "Surfin' USA". He was subsequently replaced by Alan Jardine for live performances. "Unsurprisingly, Brian decided that this was too much for one person, and so in April 1963, just after the release of Surf in USA, he decided to quit touring with the group."
**David Marks' Departure (October 1963)** : David Marks' exit from the band was precipitated by a series of events, including manager Murray Wilson's long-standing dislike for him (rooted in Murray's failed affair with David's mother) and Murray's fury after discovering David and Dennis Wilson had both contracted an STI from the same sex worker on David's 15th birthday. Murray's subsequent harassment, combined with David's mother's encouragement to leave and Brian Wilson's refusal to include David's song "Custom Car Show" on the "Little Deuce Coupe" album, led to his resignation, which was backdated to his initial "I quit" statement. He played his final show on October 5, 1963. "At first, the rest of the group thought that he was joking, and even he wasn't at all sure that he wanted to leave the group altogether."
**Alan Jardine's Role (1963-1964)** : Alan Jardine initially rejoined The Beach Boys in April 1963 to act as a touring substitute for Brian Wilson, performing bass on stage and singing Brian's vocal parts. He soon began recording with the group, significantly enriching their studio sound by allowing Brian to play keyboards and enhancing their vocal harmonies. Despite his contributions, he was not credited on the covers of the first few albums he appeared on. By October 1963, he was recognized as a full member for publicity purposes, transitioning to rhythm guitar as Brian temporarily returned to bass, although he remained on a salary rather than an equal partnership for many years. "Jardine was now, for publicity purposes, a full member of the group, though he would remain on a salary rather than an equal partner for many years."
**Brian Wilson's Relationship with Marilyn Revol (Early 1960s)** : During this period, Brian Wilson was in the process of ending a relationship with his then-girlfriend and beginning a new one with Marilyn Revol. Marilyn, along with her sister Diane and cousin Ginger Blake, had formed a girl group for whom Brian became actively involved in writing and producing records. "Wilson was also in the process of breaking up with his girlfriend and starting a relationship with a young woman named Marilyn Revol."
**Brian Wilson's Mental Breakdown (December 1964)** : The extreme mental strain caused by constant touring finally led to Brian Wilson suffering a breakdown in December 1964 while on a plane to Texas, where he was heard screaming and refused to disembark. Although he managed to perform that night's show, he immediately flew back to Los Angeles, effectively retiring from the road. Glenn Campbell was subsequently brought in to replace him on stage for the next five months. "The mental strain of touring had finally got too much for Brian, and in December, on a plane to Texas, he'd had a breakdown, screaming on the plane and refusing to get off."
**Response to The Beatles (1963-1964)** : The Beach Boys, particularly Brian Wilson, consciously elevated their musical artistry and sophistication in direct response to the emergence and success of The Beatles. This determination to "up his game" was intensified after their song "Fun, Fun, Fun" reached number five on the charts, while The Beatles simultaneously held the top three positions. "Brian Wilson realised that he needed to up his game if he was going to compete, and he did."
**Brian Wilson's Production Style Evolution (1964)** : As recording sessions progressed in 1964, Brian Wilson's artistic ambition significantly increased, evident in his focus on the emotional impact of the music and the complexity of his arrangements. He increasingly employed session musicians to augment the group's sound and developed a distinctive production style. This style was a variation of Phil Spector's "Wall of Sound," but one that prioritized "gentle fragility" over Spector's characteristic "storm and drang." "As these recordings progressed, Brian Wilson was more and more ambitious, both in terms of the emotional effect of the music and his arrangements, increasingly using session musicians to augment the group, and trying for a variance of full spectre's production style, but one which emphasised gentle fragility rather than storm and drang."
**Re-purposing Girl Group Style (1964)** : In songs such as "Don't Worry Baby," Brian Wilson demonstrated a unique ability to adapt the emotional and musical aesthetics of girl group songs to explore themes of emotional vulnerability. This approach often delved into acknowledging and processing aspects of what is now termed "toxic masculinity," a subject rarely tackled in music of that era. "This repurposing of the emotional and musical style of girl group songs, to deal with the emotional vulnerability that comes from acknowledging and attempting to process toxic masculinity, is something that few other songwriters were capable of at this point."
**Unreleased Track for Glenn Campbell (1964)** : What was considered potentially Brian Wilson's greatest track created in 1964 was not utilized by The Beach Boys themselves. Instead, it was given to Glenn Campbell as a gesture of gratitude for his crucial role in temporarily filling in for Brian on tour after his mental breakdown. "Possibly the greatest track he created in 1964 ended up not being used by The Beach Boys, though, but was given to Glenn Campbell."

## curiosities
**Formation and Lack of Commercial Success (Post-October 1963)** : The Marksmen were formed by David Marks after his departure from The Beach Boys on October 5, 1963, and they subsequently signed with A&M Records. Despite rumors suggesting manager Murray Wilson threatened DJs to prevent airplay, the band's lack of commercial success was primarily attributed to the perceived poor quality of their "shoddy sounding, garage rock records," which had minimal commercial potential in the 1963 market. Their "flatter and more affectless vocals" are noted as prefiguring certain punk singers, though this style lacked commercial viability at the time. "He played his last show with the group on October the 5th, 1963, and then formed his own band, The Marksman, who sang to A&M."

# artist - Chuck Berry

## songs
**Sweet Little Sixteen (1958)** : Brian Wilson drew direct inspiration from this song, specifically its melody and the opening verse's distinctive recitation of place names, which he subsequently adapted for The Beach Boys' "Surfin' USA." Due to the pronounced similarities, Chuck Berry was awarded the sole songwriting credit for "Surfin' USA," a decision that proved financially advantageous for him as "Surfin' USA" became a perennial oldies hit, unlike "Sweet Little Sixteen" itself. "Brian Wilson had been inspired by Chuck Berry's sweet little 16, and in particular the opening verse, which had just listed a lot of places."
**Johnny B. Good (1958)** : The Beach Boys' song "Fun, Fun, Fun" distinctively opens with an instrumental intro that is directly appropriated from Chuck Berry's iconic "Johnny B. Good." "Fun, fun, fun, have lyrics by Mike Love, saw of his wittiest, and start out with an intro taken straight from Johnny B. Good."

# genre - Teen Tragedy Records

## curiosities
**Common Narrative Structure (1960s Pop Music)** : Teen tragedy records prevalent in the 1960s frequently featured dramatic narratives, often centered around car races or other perilous situations, culminating in the protagonist's untimely death, as exemplified by songs like "Dead Man's Curve" or "Tell Laura I Love Her." The Beach Boys' "Don't Worry Baby" initially sets up this familiar scenario but then subverts it by focusing on the protagonist's intense emotional anxiety rather than a physical catastrophe. "The lyric is ostensibly about a car race, and indeed it seems to be setting up precisely the kind of situation that was common in teen tragedy records of the period."

# curiosity

**Rare Instance of Black Artist Benefiting from Rip-off (Rock Music History)** : In the annals of rock music history, it is an exceptionally rare occurrence for a black artist to financially benefit when a white artist "rips off" or heavily borrows from their work. This unique situation transpired with Chuck Berry, who was awarded the sole songwriting credit and accrued substantial financial gains from The Beach Boys' "Surfin' USA" due to its close melodic and structural resemblance to his song "Sweet Little Sixteen." "As the song was so close to sweet little 16, Chuck Berry got the soul songwriting credit, one of the only times in rock music history where a white artist has ripped off a black one, and a black artist has actually benefited from it."
**Toxic Masculinity and Emotional Vulnerability (General Music Theme)** : The exploration of emotional vulnerability that stems from acknowledging and attempting to process "toxic masculinity" was a nascent and uncommon theme in the music of the era. Brian Wilson's "Don't Worry Baby" is highlighted as a significant early example, providing an emotional truth often absent in contemporary music, even if it presented a flawed view of gender relations. John Lennon's later work is also noted for approaching similar emotional landscapes. "What we have here is someone trying to deal with a particular kind of anxiety, thought about by what we now refer to as toxic masculinity."
**The Supernaturally Good Woman Trope (General Music Theme)** : This recurring trope describes a specific female character in Brian Wilson's songs (and later in John Lennon's work): a "supernaturally good woman" who deeply understands and loves the protagonist, often despite or because of his flaws, even appearing "too good for him." She serves as a "saved breath figure" who enables the man to cope with his emotional problems, highlighting male insecurity and the perceived inability to manage emotional burdens without female intervention, as seen in "Don't Worry Baby." "His saved breath figure will see a lot more of him Brian's songs, whoever the lyricist, the supernaturally good woman who understands the protagonist and loves him despite, or because of, his faults, even though she's too good for him."
