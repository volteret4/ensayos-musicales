## albums

## songs
**Spanish Harlem (Year Not Specified, pre-Stand By Me)** : This song was King's third solo record after leaving The Drifters, and it marked his first significant solo success, making the top ten. It was co-written by Libra and Specter, with Stuller later adding a prominent instrumental line for which he didn't claim credit. Initially planned as a B-side, its popularity quickly led to the record being flipped to the A-side. "Spanish Harlem made the top ten."
**Amore (Year Not Specified, post-Stand By Me)** : Benny King recorded a version of this old standard, which reached number 19 on the charts after the initial success of "Stand By Me." However, his subsequent two records performed less well, topping out at 66 and 56. "He recorded a version of the old Standard Amore, which made number 19."
**Don't Play That Song (You Lied) (Year Not Specified)** : Co-written by Benny King's wife and Armater, this song reached number 11 on the pop charts and became an R&B standard, marking a return to the pop top 20 for King after some less successful singles. "He did get back in the pop-top 20, with the song co-written by his wife and armate Erdogan, don't play that song, you lied, which reached number 11 and became an R&B Standard."
**I Who Have Nothing (Year Not Specified)** : This French song, with English lyrics specifically written for King by Libra and Stuller, was part of King's attempt to transition into the adult sub-club singer genre. It managed to reach number 29, but most of his other efforts in this style were commercially unsuccessful. "While his version of I Who Have Nothing, a French song that has since become a Standard, and whose English lyrics were written for King by Libre and Stuller, managed to reach number 29."
**What Is Soul (Year Not Specified, later 60s)** : This is cited as one of the "great records" Benny King made in the later 1960s, indicating his continued work in the soul genre, even though he experienced less chart success at the time. "He made some great records in the later 60s, like What Is Soul."
**Supernatural Thing (1975)** : After re-signing to Atlantic, Benny King achieved a one-off top 10 hit with this song in 1975, marking a brief return to significant chart success after several years of touring and recording for smaller labels. "Benny King, meanwhile, had signed again to Atlantic and had a one-off top 10 hit with supernatural thing in 1975."
**Stand By Me (1986 Resurgence)** : The song experienced a dramatic resurgence in 1986 when it was used as the theme song for the film "Stand By Me." This led to the single re-entering the US top 10 and reaching number one in the UK, 25 years after its initial success, making King the first person to hit the top 10 in the US across four decades. "On the back of the film's success, the song re-entered the top 10, 25 years after its initial success, and made number one in the UK."

## curiosities
**Early Solo Career - Unsuccessful Records** : After leaving The Drifters, King released a couple of solo records before "Spanish Harlem," including a duet with Lover and Baker, which were not commercially successful. "The first of these was not a success, and Noah was the second, a duet with Lover and Baker."
**Move to Adult Sub-Club Singer** : Benny King attempted to move into the more lucrative world of adult sub-club singing, performing songs like "I Could Have Danced All Night" and "What Now My Love," but this genre did not resonate with his audience who expected R&B from him, leading to poor chart performance for these efforts. "He sang I Could Have Dance Doll Night, and what now my love, perfectly well, but that wasn't what the audience wanted from him."
**Departure from Atlantic (1969)** : In 1969, Benny King requested to be released from his contract with Atlantic Records, subsequently spending several years in the early 1970s recording for smaller labels. "He asked to be let go from his contract with Atlantic in 1969, and spent a few years in the early 70s recording for small labels."
**Joining UK Drifters (1980s)** : In the 1980s, Benny King moved to the UK and joined the UK version of The Drifters, performing his old hits on the nostalgia circuit. His presence was seen as adding more authenticity to the Johnny Moor lineup of the group. "And in the 80s, he moved to the UK and joined the UK version of the Drifters, singing his old hits on the nostalgia circuit with them, and adding more authenticity to the Johnny Moor lineup of the group."
**Four-Decade US Top 10 Artist** : Following the 1986 resurgence of "Stand By Me," Benny King became the first person to have achieved a top 10 hit in the US across four different decades: the 1950s, 1960s, 1970s, and 1980s, a remarkable achievement given his relatively few overall hits. "King became the first person to have hit the top 10 in the US, in the 50s, 60s, 70s and 80s."
**Legacy and Death (2015)** : Benny King, described as the only member of The Drifters other than Clyde McPhatter to break away and achieve solo success, spent the last 29 years of his life touring as a solo artist after the renewed success of "Stand By Me." He passed away in 2015, leaving a lasting impact on rock, pop, soul, and R&B music through his signature song. "He died in 2015, but as long as people listen to Rock, Pop, Soul or R and B, there'll be people listening to stand by me."

## curiosities
**Legal Problems and King's Contract (Pre-solo)** : The Drifters faced legal issues when contracts for individual members were sold to George Tradwell, owner of the Drifters name. Benny King's contract was not included in this sale, leading to an unusual situation where King sang lead on studio records but was not allowed to tour with the group, necessitating a soundalike singer for live performances. "When the contracts for the individual members had been sold to George Tradwell, the owner of the drifters name, Benny King's contract had not been sold with the rest."
**Studio vs. Touring Lineup (Pre-solo)** : Due to Benny King's contract not being sold with the rest of the group, there were five Drifters in the studio (including King) but only four in the touring group, with Johnny Lee Williams singing King's parts on stage. This "unworkable arrangement" was surprisingly successful initially. "So there were now five drifters in the studio, but only four of them in the touring group."
**Benny King's Departure** : After "Save the Last Dance for Me" became a number one hit and King's last single with the group, his manager Lover Patterson reached an agreement with Drifters manager George Treadwell, allowing King to transition smoothly into a solo career. There were also reports of King Records negotiating to buy the Drifters' contract from Atlantic. "After King's manager Lover Patterson came to an agreement with the drifters manager George Treadwell, the woodluck King moves smoothly into a solo career."
**Post-King Personnel Shifts** : After Benny King's departure, Atlantic Records began releasing material from their vault, including songs with King's leads and older records. The group then experienced further significant personnel changes. "After King left, Atlantic started releasing whatever material they had in their vault, both songs with King's Leeds and older records from the earlier lineup of drifters."
**Johnny Lee Williams' Departure (Mobile, Alabama)** : During a tour, Johnny Lee Williams decided to leave the Drifters in Mobile, Alabama, expressing dissatisfaction with not getting to sing lead vocals. He was originally from Mobile. His departure necessitated finding a new replacement. "When they were on tour and got to Mobile, Alabama, Johnny Lee Williams said that he was just going to stay there and not continue on the tour."
**James Poindexter and Stage Fright** : James Poindexter was brought in as a replacement for Johnny Lee Williams but suffered from stage fright, leading the group to perform as a trio for several months before Rudy Lewis joined as the new lead singer. "The drifters got in a replacement for Williams, James Pointexter, but he turned out to have stage fright, and the group spent several months as a trio."
**Al's Baby Hubbed Drafted** : Al's Baby Hubbed, the group's bass singer, was drafted, leading to further lineup instability as the group went through several singers before settling on Tommy Evans, who had previous experience with earlier Drifters lineups. "And then Al's Baby Hubbed, the group's bass singer, was drafted, and the group got in a couple of different singers before settling on Tommy Evans."
**Rudy Lewis's Solo Aspirations and Death** : Rudy Lewis, who had been the lead singer since shortly after King's departure, considered a solo career and even released an unsuccessful solo single. Tragically, he died suddenly on May 21, 1964, the same day the Drifters were scheduled to record their last US top 10 hit. "But Rudy Lewis, the lead singer of the group since just after King had left, was thinking of going solo and even released one solo single."
**George Treadwell's Insurance Plan** : Aware of Rudy Lewis's potential solo departure, manager George Treadwell brought back Johnny Moore, who had been in the group in the 1950s and had recently left The Drapers, to trade off lead vocals with Lewis in the studio for several months as a contingency. "So he got Johnny Moore, who had been in the group in the 50s and had just left the drapers to join, and for a few months Lewis and Moore traded off leads in the studio."
**Rudy Lewis's Sudden Death (May 21, 1964)** : Tragedy struck on May 21, 1964, when Rudy Lewis died suddenly. Johnny Moore was informed of the news by Sylvia Van Topoe of Mickey and Sylvia. Despite the devastating news, the group proceeded to the studio to record as scheduled, including songs like "I Don't Want to Go On Without You." "Trashety struck on the day the drifters recorded what would be their last US top 10 hit, the 21st of May 1964."
**Longest Lasting Lineup Under Treadwell (Post-Lewis)** : The lineup of Johnny Moore, Charlie Thomas, Gene Pearson, and Johnny Terry was the longest lasting of all the versions of the group managed by George Treadwell, remaining together for two years, following the success of "Under the Boardwalk." "This version of the group, Johnny Moore, Charlie Thomas, Jean Pearson and Johnny Terry, would be the longest lasting of all the versions of the group managed by George Treadwell, staying together for two years."
**End of US Top 10 Success** : After "Under the Boardwalk" reached number 4, the Drifters had no more top 10 hits in the US, with their best subsequent chart performance being "Saturday Night at the Movies" which scraped the top 20. "But after under the boardwalk, which went to number 4, they had no more top 10 hits in the US."
**George Treadwell's Death and Faye Treadwell's Takeover (1967)** : George Treadwell, the Drifters' manager, died in 1967. His wife, Faye Treadwell, subsequently took over the management of the group. This change marked a significant shift in the group's history and led to further personnel changes and internal disputes. "There were several more line-up changes, but the big change came in 1967 when George Treadwell died."
**Charlie Thomas's Departure (After 9 Years)** : Shortly after Faye Treadwell took over management in 1967, Charlie Thomas, who had been in the group for the longest continuous period (nine years at that point), decided to leave, contributing to further lineup instability and the eventual splintering of the group. "And shortly after that, Charlie Thomas, the person who had been in the group for the longest continuous time, nine years at that point, decided to leave."
**End of Atlantic Records Career (1970)** : By 1970, after numerous lineup changes, squabbles, and splinter groups, the Drifters' career with Atlantic Records came to an end. "And by 1970 the Drifter's career on Atlantic was over."
**Three Versions of The Drifters (1970s)** : By 1970, three distinct versions of The Drifters existed: "The Original Drifters" centered around Bill Pinkney, a group led by Charlie Thomas that eventually won the right to use the name in the USA, and a UK-based group managed by Faye Treadwell with Johnny Moore as lead singer. "By this point there were three different versions of the Drifters."
**The Original Drifters (Bill Pinkney)** : This version formed in 1958 after the first set of Drifters was fired. It was originally made up of members from the early 1950s lineups and evolved into a revolving door group based around Bill Pinkney, the bass singer from the Clyde McFrattle lineup, operating until Pinkney's death in 2007. "There was a group called, The Original Drifters, which had formed in 1958 after the first set of Drifters had been fired, and was originally made up entirely of members of the early 50s line-ups, but which was now a revolving dog group based around Bill Pinkney, the bass singer of the Clyde McFrattle lineup, and stayed that way until Pinkney's death in 2007."
**Charlie Thomas's Drifters (USA)** : This version consisted of Doc Green, Charlie Thomas, and Del's Bayri Hubs, members from Benny King's era of the group. Charlie Thomas won the legal right to use the Drifters name in the USA in 1972 and continues to tour with his own group, though no other members from that specific lineup are still with him. "Then there was a version of the Drifters that consisted of Doc Green, Charlie Thomas and Del's Bayri Hubs, the people who had been in Benny King's version of the group."
**UK-Based Drifters (Faye Treadwell & Johnny Moore)** : Managed by Faye Treadwell with Johnny Moore as lead singer, this group moved to the UK in 1972 and scored significant hits with re-releases of mid-1960s records that had originally been flops, such as "Saturday Night at the Movies," "At the Club," and "Come On Over to My Place," all making the UK top 10. They subsequently had nine more UK top 10 hits with new material between 1973 and 1976. "Then there was a UK-based group, managed by Fade Redwell, with Johnny Moorvers lead singer."
**Johnny Moore's Significance (UK Drifters)** : Johnny Moore served as the lead singer for the UK version of The Drifters until his death in 1998. His long tenure and prior involvement with hits in the 1950s and 1960s meant that approximately 80% of all records released under the Drifters name featured his vocals, making his group considered a "real version" of the Drifters. "Roughly 80% of records released as by the Drifters had had more singing on them."
**Post-Johnny Moore Confusion and Lawsuits** : After Johnny Moore's death in 1998, the situation surrounding The Drifters became very confusing, involving the Treadwell family reportedly abandoning and then reclaiming the trademark, leading to a series of lawsuits. "But after Moor's death, it gets very confusing."
**Current UK Drifters Lineup** : The current UK version of The Drifters is managed by George and Faye Treadwell's daughter and does not include any members who were part of the group before 2010. Despite the lack of original members, they continue to fill medium-sized theaters on national tours, as audiences prioritize hearing the classic songs. "The current UK version of the Drifters has nobody who was in the group before 2010, and is managed by George and Fay Treadwell's daughter."
**Total Members on Atlantic (34 individuals)** : During their tenure with Atlantic Records, a total of 34 different individuals were members of The Drifters, illustrating a highly fluid lineup where the group's identity often superseded its individual members. "In total 34 different people were members of the Drifters during their time with Atlantic records."
**Group Identity Bigger Than Members** : The Drifters are cited as a unique case where the group's identity was genuinely bigger than its members, allowing them to continue making exceptional records regardless of who was involved. "It's the only case I know where a group identity was genuinely bigger than the members, where whoever was involved somehow they carried on making exceptional records."

# genre - Gospel

## curiosities
**Influence on Pop and Soul** : Gospel music served as a fundamental basis for many early soul songs, with artists like Ray Charles and Sam Cork pioneering the crossover into secular music. Benny King's "Stand By Me" is a prime example of this transition, directly adapting a gospel song into a secular realm while retaining the lyrical style and concerns of apocalyptic visions, rather than just sweet love songs. "Like many early soul songs, it had its basis in gospel music."
**Secular Adaptation of Lyrical Concerns** : Unlike earlier secular reworkings of gospel songs that often required wholesale lyrical changes to fit romantic themes, "Stand By Me" was distinctive in bringing the lyrical concerns and apocalyptic style of gospel firmly into the secular realm. This approach contributed to the development of the "more gospel-inflected lyric" common in the soul music of black performers in subsequent years. "It brings the lyrical concerns and style of gospel firmly into the secular realm."

# instrument - Bass Line

## curiosities
**Stuller's Contribution to "Stand By Me"** : Libra consistently credited Stuller for the success of "Stand By Me," stating that while the lyrics and melody were good and King's performance exceptional, it was the distinctive bass line Stuller created that made the song a hit. This bass line, largely following the root notes of Stuller's innovative chord sequence, became one of the most immediately recognizable pieces of music of the early 1960s. "It was the bass line that Stuller came up with which made the song the success it was."

# curiosity

**Leiber and Stuller - Mentoring Phil Specter** : As a favor to their old friend Lester Sill, the songwriting and producing duo Libra and Stuller undertook the mentoring of a young Phil Specter. Specter, who claimed trauma from his father's death, was described as annoying, following Libra everywhere, and persistently sought collaboration. "At this point, as a favour to their old friend Lester Sill, Libra and Stuller were mentoring a kid that Sill thought had promised, named Phil Specter."
**Leiber and Stuller - Songwriting Collaboration Dynamics** : Phil Specter's persistent attempts to collaborate with Libra and Stuller were initially rebuffed, with Libra insisting he wrote with Stuller. Specter then proposed Stuller could write with them, prompting Libra to clarify that any collaboration would involve Specter writing with the established duo, not Specter graciously allowing Stuller to join. Despite Stuller's absence at the first session, Libra and Specter proceeded to write "Spanish Harlem." "Libra explained to him that that wasn't how things worked, and that if there was any collaboration, it would be Libra and Stuller letting Specter write with them, not Specter graciously allowing Stuller to write with him and Libra."
**Doc Pommas - Personal Inspiration for "Save the Last Dance for Me"** : Doc Pommas, a paraplegic since childhood due to polio, was unable to dance with his wife on their wedding day. This emotional experience, watching her dance with others, stayed with him for years and eventually inspired the lyrics for "Save the Last Dance for Me," which Mort Schumann then set to a vaguely Latin-infused music. "On their wedding day, Pommas was unable to dance with her himself, and watched as she danced with a succession of other people."
**Songwriting Credits for Stand By Me** : The songwriting credit for "Stand By Me" was structured with 50% of the royalties going to Benny King for his basic idea, and 25% each to Libra and Stuller for their contributions; Libra helped finish the lyric, and Stuller helped with the music and developed the distinctive bass line and chord sequence. "The resulting songwriting credit gave 50% of the royalties to King, and 25% each to Libra and Stuller as a result."
**Stand By Me - Gospel Roots** : "Stand By Me" has a long prehistory rooted in gospel music, tracing back to a 1905 spiritual by Reverend Charles Albert Tindley. **Benny King's Secular Adaptation of Gospel** : Working with Libra and Stuller, Benny King carefully crafted "Stand By Me" as a secular adaptation of gospel themes. He wrote a lyric that could function equally well as a gospel song or a love song, only distinguishing it with the "darling darling" in the chorus, maintaining the profound lyrical concerns of gospel in a pop context. "Working with Libra and Stuller, he carefully crafted his secular adaptation of it, writing a lyric that worked equally well as a gospel song or as a song to a lover, other than the words darling darling in the chorus."
**Stand By Me - Chord Sequence Innovation** : Stuller adapted the standard doo-wop chord changes (1-6th-4th-5th), making the 1 and 6th chords last two bars each, followed by the 4 and 5 chords lasting one bar each, and then two more bars of the 1 chord. This hybridization of doo-wop and 12-bar blues progressions resulted in a new, instantly recognizable sequence that significantly contributed to the song's success. "Stuller has taken the two most hackney chord sequences in rock and roll music and hybridized them to turn them into a single new sequence that's instantly recognizable."
**Originality Through Repurposing in Stand By Me** : "Stand By Me" stood out for its remarkable originality, despite being constructed almost entirely from repurposed elements of other songs. This demonstrated that originality in music doesn't always mean creating something entirely new, but can involve taking familiar elements and applying a fresh, light twist to them. "The record sounded remarkably original for something that was made up almost entirely out of repurposed elements from other songs, and it shows more clearly than perhaps any other song that originality doesn't mean creating something entirely abinitio, but can mean taking a fresh look at things that are familiar and putting just as light twist on them."
**Literary Gospel Style in Stand By Me** : "Stand By Me" marked a significant departure from typical secular reworkings of gospel, which often required wholesale lyrical changes to shift from divine to romantic themes. Instead, King's song brought the apocalyptic visions and lyrical style of gospel directly into the secular realm, using imagery like the sky tumbling or mountains crumbling, which became a blueprint for more gospel-inflected lyrics in soul music. "Stand by me is different. It brings the lyrical concerns and style of gospel firmly into the secular realm."
