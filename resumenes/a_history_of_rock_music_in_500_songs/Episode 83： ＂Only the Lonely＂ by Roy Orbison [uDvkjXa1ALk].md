## albums
**Class of '55 (1986)** : A collaboration album with fellow ex-Sun performers Johnny Cash, Carl Perkins, and Jerry Lee Lewis, released in 1986. This album made the top 20 on the country chart, marking the first time Orbison had an album chart since 1966, signaling the beginning of his resurgence. "That came out in 1986 and made the top 20 on the country chart."
**Re-recordings of Greatest Hits (198?)** : An album recorded featuring new versions of his greatest hits. This album made it to the lower reaches of the charts, contributing to his mid-1980s comeback. "He recorded a new album of re-recordings of his greatest hit, which made the lower reaches of the chart."
**The Traveling Wilburys Vol. 1 (1988)** : The debut album by the supergroup The Traveling Wilburys, featuring Roy Orbison alongside George Harrison, Jeff Lynne, Tom Petty, and Bob Dylan. This album was highly successful, reaching the top 5. "The Traveling Willberies, whose first album made the top 5."
**Mystery Girl (1989)** : Orbison's new album of original material, widely considered his best in decades. Produced by Jeff Lynne, it featured songs written by Orbison, Lynne, and Petty, with additional contributions from Bono and The Edge of U2. Released posthumously in early 1989 after Orbison's death in December 1988, it rapidly rose to the top 5 or better almost everywhere, with two albums (this and a hits compilation in the UK, or The Traveling Wilburys album in the US) simultaneously in the top 5 in the UK and US. "Mr. E.G.L. produced by Lynn and with songs written by Orbison, Lynn and Petty, along with a couple of songs contributed by famous admirers like Bungle and the Edge of You 2."

## songs
**Ooby Dooby (1956)** : Orbison's sole minor hit on Sun Records. He was signed to the label with The Teen Kings as a rocker-billy singer, despite his personal desire to be a ballad singer. "He had one minor hit on Sun, Ruby Doobie."
**Claudette (1958)** : A song written by Roy Orbison that became a hit for the Everly Brothers. Orbison placed this song with them through Wesley Rose, who was both his manager and publisher, as well as the Everly Brothers'. "writing Claudette, which became a hit for the Avley Brothers."
**Paperboy (1959)** : One of two songs (the other being "With the Bug") from Orbison's last RCA session that RCA had not released. These songs were cut again for his first Monument single, but it made no impact, marking the end of his period as a commercial failure. "That last session at RCA had included two songs, Paperboy and with the bug that RCA had not bothered to release."
**With the Bug (1959)** : One of two songs (the other being "Paperboy") from Orbison's last RCA session that RCA had not released. These songs were re-recorded for his first Monument single, but it failed to make any impact, serving as the conclusion of his unsuccessful early career phase. "That last session at RCA had included two songs, Paperboy and with the bug that RCA had not bothered to release."
**Uptown (1960)** : Roy Orbison's second single for Monument Records and his first collaboration with Joe Melson to be released. This bluesy rock and roll track marked a significant change in Orbison's style with the introduction of a string section alongside the Nashville A-team. The string arrangement was by Anita Kerr, and her vocal group, with Joe Melson, provided backing vocals. It reached number 72 on the pop chart, his best performance since "Ooby Dooby" three years prior. "A bluesy rock and roll track which saw the first big change in Orbison's style, the introduction of a string section along with the Nashville A team."
**Only the Lonely (1960)** : An Orbison-Melson collaboration that became his breakthrough hit, making him a star. It had roots in Melson's song "Cry," reworked with a vocal hook from a discarded "Teen Angel" knockoff called "Come Back to Me My Love." The song's structure is notably bizarre, featuring alternating lead and backing vocals out of phase, creating five-bar phrases, and a bass line that prolongs chords to give the impression of a six-beat bar, blending elements of a twelve-bar blues and an eight-bar doo-wop song in an unusual 24-bar sequence (5, 6, 8, 5). Initially turned down by Elvis and the Everly Brothers, it reached number two in the US and number one in the UK. "But it's that wrongness, that stringeness of course, along with Orbison's magnificent voice, that made the record a hit, expressing perfectly the confusion and disorientation felt by the song's protagonist."
**Blue Angel (1960)** : A follow-up single to "Only the Lonely," which was a hit but not as big. Orbison toured with Patsy Cline to promote this song. "A couple of slightly more conventional singles followed. Blue Angel and I'm Hurtin, and they were both hit, but nowhere near as big as only the lonely."
**I'm Hurtin' (1960)** : A follow-up single to "Only the Lonely," which achieved hit status but did not match the massive success of its predecessor. "A couple of slightly more conventional singles followed. Blue Angel and I'm Hurtin, and they were both hit, but nowhere near as big as only the lonely."
**Running Scared (1961)** : Roy Orbison's first number one hit in the US, inspired by Ravel's Boléro. The song featured a significant section in rubato (free time), where musicians could slightly speed up or slow down for expressive effect. This element met resistance from the Nashville A-team, particularly bassist Bob Moore, who preferred a steady beat for pop music. Orbison eventually convinced them to play it as written. This session may have also pioneered a new vocal isolation technique in Nashville, using a coat rack covered with coats to partition the studio and prevent instruments from overpowering Orbison's voice, especially a difficult high 'A' at the song's end. "I think we should play it the way it was written."
**In Dreams (1963)** : Many consider this to be Roy Orbison's greatest record. It was written by Orbison solo during his fruitful period of collaboration with Joe Melson. The song gained a new audience in 1986 when it was used by David Lynch in his film "Blue Velvet." "although Orbison also wrote several hits solo during this time, including End Dreams, which many consider his greatest record."
**Blue Bayou (1963)** : Roy Orbison's last major collaboration with Joe Melson before Melson stopped working with him entirely. This song marked a turning point, signaling the beginning of the collapse of Orbison's personal and professional life. "Eventually, Melson stopped working with Orbison all together after their last major collaboration, Blue Bayou."
**Oh, Pretty Woman (1964)** : An early collaboration between Roy Orbison and Bill Dees, which became one of Orbison's most well-known songs, reaching number one in both the US and UK. It was one of only two records by an American artist to hit number one in the UK between August 1963 and February 1965, during the height of Beatlemania. Bill Dees sang harmony vocals on the track and felt he wasn't getting enough credit for his contribution. The song was inspired by Orbison's wife, Claudette. "O Pretty Woman, also went to number one in the US and became one of his most well-known songs."
**Crying (1987)** : A new version of his hit song, recorded in collaboration with K.D. Lang, who was a popular new singer in country music at the time. This duet later won a Grammy Award. "He collaborated with K.D. Lang, who was then one of the hottest new singers in country music on the new version of his hit Crane. That later won a Grammy."

## curiosities
**Early Career Struggle (1959)** : By mid-1959, Roy Orbison was considered an "absolute failure" despite being produced by notable figures like Sam Phillips and Chet Atkins. He had been dropped by both Sun Records and RCA, and the money from his songwriting success for the Everly Brothers had run out. "By the middle of 1959, Roy Orbison was an absolute failure."
**Signing with Monument Records** : Orbison's manager and publisher, Wesley Rose, introduced him to Fred Foster, the owner of the tiny, new Monument Records label. This move was surprising given Orbison's recent failures and Monument's lack of track record. "It seems odd that Roy Orbison could thank Wesley Rose for introducing him to monument records."
**Joe Melson Collaboration Origin** : The legend states that Roy Orbison began his pivotal songwriting collaboration with Joe Melson after Melson saw Orbison writing songs in his car. Orbison, having just had a son, Roy DeWayne Orbison (named after Duane Eddy), lived in a flat so small that he had to write in his car to avoid disturbing the baby. Melson tapped on the window, and upon hearing Orbison's explanation, suggested they work together. "Orbison and his wife Claudette had recently had a son, Roy DeWayne Orbison. His middle name was after Orbison's friend Dwayne Eddie, though spelled differently, and the flat they were living in was so small that the only way Orbison could write any songs without disturbing the baby was to go and write them in the car."
**Wesley Rose's Doubt** : Despite Orbison's growing success with "Uptown," his manager Wesley Rose remained unsure that Orbison could ever be a star, primarily because he considered Orbison to be "odd looking." "Wesley Rose was still unsure that Orbison could ever be a star, mostly because he thought he was so odd looking."
**Unusual Song Structure Philosophy** : Following the success of "Only the Lonely," Orbison and Joe Melson were convinced to follow their instincts and create songs with structures that deviated from the norm. They aimed for "through composed pieces" where a single melody developed with twists and turns, often without repeating sections, contrasting with the common verse-chorus-bridge structures of the time. "Instead, a single melody develops and takes twists and turns over the course of a couple of minutes, with Orbison usually singing throughout."
**Discomfort with Stage Movement** : Orbison and Melson's preference for through-composed songs also had a practical advantage for Orbison: their songs rarely had space for instrumental breaks. This meant Orbison did not have to engage in the typical rock and roll star performance of moving around and dancing while instrumentalists soloed, an activity he felt uncomfortable doing. Instead, he could remain perfectly still at the microphone and sing. "Instead, he could just stand perfectly still at the microphone and sing."
**Vocal Isolation Booth Origin** : During the recording of "Running Scared" (or possibly other early Orbison songs), a pioneering recording technique for Nashville was developed. Orbison's voice, though great, was not powerful enough to overcome the band and strings when recorded together in one room, leading to instruments bleeding into his vocal mic. To solve this, a coat rack covered with coats was brought into the studio and used to partition the space, with Orbison on one side and the band on the other. This makeshift isolation allowed his voice to be the primary sound on his mic, especially crucial for hitting a difficult high 'A' at the song's end. Chet Atkins was reportedly impressed enough to have RCA build a proper vocal isolation booth. "Eventually they brought a coat rack covered with coats into the studio, and used it to partition the space."
**Signature Sunglasses Image** : Orbison's iconic image of wearing dark sunglasses constantly originated by accident. While on tour with Patsy Cline promoting "Blue Angel," he left his regular glasses on a plane. Unable to see clearly without them, he wore his prescription sunglasses on stage and found that the audience perceived him as "cool and brooding," rather than "dorky" or "odd looking." From that point on, he adopted them as a permanent part of his image. "From that point on, he wore them constantly."
**Joe Melson's Departure** : Joe Melson, after several years of successful collaboration with Orbison, became increasingly convinced that he was the primary talent in their songwriting partnership. Melson also began releasing his own solo singles, culminating in his decision to stop working with Orbison after their last major collaboration, "Blue Bayou." "Melson was becoming increasingly convinced that he was the real talent in the partnership."
**Beatlemania Resistance** : During the peak of Beatlemania in the UK and later the US, Roy Orbison was one of a very small number of American artists who continued to have hit records. In fact, two of his early collaborations with Bill Dees were the only two records by an American artist to reach number one in the UK between August 1963 and February 1965. "Indeed, two of the early collaborations by Orbison and Dees were the only two records by an American artist to go to number one in the UK between August 1963 and February 1965."
**MGM Contract and Decline (1965)** : Despite his continued success, Orbison's manager Wesley Rose, who had a strained relationship with Monument owner Fred Foster, moved Orbison to MGM Records. MGM offered him a substantial advance of a million dollars, but immediately after this move, his string of hits dried up. "Wesley Rose and Fred Foster had never got on, and Rose decided that he was going to move Orbison over to MGM Records, who gave him her advance of a million dollars, but immediately the hits dried up."
**Personal Tragedies (1964-1968)** : The period following 1964 was marked by severe personal tragedies for Roy Orbison. He divorced his wife Claudette in November 1964 due to her infidelity, though they reconciled and remarried in December 1965. Just six months later, in June 1966, Claudette died in a motorcycle accident. A little over two years after that, while Orbison was on tour in the UK, his house burned down, killing two of his three children. "The events of the next few years were the kind of thing that would break almost anyone."
**Wilderness Period (1965-1985)** : For approximately 20 years, from 1965 to 1985, Orbison entered a "wilderness period." Plagued by personal tragedies, shifting musical tastes, and a heart condition developed in the 1970s, he struggled to produce records that resonated with the public. Compounding this, Monument Records went bankrupt in the 1970s, leading to his old hits being tied up in legal battles over ownership, making them difficult to access. "But for about 20 years, from 1965 through to 1985, he was in a wilderness period."
**Posthumous Success (1989)** : Roy Orbison died in December 1988 at the age of 52 from a heart attack, just as his career was experiencing a major resurgence. His new album, "Mystery Girl," released a couple of months later, soared to the top 5 or better globally. At one point, he had two albums simultaneously in the top 5 in both the UK (with a hits compilation) and the US (with The Traveling Wilburys album). "But by the time that came out, Orbison was dead."
**Legacy as Tragic Figure** : Roy Orbison's life is considered one of the saddest stories in rock music, marked by heartbreaking tragedies that derailed his career for decades. He died just as he was finally receiving the widespread recognition he deserved, leaving behind a body of work that continued to inspire artists long after his death. "Orbison is one of the saddest stories in rock music, with one of the greatest dance in its history getting derailed for decades by heartbreaking tragedies unimaginable to most of us."

## curiosities
**Roy Orbison Collaborator** : Joe Melson became Roy Orbison's crucial songwriting collaborator, starting with "Uptown" and co-writing hits like "Only the Lonely" and "Running Scared." Their collaboration led to a distinctive style of "through-composed" songs, different from anything else in popular music at the time. "That Golden period came when he started collaborating with Joe Melson."
**Writing in Orbison's Car** : The legend states Melson discovered Orbison writing songs in his car because his flat was too small to avoid disturbing his new baby. Melson suggested they work together, leading to their pivotal collaboration. "Melson apparently tapped on the car window and asked what Roy was doing, and when Roy explained, he suggested that the two of them start working together."
**Contribution to Orbison's Backing Vocals** : Joe Melson frequently added a single male voice to the Anita Kerr Singers, providing the backing vocals for Roy Orbison's records for several years. "her vocal group, with Joe Melson adding a single male voice, would provide the backing vocals on them for the next few years."
**Belief in Own Talent and Departure** : Melson grew increasingly convinced that he was the "real talent" in his partnership with Roy Orbison. He began releasing his own solo singles and eventually stopped working with Orbison after their last major collaboration, "Blue Bayou." "Melson was becoming increasingly convinced that he was the real talent in the partnership."

## curiosities
**Foundation and Ownership (1958)** : Monument Records was started by Fred Foster, who invested his entire life savings into the venture, initially co-owning it with a DJ named Buddy Dean. They were based in Washington at this time. "He then put his entire life savings into starting up his own company, Monument, which he initially co-owned with a DJ named Buddy Dean."
**Label Name and Logo Origin** : As Fred Foster and Buddy Dean were based in Washington, D.C., they used an image of the Washington Monument as the label's logo, which also inspired the company's name. "As Foster and Dean were based in Washington at this time, they used an image of the Washington Monument as the label's logo, and that also inspired the name."
**Early Copyright Dispute** : The label's first single, Billy Grammar's "Done Laid Around," caused significant problems due to a copyright issue. Monument believed the folk song was in the public domain, but Fred Foster later discovered it was copyrighted to folk singer Paul Clayton. This required withdrawing the record from sale and reissuing it as "Gotta Travel On" with Clayton credited, costing the new label a substantial amount of money, though the record still became a hit. "But when Clayton wrote the song or not, done later around had to be withdrawn from sale, and reissued under the name Gotta Travel On, with Clayton credited as the composer, something which cost the new label a substantial amount of money."
**Move to Nashville and Growth** : After the success of Billy Grammar's first single, Fred Foster bought out his co-owner Buddy Dean and relocated the label to Nashville. This move allowed Foster to start working with the renowned Nashville A-team of session musicians. "After that success, Foster bought out Buddy Dean and moved the label down to Nashville."
**Bob Moore's Secret Ownership** : Bob Moore, a prominent bass player on the Nashville A-team and an uncredited co-producer and arranger for Roy Orbison, secretly owned 37% of Monument Records. This was an example of the backroom deals common in the music industry at the time, unknown to his fellow session players. "For example, none of Bob Moore's fellow session players on the A team knew that he secretly owned 37% of monument records."
**Bankruptcy and Legal Battles (1970s)** : Monument Records went bankrupt in the 1970s. This bankruptcy complicated efforts to reissue Roy Orbison's old songs, as their ownership became tied up in legal battles, making them difficult to acquire during his "wilderness period." "Monument records went bankrupt in the 70s, and reissues of his old songs were tied up in legal battles over their ownership."

# instrument - Rubato (Free Time)

## curiosities
**Nashville A-Team Resistance** : The use of rubato, or free time, where musicians slightly speed up or slow down for expressive effect, met significant resistance from the Nashville A-team during the recording of Roy Orbison's "Running Scared." Bassist Bob Moore, in particular, was uncomfortable with it, believing pop music for dancing required a steady beat. "A chunk of the song is in Rubato, or Free Time, where the musicians speed or pass low down slightly to make the music more expressive. This was not something that Bob Moore, in particular, was comfortable with."

# curiosity

**Wesley Rose's Industry Role** : Wesley Rose was a prominent figure in the music industry, serving as the co-owner of Acuff-Rose Publishing (the biggest country music publishing company) and also acting as a manager for artists like Roy Orbison and the Everly Brothers. This dual role of manager and publisher would be considered a conflict of interest by modern standards but was common practice at the time, facilitating "backroom deals" such as placing Orbison's song "Claudette" with the Everly Brothers. "Rose was the co-owner of Acre Rose Publishing, the biggest country music publishing company in the world, and the company to which Orbison had signed as a songwriter."
**Fred Foster's Producer Style** : Fred Foster, owner of Monument Records, compensated for his lack of significant musical or technical ability with a keen ear for talent. He built his career by identifying good artists and allowing them the freedom to create the music they desired. "He did though have a good ear for artists, and he made his career in the business by picking good people and letting them do the music they wanted."
**Nashville "New Wave" Producers** : Fred Foster was part of a "new wave" of Nashville record makers, a group that also included Owen Bradley and Chet Atkins, who were pushing new sounds and approaches in the historically conservative Nashville music industry. "Foster was part of the new wave of Nashville record makers that also included Owen Bradley and Chetat Kins."
**Copyrighting Folk Songs** : It was common practice during the era for folk songs, which might otherwise be considered in the public domain, to be copyrighted in the name of an artist. This practice led to issues like the copyright dispute over Billy Grammar's "Done Laid Around" / "Gotta Travel On." "It was common practice at that time, for folk songs to be copyrighted in the name of an artist."
**Nashville A-Team Session Musicians** : The Nashville A-team was a collective of highly skilled session musicians, including bassist Bob Moore, who played on almost every important record coming out of Nashville at the time, including those by Elvis Presley, Brenda Lee, and later Roy Orbison. They were instrumental in shaping the sound of countless hits. "Like Bob Moore, the bass player who played on almost every important record to come out of Nashville at that time, including the Elvis records we looked at last week."
**Bob Moore's Uncredited Production Role** : While Fred Foster was credited as the producer for most of Roy Orbison's sessions on Monument, Bob Moore, a Nashville A-team bassist, functioned as an uncredited co-producer. He was the arranger for all the records and was responsible for booking the other musicians for the sessions. "While Fred Foster is credited as the producer and most of Orbison's sessions from this point on, it's probably reasonable to think of Bob Moore as at the very least an uncredited co-producer."
**Orbison's Early Confusion over Signing** : After signing with Monument Records, a depressed Roy Orbison was convinced that Fred Foster had confused his own early minor hit, "Ooby Dooby," with a similar-sounding song, Warren Smith's "Rock and Roll Ruby," believing this was the reason Foster wanted to sign him. "He was convinced, even after signing, that Fred Foster was confusing his own Ubidubi, with another son single, Warren Smith's similar sounding, Rock and Roll, Ubidubi."
**Impact of String Sections in Nashville** : The introduction of string sections into what would otherwise be rockabilly tracks, such as Roy Orbison's "Uptown," was a relatively new and initially perplexing concept in Nashville, where traditionalists questioned its appropriateness. "This was something that was only just starting to be done in Nashville, and it made little sense to most people involved that Orbison would want strings and would otherwise be a rockabilly track, but they went ahead."
**Pop Song Structure Norms** : Most pop songs, regardless of genre (country, blues, rock and roll, doo-wop), typically adhere to a structure based on four-bar phrases that build into eight- or twelve-bar verses, choruses, and bridges. Roy Orbison and Joe Melson's "Only the Lonely" dramatically broke these norms, contributing to its unique sound. "Pop songs of whatever genre. Country, or blues, or rock and roll, or do-wop, or whatever. Almost all work in fours."
**Music Industry Conflict Management** : Roy Orbison was notably poor at handling conflict. When faced with resistance from the Nashville A-team regarding the rubato section in "Running Scared," he chose to retreat to the control room and wait, rather than directly confronting the musicians, eventually returning to politely suggest playing the song as written. "Orbison wasn't very good at all at dealing with conflict, and wherever possible he would try to take the most positive attitude possible."
