## songs
**Rock Me (1938)** : Her first record, a rewrite of Thomas Dorsey's "Hide Me and Vibosum." Tharp changed "singing" to "swinging" in the lyrics, and the song is often noted for having a potential double entendre, suggesting more worldly matters than Dorsey's original intent, which likely influenced the decision to make it her debut single. "Her arrangement largely stuck to Dorsey's original, with one important exception, where he had written singing, Tharp sang, Swinging."
**That's All (Year unknown)** : Performed at the Spirituals to Swing concerts, backed by Albert Ammons. The live recording provides a clearer sense of Tharp's raw power compared to studio versions. "in the recording of that we can hear, rather better than in the studio recording, the raw power of Tharp's performance."
**Rock Me (1941) - Remake with Lucky Millinder's band** : This remake showcased a significant artistic improvement in Tharp's guitar playing, three years after her initial recording. It highlights her transition to electric guitar and her development of a distinctive single-note lead style. "Her remake of Rock Me, with Melinda's band from 1941, shows just how much her artistry had improved in just three years."
**Strange Things Happening Every Day (1944)** : Recorded in September 1944, marking a significant collaboration with Sam Price on piano. This song is frequently credited as being one of the first rock and roll records and ushered in a new style for Tharp. "but nonetheless strange things happening every day, marks out the start of yet another new style for Tharp, and its yet another song, often credited as the first rock and roll record."
**This Train (1947) - Remake** : Recorded as a solo B-side to one of her singles with Marie Knight, featuring Sam Price on piano. This 1947 remake of a song first recorded in 1938 is considered Tharp's most important recording and clearly articulates the theology of the Pentecostal Church regarding "outward holiness." "While Tharp and Knight made many great records together, probably Tharp's most important recording was a solo b-side to one of their singles."
**Didn't It Rain (Year unknown)** : Tharp opened her short set at the Manchester 1964 TV special with this song, a fitting choice for an open-air show in the city. "Fittingly for an open air show in Manchester, Tharp opened her short set with Dittintit Rain."

## curiosities
**Pioneer of Rock and Roll Guitar** : Sister Rosetta Tharp is credited as being as responsible as anyone for the style of rock and roll, particularly its focus on the guitar, and essentially invented the guitar style later adapted by blues legends like T-Bone Walker and B.B. King. "But one black woman is as responsible as anyone for the style of rock and roll, and in particular for its focus on the guitar."
**Portrayal at Spirituals to Swing Concerts** : At John Hammond's 1938 Spirituals to Swing concerts, despite her innovative style, Tharp was portrayed as less sophisticated than big band music on the same bill, primarily because she was performing religious music. "but portrayed her as somehow less sophisticated than the big band music on the same bill."
**Gospel Music Innovator** : Tharp made modern and cutting-edge music in the 1930s and 40s, which, despite John Hammond's view, would prove far more important than most swing bands. She was distinct from other gospel performers like Thomas Dorsey and Mahalia Jackson, as she traveled extensively for revival meetings rather than staying in one city. "Sister Rosetta Tharp was one of the great singers of her generation, and one of the great guitar players of all time, and she was making music that was as modern and cutting edge as anything else made in the 1930s and 40s."
**Taking Gospel to Sinners** : After her first marriage ended, Tharp moved to New York and began playing in nightclubs and theaters for secular audiences, performing gospel music that encouraged dancing and excitement, a departure from Mahalia Jackson's approach of playing church music for church people. "Rosetta Tharp was taking the gospel to the sinners."
**Cotton Club Debut (1938)** : She began performing at the Cotton Club in 1938, initially on a two-week trial engagement for $500 a week. Her performances there involved a parody of church services, with dancers acting as "holy rollers," mocking her religion while her music remained devoted to it. "She started playing the Cotton Club in 1938."
**Media Attention** : Her performances at the Cotton Club garnered significant attention, with Time Magazine featuring her and remarking on her ability to "swing" the same songs in both church and nightclub settings. "Time Magazine even did a feature on her, and how she swings same songs in church and nightclub."
**Transition to Electric Guitar** : While with Lucky Millinder's band, Tharp switched from acoustic to electric guitar, which revolutionized her playing style. The electric guitar's sustain allowed her to play single-note lead lines, relying on the orchestra for chordal pads, contrasting with her earlier need to constantly play new notes on acoustic guitar. "She'd also switched to playing an electric guitar rather than an acoustic, and the effect on her guitar playing was extraordinary."
**Quitting Lucky Millinder's Band** : She eventually quit Lucky Millinder's band without notice because she was increasingly pressured to sing secular numbers, like "Tall Skinny Papa," which made her uncomfortable, despite enjoying the band and making lifelong friends like Dizzy Gillespie. "So eventually she quit the millinda band without giving notice and went back to performing entirely solo, at least at first."
**Collaboration with Sam Price** : Tharp's collaboration with pianist Sam Price was musically significant but personally strained. Price initially disliked her open tuning and non-jazz key playing, insisting on a capo, and also combining gospel with boogie-woogie. He was eventually persuaded by Tharp's intimidating mother, and despite the success of their hit "Strange Things Happening Every Day," he resented only receiving a flat fee. "Sam Price did not get along with Tharp."
**Partnership with Marie Knight** : Her partnership with fellow gospel singer Marie Knight was highly successful, creating a unique gospel sound. Their relationship was an "open secret" within the gospel music community, which was more tolerant of homosexuality/bisexuality. Knight, however, denied the relationship throughout her life, likely due to her conservative church devotion and career dependence on it. They stopped working together in 1950, but Knight handled Tharp's funeral in 1973. "Her partnership with Marie Knight may have been a partnership in more than one sense."
**Touring in Segregated America** : Tharp and Knight (and later Tharp solo) toured in their own bus driven by a white man. This offered crucial advantages in the deeply segregated US, allowing them access to public places, acquire takeout food from white-only restaurants, and have accommodation on the bus when no hotels would serve black people, leveraging a sexual double standard that made a black woman with a white man (assumed to be sleeping together) acceptable. "This gave them a number of advantages in a deeply segregated and racist country."
**First Stadium Rock and Roll Guitarist** : Tharp is considered the first person to play rock and roll guitar in stadiums. This notable event was her third wedding, which took place at Griffith Stadium (home of the Washington Senators), drawing 20,000 attendees for her marriage ceremony and subsequent gospel show, featuring fireworks shaped like her playing guitar. She signed the wedding contract seven months before finding a husband. "She is probably the first person to ever play rock and roll guitar in stadiums, and not only that, she played rock and roll guitar in a stadium, at her wedding, her third wedding to be precise, which took place at Griffith Stadium, the home of the Washington Senators and the homestead 20,000 people came to see her get married and perform a gospel show afterwards."
**Declining Popularity in US, Rise in Europe** : Her popularity in the US waned in the 1950s, but she maintained a strong following in Europe. "Rosetta Tharp's popularity started to wane in the 1950s, at least in her home country, but she retained her following in Europe."
**Manchester 1964 Performance** : In 1964, Tharp was filmed for the "Wigrenard the TV" blues TV program at the abandoned World War Hymn Road railway station in Manchester, performing alongside other black American music greats like Muddy Waters. By this time, she was primarily known as a blues musician, despite her consistent style, due to music evolving and re-categorizing her. "There's fascinating footage of her, in 1964, film Wigrenard the TV, playing at the abandoned World War Hymn Road railway station in Manchester."
**Influence on Jerry and the Pacemakers** : Her version of "This Train" significantly influenced later artists, including Jerry and the Pacemakers, a major Mercybeat band, who recorded their own version of "My Babe" months after Tharp's UK performances, demonstrating an unexpected connection between gospel and their sound. "Indeed, only a few months after Rosetta Tharp's UK performances, Jerry and the Pacemakers, one of the biggest bands of the new Mercybeat sound, who'd had three number one records that year in the UK, were recording their own version of My Babe."
**Posthumous Recognition** : Sister Rosetta Tharp died in 1973 and received overdue recognition when she was inducted into the Rock Hall of Fame in "last year" (from the perspective of the podcast recording). "Rosetta Tharp died in 1973 and never really got the recognition she deserved."

## songs
**It's Tight Like That (1928)** : A collaboration with Tampa Red under the name Georgia Tom. This song is one of the earliest "hocombe records," responsible for many clichés of the form, and sold seven million copies. It is considered one of the most important records ever made, influencing hocom blues, R&B, and virtually all subsequent black American music. "Their song It's Tight Like That from 1928 is one of the earliest hocombe records, and is largely responsible for a lot of the cliches of the form, and it sold seven million copies."
**Peace in the Valley (Year unknown)** : One of the most well-known religious songs written by Dorsey after he settled on making religious music following personal struggles. "and the songs he wrote include some of the most well-known songs of all time, like Peace in the Valley, and Take My Hand Precious Lord."
**Take My Hand Precious Lord (1932)** : Written in 1932 after the tragic deaths of his wife in childbirth and his newborn son days later. This song is a profound expression of personal grief and stands as great art. "That's a song he wrote in 1932, after his wife died in childbirth and his newborn son died a couple of days later."
**Hide Me and Vibosum (Original title of Rock Me)** : The original title of the song later rewritten and recorded by Sister Rosetta Tharp as "Rock Me." "Originally titled, Hide Me and Vibosum, but given the newer title, Rock Me by Tharp."

## curiosities
**Inventor of Gospel Music** : Thomas Dorsey is largely credited with inventing gospel music as it is known today, around 1930. He combined themes and melodies from traditional Christian hymns with the feel of blues and jazz, incorporating popular music idioms into the genre. "gospel music as we know it basically was invented by Thomas Dorsey."
**Personal Struggle and Career Shift** : Dorsey, a blues and jazz musician (also known as Georgia Tom), experienced multiple breakdowns due to the conflict between his faith and his secular music career. At one point, he was unable to play the piano on stage with Marraini. This led him to eventually focus entirely on making religious music, though he maintained parallel careers for a while. "He did this, because he was so torn between his faith and his work as a blues musician, that he had multiple breakdowns, at one point finding himself on stage with Marraini, and completely unable to move his fingers to play the piano."
**Revolutionary Musician** : Dorsey is described as a man who revolutionized music multiple times over, through his foundational work in hocombe blues with Tampa Red and his invention of modern gospel music. "That's one man, a man who personally revolutionized music multiple times over, writing about his own personal grief, and creating something that stands as great art, without having to be patronised or given special consideration."

# genre - Rock and Roll

## curiosities
**Dominated by Men** : The history of rock and roll is depicted as a narrative of men crowding out women, white men crowding out black men, and eventually rich white men crowding out poorer white men, leading to a conservative genre. "Indeed, the story of rock and roll is the story of men crowding out women, and white men crowding out black men, and finally, a rich white man crowding out poorer white men, until it eventually becomes a dull, conservative genre."
**Pentecostal Influence** : Many early rock and rollers attended Pentecostal churches, which fostered an environment of wild, unrestrained, passionate music and dancing, helping to induce spiritual experiences. "It's no surprise that a lot of the early rock and rollers went to Pentecostal churches."

# genre - Gospel Music

## curiosities
**Origin and Creator** : Modern black gospel music largely originated around the same time as big bands and was primarily created by Thomas Dorsey around 1930. He integrated popular music idioms, such as blues and jazz, with traditional Christian hymns, making it distinct from earlier forms of religious singing. "gospel music, as we talk about it now, was largely the creation of one man, Thomas Dorsey."
**Community's Open Secret** : The gospel music community had a more relaxed attitude towards homosexuality and bisexuality compared to other parts of the church, allowing relationships like that between Sister Rosetta Tharp and Marie Knight to be an "open secret." "their relationship was regarded as an open secret within the gospel music community, which had a rather more relaxed attitude to homosexuality and bisexuality than the rest of the church."

# genre - Pentecostalism

## curiosities
**Core Beliefs - Sanctification** : Pentecostals differ from many other Christians by believing that some individuals can become "truly holy" and sinless on earth if they truly believe and are "born again." This state is achieved through "baptism in the Holy Ghost," where the Holy Spirit descends, filling the person with joy and sanctity, often leading to speaking in tongues. "Pentecostals believe that it is possible for some people, if they truly believe, if they're born again... to become truly holy."
**Outward Holiness** : To maintain holiness after being sanctified by the Holy Spirit, Pentecostals practice "outward holiness," which includes conservative dress and abstaining from swearing, drinking, gambling, smoking, dancing suggestively, or wearing makeup. Failure to adhere to these practices is feared to lead to the devil's influence, reflecting a belief in a literal heaven and hell. "so long as you continue with what's called outward holiness, and so you have to dress conservatively to avoid swearing, to avoid drinking or gambling or smoking or dancing suggestively or wearing makeup."
**Inspiration for Musicians** : The fear of hell and the elation from experiencing the Holy Spirit in Pentecostalism have significantly inspired many important musicians in rock and roll. "This is a very real fear for many Pentecostals, who have a belief in a literal heaven and hell, and it's a fear that has inspired a lot of the most important musicians in rock and roll."
**Musical Expression** : Pentecostal churches, particularly in the Southern US, foster a culture of wild, unrestrained, and passionate music and dancing. This musical environment is intended to help people enter a receptive mood for the Holy Spirit. "At many of them, especially in the south of the US, there's a culture of absolutely wild, unrestrained, passionate music and dancing to get people into the mood to have the Spirit enter them."

# curiosity

**"Primitive Music" Narrative** : The prevalent narrative among "progressive white men" like John Hammond and the Lomaxes, which characterized music by black people in the 1930s as "primitive" or less sophisticated due to its sound, was deeply mistaken. The perceived lack of sophistication was actually due to socio-economic factors like inability to afford expensive instruments and recording on cheaper equipment, rather than inherent musical underdevelopment. "The fact is, most of the narrative about primitive music, a narrative that was put forward by very progressive white men like John Hammond or the Lomaxes, is deeply mistaken."
**Spiritual vs. Worldly in Rock** : A recurring theme in the history of rock music is the tension between the spiritual and the worldly, exemplified by artists like Sister Rosetta Tharp who navigated sacred and secular performance spaces. "The pull between the spiritual and the worldly."
**The Cotton Club** : The most famous club in New York, which moved from Harlem (after 1936 riots) to Broadway in 1938. In its prime (20s-30s), it launched careers for Duke Ellington and Cab Calloway. It was strictly segregated, with black performers on stage but only white audiences, often presenting black artists in stereotypical ways, even displaying pictures of white band leaders in blackface. "The Cotton Club was the most famous club in New York."
**Cafe Society** : An integrated club booked by John Hammond, which became a more fitting venue for Sister Rosetta Tharp after the Cotton Club closed. "the Cafe Society, a venue booked by John Hammond, which was an integrated club and which fit her rather better."
**The Savoy Ballroom Closure** : This popular New York venue, where Lucky Millinder's band held a residency, was shut down, ostensibly for prostitution. However, it is more likely that its closure was due to the city of New York's disapproval of white women dancing with black men, a stance also commented on by Malcolm X. "The Savoy Ballroom was closed down not long after, allegedly for prostitution, but more likely because it allowed white women to dance with black men, and the city of New York wouldn't allow that."
**Musician's Union Strike** : Sister Rosetta Tharp's solo career resurgence after quitting Lucky Millinder's band coincided with the middle of a musician's union strike, which briefly impacted recording activities. **Segregation and Touring** : In deeply segregated and racist America, black touring artists like Sister Rosetta Tharp and Marie Knight gained significant advantages by traveling in a bus driven by a white man. This arrangement allowed them to access public places, acquire takeout food from white-only restaurants, and have accommodation on the bus when no hotels would serve black people, leveraging a sexual double standard that made a black woman with a white man (assumed to be sleeping together) acceptable. "This gave them a number of advantages in a deeply segregated and racist country."
**Sexual Double Standard (Slavery Era Roots)** : During segregation, a sexual double standard persisted: if a black woman was with a white man, it was assumed they were sleeping together, and this was considered acceptable, unlike a white woman with a black man. This standard offered a perverse form of protection for black female performers and had roots in the era of slavery. "If a black woman was with a white man, it was just assumed she was sleeping with him, and unlike a white woman sleeping with a black man, this was considered absolutely acceptable, a sexual double standard that dated back to slavery."
**Rock Hall of Fame Induction** : Sister Rosetta Tharp was posthumously inducted into the Rock Hall of Fame, receiving recognition for her foundational contributions to rock guitar.
