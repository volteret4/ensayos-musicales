## songs
**Move It (1958)** : This song was the first ever written by Ian Sanwell, created as an angry response to an article in The Maldives Maker that declared Rock and Roll dead. Initially, it was placed on the B-side of the proposed single, largely due to a lack of better options, and was quickly recorded. During its recording, the main engineer left for the opera, leaving junior engineer Malcolm Addy to record the track. Addy, being young and understanding of rock and roll sound, produced a much louder, more resonant sound, akin to Sun Records, making a significant difference to the track. Jack Good later heard it and insisted Cliff perform it on "Oh Boy!", leading the record label to flip the single to make "Move It" the A-side. Cliff worked with Good for a full week on his performance, meticulously instructed on every movement and facial expression. It became an immediate hit upon his TV debut in September 1958, reaching number two in the charts. "That track was still intended for the B-side, until the point that Jack Good heard it."
**High Class Baby (recorded after Move It)** : This song was another composition by Ian Sanwell and was recorded by Cliff with his new backing group. However, at the time of its recording, Sanwell was unaware that Cliff was already planning to replace him with Jett Harris, as Sanwell's musical shortcomings were becoming apparent alongside more proficient musicians. "The new group recorded another Ian Samuel song, High Class Baby."

## curiosities
**Birth and Early Life (1940)** : Born Harry Webb in India in 1940 to a family of British descent who had lived there for generations, benefiting greatly from the British Empire. After Indian independence and partition following World War II, his family moved to Britain, a country they had never inhabited, where they found themselves homeless and jobless. They were forced to live in a single room of a three-bedroom house, with another family of eight occupying the other rooms. "Harry Webb, who was born in India in 1940, to a family who were of British descent, but who had been in India for a couple of generations."
**Experience with Racism** : While he had benefited from racism in India, upon moving to Britain, he became a victim of it. Despite being white, his dark complexion, Anglo-Indian accent, and origin from India led schoolmates to mistakenly assume he was Indian and question him about his "week one," referencing caricatured depictions of Indians in cowboy films. "in Britain he was a victim of it."
**Accent Development** : He eventually shed his Anglo-Indian accent, developing a generic, regionless middle-class accent with a hint of mid-Atlantic, which later became a common media accent, replacing the previously dominant "received pronunciation." This neutral accent provided a distinct advantage when he became a pop star, as it didn't tie him to a specific region. "Webb's accent would later become almost the default for people in the media."
**Elvis Presley Obsession** : At 16, an Elvis Presley record profoundly changed his life, leading him to become obsessed with Elvis and strive to emulate his appearance. He later insisted on performing with an Elvis-style vocal imitation and hip thrusts, even when singing skiffle music. "Young Harry became obsessed with Elvis Presley, and tried to make himself look as much like Elvis as possible."
**First Public Performance** : His initial public performance occurred with a vocal group he formed at school, where he performed a solo on the song "Harbbeke Hotel." "His first public performance was with a vocal group he formed at school, and he took a solo on Harbbeke Hotel."
**Entry into Music via Terry Smart** : After leaving school and failing nearly all his exams, he decided to pursue a career as a rock and roll star. His opportunity arose when his bike broke, forcing him to take a bus where he encountered his old school friend, Terry Smart, a drummer in a Skiffle group. Smart, whose group needed a new singer as theirs had been drafted, remembered Harry's singing ability and invited him to join. "On the same bus was an old school friend, Terry Smart, who was the drummer in a Skiffle group."
**Skiffle Group - Dictig Skiffle Group** : He joined the Dictig Skiffle Group as their lead singer, diligently learning their material despite his personal musical tastes not aligning with skiffle. He controversially insisted on singing in an Elvis-style voice and thrusting his hips, a style that didn't suit the traditional skiffle songs. "So Harry Webb joined Dictig Skiffle Group and became their lead singer."
**Formation of The Drifters** : While still performing with the Skiffle group, he secretly conspired with Terry Smart to form their own rock and roll group. His friend Norman Midham attended the Skiffle group's rehearsals, intently observing the guitarist to learn material for their new, clandestine group. After leaving the Dictig Skiffle group, Webb, Smart, and Midham officially formed "The Drifters." "Webb and Smart left the Dictig Skiffle group, and with Midham they formed a new rock-and-roll group."
**John Foster as Manager** : John Foster, who worked at a sewage farm and lacked any prior music business experience, became the group's manager. His key qualifications in 1958 were possessing a telephone (a sign of importance) and having a casual acquaintance with the managers of The Two I's coffee bar, a famous music venue where he believed he could secure them a gig. "a man named John Foster came up and introduced himself to them."
**Name Change to Cliff Richard** : A booking agent, after seeing the group perform at The Two I's, wanted to book them as a lead singer backed by a group. He deemed "Harry Webb" an unsuitable name for a star, changing it to Cliff Richard, a name Harry Webb subsequently adopted fully in his personal life. "So the Drifters became Cliff Richard and the Drifters, and Harry Webb soon told everyone in his life that he was only to be addressed as Cliff from now on."
**Career Over Love** : At the point of signing his recording contract, Cliff was in a relationship. However, he realized that to be perceived as available to teenage girls, a necessity for a rock and roll star, he had to end the relationship. His girlfriend understood his choice between his burgeoning career and their love. "he knew that if he was going to be a rock and roll star, he had to appear available to the teenage girls. So he dumped her."
**Recording Contract Terms** : For his first single recorded at Abbey Road Studios, only Cliff Richard's name was featured on the recording contract. While the record label would credit "Cliff Richard and the Drifters," the other group members were compensated only with a flat session fee, whereas Cliff alone received artist royalties. "But only Cliff Richard's name was on the recording contract."
**Norman Midham's Departure** : Ian Sanwell convinced Cliff that Norman Midham, who played rhythm guitar like Cliff, was redundant. Sanwell argued against having three guitarists and dividing the earnings three ways instead of two. Consequently, Midham, who had been friends with Cliff since childhood, was removed from the group. "So Mitham, who had been friends with Cliff since he were both nine, was out of the group."
**Session Musicians for First Single** : Given Cliff's limited guitar skills, Ian Sanwell switched to rhythm guitar for the first recording session, and session musician Ernie Sheer was brought in to play lead guitar. The group was further augmented in the studio by double bass player Frank Clark and the Mike Sam Singers for backing vocals. "Cliff didn't play guitar especially well, so for the session, Samuel switched to Rhythm, and the session player, Ernie Sheer, was brought into play lead."
**TV Debut and Image** : Made his television debut on "Oh Boy!" in September 1958, shortly before his 18th birthday. Jack Good insisted that Cliff perform "Move It" and personally coached him for a full week on every aspect of his performance, including specific blinks, gestures (like clutching his arm as if in pain), and how to look down instead of directly at the audience. Good also selected his shocking pink outfit (which was not visible on black and white TV but intended to excite the audience) and had his hair styled to be less like Elvis's. "Good work with Cliff for a full week on his performance of the song, instructing him in every blink, every time he should clutch his arm as if in pain, the way he should look down, not straight at the audience, everything."
**Media Outcry over Sexuality** : Following his successful TV debut and the chart performance of "Move It," Cliff Richard faced a media storm regarding his " brazen sexuality." The enemy accused him of "crude exhibitionism," while the Daily Sketch ran a headline asking if he was "too sexy." "There was a media outcry over Cliff's brazen sexuality, with the enemy accusing him of crude exhibitionism, while the daily sketch would ask, is this boy TV star too sexy?"
**Band Lineup Changes Post-Debut** : After his TV debut, Cliff ceased playing guitar while singing. Ian Sanwell transitioned to playing bass, and the group sought a new guitarist, resulting in Hank Marvin and Bruce Welch joining the band. "Cliff was no longer playing guitar while he sang, and the group also needed a bass player, so Ian Samuel switched a bass, and they went looking for a new guitarist."
**Ian Sanwell's Firing** : Cliff secretly planned to replace Ian Sanwell with Jett Harris, a musician who had played with Hank Marvin and The Vipers. Sanwell's perceived musical shortcomings became apparent as Cliff began playing with more skilled musicians. Cliff did not inform Sanwell directly, instead delegating the task of firing him to John Foster. Sanwell later achieved success as a songwriter and record producer, notably producing "Horse with No Name" for the band America. "Cliff didn't tell Samuel himself. He got John Foster to fire him."
**John Foster's Departure** : After Ian Sanwell's firing, John Foster, who had been the group's manager, was first demoted to roadie. Subsequently, he received two weeks' notice of termination in a letter written by Cliff's father, leading to his final departure from the group. "Shortly after that, Foster was gone as well, first demoted from manager to roadie, then given two weeks notice in a letter from Cliff's dad."
**Terry Smart's Replacement** : Cliff replaced his childhood friend Terry Smart, who had initially invited him into the group, with Tony Meahin, another former member of The Vipers. "Cliff replaced Terry Smart, his old school friend, the person who had invited him into his group with Tony Meahin, another ex-viper."
**Evolution to The Shadows** : By Cliff Richard's 19th birthday, the only remaining original element of his backing band, The Drifters, was its name. This name too would soon change, as "Cliff Richard and the Drifters" became "Cliff Richard and The Shadows." "By Cliff's 19th birthday, the only thing left of the original drifters was the name, and soon that would change too, as Cliff Richard and the drifters became Cliff Richard and the shadows."
**Personal Habits** : Even as a teenager, he maintained a strict code of conduct, refusing to smoke, drink, or use blasphemous language. He was known for his softly spoken manner. When asked if he had a girlfriend during his teenage years, he would produce a photograph of his mother from his pocket. "He refused to smoke, drink or blaspheme. He was very softly spoken, and as a teenager when asked if he had a girlfriend he would say, yes, I've got a picture of her in my pocket, I would pull out a photo of his mother."
**Driven and Utilitarian Nature** : He possessed an incredible drive and was willing to leverage anyone around him for precisely as long as they could assist him in achieving his goals. If musicians wished to play skiffle, he would play skiffle for the moment, demonstrating an adaptability to serve his ambition. "But on the other hand he was incredibly driven and was willing to make use of anyone around him for precisely as long as it would take for them to help him achieve his goals."

**Origin of Name** : Formed by Harry Webb, Terry Smart, and Norman Midham, the group initially considered calling themselves "The Planet" after the launch of Sputnik. However, after researching the etymology of "planet" and discovering it came from the Greek for "wanderer" or "Drifter," they adopted the name "The Drifters," unaware of the existence of an American group with the identical name. "Inspired by the recent launch of Sputnik, they thought of calling themselves the planet, but they decided that wasn't quite right, and looked up the etymology of planet and found it came from the Greek for wanderer or Drifter, and so they became the Drifters, unaware there was an American group of the same name."
**Original Lineup Evolution** : The initial formation included Harry Webb, Terry Smart, and Norman Midham. Ian Sanwell later joined as the lead guitarist. The lineup then evolved to include Ian Sanwell on bass, Terry Smart, Hank Marvin, and Bruce Welch. Eventually, only the name "The Drifters" remained from the original configuration before the band was rebranded as "The Shadows." "Webb and Smart left the Dictig Skiffle group, and with Midham they formed a new rock-and-roll group."

# artist - Jack Good

## member of
BBC (producer of 6-5 Special)
ABC (producer of Oh Boy!)

## curiosities
**Most Important Music TV Person** : He is regarded as possibly the most influential individual ever involved in music television, not only in Britain but globally. "Jack Good was possibly the most most important person ever to be involved in music TV, not just in Britain, but in the world."
**Inspired by Rock Around the Clock** : Originally an actor, his perspective was transformed after watching the film "Rock Around the Clock" in the cinema and observing the audience's reactions. This experience convinced him that audience engagement was a critical component of Rock and Roll and that live performances could achieve a cathartic effect similar to classical Greek drama. "Good had been an actor, until he saw a rock around the clock in the cinema, and saw the way that audiences reacted to the film."
**Vision for Rock and Roll TV** : He applied his innovative ideas to the BBC's new teenage program, "6-5 Special," making the audience an integral part of the show and positioning performers among the crowd rather than separating them. Despite his vision for a purely rock and roll show, he had to compromise with his co-producer, leading to the inclusion of light music, educational segments, and sports. "6-5 Special embodied many of good side ideas about how to broadcast rock and roll music."
**Creator of Oh Boy!** : Following creative differences at the BBC, he moved to ABC, an ITV station, where he launched a rival show named "Oh Boy!" This program was entirely focused on music, reflecting Good's strong artistic ideas drawn from diverse sources, including Shakespearean theatrical companies that used stark, all-black backgrounds and a book on the physiology of brainwashing, aiming to create something powerful. "So good moved over to ABC, one of the ITV stations, and started a rival show, Oh Boy."
**Shaping Rock and Roll Image** : More than any other individual, Jack Good was instrumental in developing the visual persona of the rock and roll star. His influence was evident in virtually every visual depiction of rock and roll music from the early 1960s onwards. "It was Jack Good, who, more than anyone else, came up with the image of the Rock and Roll star, and his influence could be seen in literally every visual depiction of Rock and Roll music from the early 60s on."
**Mentoring Cliff Richard** : He insisted that Cliff Richard perform "Move It" on his show and intensively coached Cliff for a full week on his performance. Good provided detailed instructions covering every blink, every instance to clutch his arm as if in pain, and how he should look down rather than directly at the audience. He also selected Cliff's shocking pink outfit (designed to cause a frenzy among the girls, though not visible on black and white TV) and directed the styling of Cliff's hair to be less like Elvis's. "Good work with Cliff for a full week on his performance of the song, instructing him in every blink, every time he should clutch his arm as if in pain, the way he should look down, not straight at the audience, everything."

# genre - Skiffle

## curiosities
**Early British Youth Music Stream** : In the mid-1950s, skiffle emerged as one of two significant musical streams designed to attract young people in Britain. This genre is recognized as the foundational branch that ultimately led to all British rock and roll from the 1960s onward. "Britain was Skiffle, and that's the branch of music that eventually led to all British rock and roll from the 60s onwards."
**Skiffle Craze** : This musical craze was a major phenomenon in Britain for approximately two years. Following its decline, it fragmented into three distinct, though overlapping, groups: folk revivalists, British blues enthusiasts, and rock and rollers. "but the Skiffle craze was a big, big thing for about two years, and when it finally died down, it splintered into three different, overlapping groups."
**Excitement but Different Marketing** : Skiffle possessed much of the excitement and appeal found in American rock and roll. However, it was not marketed explicitly as rock and roll and tended to cater to a slightly more bohemian audience. "Skiffle had everything that people found exciting and interesting about American rock and roll. At least, it had much of the excitement of the rock and roll music, but it wasn't marketed as rock and roll, and it tended to aim at a slightly more bohemian audience."
**Formalized Genre by Late 1950s** : By the late 1950s, skiffle had developed into a highly formalized genre. Its typical instrumentation almost invariably included an acoustic guitar, a tea-chest bass, and a washboard. The repertoire primarily consisted of songs by Lead Belly, Woody Guthrie, and Big Bill Broonzy, supplemented by traditional folk songs and sea shanties. "Harry's musical taste didn't really run to Skiffle, which by this time had become a very formalized genre, but the instruments almost always consisting of acoustic guitar, T-Chess bass and washboard, and a repertoire that was made up primarily of songs by Lead Belly, Woody Guthrie and Big Bill Brunze."

# genre - Rock and Roll

## curiosities
**British vs. American Origins** : The early stages of rock and roll in Britain were markedly different from its American counterpart, to the extent that discussing the two in the same context felt absurd. Early British commercial rock and roll was often created by long-time professional musicians who merely shifted from performing anemic copies of swing music to anemic copies of Bill Haley, contrasting sharply with the American records by artists like Buddy Holly or Elvis that British teenagers actually preferred. "We've logged a little bit at the start of rock and roll in Britain, which was so different from the American music that it feels absurd to talk of the two in the same breath."
**Film Influence on British Scene** : The initial understanding and concept of rock and roll within the British music business during its early years were entirely derived from the film "Rock Around the Clock." This movie prominently featured Bill Haley, The Platters, and Fettie Bell and his Bell Boys, influencing British groups to emulate even the less successful American acts. "The idea of rock and roll in the British music business in those early years came entirely from the film Rock Around the Clock."
**Need for British Elvis** : For British rock and roll to gain significance, it was crucial for it to align with the sound and appeal of American records. It particularly needed its own equivalent of Elvis, ideally an artist who emerged from the skiffle scene but demonstrated a stronger orientation towards rock and roll than most traditional skifflers. "For British rock and roll to matter, it had to at least catch up to what the American records were doing. It needed its own Elvis, and that Elvis would ideally be someone who came from the Skiffle scene."

# curiosity

**British Empire Decline and Impact (Post-WWII)** : The end of the British Empire profoundly affected Britain's international standing, transforming it from the largest and most powerful empire in human history in the 1940s to a small island nation by the early 1970s, desperately seeking entry into the EEC due to a lack of independent economic or political power. This dramatic shift in status inflicted a significant "psychic shock" on multiple generations of British people, with much of British history since 1945 explainable by the nation's attempts to convince itself and the world of its continued importance. Harry Webb's family, who had enjoyed significant benefits from the empire in India, were among those dramatically impacted by this change. "It is literally impossible to understand anything about Britain for the last 80 years, without understanding that at the start of the 1940s, the British Empire was the largest, most powerful empire that had ever been seen in human history."
**Indian Independence and Partition (Post-WWII)** : Following World War II, Indian independence and its subsequent partition dramatically altered the lives of British families living there. Harry Webb's family, of British descent and moderately affluent by white British standards in India, found themselves homeless and jobless in Britain, a country they had never resided in. "But then, after World War II, came Indian independence and partition, and the Webb family found themselves in Britain, a country they'd never lived in, homeless and jobless."
**Early British Rock and Roll Stagnation** : In the mid-1950s, British rock and roll was characterized as "lifeless" because it was largely performed by established professional musicians who produced "anemic copies" of either swing music or Bill Haley's style. This failed to resonate with British teenagers, who were instead listening to vibrant American records by artists like Buddy Holly or Elvis. "When we've looked at British rock and roll to this point, it's been rather lifeless, and there's a reason for that."
**Rock Around the Clock (Film) Influence** : This film was entirely responsible for shaping the initial understanding and perception of rock and roll within the British music business. It featured key performers like Bill Haley, The Platters, and Fettie Bell and his Bell Boys, which led British groups to emulate even the second-rate American acts. "The idea of rock and roll in the British music business in those early years came entirely from the film Rock Around the Clock."
**The Tobler's Trousse (British TV Practice)** : Until mid-1956, it was standard practice in British television to halt transmissions for an hour, from 6 PM to 7 PM, to allow parents to put their children to bed. This became a contentious issue after the commercial network ITV began broadcasting in 1955. While the BBC saved money during this hour and still received TV licence fees, a commercial channel like ITV lost an hour of potential advertising revenue, making the practice controversial. "This was known as the Tobler's Trousse, but after the commercial network ITV began broadcasting in 1955, the practice became controversial."
**6-5 Special (BBC TV Show)** : This was the BBC's first television program specifically designed for young people, broadcast at 6:05 PM on Saturdays after the "Tobler's Trousse" rules were eventually changed. It incorporated many of Jack Good's pioneering ideas, notably by making the audience an integral part of the show and having performers mingle among the crowd. However, Good had to compromise his vision, as the program also included elements of light music (approved by parents), educational segments, and sports features. "And it was to be called 6-5 Special."
**Oh Boy! (ITV TV Show)** : Launched by Jack Good on ABC (an ITV station) in September 1958, following two pilot episodes in June, "Oh Boy!" was conceived as a rival show to "6-5 Special." Its entire focus was on music, driven by Good's strong visual concepts inspired by diverse sources such as theatrical companies performing Shakespeare with stark black backgrounds and studies on brainwashing physiology. The show was characterized by its rapid pace, featuring as many as 17 non-stop songs performed by different artists within a 25-minute live broadcast, without any artist performing two songs consecutively, and highlighting performers with a white spotlight against a black background. "So good moved over to ABC, one of the ITV stations, and started a rival show, Oh Boy."
**Malcolm Addy's Engineering** : Malcolm Addy, a junior engineer, was tasked with recording Cliff Richard's B-side "Move It" because the main engineer chose to attend the opera instead. Addy, being young enough to have his own conception of what a rock and roll record should sound like, produced a significantly louder and more resonant sound for the track, which bore a strong resemblance to the records being released by Sun, ultimately making a substantial difference to its impact. "And that made a big difference. Addy was young enough to have some idea himself as to what a Rock and Roll record should sound like, and he came up with a much louder, more resonant sound than anything that had been heard in a British recording session."
**The Maldives Maker (Music Publication)** : This publication printed an article asserting that "Rock and Roll was dead," a claim that provoked Ian Sanwell to write the song "Move It" as an angry rebuttal. "It was the first song that Ian Samuel had ever written, an angry response to an article in The Maldives Maker, arguing that Rock and Roll was dead."
