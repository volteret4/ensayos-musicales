## songs
**Mama He Treat Your Daughter Mean (Undated)** : This R&B song by Ruth Brown changed Janice Martin's musical direction when she heard it on the radio at age 13. She immediately decided she wanted to sing "Black R&B, not country music," incorporated it into her set, and it remained her favorite song. "She immediately incorporated Mama He Treat Your Daughter Me into her set."
**Will You, Will You (1956)** : Written by Carl Stutz and Carl Barefoot of WRVA, this song was Janice Martin's big break and intended first single. It's described as a standard boogie rhythm country song that name-checks "Dancing With Henry," a reference to "The Wallflower." The demo caught the ear of Steve Sholes at RCA. "The song itself was not hugely impressive. It's a standard boogie rhythm country song."
**Drugstore Rock 'n' Roll (1956)** : Janice Martin's first attempt at songwriting, written in 10-15 minutes, became the B-side to "Will You, Will You." This song is notable as one of the first records to specifically address the experience of white teenagers from their own perspective, a turning point in rock and roll history. "Her very first attempt at writing a song took her by her own accounts 10 or 15 minutes to write, and ended up as the B side."
**Will I Be Doobie (Undated)** : This was Janice Martin's follow-up single, a cover song. It did not perform as well as her debut single, failing to replicate its initial success. "But the follow-up will be Doobie. A cover of a song we'll be dealing with in a future episode didn't do quite so well."
**My Boy Elvis (1956)** : As her third single, this song was an attempt by RCA to capitalize on the Elvis Presley comparison that was central to Janice Martin's marketing. She was not keen on the song and had no control over the material given to her by the label, and it ultimately did not find success. "She wasn't particularly keen on the song, but she had no control over the material she was given."
**Let's a Look Baby (Undated)** : This song was chosen by RCA for Janice Martin to record at her first session. Ironically, the song described her actual experiences better than the label realized, highlighting a disconnect between her real life and the innocent image RCA tried to maintain. "Even though one of the songs they'd chosen for her to record at her first session, let's a look baby, had described her actual experiences rather better than they'd realized."

## curiosities
**First Successful Female Rockabilly Artist** : Janice Martin is identified as the first successful female rockabilly artist, making her a true pioneer of the genre. "Janice Martin, who as far as I can tell, was the first successful female rockabilly artist."
**Pioneer of Rockabilly Music** : Despite a lack of extensive documentation like books or films, Janice Martin's story is marked by a handful of articles in obscure fanzines and pages on unvisited websites, recognizing her as a significant pioneer of rockabilly music. "to mark the story of a true pioneer of rockabilly music."
**Career Stalled by Misogyny** : The narrative suggests that Janice Martin's career stalled not due to the "female Elvis" label, but due to misogyny within the music industry, which imposed limitations and expectations on her. "I think it stalled because of misogyny, plain and simple."
**Child Country & Western Act (Born 1940)** : Janice Martin was born in 1940 and began her career in early childhood as a country & western performer, pushed into this role by her dominating mother. "Janice Martin was born in 1940 and spent her early years as a child country in western act."
**Guitarist from Age Four** : She started playing the guitar at the exceptionally young age of four, holding it upright because she was too small to play it conventionally, demonstrating early musical talent. "She started playing the guitar when she was only four, holding it upright because she wasn't big enough yet to play it normally."
**Regular on Old Dominion Barn Dance at 11** : By the age of 11, Janice Martin had become a regular performer on the Old Dominion Barn Dance, a notable achievement for her age in the country music scene. "by the age of 11 she was a regular on the old Dominion Barn Dance."
**Disinterest in Pure Country, Preference for Honky-Tonk & R&B** : Despite being a country singer, Janice Martin was not passionate about country music, disliking bluegrass and slow country ballads. She preferred "honky-tonk stuff" and admired Hank Williams for the "little rock" in his music. "Young Janice Martin was a country singer, pushed into the role by her dominating mother, but she wasn't massively interested in country music."
**Style Change at 13 through Radio Discovery** : At 13, Janice Martin dramatically changed her musical style after discovering Ruth Brown's "Mama He Treat Your Daughter Mean" on the radio. This led her to embrace Black R&B music, incorporating it and similar songs by LeVern Baker and Dinah Washington into her set, much to the surprise of some. "She changed her whole style with one twist of a radio dial when she was 13."
**Performing Jazz-Influenced R&B with Country Instrumentation** : Janice Martin's unique approach involved performing jazz-influenced R&B songs by artists like Ruth Brown, LeVern Baker, and Dinah Washington with country instrumentation, a practice considered unusual even among country musicians covering R&B. "it's actually quite hard for me to imagine how one could perform Mama He Treat Your Daughter Mean in with country instrumentation. But this was what Janice Martin was doing."
**"Overtly Sexual" Performance Style & Elvis Comparison (Ages 13-16)** : At 13 and 14, Janice Martin began dancing on stage in a manner interpreted as overtly sexual, often compared to Elvis Presley, whom she hadn't heard of and wasn't impressed by, preferring Carl Perkins. By 15 or 16, her moves were deemed "vulgar" by some, contrasting with earlier reactions when it was considered "cute." "She started performing in a manner that they interpreted as overtly sexual."
**Signed to RCA Records at 15 (March 1956)** : In March 1956, at just 15 years old, Janice Martin was signed to RCA Records, one of the biggest labels, by A&R man Steve Sholes, who had also signed Elvis Presley a few months prior. "in March 1956, aged just 15, Janice Martin was signed to RCA Records."
**Encouraged to Write Songs by Steve Sholes** : Steve Sholes, the A&R man who signed her, not only wanted to record "Will You, Will You" as her first single but also suggested that Janice Martin try writing songs herself, leading to her first original track, "Drugstore Rock 'n' Roll." "Shoals wanted to record Will You, Will You, as her first single, but had also suggested that she try writing songs herself."
**Marketed as "Female Elvis" by RCA** : RCA Records actively marketed Janice Martin as their "female Elvis," seeking permission from Elvis and Colonel Parker to do so, due to her perceived similar movements and career path. "RCA decided to market her as their female Elvis."
**Limited Interaction with Elvis Presley** : Despite the marketing, Janice Martin only met Elvis Presley twice and exchanged very few words with him during those encounters. "Martin only ever met Elvis twice, and barely exchanged a couple of words with him when she did."
**Backed by Elvis's Session Musicians (Chet Atkins, Floyd Cramer, The Jordanaires)** : To achieve a sound similar to Elvis's records, RCA brought in key session musicians from his recordings, including Chet Atkins (who also produced her sessions), Floyd Cramer on most early recordings, and The Jordanaires providing backing vocals on some tracks. "Chet Atkins, who also produced Janice's sessions, and Floyd Kramer were both on most of Janice's early recordings, and came up with a very similar sound to the Elvis records, and on at least some of her records, the Jordanaires provided backing vocals as they did for Elvis."
**Debut Single Success: #35 Pop Charts, 750,000 Sales, Grand Ole Opry Performance (1956)** : Her first single, "Will You, Will You" backed with "Drugstore Rock 'n' Roll," was a hit, reaching number 35 on the pop charts and selling three-quarters of a million copies. This success led to performances on major TV shows and the Grand Ole Opry. "The first single, Will You William, backed with drugstore rock and roll, was a hit, and went to number 35 in the pop charts. It sold three quarters of a million copies, and led to performances on most of the big TV shows, as well as on the Grand Ole Opry."
**No Control Over Material** : Janice Martin, like many artists on major labels at the time, had no control over the songs she was given to record, leading to her having to perform songs she disliked, such as "My Boy Elvis." "She wasn't particularly keen on the song, but she had no control over the material she was given."
**Elvis Fanbase Resented "Female Elvis" Comparison** : The teenage girl audience, who were Elvis's biggest fans, disliked and resented RCA's marketing of Janice Martin as the "female Elvis," preferring Elvis to be unique. "the teenage girl audience who were Elvis' biggest fan base started to resent the marketing, which she hadn't chosen herself, comparing her to Elvis."
**Sales Below RCA's Expectations** : While Janice Martin sold records, her sales did not meet the ambitious levels RCA initially hoped for, and the label struggled to build her audience. "Janice was selling records, but not quite at the level RCA initially hoped."
**Parents Refused Colonel Parker's Double-Bill Offer** : Colonel Parker, Elvis Presley's manager, sought to sign Janice Martin to a management contract and promote Elvis and Janice as a male/female double bill. However, her parents, concerned after Elvis's public collapse from overwork, rejected the offer, costing Janice the opportunity to tour with Elvis. "But her parents thought this was a bad idea."
**Incompatible Touring with RCA Country Artists** : Because she was signed by RCA's country department and her manager was Steve Sholes, Janice Martin was placed on bills with traditional RCA country artists like Hank Snow. Her rock and roll style clashed with these acts, and the audiences were unimpressed. "Martin's rock and roll style didn't really fit on the bills, and the audiences were unimpressed."
**MGM Screen Test** : Janice Martin had a screen test with MGM, the film studio that had successfully signed Elvis Presley, indicating potential film career aspirations. "She even had a screen test with MGM, the film studio, the third signed Elvis up so successfully."
**Secret Marriage Before RCA Signing (1955/1956)** : A few months before signing with RCA, Janice Martin secretly eloped and married a paratrooper who was subsequently stationed in Germany. She initially kept the marriage a secret from her parents and RCA, maintaining the innocent teenage image the label desired. "A few months before she was signed to RCA, she had got married."
**Pregnancy Ended RCA Contract (1957)** : During her 1957 European tour, her husband joined her for 30 days, and upon her return to the US, she discovered she was pregnant. RCA was furious, finding her pregnancy incompatible with her 17-year-old "nation's sweetheart" image. "When she informed RCA, they were furious. They couldn't have their 17-year-old nation's sweet heart going around being visibly pregnant."
**Refused Abortion, Dropped by RCA (1957)** : RCA attempted to persuade Janice Martin to get an abortion, which was illegal in the US at the time, to preserve her image. She refused, and as a result, the label dropped her. "She refused, and the label dropped her."
**Signed with Tiny Belgian Label Palette** : After being dropped by RCA, Janice Martin turned down offers from larger labels like King and Decca Records, choosing instead to sign with the small Belgian label, Palette. "She turned down offers from King and Decker records, and instead went with the tiny Belgian label palette."
**Second Marriage and Retirement from Music (1960-1973)** : By 1960, at age 20, Janice Martin entered her second marriage. Her second husband eventually gave her an ultimatum to choose between him and her music career, leading her to quit the music industry for the next 13 years. "By 1960, aged 20, she was on to her second marriage. Her second husband toured with her for a while, but soon told her that if she wanted to stay with him, she would have to give up on the music industry."
**Formed Janice Martin and the Variations and Resumed Performing (After 1973)** : After her second marriage ended, Janice Martin formed a new band, Janice Martin and the Variations, and began performing gigs again, finding renewed joy in it after years of being controlled by others. "after her second marriage ended, she put together a band, Janice Martin and the Variations, and started playing gigs again."
**Day Job as Assistant Manager of a Country Club in Virginia** : Not immediately returning to full-time music, Janice Martin held a day job as the assistant manager of a country club in Virginia, balancing this with her re-emerging music career. "She held down a day job as the assistant major of a country club in Virginia."
**Rediscovered by European Fans & Bear Family Records (Late 1970s)** : In the late 1970s, Janice Martin found a new fanbase, particularly in Europe, thanks to Bear Family Records, which reissued her 1950s recordings. This led to a resurgence of interest and new fans in both Europe and the USA. "But she found that she still had fans, especially in Europe."
**First European Tour Since 1957 (1982)** : In 1982, Janice Martin embarked on her first European tour since 1957, performing for audiences who primarily wanted to hear her signature song, "Drugstore Rock and Roll." "So in 1982, she travelled to Europe for the first time since that 1957 tour."
**Toured Regularly Despite Hating Flying (Last Few Decades of Life)** : For the final decades of her life, Janice Martin regularly toured, often with her son playing drums in her band, despite her aversion to flying. She felt a strong obligation to her fans to perform for them. "For the last few decades of her life, Janice Martin would regularly tour, even though she hated flying, because she felt she owed it to the fans to let them see her perform."
**Third Marriage to a Fan (1977)** : In 1977, Janice Martin entered her third marriage, this time to a man who had been a fan of hers during her initial career. This marriage lasted the rest of her life, bringing her happiness. "Her third marriage, in 1977, was to a man who had been a fan of hers during her first career, and lasted the rest of her life."
**Performed with Ruth Brown, Fulfilling a Lifetime Ambition (2006)** : In 2006, as part of her comeback, Janice Martin finally achieved a lifetime ambition by performing on stage with Ruth Brown, singing "Mama He Treat Your Daughter Mean," the song that had inspired her over 50 years earlier. "she was finally able to fulfill her lifetime ambition, and perform on stage with Ruth Brown, singing the song that changed everything for her."
**One-Time Performance with Ruth Brown** : The 2006 performance was the only time Janice Martin and Ruth Brown ever met and performed together. "That was the first and only time Janice Martin and Ruth Brown would meet and perform together."
**Death from Cancer (September 2007)** : Janice Martin passed away from cancer in September 2007, having outlived Elvis Presley by more than 30 years and seeing her work embraced by new generations of fans. "Janice herself died of cancer in September 2007."

# genre - Country & Western

## curiosities
**Dominance of Live Variety Radio Shows (1950s)** : In the 1950s, country and western music was primarily dominated by live variety shows broadcast by different radio stations, which formed a distinct hierarchy with the Grand Ole Opry at the top. "the dominant force in country and western music was a series of live variety shows that would be broadcast by different radio stations."
**Hierarchy of Live Shows** : There was a clear hierarchy among country and western live variety shows, with the Grand Ole Opry at the pinnacle, followed by the Louisiana Hayride, and then shows like the Old Dominion Barn Dance. This hierarchy dictated an artist's status and audience reach. "At the very top of the chain was the grand ol' Opry."
**Conservative Nature of Top-Tier Shows** : Higher-tier country shows like the Grand Ole Opry, while popular, were extraordinarily conservative, adhering to strict formulas and limiting experimentation. "Girls like the Upree, while the popular, were also extraordinarily conservative."
**Experimental Nature of Lower-Tier Shows** : Lower-rung hillbilly shows, such as the Old Dominion Barn Dance, were more experimental and allowed for greater deviation from the straight formula insisted upon by the top shows, providing a space for artists like Janice Martin to try different styles. "the further down the rungs you got, the more experimental the hillbilly shows could be."
**Crossover with R&B for Male Jump Band Artists** : Generally, the few country musicians who played R&B songs primarily covered music originally recorded by male jump band artists like Louis Jordan or Big Joe Turner, rather than female R&B artists whose music was closer to jazz. "Generally, the few who did that were performing music originally recorded by male jump band artists like Louis Jordan or Big Joe Turner."

# genre - Rock and Roll

## curiosities
**Shift in Focus to White Teenage Experience (1950s)** : "Drugstore Rock 'n' Roll" by Janice Martin is identified as one of the first records to specifically address the experience of white teenagers, who were becoming the music's largest audience, from their own perspective, marking a turning point in rock and roll history. "Drugstore rock n roll is the first record we've looked at, and one of the first records ever made, to deal specifically with the experience of the white teenagers who were now the music's biggest audience, and deal with it from their own perspective."
**Appropriation and Marketing by Conglomerates** : Massive conglomerates took musical styles created by marginalized groups, stripped away distinctive elements, and marketed them to affluent, privileged white audiences, happening concurrently with individual artists making sincere music. "a massive cut for conglomerate, taking musical styles created by marginalized groups, removing those elements that made them distinctive to those groups, and marketing them at a more affluent, thrift-validged audience."
**Transition from Black to White Dominance** : Over a few years, rock and roll transitioned from being dominated by black artists for primarily black audiences to being created by and for privileged white teenagers, even with well-meaning white artists entering the scene. "Would still, in aggregate, turn rock and roll from a music that was dominated by black artists, and created for a primarily black audience, into one that was created by and for thrift-validged white teenagers."
**Lyrical Shift: From Adult Themes to Teenage Innocence** : The most popular rock and roll artists shifted from black men singing about "gay sex" and poor white sharecroppers singing about "drinking liquor from an old fruit jar" to "perky teenagers singing about sock hops and going steady." "the most popular artists in rock and roll music would go from being black men singing about gay sex, and poor white sharecroppers singing about drinking liquor from an old fruit jar, to being perky teenagers singing about sock hops and going steady."
**Record Labels' Struggle with White Rock and Roll (1956)** : In 1956, record labels struggled to understand how to market and manage white rock and roll acts for the teenage crowd, beyond established stars like Bill Haley and Elvis Presley or packaged Sun Records artists. "record labels had no idea what to do with white rock and roll and the team crowd."
**Absence of Mid-Range Rock and Roll Stars (Mid-1950s)** : In the mid-1950s, there was no concept or infrastructure for developing mid-range rock and roll stars; artists were either massive chart-toppers or largely ignored by major labels' promotional teams. "There was not yet any or at a pot amid range rock and roll star."
