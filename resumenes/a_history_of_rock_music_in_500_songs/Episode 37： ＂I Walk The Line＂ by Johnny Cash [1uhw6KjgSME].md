## albums
**Gordon Jenkins Album - Crescent City Blues Inspiration** : Before joining the Air Force, Cash heard a song called "Crescent City Blues" from a Gordon Jenkins album owned by a fellow airman in Germany. This song's melody and theme of being "stuck in Crescent City" significantly inspired Cash's later work, particularly "Folsom Prison Blues," mirroring its structure and sentiment. "But I'm stuck in Crescent City just watching live and mosey by."

## songs
**Belchezard** : This was the first song Johnny Cash wrote himself, based on a Bible story. He brought it to a jam session with Luther Perkins and Marshall Grant, who were astonished by the very idea of writing a song, as it had not occurred to them. "The other two were amazed, not so much by the song itself, but by the fact that you could write a song at all."
**Hey Porter** : Inspired by the sound of the railway, this song featured a distinctive "boom-chick-a-boom" rhythm that would later become a trademark of Johnny Cash's sound. Sam Phillips liked it and the Tennessee 3 (later Johnny Cash and the Tennessee 2) set to record it. The lyrics describe a traveler eagerly anticipating crossing the Mason-Dixon Line and wishing for the train to slow down or stop to allow exploration. "Hey Porter, hey Porter, would you tell me the time?"
**Cry! Cry! Cry!** : This sad song, written by Johnny Cash, was chosen by Sam Phillips as the B-side to "Hey Porter" after Phillips deemed "Folsom Prison Blues" unsuitable for the first single. "Cry! Cry! Cry! both charted"
**Folsom Prison Blues (1955)** : This song became Johnny Cash's second single and a defining song for his career, reaching number 4 on the country and western chart and establishing him as a genuine country music star. The concept was inspired by the film "Inside the Walls of Folsom Prison" and musically influenced by Gordon Jenkins' "Crescent City Blues" and Jimmie Rodgers' "Blue Yodel No. 1," particularly the line about shooting Thelma. Cash combined the bleak aimlessness of killing for pleasure with the prison theme, adapting "Crescent City Blues" lyrics. "Folsom Prison Blues became Cash's second single and one of the songs that would define him for the rest of his career."
**All My Love Was Children** : Johnny Cash co-wrote this song with Carl Perkins, which became the B-side to Perkins' "Blue Suede Shoes." While not considered the best work by either artist, it marked the beginning of a significant working relationship that lasted for decades, benefiting both men. "It's not the greatest song either man ever wrote by any means but it was the start of a working relationship that would continue often done for decades and which both men would benefit from significantly."
**I Walk the Line (1956)** : This song was written by Johnny Cash while on the road, reflecting on his struggle to remain faithful to his wife and, implicitly, to his God. It was possibly inspired by a line in Tennessee Ernie Ford's hit "Sixteen Tons." Cash wanted to record it as a slow, plaintive ballad, but Sam Phillips insisted on a faster, rock and roll tempo to suit Cash's rock and roll audience, seeking a strong rhythm for "universality." They compromised by recording two versions, and Phillips released the faster one, much to Cash's initial dismay. Despite Cash's initial dislike of the faster sound, the song became a massive hit, reaching number one on the country jukebox chart, making the top 20 in the pop chart, and selling over two million copies as a single, proving Phillips' commercial instincts. "I Walk the Line" features a highly unusual structure with a key change after every verse: starting in F, moving to B flat, then E flat, returning to B flat, and finally back to F (an octave lower), suggesting a natural cycle mirroring the lyrical metaphors of tides, heartbeat, day and night. The verses use only three chords, but uniquely start with the dominant chord, an unstable choice inspired by Cash having once threaded a tape backwards. The humming at the start of each verse, Cash claimed, was to help him find the right note due to the frequent key changes. "Phillips had unquestionably had the right instincts commercially at least."
**Get Rhythm** : This rock and roll song was written by Johnny Cash to give to Elvis Presley, and Cash cared about it much less than "I Walk the Line." Its lyrics included the line "get rhythm if you get the blues." The song eventually became a hit for Cash himself and a regular feature in his live performances. "That song itself would go on to become a hit for Cash and a staple of his live shows."

## curiosities
**Birth Name and Name Change** : Johnny Cash's birth name was J.R. Cash, initials chosen by his parents who disagreed on a full name. When he joined the Air Force, he was not allowed to use just initials, so he changed his name to John R. Cash. He adopted "Johnny Cash" only after becoming a professional musician, still without a middle name, just the middle initial. "His parents apparently had a disagreement over what their son's name should be, and so rather than give him full names, they just gave him initials."
**First American to Learn of Stalin's Death** : While serving in the military, Johnny Cash was the radio operator who intercepted and decoded the Russian transmissions announcing Stalin's death, making him the very first American to receive this information. "he'd been the very first American to learn that Stalin had died as he'd been the radio operator who'd intercepted and decoded the Russian transmissions about it."
**Move to Memphis and Band Formation (Age 22)** : After returning to the US from his Air Force stint in Germany at age 22, Cash married and moved to Memphis to be near his brother. His brother introduced him to Luther Perkins and Marshall Grant, with whom he started playing music. "After returning to the US from his stint in the Air Force in Germany, aged 22, Cash got married and moved to Memphis to be near his brother."
**Voice Description** : Even as a young man, Johnny Cash's voice was described as if "carved out of rock and imbued with the spirit of an Old Testament prophet." He didn't have a huge range but possessed an astonishingly sonorous, resonant bass-baritone that commanded attention. "Cash never had a huge range, but his voice had a sonority to it that was quite astonishing, a resonant bass baritone that demanded you pay attention to what it had to say."
**Early Musical Ambition and Gospel Roots** : Cash was determined to become a famous singer despite not knowing how. Initially, he and his band mostly performed white gospel songs, music he loved. He had a religious experience as a child when his elder brother, who wanted to be a priest, died after a deathbed vision of heaven and hell, inspiring Cash to become a gospel singer in tribute. "Cash had had a religious experience as a kid when his elder brother, who had wanted to become a priest, had died, and had had a deathbed vision of heaven and hell, and Cash wanted to become a gospel singer to pay tribute to his brother while also indulging his own love of music."
**Encounter with Elvis Presley** : Johnny Cash, along with Luther Perkins and Marshall Grant, went to see Elvis, Scotty, and Bill perform on the back of a flatbed truck. Cash, then 22, was worried he was too old to become a star compared to the teenage Elvis. He later spoke with Scotty Moore, who advised him to contact Sam Phillips at Sun Records. "Cash was immediately worried, Elvis was clearly a teenager, and Cash himself was a grown man of 22."
**Solo Audition for Sam Phillips** : After failing to reach Sam Phillips by phone, Johnny Cash went to see Phillips alone for his first audition. Phillips was immediately impressed by Cash's dignified bearing, presence, and gravitas, seeing him as having the makings of a star. "Phillips was immediately impressed by Cash's bearing and presence. He was taller and more dignified than most of the people who came into audition for Phillips."
**Rejection of Gospel Music by Sam Phillips** : During an audition, Sam Phillips expressed disinterest in releasing gospel music, stating he had no sales success with it and wanted something more commercial. While an urban legend suggests Phillips told Cash to "go home and sin, then come back with a song I can sell," Cash denied this, but he did take the hint and began writing secular songs. "Phillips liked their sound, but explained that he wasn't particularly interested in putting out gospel music."
**Contract with Sun Records** : When the contract was drawn up, only Johnny Cash was officially signed to Sun Records, as Sam Phillips did not want to be tied to the other two musicians (Luther Perkins and Marshall Grant). Despite this, Cash split the money with them, either in a 40-30-30 split or an entirely equal split, according to different sources. "When the contract was drawn up the only person who was actually signed to son was Cash."
**Resentment Towards Sam Phillips' Favoritism** : Johnny Cash resented Sam Phillips for seemingly treating him as less important after Phillips signed Carl Perkins, believing Phillips was only interested in his new star and wouldn't promote Cash's records. This pattern of perceived favoritism would recur with Phillips' new discoveries and contributed to the eventual downfall of Sun Records as artists left for other labels. "Cash resented the way that he was being treated by Phillips as being less important than Perkins."
**Close Friendship with Carl and Clayton Perkins** : Despite his resentment towards Sam Phillips' perceived favoritism of Carl Perkins, Cash became close friends with Carl. He grew even closer to Carl's wayward brother, Clayton Perkins, whose juvenile sense of humor appealed to Cash. "The two became close friends though Cash became even closer with Clayton Perkins, Carl's wayward brother, who had a juvenile sense of humour that appealed to Cash."
**Management by Bob Neal and Promotion as Rock and Roller** : Johnny Cash followed Elvis Presley's career path, being managed by Elvis's first proper manager, Bob Neal, and securing a regular slot on the Louisiana Hayride. This led to Cash being promoted as a rock and roll star alongside Carl Perkins, which broadened his appeal beyond country audiences and contributed to his long-term career success, notably his comeback with the American Recording series decades later. "He was managed by Elvis's first proper manager Bob Neal and he was given a regular slot on the Louisiana Hayride, the country music radio show that Elvis had built his reputation on."
**Lifelong Fan of Miles Travis** : Johnny Cash became a lifelong fan of Miles Travis, the writer of Tennessee Ernie Ford's hit song "Sixteen Tons," which Cash loved when it came out. The imagery in "Sixteen Tons" likely influenced Cash's songwriting for "I Walk the Line." "he made Cash a lifelong fan of his writer, Miles Travis."
**Dispute with Sam Phillips over "I Walk the Line" Tempo** : Johnny Cash fiercely debated with Sam Phillips about the tempo of "I Walk the Line." Cash envisioned it as a slow, plaintive ballad, while Phillips insisted on a faster, rock and roll tempo for commercial appeal. Phillips ultimately released the faster version, devastating Cash initially, but its massive commercial success led Cash to re-evaluate his opinion. "Cash walked out convinced that Phillips would see reason and released the slower version. He was devastated to find that Phillips had released the faster version."
**Marriage Troubles and Move to Columbia** : Despite writing movingly about maintaining his marriage, Johnny Cash's marriage was falling apart. He began to consider moving to a major label, like Elvis Presley had, and was soon signed to Columbia Records, where he would spend most of his career. "His life was in turmoil the marriage that he had written so movingly about trying to keep together was falling apart and he was beginning to think that he would do better doing as Elvis had and moving to a major label soon he would be signed to Columbia the label where he would spend almost all his career."

## curiosities
**Formation and Early Sound** : The group initially formed as Johnny Cash, Luther Perkins, and Marshall Grant, sometimes including steel player Red Carnotle. They were unskilled but possessed a willingness to experiment and Cash's distinctive voice. They primarily played gospel songs, aiming to sound like The Louvin Brothers. "Cash, Perkins and Grant all played acoustic guitar at first, and none of them were particularly good."
**Musical Limitations and Innovative Sound** : Their limitations as musicians forced them to develop a unique sound. Luther Perkins played electric guitar lead lines that were essentially boogie-woogie bass lines transposed up an octave. Marshall Grant, who had never played double bass before, taped markers on his instrument and could only play single notes on every other beat, creating big gaps. These limitations paradoxically led to their signature sound, focusing on rhythm rather than virtuosity. "Their limitations as musicians meant that they had to find ways to make the songs work without relying on complicated parts of virtuoso playing."
**Audition for Sam Phillips and Reduction to The Tennessee 3** : Johnny Cash brought Luther Perkins, Marshall Grant, and Red Carnotle to meet Sam Phillips. When it came time to audition, the steel player, Red Carnotle, became so nervous he couldn't tune his guitar and decided to leave, feeling he was holding the others back. The remaining trio became "The Tennessee 3," a name they chose because, though they all lived in Tennessee, none were originally from there. "Eventually he decided that he was holding the other three back, and left the studio, and the audition continued with just the group who had now become the Tennessee 3."

## curiosities
**Name Change from The Tennessee 3** : When "Hey Porter" was released, Sam Phillips suggested a slight name change from The Tennessee 3, and the single came out under the name Johnny Cash and the Tennessee 2. "Or at least that was what they were called when they recorded it, but by the time it was released, Sam Phillips had suggested a slight name change and the single came out under the name Johnny Cash and the Tennessee 2."
**Development of the "Boom-Chick-a-Boom" Rhythm** : Since the group did not have a drummer, Johnny Cash innovated by placing paper between the strings and the fretboard of his acoustic guitar. This deadened the sound, approximating a snare drum, and resulted in the "boom chick" sound, which became a signature of Cash's recordings for decades. This unique country music take on the two-beat rhythm was largely born out of instrumental necessity. "As the Tennessee 2 didn't have a drummer, Cash put paper between the strings and the fretboard of his acoustic guitar to deaden the sound and turn it into something that approximated the sound of the snare drum."

# label - Sun Records

## curiosities
**Location in Memphis** : Sun Records was a record label based in Memphis, Tennessee. Its local presence motivated Johnny Cash to pursue a recording deal there after hearing a record released on the label. "the record was out on some records, a label based in Memphis itself."
**Sam Phillips' Role and Business Strategy** : Sam Phillips, the head of Sun Records, was often on the road, actively pushing the label's records to distributors and radio stations. He sought commercially viable music, disfavoring gospel music due to a lack of sales success. Phillips' strategy involved investing more time in new discoveries to build their careers, which sometimes led established artists to feel neglected and eventually leave the label. "Phillips was out on the road a lot of the time, pushing the label's records to distributors and radio stations."
**Downfall Due to Artist Departures** : Sun Records' downfall was ultimately caused by its discovered stars, one by one, moving to other labels. These artists felt they were valued more elsewhere, as Sam Phillips' focus on building up new talent often led previous favorites to believe he no longer cared about them. "This is ultimately what led to sons downfall. As one by one his discoveries moved on to other labels that they believed valued them more than Phillips did."
**Commercial Success of "I Walk the Line"** : "I Walk the Line" by Johnny Cash is considered a strong contender for the best record Sun Records ever put out, a high bar given the label's output. Its sale of two million copies in a country market often stereotyped as conservative demonstrated the record's exceptional quality and broad appeal. "it's an utterly idiosyncratic utterly strange record and a very strong contender for the best thing son records ever put out which is a high bar to meet"
