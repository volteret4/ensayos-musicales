## curiosities
**Early Blackface Performances** : Bob Wills initially performed as a blackface comedian, a practice that was very common in the US in the 1930s and even in the UK for decades longer. He later gained renown as a fiddle player and singer. "Will's originally performed as a blackface comedian."
**Creative Tension with Milton Brown** : In the original lineup of the Light Crust Doughboys, Wills experienced a creative tension with singer Milton Brown. Brown sought to experiment more with jazz styles, while Wills preferred a traditional hill-billy string band sound, a dynamic that ultimately led to the creation of a new form of music. "Milton Brown, by all accounts, wanted to experiment more with the jazz style, while Bob Will's wanted to stick with a more traditional hill-billy string band sound."
**Fired by W. Lee "Pappy" O'Daniel** : In 1933, Bob Wills was fired from the Light Crust Doughboys by manager W. Lee "Pappy" O'Daniel. This was partly due to a disagreement over Wills' desire to include his brother in the band, and partly because Wills' increasing drinking habits were affecting his professionalism. "But in 1933, Will's was fired by O'Daniel, partly because of Rouse over Will's wanting his brother in the band, and partly because Will's drinking was already starting to affect his professionalism."
**"King of Western Swing" Title** : Bob Wills' band quickly became the most popular act in the Western Swing genre, earning him the widespread moniker "King of Western Swing." His music blended elements from country, blues, jazz, and other styles to achieve broad appeal. "Will's band quickly became the most popular band in the genre, and he became widely known as the King of Western Swing."
**Mutual Respect with Les Paul and Charlie Christian** : Bob Wills was admired by and admired musicians across racial boundaries. In 1938, jazz guitarist Les Paul traveled from Wisconsin to Tulsa, Oklahoma, specifically to hear Wills' band play, eventually jamming on stage and introducing Charlie Christian to Paul during an interval, in what was likely a rare integrated audience. "He was someone who had the respect of his peers, and he was part of a chain of influences crossing racial and stylistic boundaries."
**Accusation of Plagiarism** : While generally fostering mutual influence, Bob Wills' band was involved in at least one instance of outright stealing a Black musician's song, crediting it to a White musician, and turning it into a massive hit, which is described as "just wrong." "And suddenly that happened with Bob Willes's band at least once."

## curiosities
**Formation and Early Lineup (1929)** : The band was formed in 1929 when Bob Wills, Milton Brown, and Herman Arnspiger first performed together at a Christmas dance party. They soon added Durwood Brown on guitar and John Dunham on fiddle, establishing their initial five-member lineup. "In 1929, Will's, the singer Milton Brown and guitarist Herman Armspiger... Got together to perform a song at a Christmas dance party."
**Early Sponsorship and Name Changes** : The band's original name was The Aladdin Laddies, a result of sponsorship by the Aladdin Lamp Company for a WBAP radio program. After this sponsorship ended, they performed briefly as The Wills Fiddle Band before securing a new sponsor, W. Lee "Pappy" O'Daniel. "Their name was originally the Aladdin Laddies, as they got sponsored by the Aladdin Lamb Company to perform a WBAP video under that name."
**W. Lee "Pappy" O'Daniel Sponsorship** : In the late 1920s and early 1930s, W. Lee "Pappy" O'Daniel, then head of advertising for Burrus Mill and Elevator Company (makers of Light Crust Flour), became the band's sponsor. Initially, O'Daniel disliked their "hill-billy music" and intended to cancel their radio show after two weeks. However, Wills invited him to participate, leading O'Daniel to become the show's MC, the band's manager, and even the writer of their original material. "Papio Daniel at first didn't like this hill-billy music being played on the radio show he was paying for."
**Low Pay and Day Jobs** : Despite the band's immense success, O'Daniel paid them poorly, only $15 a week, and insisted that all members also work day jobs at the mill. For example, Bob Wills worked as a truck driver. This low pay eventually led to members, like Milton Brown, leaving for more lucrative opportunities. "O'Daniel insisted that everyone in the band would have to actually work a day job at the mill as well."
**Creative Tension and Musical Direction** : The band's original lineup was marked by a creative tension between Milton Brown, who favored experimenting with jazz styles, and Bob Wills, who preferred a more traditional hill-billy string band sound. This dynamic is considered a prototypical example of creative tension leading to the development of new musical forms, laying groundwork for Western Swing. "That creative tension led them to create a totally new form of music."
**O'Daniel's Restrictive Policies** : W. Lee "Pappy" O'Daniel placed severe restrictions on the band, including forbidding them from playing venues where alcohol was served or performing at dances. These policies, coupled with the low pay (band members could earn $40 a night from gigs compared to $15 a week from O'Daniel), caused key members like Milton Brown and Bob Wills to eventually leave. "In particular, O'Daniel wouldn't let the doboys play any venues where alcohol was served, or played dances generally."
**Continuation of the Band** : A version of The Light Crust Doughboys has continued to perform to the present day. Smokey Montgomery, who joined the band in 1935, remained with them until his death in 2001, demonstrating the band's enduring legacy. "A version of the light for Sto boy's continues today."

## curiosities
**Formation and Lawsuit (1933)** : Bob Wills formed this band in 1933 after being fired from the Light Crust Doughboys. They advertised themselves as "formerly the Light Crust Doughboys," leading to an unsuccessful lawsuit from W. Lee "Pappy" O'Daniel, who claimed ownership of the name and the band's legacy. "They advertised themselves as formerly the light cross-doboys, although that wasn't entirely true, as they weren't the whole band, though they were the core of it, and Papio Daniel sued them unsuccessfully."
**First Western Swing Band with a Drum Kit** : The Texas Playboys became the first Western Swing band to integrate a full drum kit into their lineup, making them a more overtly rhythm-oriented band. This was a significant departure from traditional country music, which often banned drums from performances. "And the Texas playboys then became the first Western swing band to add a drum kit and become a more obviously rhythm-oriented band."
**Massive Commercial Success** : They were the first massively successful Western Swing band, achieving widespread popularity with a style that blended elements from diverse musical traditions. "The Texas playboys were the first massively, massively successful Western swing band, and their style was one that involved taking elements from everywhere and putting them together."
**Eclectic Musical Style** : The band's style was characterized by its ability to synthesize elements from various genres. They incorporated drums and horns typical of jazz bands, guitars and fiddles from country and western music, and the steel guitar often associated with Hawaiian bands. This diverse instrumentation allowed them to perform and blend jazz, western, blues, and pop, creating a unique sound primarily for dancing, which included a strong backbeat. "They had the drums and horns that the Jazz band would have. The guitars and fiddles that country or western bands would have, the steel guitar that a Hawaiian band would have, and that meant that they could play all those styles of music if they wanted to."
**Influence on Rock and Roll Backbeat** : The band's music, designed for dancing, contained many elements that later became integral to rock and roll, particularly its prominent backbeat, although swung less than in some other forms. Polka music was a significant influence on their backbeat. "As music for dancing, it had a lot of aspects that would later make their way into rock and roll. In particular, it had that backbeat we talked about in episode 2, although here it was swung less."
**Evolving Band Lineup and Amplification (1938-1944)** : In 1938, the Texas Playboys featured a large lineup including a singer, two guitars (one doubling on fiddle), three fiddlers, a banjo, steel guitar, bass, drums, piano, trumpet, trombone, and two saxes. However, with the advent of electric guitars around 1938, Wills began to use them, enabling him to significantly reduce the band's size. By 1944, he had eliminated most of the horn section (except for one trumpet), with electric guitars taking on horn lines. This resulted in a smaller, electrified band consisting of two fiddles, two basses, two electric guitars, steel guitar, drums, and a trumpet, a lineup much closer to the rock bands of the 1950s and 60s. "So by 1944, the band would consist of two fiddles, two basses, two electric guitars, steel guitar, drums, and a trumpet."

# genre - Rock and Roll

## curiosities
**Bob Wills' Definition of Rock and Roll (1957)** : Bob Wills famously stated in 1957 that what was being called "Rock and Roll" was essentially the same basic rhythm music he and others had been playing since 1928, just under a different name. He emphasized that the rhythm is the most important element, whether accompanied by minimal or many instruments. "Rock and Roll. Why, man, that's the same kind of music we've been playing since 1928."
**Influence of Western Swing and Polka** : Western Swing music, particularly its strong backbeat and dance-oriented nature, incorporated many elements that later became fundamental to rock and roll. Polka music, performed in two-four time, was a significant influence on Western Swing musicians and, through them, on the rock and roll backbeat, highlighting a strong connection between the two. "Polka music is performed in two four times, and there's a very, very strong connection between the polka beat and the backbeat."

# genre - Western music

## curiosities
**Geographic and Thematic Characteristics** : Western music originated in Texas, Oklahoma, and California, featuring subject matter largely focused on cowboys and outlaws. It often employed similar instrumentation to country music, such as violins and guitars, but developed a distinct identity. "That was the music being made in Texas, Oklahoma and California, and it tended to use similar instrumentation to country music, violins and guitars and so on."
**"Slicker" Sound in the 1930s-40s** : In the 1930s and 40s, Western music was notably "slicker" than country music, influenced by the "Hollywoodization" of the genre. This period saw the rise of singing cowboy films starring figures like Gene Autry and Roy Rogers, which introduced elements such as crooning and orchestras into the music. "And at the time we're talking about the 30s and 40s, it was a little bit slicker than Country music."

# genre - Western Swing

## curiosities
**Fusion of Swing and Western Styles** : Western Swing emerged as a fusion of swing music, primarily made in the western US (especially California), by musicians with strong country music influences. It combined the rhythms of swing with the instrumentation common in country music, such as fiddle, banjo, guitar, and stand-up bass. "Western Swing is to simplify it ridiculously. Swing music made in the west of the USA."
**Distinctive Instrumentation and Drum Kit Inclusion** : Western Swing typically included instruments like the steel guitar and, notably, a drum kit, which set it apart from traditional country music, where drums were often banned. It also incorporated other instruments not usually found in country or western music at the time, such as piano, and occasionally saxophone or trumpet. "Western Swing also usually included a drum kit, which was one of the big ways it differed from country music as it was then."
**Precursor to Rock Band Lineup** : The typical Western Swing lineup, often featuring guitar, steel guitar, one or two violins, piano, bass, and drums, is identified as a nascent form of the modern rock band lineup, illustrating a gradual evolution. "You can see the rock band line up starting to form."
**Polka Music Influence** : Polka music was a significant influence on Western Swing musicians, contributing to the genre's rhythmic characteristics, particularly its strong backbeat. This influence later extended to rock and roll. "You can hear the influence of polka music, which was a big influence on all the western swing musicians, and through them, unrock and roll."

# instrument - Drum kit

# instrument - Electric guitars

## curiosities
**Emergence as a Musical Instrument (1938)** : Electric guitars only truly established their musical significance around 1938. Many bands, including Benny Goodman's and Bob Wills', began incorporating them at this time, marking a pivotal moment in music history. "Electric guitars only really became a thing in 1938 musically, and a lot of people started using them at the same time."
**Western Musicians' Early Adoption** : Western musicians were among the first to widely adopt electric guitars, largely because they were already familiar with and utilizing amplified steel guitars, which paved the way for the acceptance of other amplified string instruments. "Will's band was one of the first to use them, and western musicians generally were more likely to use them, as they were already using amplified steel guitars."
**Role in Big Band Decline and Smaller Lineups** : The rise of electric guitars, particularly in Bob Wills' band, contributed to the decline of large big bands between 1942 and 1944. Amplification allowed Wills to significantly reduce his band's size, with electric guitars taking over parts previously played by horn sections, leading to smaller, more compact, and electrified lineups resembling later rock bands. "Will's was able to make his band considerably smaller with the aid of amplification."

# curiosity

**Genre Naming Conventions** : Many popular genre names, such as "Rock and Roll," "Rhythm and Blues," and "Country and Western," incorporate the word "and." For "Rhythm and Blues" and "Country and Western," these were initially coined by Billboard magazine as umbrella chart titles for collections of diverse genres, rather than singular stylistic descriptors, but eventually led to the emergence of distinct genres. "Genre names have a strange thing, aren't they? In particular, did you ever notice how many of them had the word AND in them?"
**"Race Records" Terminology** : The term "race records" was an earlier, now-considered-demeaning name used for a chart category specifically for music made by Black people. It was later replaced by "Rhythm and Blues" by Billboard magazine. "Rhythm and Blues is a name that was used to replace the earlier name, race records, because that was thought a bit demeaning."
**Singing Cowboy Films** : The 1930s and 1940s marked the peak era for singing cowboy films, starring figures like Gene Autry and Roy Rogers, who became massive stars. This cinematic trend led to a "Hollywoodization" of Western music, incorporating more refined elements such as crooning and orchestras. "But the 30s and 40s were the heyday of singing cowboy films with people like Gene Ortrie and Roy Rogers becoming massive, massive stars."
**Evolution of the Rock Band Lineup** : The typical lineup of Western Swing bands, comprising instruments like guitar, steel guitar, violin, piano, bass, and drums, is identified as a foundational precursor to the modern rock band lineup. This evolution was a gradual process, further refined by bands like Bob Wills and His Texas Playboys. "You can see the rock band line up starting to form."
**Blackface Performances** : Blackface performances were a lamentably common practice in the United States during the 1930s and persisted in the UK well into later decades. Bob Wills himself began his career as a blackface comedian, highlighting the widespread nature of this practice. "Sadly, blackface performances were very, very common in the US in the 1930s, but then they were common in the UK well into my lifetime."
**W. Lee "Pappy" O'Daniel - Political and Musical Figure** : W. Lee "Pappy" O'Daniel, initially an advertising executive for Burrus Mill and Elevator Company (Light Crust Flour), became a prominent sponsor, manager, and MC for the Light Crust Doughboys. Despite initially disliking "hill-billy music," he leveraged his involvement into a political career, becoming the governor of Texas in the 1940s. "The real Papio Daniel didn't actually become governor of Mississippi, but he did become the governor of Texas in the 1940s."
**Creative Tension in Band Dynamics** : The dynamic between band members, such as the tension between Milton Brown's desire for jazz experimentation and Bob Wills' preference for a traditional hill-billy sound within the Light Crust Doughboys, is highlighted as a prototypical example of creative tension. This friction often leads to the development of entirely new musical forms. "That creative tension led them to create a totally new form of music."
**Big Band Decline (1942-1944)** : Between 1942 and 1944, big bands experienced a significant decline. This period saw musicians like Bob Wills adapt by utilizing amplification to drastically reduce the size of their ensembles, transitioning from large horn sections to more compact electric guitar-driven lineups. "The big bands died between 1942 and 1944, and Will's was able to make his band considerably smaller with the aid of amplification."
**Black Influence, Racism, and Plagiarism in Music** : The relationship between Black and White musicians in early popular music was complex, marked by both mutual influence and systemic racism. While many musicians respected talent across racial lines, there were also instances of white artists, such as Pat Boone, recording "sound-alike versions" of Black artists' hits to profit from segregation, and outright plagiarism, which is characterized as ethically "very, very dodgy" and "just wrong." "The question of black influence on white music is a fraud one, and one that will come up a lot in the course of this history, and a lot of the time people get things wrong."
**Integrated Audiences in 1938 Oklahoma** : The story of Les Paul meeting Charlie Christian at a Bob Wills concert in Tulsa, Oklahoma, in 1938 suggests the presence of an integrated audience. This would have been highly unusual and progressive for Oklahoma in that era, providing a rare glimpse of racial interaction in a public setting. "Forgimably this must have been an integrated audience, which would have been very unusual in 1938 in Oklahoma."
**Shared Songs in Early Music Charts** : In earlier periods of music history, there was less emphasis on a single artist's exclusive claim to a song. It was common for five or six different artists to have hit versions of the same song in the charts simultaneously, often in various styles (country, blues, swing), and this was accepted practice rather than considered plagiarism. "You'd sometimes have five or six people in the charts with hit versions of the same song."
**Development of the Rock Backbeat Scholarship** : The intricate development of the rock backbeat has been the subject of extensive academic research, including a 400-page PhD thesis from 20 years ago. This signifies the profound and detailed historical evolution of fundamental rhythmic elements in popular music. "I found a hugely informative but very detailed look at the development of the rock backbeat, so on Ph.D. Thesis from 20 years ago, 400 pages just on that topic."
