## albums
**The Kinks (1964) - Debut Album** : This album was rushed into production due to the sudden success of "You Really Got Me," recorded by the end of August 1964 after their single hit number one. It is described as a "patchy affair" composed mainly of "poor R&B covers," but it also featured "interesting moments" and a significant leap forward in Ray Davis's songwriting with the track "Stop Your Sobbing." "The resulting album is, as one might expect, a patchy affair, made up mostly of poor R&B covers, but there were some interesting moments, and one song from the album in particular, Stop Your Subbing, showed a giant leap forward in Ray's songwriting."

## songs
**Long Tall Sally (1964)** : This cover of Little Richard's song was suggested by promoter Arthur Howes, who noticed its popularity at a Beatles concert in Paris in January 1964. The Kinks recorded their version three days later with Ray Davis on lead vocals. Despite Howes's belief it would be a hit, the recording lacked the power of the original or the Beatles' version and only reached number 42 on the charts, marking it as a "flop" in the studio's eyes. "The Kinks version had none of the power of Little Richard's original, or of the Beatles version, and only squigged its weight in number 42 on the charts."
**You Still Want Me (1964)** : Released as the group's second single, this track was a "Merseybeat pastiche" developed by Ray Davis under Larry Page's songwriting guidance, which emphasized including "I," "me," and "you" in the title and lyrics for audience connection. However, it performed even worse than their debut single, "Long Tall Sally," contributing to the group's contractual troubles and putting them on the verge of being dropped by their label. "You still want me, a mercy beat pastiche that didn't even do as well as the group's first record."
**You Really Got Me (1964)** : Often cited as the first heavy metal record, this song introduced heavily distorted guitar riffs to the top of the UK charts. Ray Davis developed the basic melody years earlier, evolving from a jazz piano piece (inspired by Jerry Mulligan's "Jazz On A Summer's Day") to a Chet Atkins-style instrumental, and then a Mose Allison-style piece. Its final form was heavily influenced by Larry Page, who suggested focusing on riffs due to Ray's singing, pointing to The Kingsmen's "Louie Louie" as inspiration, and changing the opening lyric from "yeah" to "girl" for audience connection and to address subtle questions about the group's sexuality. The defining distorted sound came from Dave Davis, who slashed his amplifier cone with a knife and fed the sound through a larger amp, a technique almost unknown in Britain at the time that helped standardize fuzz guitar in rock. The original recording was overproduced by Shell Tauhmey with excessive echo and a Phil Spector-like sound, which Ray despised. Larry Page, leveraging his publishing rights with Eddie Casna, insisted on a re-recording, which Robert Wace funded. The second take of the re-recording session, featuring Bobby Graham on drums and Arthur Greenslade on piano, was deemed "extraordinary" and captured Dave Davis's iconic solo, despite Ray's excited "oh no" mid-take. Released on August 4, 1964, it became a smash hit, reaching number one in the UK in September and the Top 10 in the US. "You really got me was released on the 4th of August 1964, and became a smash hit, reaching number one in September."
**Stop Your Sobbing (1964)** : This song, featured on the debut album, represented a "giant leap forward" in Ray Davis's songwriting. It uniquely included backing vocals from Rasa Didzpetris, Ray Davis's new girlfriend, who would become a regular presence on the group's recordings for the subsequent decade and exert a significant influence on Ray's musical direction. "Stop Your Subbing features backing vocals by someone new to the kinks circle. Raise new girlfriend, Racer Ditz Petriss, who would become a regular feature on the group's records for the next decade."

## curiosities
**Early Creativity vs. Label Sabotage** : The Kinks were regarded as second only to The Beatles among British groups in 1960s creativity, but their record label was reportedly more interested in "short term chart success than in artist development," which "sabotaged" their potential. "who were always sabotaged by a record label more interested in short term chart success than in artist development."
**Flexible Early Band Names** : In their initial stages, the group was known as the Ray Davis Quartet. However, the name was flexible; if Pete Quayfe or Dave Davis secured a booking, they would sometimes be credited as the Pete Quayfe Quartet or the Dave Davis Quartet, reflecting the fluid leadership in securing early gigs. "they became the Ray Davis quartet, though sometimes if Pete or Dave rather than Ray got them the booking, there would be the Pete Quayfe quartet or the Dave Davis quartet."
**The Ram Rods (Early Name)** : The group renamed themselves The Ram Rods, taking inspiration from a track by Duane Eddy, who was a significant early rock and roll influence for Ray and Dave Davis. "The group had by now renamed themselves the Ram Rhydts, after a track by Dwain Eddie, who was the first rock and roll musician, Ray and Dave at C&M."
**The Bull Weevils (Early Name)** : Following their stint as The Ram Rods, the group changed their name again to The Bull Weevils. This was the name they held when they recorded their demo, connected with Robert Wace, and had a rehearsal observed by Brian Epstein. "shortly after Ray got serious about the Ram Rhyds, who soon changed their name again to the Bull Weevils."
**The Ravens (Early Name)** : Around the time of their demo recording session, The Bull Weevils adopted yet another name, becoming The Ravens. This name was chosen in homage to a recent film starring Vincent Price, which itself was based on an Edgar Allan Poe poem. "the Bull Weevils changed their name yet again to the Ravens, naming themselves after the recent film, starring Vincent Price, based on the Edgar Allan Pope poem."
**Origin of "The Kinks" Name** : Larry Page suggested the name "The Kinks" for the group, capitalizing on the "kinky" buzzword of the day, which gained popularity in the wake of the Profumo sex scandal and the associated rumors of BDSM. The name was chosen to generate "plenty of outrage" but remain "just about broadcastable," also considering the "camp demeanor" of Dave Davis and Pete Quayfe. Despite the group members initially disliking the name, they ultimately accepted it. "Page decided that this was too good an opportunity to miss... it would make sense to call the group The Kinks, as a name that would generate plenty of outrage, but was still just about broadcastable."
**Rolling Stones Clone Perception** : During their first major tour, The Kinks were largely dismissed by audiences as being a "clone of the Rolling Stones." This perception arose because, like the Stones, they frequently performed a similar repertoire of R&B standards common among London R&B bands at the time, with the Stones being the most recognized reference point for that musical style. "mostly being dismissed as being a clone of the Rolling Stones, because like the Stones, they were relying on the same set of R&B standards that all the London R&B bands played."
**Graham Nash's Stage Act Intervention** : Promoter Arthur Howes sent someone to help The Kinks develop a more "showbiz" stage act. However, this effort was halted when Graham Nash of The Hollies intervened, telling the person to "leave the Kinks alone," asserting that the band was "okay as they are" and did not need further grooming. "Graham Nash of the Hollies ordered him to leave the Kinks alone, saying they were okay as they are."
**Contractual Troubles (Two Flops)** : After releasing two consecutive flop singles ("Long Tall Sally" and "You Still Want Me") on their three-single contract with Pye Records, The Kinks found themselves in a precarious position. It seemed "entirely likely" that the record label would drop them after the release of their next single, making the subsequent success of "You Really Got Me" critical for their survival. "They'd had two flop singles in a row on a three single contract. It seemed entirely likely that the label would drop them after the next single."

**Difficult Sibling Relationship** : Ray had a "difficult relationship" with his younger brother Dave, partly due to their "diametrically opposed personalities." Ray was described as "introverted, thoughtful, and notoriously selfish," and as the former youngest child and only boy, he "resented" Dave taking attention he considered his by right. "Ray was introverted, thoughtful, and notoriously selfish... Ray... resented his younger brother coming along and taking the attention he saw as his by right."
**Sister's Death and First Guitar (1950s)** : Ray's sister, Really, gave him his first guitar as a 13th birthday present, after he had been asking his parents for one for nearly a year since Elvis became popular. That same night, Really, who had a heart condition from childhood rheumatic fever and was advised to avoid exercise but loved dancing, died at a dance hall at age 31. This traumatic event, where his last memory of her was receiving the guitar, deepened his introversion and led him to believe he was "intended by faith to play the guitar." "The last time Ray ever saw his sister was when she was giving him his guitar."
**Child Psychologist (1950s)** : Following his sister's sudden death, Ray became even more introverted, leading him to see a child psychologist. This was an "as far from the normal experience as it's possible to imagine" for a working-class child in the 1950s, highlighting the profound impact of his trauma. "For the next year, Ray was even more introverted than normal, to the point that he actually ended up seeing a child psychologist."
**Cynical Motivation for Early Collaboration** : Ray fostered closeness with his younger brother Dave due to a "cynical motive" related to their pocket money. Ray would advise Dave on "good new records," prompting Dave to buy them, which allowed Ray to play them while spending his own money on other things. "Dave would go out and buy them, and then Ray could play them and spend his own money on other things."
**Big Bill Broonzy Influence & Working-Class Identity** : A 16-minute documentary about Big Bill Broonzy's tour of Belgium, "Low Light and Blue Smoke," deeply affected Ray. His father's approval of Broonzy, describing him as a "real man" unlike "high-voiced girly-sounding pop singers," resonated with Ray's respect for older generations' musical tastes. Crucially, Ray felt Broonzy's performance sounded "authentic" and "working class," which aligned with Ray's own working-class background, distinguishing him from "comparatively privileged" contemporaries like John Lennon or Mick Jagger. "He said later that he thought that Boonsy sounded like him, even though Boonsy was black and American, he sounded working class, and unlike many of his contemporaries, Ray Davis did have a working class background."
**Hornsey Art School Experience** : After leaving school, Ray attended Hornsey Art School, where he felt completely "in his element." Despite his working-class background, Ray harbored a desire to "climb the social ladder" and thrived among people interested in art and music. His particular passion at the time was cinema, regularly attending college film society showings of works by directors such as Bergman, Kurosawa, Truffaut, Eisenstein, and Griffith, although he would complain about the one-shilling entry fee. "Ray, on the other hand, was in his element. While Ray grew up on a council estate and was thoroughly working class, he had always had a tendency to want to climb the social ladder, and he was delighted to be surrounded by people who were interested in art and music."
**Experimental Film Acting** : During his time at Hornsey Art School, Ray Davis starred in several "now-lost experimental films" created by the individual who managed the college's film society, indicating his early artistic ventures beyond music. "Davis also starred in some now-lost experimental films, made by the person who ran the film's society."
**Advice from Alexis Korner** : Following a gig at the art college where Alexis Korner performed with The Rolling Stones as support, Ray Davis approached Korner for advice on advancing his music career. Korner recommended he speak with Giorgio Gomelsky, a promoter and manager known for organizing many of the Stones' early performances. "Davis went up to Conor and asked him for advice about moving on in the music world."
**Temporary Membership in Dave Hunt Rhythm and Blues band** : Ray joined the Dave Hunt Rhythm and Blues band after being offered an audition by Giorgio Gomelsky. While with this group, he played Trad and blues music, including pieces like "Honky Tonk Train Blues" in the style of Bob Crosby's Bobcats. This period offered him "more prestigious gigs" and he learned from jazz musicians, temporarily causing the Ray Davis Quartet to seem "over." "for a while it looked like the Ray Davis Quartet were over, as Ray was getting more prestigious gigs with the Dave Hunt group."
**Return to Brother's Band** : After spending time learning from jazz musicians in the Dave Hunt Rhythm and Blues band, Ray ultimately decided to rejoin his brother Dave's group. This decision was notably influenced by Dave playing him Civil Davis's "Country Line Special," which Ray saw as a bridge between the music he was playing and his desired sound, described as "Big Bill Broonzy with drums." "Ray later remembered that the thing that swung it for him was when Dave played him a record by civil Davis, country line special, which he thought of as a bridge between the kind of music he was playing with Dave Hunt, and the kind of music he wanted to be playing, which he described as big build runesy with drums."
**Brian Epstein's Solo Interest** : During a rehearsal of The Bull Weevils (an early incarnation of The Kinks), Brian Epstein, manager of The Beatles, attended. He expressed "vague" interest in signing Ray as a solo artist but showed no interest in the group itself. This was considered "odd" as Ray was not performing solo leads at the time, with Robert Wace and Dave Davis handling most vocals. "Epstein said he was vaguely interested in sounding Ray as a solo artist, but didn't want the group, and nothing further came of it."
**Career Expectations & Segovia Ambition** : Upon quitting art school to pursue music full-time, Ray Davis initially harbored modest expectations for his pop star career. He hoped that six months as a professional musician would earn him enough money to move to Spain and take guitar lessons from the legendary classical guitarist Andrés Segovia. "He did, though, think that if he was lucky, he could make enough money from six months of being a full-time pop musician, that he could move to Spain and take guitar lessons from psychovia."
**Disputed Session Guitarist Credit (Jimmy Page)** : It has been reported by various sources, notably producer Shell Tauhmey, that session guitarist Jimmy Page played Ray Davis's rhythm parts on most of The Kinks' early recordings. However, this claim is disputed by other sources, including Ray Davis himself, who "insisted he played the parts." "It's also been reported by various people, notably Shell Tommie, that the session guitar is Jimmy Page played Ray Davis's rhythm parts for him, and most of the groups early recordings, although other sources dispute that, including Ray himself, who insisted he played the parts."
**Songwriting Mentorship by Larry Page** : Larry Page actively worked with Ray Davis, focusing on teaching him the "craft of songwriting." Page had a clear philosophy that hit songs needed to connect with the audience, specifically advising the brothers to write songs with "I, me, and you in the title, and repeat those words as much as possible." "Page was working hard with Ray, who was the brother who was Morrie get to learn the craft of songwriting."
**Dislike of Original "You Really Got Me" Recording** : Ray Davis was "devastated" by Shell Tauhmey's initial overproduced recording of "You Really Got Me," which he felt was a "Phil Spector sound-alike" drenched in echo, and did not want it released. He, along with Dave, described it as being taken "much slower." "Ray in particular was devastated by the result, and didn't want it released."
**"Oh No" During "You Really Got Me" Solo** : During the second, successful take of the "You Really Got Me" re-recording session, Ray Davis "yelled, oh no, with excitement" as Dave Davis launched into his iconic guitar solo. Ray initially believed he had distracted Dave at this crucial moment, but Dave instead "smirked and delivered live the defining solos of the rock genre." "Going into the solo, Ray yelled, oh no, with excitement, looking over at Dave, and became convinced that he'd distracted Dave at the crucial moment."
**Exploration of Gender and Sexuality** : Ray Davis has "always been keen to play around with notions of gender and sexuality" in his work. This tendency contributed to manager Larry Page's decision to change a lyric in "You Really Got Me" from "yeah you really got me going" to "girl you really got me going," partly to address subtle questions about the group's sexuality. "Ray has always been keen to play around with notions of gender and sexuality."

## curiosities
**Not the Google Co-founder** : The Larry Page involved in managing The Kinks is explicitly identified as "not the Larry Page who would later co-found Google." This distinction is made to avoid confusion between the music manager and the tech entrepreneur. "This is not the Larry Page who would later co-found Google."
**Failed Singing Career ("The Teenage Rage")** : Before becoming a manager, Larry Page attempted a career as a British teen idol under the stage name "Larry Page the teenage rage." This career was short-lived and "somewhat sabotaged by his inability to sing," along with his producers' insistence on recording a particular song whose original version was deemed too poor to be a UK hit. **Partnership with Eddie Casna and Shell Tauhmey** : After his singing career ended, Larry Page ventured into management, forming a "complicated business arrangement" with Austrian songwriter and publisher Eddie Casna and independent producer Shell Tauhmey. This arrangement saw Page manage artists, Casna publish their songs (including those written by Page's acts), and Tauhmey produce them, creating an integrated system to guarantee record deals. "Page and Casna had entered into a complicated business arrangement... Casna and Page had a third partner in the complicated arrangements, independent producer Shell Tauhmey."
**Integrated Music Business Model** : Larry Page, Eddie Casna, Shell Tauhmey, and Decca's Dick Rowe operated in a close, "hand in glove" business model. Page managed artists, Casna published their songs, Tauhmey produced them, and Rowe signed them to his label. This comprehensive structure made contacting Page a virtual guarantee of a record deal for the Ravens (The Kinks). "Paige, Casta, Tauhmey and Rowe all worked hand in glove with each other. With Paige managing artists, Casta publishing the songs they recorded, Tauhmey producing them, and Rowe signing them to his record label."
**Songwriting Mentorship** : Larry Page actively worked to develop Ray and Dave Davis as songwriters, particularly Ray, teaching them the "craft of songwriting." His philosophy centered on creating a strong connection between the singer and audience, leading him to advise the brothers to use words like "I," "me," and "you" frequently in titles and lyrics. "He told the brothers that they needed to write songs with the words I, me, and you in the title, and repeat those words as much as possible."
**Naming of "The Kinks"** : Larry Page was responsible for naming the group "The Kinks." He saw an opportunity in the contemporary "kinky" buzzword, which arose from the Profumo sex scandal and was popularized by "Kinky Boots" in "The Avengers." He believed the name, especially given the "camp demeanor" of Dave Davis and Pete Quayfe, would create "plenty of outrage" but still be "just about broadcastable." "Page decided that this was too good an opportunity to miss... it would make sense to call the group The Kinks, as a name that would generate plenty of outrage, but was still just about broadcastable."
**Influence on "You Really Got Me" Lyrics** : Larry Page made a minor but significant change to the lyrics of "You Really Got Me." Ray Davis had initially started the song with "yeah you really got me going," but Page suggested altering it to "girl you really got me going." This change aimed to enhance audience connection, add variety to the repetitive lyrics, and subtly address emerging questions about the group's sexuality.
