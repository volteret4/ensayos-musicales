## songs
**First Primettes Single (Year Unknown)** : The Primettes eventually released their own single, featuring Mary Wilson on lead vocals for the B-side, which also showcased a prominent high vocal part from the band. "The group also eventually got to put out their own single. While the B side had Wilson singing lead, but also featured a prominent high part from the band."
**I Want a Guy (Year Unknown) / Never Again (Year Unknown) - First Single as The Supremes** : This single marked the group's debut under their new name, The Supremes. The B-side, "Never Again," was co-written by Barry Gordy and described as a "knockoff" of "Maybe Better Chanteaux." The single ultimately "flocked" (flopped). "That came out under the name The Supremes, with the Bavigordi song on the B side, and knockoff of maybe better chanteaux called Never Again."
**Butter Pop Corn (Year Unknown)** : The Supremes' second single, with Florence Ballard on lead vocals, was a "knockoff over knockoff." Barry Gordy re-worked "Peanut Butter" (a remake of "Hully Gully" by The Olympics) into this song, which was characterized by "extremely weak doable entendres." It was a commercial flop and marked Florence Ballard's last lead vocal on a Supremes single. "Gordi chose the rework that song as Butter Pup Corn, a song that's just an excuse for extremely weak doable entendres, and Florence got to sing lead."
**Your Heart Belongs To Me (Year Unknown)** : Written and produced by Smokey Robinson, this song was The Supremes' third single, following two consecutive flops. It achieved a modest chart position, reaching number 95. "your heart belongs to me, written and produced for them by Smoky Robinson, did make number 95 in the charts."
**The Man with the Rock and Roll Banjo Band (Year Unknown)** : This B-side song, considered one of The Supremes' "truly terrible" flops, was likely an attempt to emulate "Buenos Aires Dance with the Guitar Man." However, it replaced the electric guitar with a banjo, an instrument deemed incompatible with the characteristic Motown sound. "the man with the Rock and Roll banjo band, a song that seems to have been based around Buenos Aires dance with the guitar man. But instead of the electric guitar, the Supremes song was about the banjo, and instruments which has many virtues, but which does not really fit into the Motown sound."
**When the Love Light Starts Shining Through His Eyes (1963)** : This was the first song written by the Holland-Dozier-Holland team for The Supremes, marking a stylistic departure from HDH's previous heavy soul records. Lamont Dozier cited Phil Spector and possibly Brian Wilson as influences, resulting in a "wall of sound feel" filtered through Motown's funkier style and a "Bo Diddley influence." It featured additional backing vocals from The Four Tops and became The Supremes' first bona fide hit, reaching number 23 on the charts. "When the love light start shining through his eyes, once in number 23, the first bonafide hit the Supremes it ever had."
**Run Run Run (Year Unknown)** : Intended as a sure-fire smash follow-up to "When the Love Light Starts Shining Through His Eyes," this Holland-Dozier-Holland production was a "Phil Specter knockoff" recorded before "Love Light" but held back for strategic release. Despite high hopes, it only reached number 94 on the charts, leaving the group at a low point and fearing their first hit was a fluke. "Run Run Run only went to number 94."
**Surfer Boy (1965)** : Written by Holland-Dozier-Holland for The Supremes, this song demonstrates their potential attention to Brian Wilson's work, even though Lamont Dozier's recollection of Wilson's early influence on "When the Love Light" might have been misremembered given Wilson's earlier surf rock style. "After all, they wrote surfer boy for the Supremes in 1965."
**Where Did Our Love Go (June, Year Unknown)** : This iconic song was initially conceived by Holland-Dozier-Holland for a proposed Marvelettes album, featuring a simple, repetitive 8-bar verse without a bridge, chorus, or middle eight. Rejected by The Marvelettes, the backing track was passed to The Supremes, who were pressured to record it. The session was fraught with conflict over who would sing lead (Mary Wilson initially, then Diana Ross by executive decision), Diana's dissatisfaction with the song's key and "terrible" quality, and the eventual spontaneous "baby baby" backing vocals. Despite the group's unhappiness, Barry Gordy immediately recognized its hit potential and promised them future success. "Gordy, much to Ross's shock, was convinced it was a hit, and said to them, cheer up everybody, from now on you're the big hit Supremes."
**Baby Love (October 1964)** : Following the success of "Where Did Our Love Go," which prominently featured the word "baby" 68 times, the production team decided to replicate the winning formula. "Baby Love" was released and reached number one by the end of October 1964, making The Supremes the first Motown act to achieve two number-one singles. "That went to number one by the end of October 1964, making the Supremes the first motown act to have two number ones."

## curiosities
**Most Successful Vocal Group of the 60s** : The Supremes became the most successful vocal group of the 1960s, also recognized as the most important act from Motown. In terms of chart success, they were surpassed only by The Beatles and Elvis during that decade. "The group who have become the most successful vocal group of the 60s."
**"The No Hit Supremes" Nickname** : Prior to the release of "Where Did Our Love Go," the group was infamously known within Motown as "the No Hit Supremes" due to their string of unsuccessful singles. "up until this record was known in Motown as the No Hit Supremes."
**Detroit Origins** : Like many Motown acts, The Supremes' story begins in Detroit, specifically evolving from an earlier male vocal group called The Primes. "the Supremes starts, like almost every Motown act, in Detroit."
**Formation of The Primettes** : The formation of The Primettes by Milton Jenkins involved various approaches, including stopping girls in their housing project. Betty McGlown was chosen due to her relationship with Paul Williams of The Primes, while Mary Wilson and Florence Ballard, who knew each other and were strong singers, joined together, with Ballard initially taking the role of the group's leader. "Betty McGlown was dating Paul Williams, so she was an obvious choice. Mary Wilson and Florence Ballard knew each other and were good singers, especially Ballard, and they joined together, with Ballard becoming the new group's leader."
**Diana Ross's Entry and Early Group Dynamics** : While Diana Ross claimed to be singing around the neighborhood, Mary Wilson stated they didn't know her, suggesting Jenkins brought her in. The group's members, although from the same area, were assembled by a third party rather than forming organically as friends, a dynamic that significantly impacted their relationships over time. "this wasn't a group that came together as friends, but people who were put together by a third party."
**Early Reputation of Diana Ross** : Before joining The Primettes, Diana Ross had an established reputation among those involved in Detroit's talent shows, with friends like Melvin Franklin (from The Distants) praising her singing abilities. This early recognition likely played a role in Jenkins inviting her to the group. "Ross was probably introduced to the group, because she already had a reputation among the people who were playing Detroit's talent shows."
**Diana Ross's Name Spelling** : Diana Ross was initially known by the spelling "Diane" on her birth certificate, which she attributes to a misspelling by the person filling out the document. She asserts her parents always intended her name to be "Diana," the spelling she has consistently used for many decades since. "she says herself that her parents always intended to name her Diana, and the person filling in the birth certificate misspelled it, and she's used Diana for many decades now."
**Misogynistic Portrayal in Biographies** : Diana Ross has faced significant criticism, often rooted in misogyny, from colleagues and biographers. A male biographer, for instance, described her as looking "emaciated and vacant" (noting her struggle with anorexia), compared her face to E.T., and suggested she used her sexuality to advance her career, particularly in criticisms regarding her affair with Barry Gordy. "almost all the negative things said about her have a deep undercurrent of misogyny."
**Affair with Barry Gordy and Ethical Scrutiny** : Diana Ross's affair with Motown head Barry Gordy has been a recurring point of criticism, with accusations that she used it to further her ambitions. The narrative highlights that such criticism is often misogynistic and suggests that if there was ethical misconduct in the relationship between a powerful, older, married employer and his much younger employee, the primary fault is unlikely to lie with Ross. "criticises her for having affairs with prominent people at Motown, most notably Barry Gordy, and accuses her of doing this in order to further her own ambitions."
**Transition from Backing Group to Standalone Act** : Initially formed as a potential backing group for The Primes, Milton Jenkins soon recognized the potential of The Primettes as a distinct vocal group in their own right. "Jenkins soon realised that this group could be better used as a group in their own right, rather than merely as backing vocalists for the primes."
**Florence Ballard as Early Group Leader** : In the early stages of The Primettes, Florence Ballard was unequivocally the group's leader, possessing the most outspoken personality and the strongest voice. She was the first to sing out during auditions, performing Ray Charles's "Night Time is the Right Time." "Florence Ballard was the leading with a group. She had the most outspoken personality and also had the best voice."
**Early Repertoire and Lead Vocal Distribution** : The Primettes' early acts frequently featured songs like Ray Charles's "Night Time is the Right Time," "The Twist," and "There Goes My Baby." While all members took lead vocals on stage, Florence Ballard was considered the primary lead. "That would become a staple of the girls' early acts. Along with the twist, and there goes my baby."
**Contrasting Vocal Styles and Ambition (Ballard vs. Ross)** : Florence Ballard was a natural, passionate singer who deeply moved audiences with seemingly little effort, whereas Diana Ross was a more calculated performer, diligently working to improve her vocals and learn what resonated with audiences, despite Ballard initially believing Ross shouldn't be a lead singer. "Ballard was a natural singer, who sang passionately in a way that apparently blew audiences away with relatively little effort because she was singing from the heart. Ross, on the other hand, was a calculated performer, who was deliberately trying to gain the audience's popularity and was improving with every show as she learned what worked."
**Mary Wilson as Peacemaker** : Mary Wilson consistently acted as the group's peacemaker, adept at resolving conflicts and mediating disputes between Florence Ballard and Diana Ross due to her strong aversion to conflict. "Mary Wilson was always the peacemaker, someone who was so conflict diverse, she would find a way to get Florence and Diana to stop fracking no matter what."
**Betty McGlown's Lack of Commitment** : Betty McGlown was the least committed member of the original group, participating primarily as a favor to her boyfriend, Paul Williams of The Primes. "Betty was the least interested in being in a group she was just doing it as a favour for her boyfriend."
**Marvin Tarplan - The Primettes' Guitarist** : Marvin Tarplan was a unique fifth member of The Primettes, who, despite not singing, played guitar, making them one of the few vocal groups in Detroit at the time to have their own instrumental accompaniment. "Marvin Tarplan, who didn't sing but in play guitar, which made them one of the few vocal groups in the city, who had their own accompaniment."
**Management Transition and Self-Reliance** : After their initial manager, Milton Jenkins, left management following a hospital stay, The Primettes began to manage themselves, taking over their own affairs. "Franklin dropped out of management, he spent some time in hospital, and after getting out he just never got back in touch with the girls, and the primates took over looking after themselves."
**First Motown Contact via Diana Ross and Smokey Robinson** : The Primettes' initial significant contact with Motown Records was facilitated by Diana Ross, who leveraged her prior friendship and adolescent crush on Smokey Robinson. Knowing Robinson was recording for Motown, Ross reached out, leading him to suggest an audition for her group, with the promise of an introduction to Barry Gordy if successful. "their first real contact with Motown came through Ross."
**Audition for Smokey Robinson and Marvin Tarplan's Departure** : During their audition for Smokey Robinson, he was largely unimpressed by The Primettes' vocals but highly regarded their guitarist, Marvin Tarplan. Robinson then struck a deal: he would secure a Motown audition for the girls if he could borrow Tarplan for an upcoming Miracles tour, a "temporary" arrangement that ultimately lasted 50 years. "Robinson wasn't hugely impressed, except with their guitarist, so Robinson made a deal with them."
**Audition for Barry Gordy and Early Rejection** : The Primettes successfully secured an audition with Motown head Barry Gordy, who offered encouragement but advised them to return after completing their schooling. "the girls did indeed audition for Barry Gordy, who was encouraging, but told them to come back after they had finished school."
**Session Work with Other Labels** : Despite Barry Gordy's initial deferral, Motown producers Richard Marvis and Robert Bateman chose not to wait, instead becoming The Primettes' managers and securing them session work on other labels, including early recordings for future soul legends like Wilson Pickett. "Marvis became their major, and they started getting session work on early recordings by future soul legends, like Wilson Pickett."
**Betty McGlown's Departure** : Betty McGlown, who had always been less committed to The Primettes than the other members, decided to leave the group to get married, leading to her quick replacement by Barbara Martin. "Betty decided to leave the group to get married."
**Barbara Martin Joins The Primettes** : After Betty McGlown's departure, Barbara Martin joined The Primettes as her replacement. "she was quickly replaced with a new singer, Barbara Martin."
**Florence Ballard's Traumatic Experience** : Florence Ballard endured a devastating experience when she was raped by an acquaintance, leading to profound and lifelong trauma. This event left her unable to trust anyone, including her friends, and resulted in what would now be diagnosed as post-traumatic stress disorder, significantly influencing her later interactions and conflicts within the group, particularly with Diana Ross. "Florence Ballard was raped by an acquaintance. This traumatized Ballard deeply, and from this point on she became unable to trust anyone, even her friends."
**Persistence and Strategy to Join Motown** : Despite facing multiple setbacks, including Florence Ballard's trauma and their manager's imprisonment, The Primettes demonstrated ambition and determination to join Motown. They proactively frequented Hitsville U.S.A., performing minor session work like handclaps and backing vocals, effectively immersing themselves in the studio environment until they gained an opportunity. "the primates were ambitious, and they decided that they were going to force their way into Motown where the very good he wanted them or not."
**Breakthrough with Freddie Gohrmann and Brian Holland** : The Primettes' persistence at Motown's studio paid off when Freddie Gohrmann, a postman and songwriter, was working on a song with Brian Holland. Gohrmann suggested the girls try out the song with harmonies, and the positive result led to Barry Gordy's involvement in tinkering with the song and co-producing the session. "Gohrmann suggested they try the song out to see what it sounded like with harmonies, and the result was good enough that Holland and Gohrmann called in Gordy, who tinkered with the song to get his name on the credits, and then helped produce the session."
**Name Change: The Primettes to The Supremes** : The group officially changed its name from The Primettes to The Supremes with the release of their first single under the new moniker. The origin of the name was contentious, leading to later legal disputes where Florence Ballard claimed intellectual property rights as the one who chose it. "the primates had now become the Supremes."
**Florence Ballard's Role in Name Selection** : While details are disputed, the consensus is that Florence Ballard selected the name "The Supremes" from a short list. Disagreements persist over whether the list was generated by group members or Janie Bradford, and whether Ballard's choice was deliberate or random. "it seems to be the consensus that Ballard did pick the name from a short list."
**Barry Gordy's Early Focus on Diana Ross** : From the outset, Barry Gordy demonstrated a primary interest in Diana Ross as an individual star, rather than The Supremes as a collective group. While other members initially took lead on album tracks, singles were generally reserved for Diana, with one notable exception. "he was only interested in Diana Russ as an individual, though at least at first all the members would get to take lead vocals on album tracks, though the singles would be safe for Diana, with one exception."
**Florence Ballard's Last Lead Vocal on a Single** : Florence Ballard sang lead on The Supremes' second single, "Butter Pop Corn." However, the song was a commercial failure, proving "no more successful than I want to go," and marked the final occasion Florence Ballard would ever sing lead on a Supremes single. "that would be the last time Florence Ballard ever got to sing lead on a Supremes single."
**Barbara Martin's Departure and Transition to Trio** : Barbara Martin, who had recorded material with The Supremes that was later released, left the group due to pregnancy. Following her departure, the remaining three members—Diana Ross, Mary Wilson, and Florence Ballard—decided to continue as a trio. "she became pregnant and decided to leave the group. Having decided that they clearly couldn't keep a fourth single around, the other three decided to continue on as a trio."
**Continued String of Flops and "No Hit Supremes" Nickname** : Following "Your Heart Belongs To Me," The Supremes released a series of commercially unsuccessful singles, many described as "truly terrible," that barely scraped the bottom of the Top 100 charts but were not considered hits. This period solidified their nickname as "the No Hit Supremes," as they were often given songs deemed unsuitable for other Motown groups. "The group got the nickname the No Hit Supremes, and tended to get the songs that wouldn't pass muster for all the groups."
**Struggles with Chart Success (Two-Year Period)** : For two years, The Supremes continued to struggle with chart success, consistently being surpassed by other Motown acts like The Marvelettes and The Vandellas, who had signed after them but achieved hits sooner. Despite various producers, including Lamont Dozier, Brian Holland, Barry Gordy, and Smokey Robinson, attempting to record with them, their singles rarely charted above the bottom of the Top 100. "This sort of thing continued for two years, with the Supremes now being passed in chart success, not only by the Marvelettes, but also by the Van Dellers."
**Breakthrough with Holland-Dozier-Holland** : The Supremes' fortunes finally turned when they were paired with Holland-Dozier-Holland, who had recently achieved success with The Vandellas. This partnership, driven by Barry Gordy's persistent belief in Diana Ross's star potential, ultimately led to their first bona fide hit. "nothing happened until they were paired with Holland Dozier and Holland, fresh off their success with the Van Dellers."
**Coerced Recording of "Where Did Our Love Go"** : The Supremes were compelled to record "Where Did Our Love Go," a song previously rejected by the more established Marvelettes, and were advised by them to avoid it. Unlike other Motown artists, The Supremes, due to their lack of success at the time, had no choice but to record the track despite their strong dislike for it and feeling they were getting "everyone else's leftovers." "the Supremes weren't yet, and didn't have any choice, they were going to record the song whether they liked it or not."
**Studio Conflict and Lead Vocal Dispute for "Where Did Our Love Go"** : The recording session for "Where Did Our Love Go" was marked by significant tension, largely due to a dispute over the lead vocalist. Mary Wilson was initially told she would sing lead, but Barry Gordy's directive (or a producer's insistence, accounts vary) shifted lead duties to Diana Ross, devastating Wilson and annoying Ross, who disliked the song and its low key. "Mary was devastated, while Ross was annoyed that she was having to sing what she thought was a terrible song, in a key that was much lower than she was used to."
**Diana Ross's Reluctance and Studio Behavior during "Where Did Our Love Go"** : Diana Ross expressed strong displeasure about singing "Where Did Our Love Go," viewing it as a "terrible song" and "everyone else's leftovers." She resisted Eddie Holland's coaching on phrasing and threatened to call Barry Gordy. She performed the lead vocal with minimal emotion, "glaring daggers at the producers," and stormed off immediately after finishing her take. "She sang through her lead, putting this little emotion as she could into her voice, while glaring daggers at the producers, before storming off as soon as she completed the take they wanted, complaining about being given everyone else's leftovers."
**Spontaneous "Baby Baby" Backing Vocals for "Where Did Our Love Go"** : During the recording of "Where Did Our Love Go," the other two Supremes struggled with the difficult backing vocal parts devised by Lamont Dozier, exacerbated by their poor mood and Mary Wilson's disappointment. A frustrated Dozier eventually instructed them to simply sing "baby baby" when prompted, which became the iconic backing vocal. "frustrated Dozier told them to just sing baby baby when he pointed, and they went with that."
**Barry Gordy's Vision for "Where Did Our Love Go"** : Despite the group's and producers' unhappiness with the recording session for "Where Did Our Love Go," Barry Gordy, after hearing the playback, was immediately convinced it would be a hit. His enthusiasm was pivotal, and he declared, "cheer up everybody, from now on you're the big hit Supremes," initiating a massive promotional push for the song. "Gordy, much to Ross's shock, was convinced it was a hit, and said to them, cheer up everybody, from now on you're the big hit Supremes."
**Rapid Ascent to Number One on Dick Clark Tour** : Upon the release of "Where Did Our Love Go" in June, The Supremes were the opening act at the bottom of the bill on Dick Clark's Caravan of Stars tour, performing alongside over a dozen other acts. Their single rapidly ascended the charts, reaching number one by the tour's conclusion. "By the end of the tour, their record was at number one in the chart."
**First Motown Act with Two Number Ones (1964)** : With "Baby Love" reaching number one by October 1964, The Supremes became the first act on the Motown label to achieve two number-one singles, signaling their burgeoning dominance in the music industry. "making the Supremes the first motown act to have two number ones."
**Internal Strife and Rumors of Diana Ross's Solo Ambitions** : Despite their commercial success, significant internal conflict began to emerge within The Supremes, even during the Dick Clark tour. Rumors circulated about Diana Ross's desire for a solo career and discussions arose about her potential efforts to force Florence Ballard out of the group. "Even on the Dick Clark tour bus, there were rumours that Diana Ross wanted a solo career, and there was talk of her forcing Florence Ballard out of the group."
**Mary Wilson's Legacy and Passing** : Mary Wilson was the only member of The Supremes to remain with the group from its inception until its split in 1977. Despite The Supremes being the second most commercially successful group of the 1960s after The Beatles, Wilson was considered "under rewarded" in life, and her recent passing was "under reported." "Mary Wilson, the only member of the Supremes who stayed with the group from the beginning right through to their split in 1977."

**Influence on Motown Sound** : The "heavy soulful records" were a specialized genre for Holland-Dozier-Holland, distinct from the style they employed for The Supremes' "When the Love Light Starts Shining Through His Eyes," indicating the diverse stylistic range within Motown's production. "heavy soulful records they'd specialised in up until that point."

# genre - Surf Rock

## curiosities
**Early Brian Wilson Style** : In 1963, Brian Wilson was noted for creating "rather garrison surf rock records," a genre distinct from the "symphonic pop" he would develop later. "still making rather garrison surf rock records."

# genre - Symphonic Pop

## curiosities
**Brian Wilson's Later Specialization** : Brian Wilson would later specialize in "symphonic pop," a genre he began to develop the year after 1963, moving beyond his earlier "surf rock" style. "the symphonic pop he would start to specialise in the next year."

# label - Motown Records

## curiosities
**Hitsville U.S.A. as a Creative Hub** : Hitsville U.S.A., Motown's studio, was a bustling environment where aspiring artists, including The Primettes, would "hang around" and often find opportunities to contribute minor session work, such as handclaps and backing vocals, fostering a unique pathway into the label. "They took to hanging around Hitzville, acting like they belonged there, and they soon found themselves during minor bits of work on sessions, handclaps and backing vocals and so on."
**Artist Lack of Creative Control** : Typically, Motown artists had "no say in what songs they recorded," reflecting a strict hierarchical control over their creative output. This policy meant groups like The Supremes often had to record material they disliked. "Normally, Motown artists have no say in what songs they recorded."
**Financial Structure for Creative Workers** : Motown's financial structure imposed royalty charges on creative workers, including producers and songwriters, for studio time. This system meant that if a recorded track was not released, the creative team would incur financial losses, influencing decisions like passing on rejected songs to other artists. "Motown caused problems for its creative workers was that they would be charged against royalties for studio time."
**Slump Period (Pre-"Where Did Our Love Go")** : Prior to the success of "Where Did Our Love Go," Motown Records was experiencing a slump, characterized by disappointing follow-ups from several major stars and the departure of one of its biggest artists, Mary Wells, to another label. "Mo Tan was in a bit of a slump at that point. Several of the label's big stars had had disappointing follow-ups to their hits, and they'd just lost Mary Wells one of their biggest stars to another label."

# concert - Dick Clark's Caravan of Stars Tour

## curiosities
**Platform for "Where Did Our Love Go" Success** : Dick Clark's Caravan of Stars tour provided a crucial platform for The Supremes' "Where Did Our Love Go." Initially performing as the opening act at the bottom of a bill featuring over a dozen groups, the tour coincided with the song's rapid ascent to number one on the charts. "When the record came out in June, they were at the bottom of the bill, opening the show and a bill with more than a dozen of the acts, from the zombies to the charelles to Freddy Boom Boom Cannon above them."

# instrument - Banjo

## curiosities
**Incongruous with Motown Sound** : The banjo was featured in The Supremes' song "The Man with the Rock and Roll Banjo Band," which aimed to replicate the feel of "Buenos Aires Dance with the Guitar Man." Despite its virtues, the instrument was considered ill-fitting for the characteristic Motown sound, contributing to the song's perceived poor quality. "the banjo, and instruments which has many virtues, but which does not really fit into the Motown sound."

# curiosity

**Holland, Dozier and Holland - Most Important Motown Team** : The success of "Where Did Our Love Go" elevated Holland, Dozier and Holland to become the most important songwriting and production team within Motown Records. "made Holland, Dozier and Holland the most important team in Motown."
**Holland, Dozier and Holland - Prior Success with The Vandellas** : Before achieving their breakthrough with The Supremes, Holland, Dozier and Holland had already established themselves as a successful production team, notably through their work with The Vandellas. "fresh off their success with the Van Dellers."
**Holland, Dozier and Holland - Evolution of Sound for The Supremes** : Holland, Dozier and Holland's initial song for The Supremes, "When the Love Light Starts Shining Through His Eyes," represented a distinct shift from the "heavy soulful records" they typically produced, incorporating influences like Phil Spector's "wall of sound" and Bo Diddley. "The first song that Holland Dozier and Holland wrote as a team for the Supremes is very different from the heavy soulful records they'd specialised in up until that point."
**Holland, Dozier and Holland - Influences: Phil Spector and Brian Wilson** : Lamont Dozier stated that when conceiving "When the Love Light Starts Shining Through His Eyes," he was thinking of Phil Spector and Brian Wilson, aiming for a "wall of sound feel." While the Spector influence is plausible, the Brian Wilson influence in 1963 is questioned, as Wilson was then known for "surf rock" rather than "symphonic pop," though HDH did write "Surfer Boy" for The Supremes in 1965. "Lamont Dozier has said that when he came up with the idea for when the love light start shining in his eyes, he was thinking of Phil Specter and Brian Wilson."
**Holland, Dozier and Holland - "Where Did Our Love Go" - Rejected by The Marvelettes** : Holland-Dozier-Holland originally created "Where Did Our Love Go," a simple 8-bar verse song, for a proposed Marvelettes album. However, The Marvelettes rejected the backing track in favor of other options, creating a problem for HDH, who faced royalty charges for unreleased studio time. "Holland Dozier and Holland were working on tracks for a proposed Marvelettes album and came up with a simple stumping song based on a repetitive 8 bar verse, with no bridge, chorus or middle 8."
**Holland, Dozier and Holland - Producer Disagreement on Lead Vocalist for "Where Did Our Love Go"** : There were conflicting accounts among the Holland-Dozier-Holland team regarding who should sing lead on "Where Did Our Love Go." Eddie Holland recalled favoring Mary Wilson, while Brian Holland and Lamont Dozier insisted on Diana Ross, or vice-versa, indicating internal friction exacerbated by Barry Gordy's directive for Ross to lead all Supremes singles. "Everyone seems to read that Brian Holland insisted on Diana Ross singing if instead, but Eddie Holland remembers that he thought that Wilson should sing, and it was Brian and Dozier who insisted on Ross."
**Barry Gordy - Initial Encouragement and Deferral to The Primettes** : Barry Gordy, head of Motown, auditioned The Primettes and, while encouraging, advised them to finish school before pursuing a recording career, an instruction that other Motown producers chose to circumvent. "Barry Gordy, who was encouraging, but told them to come back after they had finished school."
**Barry Gordy - Contribution to Early Supremes Session** : Barry Gordy was called into a session by Brian Holland and Freddie Gohrmann to hear The Primettes' harmonies on a new song. He then contributed by "tinkering" with the song to earn a credit and assisted in producing the session, marking his early direct production involvement with the group. "Gordy, who tinkered with the song to get his name on the credits, and then helped produce the session."
**Barry Gordy - Persistent Belief in Diana Ross's Star Potential** : Despite The Supremes' prolonged lack of chart success and numerous failed attempts by various producers, Barry Gordy maintained a strong belief in Diana Ross's individual star potential, which ultimately led to the group being paired with Holland-Dozier-Holland. "because of Gordy's belief in Ross's star potential."
**Smokey Robinson - Facilitator for The Primettes' Motown Audition** : Smokey Robinson, already recording for Motown, played a crucial role in The Primettes' early career by arranging an audition for them after Diana Ross, a former acquaintance with an adolescent crush on him, contacted him. He offered to introduce them to Barry Gordy if he found their audition satisfactory. "Knowing that Robinson was now recording from Motown, she got in touch with him, and he made a suggestion. Her group should audition for him, and if he thought they were good enough, he'd get them an appointment with Barry Gordy."
**Milton Jenkins - Manager of The Primes and Creator of The Primettes** : Milton Jenkins served as the manager for The Primes and was inspired by The Ray Lettes to create a female backing group, The Primettes, for his male vocalists. "their manager Milton Jenkins was inspired by Rachel's backing vocalists, The Ray Lettes."
**Richard Marvis - Primettes' Manager and Session Work Facilitator** : Richard Marvis, a Motown producer, became The Primettes' manager after Barry Gordy initially deferred signing them. He secured them session work on other labels, including early recordings for artists like Wilson Pickett. "Marvis became their major, and they started getting session work on early recordings by future soul legends, like Wilson Pickett."
**Richard Marvis - Imprisonment for Parole Violation** : Shortly after Florence Ballard's traumatic experience, Richard Marvis, who had been The Primettes' manager, was arrested for a parole violation and subsequently imprisoned. "Richard Marvis was arrested for a parole violation, and found himself in prison."
**Robert Bateman - Collaboration on Primettes' Session Work** : Robert Bateman, a Motown producer, collaborated with Richard Marvis to secure session work for The Primettes on other labels, bypassing Barry Gordy's initial decision to have the group finish school first. "two other producers at Motown, Richard, Marvis and Robert Bateman, decided they weren't going to wait around."
**Wilson Pickett - Early Recording Collaboration with The Primettes** : The Primettes provided session work, including backing vocals, on early recordings for future soul legend Wilson Pickett, demonstrating their early industry presence before achieving mainstream success. "early recordings by future soul legends, like Wilson Pickett."
**Janie Bradford - Involvement in The Supremes' Name Selection** : Janie Bradford is mentioned in the context of the contentious process of selecting The Supremes' new name, specifically as a potential creator of the short list from which Florence Ballard ultimately chose the moniker. "whether it was created by Janie Bradford."
**Dick Clark - Promotional Support for "Where Did Our Love Go"** : To give "Where Did Our Love Go" a "huge push" during a Motown slump, Barry Gordy persuaded Dick Clark to include The Supremes on his "Caravan of Stars" tour, a crucial step in promoting the single. "Gordy decided that they were going to give Where did I Love Go, a huge push, and persuaded Dick Clark to put the Supremes on his camera van of stars tour."
**Phil Spector - Influence on Holland-Dozier-Holland** : Phil Spector's "wall of sound" production technique was a direct influence on Lamont Dozier when he developed the idea for The Supremes' "When the Love Light Starts Shining Through His Eyes," and Holland-Dozier-Holland later produced "Phil Specter knockoff" "Run Run Run." "it's entirely plausible though that he was thinking of Specter, and the song definitely has a wall of sound feel."
**Brian Wilson - Questionable Early Influence on Holland-Dozier-Holland** : While Lamont Dozier claimed to be thinking of Brian Wilson when creating "When the Love Light Starts Shining Through His Eyes" in 1963, this is deemed unlikely, as Wilson was primarily associated with "surf rock records" at that time, not the "symphonic pop" he would later specialize in. However, Holland-Dozier-Holland did write "Surfer Boy" for The Supremes in 1965, suggesting later attention to his work. "unlikely he was actually thinking of Wilson, who at this point in 1963 was still making rather garrison surf rock records, rather than the symphonic pop he would start to specialise in the next year."
**Eddie Holland - Producer Role in "Where Did Our Love Go"** : Eddie Holland was involved in the production of "Where Did Our Love Go," coaching Diana Ross on her vocal phrasing, insisting she "sing it straight," and asserting the producers' authority when Ross threatened to call Barry Gordy. "Eddie Holland kept coaching her on how he wanted the song sung. She was playing with the phrasing, and Holland insisted she sing it straight."
**Lamont Dozier - Backing Vocals for "Where Did Our Love Go"** : Lamont Dozier devised the initial, difficult backing vocal parts for "Where Did Our Love Go." When the Supremes struggled, a frustrated Dozier instructed them to simply sing "baby baby," creating the memorable vocal hook. "Le Mans Dozier had come up with were difficult...frustrated Dozier told them to just sing baby baby when he pointed, and they went with that."
**Mary Wells - Departure from Motown** : Mary Wells, described as one of Motown's "biggest stars," departed the label for another, contributing to a slump period for Motown just before The Supremes' breakthrough with "Where Did Our Love Go." "they'd just lost Mary Wells one of their biggest stars to another label."
