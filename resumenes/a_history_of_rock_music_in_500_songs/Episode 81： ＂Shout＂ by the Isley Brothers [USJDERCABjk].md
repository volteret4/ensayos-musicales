## albums
**Shout (1959)** : This album, also titled "Shout," was recorded following the success of their single, but marked a return to the "old Hugo and Luigi style." Apart from one new original, "Respectable," which became their next single, the rest of the album consisted of old standards rearranged in the "Shout" style. While sometimes this approach worked, such as on "Wring-a-Ling, Let the Wedding Bells Ring" where Ronald Isley's screaming suited the Little Richard-style lyrics, it was largely unsuccessful on other tracks. Their interpretations of songs like Irving Berlin's "How Deep Is The Ocean" or "He's Got The Whole World In His Hands" did not improve either the songs or the group. "Unfortunately for the album, also titled Shout, the old Hugo and the Ouija style came out, and apart from one new Asli's original, respectable, which became their next single, the rest of the album was made up of old standards, rearranged in the Shout style."

## songs
**Angels Crate (1957)** : This was the first single released by the Isley Brothers on Teenage Records in June 1957. Unfortunately, the single did not achieve any real success, prompting the group to seek a better label. "in June 1957, the first I's Li-Fothers single, Angels Crate, came out."
**Don't Be Jellis** : This was the Isley Brothers' first record produced by Richie Barrett for Gone Records. It was a fairly standard doo-wop ballad written by Ronald Isley. There is some suggestion that Richie Barrett himself also sang on this recording, as it sounds like four voices rather than just three. The song did not strongly showcase the distinctive style that the Isley Brothers would later make their own. "The Isleys' first record with Barrett, Don't Be Jellis, was a fairly standard Do-Walk Ballard, written by Rona Isley."
**I Want to Know** : This single came closer to the Isley Brothers' future sound, showing clear attempts to incorporate elements from other successful records. Ronald Isley's vocals on the track owe a lot to Little Richard, while the piano playing features the "piano ripping" style made famous by Jerry Lee Lewis. Although it hinted at their future success, the song did not achieve significant sales. "The next single, I want to know, came closer."
**Shout (1959)** : Originating from extended, improvised call-and-response endings to their live performances of Jackie Wilson's "Lonely Teardrops," this song gradually developed with elements from Roquel's records like "What Did I Say" and "I Got A Woman." Producers Hugo and Luigi suggested cutting out the "Lonely Teardrops" part and recording just the extended tag. The resulting song, split into "Shout Parts One and Two" across a single, became the Isley Brothers' first chart hit, reaching number 47. Credited to the three brothers, it generated significant income from numerous successful cover versions, and sold over a million copies despite not breaking the Top 40. "That was nothing like anything that Hugo and Luigi had ever produced before, and it became the Isle Brothers' first chart hit, reaching number 47."
**Respectable** : This was the only new Isley Brothers original featured on their 1959 album "Shout" and subsequently released as their next single. "apart from one new Asli's original, respectable, which became their next single"
**Teach Me How To Shimmy** : This gospel-infused soul party song was one of the A-sides recorded by The Isley Brothers with producers Lieber and Stoller at Atlantic Records. However, it did not achieve chart success. "Like the Lieber and Stuller song, teach me how to shimmy"
**Standing On The Dance Floor** : This original song by The Isley Brothers was recorded with producers Lieber and Stoller at Atlantic Records as an A-side. It was a re-imagining of an old gospel song titled "Standing at the Judgment." Despite its gospel-infused soul party style, it failed to chart. "and the Asli's own, standing on the dance floor, a re-veg to have an old gospel song called Standing at the Judgment."

## curiosities
**Early Touring and Modern Relevance** : The Isley Brothers are noted for their exceptional touring longevity, remaining active today while many contemporaries perform only locally. Even though the current touring lineup differs from the 1950s group, lead singer Ronald Isley continues to perform and has maintained artistic relevance through collaborations with prominent hip-hop artists. "Of course, the version of the Asli brothers touring today isn't quite the same as the group from the 1950s, but Ronald Asli, the group's lead singer, remains in the group, and indeed has remained artistically relevant, with collaborations with several prominent hip-hop artists."
**Multi-Decade Chart Success** : The Isley Brothers achieved top 40 hits across five decades (60s, 70s, 80s, 90s, and 2000s). As recently as 2006, they had an album reach number one on the R&B charts. "The Asli's had top 40 hits in the 60s, 70s, 80s, 90s and 2000s, and as recently as 2006, they had an album go to number one on the R&B charts."
**Vaudeville Family Legacy** : The Isley Brothers were born into a musical family, with their father, O'Cally Isley Sr., a former vaudeville performer, explicitly stating his intention to have four sons who would become "the next Mill's brothers" even before his marriage. The boys' entry into music was largely driven by their parents' aspirations. "When O'Cally Iseley Sr. was discussing his marriage proposal with his future in laws, he told his father-in-law to be that he intended to have four sons, and that they were going to be the next mill's brothers."
**Original Lineup and Parental Training** : O'Cally and Sally Isley had six sons, the eldest four of whom (O'Cally Jr., Rudolph, Ronald, and Vernon) formed a vocal group. Like many black vocal groups of the early 50s, they began as a gospel group, performing around the church as teenagers under their parents' training. "O'Cally, Jr., Rudolph, Ronald and Vernon Iseley started performing around the church as Incincenti as teenagers, having been trained by their parents."
**Ted Max Amateur Hour Win** : The Isley Brothers appeared on "Ted Max Amateur Hour," a popular talent show responsible for launching many entertainers' careers, and won. Their prize was a "dueled watch" that the boys shared, taking turns wearing it. "They appeared on Ted Max Amateur Hour, the popularity-v-talent show which launched the careers of many entertainers, and won."
**Tragic Death of Vernon Isley** : Vernon Isley, the youngest of the four singing brothers and widely considered the most talented singer in the group, was tragically killed at the age of 13 after being hit by a car while riding his bike. This event devastated the brothers and led them to believe their singing career was over, resulting in a two-year hiatus from live performance. "Vernon, the youngest of the four singing Iseley's, and the one who was generally considered to be far and away the most talented singer in the group, was hit by a car and killed while he was riding his bike, aged only 13."
**Shift from Gospel to Doo-Wop** : Following Vernon's death, their parents pressured them to continue singing to honor him. A compromise was reached where Ronald agreed to attempt lead vocals if the group could transition from gospel music to doo-wop, influenced by their favorite act, Billy Ward and The Dominoes. "Ron agreed that he would attempt to sing lead. If in turn, the group could stop singing gospel music and start singing duwop songs, like the Brothers' favourite act, Billy Ward and the Dominoes."
**Ronald Isley's Vocal Comparisons and Mentorship** : Once Ronald Isley began singing lead, he was immediately compared to Clyde McPhatter and Jackie Wilson. Ronald himself cited McPhatter as a mentor who taught the brothers how to sing, though the exact timing of this connection remains unclear. "As soon as Ronaldizely started singing lead, people started making comparisons both to McFatter and to Wilson."
**Robbery in New York** : Upon arriving in New York to pursue their musical fortunes, the three eldest Isley Brothers (O'Kelly, Rudolph, and Ronald) were robbed on their second day in the city, losing all their money despite having paid for accommodation in advance. "When they got to New York, while they had paid in advance for somewhere to stay, they were robbed on their second day in the city and had no money at all."
**Financial Success in Live Performances** : After impressing agent Nat Nazarro, the Isley Brothers' bookings dramatically improved. They went from having no money to playing on Broadway for $750 a week, and later at the Apollo for $950 a week, an astonishing sum for teenagers in the late 1950s, according to O'Kelly Isley Jr.'s recollection. "They went from having no money at all to playing on Broadway for $750 a week and then playing the Apollo for $950 a week, at least according to Oak Kelly as the junior's later recollection."
**Early Live Repertoire of Cover Versions** : Before making their mark with original recordings, the Isley Brothers' live sets largely consisted of cover versions of other artists' songs, such as "Rock and Roll Waltz" by K-Star, which was quite different from the material they would later become famous for. "their sets were based on cover versions of songs by other people, things like Rock and Roll Waltz by K-Star."
**Contract Swiping from Teenage Records** : After their unsuccessful first single, the Isley Brothers wanted to leave Teenage Records, which claimed to hold them under contract. O'Kelly Isley revealed they had signed a contract, but it was left unfiled on a desk, so they "swiped it" when no one was looking, leaving Teenage Records with no proof of the agreement and freeing the brothers to sign with Gone Records. "But then the contract had just been left on a desk after they'd signed it, rather than being filed, and they'd swiped it from the desk when no one was looking."
**Lack of Success on Gone Records** : The Isley Brothers released several singles on Gone Records, including "Don't Be Jellis" and "I Want to Know," often pairing a new B-side with a previously released track. However, none of these releases achieved sufficient success to warrant their continued stay with George Goldner's label. "A few more singles were released on Gone, often pairing a previously released track with a new B side, but nothing was successful enough to justify them staying on with Goldner's label."
**Creative Differences with Hugo and Luigi on RCA** : The Isley Brothers' first single with RCA producers Hugo and Luigi, "I'm Gonna Knock on Your Door," highlighted potential creative differences. Despite powerful vocals and a forward-looking, "groundbreaking" sound that resembled early 60s pop, the song's lightweight backing and generic writing (by Elvis film songwriters) meant it didn't quite convince as either R&B or pop. "The vocals have some power to them, and the record is pleasant, and in some ways even groundbreaking."
**Live Act Reputation and Cover Songs** : At the time of their RCA recordings, the Isley Brothers were more successful as a live act than a recording act, primarily performing cover versions of other artists' songs. A regular live staple was "Lonely Teardrops," written by Berry Gordy, Gwen Gordy, and Roquel Davis for Jackie Wilson. "At this point the Ayersley's were a more successful live act than recording act, and they would mostly perform songs by other people."
**Origin of "Shout" from Live Improvisation** : The hit song "Shout" originated from increasingly long and excited improvised extended endings to their live performances of Jackie Wilson's "Lonely Teardrops." Ronald Isley would engage in call-and-response with his brothers, shouting lines and getting them to sing the response. These live tags eventually incorporated elements from Roquel's records, particularly "What Did I Say" and "I Got A Woman." "These improvised extended endings to the song got longer and longer, and got the crowds more and more excited, and they started incorporating elements from Raquel's records, especially what did I say and I got a woman."
**Shout's Impact and Cover Versions** : "Shout" was credited to the three Isley Brothers, ensuring they profited from its widespread success, particularly from numerous cover versions that charted higher than their original. Joey Dee and The Starliters reached number six in the USA in 1962, Lulu and The Lovers hit number seven in the UK in 1964, and Johnny O'Keefe's version reached number two in Australia just a month after the Isleys' release. Despite these covers, the Isleys' version sold over a million copies and remains definitive. "More importantly for them, the song was credited to the three brothers, so they made money from the cover versions of the song that charted much higher."
**Further RCA Singles' Lack of Success** : Following the success of "Shout," the Isley Brothers released several more singles on RCA, but none managed to repeat its chart performance. "They released several more singles on RCA, but none of them repeated the success of Shout."
**"One-Hit Wonder" Perception** : After a year with Atlantic Records where their gospel-infused soul party songs produced by Lieber and Stoller failed to chart, the Isley Brothers left Atlantic for the tiny Scepter label. This marked a downward trajectory in label size, and despite remaining a strong live act, they appeared to be a "one-hit wonder" after "Shout." "They were still a great live act, but they appeared to be a one-hit wonder."
**Teaser for "Twist and Shout"** : The narrative concludes by hinting that the Isley Brothers were on the verge of making "one of the most important records of the 1960s" with a cover version of a flop single, inspired by their hit "Shout" and combined with a dance craze, referring to "Twist and Shout." "The Asli brothers were about to make one of the most important records of the 1960s, but Twiston Shout is a story for another time."

# artist - Buella Bryant

## genres

## curiosities
**Real Mentor to The Isley Brothers** : Buella Bryant was a less well-known professional blues shouter who served as the "real mentor" for the three eldest Isley Brothers (O'Kelly, Rudolph, and Ronald). They met her while traveling from Boston to New York. Bryant had a moderately successful career from the 1950s to the 1980s, primarily through live performances, and made a handful of "very listenable records." She used her extensive contacts in the music industry to help the young Isley Brothers secure bookings when they were robbed and stranded in New York. "But their real mentor was a much less well-known singer, Buella Bryant."

## curiosities
**Children's Records to Pop Producers** : Hugo Peretti and Luigi Criatore, known as Hugo and Luigi, began their careers making children's records. They were later hired by Mercury Records, marking their entry into popular music production. "had started out making children's records, before being hired by Mercury Records"
**Ethically and Musically Appalling Covers** : At Mercury Records, Hugo and Luigi were responsible for producing Georgia Gibbs' cover versions of "Black Records," which were criticized as being "both ethically and musically appalling" for their appropriation and often inferior quality. "The cover versions by Georgia Gibbs of Black Records that we've talked about previously, and which were both ethically and musically appalling."
**Founders and Stake in Roulette Records** : Hugo and Luigi were instrumental in the founding of Roulette Records alongside Morris Levy, George Goldner, and Alan Freed. While Goldner and Freed quickly exited, Hugo and Luigi maintained a significant 50% stake in the new label. "Morris Levy, who was setting up a new label, Roulette, with George Goldner and Alan Freed."
**Lack of Understanding for Rock and Roll** : Despite their success, Hugo and Luigi were perceived as not truly understanding rock and roll music. They frequently produced follow-up singles for acts who had previous hits before working with them, only for those acts to stop having hits once the duo took over production. "While they were there, they showed they didn't really get rock and roll music at all."
**Unfair Departure from Roulette** : After approximately a year with Roulette, Hugo and Luigi were poached by RCA. Morris Levy, the label's owner, permitted their departure only on the condition that they surrendered their 50% shares in Roulette for "far less than they were worth," demonstrating an unfair business practice. "Morris Levy let them go, so long as they gave up their shares in Roulette, for far less than they were worth."
**Success with "Poppy Background and Soulful Vocals"** : Although their production style didn't immediately work for The Isley Brothers with "I'm Gonna Knock on Your Door," their combination of a "poppy background and soulful vocals" would later prove very fruitful for another artist they would produce. "That combination of a poppy background and soulful vocals would soon bear a lot of fruit for another artist Hugo and Luigi were going to start working with, but it didn't quite work for the Ayersley's yet."

# label - Gone Records

## owner

## producer

## curiosities
**Family of Labels** : Gone Records was one of a family of labels owned and run by George Goldner. Goldner assigned Richie Barrett as talent scout, producer, and arranger for the Isley Brothers, a role Barrett previously held for acts like Frankie Lymon and The Teenagers, The Chantels, and Goldner's own group, The Valentines. "They chose to sign to Gun Records, one of the family of labels that was owned and run by George Goldner."
**Richie Barrett's "Assembly Line" Production** : Richie Barrett, the producer assigned to the Isley Brothers at Gone Records, was known for his established "assembly line method" of making records. This involved booking a studio for up to 24 hours with backing musicians, and recording dozens of tracks with as many as ten different vocal groups in a row. "He would block Booker Studio and some backing musicians for up to 24 hours, get as many as 10 different vocal groups into the studio, and record dozens of tracks in a row, usually songs written by either group members or by Barrett."

# concert - Harlem Apollo

## curiosities
**Early Performances and Later Success** : In their early days in New York, the Isley Brothers frequently "hung around" the Harlem Apollo and occasionally performed at its amateur night. Later, after securing agent Nat Nazarro, they returned to play the Apollo for $950 a week, an astonishing sum for teenagers in the late 1950s. "At first she was unsuccessful and the group just hung around the Harlem Apollo and occasionally performed at their amateur night."

# curiosity

**O'Cally Iseley Sr.'s Vaudeville Background and Family Vision** : O'Cally Iseley Sr. was a vaudeville performer who envisioned his future sons forming a vocal group to become "the next Mill's Brothers" before they were even born. The Isley children went into music, seemingly more to please their parents than for their own desire. "Iseley Sr. had been a voidville performer himself, and as with so many family groups, the Iseley's seemed to have gone into the music business more to please their parents than because they wanted to do it themselves."
**Parental Pressure and Musical Compromise** : After Vernon's death, the Isley parents pressured the remaining brothers to continue singing as a way to honor Vernon, leading to a compromise where Ronald Isley would sing lead if the group could switch from gospel to doo-wop, influenced by Billy Ward and The Dominoes. "Eventually, a compromise was reached between parents and brothers. Ron agreed that he would attempt to sing lead. If in turn, the group could stop singing gospel music and start singing duwop songs, like the Brothers' favourite act, Billy Ward and the Dominoes."
**Buella Bryant - The "Real" Mentor** : Despite Ronald Isley acknowledging Clyde McPhatter as a mentor, the brothers' true mentor was the lesser-known blues singer Buella Bryant, whom they met traveling to New York. Bryant, a professional blues shouter with a moderately successful live career from the 50s to 80s, used her industry contacts to secure bookings for the Isley Brothers when they were robbed and stranded in New York. "But their real mentor was a much less well-known singer, Buella Bryant."
**Nat Nazarro - The "Monster Agent"** : Nat Nazarro was an important New York booking agent known as the "Monster Agent" for his unethical practice of writing himself as a fourth member on contracts for three-person acts, taking an equal share of performance money in addition to his regular agent's fee. He was initially skeptical of the Isley Brothers' talent over the phone, believing they were playing a record, but was highly impressed in person and secured them lucrative bookings on Broadway and at the Apollo. "Ezaro was known as the Monster Agent."
**Ben Smith - Co-owner of Teenage Records** : Ben Smith was a co-owner of Teenage Records, the Isley Brothers' first label. While there was some historical confusion about his identity (singer vs. sax player), he was likely the sax player who performed on some Teenage records, played alto sax for Lucky Millinder's band, and wrote Glenn Miller's hit "I Dreamed I Dwelt in Harlem." "As Ben Smith the sax player seems to have played on some records for Teenage, it was probably him."
**Bill Bass Gordon - Co-owner of Teenage Records** : Bill Bass Gordon was a co-owner of Teenage Records and was also the leader of the doo-wop group Bill Bass Gordon and The Colonials, who had recorded the track "Two Loves Have I." "It's more certain exactly who Bill, Bass, Gordon was. He was the leader of Bill, Bass, Gordon, and the Colonials, who had recorded the Dewupt track, Two Loves Have I."
**K-Star - Early Live Repertoire** : K-Star was an artist whose song "Rock and Roll Waltz" was a cover version performed by the Isley Brothers early in their live sets, before they achieved fame with their own material. "their sets were based on cover versions of songs by other people, things like Rock and Roll Waltz by K-Star."
**Aaron Schroeder and Sid Wayne - Songwriters for Elvis** : Aaron Schroeder and Sid Wayne were the writers of "I'm Gonna Knock on Your Door," the Isley Brothers' first single for RCA. They were best known for composing some of the "less interesting songs" for Elvis Presley's films. "I'm going to knock on your door, was written by Air and Shrouda and Sid Wayne, two people who are best known for writing some of the less interesting songs for Elvis' films"
**Berry Gordy, Gwen Gordy, and Roquel Davis - Songwriters of Lonely Teardrops** : This trio wrote the song "Lonely Teardrops" for Jackie Wilson. This song was a regular live performance staple for the Isley Brothers, and its extended endings eventually evolved into their hit song "Shout." "Lonely Teardrops, the song that Barry and Gwen Gordy and Raquel Davis had written for Jackie Wilson."
**Roquel - Influence on "Shout"** : Roquel's records, specifically "What Did I Say" and "I Got A Woman," were incorporated into the improvised, extended endings of the Isley Brothers' live performances of "Lonely Teardrops," which ultimately led to the creation of "Shout." "they started incorporating elements from Raquel's records, especially what did I say and I got a woman."
**Lieber and Stoller - Producers for Atlantic** : Jerry Leiber and Mike Stoller produced for the Isley Brothers at Atlantic Records. They opted for gospel-infused soul party songs for the A-sides and old standards for the B-sides, but none of these tracks achieved chart success. "At this point they moved across to Atlantic, where they started working with Lieber and Stuller."
