**Rhapsody in Blue (Performance)** : Paul Whiteman, a violinist and conductor, was known as the King of Jazz for leading a 35-musician, all-white band that played from three written charts, offering a polite and clean version of jazz. ## curiosities

His band played from written charts, essentially cleaning up jazz and making it more palatable for polite society. "Whiteman was a violin player and conductor, and he became known as the King of Jazz for being the band leader of an all-white band of musicians."

The band members were on a plane from LA to Atlantic City when Hampton started humming the riff. Goodman and Hampton were credited as writers, although John Hammond later claimed Charlie Christian improvised the riff first. "The first song with the new Goodman sex-tat recorded, on October 2nd 1939, 79 years ago this week, was a piece called Flying Home."

## curiosities
**King of Swing Title** : Benny Goodman was widely known as the "King of Swing" and led the most popular of the three war swing bands. He was also an excellent clarinet player, blending his instrumental prowess with his leadership role. "Benny Goodman, the King of Swing, was the leader of the most popular of the three war swing bands, as well as being an excellent Clameneck player."
**Pioneer of Integrated Bands** : Goodman was one of the first band leaders to lead an integrated band during the segregation era, starting with a trio comprising himself (white and Jewish), drummer Dramagine Cooper (white), and pianist Teddy Wilson (black). This initiative, along with recruiting black arranger Fletcher Henderson, was largely spearheaded by his brother-in-law, John Hammond, a wealthy man dedicated to racial integration. "And importantly, Goodman was one of the first band leaders to lead an integrated band during the segregation era."
**Classical Music Pioneer** : Goodman was one of the first individuals to advocate for the mainstream acceptance of jazz music within the classical music world. This effort, aimed at integrating jazz into a conservative environment, co-existed with his equally radical push for the integration of musicians in his bands. "On the one hand, he was one of the first people to push for mainstream acceptance of jazz music in the classical music world, which would suggest trying to be as conservative as possible."
**Progressive Stance on Integration** : Goodman’s inclusion of black musicians, notably Teddy Wilson when Goodman was arguably the most popular musician in the USA, was a genuinely progressive statement for his time. Colonel Hampton later quoted Goodman as saying, "we need both the Black keys and the White keys to play music," an assertion made 80 years ago that predates similar well-meaning liberal comparisons. "So Goodman saying it 80 years ago, there's a genuinely progressive statement for the time."
**Carnegie Hall Integrated Performance (1938)** : By 1938, Goodman achieved a significant milestone by bringing an integrated band, which included black musicians like Count Basie, Leste Jung, Teddy Wilson, and Lionel Hampton, alongside white musicians such as himself and Crouper, onto the prestigious stage of Carnegie Hall, the US's most renowned music venue at the time. "By 1938, Goodman actually reached the point where he was able to bring an integrated band, featuring Count Basie, Leste Jung, Teddy Wilson and Lionel Hampton. Plus other black musicians, along with white musicians such as Goodman and Crouper, onto the stage of Carnegie Hall, at the time the US's most prestigious music venue."
**Sextet Formation (1939)** : After Teddy Wilson and Dramagine Cooper departed in 1939, Goodman formed the Benny Goodman Sextet to compensate for the loss of these exceptional musicians. This rotating lineup revolved around three primary soloists: Goodman on clarinet, Hampton on vibraphone, and a new, highly influential addition, guitarist Charlie Christian. "But he also realised the need to make up for the loss of two such exceptional musicians by incorporating more, and so the Benny Goodman's sex sets were formed."
**Small Group Head Arrangements** : While Benny Goodman claimed responsibility for the head arrangements used by his small groups, many people believe that Charlie Christian was actually responsible for them. The sextet's music notably possessed a much more exhilarating feel compared to Goodman's earlier quartet or trio work. "While Goodman claimed responsibility for the head arrangements the small groups used, a lot of people think the Christian was responsible for these too, and certainly the sex-tats music has a much more exhilarating feel than the early quartet or trio work."
**Flying Home as Prototypical Rock** : The Benny Goodman Sextet's 1939 recording of "Flying Home" is described as almost a prototypical rock band, featuring piano, electric guitar, double bass, and drums—instruments that would become hallmarks of the rock genre 20 years later. It anticipated many aspects of rock, especially Charlie Christian's soloing, which was astonishingly advanced for its time. "Other than the vibraphone in clarinet, though, this small group was almost a prototypical rock band."
**Unison Riff Innovation on Flying Home** : The main riff of "Flying Home" features a profound clarinet and an electric guitar playing the same riff in unison, a sound that had never been recorded before its 1939 release. This unusual combination created a unique harmonic tension from the outset. "This is a very profound, acclarenet, and an electric guitar, all playing the same riff in unison. That's a sound that had never been recorded before."

## songs
**Memories of You (1930)** : Lionel Hampton is believed to have made the first recorded jazz appearance of the vibraphone on this Louis Armstrong recording. Prior to this, the vibraphone was only used as a novelty instrument, often for radio intermission signals. "He appears to have been the first person to use the vibraphone on Air Jazz record, on a recording by Louis Armstrong of the Song Memories of You from 1930."
**Flying Home (1939)** : Hampton co-wrote this signature song of the Benny Goodman Sextet after humming the riff on a flight from LA to Atlantic City. After leaving Goodman's band a couple of years later, "Flying Home" became the signature song for his own big band, and he recorded it multiple times, significantly influencing the nascent Jump band style. "Goodman asked, what's that you're singing? And Hampton said, I don't know, we can call it Flying Home I guess."
**Flying Home (1942) - Decker Version** : Lionel Hampton recorded his most famous version of "Flying Home" for Decker in 1942, featuring Illinois Jacket on saxophone, labeled as an instrumental "Fox Strott." This recording introduced a whole new sound to listeners, particularly Jacket's distinctive "hunk and scrunk" tenor sax playing, which defined the emerging genre of rhythm and blues. "but the most famous example is the version he recorded in 1942 for Decker, with instrumental Fox Strott on the label."

## curiosities
**Vibraphone Pioneer** : Lionel Hampton was a virtuoso vibraphone player who "more or less defined how that instrument was incorporated into jazz," and appears to be the first person to use it on a recorded jazz track (Louis Armstrong's "Memories of You" in 1930). At the time, the vibraphone was so new its name wasn't settled, and Hampton played an instrument branded "Vibrahap," which he called it for life. "He was a virtuoso vibraphoneist who more or less defined how that instrument was incorporated into jazz."
**Trained as Drummer** : Before becoming a vibraphone player, Hampton trained as a drummer and was often billed as "the fastest drummer in the world." His unique melodic sensibility allowed him to become the premier soloist on the new instrument, the vibraphone. "Hampton had trained us at rumour before becoming a vibraphone player and was often billed as the fastest drummer in the world."
**Most Respected Vibraphone Musician** : To this day, Lionel Hampton is considered probably the most respected musician ever to play the vibes. His profound contributions significantly shaped the instrument's role and reputation in jazz. "Indeed, to this day, Hampton is probably the most respected musician ever to play the vibes."
**Paved Way for Jackie Robinson** : Lionel Hampton often claimed that Benny Goodman's decision to hire him and Teddy Wilson, and later other black musicians, directly "paved the way for Robinson's more well-known achievement" of breaking the color barrier in baseball, which occurred nine years later. "In fact, Lionel Hampton would often later claim that it was Goodman hiring him and Wilson and later other black musicians that paved the way for Robinson's more well-known achievement."
**Formed Own Big Band** : A couple of years after the 1939 "Flying Home" recording, Lionel Hampton left Benny Goodman's band to form his own big band. This band became a pioneer in "Jump band music," which was rooted in swing but emphasized riffs and amplified instruments, eventually evolving into rhythm and blues. "A couple of years later, Lionel Hampton left Goodman's band to form his own big band and flying home became their signature song."
**Rock Music Pioneer Recognition** : In the late 1940s and early 1950s, as big bands lost popularity, Hampton became a great B-bop musician, but he was also recognized by rock musicians as a pioneer. He appeared in the 1957 Alan Fried film "Mr. Rock and Roll" as the only jazz musician alongside rock and roll stars like Little Richard and Clyde McFatter. "But he was also recognised by the rock musicians as a pioneer."

# artist - Charlie Christian

## member of
Benny Goodman Sextet

## genres
Jazz
Bop
Rockabilly (influence)

## instruments
Electric guitar

## songs
**Flying Home (1939)** : Christian's solo on the Benny Goodman Sextet's "Flying Home" was astonishingly advanced for its time, both in technique and sound. His short bursts of single-note guitar lines are considered early rockabilly, anticipating the playing of Scotty Moore 16 years later, and were revolutionary given the guitar's previous role as a rhythm instrument. "Christian's short bursts of single note guitar line are, to all intents and purposes, rockabilly."

## curiosities
**Electric Guitar Pioneer** : Charlie Christian was a pioneer of the electric guitar, significantly elevating its prominence as a solo instrument. While he was not the first electric guitarist, nor the first to record it, his innovative playing fundamentally changed the approach to guitar in popular music by allowing it to play loud, single melody lines on par with horns. "Christian is sometimes erroneously called the first electric guitarist, or the first person to play electric guitar on record, or even the inventor of the electric guitar. He was none of those things, but he was a pioneer in the instrument, and the first person to really bring it to prominence as a solo instrument."
**Influence on Bop** : Christian was a huge influence on Bop music, which would emerge many years after his death. His advanced technique and unique amplified sound were foundational to the genre's development. "He was a huge influence on Bop, which wouldn't come along for many more years, and in just the sound of it."
**Early Rockabilly Style** : Christian's soloing on "Flying Home," characterized by short bursts of single-note guitar lines, is effectively rockabilly and is compared to the playing of Scotty Moore 16 years later. This was revolutionary at a time when the guitar was predominantly a rhythm instrument in jazz. "Christian's short bursts of single note guitar line are, to all intents and purposes, rockabilly."
**Influence on Chuck Berry** : Chuck Berry later identified Christian as one of the biggest influences on his guitar playing, though Berry mistakenly believed Christian played with Tommy Dorsey's band rather than Goodman's. This highlights Christian's profound, albeit sometimes misattributed, impact on early rock and roll guitarists. "Chuck Berry later said the Christian was one of the biggest influences on his guitar playing, though he wrongly said the Christian played with Tommy Dorses band, arrival to Goodman's."
**Coined "B-bop" Name** : Christian was responsible for the name "B-bop" being given to the form of music he helped create in jam sessions after his regular work. This demonstrates his deep involvement in the early development of a new jazz subgenre. "While Christian was responsible for their name B-bop, being given to the form of music, he helped create in jam sessions after his regular work."
**Early Death (1941)** : Charlie Christian suffered from tuberculosis as early as 1939 when "Flying Home" was recorded, and he tragically died on March 2, 1941, at the young age of 25. His grave remained unmarked for 53 years, and even a later memorial was placed incorrectly, underscoring his underappreciated legacy. "And on March 2, 1941, aged only 25, Charlie Christian died."

"Swing was a form of music that had its roots in 1920s jazz."

# genre - Jazz

## curiosities
**Dixieland Moral Panic (1920s)** : When Dixieland jazz first gained popularity in the early 1920s, polite society reacted with moral outrage, labeling it "evil, jungle music" that promoted "ludeness and intoxication" and incited violence. This reaction mirrored later moral panics concerning rock and roll and hip-hop. "It was going to be the end of the world. It was evil, jungle music. It was causing our children to engage in acts of ludeness and intoxication. It was inciting violence."
**Early Guitar Role** : Before electrification, the guitar in jazz primarily functioned either as a complement to a single vocalist or as a barely audible rhythm instrument, often drowned out by louder pianos and horns. This limitation prevented it from playing single-note lead lines effectively over other instruments. "Up to this point, the guitar had essentially only been a rhythm instrument in jazz, with a very small handful of exceptions like Django Rhynehart."

# instrument - Vibraphone

## curiosities
**Early Novelty Use** : Before 1930, the vibraphone was exclusively used as a novelty instrument, primarily for radio intermission signals, playing only "a couple of chimes." Lionel Hampton's use of it on Louis Armstrong's "Memories of You" in 1930 marked its first recorded appearance in jazz. "Before that, the vibraphone had only ever been used as a novelty instrument. It was most used for radio intermission signals playing a couple of chimes."
**Unsettled Name (1930s)** : The vibraphone was such a new instrument in the 1930s that its name was not yet settled. "Vibraphone" was just one of several trademarks used by different manufacturing companies; for example, Lionel Hampton played an instrument branded "Vibrahap" and called it that for the rest of his life. "In fact, the vibraphone was so new as an instrument that its name had never been settled."

# instrument - Electric guitar

## curiosities
**Revolutionary for Guitar Playing** : The advent of the electric guitar fundamentally transformed guitar playing, allowing it to move beyond its previous roles as a quiet accompaniment or rhythm instrument. Now, it could play single melody lines as loudly as trumpets or saxophones, establishing itself as a viable solo instrument in ensembles. "Now the guitar could play single melody lines as loudly as any trumpeter saxophone, and could be used as a solo instrument in an ensemble in the same way as those instruments."
**Early Amplification and Recording (1939)** : In 1939, when "Flying Home" was recorded, electronic amplification was a new concept, and only one electric guitar had ever been recorded before the Benny Goodman Sextet sessions. The "Flying Home" record was notable for featuring two separate electronically amplified instruments: Charlie Christian's guitar and Lionel Hampton's vibraphone. "That only one electric guitar had ever been recorded before the sex-tat sessions, and that the record contained two separate electronically amplified instruments, Christians guitar and Hampton's vibraphone."

# curiosity

**Early 20th Century Moral Panics over Black Music** : The moral panic surrounding Dixieland jazz in the early 1920s, which involved accusations of it being "evil, jungle music" inciting "ludeness and intoxication" and violence, was a precursor to similar societal reactions against rock and roll and hip-hop. This pattern reflects a recurring apprehension in polite society towards black musical forms. "It was, in short, everything that was later said about rock and roll, about hip-hop, and you get the idea."
**John Hammond's Philanthropy and Influence** : John Hammond, Benny Goodman's immensely privileged and wealthy brother-in-law (whose mother was a Vanderbilt), used his fortune to promote racial integration and "Roots of Emarycana music" (pre-bop jazz, folk, blues, gospel). As a DJ, music journalist, and record producer, he funded recordings and airplay that would have otherwise been inaccessible, significantly influencing musical integration and diverse genres. "Hammond was an immensely privileged and wealthy man, his mother was a Vanderbilt, who had decided to use his immense wealth in the service of two goals. The first of those was racial integration, and the second of them was to promote what would now be called Roots of Emarycana music, three-bop jazz, folk, blues, and gospel."
**Integrated Band Precursors (1920s)** : While Benny Goodman was a significant figure in leading integrated bands, he was not the absolute first white band leader in America to hire black musicians; there had been three such instances in the 1920s. However, when Goodman hired Teddy Wilson, no one had led an integrated group for seven years, making his actions uniquely impactful. "Goodman wasn't the first White band leader in America to hire Black musicians, there had been three in the 1920s, but when he hired Teddy Wilson, no one had led an integrated group for seven years."
**Jackie Robinson Comparison** : The integration achieved by Benny Goodman's band, particularly his performance at Carnegie Hall in 1938, was an extraordinary social statement. It predated Jackie Robinson breaking the color bar in baseball by nine years, highlighting the radical nature of Goodman's actions in a segregated America. "For US cultural context, it would be another nine years before Jackie Robinson was able to break the colour bar in baseball, to give some idea of how extraordinary this actually was."
