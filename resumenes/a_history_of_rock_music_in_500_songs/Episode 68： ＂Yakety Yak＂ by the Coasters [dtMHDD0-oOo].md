## albums
**One by One (Year Not Specified) - First Proper Album** : This was the group's first album recorded with the intention of being an LP, rather than a compilation of singles. It featured the group backed by an orchestra, singing old standards, with each song having a single lead vocalist and the others providing backing vocals. Carl Gardner took the lead on four songs, hoping it would be his chance to transition into a solo singing career, but the album was not commercially or critically successful. Cornell Gunter, who had always been relegated to backing roles, shone with his imitation of Dinah Washington's style on three solo lead songs, despite his sweeter voice not typically suiting the group's famous material. "The album wasn't a particular success, either commercially or critically, but to the extent that anyone noticed it at all, they mostly commented on how good Cornell Gunter sounded."

## songs
**Yakati Yak (Year Not Specified)** : This song featured a new style for the Coasters, with a unison lead vocal by Carl Gardner and Billy Guy, harmonized by Cornel Gunter, rather than the previous alternating single lead vocalist. The chorus had the group singing "Yakati Yak," with Dub Jones taking the single line "don't talk back." The formula of unison vocals and King Curtis's saxophone part became a staple for the group. Max Stoller was convinced it would be a massive success, but Jerry Lieber initially wanted a proven, safe song for the B-side. The B-side featured a version of the old song "Zing went the strings of my heart," performed as a duet by Dub Jones and Cornel Gunter. "Yakati Yak was, of course, a number one hit single."
**Zing went the strings of my heart (Year Not Specified)** : This old song was used as the B-side for "Yakati Yak," performed as a duet by Dub Jones and Cornel Gunter. Jerry Lieber opted for this "proven song" as a safe choice, in case "Yakati Yak" was a flop, despite Max Stoller's confidence in the A-side. "Lieber, though, thought they needed a proven song for the B side."
**The Shadow Knows (Year Not Specified)** : Released after "Yakati Yak," this was a blues song based on the "Radio Mystery Series" that starred Orson Welles. While Lieber and Stoller often drew inspiration from radio plays for their songs, this one did not translate to chart success. It was considered a more adult record and was largely ignored by the Coasters' white teenage audience, to the extent that several online discographies fail to mention its existence, and Lieber and Stoller skip over it in their autobiography. "It's a more adult record than Yakite Yak, and seems to have been completely ignored by the coasters' white teenage audience."
**Charlie Brown (Year Not Specified)** : This song was widely referred to as the follow-up to "Yakati Yak," although "The Shadow Knows" was released prior. It took significantly longer to write than "Yakati Yak," with Max Stoller quickly coming up with appropriate music, but Jerry Lieber agonizing for weeks before finding the title and basic lyric idea, which Stoller helped refine. The songwriters deliberately tried to replicate the sound of "Yakati Yak." The song reached number two on the pop charts, making it almost as big a hit as "Yakati Yak." "Charlie Brown was almost as big a hit as Yakite Yak, reaching number two on the pop charts."
**Along Came Jones (Year Not Specified)** : This was the third song in a similar style to "Yakati Yak" and "Charlie Brown," making fun of the plethora of Western TV series prevalent at the time. It reached number nine in the pop charts, which was still a success, but not as significant as its predecessors. Its performance may have been affected by The Olympics having a recent hit with a similar record, "Western Movies." "Still a big success, but nowhere near as big as Charlie Brown or Yakite Yak."
**I Am A Hogfew Baby (Year Not Specified)** : This song was intended as the next single, following the established formula of unison vocals and honking sax, but with a more bluesy feel and distinctly more adult lyrics. However, listeners instead gravitated towards its B-side, "Poison Ivy." "While the next single was meant to be a song called I Am A Hogfew Baby, which was another iteration of the same formula, although with the more bluesy feel, added a distinctly more adult tone to the lyrics."
**Poison Ivy (Year Not Specified)** : This B-side became the Coasters' biggest hit among black audiences, their fourth and final R&B number one, and their last Top 10 pop hit. The song is blatantly about sexually transmitted diseases, featuring a character named Poison Ivy and mentioning mumps, measles, and chickenpox before warning, "Poison Ivy will make you which, and you can look but you better not touch." "This one was a song called Poison Ivy, and it's frankly amazing that it was even released, given that it's blatantly about sexually transmitted diseases."
**What About Us (Year Not Specified)** : Released after "Poison Ivy," this song represented a shift towards more adult and socially commentative themes, in the older style Lieber and Stoller had used with The Robins. It was a protest song about the easy lives of rich (and by implication, white) people compared to the struggles of the singers. The song only reached number seventeen on the charts. "The next single, What About Us, which was a protest song about how rich and by implication white people had an easy life while the singers didn't have anything, only reached number seventeen."
**Bessermé Moucho (Year Not Specified)** : This was an old standard released as a single by The Coasters, extending over two sides, with the second side mostly comprising a King Curtis saxophone solo. It was part of a period where the group was "flailing about to try new styles" following their decline in pop chart success. The song only reached number seventy on the charts. "That only went to number seventy."
**Wake me Shake Me (Year Not Specified)** : This was the first single written by a member of The Coasters, specifically Billy Guy. It was backed by the old folk song "Stubol" and did not achieve significant chart success, reaching number fifty-one. "Then they released the first single written by a member of the group. Wake me Shake Me, which was written by Billy Guy."
**Stubol (Year Not Specified)** : An old folk song that was used as the B-side for The Coasters' single "Wake me Shake Me." "That was backed by the old folk song Stubol, and didn't do much better, reaching number fifty-one on the charts."
**Shopping for Clothes (Year Not Specified)** : This comedy blues song, originally titled "Close Line, Wrapped Up" by Kent Harris (performing as Bougaloo and his solid crew), became one of The Coasters' great classics despite poor chart performance. The Coasters' version was reconstructed from Billy Guy's vague memory of half the lyrics, and because the original writer was unknown, it was initially credited to the pseudonym Elmo Glick. The song was reworked significantly into a dialogue, with Billy Guy playing the shopper and Dub Jones playing the sales assistant. It reached only number 83 on the chart, and Kent Harris later sued, being awarded joint writing credit. "While it didn't chart, it is usually regarded as one of the coasters very best records."
**Little Egypt (Year Not Specified)** : This song about a stripper, written by Lieber and Stoller, marked the last time The Coasters would have a significant hit. The title "Little Egypt" was originally the stage name of famous belly dancers in the 1890s, one of whom performed in Chicago and introduced belly dancing to the American public, and another who performed in New York and was involved in a scandal for planning to perform nude. Lieber and Stoller may have been inspired by a highly fictionalized film about these dancers or by exotic dancers who continued to use variations of the name. "Their song about a stripper would be the last time the coasters would have a significant hit."

## curiosities
**Classic Lineup Formed (1957)** : The Coasters' classic lineup, featuring Cornel Gunter and Doug Jones, who replaced Leon Hughes and Bobby Nun, finally fell into place. This formation occurred after a year during which the group had not had a hit, as their writing and production team, Jerry Lieber and Mike Stoller, were focused on more lucrative work with artists like Elvis Presley. "The classic lineup of the coasters had finally fallen into place, but it had been a year since they had had a hit."
**Lieber & Stoller's Direction** : From the beginning of Lieber and Stoller's career, Jerry Lieber consistently directed the lead vocalist on how to sing his lines. This approach was applied to all artists they produced, including Elvis, Ruth Brown, and The Coasters, requiring them to follow Lieber's phrasing as closely as possible, a method that had previously annoyed Big Mama Thornton with "Hound Dog." "When Lieber and Stoller produced an artist, whether it was Elvis or Ruth Brown or the coasters or whoever, they would get them to follow Lieber's phrasing as closely as possible."
**Racist Stereotypes Criticism** : The Coasters' records faced criticism, notably from Johnny Otis and Charlie Gillett, for allegedly perpetuating racist stereotypes. Otis, who had personal issues with Lieber and Stoller regarding "Hound Dog" credits, specifically singled out "Charlie Brown." The group's performances, particularly Dub Jones' vocal parts, were clearly rooted in traditions of African American comedy that reappropriated stereotypes, but the controversy arose because the black performers were directed by two white writers, Lieber and Stoller, on how to perform these songs. "Johnny Otis in particular would make this criticism of the group's records, and it's one that must be taken seriously."
**Audience Segmentation** : The Coasters had two distinct audiences: an R&B audience, largely composed of older black people, and a white teenage audience. Different songs appealed more to each group, highlighting a distinction in how black performers' music was received when singing for a black audience versus a white one. "I think it makes a difference, as well, that the coasters had two different audiences. They had an R&B audience, who were mostly older black people, and they had a white teenage audience."
**Shift from Teenage Focus** : After "Poison Ivy," The Coasters' last Top 10 hit, Jerry Lieber and Mike Stoller seemed to grow bored of writing songs aimed at a teenage audience. Subsequently, most of the group's songs reverted to an older style, similar to that used with The Robins, focusing on social commentary and more adult topics. "Poison Ivy would be the group's last top ten hit, and it seemed to signal Lee Brunstoller getting bored with writing songs aimed at an audience of teenagers."
**Desperate Flailing for New Styles** : Following a decline in chart success and a shift away from teenage-oriented material, The Coasters entered a period described as "desperate flailing about to try new styles." This included releasing an old standard, "Bessermé Moucho," a single written by a group member, Billy Guy's "Wake me Shake Me," and attempting yet another style with "Shopping for Clothes." "There seems to have been a sort of desperate flailing about to try new styles."
**Manager Lester Sill Mentors Phil Spector** : Lester Sill, who was Lieber and Stoller's mentor in their early years and The Coasters' manager, introduced a young musician named Phil Spector to the songwriting and production duo. Spector, an admirer of Lieber and Stoller who had a hit with "To Know Him Is To Love Him" with his group The Teddy Bears, was seeking an apprenticeship. As a favor to Sill, Lieber and Stoller signed Spector to a publishing contract, gave him jobs as a session guitarist, and allowed him to sleep in their office or Lieber's spare room. "Lester Siller said that the kid in question was a huge admirer of Leibor and Stuller, and wanted to learn from them."
**Transition to Adult Market Attempt** : Around the release of "Shopping for Clothes," The Coasters became aware that their time as a pop chart act with a teenage fan base was ending. Carl Gardner, who had long wished to be a big band singer, pushed the group to attempt a transition into the adult white market, targeting audiences who bought records by artists like Tony Bennett or Andy Williams, a move encouraged by his friend Johnny Otis. "Around the time that shopping for clothes came out, the group became conscious that their time as a pop chart act with a teenage fan base was probably close to its end, and they decided to do something that Carl Gardner had wanted to do for a while, and tried to transition into the adult white market."
**Commercial Decline and Production Changes** : Following the attempt to transition to the adult market and the album "One by One" failing to garner success, The Coasters' commercial fortunes entered a terminal decline. During this period, Lieber and Stoller ceased to be the exclusive producers for the group; several recordings were instead credited to Sill and Hazelwood, though these efforts also failed to achieve significant commercial success. "By this time, the group's commercial fortunes seemed to be in terminal decline."
**Post-Hit Lineup Instability and Rival Groups** : After their last significant hit, "Little Egypt," Cornel Gunter left to sing with Dinah Washington, later forming his own "Coasters" group which, despite not being allowed to record under that name, toured extensively with the support of Dick Clark. Billy Guy also pursued a solo career, often sending a replacement on the road while still recording with the main group, and his solo records sometimes were reissued under The Coasters' name without other members' involvement. Over decades, nearly everyone involved with The Coasters started rival versions of the group, with Carl Gardner retaining legal rights to the name, but others performing as "Cornel Gunter's Coasters" or "Leon Hughes and his Coasters." "Over the next few decades, pretty much everyone who'd been involved with The Coasters started up their own rival version of the group."
**Violent Deaths of Associates** : Several individuals associated with The Coasters met violent ends. King Curtis was stabbed to death in the street in 1971 at age 37 during an altercation outside his apartment building when he asked two people engaged in a drug deal to move so he could carry in an air conditioning unit. In 1980, one of Cornel Gunter's Coasters was murdered by Gunter's manager after threatening to expose the manager's criminal activities. Finally, Cornel Gunter himself was shot dead in 1990, with his killer never found. "Sadly, several people associated with the coasters ended up dying violently."
**Legacy as Comedy Group** : The Coasters were often dismissed as merely a "comedy group," yet their unique ability to create music that was both funny and musically worthwhile is considered one of the hardest achievements in the industry. Their comedy music has remained enjoyable for over 60 years, a feat that no other rock and roll act has replicated. "They were often dismissed as just a comedy group but as anyone who has ever tried it knows making music that is both funny and musically worthwhile is one of the hardest things you can do."

## curiosities
**Independent Writer-Producers** : Jerry Lieber and Mike Stoller had a unique and unusual setup for the 1950s, operating as independent writer-producers. They were effectively under contract to two different labels, RCA and Atlantic, whose markets and audiences did not significantly overlap. "They were independent writer producers, an unusual state in itself in the 1950s."
**Dual Label Contracts** : Lieber and Stoller held simultaneous contracts with two distinct labels. For RCA, they worked with white pop stars like Elvis Presley and Perry Como, a collaboration that produced some hits but was uncomfortable for both parties and ended after about a year. Concurrently, for Atlantic, they exclusively recorded with black performers they admired, such as Ruth Brown, LeVern Baker, and The Drifters, with The Coasters being their personal project. "They were contracted to RCA to work with white pop stars. Not just Elvis, though he was obviously important to them, but people like Perry Como, who were very far from Lieber and Stoller's normal music."
**Classic Songwriting Split** : Jerry Lieber and Mike Stoller embodied a classic songwriting split: Lieber was primarily a lyricist who could not play an instrument but composed tunes in his head, while Stoller was an accomplished and sophisticated jazz pianist responsible for the music. Though they wrote together, they occasionally contributed ideas to each other's domain. "Lieber had started out as a songwriter who couldn't play an instrument or like music. He'd just written the mix down and remembered the tune in his head. While Stoller was already an accomplished and sophisticated jazz pianist by the time the two started collaborating."
**"Yakati Yak" Writing Process** : The song "Yakati Yak" was famously written by Lieber and Stoller in about 10 minutes during a session at Lieber's house. Stoller was playing a piano rhythm that he thought might suit The Coasters when Lieber, from the kitchen, called out the first line: "take out the papers and the trash." Stoller immediately responded with "or you don't get no spending cash," and they rapidly traded lines to complete the song. "They traded off lines and had the song written in about 10 minutes."
**Studio Roles** : In the studio, Mike Stoller was responsible for writing the parts for the musicians and playing the piano, while Jerry Lieber supervised from the control room. This division of labor contributed to The Coasters becoming a "hit making machine" with their new lineup. "Stoller would write the parts for the musicians and play the piano, while Lieber supervised in the control room."
**Criticism Over Racial Portrayals** : Lieber and Stoller faced criticism, notably from Johnny Otis, that their songs for The Coasters perpetuated racist stereotypes. Otis suggested they "dwelled entirely on a sort of street society," which, while perhaps reflecting a slice of life, wasn't typical of the black community. The controversy was heightened by their direction of black performers on songs written by white writers, drawing an analogy to the racial implications of "Amos 'n' Andy." "Johnny Otis in particular would make this criticism of the group's records, and it's one that must be taken seriously."
**Jewish Identity and Black Culture Involvement** : In the context of the racial stereotyping criticism, it was noted that Lieber and Stoller were Jewish, and at the time, were "only on the borders of whiteness themselves." They were also deeply involved in black culture. Despite this, they claimed on several occasions that their songs were not specifically about black people. "What can I say if it makes a difference that Lieber and Stoller would Jewish, and so were only on the borders of whiteness themselves at the time, or that they were deeply involved in black culture themselves?"
**"Charlie Brown" Extended Writing Process** : Unlike the rapid creation of "Yakati Yak," the song "Charlie Brown" took significantly longer to develop. While Mike Stoller quickly came up with appropriate music, Jerry Lieber spent weeks agonizing before he found the title and conceived the basic idea for the lyrics, with Stoller assisting in the final refinement. "Charlie Brown took significantly longer to come up with than the ten minute that Yakite had taken."
**Pseudonym "Elmo Glick"** : Lieber and Stoller sometimes used the pseudonym "Elmo Glick." This name was credited as the writer for The Coasters' song "Shopping for Clothes" because they were unable to identify the original writer, Kent Harris, at the time of its recording. "They'd been unable to find out who had written it, so it just quit as it, to Elmo Glick, a pseudonym they sometimes used."
**Mentored Phil Spector** : Jerry Lieber and Mike Stoller played a significant role in the early career of Phil Spector. As a favor to their mentor Lester Sill, they took Spector, who was a huge admirer and wanted to learn from them, under their wing. They signed him to a publishing contract, provided him with jobs as a session guitarist, and even allowed him to stay in their office or Lieber's spare room for a period. "So as a favour to Sill, and even though they found this like the kid once he got to New New York, they signed him to a publishing contract, gave him jobs as a session guitarist, and even let him sleep in their office or in Leibor's spare room for a while."

# curiosity

**Independent Writer-Producers in the 1950s** : In the 1950s, the concept of independent writer-producers was highly unusual. Jerry Lieber and Mike Stoller were pioneering in this setup, operating outside the direct employ of a single record label for their songwriting and production services. "They were independent writer producers, an unusual state in itself in the 1950s."
**Amos 'n' Andy Analogy** : The racial implications of The Coasters (black performers directed by white writers to perform certain material) were compared to the transition of "Amos 'n' Andy" from radio to TV. In the TV version, black actors portrayed characters, imitating the voices of the white comedians who originally created them, raising questions about authenticity and representation. "To continue the aimous and anti-anology, is this like when aimous and anti transferred from the radio to the TV, and the characters were played by black actors, imitating the voices of the white comedians who had created the characters?"
**Gangbusters Radio Drama Inspiration** : The radio drama "Gangbusters" served as an inspiration for the Lieber and Stoller song "Riot in Cell Block Number Nine." Jerry Lieber claimed that the characters' voices in the song "just happened to be black" and could have been white, drawing a parallel to how voices were depicted on radio. "Lieber said of riot in cell block number nine, it was inspired by the gangbusters radio drama."
