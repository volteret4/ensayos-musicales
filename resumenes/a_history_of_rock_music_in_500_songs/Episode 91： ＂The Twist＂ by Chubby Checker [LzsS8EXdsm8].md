## songs
**The Twist (1960)** : This version of "The Twist" went to number one on the Billboard Pop Chart as a result of Dick Clark's constant plugging on his TV shows. It was so similar to Hank Ballard's original version that Ballard initially believed it was his own record. Key differences included Checker's drummer playing more on the cymbals and his saxophone player playing all the way through the song, rather than just a solo. The record re-entered the charts 18 months after its initial release, returning to number one, making it the only non-Christmas themed record to achieve this feat in American history. As recently as 2018, Billboard listed it as the all-time number one record on the Hot 100. "It was so close to Ballard's version that when Ballard first heard it on the radio, he was convinced it was his own record."
**Mary Had a Little Lamb (renamed The Class) (Year?)** : This song, recorded in the style of impressions, made number 38 on the chart. Its success was largely thanks to promotion from Dick Clark. "made number 38 on the chart thanks to promotion from Clark."
**Whole Lot of Laughing (Year?)** : One of two singles that followed "The Class" in the same impressionistic vein. However, this single did not achieve commercial success. "Two more singles in that vein followed, whole lot of laughing and dancing dinosaur, but neither was a success."
**Dancing Dinosaur (Year?)** : One of two singles released after "The Class" that attempted to replicate its style. This single also failed to find success. "Two more singles in that vein followed, whole lot of laughing and dancing dinosaur, but neither was a success."
**Pony Time (Year?)** : A cover version of Don Covay's song, released only a few months after the original. This cover became Chubby Checker's second number one hit. "a quick cover of Don Coves' Pony Time, released only a few months before, which became Checker's second number one."
**Dance the Mess Around (Year?)** : This song was a hit for Chubby Checker, part of a series of follow-up hits that were covers of R&B songs about dancers. "All of these were hit, and it seemed like Chubby Checker would be associated with dancers in general, rather than with The Twist in particular."
**Let's Twist Again (1961)** : This was Chubby Checker's second "twist" hit, released a year after the original "The Twist." The song's lyrics, "Let's Twist Again, like we did last summer," directly referenced the earlier hit. It was written by the two owners of Cameo Parkway, who also had parallel careers as writers of novelty songs and had previously written Elvis's "Teddy Bear." "That was written by the two owners of Cameo Parkway, who had parallel careers as writers of novelty songs."
**Do The Freddy (1965)** : This song marked Chubby Checker's last big hit. By 1965, the British Invasion had thoroughly dominated the American charts, and this track was about the dance performed on stage by Freddy Garrity of Freddy and the Dreamers. "Checker's last big hit came in 1965, by which point the British invasion had taken over the American charts so comprehensively that Checker was recording Do The Freddy."

## curiosities
**Stage Name Origin** : Ernest Evans was given his stage name, Chubby Checa, by Dick Clark's wife, Barbara. This name was a playful reference to Evans' impression of Fats Domino, combining "Chubby" (like "Fats") with "Checa" (a twist on "Domino"). "Dick Clark's wife Barbara was there when he was doing the recording, and she called him Chubby Checa as a play on Fat Stomano."
**Dick Clark's Christmas Card Recording** : Before his success with "The Twist," Ernest Evans recorded an audio Christmas card for Dick Clark's friends. This private recording featured Evans singing jingle bells while performing impressions of various singers, including Fats Domino, Elvis Presley, and The Chipmunks. A later public re-recording as a duet with Bobby Rydell included these same impressions. "Evan said told the people at Cameo Parkway he could do impressions of different singers, and so they'd ask him to record it."
**Dick Clark's Promotion for "The Twist"** : Dick Clark deliberately chose Chubby Checker to remake "The Twist" instead of Hank Ballard. Clark sought a performer who was known to him, "unthreatening" (which is generally considered to have meant "less obviously black"), younger, not associated with an "adult act," and with whom Clark might have financial connections. Checker's ability to do impressions and perform in the style of Ballard made him an easy choice. Clark then ensured Checker performed a toned-down, television-appropriate version of the dance on his shows. "So when Clark wanted a record that sounded exactly like Hank Ballard in the Midnighters singing The Twist, it was easy enough for Chacker to do a Ballard in Fashion."
**Marriage to Catherine Allodders** : In 1964, Chubby Checker married Catherine Allodders, who had been crowned Miss World 1962. Their marriage has proven remarkably enduring, lasting for nearly 60 years to the present day, which is considered unusual for a union between a rock star and a beauty queen. "Rather amazingly from marriage between a rock star and a beauty queen, they remained married to this day nearly 60 years later."
**Bitterness and Perceived Erasure from History** : In recent decades, Chubby Checker has expressed significant bitterness and felt overlooked regarding his place in rock and roll history, despite his massive commercial success. He is convinced there is an attempt to erase him from the genre's narrative and believes he deserves the same prominence as figures like Elvis Presley and The Beatles. "Recent interviews show that Checker is convinced he is the victim of an attempt to erase him from rock and roll history, and believes he deserves equal prominence with Elvis and the Beatles."
**Claims of Racism and Hall of Fame Omission** : Checker attributes his lack of recognition, particularly his non-induction into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame, to racism. He specifically points to his marriage to a white woman as a contributing factor. He has publicly protested outside the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame regarding this perceived oversight. "He believes his lack of recognition is down to racism, as he married a white woman and has protested outside the rock and roll whole of fame at his lack of induction."
**Unavailable Recordings and Lost Royalties** : For a prolonged period, Chubby Checker's most successful records were legally unavailable. After Cameo Parkway was acquired by music industry executive Allen Klein in the late 1960s (reportedly as a tax write-off), the label's recordings went out of print and were not reissued on CD between 1975 and 2005. This meant Checker did not receive royalties he would have otherwise earned from 30 years of nostalgia compilation albums. "And between 1975 and 2005, there was no legal way to get any of the recordings on that label, as they went out of print and weren't issued on CD, so Checker didn't get the royalties he could have been getting from 30 years of nostalgia compilation albums."
**Hip-Hop Remake of "The Twist"** : In the late 1980s, Chubby Checker achieved a novelty hit by collaborating with The Fat Boys on a hip-hop remake of "The Twist." "even scoring a novelty hit in the late 80s with a hip-hop remake of The Twist with the fat boys."

## songs
**Work with Miami (Year?)** : This song served as the basis for their more famous hit, "The Wallflower." "We talked about the most in the episode on the Wallflower, which was based on their hit Work with Miami."
**The Twist (1959)** : Initially released in early 1959 as the B-side of the ballad "Teardrops on Your Letter," this song reached number 16 on the R&B chart. It was recorded in late 1958 and stemmed from a 15-bar blues song by the Sensational Nightingales, which Ballard and guitarist Cal Green adapted to a pre-existing 12-bar melody previously used on their song "Is Your Love For Real." This melody had a long ancestry, inspired by Clyde McPhatter and The Drifters and showing influence from The Radio 4. Initially, it featured a Jimmy Reed-style blues beat, which was changed for the studio recording in November 1958 with a new intro. An earlier demo for VJ Records reportedly contained sexually suggestive lyrics from the Nightingales' original. Ballard was credited as the sole writer, omitting the Nightingales, Green, and other contributing songwriters. Years later, King Records overdubbed an identical saxophone part onto Ballard's track and reissued it to make it sound more like Chubby Checker's successful version, which led to Ballard's version reaching number 28 on the pop charts. "It was released with Ballard as the sole credited writer as the base side of a ballad called Teardrops on Your Letter."
**Is Your Love For Real (Year?)** : This song provided the pre-existing 12-bar melody that Hank Ballard and Cal Green used to adapt the lyrics of "The Twist" from the Sensational Nightingales. The melody itself had a long ancestry, having been inspired by a track by Clyde McPhatter and The Drifters. "The melody they used was one they'd used previously on a song called Is Your Love For Real."
**Teardrops on Your Letter (1959)** : This ballad served as the A-side to their single, with "The Twist" being the B-side upon its release in early 1959. "It was released with Ballard as the sole credited writer as the base side of a ballad called Teardrops on Your Letter."
**Fingerpop in Time (Year?)** : This dance-themed song became a much bigger hit for the band compared to their earlier minor successes. Its increased popularity was partly due to an appearance on Dick Clark's TV show, American Bandstand. "The success of that saw the twist start to become a minor hit again and it made the lower reaches of the chart."

## curiosities
**Perennial Background Player** : The group was noted as a "perennial background player" in the history of rock music, having been discussed in the episode on "The Wallflower" and mentioned in passing in the episode on Jackie Wilson. "One of the groups that have been a perennial background player in our story so far has been Hank Ballard in the Midnighters."
**Decline by 1958** : By 1958, Hank Ballard and the Midnighters were largely a forgotten group. Their R&B style, rooted in the LA sound pioneered by Johnny Otis, had been superseded by newer sounds from Memphis, Chicago, and New York. They had not had a hit in three years and were concerned about being dropped by their record label. "By 1958 though, they were largely a forgotten group."
**Discovery of "The Twist"** : Despite their decline in chart success, the group remained a popular live act and toured regularly. While performing in Florida (either Tampa or Miami), they played on the same bill as the gospel group the Sensational Nightingales, who offered them the secular song "The Twist" as they could not perform it themselves. "But they were still a popular ally of Act, and they were touring regularly, and in Florida, some sources say they were in Tampa or there's Miami. They happened to play on the same bill as a gospel group called the Sensational Nightingales."
**Guitarist Cal Green's Imprisonment** : Cal Green, the Midnighters' guitarist who helped adapt "The Twist" melody, ended up in prison for two years around this time due to marijuana possession. This meant he missed out on almost the entire story of "The Twist" craze and its subsequent success. "Green, who ended up in prison for two years from marijuana possession around this time, and missed out on almost all of this story."
**Dick Clark's Reasons for Exclusion** : Dick Clark deliberately did not invite Hank Ballard and the Midnighters onto his shows to perform "The Twist," even as it began to chart. Various reasons are cited: they might have been too busy touring, Clark preferred someone "less threatening" (understood to mean "less obviously Black"), someone younger, concerns about the "adult nature" of Ballard's act, or Clark's preference for performers with whom he had a financial link. "Now, there were several stories about why Clark didn't ask Hank Ballard and the Midnighters onto the show."
**Reissue with Overdubbed Saxophone** : Upon hearing Chubby Checker's version of "The Twist," which sounded nearly identical to his own, King Records swiftly brought a saxophone player into the studio. They overdubbed an identical saxophone part onto Ballard's original track and reissued it to make it sound even more like Checker's successful sound-alike. "King Records quickly got a saxophone player into the studio to overdub an identical part on Ballard's track and reissue it, to make it sound more like the sound alike."

# genre - Blues

## curiosities
**15-Bar Blues Structure** : The original version of "The Twist," as written by the Sensational Nightingales, was structured as a 15-bar blues. This particular structure was considered "an awkward, uncommercial number," which prompted Hank Ballard and Cal Green to adapt it. "The song as the sensational Nightingale sang it was a 15 bar blues and 15 bars is an awkward, uncommercial number."
**Adaptation to 12-Bar Melody** : Hank Ballard and the Midnighters' guitarist Cal Green, along with Ballard, took the 15-bar blues structure of "The Twist" and adapted its lyrics to a pre-existing 12-bar melody. This was done to make the song more commercially viable than its original awkward 15-bar format. "So he and the Midnighters guitarist Cal Green took the song that the Nightingale sang and fit the lyrics to a pre-existing 12 bar melody."

**Dick Clark's Curated Dance Trends** : Dick Clark was acutely aware of racial sensitivities in television, having learned from Alan Freed's previous mistakes. To identify and promote new dance crazes, Clark relied on regulars in his audience who frequented Black clubs to learn the latest moves. He would then have these teenagers demonstrate the dances on his show, creating the impression that they had invented them. "Clark knew that most of the things that would become cool were coming from Black kids and so there were several regulars in the audience who Clark knew went to Black Clubs and learned the latest dance moves."

# concert - The Peppermint Lounge

## curiosities
**Manhattan Venue Description** : The Peppermint Lounge was described as a "tiny, dingy, bad smelling bar" in Manhattan, which had a capacity of approximately 200 people. It was also noted as being "Working Class Mafia-owned." "They played a three-day weekend at the CD Working Class Mafia-owned bar called The Peppermint Lounge in Manhattan."
**New Jersey Fan Base** : The venue, despite its small size, was consistently packed during Joey D and the Starlighters' residency, primarily with their fans from New Jersey. Many of these patrons were 18 and 19-year-olds who traveled from New Jersey, where the legal drinking age was 21, to New York, where it was 18. "Soon the timey venue, which had a capacity of about 200 people, was packed, largely with the band's fans from New Jersey."
**Publicity Stunt Success** : The owners of The Peppermint Lounge sought publicity for their club, hiring a publicist who enlisted Celebrity Services. This service aimed to attract minor celebrities, hoping for brief mentions in gossip columns. Unexpectedly, this effort generated enough intrigue to draw a wave of major celebrities, transforming the lounge into a sensation. "Because the owners of The Peppermint Lounge decided that they wanted a little publicity for their club, and they hired a publicist, who in turn got in touch with a company called Celebrity Services."
**Celebrity Hotspot** : The lounge, conveniently located near Broadway, became an unexpected celebrity hotspot. Broadway stars initially started dropping in, followed by other famous figures like Noel Coward, Judy Garland, Marilyn Monroe, Tallulah Bankhead, Norman Mailer (who danced the twist with Lord Beaverbrook's granddaughter), Tennessee Williams, and even Greta Garbo. These celebrities either danced to Joey D and the Starlighters or observed the younger patrons dancing. "The peppermint lounge became a sensation, and the stories all focused on the dance these people were doing."
**Jackie Kennedy Rumor** : There were persistent rumors, which later proved false, that First Lady Jackie Kennedy had visited The Peppermint Lounge. However, it was noted that she apparently did enjoy dancing the twist herself. "There were even rumours which turned out to be false that Jackie Kennedy had gone to the peppermint lounge, though she did apparently enjoy dancing the twist herself."
**Inaccurate Film Portrayal** : The film "Hey Let's Twist," starring Joey D and the Starlighters, inaccurately portrayed The Peppermint Lounge. Instead of its true identity as a mafia-owned bar, the movie depicted it as a family-run Italian restaurant. "in a film which portrayed the peppermint lounge as a family run Italian restaurant rather than a mafia run bar."

## curiosities
**Lindy Hop Origin** : The Savoy Ballroom in New York City was the venue where the Lindy Hop dance was performed in the late 1930s. This ballroom played a crucial role in the development and popularization of the dance. "we talked there about a dance that was performed in the Savoy Ballroom in New York in the late 30s called the Lindy Hop."

# curiosity

**"The Twist" - Unique Chart Achievement** : "The Twist" holds a unique place in American chart history as the only non-Christmas themed record to achieve number one on the Billboard Pop Chart, subsequently drop off, and then return to the number one position later. This rare feat highlights its enduring popularity and cultural impact. "It is the only non-Christmas themed record ever to go to number one on the Billboard Pop Chart, drop off and go back to number one again later."
**The Twist Dance - Original Form** : The specific dance move known as "The Twist" was originally characterized as a "thrusting, grinding move." It was not a formalized, standalone dance but rather a movement that people incorporated into other dances. Details of its exact form are difficult to ascertain today due to its informal nature. "There was, though, a specific dance move that was known as The Twist, which was a sort of thrusting, grinding move."
**Lindy Hop Dance** : Originating in the Savoy Ballroom in New York during the late 1930s, the Lindy Hop comprised two distinct parts. One was a relatively formalized partner dance involving partners holding each other and swinging around, which white culture later adopted and renamed the Jitterbug. The other part was known as the "Breakaway." "There were two parts of the Lindy Hop. One of those was a relatively formalized dance, with the partners holding each other, swinging each other around and so on."
**Breakaway Dance** : The "Breakaway" was a component of the Lindy Hop where the two dancers would separate to showcase their individual moves before rejoining. This section often included "twisting in the old sense" alongside various other movements. While the formalized part of the Lindy Hop was adopted by white culture, the Breakaway segment continued to be performed in Black clubs. "But there was another part of the dance known as the Breakaway, where the two dancers would separate and show off their own individual moves before coming back together."
**Toned-Down Twist Dance for Television** : For television audiences, particularly on shows like Dick Clark's, the dance known as "The Twist" was significantly toned down from its more overtly sexual original form. The simplified version demonstrated basically consisted of "mowing a towel in your buttocks while stubbing out a cigarette with your foot," a movement simple enough for anyone to replicate. "This version of the dance basically consisted of mowing towel in your buttocks while stubbing out a cigarette with your foot, and was simply enough that anyone could do it."
**Dick Clark's Immense Industry Power** : At a certain point in time, Dick Clark was considered "the single most powerful person in the music industry." His endorsement and promotion of an artist or record virtually guaranteed their success. He was also known for frequently promoting records in which he held some form of financial interest. "Clark was the single most powerful person in the music industry at this point, and if he plugged an artist, they were going to be a success."
**Celebrity Services for Publicity** : Celebrity Services was a company that, for a fee, would arrange for minor celebrities to visit a specific venue for a drink or meal. Their objective was to generate mentions in gossip columns, thereby providing publicity for the venue. "What celebrity services did? Was, for a fee, they would get some minus celebrity over there to go to a venue and have a drink over a meal, and they would let the gossip columnists snore about it, so the venue would then get a mention in the newspapers."
**Evolution of The Twist Craze** : The "Twist" craze began with working-class Black adults, then spread to working-class white teenagers, evolved to young middle-class white adults, and ultimately reached "middle aged and elderly rich white people." This progression through different social strata ultimately caused the dance to lose its "cool" factor among rebellious teenagers almost immediately. "The twist was a massive sensation, but it had moved first from working class black adults, to working class white teenagers to young middle class white adults, and now to middle aged and elderly rich white people who thought it was the latest in thing."
**Proliferation of Twist Cash-in Records** : The immense popularity of "The Twist" led to the release of "thousands of twist cash-in records." Examples included "Twisting Matilda" (a remake of "Waltzing Matilda"), "The Alvin Twist" by The Chipmunks, and "Bristol Twist in Annie" by The Devils. Many of these quick productions were of low quality, described as "terrible records." "in the meantime there were thousands of twist cash-in records."
**Rapid Production of Twist Films** : The twist craze sparked a rapid response from the film industry. By December 1961, just a month after the craze began in November 1961, two films had already been released in cinemas: "Hey Let's Twist" and "Twist Around the Clock." "The twist craze proper started in November 1961, and by December there were already two films out in the cinemas."
**"Twist Around the Clock" Film Production** : The film "Twist Around the Clock," starring Chubby Checker, was reportedly made in "a whole week." Its rapid production was facilitated by producer Sam Kertzmann, who had previously produced 1950s rock films like "Rock Around the Clock." Kertzmann simply retagged his existing script for "Rock Around the Clock" over a weekend, changing references to "rocking" to "twisting" and starring Chubby Checker instead of Bill Haley. "Twist around the clock was an easy film to make because Sam Kertzmann, who produced it, had produced several rock films in the 50s, including Rock Around the Clock."
**"Don't Knock the Twist" Film** : Chubby Checker's second film, "Don't Knock the Twist," was a direct follow-up to "Twist Around the Clock." This title mirrored Bill Haley's second film, "Don't Knock the Rock," both of which were produced by Sam Kertzmann, highlighting a pattern of reusing successful formulas. "As Kertzmann had also made Bill Haley's second film, Don't Knock the Rock, so Checker's second film became Don't Knock the Twist."
**"It's Trad Dad" Film Rework for Twist Music** : The British film "It's Trad Dad," initially conceived as a "cheap Trad Jazz cash-in," was quickly reworked at the last minute to incorporate twist music as the craze took hold. Director Richard Lester flew to the USA for a couple of days to film Chubby Checker and other artists performing to their records. This footage was then intercut with scenes of British teenagers dancing, creating the illusion that they were dancing to Checker's music. "That was a cheap Trad Jazz cash-in, but at the last minute they decided to rework it so it included twist music as well as Trad."
**The Twist as Last Pre-Beatles Fad** : "The Twist" is characterized as the "last great fad of the pre-Beatles 60s." Its eventual waning and limited lasting cultural impact are seen as indicative of the profound changes that the British Invasion would soon bring, effectively sweeping away the memory of earlier trends. "The twist was the last great fad of the pre-Beatle's 60s, that it left so little of a cultural mark, says a lot about the changes that were to come, and which would sweep away all memory of the previous few years."
**British Invasion's Dominance by 1965** : By 1965, the British Invasion had achieved such comprehensive dominance over the American charts that its influence was felt across the music industry. This era saw artists like Chubby Checker recording songs such as "Do The Freddy," which was about a dance performed by Freddy Garrity of the British group Freddy and the Dreamers. "by which point the British invasion had taken over the American charts so comprehensively that Checker was recording Do The Freddy."
