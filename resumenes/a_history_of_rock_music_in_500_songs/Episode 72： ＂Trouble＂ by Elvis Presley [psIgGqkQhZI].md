## albums
**King Creole (1958) - Soundtrack** : This film soundtrack was the first for an Elvis movie based on a book, Harold Robbins' "A Stone for Danny Fisher." The original plot about a boxer was changed to a singer, and the setting moved to New Orleans, bearing similarities to "A Walk on the Wild Side," an earlier proposed project for Elvis. Directed by Michael Curtiz and featuring Walter Matthau, the film itself was considered good, but many of the songs, particularly those by Leiber and Stoller, were created on "autopilot" to serve the film's narrative rather than stand alone as great musical works. "King Creole is, in fact, a rather good film."

## songs
**Steadfast Loyal and True (1958)** : Written by Jerry Leiber and Mike Stoller for the "King Creole" soundtrack, this song is widely considered the worst they ever wrote. Its quality reflected their "autopilot" approach to writing for Elvis's films at a time when they felt creatively constrained and bored with the routine. "Steadfast Loyal and True, which is widely regarded as the very worst song they ever wrote."
**Crawfish (1958)** : A jazz song from the "King Creole" soundtrack, performed as a duet with Kitty White. This song is highlighted as the most artistically interesting on the album, notably because it was not written by Leiber and Stoller, who were putting minimal effort into their contributions for the film at the time. "Indeed, the most artistically interesting song on the soundtrack is one that was not written by Libre and Stuller at all."
**Trouble (1958)** : Co-written by Jerry Leiber and Mike Stoller, this song was initially conceived by them as a joke, comparing it to "Black Denim Trousers and Motorcycle Boots." It notably incorporates a "stop time blues riff" previously used in Muddy Waters' "Hoochie Coochie Man" and other Leiber and Stoller compositions. Despite the writers' initial skepticism about its suitability for Elvis, he performed it with such ferocity that it resonated with audiences and was chosen as the opening song for his 1968 "comeback special." "Either way, Elvis performs the song with enough ferocity that it sounded right to a lot of other people."
**Love Me (1956)** : This song was written by Jerry Leiber and Mike Stoller as a parody of a particular style of ballad. Elvis Presley, however, performed it "entirely straight," demonstrating his ability to interpret material intended as a joke with sincerity. "They'd done this before with Alvis, when they'd written Love Me as a parody of a particular kind of ballad, and he'd then song it entirely straight."
**Santa Claus is Back in Town (1957)** : Written for Elvis by Jerry Leiber and Mike Stoller, this song features the distinctive "stop time blues riff" that was a signature element in their songwriting. This riff had been previously used in Muddy Waters' "Hoochie Coochie Man" and Leiber and Stoller's own "Riot in Cell Block No. 9." "They used the same riff, not only in Riott and Cell Block No. 9 and Trouble, but also I'm a woman for Peggy Lee and Santa Claus is back in town for Alvis."

## curiosities
**Relationships with Band Members Strained (1957-1958)** : Between late 1957 and early 1958, Elvis Presley's personal life and relationships, particularly with his band members Scotty Moore and Bill Black, began to deteriorate. Moore and Black felt increasingly isolated by Colonel Parker and underpaid despite Elvis's immense earnings, leading to significant friction. "In particular, his relationships with everyone involved in his career, everyone except the Colonel, of course, were getting weakened."
**Band Members' Resignation and Initial Replacements (September 1957)** : Scotty Moore and Bill Black formally resigned from Elvis's band in September 1957. Their grievances stemmed from being placed on a salary rather than a profit split, a drastically reduced concert schedule (only 14 shows that year), accumulating debt, and Colonel Parker's tight control, which prevented them from even speaking directly with Elvis. Initially incensed, Elvis replaced them for a show in Tupelo, Mississippi, with country session guitarist Hank Garland and bassist Chuck Wigginson. "Elvis was incensed, and decided that he didn't need them anyway."
**Rehiring and Altered Band Dynamics (1958)** : After performing without Scotty Moore and Bill Black, Elvis realized the live sound wasn't the same and offered them their jobs back. While they received an improved per diem, their roles significantly changed: Bill Black never recorded with Elvis again after the next session, being replaced by Bob Moore in the studio, and Hank Garland became the new lead guitar player on Elvis's records, with Scotty Moore relegated to rhythm guitar. "But after the next recording sessions, Bill never again recorded with Elvis."
**Colonel Parker's View of Band Members as Replaceable (1958)** : The disputes and subsequent changes in Elvis's band solidified Colonel Parker's belief that Scotty Moore and Bill Black were replaceable. He considered them unnecessary for Elvis's continued success in making hit records, a stance that permanently altered Elvis's relationship with his original sidemen. "It was now very clear that the kernel, at least, saw black and Moore as replaceable, and neither of them were necessary for Elvis to continue making hit records."
**Colonel Parker's Intent to Remove Leiber and Stoller (c. 1958)** : Colonel Parker viewed songwriters Jerry Leiber and Mike Stoller as a threat ("dangerous") because they commanded Elvis's respect, disliked Parker, were not under his control, and had independent careers. Although they were generating hits and working for RCA, Parker saw them as "loose cannons" and actively sought opportunities to remove them from Elvis's circle. "The two of them were dangerous as far as the kernel was concerned."
**Leiber and Stoller's Artistic Frustrations (c. 1958)** : While Jerry Leiber and Mike Stoller liked Elvis personally and admired his work ethic, they found writing for his films to be creatively unfulfilling, not "real art" compared to their R&B work. They considered it "easy money" because anything Elvis recorded sold well, leading to boredom with the routine of producing formulaic songs to "plug into gaps" in his movies. "They didn't have to try too hard to write anything particularly good for him, but they didn't like the kernel, and they were already, after a couple of films, getting bored with the routine nature of writing for Elvis's films."
**Rejected "A Walk on the Wild Side" Film Project (c. 1958)** : Jerry Leiber and Mike Stoller proposed a serious film project for Elvis, an adaptation of the novel "A Walk on the Wild Side," set in New Orleans, with producer Charles Feldman and director Ilya Kazan (who had worked with Marlon Brando, one of Elvis's idols). They envisioned this as an opportunity to elevate Elvis's film career, making him respected as both an actor and a singer like Frank Sinatra. This artistic proposition was, however, flatly rejected by Jean Abrabatch of Hill and Range, who warned them against interfering with "the business or artistic workings of the process known as Elvis Presley." "Abrabatch listened to the proposal, called the Colonel to relay the idea, and then said, if you ever try to interfere with the business or artistic workings of the process known as Elvis Presley, if you ever start thinking in this direction again, you will never work for us again."
**Film Songs as Contextual Storytelling (General)** : Many songs Elvis recorded for his films, especially in later years (e.g., "There's No Room to Rumba in a Sports Car," "Yoga Is as Yoga Does," "Queenie Wahini's Papaya," "Ito Eats"), are often criticized as terrible when heard in isolation. This is because they were primarily intended to function within the film's narrative, serving as part of the storytelling rather than as standalone musical compositions. "But in part, that's because they are not intended to work as songs divorced from their context in the film."
**Leiber and Stoller's Final Separation (June 1958)** : The collaboration between Elvis and Jerry Leiber and Mike Stoller ended permanently after Leiber contracted pneumonia and could not travel for a June 1958 recording session, which Chet Atkins produced. Colonel Parker's insistence on their presence led to Mike Stoller's defiant instruction to Leiber to refuse in strong terms. This incident resulted in Leiber and Stoller never working in the studio with Elvis or being commissioned to write songs for him again. "From this point on, Leighbaugh and Stuller would never work in the studio with Elvis again, and nor would they ever again be commissioned to write a song for him."
**Colonel Parker Forces Elvis into the Army (1958)** : Colonel Parker deliberately manipulated Elvis's military service. He leaked stories to the press alleging "special treatment" for Elvis, then indignantly denied them, ensuring Elvis would serve as a regular soldier rather than in special services, where he could continue his career. Parker's motives included preventing Elvis from devaluing his image with free shows, curbing Elvis's growing independence by isolating him from industry influences in Germany, and making RCA realize their dependence on the Colonel by limiting Elvis's pre-army recording backlog. "Colonel Parker had other ideas."
**Private Recordings During Army Service (1958-1960)** : During his two-year army service, Elvis made no official recordings. The only music he recorded were private sessions with himself and his army friends, captured during their downtime. This lack of a professional backlog was a deliberate strategy by Colonel Parker to exert leverage over RCA. "For the next two years, the only recording Elvis would make would be private ones, of himself and his army friends playing and singing during their downtime."
**Mother's Illness and Death (August 14, 1958)** : Elvis's mother, Gladys Presley, suffered from an illness whose descriptions closely resemble Elvis's own later final illness, leading to speculation about hereditary autoimmune problems. Her condition was potentially exacerbated by diet pills and "fat diets" driven by Colonel Parker's pressure for her to appear thinner in photos with Elvis. She was hospitalized with hepatitis and died on August 14, 1958, at the age of 46, a traumatic event for Elvis who was granted compassionate leave to be with her. "When she died on August 14, aged 46, Elvis was distraught."
**Gladys Presley's Funeral and Profound Impact (1958)** : At Gladys Presley's funeral, Elvis arranged for her favorite gospel quartet, the Blackwood Brothers, to sing "Precious Memories." Her death was arguably the greatest trauma Elvis ever experienced and, even more than his army induction, marked the definitive end of the first phase of his life and career. Her passing removed the only person in his life he could utterly trust, leaving Colonel Parker unchallenged. "Gladys' death, even more than his induction into the army, was the real end of the first phase of Elvis's life in career."
**Colonel Parker's Control Solidified (1958)** : Following Gladys Presley's death and Elvis's forced entry into the army, Colonel Parker faced no remaining obstacles to his complete control over Elvis's career. Gladys had never liked or trusted Parker, but Elvis's father, Vernon Presley, viewed Parker as a necessary business partner, enabling Parker to become the sole, undisputed decision-maker for Elvis's future. "Colonel Parker now had nobody standing in his way."

**Resignation from Elvis's Band (September 1957)** : Scotty Moore, alongside Bill Black, resigned from Elvis's band in September 1957 due to financial grievances and frustration with Colonel Parker's control. "They'd be getting paid a per DM whether or not they were performing, which was something."
**Career as Record Producer (Post-1958)** : While Elvis was serving in the army, Scotty Moore transitioned into record production. He produced "Tragedy" for Thomas Wayne (Thomas Wayne Perkins), which became a pop hit, reaching number five on the charts. "Scotty became a record producer, producing the record tragedy for Thomas Wayne."
**Work for Sam Phillips (Post-1958)** : After his success as a producer, Scotty Moore took a job working for Sam Phillips. He continued this employment for several years, even after rejoining Elvis's studio band following Elvis's return from military service. "Scotty took a job working for Sam Phillips, and when Elvis got out of the army and Scotty rejoined him, he continued working for Phillips for a number of years."

# artist - Jerry Leiber

## member of
Leiber and Stoller

## curiosities
**Complex Artistic Attitude Towards Elvis's Work (c. 1958)** : Jerry Leiber, as half of the songwriting duo Leiber and Stoller, held a nuanced view of his work with Elvis. While he liked Elvis and respected his studio work ethic, he considered much of their output for Elvis, particularly film songs, to be "easy money" rather than "real art" in the way their R&B records were. "But at the same time, they didn't consider the work they were doing with Elvis to be real art, in the way they considered their R and B records to be."
**Belief in Missed Artistic Potential with Elvis (c. 1958)** : Jerry Leiber expressed the conviction that with proper artistic freedom, he and Mike Stoller could have "made history" with Elvis. He believed that Elvis's management team was solely focused on financial gain, leading to a routine of producing generic songs for films that stifled creative ambition. "But the gist of it is that they believed that if they were given proper artistic freedom with Elvis, they could have made history, but that the people in his management team only wanted money."
**Contemptuous Comparison of "Trouble" (1958)** : Jerry Leiber compared "Trouble," a song he co-wrote as a joke, to "Black Denim Trousers and Motorcycle Boots." He expressed a sense of "contempt" for the idea that only "Hell's Angels and Elvis Presley" would take such songs seriously, implying that Elvis's rendition of the bluesy track didn't sound "right" to him. "Leighbaugh later said of Trouble, comparing it to that song. The only people who were going to take them seriously are Hell's Angels and Elvis Presley."
**Pneumonia and Final Break with Elvis (June 1958)** : Jerry Leiber contracted pneumonia, which prevented him from traveling to an Elvis recording session in June 1958. This health issue, combined with Colonel Parker's insistence and Mike Stoller's defiant advice, ultimately led to the permanent separation of Leiber and Stoller from working in the studio with Elvis and writing songs specifically for him. "Jerry Leighbaugh got pneumonia and was unable to travel to a session."

## curiosities
**Shared Musical Taste with Elvis (c. 1958)** : Jerry Leiber and Mike Stoller shared a mutual admiration for Elvis Presley's work ethic in the studio and his taste in blues music, which initially formed a strong basis for their creative collaboration. "They liked Elvis himself a lot, and they admired and respected his work ethic in the studio, and shared his taste in blues music."
**Boredom with Elvis Film Routines (c. 1958)** : After contributing to a couple of Elvis's films, Leiber and Stoller grew bored with the repetitive and routine nature of writing songs for them. They perceived the process as merely filling "gaps" with similar types of songs, stifling their creative expression. "They were already, after a couple of films, getting bored with the routine nature of writing for Elvis's films."
**Obsession with Stop-Time Blues Riff (c. 1954 onwards)** : Jerry Leiber and Mike Stoller developed a strong "obsession" with the "stop time blues riff," which originated in Muddy Waters' "Hoochie Coochie Man" in 1954. They consistently used this riff as a defining "signifier of the blues" in many of their compositions, making it a generic indicator of the genre. "Libre and Stuller had latched onto that riff."
**Writing Songs as Jokes for Serious Singers (Various)** : Leiber and Stoller had a distinctive habit of writing songs humorously, often as jokes or parodies, and then providing them to singers who would perform them with complete seriousness. This was evident with "Love Me" and "Trouble" for Elvis, with Jerry Leiber noting the irony of Elvis's earnest delivery. "The other aspect of their songwriting that Trouble shows is their habit of writing songs as jokes and then giving them to singers as serious songs."
**Loss of RCA Jobs and Shift to R&B Focus (Post-June 1958)** : Following their definitive split from working with Elvis, Leiber and Stoller soon lost their jobs at RCA. This enabled them to redirect their focus entirely to their work with R&B artists, including notable groups such as The Clovers, The Coasters, and The Drifters. "They soon lost their jobs at RCA, which left them to concentrate on their work with R&B artists, like the Clovers, the Coasters, and the Drifters."
**Brief but Defining Collaboration with Elvis (April 1957 - February 1958)** : Jerry Leiber and Mike Stoller's active collaboration with Elvis Presley, which significantly shaped public perception of all involved, lasted a mere 10 months, from April 1957 to February 1958. Despite the end of their direct studio work, Elvis continued to record many of their songs written for other artists. "Their active collaboration with Elvis, a collaboration that would define all of them in the eyes of the public, had lasted only 10 months, from April 1957 through February 1958."
**Elvis Continued to Record Their Songs (Post-1958)** : Even after their direct studio collaboration ceased, Elvis Presley continued to "keep an eye on their careers" and recorded many more of Leiber and Stoller's songs, although they were no longer commissioned to write material specifically for him. These later recordings included songs they had written for other artists like The Clovers and The Coasters. "He'd just never again have them write a song specifically for him."

**Colonel Parker's Leverage Against RCA (1958)** : Colonel Parker intentionally orchestrated Elvis's full military service to limit the creation of a large backlog of recordings for RCA. His strategic goal was to make RCA keenly aware of their dependence on "the Colonel" during Elvis's two-year absence from professional recording. "If he didn't give RCA much of a backlog to release while he was away, RCA would realise how much they needed the Colonel."

# curiosity

**US Military Draft and Elvis (1958)** : In 1958, the US military maintained a draft system requiring most men in their early 20s to serve for two years. Elvis Presley was subject to this draft. Although it was initially expected he would join special services, Colonel Parker deliberately maneuvered to ensure Elvis served as a regular soldier, preventing him from continuing his career during his service. "At the time, and for many years afterwards, the US military still drafted every man in his early 20s for two years, and so, of course, Elvis was going to be drafted."
**Hereditary Autoimmune Problems Theory (Post-1958)** : Recent reports suggest that Elvis Presley may have suffered from hereditary autoimmune problems, given the medical descriptions of his final illness. This theory gains credence from the strikingly similar descriptions of his mother Gladys Presley's illness and death at age 46, implying a potential familial predisposition to these health issues. "Recent reports have suggested that Elvis may have had hereditary autoimmune problems, and that would seem to make a lot of sense given everything we know about him."
**Colonel Parker's Pressure on Gladys Presley (Pre-1958)** : Colonel Parker reportedly pressured Gladys Presley, Elvis's mother, to appear thinner in photographs taken with her son. This pressure led her to undertake various "fat diets" and take diet pills, which may have contributed to her deteriorating health. "It's also one to have helped that she was on a series of fat diets, and taking diet pills, in order to lose weight, as the Colonel kept pressuring her to look thinner in photos with Elvis."
