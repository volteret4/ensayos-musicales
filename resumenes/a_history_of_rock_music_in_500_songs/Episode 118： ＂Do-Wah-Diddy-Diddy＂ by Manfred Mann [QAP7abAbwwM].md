## curiosities
  **Birth Name and Origin** : Born Manfred Lubawitz in South Africa, he came from a wealthy family but was strongly opposed to the system of apartheid and considered himself anti-racist. He was a lover of jazz music, especially progressive musicians like Ornette Coleman, Charles Mingus, and John Coltrane. "Manfrid man, the human being, as opposed to Manfrid man the group, was born Manfrid Lubawitz in South Africa, and while he was from a wealthy family, he was very opposed to the vicious South African system of apartheid, and considered himself strongly anti-racist."
  **Early Jazz Career** : Lubawitz became a very competent jazz pianist, playing with musicians like Hugh Massacala. This was at a time when fraternisation between people of different races was strongly frowned upon in South Africa. "He soon became a very competent jazz pianist, playing with musicians like Hugh Massacala, at the time when that kind of fraternisation between people of different races was very much frowned upon in South Africa."
  **Emigration to the UK (June 1961)** : Desperate to leave South Africa, Lubawitz seized an opportunity in June 1961 when he was still a Commonwealth citizen, just before South Africa lost its Commonwealth membership and its citizens' automatic right to immigrate to the UK. He was part of a year's grace period for this change. "Manfrid desperately wanted to get out of South Africa, and he took his chance in June 1961, at the last point at which he was a Commonwealth citizen."
  **Early London Life and Pseudonym** : After moving to England, he started playing jazz keyboards and giving piano lessons. He also earned money by writing record reviews, for which he adopted the pseudonym "Manfred Man" (initially spelled with a 't' on the end, which he later dropped), taken from jazz drummer Shelley Man. "For those reviews, rather than credit himself as Manfred Lubawitz, he decided to use a pseudonym taken from the jazz drummer Shelley Man, and he became Manfred Man, spelled with a salinity on the end, which he later dropped."
  **Temporary Job with Graham Bond** : Needing gigs, Manfred took a job playing with a holiday camp band as a substitute keyboard player. Graham Bond, leader of the Graham Bond Organisation, was playing Hammond organ in the same band but only wanted to play a few days a week, allowing Manfred to fill in. "Man was rather desperate for gates, and he ended up taking a job playing with a band that had put the insolidate camp. Graham Bond, who we've seen in several previous episodes as the leader of the Graham Bond organisation, was at that time playing Hammondog in there, but only wanted to play a few days a week."

## albums
  **The Five Faces of Manfred Mann (1964) - Debut Album** : This album was rushed out by EMI after the success of "Doo Wah Diddy Diddy," but it caused confusion for buyers expecting more pop songs. Half of its 14 tracks were covers of blues and R&B, mostly by Chess artists like Muddy Waters, Howlin' Wolf, and Bo Diddley. It also featured five original songs by Jones in the same style, plus instrumentals, including a cover of Cannonball Adderley's jazz classic "Sack o' Woe," arranged to showcase the group's skills. "Half the album's 14 tracks were covers of Blues and R&B, mostly by chess artists. There were covers of Muddy Waters, Howling Wolf, Bo Didi, I Canteen Eterna and more."
  **Mann Made (1965) - Second Album** : This album was recorded before Vickers' departure and was noted for being even more eclectic than their debut. It included an original jazz instrumental for flute and vibraphone called "Barefoot." "Barehook, an original jazz instrumental for flute and vibraphone."
  **Mighty Garvey (1968) - Fourth Album** : This album is considered the closest the group came to a truly great album. Unlike earlier albums dominated by blues covers, "Mighty Garvey" was mostly made up of original material by Mike Hugg or Mike Davo. It adopted a pastoral Baroque pop style, drawing comparisons to The Kinks or The Zombies' work of the period, but with a self-mocking, comedy-edge and some songs closer to the Bonzo Dog Doo-Dah Band. The highlight was likely the Mellotron-driven "It's So Easy Falling," but the album ultimately did not chart. "While the groups earlier albums were mostly blues covers, this was mostly made up of original material by either Hugg or Dabo, in a pastoral Baroque pop style that invites comparisons to the kinks or the zombies' material of that period, but with a self-mocking comedy-edge and several songs that was closer to the Bons of Dog Doodar band."

## songs
  **Why Should We Not? (1963)** : This was the group's first single, showcasing their jazz roots despite having evolved into a blues band and then being produced by a pop producer. It was an instrumental led by Vickers' saxophone, Mann's organ, and Jones' harmonica. The B-side was a jazz instrumental version of "Frère Jacques," written in 1963, a year when Jerry and the Pacemakers and Freddie and the Dreamers were having hits, not jazz instrumentals, and neither track charted. "Why should we not? It's an instrumental led by Vickers' saxophone, Manzogun and Jones' harmonica."
  **Cockerhoop (1963)** : Written by Paul Jones, this R&B song was the group's second single and performed slightly better than their debut instrumental. "The next single, an R&B song called Cockerhoop written by Jones, did a little better."
  **5-4-3-2-1 (1964)** : This song was created by Manfred Mann for the theme music of the popular British pop music show "Ready Steady Go!" after The Animals' attempt failed. It perfectly fit the show's style and also promoted the group, becoming a top five hit in the UK. However, by the time it charted, the group's lineup had already changed with Dave Richmond being replaced by Tom McGuinness. "That was taken on as Ready Steady Go's theme song, and made the top five in the UK, but by the time it charted, the group had already changed line-up."
  **Hubble Bubble Trouble and Strife (1964)** : This original song reached number eleven on the charts, but the group considered it a failure to the extent that they tried to erase it from their history. An eleven-CD box set of their 60s work, encompassing every studio album, compilation, EP collection, and BBC session, notably excludes this track, despite it being a "perfectly serviceable, if unexceptional, piece of pop R&B." "Hubble Bubble Trial in Trouble doesn't appear at all, which is quite odd, as it's a perfectly serviceable, if unexceptional, piece of pop R&B."
  **Doo Wah Diddy Diddy (1964)** : This song became the group's biggest hit, reaching number one in both the UK and the US. It was a cover of The Exciters' song, originally written by Jeff Barry and Ellie Greenwich, and represented a departure from the group's jazz and R&B inspirations, being a "perfect piece of bubblegum pop." Most members were initially unenthusiastic about recording it, but Paul Jones saw its potential. The group's version featured an organ-driven, stomping pop sound, with Manfred's Hammond organ solo, although producer John Burgess cut it out of the finished recording. "The result was an organ driven stomping pop song, which had more in common with the Dave Clark 5 than with anything else the group were doing."
  **Sha La La (1964)** : Following the success of "Doo Wah Diddy Diddy," the group covered another girl group track with a nonsense-word chorus and title, The Shevelles' "Sha La La." Their version reached number three on the UK chart and number twelve in the US. "Their version of Shalala by The Shevelles took them to number three on the UK chart and number 12 in the US."
  **Come Tomorrow (1965)** : This ballad was one of the few secular songs recorded by gospel singer Mary Knight, known as Sister Rosetta Tharpe's duet partner. Manfred Mann's cover followed "Sha La La." "They followed that with a ballad, come tomorrow, one of the few secular songs ever recorded by Mary Knight, the gospel singer, who we discussed briefly way back in episode five, who was sister Rosetta Tharpe's Duwad partner, and quite possibly her partner in other senses."
  **The One in the Middle (1965)** : Written by Paul Jones for The Yardbirds, who turned it down, Jones rewrote the song to be about Manfred Mann, particularly himself, expressing his growing frustration with the group's crediting. Released on an EP, it was so successful that the EP topped the EP charts and reached number ten on the regular singles charts, with "The One in the Middle" as the lead track. "Paul Jones had written The One in the Middle for his friends, The Are Birds, but when they turned it down, he rewrote the song to be about Manfid Man, and especially about himself."
  **If You Gotta Go, Go Now (1965)** : Manfred Mann heard Bob Dylan perform this unreleased song live, contacted Dylan's publishers for a demo, and the group became the first to release a version of it, reaching number two in the charts. This was the last single released while Mike Vickers was a member of the group. "He contacted Dylan's publishers, got hold of a demo, and the group became the first to release a version of the song, making number two in the charts."
  **Just Like a Woman (1966)** : This was the group's first single with new singer Mike Davo, a version of a Bob Dylan song. Despite concerns from the band and record label issues (their label stuck with Paul Jones for his solo career), "Just Like a Woman" made the top ten, allowing the group's career to continue. "Luckily for everyone involved, just like a woman made the top ten, and the group's career was able to continue."
  **Semi-Detached Suburban Mr. James (1966)** : Originally intended by its writers to be "Semi-Detached Suburban Mr. Jones," the group changed the title to "Mr. James" to avoid any misinterpretation as an insult towards Paul Jones, who had recently left. This song, told from the perspective of a spurned lover insulting their ex's new fiancé, reached number two on the charts. "As the group felt that a song insulting semi-detached suburban Mr Jones, might be taken the wrong way. Lightly retangled, semi-detached suburban Mr James, made number two."
  **Ha! Ha! Said the Clown (1967)** : This song followed "Semi-Detached Suburban Mr. James" and reached number four on the charts. "Ha Ha said the clown, made number four."
  **Sweet Pea (1967)** : An instrumental Hammond organ version of Tommy Roe's song, this was a somewhat bizarre choice for a single and made number 36 on the charts. "An instrumental Hammond organ version of Tommy Rose's sweet pea, which made number 36."
  **So Long, Dad (1967)** : A cover of Randy Newman's "bitterly cynical" song, this single did not chart at all, marking a period of unsuccessful single choices for the group. "A version of Randy Newman's bitterly cynical, so long dad, which didn't make the charts at all."
  **The Mighty Quinn (Quinn the Eskimo) (1968)** : After a period of less successful singles, the group returned to Bob Dylan's material. Manfred Mann obtained a copy of Dylan's "Basement Tapes" bootleg, picked this song, secured permission, and released it, making it their third number one hit. "He picked up on one song from it, and got permission to release The Mighty Quinn, which became the group's third number one."

## curiosities
  **Formation as The Man Hugg Blues Brothers (1962)** : Following Graham Bond's departure to form his own band, Manfred Mann (Lubawitz) and Mike Hugg decided to form their own group, combining their love for modern jazz with the more commercial R&B Bond was playing. The initial lineup included Mann on keyboards, Hugg on drums and vibraphone, Mac Vickers on guitar, flute, and saxophone, Dave Richmond on bass, Tony Roberts and Don Faye on saxophone, and Ian Fenby on trumpet. "They named their group the Man Hugg Blues Brothers, and it's initially consisted of Man on keyboards, Hugg and drums and vibraphone, Mac Vickers on guitar, flute and saxophone, Dave Richmond on bass, Tony Roberts and Don Faye on saxophone, and Ian Fenby on trumpet."
  **Search for a Blues Singer** : As their experiences leaned more towards jazz, the group sought a singer more familiar with the blues. They chose Paul Pund, who had been friends with Brian Jones of The Rolling Stones and had adopted Jones's surname, performing as Paul Jones. "As their experiences were far more in the jazz field than in blues, they decided that they needed to get in a singer who was more familiar with the blues side of things."
  **Musical Direction Shift and Horn Section Departure** : With Paul Jones joining as lead singer and harmonica player after auditioning at the Crawdaddy Club called The Rolling Twenties, the group's musical direction shifted to Chicago Blues, popular at The Marquee Club, where they secured a residency. This contrasted with the soul style expected from a horn-centric lineup at the nearby Flamingo Club, leading to the horn players (Tony Roberts, Don Faye, Ian Fenby) leaving, solidifying the group as a five-piece core of Jones, Mann, Hugg, Vickers, and Richmond. "Unsurprisingly, given this, the horn players soon left, and the group became a five-piece core of Jones, Mann, Hugg, Vickers, and Richmond."
  **Name Change to Manfred Mann (1963)** : Producer John Burgess, who signed the group to HMV Records, insisted they change their name from The Man Hugg Blues Brothers to "Manfred Mann." Paul Jones strongly disagreed, and even Manfred Mann himself found it "a little unreasonable," but the group eventually conceded, reassured that publicity would emphasize all five members were equal. The members themselves often referred to the group as "the Mann Fritz." "Burgess liked the group, but he insisted that they had to change their name, and in fact, he insisted that the group change their name to Manfred Mann."
  **Dave Richmond's Departure and Tom McGuinness's Arrival (1964)** : Dave Richmond was seen as a "great jazz bass player" who wanted to emulate Charles Mingus, playing "strange cross-rhythms." The group needed someone to play "straightforward blues bass lines without complaint," closer to Willie Dixon. Tom McGuinness, a guitarist who bluffed his way into the bass player role, replaced Richmond. McGuinness had never played bass before his first gig and was only asked if he was willing to play simple parts during his interview. "Richmond was a great bass player, but he was a great jazz bass player. He wanted to be Charles Mingus, and play strange cross-vethams, and what the group needed at this point was someone who would just play straightforward blues bass lines without complaint, they needed someone closer to Willie Dixon than to Mingus."
  **Miserable American Tour (1964)** : After the success of "Doo Wah Diddy Diddy," the group embarked on an American tour packaged with Peter and Gordon. The experience proved "thoroughly miserable," leading the group to decide against returning to the US, resulting in only minor successes there for the rest of the decade, despite continued hits in Britain. "The group found the experience of touring the US, a thoroughly miserable one, and decided that they weren't going to bother going back again."
  **Paul Jones' Growing Annoyance (1965)** : Paul Jones became increasingly frustrated that despite being the frontman, lead singer, cover picker, and writer of much original material, records were credited to the group's keyboard player, Manfred Mann. "Jones was getting increasingly annoyed at the fact that the record the group was making were hit, and he was the frontman, the lead singer, the person picking the cover versions, and the writer of much of the original material, but all the records were getting credited to the group's keyboard player."
  **Mike Vickers' Departure (1965)** : Mike Vickers left the group to pursue a career in arranging film music and session work. His later projects included work for The Beatles, the music for the 1972 film "Dracula A.D.," and the opening and closing themes for "This Week in Baseball." "Mike Vickers, who went off to work in arranging film music and session work, including some work for The Beatles, the music for the film Dracula A.D. in 1972, and the opening and closing themes for this week in baseball."
  **Tom McGuinness Switches to Guitar (1965)** : Following Mike Vickers' departure, Tom McGuinness took the opportunity to switch from bass to guitar, his preferred instrument. "The Maganis took the opportunity of Vickers leaving the group to switch from bass back to playing guitar, which had always been his preferred instrument."
  **Jack Bruce's Brief Tenure (1965)** : To fill the bass vacancy, Jack Bruce was hired on Graham Bond's recommendation. Bruce had recently played with John Mayall's Bluesbreakers alongside Eric Clapton. He played bass on "Pretty Flamingo," which became the group's only UK number one. Bruce remained with the band for several months before leaving. "And its Bruce who played bass on the group's next big hit, Fitty Flamingo. The only UK number one the vues ever played on."
  **Klaus Voormann Joins (1965)** : After Jack Bruce's departure, Klaus Voormann replaced him on bass. Voormann was an old friend of The Beatles from their Hamburg days, having been taught the basics of bass by Stuart Sutcliffe, and had formed the trio Paddy, Klaus and Gibson. Like Vickers, Voormann could play the flute, and his flute playing became a regular feature on later singles. "Verman was an old friend of the Beatles from their Hamburg days, who had been taught the rudiments of bass by Stuart Sukliff, and had formed a trio, Paddy Klaus in Gibson, with two merzy-beam musicians, Paddy Chambers of the Big Three, and Gibson Camp of King's Highest Tale of the Dominoes."
  **Paul Jones' Departure (1966)** : Paul Jones announced his intention to quit the group concurrently with Mike Vickers' departure, but was persuaded to stay for nearly a year while a replacement was sought. Auditions with singers like Rod Stewart and Long John Baldry were unsuccessful. Relations between Jones and the rest of the group deteriorated to the point where they didn't inform him they were considering Mike Davo as his replacement. "But by the time that track was released, Paul Jones was out of the group. He actually announced his intention to quit the group at the same time that Mike Vickers left, but the group had persuaded him to stay on for almost a year while they looked for his replacement, or auditioning singers like Rudge Stewart and Long John Bouldery with little success."
  **Mike Davo Joins (1966)** : Mike Davo, formerly lead singer of A Band of Angels, eventually replaced Paul Jones. Jones himself unknowingly suggested Davo as a possible replacement, unaware he was already chosen. Manfred Mann recounted a silent drive to their last gig with Jones, where Dylan's "Just Like a Woman," their first single with Davo, played on the radio, highlighting the discomfort within the band. "Jones actually mentioned Davo to his bandmates as a possible replacement, not realising he was already in the group."
  **Record Label Split and Career Concerns (1966)** : When Paul Jones left, the band faced a difficult situation as their record label decided to keep Jones as a solo performer. The rest of the group had to find another label and feared their career was over without Jones. "There was a lot of discomfort in the band at this time, because their record label had decided to stick with Jones as a solo performer, and the rest of the group had had to find another label, and were worried that without Jones their career was over."
  **"No A-Sides by Group Members" Rule** : During Mike Davo's tenure as lead singer, the group operated under an implicit "no A-sides by group members" rule, which limited their material choices. Davo himself was a songwriter and wrote two hit singles, "Handbags and Glad Rags" and "Build Me Up Buttercup," that the group never recorded. "Dabo was himself a songwriter, and it shows the limitations of the No A sides by group members rule. The wild Dabo was the lead singer of Manfred Mann, he wrote two hit singles which the group never recorded."
  **Split in 1969** : By January 1969, Tom McGuinness stated, "It's not a group anymore, it's just five people who come together to make hit singles. That's the only aim of the group at the moment, to make hit singles, it's the only reason the group exists." The group formally split later that year. "The groups split up in 1969 and went their separate ways."
  **Members' Post-Split Careers (1969 onwards)** : After the split, Mike Davo appeared on the original "Jesus Christ Superstar" album and became known for writing advertising jingles, notably "A finger of fudge is just enough for Cadbury's." Tom McGuinness formed McGuinness Flint, having a hit with "When I'm Dead and Gone," and later reunited with Paul Jones to form The Blues Band. Paul Jones became a born-again Christian in the 80s, starred in the children's TV show "Uncle Jack," and hosted the BBC Radio 2 Blues program for 32 years. Manfred Mann and Mike Hugg formed Manfred Mann Chapter Three, releasing two albums. Hugg then wrote theme music for TV and films, including "Whatever Happened to the Likely Lads." Manfred Mann went on to form Manfred Mann's Earth Band, having hits like Bruce Springsteen's "Blinded By The Light." "The groups split up in 1969 and went their separate ways. Dabo appeared on the original Jesus Christ Superstar album, and then went into writing advertising jingles."
  **The Manfreds Reunion (1990s)** : Almost uniquely for an early 60s band, all members of the classic Manfred Mann lineup were still alive. Hugg, Jones, McGuinness, and Davo reunited as The Manfreds in the 1990s, with Mike Vickers also joining until 1999, and continue to tour. Manfred Mann continues to perform with his Earth Band. "Hugg, Jones, McGuinness and Davo reunited as the Manfreds in the 1990s, with Vickers also in the band until 1999, and continued to tour together."
  **Mike Vickers' Later Career** : After his time with Manfred Mann and session work, Mike Vickers now spends his time creating experimental animations. "Mike Vickers now spends his time creating experimental animations."
  **Eclectic Catalogue and Critical Reassessment** : Manfred Mann was a band with too many musical interests for a coherent image, and their reliance on outside songwriters and frequent lineup changes meant they lacked a consistent sound compared to contemporaries. However, this eclecticism created a catalog that "rewards exploration" and may warrant a major critical reassessment in the future. "Manfred Mann were a band with too many musical interests to have a coherent image, and their alliance and outside songwriters, and their frequent lineup changes, meant that they never had the consistent sound of many of their contemporaries."
