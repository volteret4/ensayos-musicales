# artist - Louis Moreau Gottschalk
## instruments
Piano
## curiosities
**Gottschalk's Posthumous Recognition - Composer vs. Performer** : During his lifetime, Louis Moreau Gottschalk was dismissed as a romantic composer, mocked by figures like Mark Twain, and largely forgotten for decades after his early death, remembered primarily as an extraordinary pianist rather than a composer. "Mark Twain made fun of his style, and he was largely forgotten for decades after his early death."
**Gottschalk - Virtuoso Pianist** : Louis Moreau Gottschalk was widely considered the greatest pianist of his generation, recognized for his flashy showmanship and unique ability to manipulate the keyboard in ways others could not. "he was considered the greatest pianist of his generation, a flashy showman of the keyboard, who could make it do things no one else could."
**Gottschalk's Contemporaries and Influences** : Louis Moreau Gottschalk was known to Chopin and Liszt, and his influence extended to teaching someone who later played for Abraham Lincoln, highlighting his connection to significant figures of his era. "someone who knew Chopin and List, someone who was writing so long ago, he taught someone who played for Abraham Lincoln."
**Gottschalk - Pioneer of New Orleans Music** : Gottschalk's compositions sound remarkably modern, akin to music from the 1920s or 1930s, because he was the first to formalize New Orleans musical elements into a permanent written form. "Gotshawk was the first person to put New Orleans music into some sort of permanent form."
**Gottschalk - Incorporator of Folk Melodies** : Gottschalk integrated folk melodies he encountered in New Orleans into his compositions, drawing from a diverse range of influences present in the city's unique musical environment. "Gotshawk knew and incorporated folk melodies he heard in New Orleans."
**Gottschalk - Romantic Composer** : Gottschalk exemplified the romantic archetype, composing pieces with evocative titles such as "The Dying Poet." "He wrote pieces called things like The Dying Poet."
**Gottschalk - Civil War Exile** : Louis Moreau Gottschalk was exiled from his Southern home for supporting the Union during the Civil War, and later left the United States to reside in South America. "He was first exiled from his home in the south due to his support for the north in the Civil War, and then later had to leave the US altogether and move to South America."
**Gottschalk - Scandal and Death** : After a scandalous affair involving his students, Gottschalk contracted yellow fever and tragically collapsed on stage, never recovering. "After a scandalous affair with the students, he eventually contracted yellow fever and collapsed on stage."
**Gottschalk - "Morte!" Performance and Death** : Gottschalk's final performance saw him play a piece titled "Morte!" (Portuguese for death), before collapsing on stage and succumbing three weeks later to a quinine overdose. "Shortly after playing a piece called Morty with an exclamation mark, which is Portuguese for death."
**Gottschalk - Precursor to 20th Century Music Careers** : Gottschalk's life and career foreshadowed those of many 20th-century musicians, with New Orleans continually revolutionizing popular music using techniques he had pioneered. "Gotshawk also presaged the careers of many 20th century musicians."

## songs
**The Fat Man (1949)** : Considered one of the first rock and roll records, this song by Fats Domino used the melody from "Juncker's Blues" with new, radio-appropriate lyrics inspired by "The Fat Man" radio show. It sold a million copies within four years. "the most important recording of it was in 1949, by a New Orleans piano player called Fat Stomino, and in his version it became one of those songs that is often considered to be the first rock and roll record."
**The Fat Man (1949) - Rhythmic Structure** : While predominantly lacking the Treheo rhythm, "The Fat Man" features sporadic bass lines that incorporate it, alongside standard boogie bass patterns and root notes, though the bass is often obscured by poor recording quality. "There are odd parts where the bass plays it, but the bass player, who it's really difficult to hear anyway because of the poor sound quality of the recording, and it comes to switch between playing a traheor, playing normal boogie bass lines, and playing just for root notes as crutchits."
**The Fat Man (1949) - "Spanish Tinge"** : Fats Domino's "The Fat Man" undeniably exhibits the "Spanish Tinge" that Jelly Roll Morton famously identified in New Orleans music. "But it does, definitely, have that Spanish tinge that Jelly Roll Morton talked about."
**The Fat Man (1949) - Unmistakably New Orleans** : "The Fat Man" is distinctly New Orleans in its musical character, unequivocally identifying Fats Domino as a musician from that city. "its music that absolutely couldn't come from anywhere else."
**The Fat Man (1949) - Foundation for Future Sound** : Although lacking the rhythmic complexity of subsequent Domino-Bartholomew collaborations, "The Fat Man" served as a crucial foundational step toward their signature sound. "it's definitely a step towards what would become their eventual sound."
**The Fat Man (1949) - Million-Selling Success** : "The Fat Man" sold a million copies within four years of its release, marking the beginning of Fats Domino's massive commercial success. "Fat Man went on to sell a million copies within four years, and was the start of what became a monster's success for Domino."
## curiosities
**Fats Domino's Persona - "Big Cheerful Fat Man"** : Fats Domino presented a cheerful, substantial figure, a stark contrast to the emerging "mean and moody and slim" rock star archetype, and spoke Louisiana Creole as his primary language. "He was a big cheerful Fatman, who spoke Louisiana Creole as his first language."
**Fats Domino's Performance Style - Dance-Oriented** : In 1949, Fats Domino's performing style, characterized by its ability to make people happy and encourage dancing, was his most significant attribute, despite not fitting the image of a sex symbol. "he had a way of performing that made people happy and made them want to dance, and in 1949, that was the most important thing for a musician to do."
**Antoine Domino's Dedication to Music - Manual Record Playback** : As a youth, Antoine Domino demonstrated his profound love for music by manually turning records with his finger on a broken wind-up player to continue listening, highlighting his early dedication. "he would sit at the record player and manually turn the records using his finger so he could still listen to them."
**Fats Domino's Early Recognition (1949) - Piano Over Vocals** : By 1949, Antoine Domino had achieved minor celebrity status within New Orleans' black music scene, primarily lauded for his exceptional piano skills rather than his singing. "By 1949, Dubinot had become a minor celebrity among black music fans in New Orleans, more for his piano playing than for his singing."
**Fats Domino's Piano Style - Triplet-Based Boogie Woogie** : Fats Domino was renowned as one of the premier Boogie Woogie pianists, distinguished by a unique style heavily reliant on triplets, setting him apart from other pianists who often favored more direct rhythms. "He was known as one of the best Boogie Boogie players around, with a unique style based on triplets, rather than the more straightforward rhythms many Boogie pianists used."
**Fats Domino's Dismissal by Roy Brown - Upstaging Incident** : Fats Domino and his band were dismissed by Roy Brown after Domino sang several numbers on stage, with Brown asserting he was only paid to play piano, not to upstage the headliner. "Domino and his entire band got dropped by Brown after Domino sang a few numbers on stage himself during a show."
**Fats Domino's Early Struggles (Age 21)** : At 21, despite local celebrity as a pianist, Fats Domino faced financial hardship, supporting a family while living in his in-laws' home, working a mattress factory job, and selling flavored syrups to make ends meet. "at age 21, Fat's Domino already had a family and was living in a room in his in-laws house with his wife and kids, working a day job at a mattress factory, and working a second job selling fresh dyes with syrups to kids to try to make ends meet."
**Fats Domino's Live Repertoire - "Juncker's Blues"** : "Juncker's Blues" was a staple in Fats Domino's live performances, known for consistently energizing audiences. "Domino had a few tunes that would always get the audience as going, and one of them was Juncker's Blues."
**"The Fat Man" Song Title Origin - Radio Show Influence** : In 1949, Dave Bartholomew, inspired by "The Fat Man" radio show about a 237-pound detective, decided to capitalize on the popular title and associate it with Fats Domino, leading to the lyrical change from the drug-themed "Juncker's Blues" to "they call me The Fat Man because I weigh 200 pounds." "Dave Bartholomew heard the show, and realized that he could capitalize on the part of the film. He could capitalize on the popular title, and tie it into his fat singer, so instead of, they call me a Juncker because I'm loaded all the time, Domino sang, they call me The Fat Man because I weigh 200 pounds."
**Fats Domino's Vocal Style - Mills Brothers Influence and Trumpet Imitation** : Fats Domino's vocal delivery on "The Fat Man" is likened to The Mills Brothers, and notably includes a trumpet imitation, a subtle yet present detail. "Domino's scattered vocals here are very reminiscent of the mill's brothers. There's a similarity in his trumpet imitation, which I've not seen any one pick up on, but is very real."
**Fats Domino's Later Records - Saxophone Solos** : Subsequent Fats Domino recordings frequently featured saxophone solos that emulated the trumpet-like vocalizations heard on "The Fat Man." "On later records, there'd be a saxophone solo doing much the same kind of thing."
**Fats Domino's Signature - Tenor Saxophone Solo** : A tenor saxophone solo, typically placed two-thirds into the track, became a hallmark of Fats Domino's later recordings, with his own voice fulfilling this role on "The Fat Man." "Domino's later records almost all featured a tenisex solo, roughly two thirds of the way through the record. In this case, it's Domino's own voice doing the job."
**Fats Domino/Bartholomew Sound - Layered Rhythmic Complexity** : The signature Domino/Bartholomew sound featured a complex layering: Earl Palmer's simple backbeat on drums, a bass line with a Treheo rhythm, an overlaid horn riff, and Fats Domino's piano playing fast triplets, creating intricate polyrhythms. "You'd have Earl Palmer and drums playing a simple backbeat, and then over that you'd lay the bass playing a traheo rhythm, and then over that you'd lay a horn riff, going across both those other rhythms, and over that you'd lay Domino's piano playing fast triplets."
**Fats Domino's Vocals - Obvious Cheeriness** : A key characteristic of Fats Domino's vocal performance was his undeniable cheerfulness, a quality that permeated his delivery. "what really matters with the vocal part is Domino's obvious cheeriness."
**Fats Domino's Reputation - "Nicest Man in Music"** : Fats Domino earned a reputation as one of the nicest individuals in the music industry, a fact that ironically makes biographical information about him scarce compared to more controversial contemporaries. "Domino was known as one of the nicest men in the music industry, to the extent that it's difficult to find much biographic information about him, compared to any of his contemporaries."
**Fats Domino's Personal Life - Stability and Community Ties** : Fats Domino maintained a stable personal life, remaining married to the same woman for 61 years, and despite his wealth, he continued to live in his lifelong neighborhood until Hurricane Katrina forced him to evacuate in 2005. "He remained married to the same woman for 61 years, and while he got himself a nice big house when he became rich, it was still in the same neighbourhood he'd lived in all his life, and he stayed there until Hurricane Katrina drove him out in 2005."
**Fats Domino's Vocal Delivery - Reflecting Personality** : Fats Domino's shy, friendly, humble, gracious, and cheerful personality was directly conveyed in his vocals, distinguishing him from the more aggressive rhythm and blues vocalists of his era. "He was shy, friendly, humble, gracious, and cheerful, and that all comes across in his vocals, while other rhythm and blues vocalists of the era were aggressive."
**Fats Domino's Braggadocio - Friendly Tone** : Even when employing sexual bragging in songs like "The Fat Man," Fats Domino's friendly vocal delivery made him sound less aggressive than his contemporaries, giving the impression he didn't entirely mean the boasts. "Domino comes across as friendly, even when, as in a song like this, he's bragging sexually, he doesn't actually sound like he means it."
**Fats Domino and Dave Bartholomew - Consistent 1950s Sound** : Fats Domino, often collaborating with Dave Bartholomew, maintained a consistent musical style throughout the 1950s, producing records that defined their signature sound. "Fat Stomano, usually with Dave Bartholomew, carried on making records that sounded just like this throughout the 50s."
**Fats Domino's Records - Massive Rock and Roll Sales** : Fats Domino's records were universally recognized as rock and roll and achieved immense commercial success, selling in massive numbers. "Everyone called them rock and roll, and they sold in massive numbers."
**Fats Domino's Commercial Dominance - Second Only to Elvis** : Fats Domino's sales in the 1950s surpassed every other rock and roll artist except Elvis, and he achieved an impressive streak of 39 consecutive charting hit singles extending into the early 1960s. "He outsold every other rock and roll artist of the 50s other than Elvis, and had 39 charting hit singles in a row in the 50s and early 60s."
**Fats Domino's Total Sales - Historical Significance** : With estimated sales between 65 million and 110 million, Fats Domino was, as late as the early 1980s, considered among the highest-selling artists in the rock era, surpassed only by Elvis, The Beatles, and Michael Jackson. "Estimates of his sales vary between 65 million and 110 million, but as late as the early 80s, he was being seriously claimed that the only people who sold more records than him in the rock era were Elvis, The Beatles, and Michael Jackson."
**Fats Domino - Claim to First Rock Star** : Fats Domino is presented as a strong candidate for the title of the "first rock star," given his early success and consistent style, which mirrored "The Fat Man." "if anyone can claim to be the first rock star, it's Fat Stomano, and as the music he was making was all in the same style as The Fat Man."

## curiosities
**Dave Bartholomew's Role in Fats Domino's Signing** : Dave Bartholomew, the leading New Orleans musician of his time and an A&R man for Imperial Records, was instrumental in suggesting the label sign Fats Domino, recognizing his talent. "Bartholomew, who was working for Imperial Records, suggested that the label signed Domino."
**Dave Bartholomew's Military Service and Musical Development** : Like many New Orleans musicians of the late 1940s, Dave Bartholomew honed and expanded his musical abilities during his service in the Army during World War II. "Dave Bartholomew learned his musical skills while he was in the Army during World War II."
**Dave Bartholomew's Trumpet Training - Louis Armstrong Connection** : Dave Bartholomew was already a proficient trumpet player, having received instruction from the same teacher who also taught the legendary Louis Armstrong. "having been taught by the same man who taught Louis Armstrong."
**Dave Bartholomew's Formal Musical Training - Military Brass Band** : His time in a military brass band necessitated Dave Bartholomew's acquisition of more formal musical skills, including composition and arrangement. "once he was put into a military brass band, he had to learn more formal musical skills, including writing and arranging."
**Dave Bartholomew's Post-Army Career - A&R and Orchestra Leader** : After his military service, Dave Bartholomew secured an A&R position with Imperial Records and established the Dave Bartholomew Orchestra, which achieved a hit with the song "Country Boy." "After getting out of the Army, he got work as an A&R man for Imperial Records, and he also formed his own band, the Dave Bartholomew Orchestra, who had a hit with Country Boy."
**"Country Boy" - Pioneering Horn Part** : "Country Boy" featured a distinct "Dan. Da da" horn part that, while clichéd today, was unprecedented in an R&B record at its time of release. "that was the first time anything like that had been used in an A&B record."
**Treheo Rhythm - Connection from Gottschalk to Bartholomew** : The distinctive horn part in "Country Boy" is linked to Louis Moreau Gottschalk's compositions through its incorporation of the Treheo rhythm. "we can link that horn part back to the gut-shock piece we heard earlier, by its use of a rhythm called the Treheo."
**Dave Bartholomew's Rhythmic Innovation - Habanera vs. Backbeat** : Dave Bartholomew pioneered the practice of placing the Habanera rhythm against a drum shuffle or backbeat, an innovation that gave New Orleans R&B its unique flavor. "That rhythm was put against drums playing a shuffle or a backbeat."
**Habanera-Backbeat Combination - Dave Bartholomew's Innovation** : Dave Bartholomew and his musicians are credited with being the first to combine the Habanera rhythm with a backbeat, a foundational element of their distinctive sound. "Dave Bartholomew and the musicians who worked with him, were the first ones to put together."
**Dave Bartholomew's Session Musicians - Consistent Lineup** : The musicians who played on "Country Boy" became Dave Bartholomew's core session band, consistently employed for numerous productions over many years. "The musicians on Country Boy, were ones that Bartholomew would continue to employ for many years on all the sessions he produced."
**Matassa and Bartholomew - Quintessential New Orleans Sound** : The collaborations between Cosimo Matassa as engineer and Dave Bartholomew with his band produced quintessential New Orleans records that, even after 70 years, remain definitive of the city's unique sound. "the records that Matassa would go onto engineer with Dave Bartholomew in his band... would be the quintessential New Orleans records that still, 70 years on, sum up the sound of that city."
**"Juncker's Blues" Lyrics - Unsuitable for Radio** : Dave Bartholomew initially wanted Fats Domino to record "Juncker's Blues," but its original lyrics, largely about heroin and cocaine, were deemed inappropriate for radio airplay. "it was felt that the lyrics weren't quite suitable for the radio, what would them be pretty much entirely about heroin and cocaine."
**"The Fat Man" Lyrics - Blues Trope of Body Positivity and Attraction** : Dave Bartholomew's lyrics for "The Fat Man" employ a common blues trope where a large man boasts about his attractiveness to women despite his size. "follow a standard blues trope of being fat but having the ability to attract women anyway."

"The combination of these pulses, rubbing up against each other, is what gave New Orleans R&B its special flavour."

# genre - Rock and Roll
## curiosities
**Charlie Gillett's Initial Typology** : In his 1970 book, "The Sound of the City," Charlie Gillett made the first serious attempt to define rock and roll by identifying five distinct styles of music that were loosely categorized under this label, similar to how country and western or rhythm and blues were grouped. "He identifies five different styles of music, all of them very different, which loosely got lumped together in much the same way that country and western, or rhythm and blues had, and labelled rock and roll."
**Indisputable Start of the Era** : The success of artists like Fats Domino, whose records were universally called rock and roll and sold in massive numbers, marked the point where the rock and roll era was undeniably established. "this is the point beyond which it's indisputable that rock and roll has started."

# genre - Tango
## curiosities
**Original Tango Rhythm - Habanera Influence** : The original Tango was founded on Habanera rhythms, featuring a "da da da" pattern (or similar, like the San Quio), rather than the beat commonly associated with the modern tango. "These rhythms were the basis of the original tango, which didn't have the beat that we now associate with the tango."

# label - J&M Studio
## curiosities
**J&M Studio - Seminal Recording Location** : Cosimo Matassa's J&M Studio in Louisiana became a pivotal recording location for the burgeoning rock and roll sound, central to the history of this new music. "Cosimo Matassa's studio, J&M, in Louisiana, which would become one of the most important places in the history of this new music."
**J&M Studio - Modest Origins** : J&M Studio was a tiny, rudimentary space located in the back room of Cosimo Matassa's family appliance store, with a bookmaker's office upstairs and a shoe shine boy working outside. "It was in the back room of his family's appliance store, which also had a bookmaker's upstairs and a shoe shine boy operating outside the studio door."
**Cosimo Matassa's Unconventional Path to Studio Owner** : Cosimo Matassa, lacking formal record production training, dropped out of chemistry studies at age 18 to establish J&M Studio, which was remarkably rudimentary compared to modern standards. "He'd been a chemistry student until he dropped out of university, aged 18, and set up the studio, which was laughably rudimentary by today's standards."
**J&M Studio Recording Equipment - Direct-to-Disk** : J&M Studio utilized a basic three-channel mixer and recorded directly to disk rather than using magnetic tape, reflecting the rudimentary technology of the time. "He had a three channel mixer, and they didn't record to tape, but directly to disk."
**J&M Studio - Dual Disk Cutter Method** : J&M Studio employed two disk cutters simultaneously: one for creating a safety copy to check audio quality, and the other for cutting the master disk, as direct-to-disk recording didn't allow for post-production changes. "One of them would cut a safety copy, which they could listen to to see if it sounded okay, while the other would be cutting the master."
**J&M Studio - Wax Debris Management** : During recording sessions at J&M Studio, an assistant was tasked with catching the wax debris cut from the disks by the two disk cutters, preventing it from falling onto the floor. "someone's job would be to stand by them and catch the wax they cut out of the disks before it dropped onto the floor."
**J&M Studio - Use of Wax Masters** : While most professional studios by this time had transitioned to more robust acetate disks for cutting, J&M Studio notably continued to use wax masters, which were more fragile. "most professional studios, if they were using this cutters at all, were using acetate disks, which are slightly more robust, but apparently J&M were still using wax."
**J&M Studio Acoustics - Small and Acoustically Dead** : J&M Studio suffered from poor acoustics, being too small and acoustically dead, often requiring record companies to add echo to masters during post-production. "It was too small and acoustically dead, and the record companies who received the masters and released them, would often end up adding echo after the fact."
**J&M Studio's Strengths - Matassa and Bartholomew** : Despite its poor acoustics, J&M Studio's success was driven by the exceptional talent of engineer Cosimo Matassa and band leader Dave Bartholomew, whose collaboration with a consistent group of musicians produced influential rock and roll music. "what they did have, was a great engineer in Matassa, and a great band leader, in Dave Bartholomew, and the band he put together for Fat Stomino's first record, would largely work together for the next few years, creating some of the greatest rock and roll music ever made."

# instrument - Drums
## curiosities
**Backbeat Definition** : The backbeat is a rhythmic emphasis on the second and fourth beats of a four-beat bar, often played on drums. "Emphasising the second and fourth beats of the bar, like that."
**Earl Palmer - Premier Drummer of His Era** : Earl Palmer was widely considered the preeminent drummer in America during his active period, known for his exceptional skill and contribution to session work. "Earl Palmer, who was bar-non, the greatest drummer working in America at that time."

# instrument - Bass
## curiosities
**"The Fat Man" (1949) - Rhythmic Structure** : While predominantly lacking the Treheo rhythm, "The Fat Man" features sporadic bass lines that incorporate it, alongside standard boogie bass patterns and root notes, though the bass is often obscured by poor recording quality. "There are odd parts where the bass plays it, but the bass player, who it's really difficult to hear anyway because of the poor sound quality of the recording, and it comes to switch between playing a traheor, playing normal boogie bass lines, and playing just for root notes as crutchits."

# curiosity

**The Sound of the City (1970) - First Rock History Book** : Written by Charlie Gillett, this book was the initial serious attempt to document the history of rock and roll and also established a typology for the music. "the first attempt at a rarely serious history of rock and roll."
**New Orleans Musical Heritage - Early 19th Century** : The musical significance of New Orleans can be traced back to the early 19th century, marked by the emergence of Louis Moreau Gottschalk, considered the first truly great American composer. "We can trace the importance of New Orleans in music, back at least to the early 19th century, and to the first truly great American composer, Louis Marot Gotshawk."
**New Orleans Music Influences - Slave Songs, Caribbean, Brass Bands** : New Orleans music was profoundly influenced by slave songs, Caribbean music due to the city's port status, and post-Civil War brass band music, as military brass instruments became cheaply available. "the music that came up from the Caribbean because of New Orleans status as a port city, and after the Civil War there was also the additional factor of the brass band music."
**"Juncker's Blues" - Quintessential New Orleans Song** : "Juncker's Blues" is identified as the song that best encapsulates the essence of New Orleans music. "if there's one song that sums up New Orleans music more than any other, it's Juncker's Blues."
**"Juncker's Blues" - Prolific Melody** : While "Juncker's Blues" as a song title never gained widespread fame, its core melody was extensively reinterpreted and reused across numerous subsequent recordings by various artists. "Juncker's Blues itself never became a well-known song, but its melody was reused over and over again."
**"Juncker's Blues" Melody - Extensive Covers** : The melody of "Juncker's Blues," originally by Drive 'Em Down Hall, has been extensively covered and adapted under various titles and lyrics by artists ranging from The Clash and Clifton Chenier to Elvis, Dr. John, and Hugh Laurie. "this one melody by a long dead, unknown New Orleans piano player has been performed under various names and with different sets of lyrics by everyone from the clash to the Zydercoa-Cordian player Clifton Cheneyre, by way of Elvis, Dr. John and even Hugh Lorry."
**"The Fat Man" (1949) - First Rock and Roll Record Claim** : Fats Domino's 1949 recording of "The Fat Man," utilizing the "Juncker's Blues" melody, is considered by many to be one of the very first rock and roll records. "the most important recording of it was in 1949, by a New Orleans piano player called Fat Stomino, and in his version it became one of those songs that is often considered to be the first rock and roll record."
**Fats Domino's Early Life - Poverty and Passion for Music** : Fats Domino grew up in significant poverty, and his family owned only a wind-up record player, which broke, but his passion for music led him to devise a way to keep listening. "He grew up in a kind of poverty that's hard to imagine now."
**Recording as a Path to Wealth in Music** : In the era, achieving wealth as a musician, particularly a pianist, was largely dependent on making records, as local celebrity alone was insufficient. "Piano playing wasn't exactly a way to make it rich, unless you got on records."
**Treheo Rhythm - Habaniera Family** : The Treheo is identified as one of several related rhythms belonging to the broader category of Habanera rhythms. "The Treheo is one of a variety of related rhythms that are all known as Habaniera rhythms."
**Habanera Rhythms - Cuban Origin and Dance Influence** : Habanera rhythms, originating from Havana, described music influenced by mid-19th century Cuban dance music, as exemplified in some of Gottschalk's compositions. "from hervana, and was used to describe any music that was influenced by the dance music, dancers like the title of the gut-shock piece, coming out of Cuba in the mid-19th century."
**Clave Rhythm - Habanera Family** : The Clave is presented as another significant rhythm stemming from the Habanera family, described as a "tuba rhythm." "The other major rhythm that came from the Habaniera is the Clarevae, which is a tuba rhythm."
**Clave Rhythm - Structure** : The Clave rhythm is structured with a Treheo in the first bar, followed by a "Bam Bam" pattern in the second. "The first bar is a Treheo, and the second is just a Bam Bam."
**Habanera Rhythm's Entry into New Orleans Music** : The Habanera rhythm was introduced into general New Orleans music through figures like Louis Moreau Gottschalk and other French-speaking Creole individuals residing in the city. "through gut-shock and people like him, French-speaking-queer people living in New Orleans, that rhythm entered New Orleans music generally."
**New Orleans Rhythmic Heritage - Gottschalk to Bartholomew** : A direct line of rhythmic influence, characterized by the New Orleans sound, can be traced from Louis Moreau Gottschalk, through Jelly Roll Morton, to Dave Bartholomew. "From gut-shock to Jelly Roll Morton to Dave Bartholomew, that was the sound of New Orleans travelling across the generations."
**Rhythmic Juxtaposition - The New Orleans Sound** : The distinctive character of the New Orleans sound emerged from juxtaposing the "da da da" Habanera rhythm against other rhythmic patterns. "what really made that rhythm interesting was when you put that da da da up against something else."
**Treheo Rhythm - Beat Emphasis** : The Treheo rhythm emphasizes the first beat, a beat halfway between the second and third, and the fourth beat of the bar. "emphasizing the first, a beat halfway between the two and the three, and the fourth beat."
**Cosimo Matassa - Bridging Racial Divides in Early Rock and Roll** : Cosimo Matassa, like other Italian-American or Jewish figures in early rock and roll, played a crucial role in connecting black and white musical communities during a period when the genre was predominantly black. "acted as a connection between the black and white communities, usually in some back room capacity."
**Cosimo Matassa - Engineer and Studio Owner** : Cosimo Matassa's role in the music industry was primarily as a recording engineer and the owner of J&M Studio. "In Matassa's case, it was as an engineer and studio owner."
**Record Production - Mother Mold Creation** : From the master disk, a "mother" mold is created, serving as a pure negative copy where grooves on the master become ridges on the mother. "a mold called the mother is made of the master. It's a pure negative copy, so that instead of a groove, it has a ridge."
**Record Production - Stamping Process from Mother** : The "mother" mold is subsequently used to stamp out multiple copies of the record until it wears, at which point a new "mother" can be generated from the preserved original master. "That mother is then used to stamp out as many copies as possible of the record before it wears out, at which point you create a new mother from the original master."
**Wax Master Fragility - Playback Limitation** : Wax masters were too delicate to be played directly by a needle, as playback would cause irreparable damage, rendering them unusable for mass production. "A wax master couldn't be played without the needle causing so much damage it couldn't be used as a master."
**Wax Master Playback Dilemma - Dual Cutter Solution** : Due to the fragility of wax masters, studios had two options for quality control: convert the master to a "mother" and hope for the best, or run two disk cutters simultaneously to allow one "safety" copy to be played for immediate sound verification. "You could either get the master made into a mother, and then use the mother to stamp out copies, and just hope they sounded okay, or you could run two disks cutters simultaneously."
**Direct-to-Wax Recording Requirements** : Direct-to-wax recording, without tape or post-production effects, demanded an exceptional engineer with musical intuition, a studio with excellent room acoustics, and highly skilled musicians. "To record like this, mixing directly onto wax with no tape effect, or any way to change anything, you needed a great engineer, with a great feel for music, a great room with a wonderful room sound, and fantastic musicians."
**"The Fat Man" Radio Show - Origin and Hammett's Involvement** : "The Fat Man" was a radio series spun off from "The Thin Man" (based on Dashiell Hammett's novel); while Hammett was credited as a creator, he reportedly had minimal actual involvement. "Hammett was credited as the creator of The Fat Man 2, but he seems to have had almost nothing to do with it."
**Layered Rhythms - Danceability and Complexity** : Despite the intricate layering of different rhythmic patterns, all elements of the Fats Domino/Bartholomew sound maintained a cohesive 4/4 time signature, making them danceable while showcasing astonishing complexity. "There's an astonishing complexity there."
