## albums
**EP (1963) - Self-Released Debut** : This EP was recorded a mere eight days after the new lineup first performed together, self-released and sold at their gigs. Approximately 500 copies were sold, with Alan Price contributing on both piano and melodica. "apparently 500 copies of the EP were sold."
**Animalisms (1966) - First Decca Album** : Released after the group signed with Decca and worked with producer Tom Wilson, John Steele left the group after this album due to financial frustrations. "After their next single, Inside Looking Out, a prism works on collected by the Lomaxes, and the album Animalisms, John Steele left the group tired of not getting any money, and went to work in a shop."
**Animalism (1966) - Second Decca Album** : Confusingly titled, this album was mostly produced by Tom Wilson. ## songs
**Roll Over Pete (Early Repertoire)** : This Big Joe Turner song was often part of the repertoire of the early groups formed by Eric Burden and John Steele, including their trad jazz and later R&B bands. "one of the songs that's often mentioned is being in the repertoire of these early groups, is Roland Pete, the Big Joe Turner song we talked about back in episode 2."
**House of the Rising Sun (1964) - Breakthrough Single** : This song, described as the group's first single, achieved significant chart success, reaching number 21 initially and later number 1 in both the UK and US. Its arrangement was largely based on a radically reworked B-side by Hogi Land, which itself adapted Bob Dylan's version. The Animals' rendition featured a distinctive arpeggiated guitar part by Hilton Valentine and a breakdown section with spoken lyrics over staccato backing, along with a Burt Berns-style call and response ending. "which made number 21."
**House of the Rising Sun (1964) - US Edit Controversy** : While a number 1 hit in both the UK and US, the single version released in the US was severely cut down to 3 minutes with "brutal edits," particularly to the organ solo, which none of the group approved of. The proper, unedited version was subsequently released as an album track everywhere. "All of the version released as a single in the US was cut down to 3 minutes, with some brutal edits, particularly to the organ solo."
**Talking About You (1964) - Unreleased TV Theme** : A Ray Charles song that the TV show "Ready Steady Go" wanted The Animals to record as their theme. The group recorded "House of the Rising Sun" as a B-side during this session, and it was chosen as the A-side instead. "The TV show Ready Steady Go wanted the animals to record a version of the old Ray Charles song, Talking About You, to use as their theme."
**Don't Let Me Be Misunderstood (1965) - Classic Single** : Written by Benny Benjamin and Horace Ott and originally recorded by Nina Simone, The Animals' version is considered a great and classic single, though compared to Simone's orchestral original, it is described as lightweight, rushed, and callow. Nina Simone was reportedly furious at their recording. "It's a great record and is deservedly considered a classic single, but compared to Simone's version, it's lightweight, rushed and calo."
**Bring It On Home To Me (1965) - Last with Alan Price** : A cover of a Sam Cooke song, this was the final single released by The Animals that featured Alan Price as a member. "Don't let me be misunderstood, was followed by a more minor single, a cover of Samcooks bring it on home to me, which would be the last single by the group to feature Alan Price."
**We Gotta Get Out of This Place (1965) - Greatest Single** : Co-written by Barry Mann and Cynthia Weil, this song was originally intended for The Righteous Brothers. The Animals heard Mann's piano demo and recorded their version first, shelving Mann's. The group significantly reworked the melody, lyrics, and completely excised an "overlong bridge," transforming a "decent song into a great one" and "one of the greatest singles of all time," despite Cynthia Weil's strong dislike of their changes. "We got to get out of this place, have been bitten by Barry Mann and Cynthia Wile, and had originally been intended for the righteous brothers, but they decided to have Mann record it himself."
**It's My Life (1965) - Post-Most Single** : This "lesser but listenable single" followed "We Gotta Get Out of This Place." Its release marked the point where The Animals parted ways with producer Mickey Most, as the group grew tired of his attempts to steer them in a more pop direction. "It's My Life, which seemed to be in force a pattern of a great animal single being followed by a melee okay one."
**Inside Looking Out (1966) - Prison Work Song** : This single, a "prison worksong collected by the Lomaxes," was released before the album "Animalisms." John Steele left the group after its release due to financial issues. "After their next single, Inside Looking Out, a prism works on collected by the Lomaxes, and the album Animalisms, John Steele left the group tired of not getting any money, and went to work in a shop."

## curiosities
**Art School Origins in Newcastle** : Like many British bands of the era, The Animals' story began at an art school in Newcastle, where Eric Burden and John Steele first met. Newcastle's isolation as a major city fostered a unique local music scene, limiting musician options and encouraging bands to play within their immediate region. "The story of the animals, like so many of the British bands of this time period, starts at art school, where two teenagers named Eric Burden and John Steele met each other."
**Evolution from Jazz to R&B** : Eric Burden and John Steele initially formed a trad jazz band called The Pagan Jazzmen. With the rise of rock and roll and skiffle, they transformed into The Pagans R&B Band, shifting to a guitar-based lineup with Steele on drums and Burden as lead singer. "Steel and burden, when they met at art school, were both jazz fanatics, and they quickly formed a trad jazz band."
**Alan Price's Integration and Musical Tensions** : Alan Price joined the group on piano and immediately "jelled," despite Eric Burden and John Steele finding his musical tastes "pedestrian" and regarding him as a "lounge player" compared to their own "hipper" R&B and blues preferences. This tension was an ongoing factor within the band. "The other members would always like to say that they didn't like Price either as a person or for his taste in music."
**Backing Sonny Boy Williamson II** : The group, then known as The Alan Price Rhythm and Blues Combo, performed live backing the prominent American blues musician Sonny Boy Williamson II at Newcastle's Club a Go-Go. This performance was recorded and showcased their distinctive electric organ sound, which impressed Graham Bond. "That sound can be heard and alive recording of them a couple of months later, backing the great American blues musician Sonny Boy Williamson II, at the club of Go Go."
**Controversial Name Change** : When the group played London gigs in December 1963, they were billed as The Animals. Members could never agree on how the name originated, and not all of them liked it. "None of the members of the group could ever agree on how they got their new name, and not all of them liked it."
**Mickey Most's First Signing** : The Animals were the first band signed by independent producer Mickey Most upon his return to the UK in 1963, following a successful period producing cover versions in South Africa. Most licensed their records to EMI, and the band moved to London in January 1964. "the animals were his first signing."
**Dispute Over "House of the Rising Sun" Royalties** : The group's manager, Mike Jeffery, allegedly told The Animals that only one name could be credited as arranger for their public domain song "House of the Rising Sun," but that royalties would be shared. Alan Price, however, was the sole credited arranger and the only one to receive songwriting royalties, which became a significant source of contention. "Whatever the truth, Price was the only one who ever got any songwriting royalties from their version of the song, which went to number 1 in the UK and the US."
**Unusual Single Length** : "House of the Rising Sun" was 4 minutes and 20 seconds long, exceeding the customary 3-minute limit for radio singles. Producer Mickey Most was eventually persuaded by Charles Chandler to release the track unedited, a bold decision for the time. "The record was 4 minutes and 20 seconds long, and you just didn't ever release a record that long."
**Dylan's "Stolen Song" Accusation** : The Animals' hit version of "House of the Rising Sun" ironically led to Bob Dylan dropping the song from his own live set, as audiences frequently asked him if he had stolen it from The Animals, providing Dave Van Ronk (from whom Dylan learned the arrangement) "the last laugh." "Incidentally Dave Van Runk had the last laugh at Dylan, who had to drop the song from his own set because people kept asking him if he'd stolen it from the animals."
**Alan Price's Abrupt Departure** : On April 28, 1965, Alan Price abruptly left The Animals on the day of a European tour. Charles Chandler, his flatmate, believed Price's first royalty check for "House of the Rising Sun" arrived that morning, prompting Price to unilaterally decide not to share the money as previously agreed. Chandler did not see Price for 18 months afterward. "Chandler believed until his death that while he was in the shower, Price's first royalty check for arranging House of the Rising Sunhood arrived, and Price had decided then and there that he wasn't going to share the money as agreed."
**Break with Mickey Most** : The Animals eventually severed ties with producer Mickey Most, citing their dissatisfaction with his persistent efforts to push them towards a more pop-oriented sound. They subsequently signed with Decca Records and enlisted Tom Wilson as their new producer. "The group were getting sick of most attempts to make them more poppy."
**Management Fraud and Disappearance of Funds** : The original group ultimately dissolved, discovering they had been extensively defrauded by their management. A tax-saving scheme involving banking their money in the Bahamas resulted in the mysterious disappearance of the bank and, consequently, their funds. "There had been a scheme to bank their money in the Bahamas for tax reasons, in a bank which mysteriously disappeared off the face of the earth."
**Reunions and Later Formations** : The original lineup of The Animals reunited sporadically between 1968 and the early 1980s, with their final performance together in 1983. Eric Burden went on to form "The New Animals" and "Eric Burdon and the Animals." John Steele, Dave Rowberry, and Hilton Valentine reformed a version in the 1990s, with Steele continuing to tour as "The Animals and Friends." "There were a handful of reunions of the original lineup of the group, between 1968 and the early 80s, but they last played together in 1983."

## curiosities
**Contentious Arrangement Acquisition** : In 1962, Bob Dylan recorded Dave Van Ronk's arrangement of "House of the Rising Sun" for his debut album without prior explicit permission, despite Van Ronk's own plans to record it. This led to a brief, angry falling out between the two folk singers. "He had been up at the Columbia Studios with John Hammond doing his first album... 'would it be okay for me to record your arrangement of House of the Rising Sun?' Expletive. 'G's Bobby, I'm going into the studio to do that myself in a few weeks. Can't it wait until your next album?'"
**Chord Simplification** : Due to being a less sophisticated musician than Dave Van Ronk, Bob Dylan simplified the chords of Van Ronk's "House of the Rising Sun" arrangement for his debut album, employing standard rock chords that fit Van Ronk's descending bass line. "Dylan took Van Runk's arrangement, but he wasn't a sophisticated musician as Van Runk, so he simplified the chords."
**Electric Overdub Experiment** : In late 1964, Bob Dylan's producer Tom Wilson experimented by hiring session musicians to overdub new electric backing onto an outtake of "House of the Rising Sun" from Dylan's first album sessions, an unreleased experiment with significant future ramifications. "In late 1964, he hired session musicians to overdub a new electric backing onto an outtake version of House of the Rising Sun, from the sessions from Dylan's first album to see what it would sound like."
**Forced to Drop Song** : Bob Dylan eventually stopped performing "House of the Rising Sun" live because audiences consistently asked him if he had stolen the song from The Animals, an irony that gave Dave Van Ronk "the last laugh." "Dylan, who had to drop the song from his own set because people kept asking him if he'd stolen it from the animals."

# artist - Dave Van Ronk

## songs
**House of the Rising Sun (Arrangement)** : Van Ronk learned this song from a 1953 recording by Halley Wood. He fundamentally rearranged it, changing chords and introducing a descending bass line, mostly in semitones, while maintaining the same chord shape. This "single trick," common in jazz and Baroque but unusual in folk, created interesting chords (e.g., A minor, A minor over G, A minor over F sharp, F) and later influenced hundreds of songs. "He introduced a descending bass line, mostly in semitones, which as Van Runk put it is a common enough progression in jazz but unusual among folk singers."

## curiosities
**Origin of Arrangement** : Dave Van Ronk's influential arrangement of "House of the Rising Sun" originated from a 1938 field recording by Alan Lomax of George Eterner from Kentucky, which Van Ronk learned from a 1953 record by Halley Wood. "Van Runk's version traces back originally to a field recording Alan Lohmach's had made in 1938 of a woman named George Eterner from Kent Tucket."
**New Orleans Prison Theory** : Van Ronk initially believed "House of the Rising Sun" was about a brothel, but later speculated it was actually about the New Orleans women's prison, which he believed bore a carving of a rising sun over its doorway. # curiosity

**British-American Musical Interplay** : The song "House of the Rising Sun" vividly illustrates the reciprocal influence between British and American music in the early 1960s, with its complex lineage tracing from potential English folk roots to an Appalachian country song, then performed by a blues band from northern England who learned it from a Minnesotan folk singer based in New York. "more than any other song we've looked at so far, shows how the influence between British and American music was working in the early 1960s."
**Newcastle's Cultural Isolation** : Despite being a major city, Newcastle's geographical isolation in Great Britain meant that in the 1960s, local bands like The Animals largely performed within their immediate region (Newcastle, Gateshead, Sunderland, Middlesbrough), fostering a unique regional scene due to limited external travel and a restricted pool of musicians. "This means Newcastle is, for a major city, incredibly isolated."
**Van Ronk's Descending Bass Line Trick** : Dave Van Ronk innovated by introducing a descending bass line, primarily in semitones, while holding a consistent chord shape in his "House of the Rising Sun" arrangement. This technique, though common in jazz and Baroque music, was novel in folk, blues, or rock and roll at the time, generating interesting chords (e.g., A minor, A minor over G, A minor over F sharp, F) and subsequently influencing hundreds of songs including "A Whiter Shade of Pale," "Sunny Afternoon," "Go Now," and "Forever." "He introduced a descending bass line, mostly in semitones, which as Van Runk put it is a common enough progression in jazz but unusual among folk singers."
**Multiple Interpretations of "House of the Rising Sun"** : The meaning of "House of the Rising Sun" has been subject to various interpretations. While often perceived as a woman's lament about a brothel, earlier variants were sung by both genders. Dave Van Ronk initially held the brothel theory but later posited it referred to the New Orleans women's prison, which he believed had a rising sun carving over its entrance. "It's often thought, for example, that the song was always sung by women and was about a brothel, but there are lots of variants of it sung by both men and women before it reached its most famous form."
**Public Domain Arranger Royalties** : In the context of public domain songs, crediting an arranger effectively designates them as the songwriter for royalty purposes. This principle fueled a significant dispute within The Animals over "House of the Rising Sun," where Alan Price received all songwriting royalties due to being the sole credited arranger, contrary to an alleged agreement for shared earnings. "when you're recording a public domain song, makes you effectively the songwriter."
**Failed Tax Evasion Scheme** : The original lineup of The Animals ultimately dissolved after discovering their management had defrauded them through a tax evasion scheme. Their money, intended to be banked in the Bahamas, vanished when the associated bank mysteriously disappeared. "There had been a scheme to bank their money in the Bahamas for tax reasons, in a bank which mysteriously disappeared off the face of the earth."
