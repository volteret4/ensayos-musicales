## songs
**You Are My Sunshine (Year Unspecified)** : Roy Orbison's father taught him the chords to this song when he bought Orbison his first guitar at age six. "When he was six years old his father had bought him a guitar and taught him the chords to you are my sunshine and by the age of ten he was already winning talent contests."
**Shake, Rattle and Roll (1954)** : On New Year's Eve 1954, an 8-minute version of this song, performed by Orbison and his band, served as the pivotal moment he decided to commit to rock and roll music forever. "That version of Shake, Rattling Roll which lasted for 8 minutes converted Roy Orbison. When he started playing it, Ruck and Roll was just another form of music but by the end he knew he wanted to play that kind of music forever."
**Ooby Dooby (Year Unspecified)** : Orbison heard this song, written by his classmates Dick Penner and Wade Moore, at university. It became a successful song for The Teen Kings. After an initial rejection by Columbia and a release on Jewel, Sam Phillips voided Orbison's Jewel contract and re-recorded it for Sun, where it reached number 59 on the pop charts and sold around 200,000 copies. "It soon entered the set of the newly named Team Kings and became one of their most successful songs."
**Go Go Go (Year Unspecified)** : This song was the B-side to Orbison's "Ooby Dooby" (Sun version). Co-written by Orbison and The Teen Kings' drummer Billy Pat Ellis, it became a rockabilly classic, later recorded by Jerry Lee Lewis, and was one of the few rockabilly songs Orbison kept in his set lists. "The B side was recorded in a single take and itself became a rockabilly classic. It was co-written by Orbison and the band's drummer, Billy Pat Ellis, but it caused problems."
**Rock House (Year Unspecified)** : The follow-up single to "Ooby Dooby," this song was originally written by Harold Jenkins (Conway Twitty). Orbison substantially reworked Jenkins' demo to earn a co-writing credit. It did not perform well. "Orbison took Jenkins's demo and substantially reworked it, earning himself a co-acting credit."
**Your My Baby (Year Unspecified)** : This song was the B-side to "Rock House." Originally written by Johnny Cash and titled "Little Wollipoga," it was renamed "Your My Baby" for Orbison's version to be more radio-friendly. "That was renamed to the rather more radio-friendly, Your My Baby, for Orbison's version."
**Voice So Long and Gone (Year Unspecified)** : Written by Roy Orbison, this song was recorded by minor Sun artist Warren Smith. It reached the lower reaches of the Hot 100, becoming the first song to earn Orbison serious money since "Ooby Dooby" a year prior. "That reached the lower reaches of the heart 100, and so became the first thing that earned Roy some serious money, since Ubidubi a year earlier."
**Claudette (Year Unspecified)** : Orbison wrote this song about his new wife, Claudette, and pitched it to The Everly Brothers, who decided to record it. Their version sold half a million copies, providing a significant upswing in Orbison's fortunes and earning him substantial money. "He decided to write a song about his new wife, Claudette, which he pitched to the Evely Brothers when they were honourbed together, on which they decided to record."
## curiosities
**Unlikely Rock and Roll Star Persona** : Roy Orbison made an unlikely rock and roll star; he was a quiet, unassuming man who, in his later years, wore dark glasses to conceal his face. His chosen music in later life, though rooted in rock and roll, was far from rebellious. "A quiet, unassuming man who rarely used a note stronger than mercy and wore dark glasses in later years to hide as much of his face as possible."
**Early Musical Aspirations** : Orbison grew up determined to be a singer. At age six, his father gave him a guitar and taught him "You Are My Sunshine," and by ten, he was already winning talent contests. "When he was six years old his father had bought him a guitar and taught him the chords to you are my sunshine and by the age of ten he was already winning talent contests."
**Inspiration from Lefty Frizel** : Seeing country singer Lefty Frizel live was a pivotal moment for Orbison, not just for Frizel's vocal influence, but because seeing Frizel get into a Cadillac after a show made Orbison realize he could make real money from singing. "What really impressed young Roy Orbison though was seeing Frizel after the show getting into a Cadillac. Orbison realised you could make real money just from singing and started to make plans."
**Johnny Cash's Unheeded Career Advice** : Johnny Cash advised Orbison to sing in a lower register and consider changing his name for musical success, advice Orbison never took. Orbison would later joke with Cash about how poor this advice was. "He also advised Orbison that if he wanted to have any success as a musician he should probably start singing in a lower register and maybe change his name. Orbison never took that advice and in later years he would joke with Cash about how terrible his advice was."
**Sam Phillips' Initial Rejection** : When Orbison called Sam Phillips, as advised by Johnny Cash, Phillips' initial response was to tell Orbison to "tell Johnny Cash he doesn't run son records I do" before slamming the phone down. "Phillips responded by saying, tell Johnny Cash he doesn't run son records I do and slamming the phone down."
**Voided Jewel Contract** : After Orbison recorded "Ooby Dooby" for the independent label Jewel, Sam Phillips discovered Orbison was under 21, making the contract void. Phillips then signed Orbison to Sun and secured an injunction against Jewel to prevent further release of their version. "He discovered that Orbison wasn't yet 21 and so the contract he'd signed with Jewel was void. Phillips signed Orbison, got an injunction taken out against Jewel, preventing them from putting out any more copies of the single, only a few hundred ever got released, and quickly went into the studio to record a new version of the song."
**Unique Relationship with Sam Phillips** : Unlike other Sun artists who owed their careers to Phillips' discovery and molding, Orbison had been discovered elsewhere and initially rejected by Phillips. This meant Orbison never felt indebted to Phillips in the same way, creating a distinct dynamic. "That meant that Orbison would never feel as Elvis or Johnny Cash or Carl Perkins did, that he owed his career to Sam Phillips."
**Consistent Performance Style** : Roy Orbison was not an improvisational artist; he meticulously planned and worked out his musical parts, adhering to them precisely in his performances and recordings. "Orbison was not a wild improviser, like many of the artists with whom Phillips worked. He would work out his parts exactly and stick to them."
**Admired Guitar Skills** : Sam Phillips was highly impressed by Orbison's guitar playing, often featuring him on other artists' records and claiming only Jerry Lee Lewis had a better sense of rhythm. Orbison's style mirrored Chuck Berry's, playing both rhythm and lead simultaneously, using bass strings for riffs and fills between vocal lines. "Rather oddly, given Orbison's later career, it wasn't primarily his singing that impressed Phillips, but rather his guitar playing."
**B-Side Royalty Dispute** : Billy Pat Ellis, drummer for The Teen Kings, co-wrote the rockabilly classic "Go Go Go" with Orbison, but was not credited on the label, leading to understandable anger and a dispute over royalties. "While Ellis had co-written the song, he wasn't credited on the label, which understandably caused him to get angry."
**Devastation of Band Desertion** : After "Rock House" failed, The Teen Kings, feeling neglected by Sam Phillips and overshadowed by Orbison, secretly packed their gear and left him during a recording session break. Orbison was devastated, feeling abandoned by friends he'd played with for years just as they began to find success. "He was absolutely devastated. The people he'd come up with as a teenager, the people he'd thought were his friends, had all deserted him."
**Temporary Homelessness and Mentorship** : Following The Teen Kings' departure, Orbison spent much of the next year effectively homeless, touring and staying with Sam Phillips, Johnny Cash, or Carl Perkins when in Memphis. Phillips, concerned for Orbison's well-being, invited him to stay in his home for several months and offered him profound life advice. "For much of the next year, Orbison was essentially homeless. He spent most of his time on tour, but considered Memphis his home base, and stayed with either Sam Phillips, Johnny Cash or Carl Perkins when he was at home."
**Commitment to Songwriting as Principal Art** : While touring with Eddie Cochrane and Gene Vincent, Orbison noticed other artists struggling to find new material beyond their one hit. This inspired him to take his songwriting seriously as his primary artistic focus, ensuring a constant supply of new songs. "And he realised that the only way to ensure he would have a ready supply of new material was to write it himself, and so he started to take his songwriting seriously as his principal art."
**Forfeiture of Songwriting Royalties** : To leave Sun and allow The Everly Brothers to record his song "Claudette" (which was tied to Acuff Rose publishing), Orbison agreed to quit his contract with Sun by assigning all songwriting royalties for his previously recorded songs to Sam Phillips, a deal he resented for the rest of his life. "The deal he made was that Orbison quit his contract and signed with Acuff Rose, but only by signing all the songwriting royalties for the songs he'd already recorded, over to Phillips."
**Post-"Claudette" Upswing and RCA Deal** : Following the success of The Everly Brothers' "Claudette," which sold half a million copies, Orbison received significant earnings, bought a Cadillac, moved to Nashville, and was quickly signed by RCA Records, who viewed him as "the next Elvis." "Roy bought a Cadillac, moved to Nashville, and quickly signed with RCA records, who saw in him the potential to be the next Elvis."
**RCA Mismanagement and Career Setback** : Despite being signed by RCA for his songwriting, Orbison was made to record material, often pop-country ballads chosen by Wesley Rose, that was not his preference and didn't suit his voice. His RCA singles performed worse than his Sun records, leading to him being dropped by the label after the money from "Claudette" ran out. "His RCA singles did even worse than his records on Sun, despite having the push of a major label behind him. Eventually the money from Claudette ran out, and he was dropped by RCA."
**Return to West Texas** : After being dropped by RCA, Orbison sold his Cadillac and moved back to West Texas. His career seemed over, and he faced the prospect of being remembered as a one-hit wonder. "Orbison sold his capillac and moved out of Nashville and back to West Texas. It looked like his career was over, and he would spend his life exactly as he'd hoped he wouldn't, as a musician who'd had one minor hit and never done anything else."

## curiosities
**Band Formation and Early Lineup** : Formed by Roy Orbison and a group of friends in his teens, the band was named after their small town of Wink. It had various lineups before settling on a two-guitar, bass, drums, and electric mandolin configuration with Orbison, Billy Pat Ellis, Jack Caneeley, Johnny Wilson, and James Morrow. "In his teens he and a group of his friends formed a country in western band, the Wink Westerners, named after the small town they lived in. That band had various lineups but it eventually settled into a two guitar, bass, drums and electric mandolin lineup of Orbison, Billy Pat Ellis, Jack Caneeley, Johnny Wilson and James Morrow."
**Local Radio Show and Backing Slim Whitman** : While Roy Orbison was still in school, The Wink Westerners secured their own weekly radio show. Their local popularity led to them being chosen as the backing group for country star Slim Whitman when he came to town. "While Orbison was still in school the band got their own radio show one day a week and became big enough that when the country star Slim Whitman came to town they were chosen as his backing group."
**Renamed The Teen Kings** : Following Roy Orbison's transformative experience with rock and roll on New Year's Eve 1954, The Wink Westerners were quickly renamed The Teen Kings. "The wink westerners were quickly renamed the team Kings."

# label - Sun Records
## curiosities
**Sam Phillips' Mismanagement of Roy Orbison** : Sam Phillips is noted for completely mismanaging Roy Orbison's career, misjudging his abilities, and doing everything wrong, almost destroying Orbison's career before it began. Phillips later admitted he "didn't understand what he had in Roy Orbison until much later" and took the blame for "not bringing Roy to fruition." "Today we're going to talk about one of the very few times that Sam Phillips and Sun records took on someone who would become a massive, massive star and completely mismanaged him, misjudged his abilities totally and did everything completely wrong to the point where he almost destroyed his career before it began."
**Controversial Business Dealings** : Most Sun artists, including Roy Orbison, Johnny Cash, and Carl Perkins, came to believe Sam Phillips treated them poorly in business dealings, alleging insufficient royalty payments, a lack of transparency regarding business information, and a greater interest in new acts than in developing the careers of his signed artists. "Most son artists came to believe that they had been treated badly in business dealings by Phillips, and that he hadn't properly recognised their talent."
**Sam Phillips' Regret** : Sam Phillips later reflected on his handling of Roy Orbison's career, acknowledging his failure to develop Orbison's potential and stating, "I really have to take the blame for not bringing Roy to fruition." "He later said, I really have to take the blame for not bringing Roy to fruition."
**Jack Clement's Role in Orbison's Sun Stint** : As Orbison's success at Sun waned, Sam Phillips increasingly delegated him to assistant Jack Clement. Clement, despite knowing Orbison didn't have the voice for them, insisted on Orbison performing ballads, contributing to Orbison's dissatisfaction and lack of hits. "As Orbison was becoming less successful, Philips increasingly parmed him off on his new assistant, Jack Clement, and Clement insisted on Orbison performing material for which he had no feeling."
