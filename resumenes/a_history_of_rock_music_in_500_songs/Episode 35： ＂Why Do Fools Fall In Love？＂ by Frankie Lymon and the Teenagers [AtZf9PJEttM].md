## songs
**Why Do Fools Fall in Love (1956)** : This song was the group's only major hit, but it became one of the biggest hits of the 1950s and remains almost universally known. It was originally meant to be sung by Herman Santiago, but was rearranged in the studio to feature Frankie Lymon's distinctive falsetto. The song reached number 6 on the pop charts, helped by a massive promotional push from Morris Levy's friend, Alan Freed, and outsold a white cover version by The Diamonds. "The Teenagers only had one really big hit, but it was one of the biggest hits of the 50s and it was a song that is almost universally known to this day."
**I Want You to Be My Girl (Year Unspecified)** : This single followed the success of "Why Do Fools Fall in Love" and performed almost as well, peaking at number 13 on the pop charts. Despite its relative success, subsequent singles did not fare as well for the group. "With the teenagers, followed the success of why the fools fall in love with, I want you to be my girl."

## curiosities
**Formation and Early Names** : The group began when two Black teenagers from New York, Jimmy Merchant and Sherman Garns, left their previous vocal group, The Earth Angels (named after The Penguins' song). They then teamed up with two Latino neighbors, Joe Negroni and Herman Santiago. They initially called themselves The Hermans, later changing to The Coop Devils in honor of The Cadillacs, and then to The Premiers before becoming The Teenagers. "The Teenagers started when two black teenagers from New York, Jimmy Merchant and Sherman Garns, left the vocal group they'd formed... and hooked up with two Latino neighbours, Joe Negroni and Herman Santiago."
**Songwriting Origin from Love Letters** : The group used to practice in the hallway of Sherman Garns' apartment block. A neighbor, annoyed by their repetitive singing, offered them his girlfriend's love letters, some written as poems, suggesting they turn them into songs. This led to the creation of "Why Do Fools Fall in Love" from the phrase "wider bird sing so gay." "The neighbour decided to bring out some love letters his girlfriend had written, some of which were in the form of poems and say to the kids, why don't you turn some of these into songs?"
**Discovery by Richie Barrett** : The group's career changed dramatically when Richie Barrett heard them singing on a street corner. Barrett, known for his 1962 single "Some Other Guy" and then the lead singer of The Valentines, was working as a talent scout and producer for George Goldner at Rama Records, a role akin to Ike Turner for Chess or Jesse Stone for Atlantic. "But everything changed for the group, when Richie Barrett heard them singing on a street corner near him."
**Name Change by George Goldner** : When their debut record was released, it was credited to "The Teenagers featuring Frankie Lymon," rather than The Premiers. George Goldner disliked their prior name and decided to emphasize their youth, particularly that of their new lead singer, Frankie Lymon, as their main selling point. "When the record came out, it wasn't credited to the premieres, but to the teenagers featuring Frankie Leimon."
**Behind-the-Scenes Production** : Much of the high-quality sound on The Teenagers' records was attributed to session saxophone player Jimmy Wright, not the group members or producer George Goldner. Wright ended up doing the arrangements for all of their records and was responsible for the distinctive bass intros performed by Sherman Garns. "Much of the work to make the record sound that good was done, not by the teenagers or by Goldner, but by the Session Saxophone player Jimmy Wright, who ended up doing the arrangements on all of the teenagers' records."
**Commercial Success Despite Cover Versions** : The Teenagers' version of "Why Do Fools Fall in Love" reached number 6 on the pop charts, outperforming an almost immediately released white cover version by the Canadian group The Diamonds, which only went to number 12. This indicated a changing market where the "novelty sound" of a young boy singing in falsetto was more appealing than adult cover artists. "But in a sign of how the times were changing, the Diamond's version of the song only went to number 12, while the teenagers version went to number 6."
**Record Label's Decision to Separate Lymon** : The record label made the unusual decision to promote Frankie Lymon as a solo star, separating him from The Teenagers, rather than maintaining a successful group. The presumed calculation was to create two hit acts, but neither Lymon nor The Teenagers had another hit after the split. "The record label soon decided that Lyman needed to become a solo star, rather than just being the lead singer of the teenagers."
**Post-Lymon Struggles and Lineup Changes** : After Frankie Lymon left, The Teenagers struggled to regain their success. They introduced several new lead singers, including Billy LeBvano, a white kid who sounded like Freddie Houston, and even tried focusing on other original members, performing as "Sherman and the Teenagers." Unable to replicate their original sound, they eventually brought in a female singer, Sandra Doyle, before ultimately splitting up in 1961. "The teenage just didn't fare much better. They went through several new lead singers."
**Later Reunions and Members' Deaths** : The Teenagers briefly reunited with Frankie Lymon in 1965, but with little success. In the 1970s, they reunited again, briefly including Lymon's brother in the lineup. Sherman Garns passed away in 1977, followed by Joe Negroni in 1978. Herman Santiago and Jimmy Merchant continued performing with various iterations of The Teenagers, with Santiago still active today and Merchant remaining with the band until his retirement a few years prior. "The other Teenagers would reunite, with Lyman's brother joining them briefly, in the 70s."
**Exploitation and Lack of Financial Gain** : The story of Frankie Lymon and the Teenagers is a cautionary tale of young people of color achieving massive musical success but making no money from their work due to wealthy businessmen, like Morris Levy, who stole their intellectual property and used mafia connections to prevent legal action. "a story of young people of colour having their work become massively successful and making no money off it because of wealthy businessmen stealing their work."

## curiosities
**Youthful Talent and Charisma** : Frankie Lymon was a very young kid when he joined The Premiers (The Teenagers), initially singing and playing percussion in a Mambo group. Richie Barrett quickly recognized his star potential due to his captivating performance style, which resembled a young James Brown, and his gorgeous falsetto voice, leading to comparisons with Michael Jackson's early career trajectory. "Lyman was a captivating performer, and when you watch video footage of him now, you can't help but think of Michael Jackson, who followed almost exactly the same early career trajectory a decade later."
**Falsetto Innovation in Rock and Roll** : Lymon's distinctive falsetto voice, attributed to his voice not yet having broken, introduced a groundbreaking new timbre to rock and roll lead vocals. While falsetto was present in doo-wop, Lymon's use of it for lead parts was a late addition to "Why Do Fools Fall in Love" (originally intended for Herman Santiago) and profoundly influenced subsequent vocal groups such as The Four Seasons, Jan and Dean, and The Beach Boys. "Limemon, by virtue of his voice not yet having broken, introduced a new tamper into rock and roll lead vocals, and he influenced almost every vocal group that followed."
**"The Big Beat" TV Appearance Controversy** : Lymon's appearance on Alan Freed's TV series, "The Big Beat," resulted in a major controversy that ultimately led to the show's cancellation within weeks. During his performance, Lymon, a Black boy, began dancing with a white girl from the predominantly white studio audience, which was not well-received by Southern network affiliates. This event, likely Lymon's first solo performance, highlighted significant racial tensions in the era. "Frankie Lyman started to dance as well, a black boy dancing with a white girl. This did not go down well at all with the Southern network affiliate, and within a couple of weeks, Fried's show had been taken off the TV."
**Post-Teenagers Career Decline** : After splitting from The Teenagers, neither Lymon nor the group ever achieved another hit. He continued to record for a couple of years, but his subsequent releases were unsuccessful, especially after his voice broke and he lost his distinctive youthful sound. "Now the Lyman, nor the teenagers, would ever have another hit after they split."
**Tragic Later Life and Heroin Addiction** : Lymon's life after The Teenagers was marked by profound tragedy. He married three times, each bigamously, and his only child died two days after birth. He developed a heroin addiction, reportedly stealing from his second wife, Zola Taylor, to support it. Despite a brief reunion with The Teenagers in 1965 and a period in the army where he appeared to get clean, securing a new record deal, he relapsed the night before a scheduled studio session. "Frankie Lyman died, aged just 25... of a heroin overdose in 1968."
**Early Death** : Frankie Lymon died at the age of 25 in 1968 from a heroin overdose. He had been a husband for almost half of his life at the time of his death. "Frankie Lyman died, aged just 25, and a husband for almost half of his life, of a heroin overdose in 1968."
**Complex Estate and Widow Disputes** : Following his death, the rights to "Why Do Fools Fall in Love" became the subject of extensive legal battles in the 1980s, as three separate women claimed to be Lymon's legitimate widow and heir. These included his first wife (married in 1964 while she was still married to someone else), Zola Taylor (who claimed a bigamous marriage a year later but lacked evidence), and a third wife (married bigamously in 1967). The third wife eventually won these legal battles and now controls his legacy. "In the 1980s, three separate women claimed to be Lyman's widow, and thus his legitimate heir."

# artist - George Goldner

## labels
Tico Records
Rama Records
G Records

## curiosities
**Club Owner and Latin Music Pioneer** : Goldner began his career in the music industry as the owner of popular nightclubs featuring Latin music. The success of these clubs led him to establish Tico Records, a label dedicated to Latin recordings, notably including early works by Tito Puente. "When Goldner had started in the music industry, it had been as the owner of a chain of night clubs which featured Latin music."
**Transition to R&B and New Labels** : The Mambo boom attracted many Black teenagers to Goldner's Latin clubs, which in turn piqued his interest in their music. This led him to found Rama Records for R&B singles, followed by G Records, which he named after "G" by The Crows, a successful record released on Rama. "He started first Rama Records, as a label for R&B singles, and then G Records, named after the most successful record that had been put out on Rama, G, by the Cros."
**Gambling Debts and Morris Levy's Acquisition** : Goldner had a business partner, Morris Levy, who owned 50% of his record companies. Goldner accumulated significant gambling debts, which he paid off by selling his record labels. Consequently, Levy eventually owned all of Goldner's companies, including the rights to songs Goldner was credited for. "Levy owned 50% of Goldeners record companies, and would soon vote at own all of them, as Goldener accumulated more gambling debts, and used his record labels to pay them off."
**Music-Oriented Producer** : Despite his later financial troubles and business dealings, George Goldner was genuinely concerned with the music itself and had a consistent track record of producing hits. For instance, he had just produced "You Baby You" for The Cleftones around the time The Premiers (The Teenagers) signed with him. "And Goldner was someone who was actually concerned with the music, and who had been producing hits consistently for the last few years."
**Interest in Latin Talent and Frankie Lymon** : Goldner was initially intrigued by The Premiers (The Teenagers) when Richie Barrett presented them, partly because two of the members were Latino, aligning with Goldner's love for Latin music. He quickly identified Frankie Lymon's potential to become a major star. "But he quickly latched on to the potential of Frankie Lyman as a star."
**Uncredited Co-writer for "Why Do Fools Fall in Love"** : George Goldner was originally credited as a co-writer for "Why Do Fools Fall in Love," although he did not contribute to its actual writing. He was, however, present in the studio during its recording. His credited rights to the song were later acquired by Morris Levy when Levy bought out Goldner's company holdings. "George Goldner was originally credited as Lyman's co-writer, and of course Goldner had never voted either. But at least he was in the studio when it was recorded."

# artist - Morris Levy

## labels
Roulette

## curiosities
**Mafia Connections and Artist Exploitation** : Morris Levy is described as one of the worst figures in music history, a terrible human being and a con artist who leveraged his mafia connections to ensure that artists he exploited would never dare to sue him, valuing their lives over legal recourse. This made him a particularly dangerous force for young, unprivileged artists. "Maris Levy was a terrible human being and a con artist, so someone who used his mafia connections to ensure that the artist's seripped off would never even think of suing him, because they valued their lives too much."
**Founding Birdland and Music Publishing** : In the 1940s, Levy founded Birdland, the world-famous jazz club. When ASCAP sought performance royalties for songwriters, Levy immediately recognized the profit potential in music publishing. He subsequently established his own publishing company, Patricia Music, and record label, Roulette, specifically to exploit artists' intellectual property for long-term gain. "Levy had been the founder of Birdland, the world famous jazz club, in the 1940s, but when ASCAP came to him... Levy had immediately seen the possibilities in music publishing."
**Attempt to Trademark "Rock and Roll"** : Driven by his desire to monetize intellectual property, Levy collaborated with his friend Alan Freed in an attempt to trademark the phrase "rock and roll." Their aim was to force any record labeled as such to pay them for its usage, but the term spread too rapidly to be successfully enforced. "Indeed, Levy was so keen to make money after UBS intellectual property, that he actually formed a company with his friend Alan Fried, which attempted to trademark the phrase rock and roll."
**Threats and Control Over Artists** : Levy's methods involved extreme intimidation; there are multiple reports of artists receiving death threats, being forced to abandon their careers, or moving thousands of miles away to escape his revenge if they defied him. This control made him a formidable figure, especially against vulnerable, teenage Black artists. "There are several people who report death threat, or having to give up their careers, or suddenly move thousands of miles away from home, to avoid Levy's revenge on artists who didn't do exactly what he said."
**Acquisition of George Goldner's Companies** : Levy initially owned 50% of George Goldner's record companies. As Goldner accumulated significant gambling debts, he used his record labels to pay them off, leading Levy to eventually acquire full ownership of all Goldner's companies, including any associated song rights. "Levy owned 50% of Goldeners record companies, and would soon vote at own all of them, as Goldener accumulated more gambling debts, and used his record labels to pay them off."
**Uncredited Co-writer for "Why Do Fools Fall in Love"** : Levy became the legal co-writer of "Why Do Fools Fall in Love" by acquiring George Goldner's credited rights to the song, despite never having heard it until it had already become a massive hit. He thus profited significantly from a song he had no part in creating. "But when Levy bought out Goldner's holdings in his companies, he also bought out his rights to songs he was credited for, so Levy became the legal co-writer of Why the Fools Fall in Love."
**Role in Song Promotion** : Levy's close friend, Alan Freed, played a crucial role in the success of The Teenagers' "Why Do Fools Fall in Love" by providing a substantial promotional push for the record. "helped by a massive push from Marvis Levy's good friend Alan Fried."

# genre - Mambo

## curiosities
**Mambo Craze of the 1950s** : Mambo was a significant musical trend that gained popularity in the pop markets during the 1950s, particularly around 1953-1954. Named after Perez Prado's song "Mambo Jambo," it was part of a broader category of "Exotica" music from various Latin American and Caribbean cultures, and for a period, it was considered a strong contender to shape the future of music alongside rock and roll. "This was a craze named after a song by the Cuban band leader Peres Prado, Mambow Jambow."

# genre - Rock and Roll

## curiosities
**Attempt to Trademark the Term** : Morris Levy and his friend Alan Freed formed a company with the intent to trademark the phrase "rock and roll." Their objective was to compel any record labeled as such to pay them for the privilege. Fortunately, the term gained widespread usage so rapidly that enforcement of the trademark became impossible, leading to its generalization. "attempted to trademark the phrase rock and roll, on the basis that this way any records that came out labeled as such would have to pay them for the privilege."
**Temporary Decline in the 1950s** : In the mid-1950s, genuine rock and roll experienced a temporary decline as major record labels actively fought back against its independent origins. During this period, TV shows like Alan Freed's "The Big Beat" often featured "pre-packaged major label acts," typically named "Bobby," reflecting the industry's attempt to sanitize and control the genre. "By this point, real rock and roll was already in a temporary decline as the major labels fought back."

# genre - Doo-wop

## curiosities
**Role of Falsetto in Vocal Blend** : While falsetto had always been an element of the doo-wop vocal blend, it was typically a minor component. Lead vocals were generally delivered in a smooth, high tenor, rarely reaching the truly high notes. Frankie Lymon's voice, not yet broken, revolutionized this by introducing a new, prominent falsetto timbre into lead vocals, influencing subsequent vocal groups. "Falsetto had, of course, always been a part of the do-wop vocal blend, but it had been a minor part up to this point."

# curiosity

**White Cover Versions of Black Artists' Songs** : It was a prevalent trend in the 1950s for white artists, such as The Diamonds, to release cover versions of songs by Black artists almost immediately after the original, often emulating the successful formula of groups like The Crew Cuts for white remakes of doo-wop tracks. However, the superior chart performance of Frankie Lymon's original "Why Do Fools Fall in Love" over The Diamonds' cover signaled a shift in the market where original Black artists were gaining more recognition. "Again, as with so many of these records, there was a white cover version that came out almost immediately."
**British Cover Versions Due to US Distribution Gaps** : A common practice in Britain, extending well into the 1960s, involved local singers like Alma Cogan recording cover versions of American rock and roll and R&B hits. This was primarily a consequence of most US record labels lacking direct distribution in the UK, meaning British audiences frequently accessed popular American songs through these native interpretations. "This sort of thing was common in Britain, well into the 60s, as most US labels didn't have distribution in the UK."
**Rock and Roll vs. Racism in Media** : The appearance of Frankie Lymon, a Black artist, dancing with a white girl on Alan Freed's television program, "The Big Beat," sparked a significant controversy. This event, perceived as a challenge to racial segregation norms, led to a backlash from Southern network affiliates and the subsequent cancellation of Freed's show within weeks, highlighting the intense racial tensions and resistance to integration prevalent in 1950s American pop culture. "Lyman inadvertently caused another crisis in the ongoing battle of rock and roll versus racism."
**Exploitation of Young Artists of Color in Early Rock** : The experience of Frankie Lymon and The Teenagers exemplifies a broader pattern of exploitation in early rock music, where young artists of color achieved massive commercial success but were systematically denied financial compensation. Wealthy businessmen, notably Morris Levy, utilized their influence and even mafia connections to appropriate artists' work and prevent them from seeking legal recourse. "a story of young people of colour having their work become massively successful and making no money off it because of wealthy businessmen stealing their work."
