## albums
**Moon Dog / LSD25 (Year Unknown, single)** : This record, with "Moon Dog" as the A-side and "LSD25" as the B-side, is considered historically significant as it signaled a huge change in musical culture. Despite the mystery surrounding the band's lineup and conflicting accounts, the B-side, "LSD25," is unequivocally the first rock and roll record to mention LSD, making it a distant ancestor of psychedelic music. "Moon Dog" was a local hit but sold poorly outside Southern California. There were follow-ups by different lineups of The Gamblers, but the band eventually ceased to exist. "The single Moon Dog backed by LSD25 by the gamblers is one of those records."

## songs
**Moon Dog (Year Unknown)** : Written by Derry Weaver, this song was the A-side of The Gamblers' single. It became a local hit in Southern California, though it did not achieve significant sales elsewhere. Nick Vene (Renee), who owned the rights to the master tape and publishing, later used his position at Capitol Records to include "Moon Dog" on the compilation album "Golden Gasses," which featured major acts like Sam Cooke and The Four Creps, thereby exposing the song to a wider audience. This led to covers by artists like The Ventures, The Surfaris, and The Beach Boys. Larry Taylor's brother, Mel Taylor, who was the drummer for The Ventures, might have helped bring the track to their attention. Nick Vene, The Beach Boys' producer, may also have influenced their cover, and Derry Weaver is credited on some session sheets for The Beach Boys' version, though this is debated as a potential credit inflation by Vene. Historians of the surf music genre generally point to "Moon Dog" as the first surf music record. "Mundoag was a local hit, but sold nothing anywhere outside southern California."
**LSD25 (Year Unknown)** : This song is the B-side of The Gamblers' single and holds the distinction of being, without any doubt, the first rock and roll record to mention LSD. This makes it a distant ancestor of psychedelic music. Co-written by Derry Weaver and Larry Taylor, both musicians later stated they knew nothing about the drug, claiming they had simply read a magazine article about "acid" and liked the name. Bruce Johnston, a likely member, held a strong anti-drug stance and never indulged. "That is, without any doubt whatsoever, the first rock and roll record to mention LSD, and so in its way a distant ancestor of psychedelic music."

## curiosities
**Conflicting Lineup Claims** : The exact lineup of The Gamblers for the "Moon Dog" / "LSD25" single is shrouded in mystery and subject to significant dispute. While the record features two guitarists, bass, drums, and keyboards, as many as 15 people have been credited over the years. Nick Renee claimed the band was originally "Nick and the Gamblers" with himself, Bruce Johnston, Sandy Nelson, Larry Taylor, James Burton, and Richie Polador engineering, with Kim Fowley involved. Kim Fowley, unusually, denied any involvement. Elliot Ingber stated the group consisted of himself, Larry Taylor, Derry Weaver, Howard Hirsch, and Rod Schaefer. Bruce Johnston had no memory of the record. Given Derry Weaver's credit as writer for "Moon Dog" and the existence of his previous band The Moon Dogs, Elliot Ingber's list is considered mostly correct, with Bruce Johnston likely contributing wordless harmonies, leading to a guessed lineup of Derry Weaver, Larry Taylor, Elliot Ingber, Bruce Johnston, and either Rod Schaefer or Sandy Nelson. "I have seen 15 people credited as having been members of the group that recorded the track."
**Mystery and Unreliable Accounts** : Details about The Gamblers are largely mysterious, pieced together from fragmented and often contradictory recollections from participants. Many individuals involved are known for credit theft, while others were jobbing musicians who, decades later, were unsure if they even played on the record. The host's own book, "California Dreaming," contains errors regarding this single. "Unfortunately everything about the gamblers is shrouded in mystery."

## curiosities
**Interracial Vocal Group** : The Debarrens, formed at University High School in Los Angeles in the late 1950s, were one of the few interracial vocal groups of their time. The group's lineup included a Black lead singer (Chuck Steel), a Japanese tenor (Wally Yagi), two Jewish members (Arnie Ginsburg, John Saligman), and two white members (Jan Berry, Dean Torrance). "And there was a vocal group at the school called Debarrens, one of the few interracial vocal groups around at the time."
**Early Formation and Split** : The Debarrens played several gigs, initially backed by instrumentalists Bruce Johnston (piano), Sandy Nelson (drums), and Dave Shostack (saxophone), all fellow University High students. The group gradually split due to parental pressure for members to focus on school or personal interests. Sandy Nelson was eventually fired by Jan Berry for playing his drums too loudly, leading to Nelson's prophetic statement about having a hit record "only drums." "This group played several gigs together, but slowly split apart as people's mothers wanted them to concentrate on school, or they got cars that they wanted to fix up."
**"Jenny Lee" Demo and Jan & Arnie Formation** : The Debarrens recorded a demo tape of a song about a local stripper, "Jenny Lee." Following this, Dean Torrance enlisted in the army reserves. While in basic training, he heard a familiar-sounding record on the radio credited to "Jan and Arnie." Jan Berry claimed the original tape had problems and he and Arnie Ginsburg had re-recorded it, leading to its discovery by Joe Lubin of Arwin Records and the formation of Jan and Arnie. "Debarrens caught a demo tape of a song about a prominent local stripper named Jenny Lee."

# artist - Jan and Arnie
## members
Jan Berry
Arnie Ginsburg

## labels

## songs
**Jenny Lee (Year Unknown)** : This song was originally recorded as a demo by The Debarrens about a local stripper. According to Jan Berry, he and Arnie Ginsburg re-recorded it due to issues with the original tape. Joe Lubin, Vice President of Arwin Records, heard an acetate of this re-recording and decided to release it, overdubbing extra instruments. The single became a significant hit, reaching number three on the charts, placing them just behind The Elderly Brothers at number one and Shab Wully (with "The Purple People Eater") at number two. Dean Torrance, in army basic training, was surprised to hear the song credited to Jan and Arnie and believed he could still hear his own voice on the track. "At one point, the elderly brothers were at number one in the charts. Number two was Shab Wully, with the purple people leader, and number three was Jan and Arnie with Jenny Lee."

## curiosities
**Discovery by Joe Lubin** : After Jan Berry and Arnie Ginsburg re-recorded "Jenny Lee," Jan took an acetate copy to a friend's party. Joe Lubin, Vice President of Arwin Records (owned by Marty Melcher, Doris Day's husband), happened to hear it from another room while Jan was having the acetate cut. Lubin was impressed and promised to make Jan and Arnie "bigger than the Elderly Brothers," eventually overdubbing instruments and releasing the track. "Joe Lubin overdubbed extra instruments on the track and released it."
**Dean Torrance's Army Enlistment** : Dean Torrance, originally part of The Debarrens, enlisted in the army reserves for six months of basic training to avoid a longer draft. While in basic training, he heard "Jenny Lee" on the radio, credited to Jan and Arnie, and was convinced he could hear his own voice, leading him to regret his military choice. "And Dean Torrance was off in the army, regretting his choices."

They frequently had a young blues guitarist named Henry Vestine sit in with them and often played at parties organized by University High student Kim Fowley. **Early LSD Exposure** : Henry Vestine, a frequent guitarist for The Sleepwalkers, had already started taking LSD during this period, though the other band members did not indulge. This indicates an early connection between LSD use and musicians within this circle, preceding its wider cultural impact. "Often a young blues player called Henry Vestine, who had already started taking LSD at this time, though none of the other band members indulged."
**Witnessing John Dolphin's Murder** : On February 1, 1958, Bruce Johnston, Dave Shostack, and Sandy Nelson went to see John Dolphin, owner of Dolphins of Hollywood, hoping to secure a record deal. While waiting, they encountered Percy Ivy, who confronted Dolphin about money owed, leading to a heated argument where Ivy shot and killed Dolphin. Dave Shostack was also hit in the leg. Johnston comforted the dying Dolphin, while Nelson, upon returning after seeking help, found Johnston trying to humor Ivy by negotiating a record deal. "Ivy pulled out a gun and shot Dolphin. One shot also hit Shostak in the leg."
**Conflicting Accounts with Kip Tyler** : There is significant confusion regarding Kip Tyler's claimed past with The Sleepwalkers. Some articles state Tyler was the lead singer of "The Sleepwalkers, the toughest rock-and-roll band in the school" at Union High School before forming The Flips, and that he was born in 1929. This creates a chronological paradox, suggesting either two distinct bands named The Sleepwalkers a decade apart, or that Tyler was still in high school at 28, or a deep factual error in these accounts. "So either there were two bands at Union High School, a decade apart, called The Sleepwalkers, one of which was a rock-and-roll band before the term had been coined, or Tyler was still at High School age 28, or someone is deeply mistaken somewhere."

**LSD-25 Discovery (April 16, 1943)** : A research scientist in Zurich, Albert Hoffman accidentally touched a tiny speck of lysergic acid diethylamide tartrate (LSD-25) at a pharmaceutical lab on April 16, 1943, leading to a curious initial experience. Three days later, on April 19, 1943, he intentionally took a tiny dose to experiment on himself, experiencing vivid hallucinations, including believing his neighbor was a witch and feeling poisoned. ## curiosities
**Credit Theft and Deception** : Nick Renee, generally credited as the producer of "Moon Dog" / "LSD25" by The Gamblers, for years falsely claimed that Derry Weaver (credited co-composer and lead guitarist) was a pseudonym for himself. When confronted with evidence, Renee admitted Weaver was real but still maintained the pseudonym claim. He further alleged Weaver had died in a car crash, allowing Renee to use Weaver's Social Security number to fraudulently claim extra money as a staff producer. This story was disproven by Renee's death in 1998 and Weaver's actual death in 2013, but Renee's lies went unchallenged for years due to Weaver only giving one interview in 2001. "Nick Renee, who is generally credited as the producer of this record, said for years that Derry Weaver, the credited co-compose with the song and the person who is generally considered to have played Lee Gitarlet, was a pseudonym for himself."
**Production Role at World Pacific Records** : Nick Renee secured a production role at World Pacific Records, where he intended to assemble another studio group, which led to some of the conflicting accounts regarding The Gamblers' formation. "Nick Renee had got a production role at World Pacific Records, and he wanted to put together yet another studio group."
**Exploitation of Moon Dog Rights** : After taking a job at Capitol Records, Nick Vene (Renee) used his position and ownership of the master tape and publishing rights for The Gamblers' "Moon Dog" to include the minor hit on the Capitol compilation album "Golden Gasses." This move significantly exposed the song to a much wider audience and led to covers by other major artists. "And Vene doing that suddenly meant that Mundoag became very important indeed."
**Beach Boys Producer and Credit Padding** : Nick Vene was the producer for The Beach Boys. It has been claimed that Derry Weaver played on The Beach Boys' version of "Moon Dog," and Weaver is credited on session sheets. However, nobody involved in the session has confirmed his presence, leading to speculation that Vene may have simply listed a friend's name to claim extra money. "Nick Vene was the Beach Boys producer. Indeed, some have claimed that Derri Weaver played on The Beach Boys version."

**Albert Hoffman's LSD-25 Experience (April 19, 1943)** : After accidentally touching a chemical three days prior, research scientist Albert Hoffman deliberately took a tiny dose of lysergic acid diethylamide tartrate (LSD-25) on April 19, 1943, to observe its effects. He experienced profound hallucinations, including believing his neighbor was a witch and feeling poisoned, followed by "unprecedented colours and plays of shapes" and "Fantastic Images" behind his closed eyes. This was the first intentional human ingestion of LSD. "Three days later he decided to experiment on himself and took a tiny dose of the chemical to see if anything happened."
**Medical Research of Dallicid (1950s)** : Throughout the 1950s, LSD-25, under the trade name Dallicid (coined by Sandos), was the subject of much medical research. It was made available as an experimental drug for psychiatrists and psychologists to investigate unusual states of consciousness, help them understand psychotic symptoms, investigate neurochemistry, and showed promise as a treatment for alcoholism and various psychiatric illnesses. "throughout the 1950s it was the subject of much medical research under the trade name Sandos came up with for it, Dallicid."
**LSD-25 Renamed (1960s)** : While known as Dallicid in the 1950s, the chemical became more widely known as LSD25 in the 1960s. "But in the 60s it became better known as LSD25."
