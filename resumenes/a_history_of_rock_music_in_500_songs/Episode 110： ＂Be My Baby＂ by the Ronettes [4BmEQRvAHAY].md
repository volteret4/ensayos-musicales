## songs
**I Want a Boy (August 1961)** : The very first single released by the group, then known as Ronnie and the Relatives, through Colpix Records. It was not a commercial success and failed to chart. Decades later, President Clinton specifically asked Ronnie to sign an album featuring these early Colpix recordings in 1997. "The first single by Raleigh and the relatives was not a success. I want a boy came out in August 1961 and didn't chart."
**I'm Going to Quit While I'm Ahead** : The second single released by Ronnie and the Relatives on Colpix Records. Like its predecessor, this song also failed to achieve commercial success or chart. "And nor did their second. I'm going to quit while I'm ahead."
**Why Don't Their Letters Fall in Love (unreleased by Ronettes)** : This song, written by Jeff Barry and Ellie Greenwich, was The Ronettes' first recording for Phil Spector. Spector chose not to release it at the time, deeming it good but not a "number one" hit. It was later released as a solo record by Ronnie under the name "Veronica." "He explained to her that it was a good record, but it wasn't the number one, and he was still working on their first number one record."
**The Twist (cover)** : A cover of a then-current dance hit, recorded by The Ronettes for Phil Spector. However, Spector released this track as an album cut on an album by The Crystals, rather than under The Ronettes' name. "Their next few recordings were covers of then current dance hits like The Twist."
**The Wah-Watusi (cover)** : Another cover of a popular dance hit, recorded by The Ronettes for Phil Spector. This recording featured Nedra Talley on lead vocals, one of the rare instances where a Ronette other than Ronnie took the lead. Like "The Twist," it was released as an album track on a Crystals' album. "And The Wawa 2C, one of the few times that one of the other Ronettes took the lead rather than as Nedra sang lead."
**Be My Baby** : The group's breakout hit and first number one, primarily written by Jeff Barry and Ellie Greenwich, with a likely contribution to the verse-chorus transition from Phil Spector. Ronnie recorded the lead vocals over three days at Gold Star Studios in Los Angeles, while backing vocals were provided by The Blossoms, Sonny Bono, and Cher, not the other Ronettes. The track is famously characterized by Hal Blaine's simple yet powerful drum intro, recorded with a distinctive sound by engineer Larry Levine. "The song that eventually became the group's first hit, Be My Baby, was mostly written by one of the many husband and wife songwriting teams that had developed at the Royal Building, Jeff Barry and Dalley Gvennich."
**Baby I Love You** : This song, co-written by Jeff Barry, Ellie Greenwich, and Phil Spector, was the follow-up single to "Be My Baby." Ronnie recorded the lead vocals in California while her cousin Elaine substituted for her on Dick Clark's Caravan of Stars tour, a situation Ronnie later recognized as Spector's attempt to isolate her from the group. "Ronnie recorded another Barry, Greenwich and Spectre song, Baby I Love You."
**The Best Part of Breaking Up (1964)** : One of three top 40 hits The Ronettes achieved in 1964, though it was described as a "mediocre record." "Nothing was as big as being my baby, but they had three more top 40 hits that year with two mediocre records, the best part of Breaking Up..."
**Do I Love You? (1964)** : Another of the three top 40 hits for The Ronettes in 1964, also characterized as "mediocre." The song was co-written by the team of Pete Andreoli and Vinnie Poncia. "Co-written by the team of Pete Andrew Oly and Vinnie Pontier..."
**Walking in the Rain (1964)** : This "magnificent" song, written by Barry Mann and Cynthia Weil, became a top 40 hit for The Ronettes in 1964 and was considered a return to form for the group. "and then a return to form with the magnificent Walking in the Rain written by Barryman and Cynthia Wile."
**(unnamed 1966 single)** : The only single released by The Ronettes in 1966, a song by Barry and Greenwich. It only managed to reach number 100 on the charts for a single week, signaling a decline in their commercial success under Phil Spector's control. "the one single they released in 1966, a return to songs by Barry and Greenwich, only made number 100 for one week."

## albums

## curiosities
**Origin as Ronnie and the Relatives** : The group began when Ronnie Bennett, inspired by Frankie Lymon, formed a vocal group with friends and relatives. At their debut at a Harlem Apollo talent show, the male lead singer suffered stage fright, prompting Ronnie to step forward and take over the lead vocal, impressing the notoriously hostile audience and leading to the formation of a smaller, regular performing group. "Ronnie stepped forward and took over the lead vocal, and the group went down well enough, even with the Apollo's notoriously hostile audience, that a smaller group of them decided to start performing regularly together."
**President Clinton's Fan Moment (1997)** : Despite their early singles on Colpix Records being commercially unsuccessful, Ronnie Spector met President Clinton in 1997, who specifically requested she sign an album of those initial recordings. "when Ronnie met President Clinton in 1997, he asked her to sign a record, and specifically got her to sign an album of those early recordings for Colpix."
**Accidental Peppermint Lounge Gig** : To circumvent age restrictions at the Peppermint Lounge during the height of the Twist craze, the underage girls dressed alike to appear older. The club manager mistook them for booked dancers who hadn't arrived, instructing them to perform, which inadvertently secured them a regular gig dancing behind Joey Dee and the Starliters. "As they were all dressed the same, the club's manager assumed they were the dancers he'd booked, who hadn't shown up."
**Ronnie's Impromptu Peppermint Lounge Vocal** : During their accidental gig at the Peppermint Lounge, when Joey Dee and the Starliters played "What Did I Say"—a favorite of Ronnie's—she danced closely with lead singer David Pregati, who then handed her the microphone as a joke, and she took over the song, solidifying their regular performance spot. "He handed her the mic as a joke, and she took over the song."
**Name Change to The Ronettes** : Inspired by other girl groups like The Bobbettes and The Marvelettes, Ronnie and her mother suggested changing the group's name from "Ronnie and the Relatives" to "Ron-Dettes," which they then shortened to The Ronettes. "Inspired by the Bobette and the Marvelette, Ronnie and her star's mother suggested changing the group's name. She suggested the run-debt, and they dropped the day, becoming the run-ette."
**Florida Segregation Experience** : While performing at a second Peppermint Lounge venue in Florida with Joey Dee and the Starliters, The Ronettes, as light-skinned mixed-race women, were mistakenly treated as "tanned white women" and served in restaurants that subsequently refused service to their darker-skinned mothers, exposing them firsthand to the realities of segregation. "That trip to Florida gave them two very different experiences. The first was that they got to see segregation first hand for the first time, and they didn't like it..."
**Meeting Murray the K** : During their Florida trip, The Ronettes met Murray the K, New York's most popular DJ, who was impressed by their dancing. He offered them a performing spot on his regular shows at the Brooklyn Fox Theatre, which they eagerly accepted, being New York residents themselves. "Murray was down in Florida for a holiday, and was impressed enough by the girls dancing that he told them if they were ever in New York, and wanted a spot on one of his regular shows at the Brooklyn Fox Theatre, they should let him know."
**Developing Signature Look** : Performing at the Brooklyn Fox Theatre, The Ronettes developed their iconic and highly popular look, which included huge hair piled high, heavy mascara, and tight skirts designed to show off their legs. This style was inspired by street fashion, distinct from other girl groups of the era. "It was at these shows as well that they developed the look that they would become famous with, huge hair piled up on top of their heads, tons of mascara, and tight skirts lit to show their legs."
**1963 New Year's Resolution** : Despite their popularity on stage and the backing of DJ Murray the K, The Ronettes' records on Colpix were not selling. Believing the label was the problem, they resolved in 1963 to seek out Phil Spector to produce them, given his growing reputation as a hitmaker. "They were going to get Phil Specter to produce them."
**Auditioning for Phil Spector** : Ronnie directly called Philles Records to reach Phil Spector, who surprisingly agreed to talk to her, revealing he was familiar with their Brooklyn Fox performances. At their audition, Spector stopped Ronnie during her rendition of "Why Do Fools Fall in Love," declaring, "That is the voice I've been looking for." "It only took her getting to the second line of the song before spec the elder to stop. That is the voice I've been looking for."
**Spector's Concealed Marriage** : While working with Phil Spector, Ronnie became romantically involved with him. During a writing session at his apartment, she discovered women's shoes and, upon asking, Spector falsely claimed they belonged to his sister, concealing his marital status. "Spectre hadn't told her he was married, and claimed to her when she asked that they belong to his sister."
**Elaine Substitutes for Ronnie on Tour** : After "Be My Baby" became a hit, The Ronettes were invited on Dick Clark's Caravan of Stars tour. However, Phil Spector insisted Ronnie remain in California to record the follow-up, so her cousin Elaine filled in for the first few weeks of the tour. Ronnie later realized this was Spector's deliberate attempt to isolate her from the group and her family. "so the girls' cousin Elaine filled in for her for the first couple of weeks of the tour, while Ronnie recorded another Barry, Greenwich and Spectre song, Baby I Love You."
**1964 UK Tour and Relationships** : During a brief tour of the UK in early 1964, The Ronettes developed friendships with both The Beatles and their support act, The Rolling Stones. Ronnie experienced a "brief chaste flirtation" with John Lennon, while Estelle had a "little more" with George Harrison. Phil Spector even sent a telegram attempting to forbid The Rolling Stones from spending time with the group. "Soon after this, the group travelled to the UK for a brief tour in early 1964, drawing which they became friendly with the Beatles, Ronnie had a brief chaste flirtation with John Lennon, and Estelle something a little more with George Harrison."
**Spector Sabotaging Career** : As Phil Spector became increasingly erratic and controlling, he recorded numerous "great records" with The Ronettes but inexplicably refused to release them, claiming they weren't "quite right." Ronnie later understood this was likely a deliberate strategy to sabotage their career and keep her exclusively to himself. "he recorded many great records with the Runette, which he refused to release, claiming they weren't quite right. Ronnie has later realised that he was probably trying to sabotage their career, so he could have her all to himself..."
**1966 Beatles Tour Invitation and Substitute** : In 1966, The Ronettes were invited to be the support act for The Beatles' last-ever tour. Phil Spector once again prevented Ronnie from participating, insisting she needed to be in the studio, leading to Elaine substituting for her, much to The Beatles' disappointment. No material from these studio sessions was ever released. "Also in 1966, the Runettes were invited by the Beatles to be their support act on their last ever tour, but once again Spectre insisted that Ronnie couldn't go because she needed to be in the studio, so Elaine substituted for her again..."
**Group Disbandment (1967)** : The Ronettes officially broke up in 1967, following a period of declining commercial success and Phil Spector's increasing control over Ronnie's career. "The group broke up in 1967..."
**Royalty Lawsuit (1998-2002)** : In 1998, The Ronettes initiated a lawsuit against Phil Spector for unpaid royalties, having collectively received only $14,482.30 from all their hit records, an amount equivalent to a single 1964 royalty payment. Spector counter-argued he was owed money due to recording costs. After multiple appeals, each group member eventually received approximately $500,000 in 2002, which was still considered a small fraction of their true earnings. "By 1998, when the Vanette finally sued Specter for unpaid royalties, they had earned between them a total of $14,482.30 in royalties from all their hit records, the amount that came from a single 1964 royalty payment."
**Rock and Roll Hall of Fame Induction (2007)** : Phil Spector, while a board member of the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame, intentionally blocked The Ronettes' induction out of spite towards Ronnie. They were finally recognized and inducted into the Hall of Fame in 2007, after Spector had been imprisoned. "Specter, who was on the board of the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame, prevented the Vanette from being inducted out of spite towards his ex until he was imprisoned, at which point they were finally recognised in 2007."

## curiosities
**Rising Hitmaker Reputation** : By the early 1960s, Phil Spector had established a strong reputation in the music industry as a hitmaker, successfully producing records for groups like The Crystals, The Blossoms, and studio groups such as Bobby Soxx and the Blue Jeans, as well as working freelance for artists like Connie Francis. "Specter was becoming very well known in the music industry as a hitmaker."
**Songwriting Contribution Debate** : There is significant debate surrounding the extent of Phil Spector's songwriting contributions when he is co-credited. While some co-writers claim he would often only alter a word or phrase in an already completed song, others, like Barry Mann (who credited Spector with the middle section of "You've Lost That Lovin' Feeling"), suggest more substantial input, though he was rarely the primary writer. "Some of his co-writers have said that he would often only change a word or phrase and get himself cut in on an already completed song."
**B-side Strategy for Singles** : Phil Spector, known for his controlling tendencies, frequently used instrumental jam sessions by studio musicians as B-sides for his singles. He would credit himself as the songwriter for these tracks, a tactic intended to deter DJs from playing the B-side instead of the A-side hit. "Spectre was enough of a control freak that he didn't want DJs ever to play the wrong side of his singles, so he stuck instrumental jam sessions by the studio musicians, with the songwriting credited to him rather than to them. On the B sides."
**Murder Conviction (2003)** : The host notes that Phil Spector murdered a woman in 2003, implying that his subsequent conviction was not surprising given his history of abusive and controlling behavior, particularly towards Ronnie Spector. "I was completely unsurprised when he murdered a woman in 2003."
**Royalty Lawsuit Defense (1998)** : In a 1998 lawsuit brought by The Ronettes for unpaid royalties, Phil Spector argued that he owed them nothing and, in fact, they still owed him money. His defense was based on the claim that the costs of recording their singles had always exceeded the revenue they generated. "In court Specter argued that he didn't owe them any more, and indeed that they still owed him money, because the cost of recording their singles meant that they had never actually earned more money than they cost."
**Preventing Rock and Roll Hall of Fame Induction** : While serving on the board of the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame, Phil Spector used his position to prevent The Ronettes' induction. This act of spite against his ex-wife, Ronnie Spector, continued until his imprisonment, after which the group was finally inducted in 2007. "Specter, who was on the board of the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame, prevented the Vanette from being inducted out of spite towards his ex until he was imprisoned, at which point they were finally recognised in 2007."

**Iconic Drum Intro on "Be My Baby"** : Hal Blaine's drum intro on The Ronettes' "Be My Baby" is described as utterly simple yet profoundly memorable. His minimalist approach, playing "for the song" rather than with flashy fills, combined with Larry Levine's powerful engineering, created one of the most recognizable intros in rock music history. "But that simple part, combined with the powerful sound that the engineer Larry Levine got, was enough to make it one of the most memorable intros in rock music history."

# artist - Carol Kaye

## instruments
Bass
Session Guitar

## curiosities
**Renowned Session Musician** : Carol Kaye was one of the most acclaimed bass players and session guitarists of the 1960s, contributing to hundreds of classic records for artists including The Beach Boys, Phil Spector, and Frank Zappa. She was notably the only woman consistently engaged in session work on a rock instrument in Los Angeles during that era. "Ms. Kay was one of the great bass players of the 60s, and also one of the better session guitarists. She played on hundreds of records in the 60s, including many, many classics from The Beach Boys, Spectre, Frank Zapper and others, and she was the only woman getting regular session work in LA on a rock instrument."
**Unreliable Credits and False Claims** : Carol Kaye is described as an unreliable and dishonest source regarding her session credits. While some memory lapses over five decades are acknowledged (e.g., "Good Vibrations"), she has made numerous unsubstantiated claims, such as playing on almost the entire recorded works of Motown's James Jamerson (claiming secret tape shipments from Detroit to LA, denied by Motown staff) or The Monkees' "I'm a Believer" (recorded in New York by New York musicians). When confronted with falsehoods, she responds with abuse and libel on her website rather than admitting error. "Carol Kay is not only an unreliable source about what records she played on, she has an utterly dishonest one."
**Did Not Play on "Be My Baby"** : Despite claims in some sources, Carol Kaye did not play bass on The Ronettes' "Be My Baby." AFM session sheets confirm that the bass players on the track were Ray Pohlman and Jimmy Bond. "So, Carol Kay did not play on this record, and we know that because we have the AFM session sheet, which show that the bass players on the track were Ray Pullman and Jimmy Bond."

# label - Colpix Records

## curiosities
**Early Ronettes Label** : Colpix Records, a label owned by film studio Columbia Pictures, was the first label to sign The Ronettes (then Ronnie and the Relatives). However, their initial singles, "I Want a Boy" and "I'm Going to Quit While I'm Ahead," released in 1961, were commercially unsuccessful and failed to chart. "but they soon reached the attention of Stu Phillips at Colpix Records, a label owned by the film studio Columbia Pictures."

# concert - Harlem Apollo's talent shows

## curiosities
**The Ronettes' Debut** : The group, then known as Ronnie and the Relatives, made their first appearance at a Harlem Apollo talent show. Their male lead singer experienced stage fright, prompting Ronnie to take over lead vocals, which was well-received by the notoriously hostile audience and led to the group performing regularly. "when they made their first appearance on one of the Harlem Apollo's talent shows, the lead singer got stage fright and couldn't start singing when he got on stage."

**The Ronettes' Regular Performance Venue** : After meeting DJ Murray the K in Florida, The Ronettes became regular performers at the Brooklyn Fox Theatre. "They became regular performers at the Brooklyn Fox, where they danced between the other bigger acts. Sang backing vocals did a song or two themselves, and took part in comedy sketches with Murray."

## curiosities
**Disputed Credits for Carol Kaye** : Carol Kaye, a renowned bass player, is noted for making unsubstantiated claims about playing bass on numerous records, including almost the entire recorded works of Motown's James Jamerson and The Monkees' "I'm a Believer" (recorded in New York). These claims are widely disputed by industry professionals and evidence. "Ms. Kay also claims... to have been responsible for playing almost the entire recorded works of James Jamison, Motown's main bass player..."
**Bassists on "Be My Baby"** : Official AFM session sheets confirm that the bass parts on The Ronettes' "Be My Baby" were played by Ray Pohlman and Jimmy Bond, definitively disproving claims by other session musicians (like Carol Kaye) to have performed on the track. "So, Carol Kay did not play on this record, and we know that because we have the AFM session sheet, which show that the bass players on the track were Ray Pullman and Jimmy Bond."

## curiosities
**Iconic Drum Intro on "Be My Baby"** : Hal Blaine's drum intro on "Be My Baby" is celebrated for its sheer simplicity and powerful sound, engineered by Larry Levine, making it one of the most recognizable openings in rock music history. Blaine's playing style was characterized by a minimalist approach, always serving the song rather than incorporating elaborate fills. "But that simple part, combined with the powerful sound that the engineer Larry Levine got, was enough to make it one of the most memorable intros in rock music history."

# curiosity

**Brill Building Songwriting Teams** : The Brill Building was a hub for several influential husband-and-wife songwriting teams in the early 1960s, including Gerry Goffin and Carole King, Barry Mann and Cynthia Weil, and later Jeff Barry and Ellie Greenwich. Each team had a distinct style: Goffin and King with sophisticated, sexually subversive themes; Mann and Weil incorporating social commentary; and Barry and Greenwich favoring catchy, "silly" hooks. "The song that eventually became the group's first hit, Be My Baby, was mostly written by one of the many husband and wife songwriting teams that had developed at the Royal Building, Jeff Barry and Dalley Gvennich."
**Alan Freed's Departure** : DJ Murray the K rose to become the most popular DJ in New York following the departure of Alan Freed, suggesting a significant shift in the city's radio landscape. "Murray the K, who, since Alan Frieda been driven out of his job, had become the most popular DJ in New York."
