## members

## songs
**Stompin' at the Savoy** : This is one of the most famous swing recordings, later covered by Benny Goodman in an even more famous version. "It's one of the most famous swing recordings ever, though it was later recorded by Benny Goodman in an even more famous version."

## curiosities
**Childhood Illness and Musical Start** : Chick Webb contracted tuberculosis of the spine as a child, which led him to take up the drums as a form of therapy. He began playing professionally at age eleven. "He was a small man, who contracted tuberculosis of the spine as a child, and he'd taken up the drums as a kind of therapy."
**Leader of Premier Swing Band** : By the age of thirty, Chick Webb was leading what was considered "bar none, the best swing band in New York for dancing." His band was known for its incredible energy in inspiring Lindy-Hop dancing. "by the time he was thirty, he was leading what was, bar non, the best swing band in New York for dancing."
**"Real King of Swing" Title** : Chick Webb was considered by many to be the person who most deserved the title "King of Swing" more than anyone else, even before Benny Goodman. "But Chick-Web was the person who deserved the title more than anyone else."
**Arrangement Sales to Benny Goodman** : Benny Goodman, admiring Webb's band, purchased many of their arrangements and used them for his own band, including most of Goodman's biggest crowd-pleasers not written by Fletcher Henderson. The arrangements were bought from Edgar Sampson, Webb's saxophone player and main arranger. "Benny Goodman admired Webb's band enough, that he bought the bands' arrangements and used them himself."
**Rivalry with Benny Goodman** : Chick Webb's band had a significant rivalry with Benny Goodman's band. While Goodman's band was larger, Webb's band was considered more popular among true connoisseurs. "Goodman's band was bigger, in every sense, but Webb's band was more popular with those who knew the real deal when they heard it."
**1937 Savoy Cutting Contest Victory** : In 1937, Chick Webb's Savoy Orchestra won a famous cutting contest against Benny Goodman's band at the Savoy Ballroom. This event attracted 4,000 dancers inside and 5,000 people outside, with Chick Webb's band emerging as the undisputed winner. "Chick Webb's band set up on one stage, Goodman's on another. Four thousand dancers crowded the inside of the ballroom, and despite a police cord on outside to keep trouble down, I know the five thousand people outside tried to hear what was happening, and Chick Webb's band won. Absolutely."
**Nearly Unbeatable in Cutting Contests** : Chick Webb's band won numerous cutting contests, only definitively losing once, to Duke Ellington, a loss considered without shame due to Ellington's stature and different musical approach. "The only time Chick Webb ever definitely lost a cutting contest was against Duke Allington, but everyone knew that Chick Webb and Duke Allington weren't really trying to do the same kind of thing."
**Lindy Hoppers Strike and Misunderstanding** : Chick Webb's band appeared less exciting than Basie's in terms of getting people dancing due to a strike by Whitey's Lindy Hoppers. Webb had mistakenly believed the dancers were disparaging his band, leading him to insult them, prompting their leader, Whitey, to instruct them to dance only to Basie's music. "Chick Webb had misheard a discussion between a couple of the dancers about how good the Basie band was going to be that night. He assumed they were saying that Basie was going to be better than him, and he got into a huff."
**Talent of Key Members** : The enduring quality of Chick Webb's band was attributed to the talent of its "two big stars," Ella Fitzgerald and Louis Jordan, an alto sax player and male lead vocalist. "That's how good Webb's band were, and it's also how good his two big stars were."
**Declining Health and Death** : Chick Webb's disability significantly worsened, leading him to continue performing out of obligation to his musicians. He often passed out after shows and tragically died at age 34 in June 1939. "Chick Webb's disability had been affecting him more, and he was only continuing to perform, because he felt he owed it to his musicians. He died aged 34 in June 1939."

## songs
**Strange Fruit (1939)** : Holiday's most famous performance, "Strange Fruit," a highly political and inflammatory song about lynching, was rejected by her label, Columbia, and her producer, John Hammond, due to its controversial nature. Due to Columbia's refusal, Billy Holiday turned to Milt Gabler, a politically left-leaning friend and founder of Commodore, who negotiated a special deal with Columbia to produce and release the song on his Commodore label. "That recording sold over a million copies and became arguably the most important recording in music history."

Its controversial nature led to its rejection by her main label, Columbia, but its eventual release by Milt Gabler on Commodore Records made it a historic success and a symbol of protest, evidenced by Rebecca Ferguson's attempt to perform it at Donald Trump's inauguration. "The song, which became Billy Holiday's best known performance, was Strange Fruit, and it was about lynching."

# artist - Louis Jordan

## member of
Chick Webb's band

## members
Fliisi Moore (wife/credited songwriter)

## genres
Jive singing

## songs
**Honey in the Bee Bowl (1938)** : Recorded on December 20, 1938, this was one of the first songs recorded by Louis Jordan and his "Alksrande v. band" (later the Tympany Five). It is described as a children's hunting game. "Louis Jordan and his Alksrande v. band went into the studio for the first time, to record Honey in the Bee Bowl, and Barnacle Bill the Sailor."
**Barnacle Bill the Sailor (1938)** : Recorded on December 20, 1938, this was one of the first songs recorded by Louis Jordan and his "Alksrande v. band" (later the Tympany Five). "Louis Jordan and his Alksrande v. band went into the studio for the first time, to record Honey in the Bee Bowl, and Barnacle Bill the Sailor."
**Caldonia** : Although primarily written by Louis Jordan, "Caldonia" is credited to his then-wife, Fliisi Moore, for contractual reasons. This song is highlighted as a prime example of Jordan's comedic jump band style. "So, for example, she's credited on Caldonia, which is a perfect example of Jordan's comedy jump and style."
**Choo Choo Ch'Boogie** : Co-written by Milt Gabler and country and western writers (one a "hillbilly singer" and Vaughn Horton), this song was conceived as a "hillbilly boogie" but became an archetypal rhythm and blues song in Jordan's interpretation, demonstrating his crossover appeal. "So Tchoo-choo-bougie was, in conception, a hillbelly boogie, but in Louis Jordan's hands, it was almost the archetypal rhythm in blues song."

## curiosities
**Frontman Role in Chick Webb's Band** : Louis Jordan was a crucial alto sax player and male lead vocalist for Chick Webb's band, often taking on the frontman role. He was so integral that some people mistakenly thought he was Chick Webb himself. "This sax player did a lot of the frontman job for Webb's band, and he was so important to the band in those years, that allegedly, some people thought he was Chick Webb."
**Exceptional Performer and Charisma** : Louis Jordan was an exceptional performer capable of selling songs with wit, humor, and a hip-ness rivaled only by Cab Calloway and Slim Gaylord at the time. He was also a superior musician, charming, funny, tuneful, and attractive. "He was someone who could absolutely sell a song, with wit and humour, and a general sense of hip-ness that could possibly be matched at that time, only by Cap Calaway and Slim Gaylord."
**Attempt to Poach Band Members and Firing** : Convinced of his and Ella Fitzgerald's importance to Chick Webb's band, Louis Jordan planned to form his own group, taking Ella and other members with him. Webb discovered the plan and fired Jordan, while most of the band, including Ella, remained loyal. "He thought that he and Ella were the two important ones in the web band, and he planned to form his own band, and take her, and much of the rest of the band, with him, web found out and fired Jordan, and Ella most of the band remained loyal to Webb."
**Formation of Tympany Five** : After being fired from Chick Webb's band, Louis Jordan formed a smaller group called the Tympany Five (initially "Alksrande v. band"), drawing members from Jesse Stone's band. "As it was, instead, he formed a much smaller group, the Alksrande v. band, made up of members of Jesse Stone's band."
**First Studio Recordings with Tympany Five** : On December 20, 1938, Louis Jordan and his "Alksrande v. band" (which soon became Louis Jordan and His Tympany Five) made their first studio recordings, laying down "Honey in the Bee Bowl" and "Barnacle Bill the Sailor." "on December 20, 1938, ten days before Roll and Pete was recorded, Louis Jordan and his Alksrande v. band went into the studio for the first time, to record Honey in the Bee Bowl, and Barnacle Bill the Sailor."
**Unique Stage Personality and Vocal Style** : Louis Jordan combined the jump band sound with his unique stage personality and highly expressive vocal style, which was characterized by "putting personality into his vocals." He was part of the jive singing tradition, known for being hip, clever, funny, sophisticated, and using made-up words or singing about taboo subjects. "But what he was very good at was putting personality into his vocals."
**Crossover Appeal and Country Market** : Louis Jordan's music had crossover success, appealing to audiences of both Bob Wills and Big Joe Turner. This led him to record songs, like "Choo Choo Ch'Boogie," originally targeted at the white country market. "to appeal to the same audience that was listening to Bob Will's, just as much as you were to the audience that was listening to Big Joe Turner, and because of this crossover success, Jordan started recording occasional songs that were originally aimed at the white country market."
**Fusion of Genres in "Choo Choo Ch'Boogie"** : Jordan's "Choo Choo Ch'Boogie" exemplified a fusion of genres, resembling Bob Wills' Western Swing (lacking only fiddle or steel guitar) while clearly showcasing the horn-driven pulse and hip, jive vocals characteristic of rhythm and blues, making it a seminal example of early rock and roll. "There's only really the lack of a fiddle or steel guitar to distinguish the styles, but you can also hear the horn driven pulse and the hip vocals that characterize rhythm and blues."
**Pivotal Influence on Early Rock and Roll** : Louis Jordan's 1940s records and style were widely emulated by nearly every musician during the early rock and roll period, making him a foundational figure for the genre. "Essentially, every musician in the early rock and roll period was to a greater or less of extent copying the style of Louis Jordan's 1940s records"
**Success through Jive Singing in Jump Band Music** : Louis Jordan's jive singing style was instrumental to the success of jump band music, as this stripped-down genre relied heavily on vocal personality to provide the character that would otherwise come from more instruments in larger bands. "His jive singing style gives the music all the character that in the larger bands would be conveyed by other instruments"

## genres
Jump band music

## curiosities
**Pioneering "Jump Band Music" Sound** : Louis Jordan's Tympany Five developed a unique sound, distinct from other contemporary bands, which became known as "jump band music." Their original lineup of Jordan plus five instruments (trumpet, sax, piano, bass, drums) managed to sound almost as full as larger big bands. "Jordan's new band had a sound, unlike anything else of the time."
**Influence on Decline of Big Bands** : The success of Louis Jordan and His Tympany Five, and their ability to produce a full sound with a smaller, cheaper lineup, contributed significantly to the decline of the big band era, as noted by Clarence "Gatemouth" Brown. "Clamants Gatemouth Vowl later claimed that Jordan had most of the responsibility for the decline of the big band, saying he could play just as good and just as loud with 5 or 17 and it was cheaper."

## curiosities
**Pioneer of Reissue Record Labels** : In 1935, Milt Gabler, a record shop owner with a passion for jazz, founded the very first reissue record label to license and re-release out-of-print recordings by artists such as Bix Beiderbecke and Bessie Smith for his customers. "So in 1935, Gabler started his own record label and licensed those out of print recordings by people like Big Spiderbeck and Bessie Smith, becoming the owner of the very first ever reissue record label."
**Innovator in Liner Notes** : Gabler's record labels pioneered the practice of including a full list of all musicians on a record on the label itself, a detail highly valued by music enthusiasts. "His labels pioneered things like putting a full list of all the musicians on a record on the label, the kind of thing that real music obsessives cared far more about than the executives who only wanted to make money."
**Founder of Commodore Records** : Following success with his reissue label, Milt Gabler expanded into producing new records, establishing the Commodore label. "After he had some success with that, he branched out into making new records on a new label, Commodore."
**Producer of "Strange Fruit"** : Milt Gabler, a friend and politically left-leaning, negotiated a special deal with Columbia Records to produce Billy Holiday's "Strange Fruit" for a single session and release it on his Commodore label. The record sold over a million copies and became a landmark recording. "He eventually negotiated a special deal with Columbia, Holiday's label, that he could produce her for one session and put out a single recording by her on Commodore."
**Move to Decca Records (1941)** : Following the immense success of "Strange Fruit," Milt Gabler was recruited by Decca Records in 1941, where he became a staff producer and was responsible for producing many significant records of the 1940s, including Lionel Hampton's "Flying Home." "As a result of Strange Fruit's success, Milt Gabler was headhunted away from his own label and became a staff producer at Decker Records in 1941."
**Long-Term Collaboration with Louis Jordan** : After joining Decca Records in 1941, Milt Gabler initiated a lengthy and impactful collaboration with Louis Jordan. "and he began a long collaboration with Louis Jordan."
**Producer of Bill Haley and His Comets** : Milt Gabler produced Bill Haley and His Comets, an act that covered Louis Jordan's "Choo Choo Ch'Boogie" and heavily based their style on Jordan's music. "another act built cable produced, an act two more than any other based their style on Jordans"

# genre - Jump band music

## curiosities
**Characteristics and Influences** : Jump band music, pioneered by Louis Jordan and His Tympany Five, was influenced by Lionel Hampton's band and Count Basie. It featured a stripped-back sound compared to big band swing, focusing on simple chord changes (often blues), riffs, solos, and vocals, driven by boogie rhythms and high energy. "The style they were playing in was a style that later became known as jump band music, and it was a style that owed a lot to Lionel Hampton's band and to Count Basie."
**Sound and Arrangement** : Jump band music, despite being performed by smaller ensembles, achieved a full sound, often with a seemingly complete horn section. Arrangements were stripped back, focusing on punchy delivery and a backbeat, emphasizing vocal personality to convey character typically handled by more instruments in larger bands. "The arrangement is stripped back to the basics to what's necessary, this is a punchy track, and it's a track that makes you want to dance."

# genre - Jive singing

## curiosities
**Tradition and Characteristics** : Jive singing, a tradition dating back to the 1920s with artists like Cab Calloway, emphasized personality over technical vocal prowess. Jive singers were "hip, and they often used made up words of their own," being "clever, and funny, and sophisticated." Their lyrical themes often included the underworld, drug use, prostitution, or nonsense words. "Jive Singers weren't usually technically great, but they had personality. They were hip, and they often used made up words of their own."

# genre - Blues

## curiosities
**Steam Train Rhythm Influence** : The mechanical rhythm of steam trains was a key influence on blues, rhythm and blues, and rock and roll. Rock and roll's emergence coincided with America's shift from a train culture to a car culture, with trains symbolizing freedom and escape in the 1940s and early 50s, a sentiment enshrined in the backbeat of songs like "Choo Choo Ch'Boogie." "That mechanical steam train rhythm is one of the key influences in blues, rhythm and blues, and rock and roll."

# concert - Savoy Ballroom

## curiosities
**Home of Lindy-Hoppers and Chick Webb** : The Savoy Ballroom was the primary venue where Whitey's Lindy Hoppers danced and where the Lindy-Hop was invented. An entire corner of the ballroom was dedicated to them. It was also the main performance venue for Chick Webb, who was considered "the real King of Swing." "The Savoy was where the Lindy-Huppers invented, and it was the place to dance, because it was where Chick-Web, the real King of Swing, played."

# concert - 1937 Savoy Cutting Contest

## curiosities
**Epic Battle of Big Bands** : This major event in 1937 at the Savoy Ballroom pitted Chick Webb's Savoy Orchestra against Benny Goodman's band. The ballroom, equipped with two bandstands for continuous music, hosted 4,000 dancers, with another 5,000 people trying to hear outside despite a police cordon. Chick Webb's band decisively won. "And that 1937 cutting contest was a big event. The Savoy had two band stands, so they would have one band start as soon as the other one finished, so people could dance all night."

# concert - Basie/Webb After-Hours Cutting Contest

## curiosities
**Star-Studded Audience** : The after-hours cutting contest between Chick Webb and Count Basie attracted a host of jazz luminaries, including members of Goodman's band, Mildred Bailey, Ivy Anderson, Red Norvo, and Duke Ellington, indicating its significant importance within the jazz community. "Everyone was in the audience. Goodman's band, Mildred Bailey, Ivy Anderson, who we heard before in that March for this clip. Red Norvo, the vibroform player, Duke Ellington, every musician who mattered in the jazz scene was there to see if Basie could beat Chick Webb."
**Chick Webb's Audience Vote Victory** : Despite a close decision, Chick Webb's band won the audience vote in the after-hours cutting contest against Count Basie, making him the victor in the eyes of the attendees, and reaffirming his status as rarely beaten (only by Duke Ellington). "Because Webb won, everyone agreed, when it came to a vote of the audience, Webb's band did win, though it was a fairly close decision."

# curiosity

**1938 - Year Zero for Rock and Roll** : The year 1938 is identified as the "real year zero for rock and roll," regardless of whether one approaches it from the perspective of Blues and Boogie, Jazz, or Country and Western music. This year is highlighted as the starting point for the genre. "1938 is apparently the real year zero for rock and roll, whether you come at it from the direction of Blues and Boogie, or Jazz, or country and western music, 1938 ends up being the place where you start."
**Lindy-Hop Dance** : The Lindy-Hop dance is mentioned, named after Charles Lindbergh, the famous airman and Nazi sympathiser. It was performed by "Whitey's Lindy Hoppers" and was associated with controversy. "It was named after Charles Lindbergh, the famous airman and Nazi sympathiser, and the people dancing it are whiteies Lindy-Huppers, and they were responsible for controversy."
**Cutting Contest Tradition** : A "cutting contest" was a musical tradition originating from the world of stride piano players, and the Boogie-Woogie music scene. It involved musicians attempting to outdo each other with faster, more inventive, or exciting performances, often by reinterpreting a previously played song, with the audience deciding the winner. "A cutting contest was a tradition that came from the world of striped piano players, the same world that Boogie-Wogie music grew out of."
**The Riff in 20th Century Popular Music** : The riff is a fundamental and crucial element in almost all 20th-century popular music. It is defined as a repeated instrumental phrase that serves as a song's backbone or a basis for variation and improvisation. In swing music, riffs often involved a call-and-response between sections like saxophones and brass. "A riff is, in its conception, fairly straightforward. It's an instrumental phrase that gets repeated over and over. It can act as the backbone to a song, but it can also be the basis for variation and improvisation."
**The Backbeat** : The backbeat, described as the "rhythm of the clickity clack," is a significant rhythmic element, particularly in songs with lyrics that also evoke the mechanical rhythm of steam trains. "it's that backbeat again, the one we've been talking about, and the lyrics here are all about that rhythm, but also about the rhythm of the steam trains."
**Steam Train Rhythm Influence** : The mechanical rhythm of steam trains was a key influence on blues, rhythm and blues, and rock and roll. Rock and roll's emergence coincided with America's shift from a train culture to a car culture, with trains symbolizing freedom and escape in the 1940s and early 50s, a sentiment enshrined in the backbeat of songs like "Choo Choo Ch'Boogie." "That mechanical steam train rhythm is one of the key influences in blues, rhythm and blues, and rock and roll."
**Train Culture to Car Culture Transition** : The birth of rock and roll closely aligned with a major cultural shift in America from a "train culture" to a "car culture." In the 1940s and early 50s, trains symbolized freedom and escape, and their rhythmic influence, like the "Choo Choo Ch'Boogie," became embedded in the backbeat of the music. "Rock and roll started at almost exactly the point that America changed from being a train culture to being a car culture"
