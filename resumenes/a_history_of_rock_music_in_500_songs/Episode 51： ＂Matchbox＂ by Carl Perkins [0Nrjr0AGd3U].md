## songs
**Blue Suede Shoes (1956)** : This was Carl Perkins' sole hit song at the time. Despite its success, Perkins continued making records every bit as good as this one throughout the rest of 1956. "Karl Perkins, who spent the rest of 1956 making records that were every bit as good as his one hit, Blue Swade Shoes."
**Boppin' the Blues (1956)** : This song was the unsuccessful follow-up to Carl Perkins' hit "Blue Suede Shoes." "After bopping the blues, the songs unsuccessful follow-up, he released Dixie Fried."
**Dixie Fried (1956)** : Released after the unsuccessful "Boppin' the Blues," this song also failed to achieve commercial success. "But that was no more successful."
**Your True Love (1956)** : Recorded on December 4, 1956, at Sun Studios with his brothers, this song was the A-side of a single. It charted at number 13 on the country charts and number 67 on the pop charts, described as "a decent country record, but not much more than that." Jerry Lee Lewis bluntly stated the song "ain't worth a damn." "The A-side, your true love, charted, but not very high."
**Matchbox (1956)** : The B-side to "Your True Love," this song originated from an idea given to Carl by his father, who recalled an old song line. Carl developed a new song around the line "sitting here wondering, will a matchbox hold my clothes? Ain't got no matches, but I've got a long way to go." Perkins never heard the original "Matchbox Blues" and based his song on his father's description, diverging significantly from Blind Lemon Jefferson's song after the chorus. The song features floating blues lyrics, including a verse from the traditional "Poor Boy, Long Way From Home." It was largely improvised in the studio, and while credited to Carl Perkins, Jerry Lee Lewis provided the boogie piano part. Perkins, impressed by Lewis's musicianship, upped his guitar playing, using a hard-driving riff on the bass strings and executing solos on the top three strings with a "Triple String" technique. He took two solos to outshine Lewis. The song became a rock and roll standard after The Beatles brought it to prominence decades later. "Matchbox shows all the sounds of having been put together in the studio, largely impovervised, as most of Perkins' songs were."

## curiosities
**Dissatisfaction with Sam Phillips** : Carl Perkins became increasingly dissatisfied with Sam Phillips' promotion of his work. This led him to strongly consider moving to another record label, a sentiment shared by Johnny Cash at the time. "Perkins was increasingly dissatisfied with the way Sam Phillips was promoting his work, and like Johnny Cash was strongly considering moving to another label."
**Improvised Songwriting Style** : Most of Carl Perkins' records, including "Matchbox," were at least semi-improvised or "largely improvised" during studio sessions. This approach was common for him, often just making up material to fill vinyl space. "Matchbox shows all the sounds of having been put together in the studio, largely impovervised, as most of Perkins' songs were."
**Rivalry with Jerry Lee Lewis on "Matchbox"** : During the recording of "Matchbox," Carl Perkins was hugely impressed by Jerry Lee Lewis's musical talent but initially unimpressed by his personality, especially after Lewis critiqued Perkins' "Your True Love." Perkins felt Lewis lacked originality and was showing off. To assert himself, Perkins decided to elevate his guitar playing, adopting a "hard-driving riff" and playing "Triple String" solos on the top three strings of his guitar, explicitly stating he was "shooting at Jerry Lee's head" and taking two solos to prevent Lewis from getting a piano break. "But fooling around and practising, I knew it would work, and that was the time to try it, because I was shooting at Jerry Lee's head."
**Appearance of Elvis Presley at Studio** : On December 4, 1956, while Carl Perkins and his brothers were recording "Matchbox" at Sun Studios, Elvis Presley walked in unexpectedly. Perkins was astonished to see Elvis, noting his significant physical transformation: dyed dark black hair (from light brown), cleared acne, and a new image as a "major sex symbol." "Earl Perkins was astonished to see Elvis there."
**Post-1956 Career Shift** : After December 4, 1956, Carl Perkins, along with Johnny Cash, increasingly moved towards country music and away from rockabilly. This date is considered a significant border in rock and roll history, marking a period before rock and roll became the dominant genre. "They both moved increasingly towards country music, and away from rockabilly."

**Conflicting Accounts of Million Dollar Quartet Participation (1956)** : Accounts vary widely regarding Johnny Cash's involvement in the legendary Million Dollar Quartet session on December 4, 1956. Cash himself claimed he was the first to arrive and the last to leave, watching his friends record and staying for the entire jam session. Other versions suggest he arrived mid-session to collect a royalty check, sang a few songs off-tape, and then left for Christmas shopping. Yet another account implies Sam Phillips, seeking publicity for Sun Records' biggest act, called Cash, along with journalists and photographers, implying Cash only appeared for a photo opportunity before departing. "Every single account of what became known as the million dollar quartet session disagrees as to how much, ever at all, Johnny Cash participated in the proceedings."
**Singing Style on Million Dollar Quartet Tapes (1956)** : On the Million Dollar Quartet jam session tapes, which were recorded on a single microphone and released in the 1980s, Johnny Cash is difficult to hear. He claimed he was positioned furthest from the microphone and sang in a higher register than usual to match the keys comfortable for the other three musicians (Elvis Presley, Jerry Lee Lewis, and Carl Perkins). "Cash always said that he was there, just the furthest from the microphone, and that he was singing in a higher register than normal, because he was trying to sing in the keys that were comfortable for the other three."

## curiosities
**Last White Rock and Roll Star Standing (1956)** : By December 1956, Elvis Presley was the only white rock and roll star from the "first wave" who was still having hits, as others like Carl Perkins, Gene Vincent, and Bill Haley had ceased to chart successfully. "By December 1956, only Elvis was left standing as a white rock and roll star from the first wave."
**Million Dollar Quartet Entrance (1956)** : On December 4, 1956, Elvis Presley walked into Sun Studios during Carl Perkins' recording session for "Matchbox." He had been driving past with a girlfriend and noticed the studio was active due to the number of cars outside. His appearance astonished Perkins, as Elvis had significantly changed his look since they last met. "Elvis had been driving past with a girlfriend, and had been able to tell that there was a session on that day, because, as Marie and Casca later put it, it looked like a cabalactio room outside."
**Physical Transformation (1956)** : By December 1956, Elvis Presley's appearance had changed considerably. He had dyed his previously light brown hair a dark black, his acne had cleared up, and he no longer needed to keep his collar up to hide his neck. He had transformed from a "spotty shy adolescent to being a major sex symbol." "His appearance had changed considerably. Elvis was now dang his hair, which had previously been a light brown, a dark black. His acne had cleared up so much that he was no longer keeping his colours up to avoid showing his neck. He'd gone from being a spotty shy adolescent to being a major sex symbol."
**Interaction with Jerry Lee Lewis (1956)** : Upon entering Sun Studios and starting to noodle on the piano, Elvis was challenged by Jerry Lee Lewis, who said, "I didn't know you could play." Elvis responded, "I can't," prompting Lewis to suggest, "well then why don't you let me sit down." Elvis simply replied, "well I'd like to try and carry on noodling." Elvis largely dismissed Lewis, detesting his arrogance. "Elvis was not himself an arrogant person, and he detested those who were, and Jerry Lee clearly was one."
**Musical Deference to Others** : At the time of the Million Dollar Quartet session, it was "completely in character for Elvis," despite being the biggest star, to defer to other musicians. This trait is evident in his comments about Jackie Wilson's superior performance of "Don't Be Cruel" and his initial lead on "Jesus' Work That Loans and Valley" before Jerry Lee Lewis took over. "It was also completely in character for Elvis, at least at this time, to differ to another musician, even though he was the biggest star around."
**Ed Sullivan Show Performance Detail** : Weeks after the Million Dollar Quartet session, Elvis Presley performed "Don't Be Cruel" on the Ed Sullivan Show. His performance included a specific "Yankee pronunciation" of the word "Telephone," which he had learned and was influenced by from Jackie Wilson's performance of the same song in Las Vegas. "You can hear just how influenced Elvis was by Wilson's performance, and that Yankee pronunciation, Telephone, that he makes fun of, by listening to his performance of the song on the Ed Sullivan show a few weeks later, where he pronounces the word the same way."
**Religious Background** : Elvis Presley was brought up in the Assembly of God, a Pentecostal holy roller church, sharing this religious culture with Jerry Lee Lewis. This shared background gave them more in common religiously than with Johnny Cash or Carl Perkins, despite the latter two also being deeply religious. "Both Elvis and Jerry Lee were brought up in the assembly of God, a Pentecostal holy roller church, which we'll talk more about when we get to an episode on Jerry Lee himself."
**Absence from Reunions** : Due to his death, Elvis Presley was not present at subsequent Million Dollar Quartet reunion performances or recordings, such as "The Survivors" (1981) or "Class of 55" (1985). "None however featured the quartet's full line up, as they happened after Elvis's death."

## curiosities
**Discovery and Early Recordings (1956)** : Jerry Lee Lewis was discovered by Sam Phillips' assistant, Cowboy Jack Clement, in November 1956. He cut his first record for Sun Records a couple of weeks before the December 4, 1956 session, and that first record was released three days prior to that session. "Jerry Lee Lewis was a new piano player, who had been discovered by Sam Phillips as assistant, Cowboy Jack Clement, the previous month."
**Session Musician for Christmas Presents (1956)** : On December 4, 1956, Jerry Lee Lewis participated in Carl Perkins' recording session for "Matchbox" not as a primary performer, but as a session musician. His motivation was to earn enough money to buy Christmas presents for his parents. "but he was just working as a session musician, trying to earn enough money to buy his parents some Christmas presents, by sitting in on the session."
**Musical Prowess and Influences** : Jerry Lee Lewis is described as "one of the most musically-fechland people who ever lived." He was a Louisiana piano player, like Fats Domino, but his biggest influence was Moon Mulligan. He absorbed every piece of music he heard and was eager to perform for people regardless of the song. "Lewis was, by every account I've ever read, one of the most musically-fechland people who ever lived."
**Initial Interaction with Carl Perkins** : After recording "Your True Love," Jerry Lee Lewis bluntly told Carl Perkins that "That song ain't worth a damn." Perkins initially thought Lewis lacked originality, hearing elements of himself, Elvis, and Hank Williams in Lewis's playing and singing of his new record. "He'd got Perkins' backup after they'd finished recording Your True Love, when he'd said, bluntly. That song ain't worth a damn."
**Confidence and Arrogance** : Jerry Lee Lewis consistently displayed immense confidence and a tendency to show off. He openly challenged Elvis Presley's piano playing and, despite being a relative newcomer with only one single out for days, he naturally took the lead during jam sessions with established stars like Elvis. This "cheeky" character trait was noted as being completely in line with his later career. "Remember at this time, Jerry Lee Lewis was nobody at all, someone who had one single out which had been out a matter of days. But here he is, she wetting with the king of rock and roll, and seeing himself as the person who should naturally be taking the lead."
**Religious Background** : Jerry Lee Lewis was brought up in the Assembly of God, a Pentecostal holy roller church, sharing this religious culture with Elvis Presley. This shared background meant they had more in common religiously than with Johnny Cash or Carl Perkins. "Both Elvis and Jerry Lee were brought up in the assembly of God, a Pentecostal holy roller church, which we'll talk more about when we get to an episode on Jerry Lee himself."
**Million Dollar Quartet as Career Catalyst** : The Million Dollar Quartet session "might well have been the making of Jerry Lee Lewis." Although the recordings were not released until decades later, Sam Phillips used the opportunity for extensive publicity, which positioned Lewis as an equal to Elvis Presley, Carl Perkins, and Johnny Cash, significantly boosting his career. "The million dollar quartet session might well have been the making of Jerry Lee Lewis, even though the recordings weren't released until decades later."

# concert - Million Dollar Quartet Session

## curiosities
**Date and Participants (December 4, 1956)** : This legendary jam session took place at Sun Studios on December 4, 1956. It famously involved Carl Perkins, Elvis Presley, and Jerry Lee Lewis, with Johnny Cash's participation disputed but likely present. The event began when Elvis walked in during Perkins' "Matchbox" recording session. "But on December 4, 1956, Perkins was still working for some, and so he was in the studio with his brothers, recording another single that was destined to do very little."
**Recording Circumstances** : The jam session was recorded on a single microphone by Sam Phillips, who only started recording after the musicians had already begun playing, realizing he might never have another chance to capture these artists together. The tapes were eventually released in the 1980s. "It was recorded on a single microphone. Phillips just started recording after they'd already started jamming, realising he may never have another chance to record these people together."
**Dominance of Elvis and Jerry Lee** : The recordings of the Million Dollar Quartet session are "completely dominated by Elvis Presley and Jerry Lee Lewis," with Carl Perkins a "distant third." While Perkins' backing band played on early tracks, the majority features Elvis on acoustic guitar or piano, Jerry Lee on piano, and both sharing most lead vocals. "The legendary million dollar quartet recordings are completely dominated by Elvis Presley and Jerry Lee Lewis, with Karl Perkins a distant third."
**Musical Content** : The music played during the session consisted of surprisingly little pure rock and roll. Instead, a "substantial proportion" was pure country music or Southern Gospel. They did play Chuck Berry's "Brown-Eyed Handsome Man" and discussed "Too Much Monkey Business," and Elvis sang a line of Little Richard's "Rip It Up." "The interesting thing about the music they play is how little of it is actually rockin' roll."
**Intense Musical Rivalry and Joy** : The session is characterized by two immense talents, Elvis Presley and Jerry Lee Lewis, "sizing each other up, out to each other," while also sharing joy in making music. This friendly rivalry is evident in Jerry Lee Lewis's swift takeover of lead vocals from Elvis on gospel songs and Carl Perkins' efforts to outplay Lewis on guitar. "But what this really is is two immense talents, both trying to size each other up, out to each other, and also at the same time share in their joy at making music together."
**Historical Document vs. Great Music** : The Million Dollar Quartet tape is described as an "interesting historical document" rather than consistently great music, except for specific "electrifying" moments when the musicians, at the height of their powers, played for pure pleasure. "The million dollar quartet tape isn't great music, except it at art moments. It's an interesting historical document, rather than anything else."
**Impact on Jerry Lee Lewis's Career** : The session "might well have been the making of Jerry Lee Lewis." Despite the recordings being released decades later, Sam Phillips used the event for extensive publicity, which positioned Lewis as an equal to Elvis Presley, Carl Perkins, and Johnny Cash, significantly boosting his career. "The million dollar quartet session might well have been the making of Jerry Lee Lewis, even though the recordings weren't released until decades later."
**Best Moment: Elvis's "That's When Your Heart Aches Again"** : The very best moment of the session is identified as Elvis Presley's solo version of "That's When Your Heart Aches Again." This performance, originally recorded by Elvis three years prior, showcased the significant maturation of his voice and improvement in his performance since his first recording at Sun Studios. "To me, the very best moment in the whole session comes near the end. When Elvis plays a solo version of that's when your heart aches again, the ink spots song he had performed three years earlier, when he had first walked into that studio to record himself for his mother."

# instrument - electric guitar

## curiosities
**Carl Perkins' "Triple String" Technique (1956)** : On "Matchbox," Carl Perkins utilized a unique "Triple String" guitar break technique, playing all three top strings at the same time with a pick. This was typically done finger-style, but Perkins adapted it and used it to assert his guitar prowess during the session, explicitly aiming to outshine Jerry Lee Lewis. "Triple String is what I was doing, playing all three strings at the same time with a pick, which is usually done finger-style."

# instrument - piano

## curiosities
**Jerry Lee Lewis's Boogie Style (1956)** : Jerry Lee Lewis, known as a Louisiana piano player influenced by Moon Mulligan, immediately launched into a boogie piano part upon hearing Carl Perkins sing the lines for "Matchbox." This spontaneous contribution significantly shaped the song's sound, making it sound "exactly like a Jerry Lee Lewis record" until the guitar solo. "And so when Karl Perkins started singing the lines for Matchbox Blues, Jerry Lee immediately started playing a boogie piano part."
**Elvis Presley's Unexpected Skill (1956)** : During the Million Dollar Quartet session, Elvis Presley played the piano "perfectly decent[ly]" for the first part of the session, despite claiming not to be able to play the instrument. His casual noodling initially prompted Jerry Lee Lewis to try and displace him. "For the first part of the session, Elvis is on the piano, and while he claimed to not be able to play, he actually does a perfectly decent job."

# curiosity

**End of First Wave Rockabilly (December 1956)** : By December 1956, the initial surge of rockabilly music was concluding, with many prominent artists like Carl Perkins, Gene Vincent, and Bill Haley no longer having chart successes. Only Elvis Presley remained a leading white rock and roll star from this era. "We're coming to the end of 1956, and with it, the end of the first wave of rockabilly."
**December 4, 1956 - A Historical Border** : This specific date is proposed as a significant "border between periods in rock and roll history." It marks the last point where rock and roll, rockabilly, gospel, and country music could be perceived as stylistically similar, before rock and roll became a distinct and dominant genre. "December 4, 1956, is as good a date as any then, to nominate as the border between periods in rock and roll history."
**Interconnectedness of Musicians and Influences** : The origins of "Matchbox," tracing back from Carl Perkins to his father's memory, to Roy Newman, Blind Lemon Jefferson, and Marraini, highlight how deeply interconnected different musicians and musical traditions were in shaping popular music. "In a coincidence, which once again shows how interconnected the different musicians were looking at are."
**"Poor Boy, Long Way From Home" Traditional Blues** : This old traditional blues song is mentioned as the source for floating lyrics used in Carl Perkins' "Matchbox," specifically the verse "I'm a poor boy and I'm a long way from home." This illustrates the borrowing and adaptation of lyrical motifs in early rock and roll. "For example, the verse starting, I'm a poor boy and I'm a long way from home, comes from the old traditional blues song, usually called, unsurprisingly, poor boy, long way from home."
**Million Dollar Quartet Reunions (Post-Elvis)** : There were several attempts at Million Dollar Quartet reunions over the years after the original session became legendary among rockabilly fans. However, none of these reunions featured the full original lineup because they occurred after Elvis Presley's death. "So were a couple of attempts at million dollar quartet reunions over the years, as the session became legendary among rockabilly fans.
