# label - Motown Records

## curiosities
**Artist Lack of Creative Control** : Typically, Motown artists had "no say in what songs they recorded," reflecting a strict hierarchical control over their creative output. This policy meant groups like The Supremes often had to record material they disliked. "Normally, Motown artists have no say in what songs they recorded." ← Episode 116： ＂Where Did Our Love Go？＂ by The Supremes | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=g2PetxEXQC4
**Financial Structure for Creative Workers** : Motown's financial structure imposed royalty charges on creative workers, including producers and songwriters, for studio time. This system meant that if a recorded track was not released, the creative team would incur financial losses, influencing decisions like passing on rejected songs to other artists. "Motown caused problems for its creative workers was that they would be charged against royalties for studio time." ← Episode 116： ＂Where Did Our Love Go？＂ by The Supremes | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=g2PetxEXQC4
**First Hit with "Money (That's What I Want)" (1960)** : "Money (That's What I Want)," written by Janie Bradford and Berry Gordy, became the first hit for the newly formed Motown Records in 1960 after Barrett Strong recorded it for the Tamla label. "It would go on to become the first hit for the new Motown Records in 1960." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 59 - Bands and Booze on the Festival Express of 1970 | https://www.ftr70.com/ ← motown-records
**Hitsville U.S.A. as a Creative Hub** : Hitsville U.S.A., Motown's studio, was a bustling environment where aspiring artists, including The Primettes, would "hang around" and often find opportunities to contribute minor session work, such as handclaps and backing vocals, fostering a unique pathway into the label. "They took to hanging around Hitzville, acting like they belonged there, and they soon found themselves during minor bits of work on sessions, handclaps and backing vocals and so on." ← Episode 116： ＂Where Did Our Love Go？＂ by The Supremes | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=g2PetxEXQC4
**Learned Distribution from Sadie DuPouy** : Berry Gordy, the founder of Motown Records, was taught how to make distribution deals for artists on his label by Sadie DuPouy. This crucial knowledge contributed significantly to Motown's eventual success and its ability to effectively get its music to audiences. "And it was she who taught Barry Gordy about how to make distribution deals for artists on his own Motown Records." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=oRR7N-YUcRE ← motown-records
**Slump Period (Pre-"Where Did Our Love Go")** : Prior to the success of "Where Did Our Love Go," Motown Records was experiencing a slump, characterized by disappointing follow-ups from several major stars and the departure of one of its biggest artists, Mary Wells, to another label. "Mo Tan was in a bit of a slump at that point. **Stevie Wonder's Contract and Artistic Freedom** : Stevie Wonder's departure from his contract with Motown Records freed him to explore new musical directions, specifically with synthesizers. This allowed him to collaborate with Malcolm Cecil and Robert Margouleff on the TONTO synthesizer to create the music he envisioned, leading to some of his most acclaimed work. "Stevie was out of his old contract with Motown Records and wanted a tutorial and synthesizers so he could finally start recording the music that he heard in his head." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6SdPPsmpgJg ← motown-records
