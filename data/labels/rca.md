# label - RCA

## curiosities
**Car Turntables (1960-1961)** : RCA entered the market for in-car record players in 1960 and 1961, offering units that played standard 45 RPM records. However, a significant drawback was that the stylus had to press very heavily on the record to prevent skipping on bumpy roads, which caused the records to wear out quickly. `"then RCA got into the act in 1960 and 1961. These ones played standard 45 RPM records, but because the stylists had to press so heavily on the record to keep from skipping on bumpy roads, records wore out really fast."` ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OuFvfZJKcpc
**Color-Coded Vinyl Records (1949)** : Upon the launch of the 7-inch single, RCA introduced a system of color-coding records by genre. Country music was pressed on green vinyl, serious classical on red, popular classical on midnight blue, children's records on yellow, international songs on sky blue, Rhythm and Blues on "saris" (not red), and everyday pop records on black vinyl. While a "nice idea," this system did not last very long. "RCA had come up with a system of color coding records by genre. Country was green. Serious classical was red. Popular classical was midnight blue. Yellow was reserved for children's records. International songs were sky blue. Rhythm and blues was saris. Not red, saris. And your everyday pop records were on block vinyl. It was a nice idea, but it didn't last very long." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VlIErdSSVOM
**Compromise on Music Video** : RCA secured a deal with The Strokes, largely because they were accommodating of the band's desire to make a music video. This flexibility played a role in RCA winning the bidding war for their US distribution rights. "They also landed a deal with RCA, largely because they were okay with The Stroke Desire to make a music video." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jEfofyobGiI
**Contract Dispute with Lou Reed (1975)** : In 1975, RCA Records was in a contract dispute with Lou Reed, who wished to terminate his agreement. The label insisted he fulfill his contractual obligation of delivering a certain number of albums. "He wanted out of his contract, but RCA was adamant. He had to provide them with the required number of albums as stipulated in his contract." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zSFhhEaiYwE
**Development of the Spider Adapter** : RCA developed the "spider" adapter, a three-armed, usually yellow or red plastic insert, to allow 45s to be played on turntables with standard LP-sized spindles. These spiders had "drive dots" that interlocked when records were stacked, preventing slippage. "This was RCA's idea. Spiders looked like a three-armed galaxy, usually yellow or red, and each was made of plastic." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yPok3MemDmE
**Diamond Dogs Censorship (1974)** : RCA, David Bowie's record label, airbrushed out the "prominent set of dog junk" from the original 1974 "Diamond Dogs" album cover, which depicted Bowie as half-man, half-dog, because the artwork was considered too outrageous. "An unknown number of copies were sold before RCA Bowie's label put an end to that by airbrushing out the dog junk." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tbmssCZ05ro
**Dynaflex Recording Promotion** : RCA promoted David Bowie's 1971 album "The Man Who Sold the World" as a "Dynaflex recording," a new development in record manufacturing. This technology aimed to provide a smoother, quieter surface and improved sound quality, while also making records lighter to eliminate warpage and turntable slippage. "RCA was very proud to mention that this album was a dino-flex recording, a new development and record manufacturing that provides a smoother quieter surface and improves sound quality to reproduce a musical sound." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KaIp6KVn-co
**Early Large Label** : RCA is mentioned as one of the historically larger record labels from an era when the industry had many independent players before market consolidation. "They were some that were bigger than others like Deca and London and RCA and EMI, but there were literally thousands of small labels that did their own thing." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dDcD8kg0wEk
**Early Vinyl Experimentation and Patent Lapse (1930s)** : In 1931, RCA experimented with polyvinyl chloride as a superior replacement for the materials used in 78 RPM records. They even produced 12-inch vinyl records, ready for public sale, but the Great Depression prevented their release. Consequently, RCA allowed many of their crucial patents related to this vinyl technology to expire, a decision later regarded as "dumb" as Columbia Records would later use similar principles for the LP. "Then in 1931, RCA was experimenting with vinyl as a replacement for the crappy materials that went into making 78s. RCA even started making vinyl records that were 12 inches in diameter, and were ready to sell them to the general public. But it was the depression. And no one was in the mood for introducing a new format into what seemed at the time to be a dying industry... So not only did RCA give up, but they also led a bunch of their patents expire." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VlIErdSSVOM
**Format War with Columbia** : Following RCA's refusal to license Columbia's LP technology, a fierce format war erupted. RCA was determined to push its 7-inch, 45 RPM single, though it did release its classical catalog on LPs, which seemed to signal a wavering in the fight. Millions were spent on advertising the advantages of their stackable records. "That led into a brand new format war... It was Columbia versus RCA, each trying to woo other labels and companies and music consumers to adopt their formats." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VlIErdSSVOM
**Initial Dislike of Raw Sound** : RCA, The Strokes' US label, initially found the raw, low-fidelity recording of "Is This It" to sound "unprofessional." They only understood the artistic intent and left the band to their methods after hearing the tracks played back on an old boombox, which replicated the intended gritty sound. "This didn't sit well with RCA, The Strokes US label. They thought everything sounded unprofessional." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jEfofyobGiI
**Introduction of 7-inch Single (1949)** : In March 1949, RCA introduced the 7-inch single, which was initially designed to be played exclusively on special turntables manufactured by RCA. These record players were relatively small, described as being the size of a squat blender, offering a degree of portability, though they still required an electrical outlet. The prominent large hole in the center was a distinctive design feature of these records. `"When RCA introduced the 7-inch single in March of 1949, the original idea was to make them playable only on special turn tables made by RCA."` ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OuFvfZJKcpc
**Introduction of the 45 RPM Record** : RCA invented and introduced the 45 RPM record in 1949 with a larger, one-and-a-half-inch center hole. Their strategy was to promote a proprietary turntable system where users could stack 45s on a spindle for continuous play, requiring RCA-specific equipment. "This goes back to RCA's introduction of the 45 back in 1949. They were the ones to invent it." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yPok3MemDmE
**Involvement in Format War (Late 1940s)** : RCA was a participant in the "format war" of the late 1940s, competing with Columbia Records. RCA championed the 7-inch 45 RPM record, which maintained the standard of one song per side, in opposition to Columbia's long-playing album format. "and RCA's 7-inch 45 RPM record, which stuck with the standard one song per side." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QBCEmZ4Jheo
**Lou Reed's "Metal Machine Music" Release (1975)** : RCA released Lou Reed's experimental double album "Metal Machine Music" in the summer of 1975, not out of artistic support, but as a retaliatory move against Reed who was trying to get out of his contract. Reed was contractually obligated to deliver more albums and delivered this avant-garde work of noise and feedback, which RCA, in an "in-your-face" response, put out to the public. "RCA said, screw you and released it as an in-your-face move back at Lou." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iGav9uJwA_w
**Madam X Project and 7-inch Single Development (1949)** : After Columbia's LP announcement, RCA initiated project "Madam X" to develop its own vinyl record format to protect its market dominance. Engineers revisited 1930s archives and found notes from 1901 indicating experiments with a single-song format. As a consumer electronics company, RCA also planned to manufacture dedicated record players for their new records. This led to the introduction of the 7-inch single in February 1949. "Meanwhile, RCA knew that they had to do something. Otherwise, their market dominance in both North America and Europe would be in jeopardy. The one thing they did realize is that vinyl was a much better raw material for records than shellac, so they drew up plans for their own version of the vinyl record. They called the project Madam X." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VlIErdSSVOM
**Proprietary Spindle System** : RCA's 45 RPM records, with their large center holes, were designed to be played on RCA record players equipped with a special spindle. This proprietary design aimed to ensure that consumers would purchase RCA's turntables to play their new format. "First of all, if you wanted to play these records, you had to buy an RCA record player with a special spindle." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yPok3MemDmE
**Quad-8 Tapes (1970)** : In 1970, RCA introduced "Quad-8" quadraphonic 8-track tapes. These tapes were ultimately unsuccessful due to their poor quality and were withdrawn from the market by 1976. "In 1970, RCA introduced a quad-8-track tape they called the Quad-8. But they weren't very good and disappeared from the marketplace by 1976." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8BZMo41DjlY
**RCA's "How to Listen to New Dimensions in Sound" (1957)** : In 1957, RCA produced a short film titled "How to Listen to New Dimensions in Sound" to educate and promote stereo sound to consumers. This film discussed the appreciation of music at home and the pursuit of "perfect fidelity." "RCA released a short film entitled How to Listen to New Dimensions in Sound." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=cbSmBrhNACg
**Reaction to Columbia's LP Unveiling (1948)** : Columbia Records invited RCA executives to a private demonstration of their new LP technology in April 1948, two months before the public press conference. RCA "freaked out" upon realizing they had been severely "scooped" by technology they once possessed but had allowed to slip away due to a "dumb clerical error" (referring to their expired patents). Despite being offered a license for the technology, RCA refused. "Columbia invited RCA executives into their boardroom in April 1948, some two months before that big press conference, to demonstrate their new technology. RCA freaked out because they knew that they'd been scooped and scooped badly, and with technology that they once possessed. But let slip away due to some dumb clerical error. They offered to license the technology to RCA, but they said, no, no thanks." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VlIErdSSVOM
**Resistance to LP Format (Early 1950s)** : In the early 1950s, RCA was difficult about adopting the 12-inch 33 1/3 RPM long-playing album, insisting that their 7-inch 45 RPM single was the future and that consumers did not want song collections. "But our CA was being difficult, insisting that no one really wanted collections of songs and that their 7 inch 45 RPM single was the way the future." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GES0Ms_kaCI
**Stereo Record Pickup Development (Late 1950s)** : RCA's radio and controller division developed, designed, and manufactured a special stereo pickup for playing "Living Stereo" records. This pickup utilized two ceramic bars, allowing a single stylus to read the two separate soundtracks etched into the V-shaped groove. "When this pickup, the stylus is passing the two ceramic bars instead of one." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8BZMo41DjlY
