# label - Musak Corporation

## curiosities
**Business Model and Success** : While wired radio didn't appeal to consumers, businesses embraced the concept of "close circuit music," tailored to their environment 24 hours a day without interruption or static. The company became the world's biggest supplier of "elevator music," earning over $400 million a year by the 1970s distributing music worldwide from its Seattle headquarters. **Origin in "Wired Radio"** : The Musak Corporation originated from Brigadier General George Owen Squire's invention of "wired radio," a technology designed to compete with wireless radio by delivering music directly into homes and businesses via wires. Squire later renamed his invention "Music," playing on the words "music" and "Kodak." "Back before he died, in 1934, he came up with a new name for his invention. Playing with the words music and Kodak as in Kodak film, he came up with music." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xdn31jMOOA4
**Unlikely Connection to Indie Scene** : In the mid-1980s, Musak's Seattle headquarters became an unexpected incubator for the local indie music scene. An employee named Bruce Pavitt used his coffee breaks in the shipping room to run an independent record label, Subpop, devoted to local music, and Musak's payroll even supported several local musicians. "The music came from the shipping room where a music employee named Bruce Pabbett spent his coffee breaks running a new independent record label devoted to the local music scene." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xdn31jMOOA4
**Unofficial Slogan** : For a time, Musak executives considered "Boring work is made less boring by boring music" to be a fitting unofficial slogan, reflecting its use as a management tool and a way to fill silence in places like department stores and dentist offices.
