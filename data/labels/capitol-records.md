**"Incomprehensible Decisions" in Artist Management (Early 1960s)** : Capitol Records made several "incomprehensible decisions" regarding The Beach Boys' early releases. **"Optical Center" 45 RPM Adapter** : Capitol Records took inspiration from Fidelatone's adapter design to create their own solution called the "optical center." This adapter was a triangular piece of cardboard built into the big hole of a 45, covering most of the open space but leaving a central hole for an LP spindle. It could be punched out but not reinserted. "But then capital records took the Fidelatone idea and created an adapter that they called the optical center that was built into the big hole." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yPok3MemDmE ← capitol-records

**Early Success with Artwork (1945)** : Capitol Records released the King Cole Trio album in 1945, which spent most of a year at number one on the charts. This commercial success was a key factor in convincing the music industry of the significant value of album artwork in driving sales. "One of the biggest early successes was the King Cole trio. It was an album from that King Cole, issued by Capital Records in 1945. It was an old school album with 478 RPM records tucked into the sleeves. **Founding and Early Focus (Early 1940s)** : Capitol Records was established in the early 1940s by songwriter Johnny Mercer, who wrote many standards for artists like Frank Sinatra, Bing Crosby, and Tony Bennett. The label initially served as a home for these traditional artists, and aside from a small country music division, it largely lacked engagement with the emerging new forms of music such as rock and roll. **Ken Nelson's Role and Reputation** : Ken Nelson served as the head of country music for Capitol Records. He held a "very mixed reputation" among music lovers: praised for his "impeccable taste in artists," demonstrated by signing Buck Owens and The Louvin Brothers, but criticized for his tendency to impose styles on artists that didn't suit them, and a general misunderstanding of rockabilly.
