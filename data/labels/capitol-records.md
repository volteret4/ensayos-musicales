# label - Capitol Records

## curiosities
**"Optical Center" 45 RPM Adapter** : Capitol Records took inspiration from Fidelatone's adapter design to create their own solution called the "optical center." This adapter was a triangular piece of cardboard built into the big hole of a 45, covering most of the open space but leaving a central hole for an LP spindle. It could be punched out but not reinserted. "But then capital records took the Fidelatone idea and created an adapter that they called the optical center that was built into the big hole." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yPok3MemDmE
**Consequences of *Paul's Boutique*'s Commercial Failure** : The initial dismal sales of *Paul's Boutique* were such a significant commercial failure that the president of Capitol Records was fired for allowing the band to be signed to the label, and his was "not the only head that rolled." "It was such a stiff that the president of capital was fired for allowing the band to be signed to the label." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l1jPP1FhGo8
**Copy Protection Controversy** : In Europe, Capitol Records, the Beastie Boys' label, utilized MacroVision CDS2000, a copy protection software on "To the 5 Boroughs" CDs. While it didn't install malware like the Sony rootkit, its resemblance to a virus caused controversy and problems for the band. "In Europe, capital records, the Beastie's label, used something called MacroVision CDS2000, which was a rather insidious form of copy protection software that mimicked a virus." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9_BTe15NooI
**Distribution Partner for "Reflektor"** : Capitol Records, a division of the Universal Music Empire, entered into a "Back in Services" agreement with Arcade Fire for the release of their "Reflektor" album. This deal provided the independent band with the extensive distribution, promotion, and marketing capabilities typically offered by a major label, crucial for the album's broad reach. "First, they struck a deal with capital records, which is part of the Universal Music Empire." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ir2FfLQJNtY
**Early Success with Artwork (1945)** : Capitol Records released the King Cole Trio album in 1945, which spent most of a year at number one on the charts. This commercial success was a key factor in convincing the music industry of the significant value of album artwork in driving sales. "One of the biggest early successes was the King Cole trio. It was an album from that King Cole, issued by Capital Records in 1945. It was an old school album with 478 RPM records tucked into the sleeves. When it spent most of its year at number one on the charts, everybody was convinced about the value of artwork." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pwebM3TfSdg
**New Wave Profitability** : Capitol Records experienced astounding profits from The Knack's 1979 debut album, "Get The Knack," which became their fastest-selling debut since "Meet the Beatles" and fastest-selling single since "I Want to Hold Your Hand," demonstrating the significant financial returns possible from investing in New Wave bands. "The profit realized by capital on the NAC was astounding." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bYsV3ozbk4M
**President Fired for Paul's Boutique** : The commercial failure of the Beastie Boys' album "Paul's Boutique" was so significant that the president of Capitol Records was fired for allowing the band to be signed to the label, indicating a major misjudgment by the label at the time. "It was such a stiff that the president of Capital was fired for allowing the band to be signed to the label." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=cYwk-B3qwwk
**Recall of "Butcher Cover" Album (1966)** : Capitol Records, The Beatles' American record label, immediately recalled all 60,000 copies of "Yesterday and Today" with the "Butcher Cover" that had been sent to stores in 1966, and held back an additional 750,000 records in warehouses due to the controversial artwork. "Capital records, the band's American record label, immediately recalled all 60,000 albums that had been sent out." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tbmssCZ05ro
**Signing the Beastie Boys and Creative Freedom (Late 1980s)** : Capitol Records signed the Beastie Boys after their contentious departure from Def Jam, offering the group "total freedom" in making their next album. However, the label was expecting a commercial success akin to *Licensed to Ill* but instead received *Paul's Boutique*, an unexpected and commercially challenging record. "But capital was expecting another license to yield. What they got was something unexpected and something very ahead of its time." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l1jPP1FhGo8
