# label - Factory Records

## curiosities
**Aesthetic Focus and Hacienda Nightclub** : The label was known for its strong aesthetic, meticulous attention to album artwork and packaging, and a quirky cataloging system. It also established the Hacienda nightclub, which became a central hub for British dance culture throughout the 1980s. "And with Factory, it was more than just about the music. It was all about the aesthetic, the care given to album artwork and packaging, the quirkiness of their cataloging system, the establishment of the Hasey end nightclub, which became ground zero for so much of British dance culture throughout the 1980s." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VOv5mT1dJd8
**Artistic Freedom Policy** : Factory Records was known for allowing its artists, such as the Happy Mondays, considerable artistic freedom, letting them "do what we want" despite having formal contracts. "They would pray. They would let us do what we want." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=q_Scyr2czh8
**Bankruptcy Cause** : Factory Records' bankruptcy was primarily due to bad management, not excessive spending by bands like the Happy Mondays, who were actually owed significant money by the label when it went under. "Bad management broke them." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KF_adyXf86Q
**Bankrupted by Happy Mondays** : The drug habits of the band Happy Mondays were so extreme that they ultimately led to the financial collapse and demise of their record label, Factory Records. "Their drug habits killed their record label factory records." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=i5lR8zz7_ZE
**Business Acumen and Bankruptcy** : Factory Records was notably inept at business. A prime example is "Blue Monday," where substantial investment in the sleeve's design and printing resulted in a loss of 30 pence per copy sold, totaling "tens of thousands of pounds." This mismanagement ultimately led to Factory going bankrupt twice. "This highlights a problem with many of those great British indie labels of the 80s. They were crap at doing business. Factory would eventually go bankrupt twice..." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zB5zxycrbnY
**Business Challenges and Bankruptcy** : Despite its musical influence, Factory Records faced significant business challenges, including never asking bands to sign formal contracts, dubious accounting practices, and a consistent lack of attention to cash flow. As a result, the label eventually went spectacularly bankrupt. "As a business, Factory was a basket case, and eventually they went broke in a spectacular way." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VOv5mT1dJd8
**Co-owner of The Hacienda** : Factory Records co-owned The Hacienda, a famous club in Manchester, along with New Order. The club was initially financially troubled but became a vibrant center of UK club culture, known for its pivotal role in the rave scene, before its eventual decline. "the infamous club owned by New Order and Factory Records." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OiqdZfxdBP0
**First Release** : Factory Records' inaugural release occurred on December 24, 1978. It consisted of two 7-inch singles bundled together, featuring samples from four bands on its roster, with Side A including two songs by Joy Division, notably "Digital." "The very first factory release was issued on December 24, 1978. Two 7-inch singles bundled together featuring samples from four bands in the roster." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VOv5mT1dJd8
**Founded by Tony Wilson** : This label was founded by Manchester TV host Tony Wilson, who was inspired after attending the Sex Pistols show in 1976. The name "Factory Records" was derived from a local nightclub night. "He called the company Factory Records after a local nightclub night." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VOv5mT1dJd8
**Founding and Mission** : Factory Records was a prominent British indie label established by Tony Wilson, a TV host from Manchester, who sought to showcase the music of his city. Its history is "lightly fictionalized" in the movie "24 Hour Party People." "Factory, which was founded by Tony Wilson, a TV host and manchester who decided that someone needed to showcase the music of his city." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zB5zxycrbnY
**Importance to The Durutti Column** : Factory Records considered The Durutti Column, a band that Dave Rowbotham was a part of, to be "very important." This highlights the label's recognition of the band's significance within the punk and post-punk scenes. "He was in a bunch of punk and post-punk bands before landing in the Deritty column, a group considered to be very important by factory records." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Q6dAX6HCjgw
**Independent Label in Post-Punk Era** : Factory Records was one of the many independent labels that emerged in the wake of punk, providing a platform for artists like Joy Division. "They recorded four factory records, one of the many independent labels that sprung up in the wake of punk." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uTORLOm5Yg
**Influential Art Design (1970s-1980s)** : Factory Records, based in Manchester, was a renowned company where Peter Saville served as the chief art design guy. Saville was responsible for a wide range of design work, including gig posters and album covers for prominent bands such as Joy Division, New Order, Roxy Music, Suede, Pulp, Martha and the Muffins, and No Means No. "Seville was the chief art design guy at factory records back in the day, the famous company based on a Manchester. He did everything from gig posters to album design for Joy Division New Order, Roxas music, Swade, pulp, Martha Nemuffins, and Noam D." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pwebM3TfSdg
**Influential British Indie Label** : Factory Records is recognized as one of the great British indie labels of all time, serving as the source for influential music from bands such as Joy Division, New Order, Happy Mondays, and Cabaret Voltaire. "Factory went on to become one of the great British indie labels of all time. So much music flowed from this company." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VOv5mT1dJd8
**Manchester-Based Independent Label** : Factory Records was an independent label based out of Manchester, which was not considered a "cool place to be" at the time compared to London. It was the label for archetypical British indie band Joy Division. "They recorded it for factory, an independent label based out of Manchester when Manchester really wasn't much of a cool place to be for anything." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LgJ7ww2AgF4
**Out of Business** : Factory Records eventually went out of business, contributing to the decline of the Madchester scene, alongside other issues faced by its prominent bands and venues. "and factory records was out of business. Again." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DSvoppf1dGY
**Relationship with Happy Mondays** : Factory Records had a complex relationship with the Happy Mondays; while head Tony Wilson loved the band, many others at the label, including other bands and managers, did not take to them. "Everyone at Factory hated us, apart from Tony loved us." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KF_adyXf86Q
**Rob Gretton's Influence** : Factory Records was a new, "very cool" indie label. Rob Gretton, manager of Joy Division, played a key role in its growth by helping to sign other significant acts like Happy Mondays and OMD to the label. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PzyvXmDKFUc
**The Haçienda Co-ownership** : Factory Records co-owned The Haçienda, the Manchester nightclub that was a central part of the acid house rave scene. "the Haseyenda, the Manchester Nightclub co-owned by New Order in factory records." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DSvoppf1dGY
**The Haçienda Management and Serial Numbers (1982-1997)** : Factory Records, headed by Tony Wilson, was the label behind New Order and the concept originator for The Haçienda nightclub. Like all its projects, the club was given a serial number, FAC51, underscoring its integrated role within the label's artistic and business ventures. "Anyway, like all factory projects and record releases, it had a serial number. FAC51, FAC51." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=2RKchFW42Jc
**Tony Wilson's Character** : Tony Wilson, the head of Factory Records, was described as more of an artist and a supporter of artists than a shrewd businessman. "Tony wasn't the greatest businessman. He was, he was an artist and a supporter of the artist." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KF_adyXf86Q
**Tony Wilson's Role** : Tony Wilson, associated with Factory Records, was a proponent of the theory that rock and pop music are engaged in a death struggle, playing out in cycles. His involvement lends credibility to historical observations about the cyclical nature of music trends. "That theory contends that rock and pop music are locked in a death struggle that plays itself out like clockwork every 13 years." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-CT7yYXonIk
