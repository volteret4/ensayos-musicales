# label - Decca Records

## curiosities
**Brief Contract with The Shirelles** : Decca Records picked up The Shirelles' song "I Met Him On A Sunday" after its minor local success, buying the group's contract from Florence Greenberg. Although the song charted at number 50, Decca dropped the group after two subsequent singles failed to achieve success. **Copyright Holder for "The Rolling Stones Songbook"** : Decca Records held the copyright for "The Rolling Stones Songbook," an album released in 1966 by The Andrew Loog Oldham Orchestra. The Verve initially negotiated with Decca to license a six-second string figure from this album for their song "Bittersweet Symphony," but Decca did not hold all the necessary rights to The Rolling Stones' catalog, leading to further legal complications. **Dominant Market Share (Early 1960s)** : In the early 1960s, Decca and EMI together controlled approximately 85% of the British record market, effectively constituting the entire record industry at the time. "EMI and Decker between them had something like 85% of the market, and basically were the record industry in Britain at the time." ← Episode 113： ＂Needles and Pins＂ by The Searchers | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=izC9ZBI0UHM
**First Recording Home for Wanda Jackson** : After being passed over by Capitol Records due to her age, Wanda Jackson was signed to Decca Records. She made her first recordings there with members of Hank Thompson's band. Her first single, a duet with Billy Gray, became a top 10 country hit. However, subsequent solo recordings were less successful, leading to her dissatisfaction and eventual departure from the label when her contract was up. ← Episode 43： ＂I Gotta Know＂ by Wanda Jackson | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gEInec4z65Q
**Gender Discrimination Policy** : Decca Records, a prominent record label of its era, maintained a discriminatory company policy that barred women from being hired in engineering or sound production roles. Women were only considered for positions such as cleaners or assistants, illustrating significant gender bias in the music industry at the time. Despite the backing of a major label, the single sold poorly, leading Jones to leave the band after only a few weeks.
