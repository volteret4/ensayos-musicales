# label - BAS (Bay Area Seating Service)

## curiosities
**Expansion and Demise of BAS Worldwide (1979)** : An attempt to expand BAS beyond its regional markets into international territories like Toronto and Vancouver, under the name "BAS Worldwide" (standing for Best Available Seating Service), ultimately collapsed in 1979. "Meanwhile, Bass Worldwide, an attempt to reach beyond the company's regional markets to places like Toronto and Vancouver, collapsed in 1979." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OCkov6FTliE
**Failed Membership Model** : BAS Worldwide offered an annual membership that provided first dibs on the same seats for every show, with members moving up in rank for better seats over time. This innovative idea, though promising, failed along with the company's collapse. "An interesting thing about them, you could elect to pay an annual membership where you had first dibs on the same seats for every show." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OCkov6FTliE
**Foundation from Stolen Code and Jerry Seltzer's Vision** : BAS was founded using stolen code from Select-A-Seat, initially standing for Bay Area Seating Service. It was spearheaded by Jerry Seltzer, who leveraged his experience as a roller derby promoter to ensure the ticketing system met the needs of the buyer. "This was the foundation of a new company called BAS, which in the beginning stood for Bay Area Seating Service." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OCkov6FTliE
**Music-Focused Distribution and Client Issues (1974)** : BAS officially began operations in December 1974, strategically placing its terminals in music-focused outlets like record stores and stereo shops. However, it encountered problems with clients like department stores (Seer stores) who disliked the "people rock concert sales brought in" and consequently dropped BAS. "Their outlets were very music focused and were installed in record stores and stereo shops. Seer stores were client at first, but they hated the people rock concert sales brought in so they dumped BAS." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OCkov6FTliE
**Rapid Development and Promoter-Centric Approach** : New code for BAS was written in under six weeks, and the company quickly became operational. Seltzer's background as a promoter provided a unique advantage, enabling him to guide coders with a ticket-buyer's perspective, leading to a very successful approach despite a lawsuit over the stolen code. "Jerry's advantage was that he was the head of the roller derby league, which made him a promoter. He knew about selling tickets." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OCkov6FTliE
**Technology and Service Charges (1970s)** : The company operated with 24 terminals connected via telephone lines using 1,200-baud modems, which was not particularly fast. BAS charged a service fee of 50 cents per ticket. This fee, representing about 8% of a typical $6 ticket face value, caused considerable public outcry and accusations of "rip off." "Things were settled on 24 terminals, all running on telephone lines using a 1,200-bodd modem. Graham became a client of BAS, which by this point had a service charge of 50 cents per ticket." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OCkov6FTliE
