## curiosities
**Cessation of MP3 Support (2017)** : In early 2017, the Fraunhofer Institute formally announced its decision to cease support and licensing for MP3 technology, acknowledging its status as an outdated format. The organization stated it would no longer pursue improvements for the MP3 and highlighted the availability of superior audio codecs, including AAC and MP8, signaling its intent to allow better technologies to supersede it. "In early 2017, it announced it was stopping support and licensing of MP3 technology." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v7DXXu_1fvM
**Failed Standards Competitions** : The Fraunhofer team's Layer 3 algorithm consistently lost against Musicam's Layer 2 in multiple standards competitions. Despite Layer 3 offering superior sound quality, it was perceived as too complex, leading to Layer 2 being adopted for various applications including digital FM radio, interactive CD-ROMs, video compact disc, digital audio tape, HDTV, and audio on DVDs. Fraunhofer recorded a 0 for 7 record in these competitions. "So Fraunhofer was 0 for 6 in standards competitions." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v7DXXu_1fvM
**First Experimental MP3 (Funky.mp3) (1994)** : In 1994, to avoid copyright issues during their experiments, the Fraunhofer team utilized an original piece of music created by one of their own research team members, Yergen Hare, titled "funky.mp3." This file was distributed with a strict 30-day license, permitting only compression and decompression for experimental purposes. "So at first, and again, this is 1994, they used a piece of music written by one of their own research team members named Yergen Hare. And he called it funky.mp3." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v7DXXu_1fvM
**Free MP3 Encoder Distribution (1994-1995)** : Between 1994 and 1995, the Fraunhofer team distributed thousands of 3.5-inch floppy disks containing a free Layer 3 encoder for personal computers at various trade shows. This strategic move was instrumental in enabling everyday consumers to create their own Layer 3 files, especially as Intel's powerful Pentium chips became widely available, providing the necessary processing power. "Thousands of those 3.5-inch floppy disks were distributed by front-hofer at trade shows through 1994-1995." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v7DXXu_1fvM
**High Quality Low Bitrate Audio Coding Research (1987)** : In 1987, the Fraunhofer group intensified its research into what they termed "high quality low bitrate audio coding." Their objective was to compress a full-sized audio file from a compact disk (WAV file) to one-twelfth its original size while preserving as much audible quality as possible, with the initial focus on creating files for personal computer storage rather than streaming. "Research intensified in 1987 into something that became known as high quality low bitrate audio coding." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v7DXXu_1fvM
**Hockey Game Audio for MP3 Algorithm Testing** : To refine their MP3 compression algorithms, Fraunhofer engineers utilized raw audio recordings from hockey games as one of their two primary test sound sources. The complex and unique sounds produced in an arena, such as skates slashing ice and pucks hitting boards, presented a significant challenge for clean compression, prompting engineers to spend thousands of hours on this specific audio. "The first was, believe it or not, recording raw audio from hockey games. There was something about how hockey sounds in an arena with the skate slashing the ice and the puck booming off the boards that proved to be really tricky to compress cleanly. Frown-hofer engineers spent thousands of hours listening to the sounds of hockey and trying to compress them properly." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fhq9WVeiE_U
**Initial Patent Attempt (Denied)** : Professor Dieter Sitzer, who mentored Carl Heinz Brandenburg, filed a patent application for transmitting speech and music over copper wires. However, his application was denied, with the examiner deeming the concept "impossible." The Fraunhofer group later succeeded in acquiring a patent for their audio compression technology, building upon the foundational work that Sitzer had pioneered. "He filed a patent on it, but was denied because the person examining the patent application told him, this is impossible and we don't issue patents for impossible things." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v7DXXu_1fvM
**MP3 Algorithm Development** : The Fraunhofer Society, a publicly funded research organization in Germany, was home to Carl Heinz Brandenburg and his team of approximately a dozen individuals who developed the software algorithm that became known as MP3. This algorithm was eventually chosen as the international standard for compressed audio on CD-ROMs, though initially met with disinterest. "He worked at the Fraunhofer Society, a publicly owned research organization based in Germany, and he too had a vision." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v7DXXu_1fvM
**MP3 Technology Development** : The Fraunhofer Society in Germany was responsible for developing the MP3 technology. Their initial goal was to find efficient ways to transmit large quantities of audio data over older copper telephone wires, which they achieved by creating an algorithm that compressed data by removing non-essential information. "This technology was developed by the frown-hofer society in Germany. Initially, they were looking for ways to send large amounts of audio data through old, telephone, copper wires. And the only way to do that was to compress the data using an algorithm that stripped out everything that was non-essential." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fhq9WVeiE_U
**MP3 Technology Licensing (1993)** : In 1993, the Fraunhofer Society licensed its MP3 compression technology broadly to various parties, contributing to its widespread adoption and impact on digital music. **NHL Broadcast Breakthrough (1995)** : The Fraunhofer team achieved its first major breakthrough when Steve Church of Tell Us licensed Zeffer boxes, which ran on MPEG Layer 3 technology, to the National Hockey League. Beginning on January 20, 1995, these boxes provided significantly superior and more cost-effective audio quality for play-by-play broadcasts than previous methods, leading to their installation in every NHL arena. "It was the break the front-hover team was looking for." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v7DXXu_1fvM
**Psychoacoustics Experiments** : The Fraunhofer team conducted an extensive series of experiments grounded in the principles of psychoacoustics. They meticulously tested ten-second audio clips from CDs, compressing them and then using highly expensive headphones to identify any audible errors or glitches, such as "Muddy" or "Hiss," and worked to mitigate artifacts like double speak and pre-echo. "Starting with the theories of psychoacoustics, the Fraunhofer team embarked on a long series of experiments." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v7DXXu_1fvM
**Public Website and Shareware (1995)** : In late 1995, the Fraunhofer Institute launched a public website specifically for their new MP3 technology, offering free downloads of the MP3 encoder as shareware for DOS, Windows, and Linux operating systems. This initiative perfectly coincided with the public's increasing engagement with the Internet, allowing early adopters to easily download and share music. "In late 1995, the Fraudhofer Institute created a public website dedicated to their new MP3 technology." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v7DXXu_1fvM
