# label - Columbia Records

## curiosities
**Development of the Micro-Groove Record (LP)** : Columbia Records was responsible for developing the micro-groove record, also known as the Long-Playing record (LP), as a solution to the limitations of 78 RPM records. Led by president Edward Wallerstein and in-house engineer Dr. Peter Goldmark, Columbia refined polyvinyl chloride (Vinylite from Union Carbide) to create records with grooves cut closer together and a smoother surface, resulting in more music per side (20 minutes on a 12-inch disc) and less noise. "It fell to Columbia Records to come up with a solution, which is where we get back to this plastic polyvinyl chloride, which had been refined by Union Carbide of the 1930s under the name vinylite. Led by a guy named Edward Wallerstein, the president of Columbia, and at in-house engineer named Dr. Peter Goldmark, Columbia started working on what they called the micro-groove record." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VlIErdSSVOM

**Introduction of 12-inch LP (June 1948)** : Columbia Records revolutionized music packaging with the introduction of the 12-inch long-playing (LP) vinyl album in June 1948. This new format, with its larger 12x12 inch double-sided packaging, demanded more content, especially as LPs were initially targeted at "high-brow" genres like classical music, Broadway show tunes, movie soundtracks, and jazz. This led to the commissioning of essays and editorial content, often written by journalists, musicologists, artists, or label executives, to educate listeners. "When Columbia Records introduced the 12-inch long playing vinyl album in June 1948, the game began to change." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KaIp6KVn-co

**Pioneer of Original Record Cover Artwork (1938)** : Columbia Records was convinced by designer Alex Steinweiss in 1938 to introduce original graphic designs for record sleeves, starting with Rodgers and Hart's "Smash Song Hits." This innovation significantly boosted record sales and led other labels to adopt the practice. "He convinced the executives at Columbia Records that some fancy artwork would be good for sales." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gP3oQeYEvso
**Pioneering Album Artwork (1938)** : Columbia Records hired 22-year-old artist Alex Steinweiss in 1938 as their art director, initially for store displays and newspaper ads. Steinweiss, recognizing the durability of new cardboard record jackets and the need to protect LP records from acidic paper sleeves, convinced the label to print artwork directly on the jackets. In 1939, for a new Rogers and Hammerstein record, he photographed a marquee he temporarily altered at the Imperial Theatre. Despite initial reluctance from management over the additional $2,500 cost, the record was released with his artwork and saw sales 900% above expectations, marking the first-ever example of album cover artwork. "In 1938, Columbia Records had an idea. They hired a 22-year-old guy named Alex Steinweiss, an artist who was very good at poster art." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pwebM3TfSdg
**Pioneering Double-Sided Discs (1908)** : Columbia Records was the first record company to introduce double-sided discs in 1908, a revolutionary development in the early recorded music industry. This innovation significantly increased the audio capacity of flat discs compared to earlier cylinders. "in 1908, Columbia Records became the first record company to offer double-sided discs. This was radical and revolutionary." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QBCEmZ4Jheo
**Pioneering Vinyl Records in Response to Shellac Crisis** : In response to the 1940s shellac shortage caused by World War II, Columbia Records tasked its chief engineer, Dr. Peter Goldmark, with finding a substitute material for phonograph records. Goldmark's team developed a solution using polyvinyl chloride (vinyl), an artificial substance previously used for sewer pipes in the 1920s. Vinyl proved more durable and allowed grooves to be cut closer together, increasing music capacity fivefold compared to 78 RPM shellac discs, thus catalyzing the recorded music industry's transformation into a multi-billion-dollar business with the introduction of 12-inch 33 1/3 RPM LPs and 7-inch 45 RPM singles. "This is where Columbia records stepped in with their chief engineer, a guy by the name of Dr. Peter Goldmark." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_Pi7JLwAeOI
**Quadraphonic Record Development (1970s)** : Columbia Records was a key player, alongside companies like Sony and EMI, in the effort to develop technologies for encoding four distinct audio channels onto a single vinyl record groove for quadraphonic sound. These companies, including Columbia, ultimately agreed upon the SQ system as a shared approach. "A lot of companies worked on this, including Sony, Columbia Records, and EMI. And they all agreed upon a system called SQ." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8BZMo41DjlY

**Special 7-inch Records for Highway High Phi** : Columbia Records produced special 7-inch records specifically for Chrysler's Highway High Phi in-car turntable system, introduced in 1955. These unique records were designed to hold approximately 75 minutes of music per side. They required many grooves and a slow playback speed of 16 and 2/3 RPM, resulting in "awful" audio quality, and were exclusively available at Chrysler dealerships. `"It was called the Highway High Phi, and it played special 7-inch records made by Columbia records that could hold about 75 minutes of music per side."` ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OuFvfZJKcpc
**The Record Jacket Invention** : Columbia Records worked on 12-inch long-playing vinyl album technology, which highlighted the problem of acidic paper sleeves damaging records. Alex Steinweiss designed a new cardboard sleeve, known as the "record jacket," which the Imperial Paper Box Company patented. This new jacket became a "palette for a new art form" suitable for printing artwork. "He designed a new sleeve made from cardboard and got a box company to develop some special equipment. The result was known as the record jacket." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pwebM3TfSdg
**Unveiling of the LP (June 18, 1948)** : Columbia Records officially unveiled the 12-inch long-playing album at the Waldorf Astoria Hotel in Park Avenue, New York, on June 18, 1948. Forty members of the press witnessed a dramatic demonstration comparing an 8-foot stack of 78 RPM records to a 15-inch stack of LPs. Edward Wallerstein demonstrated a 4-minute 78 RPM classical recording, abruptly stopping, then played a 22.5-minute uninterrupted version on an LP, stunning reporters. "The big day came on June 18, 1948 at the Waldorf Astoria Hotel in Park Avenue, New York. Forty members of the press entered a room where they saw a big stack of 78 RPM records. It was more than 8 feet high. Next to it was another stack of flat, some things, but it was only 15 inches high. Edward Wallerstein stood up. You're here to witness the unveiling of a revolutionary new technology, he said." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VlIErdSSVOM
