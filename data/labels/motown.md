# label - Motown

## curiosities
**Barry Gordy's Praise for Stevie Wonder** : Motown Founder Barry Gordy lauded Stevie Wonder's 1976 album, *Songs in the Key of Life*, remarking that Stevie successfully took his life experiences and "put them all into this album and it worked." **Bobby Jean Hall's Extensive Contributions** : Bobby Jean Hall was a prolific percussionist whose work is featured on a vast number of Motown records. Her contributions were integral to recordings by seminal artists like The Temptations, Diana Ross and The Supremes, and Smokey Robinson and The Miracles. "Bobby Jean Hall is an all-around percussionist who can be heard on a ton of Motown records, including recordings by the temptations, Diana Rosses and The Spremes, and Smokey Robinson in The Miracles." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9GHpC0kCgHY ← motown
**Criticism for Lack of Political Content** : Motown faced criticism from African Americans for its perceived reluctance to be more politically outspoken in its music. This was largely due to Berry Gordy's cautious approach, as he remained hesitant about overtly political messaging in his releases until the early 1970s. "Like James Brown Motown was also criticized by African Americans for not being a little more political but Barry Gordy was always afraid of that at least up until the early 1970s." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 22 - Power to the People - The Music of the Black Power Movement | https://seventies.libsyn.com/022-power-to-the-people-62520-11-01-am
**Crossover Goal vs. Black Power Ethos** : Historically, Motown's primary goal was to achieve crossover success with a white audience, as this guaranteed radio play and increased record sales. However, this objective became antithetical to the core principles of the Black Power movement, which prioritized black self-determination and cultural pride over seeking approval or acceptance from white audiences. "Yes, that was the goal in the past especially for Motown because crossing over to a white audience meant radio play and record sales but it would be counter to the whole goal of the black power movement to care if white people liked the soul music that was being produced." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 22 - Power to the People - The Music of the Black Power Movement | https://seventies.libsyn.com/022-power-to-the-people-62520-11-01-am
**Current Status** : Today, Motown is regarded as a mere shadow of its former self, having lost the prominence and influence it once held in the music industry. **Demo Destruction Policy** : Motown pressed about 250 demo copies of Frank Wilson's "Do I Love You Indeed I Do?" in 1965, but these were largely destroyed because Motown head Berry Gordy did not believe the demo was any good, resulting in only 2 to 5 copies surviving. "About 250 copies of the demo were pressed up because neither Wilson nor Motown had buried gordy, but that the demo was any good. Those 250 records were destroyed, except maybe between two and at most five copies." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tbmssCZ05ro ← motown
**Discovery of Important Black Artists** : Motown was renowned for discovering and launching the careers of numerous important black artists, including Lionel Richie and Tina Turner. "descubrió la nera de la niña del Richie, descubrió la tina, tarné, descubrió a todos los artistas negros importantes." ← Music Radar Clan > We are the world | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XTJKpU0lhls&list=PLBFijugu0fhAAIR-Ibh59aaU8VWg-SkLc ← motown
**Henry Cosby as Staff Producer** : Motown employed Henry Cosby as a staff producer. Cosby was also a co-writer of Stevie Wonder's 1966 hit "Uptight Everything's All Right," which later gained a share of royalties from Oasis's "Step Out" due to musical similarities. "a staff producer at Motown named Henry Cosby." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=L09R2evjyMc ← motown
**Influence on Black Music (1960s-1970s)** : Motown Records was one of the most important record labels, known for producing all significant records in black music, including genres like R&B, funk, 1960s disco, and romantic music, particularly between the 1960s and 1970s. "la visina de las discurráficas más importantes y la música que he dicho todos los discos importantes para que antes de los años 60 y 70 sobre todo en lo que fue la música negra, el ratal blar, el funky, la música disco de los años 60 incluso la música más romántica." ← Music Radar Clan > We are the world | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XTJKpU0lhls&list=PLBFijugu0fhAAIR-Ibh59aaU8VWg-SkLc ← motown
**Shift from Formulaic Sound** : By 1971, Motown began to move away from its established and highly popular formula, particularly one that had found widespread acceptance among white audiences. This evolution allowed for a "very different funky or sound," as exemplified by songs like The Undisputed Truth's "Smiling Faces Sometimes." "It's 1971 and Motown is finally starting to get out of that formula that they had that was so popular in particular with white people that's a very different funky or sound for Motown." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 22 - Power to the People - The Music of the Black Power Movement | https://seventies.libsyn.com/022-power-to-the-people-62520-11-01-am
**We Are The World Inception Point** : The initial idea to create and record the song "We Are The World," and to gather all the participating artists, was conceived at the Motown house. "la idea de grabar el disco, la idea de la canción, la idea de contra todos los artistas, se gestó en la casa de la mota." ← Music Radar Clan > We are the world | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XTJKpU0lhls&list=PLBFijugu0fhAAIR-Ibh59aaU8VWg-SkLc ← motown
