**Criticism for Lack of Political Content** : Motown faced criticism from African Americans for its perceived reluctance to be more politically outspoken in its music. This was largely due to Berry Gordy's cautious approach, as he remained hesitant about overtly political messaging in his releases until the early 1970s. "Like James Brown Motown was also criticized by African Americans for not being a little more political but Barry Gordy was always afraid of that at least up until the early 1970s." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 22 - Power to the People - The Music of the Black Power Movement | https://seventies.libsyn.com/022-power-to-the-people-62520-11-01-am ← motown
**Crossover Goal vs. Black Power Ethos** : Historically, Motown's primary goal was to achieve crossover success with a white audience, as this guaranteed radio play and increased record sales. However, this objective became antithetical to the core principles of the Black Power movement, which prioritized black self-determination and cultural pride over seeking approval or acceptance from white audiences. "Yes, that was the goal in the past especially for Motown because crossing over to a white audience meant radio play and record sales but it would be counter to the whole goal of the black power movement to care if white people liked the soul music that was being produced." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 22 - Power to the People - The Music of the Black Power Movement | https://seventies.libsyn.com/022-power-to-the-people-62520-11-01-am ← motown

**Demo Destruction Policy** : Motown pressed about 250 demo copies of Frank Wilson's "Do I Love You Indeed I Do?" in 1965, but these were largely destroyed because Motown head Berry Gordy did not believe the demo was any good, resulting in only 2 to 5 copies surviving. **Evolving Slogan: "Create, Sell, and Collect"** : Barry Gordy's original Motown slogan, "create, make, and sell," was changed to "create, sell, and collect." This revision reflected his realization that simply selling records was insufficient if the label wasn't getting paid by distributors, highlighting the crucial business aspect of the music industry. "He changed that slogan to create, sell, and collect." ← Episode 93： ＂Please Mr. Postman＂ by the Marvelettes | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EPg8Wx30-uM
**Exclusion of First #1 Artists from Anniversaries** : Despite The Marvelettes being the first group to score a number one hit for Motown, none of its original members were invited to participate in any of the label's 25th, 40th, 50th, or 60th-anniversary celebrations or TV specials. "None of the members of the first group to hit number one on the label were invited to be part of any of them." ← Episode 93： ＂Please Mr. Postman＂ by the Marvelettes | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EPg8Wx30-uM
**Financial Crisis and "Please Mr. Postman"** : Following the million-selling "Shop Around," Motown's subsequent releases performed poorly, leading to a severe financial crisis. Distributors were not paying for records, pushing the label close to bankruptcy; "Please Mr. Postman" was the critical hit that saved Motown by ensuring they got paid for past successes. "And please Mr. Postman was the record that saved Motown. I've talked before about how a hit record could put a small label out of business." ← Episode 93： ＂Please Mr. Postman＂ by the Marvelettes | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EPg8Wx30-uM

**Move to Los Angeles** : In the early 1970s, Motown Records relocated its operations from Detroit to Los Angeles. This move prompted some artists, like Martha Reeves, to decide not to make the transition with the label, leading to the dissolution of groups like Martha and the Van Dellers. "When Motown moved from Detroit to LA in the early 70s, Martha was one of those who decided not to make the move with the label" ← Episode 111： ＂Heat Wave＂ by Martha and the Vandellas | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AAiUJhSKjzs
**Multitracking System Innovation** : Mickey Stevenson instituted a new multitracking system at Motown, moving away from recording all musicians, lead singers, and backing vocalists simultaneously. This allowed backing tracks to be recorded separately by The Funk Brothers, with vocals overdubbed later, improving efficiency and flexibility. It also enabled reusing backing tracks for different artists (e.g., The Miracles' hits for Marvelettes covers) and replacing singers if unsuitable. "Now, backing tracks would be recorded by the Funt Brothers in the studio, whenever a writer produced her had something for them to record, and then the singer would come in later and overdubbed their vocals when it was convenient to do that." ← Episode 111： ＂Heat Wave＂ by Martha and the Vandellas | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AAiUJhSKjzs
**Ownership of Group Names** : Motown, rather than the artists, owned the names of its groups, a practice that allowed the label to sell off names like "The Marvelettes" after groups disbanded. This meant original members had to tour under modified names. "Motein, rather than the women, had owned the group name and had sold it off." ← Episode 93： ＂Please Mr. Postman＂ by the Marvelettes | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EPg8Wx30-uM
**Policy for Hit Follow-Ups** : Motown adopted a strict policy: after an artist had a hit, the same producers and songwriters were assigned to create new material that sounded similar to the original hit. Artists were only permitted to diverge from this formula once their current hit-making strategy ceased to be successful. "This would be the policy from this point on with Motown. If someone had a hit, the same producers and songwriters would be a sign to come up with something that sounded like the hit, and the artist would only go in a different direction once they stopped having hits with their original formula." ← Episode 93： ＂Please Mr. Postman＂ by the Marvelettes | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EPg8Wx30-uM
**Shift from Formulaic Sound** : By 1971, Motown began to move away from its established and highly popular formula, particularly one that had found widespread acceptance among white audiences. This evolution allowed for a "very different funky or sound," as exemplified by songs like The Undisputed Truth's "Smiling Faces Sometimes." "It's 1971 and Motown is finally starting to get out of that formula that they had that was so popular in particular with white people that's a very different funky or sound for Motown." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 22 - Power to the People - The Music of the Black Power Movement | https://seventies.libsyn.com/022-power-to-the-people-62520-11-01-am ← motown
**Union Rule Circumvention** : The Musician's Union had a rule requiring a vocalist to be present if musicians were cutting a vocal track. Motown, like other labels, often broke this rule. A surprise union inspection during a Mary Wells session led Mickey Stevenson to have his secretary, Martha Reeves, sing the lead to avoid penalties, ultimately leading to Martha taking over the song. "Motown, like most labels, were perfectly happy to break the union rules on occasion, but there was always the possibility of a surprise union inspection" ← Episode 111： ＂Heat Wave＂ by Martha and the Vandellas | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AAiUJhSKjzs
