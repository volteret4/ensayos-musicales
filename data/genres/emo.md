# genre - Emo

## curiosities
**Anti-Commercial Ethos in Second Wave** : The second wave of emo largely maintained an underground and anti-commercial stance. It was considered "uncool" for bands to sell t-shirts at shows, cover charges for gigs were typically five dollars or less, and charging more than ten dollars for a CD was deemed "gouging." "And everything stayed underground and very anti-commercial. For example, if you were in an emo band, it was almost considered uncool to even sell t-shirts at your show. Covered charge for a gig had to be five bucks or less. And if you charge more than ten dollars for a CD, well, that was considered gouging." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v_am7ifh7Pk
**Catharsis of Emo Shows** : Emo shows during the third wave provided a hugely cathartic experience for fans, allowing them to collectively express and scream out their pain and fear alongside hundreds or thousands of others. "Fans loved it. There was something hugely cathartic about being able to scream your pain and fear with hundreds or even thousands of people out of show." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v_am7ifh7Pk
**Commercial Success and Major Label Interest (Third Wave)** : Third wave emo bands operated in an era where commercial success was attainable. Major labels showed significant interest, hoping to position emo as "the next grunge," and radio stations actively played emo-ish music. Many emo records from bands like New Found Glory, Fall Out Boy, Panic! at the Disco, Taking Back Sunday, The All-American Rejects, Simple Plan, and Alexisonfire achieved top 10 album chart positions. "The one thing just about all these third wave emo bands had in common was that they lived in an era where commercial success wasn't impossible. Major labels were interested, very interested, in turning emo into the next grunge. Radio was interested and was playing a lot of emo-ish music. Emo records from bands like Newfound Glory, Fallout Boy, Patek at the Disco, Taking Back Sunday, All American Rejects, Simple Plan, and Alexis on Fire all rocketed into the top 10 on the album charts." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v_am7ifh7Pk
**Comparison to Punk and Ska Revivals** : Emo revivals are noted to differ from punk and ska revivals. Instead of undergoing a full period of hibernation before returning, emo has arrived in distinct, sometimes overlapping, waves. "emo is slightly different than the punk and sky revivals in that it has come at us in different waves instead of going into full-on hibernation for a while and then coming back." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v_am7ifh7Pk
**Dark Turn in Third Wave Lyrics** : As third wave emo grew, some bands incorporated darker lyrical themes, including violent imagery, references to self-harm (like cutting), and hateful expressions directed at women. This content later became a source of concern and regret for fans and bands looking back. "Some bands dipped into writing lyrics that contained violent imagery, talk of self-harm, like cutting, and more than a few hateful stabs are doing harm to women. This became a real problem later when fans and bands look back at some of the music and said, what were we thinking?" ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v_am7ifh7Pk

"Third, emo, which had been a driving force of punk pop a decade earlier, had largely gone out of fashion." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sOU54d8mr5k
**Decline of Third Wave Emo** : Between 2000 and 2010, the emo market became saturated. The music began to feel stale, and as fans matured, their interest waned. Younger generations entering their coming-of-age years sought different sounds, gravitating towards pop, K-pop, and hip-hop, leading to a decline in emo's popularity. "There was a lot of emo for everyone between 2000 and 2010, but you know how music works, right? After a while it was just too much. Things to stale, the fans grew up and grew away. They weren't nearly as depressed. Younger fans now in their coming of age years wanted something different, and for them that was pop, K-pop specifically, and hip-hop, so interest-wained." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v_am7ifh7Pk

By the early 2010s, it began to wane as fans matured and bands like Alexisonfire and My Chemical Romance disbanded, while others like Fall Out Boy, Paramore, and Panic! at the Disco moved away from their original emo sound. However, by the middle of the decade, a strong emo revival emerged, bringing back bands like Alexisonfire and My Chemical Romance, and introducing a new generation of groups, possibly with a 90s bent. **Early Characteristics and Hardcore Punk Roots** : Emo, initially a subgenre of hardcore punk, was characterized as brooding, introspective, sensitive, troubled, empathetic, vulnerable, straight-edged, confessional, and emotional. It was a "shrinking violet" within Alt Rock, often hard to find and avoiding attention. "If Alt Rock had a shrinking violet, it was definitely emo, the brooding, introspective, sensitive, troubled, empathetic, vulnerable, straight edged, confessional, emotional, subgenre, and descended of, believe it or not, hardcore punk." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v_am7ifh7Pk
**Emergence of Emo Fashion** : The second wave saw the emergence of a distinct emo fashion, characterized by horn-rimmed glasses, bead necklaces, sweater vests worn over dress shirts, dress pants, and white socks. All clothing items except the socks were typically in darker colors, contributing to a generally "nerdy" aesthetic. "Emo fashion began to emerge. Horn-rimmed glasses, bead necklaces, sweater vests over dress shirts with dress pants and white socks. Everything but the socks had to be in darker colors too. So in other words, pretty nerdy." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v_am7ifh7Pk

**Late 90s Diversification and Accessibility** : By the end of the 1990s, emo had diversified considerably in terms of sound and style. While traditional emo (maintained by bands like Fugazi) persisted, more bands incorporated catchy guitar hooks instead of droning strumming. Vocals became less screamy, and lyrics, though still emotional, were less dire, moving from depressed to melancholy, with some songs even sounding happy. This increased accessibility to a wider audience. "By the end of the 1990s, emo came in all sorts of different colors and shapes. Sure, there was traditional emo, Fugazi made sure they kept that alive. But more events were using catchy guitar hooks instead of the drony strumming. While a lot of arrangements still had that loud, soft, loud, soft dynamic, vocals started to become a little less screamy. Lyrix were still very emotional but not quite as dire. Some had moved up the scale from depressed to merely somewhat melancholy. And hey, some songs actually sounded downright happy. All this contributed to a situation where emo is more accessible, more palatable to more people." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v_am7ifh7Pk

**The Emo Tremble and Tap** : In contrast to slam dancing or mashing, emo developed its own unique dance styles. The "emo tremble" involved ringing one's hands while bouncing on the balls of the feet in time with the music, with a variation called the "emo tap" where one taps their chest to the rhythm. "Because emo wasn't about slam dancing or mashing, a new thing developed. The emo tremble. Basically, you ring your hands while bouncing off the balls of your feet in time with the music. A variation of that is sometimes called the emo tap, where you tap your chest along with the music." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v_am7ifh7Pk

"Third wave emo begins in 2000 and proceeds starting in about 2008 and moved into the background again as of around 2010. **Third Wave Crossover and Fragmentation** : The third wave was characterized by a crossover between "proper emo" and emo-like bands that were also classified as punk, pop punk, post-hardcore, and metalcore. This resulted in significant fragmentation, producing subgenres like emo-pop and screamo. Many third wave bands, some of whom had existed for years, achieved mainstream impact only in the early 2000s. "There was, in short, a crossover between proper emo, if you'll allow me to use that term, an emo-like bands, who were probably best classified as punk, pop punk, post-hardcore, and metalcore. There's a lot of fragmentation. Emo-pop, screamo bands, just a couple of examples. And not all third wave emo bands were brand new, some had been around for years.
