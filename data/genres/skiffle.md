**Dominance in 1950s Britain for Guitarists** : In Britain in 1955 and 1956, if someone played guitar, they typically played skiffle. **Early British Youth Music Stream** : In the mid-1950s, skiffle emerged as one of two significant musical streams designed to attract young people in Britain. This genre is recognized as the foundational branch that ultimately led to all British rock and roll from the 1960s onward. "Britain was Skiffle, and that's the branch of music that eventually led to all British rock and roll from the 60s onwards." ← Episode 70： ＂Move It＂ by Cliff Richard and the Drifters | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uoEWP94f6S4
**Excitement but Different Marketing** : Skiffle possessed much of the excitement and appeal found in American rock and roll. However, it was not marketed explicitly as rock and roll and tended to cater to a slightly more bohemian audience. "Skiffle had everything that people found exciting and interesting about American rock and roll. At least, it had much of the excitement of the rock and roll music, but it wasn't marketed as rock and roll, and it tended to aim at a slightly more bohemian audience." ← Episode 70： ＂Move It＂ by Cliff Richard and the Drifters | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uoEWP94f6S4

**Formalized Genre by Late 1950s** : By the late 1950s, skiffle had developed into a highly formalized genre. Its typical instrumentation almost invariably included an acoustic guitar, a tea-chest bass, and a washboard. The repertoire primarily consisted of songs by Lead Belly, Woody Guthrie, and Big Bill Broonzy, supplemented by traditional folk songs and sea shanties. "Harry's musical taste didn't really run to Skiffle, which by this time had become a very formalized genre, but the instruments almost always consisting of acoustic guitar, T-Chess bass and washboard, and a repertoire that was made up primarily of songs by Lead Belly, Woody Guthrie and Big Bill Brunze." ← Episode 70： ＂Move It＂ by Cliff Richard and the Drifters | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uoEWP94f6S4

They observed London's espresso bars turning into modern versions of old-time music halls, indicating a shift they believed would lead to skiffle's dominance. **Ramblin' Jack Elliott's Influence** : Ramblin' Jack Elliott was a major figure in the British skiffle scene due to his status as an "authentic American" and his direct connection to Woody Guthrie. "his status as an actual authentic American had meant he was one of the major figures in the British folk scene and the related skiffle scene." ← Episode 97： ＂Song to Woody＂ by Bob Dylan | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3DufLVcZyc4
**Skiffle Craze** : This musical craze was a major phenomenon in Britain for approximately two years. Following its decline, it fragmented into three distinct, though overlapping, groups: folk revivalists, British blues enthusiasts, and rock and rollers. "but the Skiffle craze was a big, big thing for about two years, and when it finally died down, it splintered into three different, overlapping groups." ← Episode 70： ＂Move It＂ by Cliff Richard and the Drifters | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uoEWP94f6S4
