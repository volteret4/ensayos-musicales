# genre - Alternative Rock

## curiosities
**Alt Rock (North America) vs Indie (UK) – Same Thing, Different Words** : The term "alternative" evolved in North America from FM radio stations in the 1970s that played slightly different music from corporate rock stations (an "alternative" to Journey, Styx, and Boston), and from bands who recorded for independent labels because major labels wouldn't have them. Campus radio stations spread the term. In the UK, the exact same thing was called "Indie," because these bands recorded for independent labels. Both terms describe left-of-center rock that descends not from the Beatles/Stones/Led Zeppelin lineage but from Iggy Pop, Bowie, the Velvet Underground, and the Ramones. "Same say, in North America these artists recorded for independent labels that were an alternative to the mainstream, hence it was alternative music. In the UK these artists recorded for Indy labels." ← Ongoing History of New Music > A Guide to Genres： Part 1 | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=M11CC71FSWE&list=PLM8bxV0E1Gse5C3n1u-mkVSTZLL5AJICG
**Breakthrough and Slacker Rock (Early 1990s)** : Alternative music experienced a significant commercial explosion in the early 1990s, driven by the massive success of grunge bands like Nirvana, Pearl Jam, Soundgarden, and Alice in Chains. Below this mainstream success, a related style known as "Slacker Rock" emerged, characterized by its laid-back attitude and ragged edges, which often incorporated lo-fi aesthetics. "Alternative music had blown up thanks to the success of brunch. You know, Nirvana, ProJM, SoundGarden, Alton Chained, and so on. But below that was what some people called Slacker Rock, a style that was laid back while still being ragged around the edges." ← Ongoing History of New Music > Lo-Fi： A History | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Jq-e-fZxXOs&list=PLM8bxV0E1Gse5C3n1u-mkVSTZLL5AJICG
**College Rock Connection (Mid-1980s)** : In the mid-1980s, "alternative rock" was often an interchangeable term with "college rock," particularly as campus radio stations began to gain significant influence in promoting new music. Independent labels, operating outside the mainstream major label system, embraced the loose, rough, raw, back-to-basics, primitive, cheap, and cheerful approach of these bands. This unfancy and reactionary style, sometimes distorted and wobbly, was considered cool and lent a specific context to the music, appealing to many listeners. "This was the era of college rock, which for a while was an interchangeable term with alternative rock. Some independent labels, companies operating outside the major label system and therefore offering an alternative really embraced this loose rough raw back-to-basics quasi-permative, cheap and cheerful approach." ← Ongoing History of New Music > Lo-Fi： A History | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Jq-e-fZxXOs&list=PLM8bxV0E1Gse5C3n1u-mkVSTZLL5AJICG

**Lo-Fi Influence and Authenticity (1980s-1990s)** : The rise of alternative rock, often grouped with post-punk acts, saw artists inspired by the viability of home recording championed by figures like Bruce Springsteen. The genre prioritized capturing authenticity and realness, actively avoiding slickness and polished production. The practice of quickly creating music, dubbing it onto cassettes, and distributing or selling them at shows became a common approach. "This was an inspiration to several post-punk acts that were being grouped under the label of Alternative Rock. **Outsider Camp in 70s-90s Rock** : From the 1970s to the 1990s, Alternative Rock represented one of two dominant "tribes" in rock music. Its adherents were considered the outsiders, weirdos, and misfits, a position they were largely content with. They were engaged in a "cold war" with mainstream rock fans, where admitting to liking mainstream acts like Led Zeppelin or Black Sabbath was considered heresy. "On the other side were the alternative kids.
