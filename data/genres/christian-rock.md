# genre - Christian Rock

## curiosities

"Christian bands played in Christian-owned clubs and signed a Christian-owned record label so sold their CDs through Christian bookstores." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=g-tBn8dsSqw
**Early Condemnation (1950s)** : In the 1950s, upon the advent of rock and roll, religious leaders of all denominations universally denounced it as "the music of the devil," deeming it immoral and evil. Some churches even conducted public record burnings, with artists like Elvis and The Beatles being primary targets. "When rock and roll first came on the scene in the 1950s, leaders of all denominations and creeds declared it to be the music of the devil. Rock and roll was condemned from pulpits everywhere as immoral and evil." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6cbL0zLQeIs
**Early Stereotypes** : Throughout the 1980s, Christian rock was often stereotyped as "well scrub preaching from behind a guitar," with mainstream fans perceiving the music as weak and watered down, clashing with rock and roll's rebellious spirit. "Mainstream fans found the music weak in water down and wasn't rock and roll supposed to be about rebellion?" ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=g-tBn8dsSqw
**Evolving Stereotypes (Late 1990s)** : Towards the end of the 1990s, the negative stereotypes associated with Christian rock began to diminish. Bands increasingly attracted fans based on their sound and musical quality, rather than solely their message. Their music became positive and current, less preachy, and visually and sonically resembled mainstream bands, albeit with a noticeable absence of typical rock and roll themes like sex and drugs. "By the end of the decade the various stereotypes began to fall by the wayside. Instead of weak and safe imitations of the real thing these bands began to attract fans for their sound and not their message." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6cbL0zLQeIs
**Fear of Mainstream Perception** : Conversely, Christian rock bands also expressed concern about being perceived as "too wholesome" by the secular world, fearing they would be dismissed as "sermonizers and proselytizers" and thus "shot down based on preconceived notions." "At the same time, these bands are worried about being too wholesome for the rest of the world. That they'll be perceived as sermonizers and proselytizers." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6cbL0zLQeIs

A sophisticated infrastructure was built to support this music, including Christian-owned clubs, record labels, and bookstores. "By the late 1990s Christian rock had really opened up. **Internal Schisms and Judgmentalism** : The Christian underground experienced "a series of schisms." Bands crossing into the mainstream worried about alienating their Christian fanbase by appearing too negative or straying into territory that could be interpreted as immoral, offensive, or anti-God. Some factions of Christian fans were highly judgmental, evaluating bands based on "Gods Per Minute" (GPMs)—the number of times "God" was invoked in lyrics—to determine a band's "holiness." "There were a series of schisms within the Christian underground. Some of the bands we've heard on this program are worried about alienating the Christian fans as they cross over into the mainstream." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6cbL0zLQeIs
**Mainstream Industry Misunderstanding** : The mainstream music industry often struggles to comprehend and manage Christian bands and their values, being more accustomed to promoting themes of rebellion, sex, and violence. "Most of the mainstream music industry doesn't know what to do with these bands and their values. **Mainstream Perceptions vs. Crossover Ambitions** : Christian rock bands often face a dilemma: they fear being "too wholesome" for the mainstream, which could lead to them being dismissed as "sermonizers and proselytizers" based on preconceived notions.
