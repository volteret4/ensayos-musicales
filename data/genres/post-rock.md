# genre - Post Rock

## curiosities
**Canadian Scene** : A small but globally defining post-rock scene emerged in Canada, with bands like Do Make Say Think in Toronto (1995) and Godspeed You! Black Emperor in Montreal (1994), characterized by instrumental music, distorted guitars, diverse instrumentation, and political themes. "And that scene, small as it was, helped define post-rock for the entire planet." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pLf3kUONewg
**Characteristics** : Post rock is characterized by non-traditional song structures, importance of texture and atmospherics, unusual subject matter, and the experimental, unconventional use of rock instruments, often without typical riffs. This type of music would historically not have been played on the radio. "Song structure is definitely non-traditional. Texture is important. Atmospheric are paramount. And the subject matter is unusual." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pLf3kUONewg
**Chicago School of Post-Rock** : This distinct school focused on instrumentation, tight performances, clean recordings, and the use of instruments such as Fender Rhodes, electro-pianos, marimbas, and vibraphones, representing music that required rock to have existed for its own development. "The focus here was on instrumentation, type performances, clean recordings, and use of things like fender roads, electro pianos, marimba's, and vibraphones." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pLf3kUONewg
**Definition and Implication of "Post"** : The term "post rock" is baffling because the prefix "post" implies that this music comes after rock, suggesting rock is finished, yet rock is still very much present. It describes music that is different enough from "normal" rock to stand apart. "The use of the prefix post implies that this music comes after rock. Rock is over done finished and this is what comes next." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pLf3kUONewg
**Early Usage of the Term** : The term "post rock" dates back to 1967, appearing in a Time Magazine article about The Beatles' "Sargent Pepper" album, but it was popularized later by music writer Simon Reynolds. "The term had been around since, get this, 1967 in a Time Magazine article about the Beatles greatly forward with the Sargent Pepper album." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pLf3kUONewg
**Global Reach and Influence** : The post-rock world is truly global, with artists from Canada, the U.S., the UK, Poland, Iceland, and Japan. Its influence extends to movie soundtracks, TV themes, and video game music. "And this world is truly global. Canada, the U.S., the UK, Poland, Iceland, Japan. It figures into movie soundtracks, TV themes, video game music, and a whole lot more." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pLf3kUONewg
**Instrumentation for Non-Rock Purposes** : Simon Reynolds defined post-rock groups as "using rock instrumentation for non-rock purposes," such as employing guitars for texture and atmospherics rather than for riffing and power chords. "Groups that, and these are Simon Reynolds words, using rock instrumentation for non-rock purposes." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pLf3kUONewg
**Retrospective Application of the Term** : Once "post rock" entered the music vernacular, people began to retrospectively identify earlier groups that met its criteria, including My Bloody Valentine, Cocteau Twins, early industrial music bands like Throbbing Gristle and Einstürzende Neubauten, and Talk Talk. "People began to look back to see if there were any other earlier groups who met this criterion. And yes, there were." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pLf3kUONewg
**Simon Reynolds' Popularization** : Music writer Simon Reynolds helped popularize the term "post rock" in the 1980s and first used it in print in 1993 to describe Bark Psychosis's album "Hex." "Reynolds helped popularize it in the 1980s. The first time he used an imprint was in 1993, when he was searching for a way to describe an album entitled Hex by a group called Barke Psychosis." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pLf3kUONewg
**UK Experimental Scene (Late 80s/Early 90s)** : The UK harbored an experimental scene in the late 1980s and early 1990s that included bands like Talk Talk, Stereolab, Lika, Seefeel, Moonshake, and Disco Inferno, characterized by jazz-style rhythms, spacious production, atmospherics, and dynamics. "Talk Talk was part of an experimental scene in the UK through the late 80s and early 1990s." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pLf3kUONewg
