# genre - Low Fidelity (Lo-Fi)

## curiosities
**"Adult Contemporary of the Alternative World" (Present)** : Currently, low-fi is sometimes referred to as "the adult contemporary music of the alternative world," a description that acknowledges its unique position and appeal. While some lo-fi can be high-energy and confrontational, much of it is characterized by a laid-back quality, making it ideal for relaxing or "slacking off." "Some people call it the adult contemporary music of the alternative world, and they're not wrong." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Jq-e-fZxXOs
**Appeal and Scene Growth (Late 1980s)** : The late 1980s marked a significant period for lo-fi rock, as it blossomed into its own distinct scene. Its appeal was driven by several factors: the increasing availability of good quality home recording equipment, a broader reaction against the overly slick and shiny production of mainstream rock, and a specific pushback against the polished aesthetic of hair metal. "The late 1980s were a great time for low-fi rock. It had become its own scene. The availability of good quality home recording equipment, the reaction against shiny, over-produced mainstream rock and the pushback against hair metal, which was very, very slick and shiny, made this approach very appealing to some people." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Jq-e-fZxXOs
**Crystallization and Subgenre Expansion (1995-Present)** : By 1995, the low-fidelity approach had fully crystallized into a legitimate and exalted method within rock music, offering a distinct path for artists. This established mindset led to the proliferation of numerous offshoot subgenres, including hip-negogic pop, chillwave, and glow-fi. The lo-fi aesthetic also penetrated hip-hop with the down-tempo style known as chill hop, and found its own niche within electronic dance music, demonstrating its broad and lasting influence across diverse musical forms. "Low five became such an established mindset and process that it began to spawn offshoot subgenres, such as hip-negogic pop and chill wave and glow five." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Jq-e-fZxXOs
**Definition and Early Perception (1977-Mid-1980s)** : The term "Low Fy" was coined in 1977 by Canadian music educator R. Murray Schafer in his book "The Tuning of the World," where he defined it as an "unfavorable signal to noise ratio," describing recordings that were noisier than desired in an era valuing high fidelity. Initially, low-fidelity music was often dismissed as simply indicating an inability to afford better recording equipment. However, by the mid-1980s, an appreciation grew among critics and fans for the warmth, realness, and unpolished authenticity of these recordings. "It was music of low quality, at least in terms of sonics. Meanwhile, punk had happened." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Jq-e-fZxXOs
**Evolution from Technique to Genre (Late 1980s)** : Initially viewed as an insult, the term "low-fi" began circulating around 1986 and 1987 as an appreciation for this style of recording and performance started to grow. By the end of the 1980s, lo-fi had evolved significantly, transforming from a mere recording technique into a full-fledged, standalone genre of rock music, moving from categorization to a term of praise. "Around 1986 and 1987, that turn began to make the rounds. And by the end of the 1980s, low-fi had evolved from an insult to a categorization, to a genre, to praise." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Jq-e-fZxXOs
**Modern Digital Emulation (Present)** : Today, the appeal of lo-fi continues, with performers using digital technology to deliberately emulate the fuzzy, buzzy analog sounds that characterized early low-fidelity recordings. Artists now intentionally add hiss and background noise to their tracks to achieve the "old school effect" that, in the past, often occurred naturally but was frequently unwanted. "Today, people use digital technology to try to emulate the fuzzy, buzzy analog sounds of the original low-fire practitioners." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Jq-e-fZxXOs
**Origins in Post-CD Backlash (1980s)** : The concept of low fidelity emerged in the 1980s as a reaction and artistic regression against the perceived perfection, shininess, and unreality of music produced with the introduction of the compact disc. Proponents of lo-fi felt that too-perfect recordings lacked the human imperfections and mistakes that made music authentic and from the heart. "For some, music was now too perfect, too shiny, too unreal. They felt it contained none of the imperfections that made it human." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Jq-e-fZxXOs
