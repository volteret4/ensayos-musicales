# genre - Rap / Hip Hop

## curiosities
**Bridging Racial and Musical Divides** : The Beastie Boys' album *Licensed to Ill* (1986) significantly bridged the gap between black and white music fans, who were previously "miles apart." While it's noted as "kind of suspect that it took a bunch of white rappers to bring them together," the album "got the job done" in fostering wider acceptance and connection between these groups. "Socially and culturally, it bridged this huge gap between black and white music fans. Before the Beastie Boys, these two groups of fans were miles apart." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l1jPP1FhGo8
**Early Acceptance Challenges (Late 1970s - Early 1980s)** : Rap and hip-hop began filtering out of New York in the late 1970s and early 1980s but struggled to gain acceptance outside of urban neighborhoods, often dismissed with phrases like "rap crap." There was a significant divide between urban and suburban audiences, people of color and white listeners, and traditional musicians versus rappers. A "sizeable majority" of the music market, industry, and fans viewed rap as a fleeting fad, expecting a swift return to traditional singing and instrument playing, not "scratching and sampling nonsense." "Rap crap was a phrase that was thrown around a lot. There was a huge divide. Urban areas versus the suburbs. People of color versus white. Workers versus rappers." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l1jPP1FhGo8
**Impact of Paul's Boutique (1989)** : While *Paul's Boutique* was a commercial failure initially, the rap and hip-hop community was "totally blown away" by the album. Its advanced artistic and technical complexity, particularly in sampling, set new benchmarks in the genre. "But the rap and hip-hop community was totally blown away by Paul's boutique." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l1jPP1FhGo8
