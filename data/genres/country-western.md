# genre - Country & Western

## curiosities
**Conservative Nature of Top-Tier Shows** : Higher-tier country shows like the Grand Ole Opry, while popular, were extraordinarily conservative, adhering to strict formulas and limiting experimentation. "Girls like the Upree, while the popular, were also extraordinarily conservative." ← Episode 40： ＂Drugstore Rock 'n' Roll＂ by Janis Martin | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l0i2p9TelN0
**Crossover with R&B for Male Jump Band Artists** : Generally, the few country musicians who played R&B songs primarily covered music originally recorded by male jump band artists like Louis Jordan or Big Joe Turner, rather than female R&B artists whose music was closer to jazz. "Generally, the few who did that were performing music originally recorded by male jump band artists like Louis Jordan or Big Joe Turner." ← Episode 40： ＂Drugstore Rock 'n' Roll＂ by Janis Martin | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l0i2p9TelN0
**Dominance of Live Variety Radio Shows (1950s)** : In the 1950s, country and western music was primarily dominated by live variety shows broadcast by different radio stations, which formed a distinct hierarchy with the Grand Ole Opry at the top. "the dominant force in country and western music was a series of live variety shows that would be broadcast by different radio stations." ← Episode 40： ＂Drugstore Rock 'n' Roll＂ by Janis Martin | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l0i2p9TelN0
**Experimental Nature of Lower-Tier Shows** : Lower-rung hillbilly shows, such as the Old Dominion Barn Dance, were more experimental and allowed for greater deviation from the straight formula insisted upon by the top shows, providing a space for artists like Janice Martin to try different styles. "the further down the rungs you got, the more experimental the hillbilly shows could be." ← Episode 40： ＂Drugstore Rock 'n' Roll＂ by Janis Martin | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l0i2p9TelN0
**Hierarchy of Live Shows** : There was a clear hierarchy among country and western live variety shows, with the Grand Ole Opry at the pinnacle, followed by the Louisiana Hayride, and then shows like the Old Dominion Barn Dance. This hierarchy dictated an artist's status and audience reach. "At the very top of the chain was the grand ol' Opry." ← Episode 40： ＂Drugstore Rock 'n' Roll＂ by Janis Martin | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l0i2p9TelN0
