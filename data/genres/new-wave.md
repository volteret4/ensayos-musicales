# genre - New Wave

## curiosities
**1978 Emergence and Characteristics** : New Wave began to appear in 1978 as punk was peaking and burning out. It resulted from a cross-pollination between punk's attitude and pop's melody, featuring twitchy, spiky, choppy, and nervous sounds with experimental structures, geeky vocals, artsy intellectualism, and a suburban vibe, contrasting with traditional rock's blues roots. "Pop and punk had a baby, and they called it New Wave." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bYsV3ozbk4M
**1978-1984 Era and Evolution** : The New Wave scene, roughly from 1978 to 1984, can be divided into two periods. The first saw a proliferation of guitar-driven alternative pop bands, liberated by punk to experiment and emerging from cities beyond New York and London, such as Toronto, Chicago, Los Angeles, and Akron, Ohio. "And as these new groups started to come to our attention, many of them came from cities other than New York and London, the two main centers of punk." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bYsV3ozbk4M
**American Radio Reluctance and MTV Breakthrough** : Despite commercial success for some acts, American FM Rock Radio remained hesitant to play New Wave, often banning the term "New Wave" on air, which hampered many bands. This resistance persisted until August 1, 1981, when MTV launched, breaking the genre wide open. "Thanks to the reluctance of American radio to play New Wave music, the whole thing seemed to be on its way out." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bYsV3ozbk4M
**American Version (Late 1970s)** : In North America, punk was deemed too political, crass, and violent. Sire Records, led by Seymour Stein, deliberately co-opted "New Wave" for its new signings (like The Ramones and Talking Heads) through a "Don't Call It Punk" marketing campaign, to make the music more palatable to the American audience. "Sire launched a marketing campaign for these new signings under the banner, don't call it punk." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9CNNsqXiL6U
**Authenticity vs. Mainstream** : New Wave music from bands like The Police, The Pretenders, and Blondie was perceived as more authentic and innovative than the corporate arena rock, lightweight pop, and "vacuous" disco dominating the mainstream at the end of the 1970s, offering a danceable yet artsy and intellectual alternative. "Songs like that from the police, along with the pretenders and blondie and others, was not only fun and different, but it was also perceived as being more authentic than what was populating the mainstream at the time." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bYsV3ozbk4M
**British Rejection of the Term** : In Britain, the "new wave" label was not widely adopted, as it was perceived as too "glossy and superficial" and didn't align with the country's mood of economic hardship, strikes, and high youth unemployment. "See in Britain, no one really took to the label of new wave. That seemed too glossy and superficial. And besides, the mood in Britain was different. The economy was still on the crapper. Everybody was going on strike and youth employment remained very, very high." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uTORLOm5Yg
**Broadening Definition in America** : While the UK developed terms like "New Pop" and "Postmodern," "New Wave" in America became a very broad term, initially encompassing artists like Tom Petty, and later defined as "like punk, but intelligent" or "like punk, except weirder," especially as acts started charting and appearing on TV. "But it became really, really broad." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bYsV3ozbk4M
**Chronological Span and Sub-periods** : The New Wave era generally extended from approximately 1978 to 1984. This period can be further divided into two main phases: an initial phase characterized by jerky, twitchy, fun power pop often based on guitars, bass, and drums, and a later, synthesizer-driven phase known as Technopop. "Now the new wave era extended roughly from 1978 through to about 1984. And we can divide that span into two periods." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5PJRCC6EwUY
**Defining Characteristics (UK, 1977-78)** : The first era of British New Wave (around 1977-78) was described as spiky, angular, and somewhat agitated, representing a shift from aggressive punk to a more existential despair ("from being about F you to we're F'd"). Some bands were also upbeat and fun, characterized by sharp suits, skinny ties, and choppy, sharp-sounding power pop with clever or geeky lyrics. "One of the best descriptions I've ever read about this era of British music is that things went from being about F you to we're F'd." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9CNNsqXiL6U
**Early Application of the Term** : The term "New Wave" was initially used in the late 1950s/early 1960s for French filmmakers, then in the early 1970s by critics for artsy bands like the New York Dolls. In 1976, it appeared in the British fanzine *Sniff and Glue* and was used by Malcolm McLaren in *Melody Maker* to describe punk-related but not strictly punk-sounding bands. "The earliest references we can find to the term New Wave are in a British fanzine called Sniff and Glue in 1976." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bYsV3ozbk4M
**Early Application to Film (1950s)** : The term "new wave" ("nouvelle vague" in French) originally applied to a new generation of French filmmakers, including Jean-Luc Godard and Francois Truffaut, who emerged in the late 1950s and rejected traditional cinema. "Now that first applied to the work of a new generation of French filmmakers who emerged in the late 1950s, guys like Jean de Goudard and Francois Truffaut, had a different way of looking at cinema, rejecting many of the traditional ways of making movies." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9CNNsqXiL6U
**Early Application to Music (Early 1970s)** : In the early 1970s, some writers used the term "new wave" to describe groups like The Velvet Underground and The New York Dolls, identifying them as artists who rejected the norms of regular blues-based rock and roll, akin to its film application. "the term had been banied about since the early 1970s, when some writers began referring to groups like the Velvet Underground and the New York dolls as new wave, or at least a new wave in rock." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9CNNsqXiL6U
**Economic Boost and Label Shift** : The disco bubble burst and a worldwide recession in the late 1970s and early 1980s led to record labels finding it cheaper to produce and release New Wave records than those by mainstream acts. This economic reality prompted more labels to sign New Wave groups, with successes like The Knack demonstrating phenomenal returns on investment. "Record labels found that it was cheaper to produce and release records by this new generation of new wave bands than it was to do the same with bigger mainstream acts." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bYsV3ozbk4M
**Ephemeral Subculture** : New Wave, prominent in the late 1970s and early 1980s, is cited as an example of a subculture that withered and died once its corresponding musical trend ran its course. "It happened with new wave back in the late 1970s and early 1980s." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=evlGhfUe6QQ
**Evolution into Technopop** : Technopop was not a separate genre from New Wave but rather an integral part of its evolution, forming a subgenre fueled by the same attitude as punk. It represented the synthesizer-driven second half of the New Wave era, emerging in earnest around 1979. "The emerging techno pop wasn't something separate from New Wave, but part of its evolution, a subgenre created by the same attitude as punk." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5PJRCC6EwUY
**Historical Influence** : New Wave music emerged as part of the broader evolution of alternative rock, particularly seeing an explosion in creativity and popularity during the 1970s and early 80s. It contributed to the diverse landscape that would eventually fall under the alternative rock umbrella. "followed by the explosion of the 1970s and early 80s, the alt-rock nation days of the 1990s and the rise of Indy Rock in the first decade of the 21st century." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-CT7yYXonIk
**Inclusion of Pop Elements** : Melody and pop-style songwriting elements were a very significant and integral part of the entire New Wave movement, demonstrating an early and widespread integration of commercial appeal and catchy structures within alternative music. "melody and pop style songwriting elements were very much part of the whole new wave thing." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tZV1lz-obQE
**MTV's Role in Definition (Early 1980s)** : The debut of MTV on August 1, 1981, was a defining moment. New Wave bands, particularly those of the second wave, were perfectly suited for television with their visual appeal (makeup, wild clothes, personas), leading to a "second British invasion" as MTV acquired more British promotional films. MTV essentially established what New Wave meant in the early 1980s, encompassing anything with a progressive sound, closer to punk than traditional blues-based rock and roll. "MTV and other video outlets established what new wave was in the early 1980s." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9CNNsqXiL6U
**MTV's Role in Popularity** : MTV, launched in 1981, significantly fueled the growth and popularity of New Wave, especially through its programming dominated by British bands, even mistakenly stating it did so "through most of the 1960s" (a likely error, as MTV started in '81). The channel extensively played New Wave groups, showcasing their visual styles. "MTV fueled the growth and popularity of New Wave through most of the 1960s." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bYsV3ozbk4M
**Malcolm McLaren's Usage (1977)** : By the late 1970s, Malcolm McLaren, a former manager of The New York Dolls, began using the term to describe "cool new bands that weren't quite punk," leading to interchangeable use of "punk" and "new wave" by the end of 1977. "Malcolm McLaren, a one-time manager of the New York dolls, started throwing at the term when he was trying to describe cool new bands that weren't quite punk." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9CNNsqXiL6U
**New Wave – Sire Records Marketing Term for Melodic Post-Punk; Short Choppy Songs** : The term New Wave appears to have come from the marketing department of Sire Records in New York City, which was packaging the more melodic, pop-oriented, less angry music emerging after punk. New Wave songs tended to be short, sharp, choppy, somewhat agitated, with odd vocals and clever spins on the pop formula, drawing on the Clash, Bowie, and Roxy Music. Examples include Blondie, the Police, the Cars, the Pretenders, XTC, and the Talking Heads. In the UK, "New Wave" was never used — they said "post-punk" instead. "This was a catch-all phrase that seemed to have come out of the marketing department of Sire Records in New York City." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=M11CC71FSWE
**North American Acceptance (1977)** : By the end of 1977, the term "New Wave" gained traction in North America, with mainstream publications like *Time* and *Newsweek* covering the "punk slash New Wave movement," despite initial confusion over its exact definition. "By the end of 1977, the phrase had caught on in North America. Time and Newsweek, which is about as mainstream as you can get, both wrote stories on the punk slash New Wave movement." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bYsV3ozbk4M
**North American Marketing Strategy** : In North America, sounds evolving from punk were branded as "new wave," positioned as a less angry, less violent, less political, and more well-behaved offshoot of punk rock, a strategy that proved successful in generating fame for bands and wealth for record labels. "In North America, these bands and these sounds were marketed as new wave, the less angry, less violent, less political, and more well-behaved cousin of punk rock. And it worked very well too. Lots of bands became famous and record labels got rich." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uTORLOm5Yg
**Paving the Way (Late 1970s-Early 1980s)** : Along with punk, New Wave "helped us through disco and paved the way for the music video generation." "Punk and New Wave helped us through disco and paved the way for the music video generation." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=MK7L0eCpDHc
**Pioneering Pilot (1981)** : Gary Numan, a prominent figure in the new wave music scene, was not only an influential musician but also an accomplished pilot. In 1981, he successfully completed a flight around the world in his own aircraft, showcasing a remarkable adventurous spirit beyond his musical career. "Gary Newman, the new wave star, turned electronic icon to guys like Back in Dave Grohl, became very accomplished as a pilot, even working as a stunt pilot in air shows." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hmnMG6f80Sg
**Post-Punk Subgenre** : New Wave is identified as one of the major post-punk genres emerging in the late 70s and early 80s, contributing to the foundations of modern music. "There was new wave, techno pop, and all its subsets, industrial music, goth, and a revival of scot." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LgJ7ww2AgF4
**Rhythmically Complex Evolution** : Bands emerging from the new wave scene in the late 1970s and early 1980s, such as Talking Heads, contributed to evolving rhythmic complexity and post-punk musicianship. "Out of all the bands to emerge from the punk and new wave scene of the late 70s in the early 1980s, the talking heads were easily the most rhythmically complex." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=CVPPymc_qrk
**Second Wave and Synthesizers (Early 1980s)** : The second wave of New Wave began around 1980 with the widespread adoption of synthesizers, which became cheaper, more portable, and powerful. Musicians, often without formal training, experimented with synths to create new and futuristic sounds, leading to the birth of synth-pop. "The second wave began in earnest in about 1980 with the widespread introduction of synthesizers." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9CNNsqXiL6U
**Sire Records Marketing Strategy** : Facing resistance to "punk rock" on mainstream radio, Sire Records, led by Seymour Stein, launched a "Don't Call It Punk, Call It New Wave" campaign around Christmas 1976. This marketing rebrand successfully removed the stigma, making the music more palatable for radio and the public. "So Sire launched a NAD campaign called Don't Call It Punk, urging people to call this new music New Wave." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bYsV3ozbk4M
**Soundtrack Licensing** : New Wave music was cost-effective to license for movie soundtracks, a factor exploited by director John Hughes for films like *Sixteen Candles*, *Pretty in Pink*, and *The Breakfast Club*, filling their albums with edgy, new bands, many from the UK. "This music was also cheap to license for movie soundtracks, something that director John Hughes used to his full advantage..." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bYsV3ozbk4M
**Subgenres and Decline (Mid-1980s)** : New Wave became a broad category, leading to the emergence of subgenres like synthpop (including New Romantics), dark guitar-focused bands (Public Image Limited, Joy Division, The Cure), "dorky" bands (Devo, B-52s, Oingo Bongo), and post-disco new wave (New Order, Dead or Alive, Eurythmics, The Human League). Some acts, like Culture Club and Wham!, stripped away punk DNA. By 1985, the concept of New Wave became too unwieldy and meaningless due to blurring genre differences, leading to its decline. "by 1985, the genre differences were just too blurry in all the commonalities had been wiped out." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9CNNsqXiL6U
**Trent Reznor's Pre-NIN Band** : Trent Reznor was a member of the new wave group Lucky Pierre, which was noted as the only one of his pre-Nine Inch Nails bands that sounded anything like his later work. "Another gig followed with a band called Slam Bamboo and then a new wave group called Lucky Pierre. Only that last one sounds anything like something Trent would do in the future." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DKdF8NgF5Bw
**Two Versions Merge (Late 1970s)** : By the end of the 1970s, there were distinct UK and American versions of New Wave, characterized as an energetic, guitar-based, slightly jittery brand of power pop, often featuring musicians in suit jackets and skinny ties. "By the end of the 70s, there were two slightly different versions of New Wave, one for America and one for the UK." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9CNNsqXiL6U
