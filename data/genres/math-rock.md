# genre - Math Rock

## curiosities
**Overlap with Post-Rock** : Math rock can overlap with post-rock, but it is typically very challenging to play due to its complicated and precise nature, demanding a lot of precision. "Math rock can overlap with post-rock. This is music that's very challenging to play because it's so complicated and precise. It requires a lot of precision." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9-v4qnOO8wY
**Pre-2010s Presence and 2010s Comeback** : Math Rock, characterized by its complicated, complex, and "impossible to dance to" nature, had a fair amount of presence prior to the 2010s, inspired by masters like Rush, King Crimson, and Yes. In the 2010s, it saw a comeback as part of a broader progressive rock resurgence, with bands using eight or nine-string guitars to achieve complex sounds. "Now to be fair, we saw a fair amount of what was known as math rock in the arts." "This was complicated, complex, hard to play, and impossible to dance to rock, inspired by masters like Russian, King Crimson, and yes." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HSvgRyGiOpE
**Structural Philosophy** : Math rock focuses on building complicated structures, in contrast to post-rock's emphasis on long, flowing fields of sound. "Math rock is about building complicated structures. Post-rock is about long flowing fields." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9-v4qnOO8wY
**Time Signatures and Tension** : A key difference from post-rock is math rock's preference for weird time signatures, in contrast to post-rock's smooth beats (almost always 4/4, occasionally 6/8). Math rock songs often feature significant tension, arising from their angularity and the physicality of the playing. "A big difference is that math rock loves weird timesignatures. Post-rock tends to prefer smooth beats, almost always 4-4, but 6-8 comes up once in a while." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9-v4qnOO8wY
