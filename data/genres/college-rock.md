# genre - College Rock

## curiosities
**Disagreement on the Term** : Despite its usage, the phrase "College Rock" was not universally accepted, indicating a search for a more definitive and encompassing term for this new wave of music. "But not everyone could agree on that phrase." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XjTWcy8RHEs
**Early R.E.M. Style (1980s)** : R.E.M. began their career as a college rock band, also characterized as indie alternative. This designation highlights their early roots in the independent music scene before their transition to a major label. "They started as an indie alternative band, a college rock band." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=vJszsJQpINQ
**Evolution to Indie/Alternative** : As the 1980s progressed, the term "college rock" became outdated. The artists previously labeled as such began to be referred to as "indie groups," and their distinct sound and independent label affiliations led to their categorization as an "alternative" to mainstream offerings, thus giving rise to the term "alternative music." "Eventually, the term college rock didn't really cut it anymore. People began referring to these artists as indie groups." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LgJ7ww2AgF4
**Growth and Transition to Major Labels** : College rock was a significant scene where bands like R.E.M. were pioneers, achieving respect and accessibility. R.E.M.'s eventual move to Warner Brothers, a major label, demonstrated that a college rock band could maintain integrity while gaining wider distribution. Sonic Youth, another cool group in the indie crowd, also made a successful leap to DGC, a Geffen subsidiary, influencing other indie bands, including Nirvana, to follow suit and change the course of music in the 1990s. "R.E.M. became the biggest of the so-called college rock indie bands to make the jump to a multi-million dollar major record label." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AymMybE33zU
**Interchangeability with Alternative Rock (Mid-1980s)** : For a period in the mid-1980s, "college rock" was used interchangeably with "alternative rock," driven by the growing influence of campus radio stations in promoting new music. This era saw independent labels embracing a loose, raw, and unpolished recording approach, often seen as cheap and primitive but considered cool for its unfancy and reactionary nature. "This was the era of college rock, which for a while was an interchangeable term with alternative rock." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Jq-e-fZxXOs
**Late 70s/Early 80s Phenomenon** : College Rock was a distinct music category in the late 1970s and early 1980s, primarily played on campus radio stations across the US and Canada. It featured unsigned acts or those on independent labels, characterized by an "underground vibe," experimentalism, and a departure from mainstream radio sounds. "This was the material played on campus radio stations throughout the US and Canada in the late 70s and early 80s." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LgJ7ww2AgF4
**Limiting Terminology** : The term "college rock" eventually proved to be somewhat limiting, particularly for listeners who were not attending college. This limitation contributed to the subsequent adoption of broader and more inclusive terms like "alternative" and "Indy" for non-mainstream rock music. "But that seemed a little limiting, especially for people who weren't in college." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Z8BTWo66SDI
**Predecessor to "Alternative" and "Indy"** : For a period throughout the 1980s and 1990s, non-mainstream rock was sometimes called "college rock." This term was used because it represented the kind of music predominantly heard on campus radio stations, with bands like R.E.M. and The Pixies beginning their careers in this scene. "For a while, some called it college rock because it seemed to be the kind of music that you heard on campus radio stations." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Z8BTWo66SDI
**Term Coined for College Radio Staples** : The term "College Rock" emerged to describe music by bands like R.E.M., The Replacements, Husker Dü, and Sonic Youth, which were regularly featured on campus radio stations. This phrase reflected the music's popularity within the college radio network. "Since REM and the replacements in Husker Dew and Sonic Youth and their ilk were staples of college radio stations, this music began to be called College Rock." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XjTWcy8RHEs
**Term Supplanted by Alternative** : "College rock" was a term used to describe music favored by campus radio stations, but it was eventually supplanted by the term "alternative." "This word alternative eventually supplanted the term college rock, which was being used to describe the music that found favor with campus radio stations." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=__7iEl3lH8g
