**Definition of Ska – 4/4 Time, Beat on 2 and 4, Guitar on 1 and 3, Bass Quarter Notes, Brass Arrangements** : Ska is a choppy staccato form of pop music in 4/4 time where the beat is emphasized on the second and fourth beats of each bar, while the guitar hits on the first, third, and sometimes fourth beats. Arrangements often feature brass instruments like trombones and saxophones, and the bass line plays steady quarter notes. "Skye is a choppy staccato form of pop music it's in 4x4 time where the beat is emphasized on the second and fourth beats of each bar." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VxWp7HrT8oE

**Four Waves of Evolution and Persistence** : Ska, born in Jamaica in the 1950s, is a genre that "never, ever goes away," constantly ebbing, flowing, mutating, and evolving. It has seen four distinct waves: the first from Jamaica (Desmond Dekker, Toots and the Maytals, The Skatalites), the second from England in the late 70s/early 80s (cross-bred with punk), the third in the 1990s (No Doubt, Mighty Mighty Bosstones, Sublime), and the fourth arriving in the late 2010s, extending into the new decade, with bands like Captain Ska, The Steady 45s, Los Aggrotones, and The Interrupters. "There have been four distinct waves of Scott since the style was born in Jamaica in the 1950s." "The fourth wave arrived late in the 2010s and bled over into the new decade." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HSvgRyGiOpE

**Jamaican Independence and Bauxite Mining – Why Jamaicans Moved to UK; Why Ska Arrived in Britain** : Jamaica's desire and ability to stop being a British colony was aided by foreign investment in bauxite mining (Jamaica became the world's number one bauxite producer by 1957 thanks to investor Sir Alfred DeCosta). Young people sold or were removed from rural land by the bauxite miners and moved to cities, settling in ghettos like Trench Town. Starting in 1948, Britain offered all colonials the right to live in the UK due to a post-war labor shortage, and many Jamaicans emigrated to UK cities like Brixton and Coventry, bringing ska (locally called "Bluebeat") with them. In Coventry, bluebeat existed alongside Northern Soul, R&B, reggae, and the mod youth movement — mods were huge fans of all these sounds. "Starting in 1948, Britain said, hey, all you colonials, you have the right to live with us." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VxWp7HrT8oE

**Origins of Ska – Jamaica 1950s; African Rhythms + Jazz/R&B + Calypso + Mento = Shuffle, Then Ska** : Ska was born in Jamaica in the years following World War II. Big Band, Dixieland, Jazz, Blues, and R&B were heard over shortwave radio from places like New Orleans and from American military personnel in the Caribbean. These were mixed with Caribbean rhythms including Calypso (from Trinidad and Tobago, descended from Biguine, a form of French music co-opted by African slaves) and Mento (Jamaican folk music based on traditional sounds from Africa). This musical stew became known as Shuffle and was heard on Jamaican national radio through the late 1950s and early 1960s. In 1962, Cecil Bustamante Campbell (Prince Buster) changed the beat emphasis to the 2 and 4, and ska was born. "So we have Dixieland, we have Jazz, Blues, R&B, Calypso and Minto. This musical stew became known as Schuffle." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VxWp7HrT8oE

**Ska Became Jamaica's National Dance – Skanking; Then Reggae Took Over by 1969** : Ska was the dominant music of Jamaica through the mid-1960s and was essentially the national dance of the new country. The dance was called skanking — bend the knees, run in place, make fists, bend the elbows, punch and step to the beat. By the late 1960s, poverty and political unrest in Kingston and Montego Bay's ghettos produced the Rude Boys — alienated, unemployed youth who ran dances with big sound systems. The music slowed down and became more menacing, becoming reggae. This ended the first wave of ska by 1969. "SCA became the semi-official sound of this new country." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VxWp7HrT8oE

**Third Wave Ska – California Radio Show "The Ska Parade" and CD Compilations Created Critical Mass** : Third wave ska gathered strength on both the East and West Coasts of the US in the late 1980s and early 1990s. Key to its spread in California was a radio show called The Ska Parade, which played classics alongside newer material by bands like Rancid, Skankin' Pickle, Real Big Fish, Let's Go Bowling, Buckle 9, and Goldfinger. CD compilations from the show further cemented ska into the alternative rock psyche of California, especially in Orange County and south of Los Angeles. "The ska became deeply entrenched into the new music psyche of California, especially in Orange County and to the area south of Los Angeles." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Yqo-YDJR0nM
**Third Wave's California Roots – Bay Area Uptones (1981) Inspired Hardcore + Ska Fusion; Secret Sauce for Third Wave** : The third wave began in the Bay Area with The Uptones (1981), a group of 15 and 16-year-olds fascinated by the Two-Tone sound. They played tons of Bay Area shows for seven years, inspiring other groups. The next generation added hardcore punk DNA to the mix — this was the secret sauce that set the stage for the third wave. Fishbone (formed 1979) also kept ska alive through the mid-1980s in California, mixing punk, funk, soul, metal, and ska with social commentary about racism, oppression, and nuclear war. "Hardcore punk rock. And this was the secret sauce that set things up for what would be the third wave of Scott." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Yqo-YDJR0nM

**Why "Ska"? – Murky Origin: Guitar Sound, Clue J. Johnson's Made-Up Word, Byron Lee, or The Skatalites** : The origin of the word "ska" is murky. Theories include: it was taken from the sound of the choppy guitar click (ska-ska-ska); a Jamaican musician named Clue J. Johnson liked to say "ska-vuvie" all the time; Byron Lee, leader of a band called the Dragonaires, coined it; or The Skatalites invented it when they changed their name from The Satellites. "The origins of the word SCA are a little murky." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VxWp7HrT8oE
