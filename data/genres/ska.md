**Emergence in Jamaica (Late 1950s)** : Ska, initially nicknamed "SCOP," developed in Jamaica from late 1959. It originated from a mix of calypso, soul music, American blues, and R&B, fostered by sound system culture in Kingston's expanding ghettos. This cultural shift was a social effect of wide-scale bauxite mining in Jamaica, which displaced thousands and led to high unemployment. "Starting at about late 1959, this mix of music began to coalesce into something with a distinctive Jamaican sound. Long story short, it was nicknamed SCOP." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=V7WjNR1ujhk

**Origins of Ska – Jamaica 1950s; African Rhythms + Jazz/R&B + Calypso + Mento = Shuffle, Then Ska** : Ska was born in Jamaica in the years following World War II. Big Band, Dixieland, Jazz, Blues, and R&B were heard over shortwave radio from places like New Orleans and from American military personnel in the Caribbean. These were mixed with Caribbean rhythms including Calypso (from Trinidad and Tobago, descended from Biguine, a form of French music co-opted by African slaves) and Mento (Jamaican folk music based on traditional sounds from Africa). This musical stew became known as Shuffle and was heard on Jamaican national radio through the late 1950s and early 1960s. In 1962, Cecil Bustamante Campbell (Prince Buster) changed the beat emphasis to the 2 and 4, and ska was born. "So we have Dixieland, we have Jazz, Blues, R&B, Calypso and Minto. This musical stew became known as Schuffle." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VxWp7HrT8oE

**Ska Became Jamaica's National Dance – Skanking; Then Reggae Took Over by 1969** : Ska was the dominant music of Jamaica through the mid-1960s and was essentially the national dance of the new country. The dance was called skanking — bend the knees, run in place, make fists, bend the elbows, punch and step to the beat. By the late 1960s, poverty and political unrest in Kingston and Montego Bay's ghettos produced the Rude Boys — alienated, unemployed youth who ran dances with big sound systems. The music slowed down and became more menacing, becoming reggae. This ended the first wave of ska by 1969.
