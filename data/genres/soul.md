# genre - Soul

## curiosities
**Black Music for Black People** : During the Black Power movement, soul music was increasingly made by and for African Americans, reflecting the movement's goals of self-determination. Artists like Curtis Mayfield emphasized that the primary intention was to create music for the black community, with crossover success to white audiences being a secondary consideration, if it happened at all. "Curtis Mayfield said that black musicians were making black music for black people and if it crossed over it did. But that was not the point." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 22 - Power to the People - The Music of the Black Power Movement | https://seventies.libsyn.com/022-power-to-the-people-62520-11-01-am ← soul
**Blueprint from "Dance to the Music"** : Sly and the Family Stone's hit "Dance to the Music" set a significant blueprint for the sound of soul music that was to emerge and define the early 1970s. "it set the blueprint for the soul and R&B sound that was to follow in the early 70s." ← For the Record - The 70s > FTR70 Sound Bite - Remembering Sly Stone | https://www.ftr70.com/
**British Youth Culture Appeal** : Soul music, alongside R&B, Reggae, and Ska (known as Bluebeat), was embraced by segments of British youth culture like the mods, rockers, and teddy boys, and later by early British punks. "This slightly evolved form of SCAT lived side by side with R&B and Soul and Reggae. **Building Block in Music** : The development of a more gospel-inflected lyric, exemplified by Benny King's "Stand By Me," became a crucial building block in the music that would later be known as Soul. This approach brought the lyrical concerns and style of gospel directly into the secular realm, setting a new trend among black performers. "Another building block in the music that would become known as Soul had been put in place." ← Episode 94： ＂Stand By Me＂, by Ben E. King | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OgNsNmzW2po

**Decline of a "Brand of Soul"** : By the mid-1970s, a particular "brand of soul" music began to fade. Radio programmers perceived it as repetitive, and listeners felt its themes about persistent life problems were no longer resonating as powerfully. This decline coincided with the rise of disco, which offered an escape through dance. "From the perspective of the music, radio programmers thought that if it was getting kind of repetitive and that the people who had been buying it felt like it had more than thin that it was time to move on." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 22 - Power to the People - The Music of the Black Power Movement | https://seventies.libsyn.com/022-power-to-the-people-62520-11-01-am ← soul
