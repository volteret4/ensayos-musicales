**Characteristics and Influence** : Dub, a Jamaican genre based on echo, empty space, and heavy bass, became one of the many genres that would define post-punk. John Lydon immersed himself in it during his time in Jamaica. "Este genero jamaycano, basado en el eco, el espacio vacío y los bajos pesados, se iba a convertir como vamos a ver en uno de los muchos géneros que acabaran definiendo el post-punk." ← La traición que salvó al punk. **Early Influence in The White Room** : "Dap Hamaycano" (likely Jamaican Dub) was mixed "de forma muy suave" in "The White Room" at London's Club Heaven in 1989. It contributed to the relaxed atmosphere alongside ambient music and Kraut Rock. Later, "DAB" (likely Dub again) became an influence for the diversifying chill-out genre in the early 90s. "se mezclaban de forma muy suave, música ambiental de brillaneno con el Kraut Rock más planeador, Dap Hamaycano y cualquier tipo de música que encajara con este ambiente de armonía y tranquilidad." ← Chill Out： La pausa que necesitaba la electrónica | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VPOAA3ZaA9s

**Introduced to Punks by Don Letts** : In the 1970s, Don Letts, a DJ, played a crucial role in introducing dub music to a new generation of punk kids in clubs. With few punk records available at the time, Jamaican music, including dub, served as the initial soundtrack for the burgeoning punk scene. "He was the DJ who introduced reggae and dub to a new generation of these punk kids in the 1970s in clubs where they hung out. See, there were no punk records yet, so that music, Jamaican music, dub music, black music became theirs until they had some punk records to listen to." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_1NNvxgx8uA ← dub ← dub
**Stuart Copeland's Distinct Feel** : Stuart Copeland of The Police was known for incorporating a distinctly reggae and dub feel into his drumming. This characteristic style was often highlighted through his liberal use of rim shots.
