# genre - Blues

## curiosities
**"Dirty Blues" Records (1950s)** : By 1952, *Jet* magazine noted an unprecedented number of "dirty blues records" on sale, often making no effort to conceal their suggestive nature. These records, using "cunning inflections" and standard "dirty record vocabulary" like "rock and roll," "writer," "grind," and "grass," were criticized for directly describing sexual activity. "Never have there been as many dirty blues records on sale as today." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=L0j9iDuHQVA
**Blue Notes and Scale Tension** : Blues music features "blue notes," which are microtonal pitches falling between a minor and a major. These notes are an inherent part of the genre's tension, existing beyond the standard 12-note Western scale and sometimes described as a "13th thing." "it's an inherent part of the tension of blues music. We only use a 12 note scale, but blues has that 13th thing there too, you know." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mjQBaX_3oL4
**CBGB's Original Music Policy** : When Hilly Crystal bought the club that would become CBGB, he initially named it to reflect his music policy, which included country, bluegrass, and blues, among "other music for uplifting gourmandizers." "He named it after his music policy, the kinds of acts he would book, country, blue grass and blues and other music for uplifting gourmandizers." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VYi3r0G-ne4
**CBGB's Original Policy** : CBGB, established in 1973, was originally named "CBGB and OMFUG" to indicate its initial musical policy, which included Blues, alongside Country and Bluegrass, and "Other Music for Uplifting Gormandizers." Despite this initial vision, the club rapidly shifted its focus to become a crucial venue for avant-garde and punk artists. "He called it CBGB an Umfug, which spells out its musical policy. Country, bluegrass, blues, and other music for uplifting Gorman Dysers." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uEe_HLUay5M
**Component of Indie Folk** : Blues music is identified as one of the genres that came together in Indie Folk, contributing to its rootsy and acoustic characteristics. "This is a corner of alt rock where folk, blues, gospel, and even a little country came together." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tdTs-4Irv8c
**Development and Spread of Blues (c. 1870-1950s)** : Concurrently with jazz, the blues developed in the US South, with roots as early as 1870, becoming widely heard by 1901-1902, facilitated by gramophones and phonographs. Simpler in arrangement than jazz, often requiring little more than a guitar and a voice, blues found its base in "Jukejoints," African American gathering places for music, dance, and drink. These venues formed the "Chitlin Circuit," a touring network for musicians stretching from Florida to New York City's Apollo Theater and Cotton Club. The blues fused with country, western, and hillbilly music, leading to the birth of rock'n'roll by the early 1950s. "And by the early 1950s, we have a descendant called Rock'n'Roll." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_Pi7JLwAeOI
**Early 20th-Century Mississippi Delta Slide Guitar** : In the early 20th century, blues players in the Mississippi Delta developed unique slide guitar techniques. Lacking proper "steels" used in Hawaiian music, they famously improvised by pressing knives or the necks of beer bottles against their guitar strings. "The earliest players didn't have proper steels used by players of the Hawaiian style. They used a knife on their strings and when a knife wasn't available, the neck of a beer bottle would do an pinch." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=D8GU8ZpSgY8
**Early Suggestive Lyrics (1920s)** : In the 1920s, early blues recordings began to feature suggestive titles and lyrics, contributing to public outrage over "dirty lyrics" in music. This trend, exemplified by songs like Tampa Red and Georgia Tom's "It's Tight Like That" (1928), indicated a growing tension between traditional morality and new forms of musical expression. "When we got to the 1920s, early blues and jazz recordings appeared with very suggestive titles and lyrics." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=L0j9iDuHQVA
**Early Use of "Rocking and Rolling"** : The terms "rocking and rolling" and "rock and roll" appeared almost exclusively among blues and R&B singers in the years following Trixie Smith's 1922 recording. This linguistic evolution within the genre laid the groundwork for the future naming of rock and roll music. "And in the coming years, those words, rocking and rolling and rocking roll, appeared in other songs, almost exclusively amongst blues and R&B singers." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=oRR7N-YUcRE
**Electric Guitar Revolution (1950s)** : The electric guitar revolutionized many types of popular music starting in the 1950s, including the blues. "Starting in the 1950s, it revolutionized many types of popular music, country, the blues, jazz and most of all, rock." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=d9HNqb9gJSU
**Evolution with Electric Guitar** : As the blues genre evolved and the electric guitar became prevalent, the slide playing technique also advanced, championed by figures like Muddy Waters and Elmore James. "With the blues evolved and the electric guitar was introduced, playing slide evolved." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=D8GU8ZpSgY8
**Foundation for R&B and Rock** : The establishment of blues as a popular recorded art form in the early 20th century, notably pioneered by artists like Mamie Smith, created fundamental groundwork for the development of R&B and, by extension, rock music approximately 30 years later. "And by doing that, it created many of the foundations of blues records that were to come, not to mention R&B, and by extension rock music some 30 plus years later." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XjQiZqpmQlE
**Foundation of Rock and Roll** : The blues is one of the primary Black Music genres from which rock and roll, and subsequently alt rock, substantially descended. **Historical Racist Criticism** : Blues music was targeted in a British newspaper review which described it as deplorable and tribal, originating from America and stemming from the "jungle," suggesting it might be "the Negro's revenge." This criticism also linked blues with other genres like ragtime, jazz, hot cha-cha, and boogie-woogie under the same racist condemnation in 1956. "It follows ragtime, blues, jazz, hot cha-cha, and the boogie-woogie, which surely originated in the jungle. We sometimes wonder whether this is the Negro's revenge." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=L0j9iDuHQVA

**Origin and Characteristics** : The blues is an African-American invention that originated in the South, characterized by simple songs recounting stories of hard lives, bad luck, real life experiences, and complex relationships between men and women. This music is also known as folk blues or rural blues. "Adjacent to that was the blues, another African-American invention that came from the South." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hQJ3r-DCoMQ
**Origins of Blues and Blue Notes (c. 1870s)** : The blues genre emerged from the US Deep South around the 1870s, following the end of slavery, drawing from slave work songs, spiritual music, and African musical traditions. It is characterized by "blue notes," which are pitches that fall between the accepted tones found on a conventional piano keyboard, also known as "bent notes." These notes represent the kind of "natural imperfections" that Pythagoras sought to eliminate from music, favoring discrete tones, while blues players embraced them. "The blues came out of the deep south beginning in about the 1870s following the end of slavery." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_Pi7JLwAeOI
**Post-Civil War Spread and Delta Roots** : Folk blues or rural blues began to spread across the Deep South following the American Civil War. The three-line stanza structure of these songs can be traced back to the field songs prevalent in the Mississippi Delta, forming foundational elements for genres like rock, R&B, funk, and hip hop. "The structure of these songs with their three line stances can trace back to the field songs in the Mississippi Delta." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hQJ3r-DCoMQ
**Prominence at Fame Recording Studios** : Fame Recording Studios in Sheffield, Alabama, developed a strong reputation as the ideal location for blues musicians to make records. "A Gainer reputation is the place for soul, blues, and R&B musicians to make records." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QEkGSpxeuMk
**Radio Broadcasts (1920s)** : Radio broadcasting in the 1920s enabled the live performance of blues music to reach widespread audiences directly in their homes. This expansion of reach through radio contributed significantly to the genre's growing popularity and accessibility. "People across vast distances could enjoy classical music, opera, hymns, jazz, and blues simply by turning on their radios." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ak6OuOUkRrQ
**Reunion Rehearsal Foundation** : John Frusciante suggested that the Red Hot Chili Peppers learn old blues songs as a foundational exercise during their reunion. This was part of a strategy to start from scratch and build basic rock and roll building blocks, making the songwriting process feel more natural. "He was like, let's learn some old blue songs and let's learn some really old red hot chili pepper songs." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Nr-3Y3UWQcI
