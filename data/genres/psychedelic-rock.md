# genre - Psychedelic Rock

## curiosities
**Beatles' "Tomorrow Never Knows" (1966) – Blueprint for Psychedelic Music** : The Beatles' "Tomorrow Never Knows" from Revolver (1966) contains all the elements that defined early psychedelic music: drug references, an unusual rhythm track, sitar drone, backwards tape, sound effects, and samples. By 1966 the Beatles were deep into the drug culture that had lived in the jazz and folk world for years, incorporating their time in India with Maharishi Mahesh Yogi and their use of LSD. The Beach Boys' Pet Sounds (1966), Jefferson Airplane's "White Rabbit," the 13th Floor Elevators' album The Psychedelic Sounds of the 13th Floor Elevators, early Pink Floyd (including "Interstellar Overdrive," 1967), and the Velvet Underground (playing Andy Warhol happenings) all followed in this tradition. "That track has everything psychedelic music would need to get started." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=B2ZbaaYx0MI
**Contemporary Neo-Psych (2000s–Present) – Flaming Lips, Original Scream, Animal Collective, Tame Impala** : By the 2000s, neo-psychedelia had dispersed across many sounds united more by effect than style. Artists frequently described as psychedelic include the Flaming Lips, MGMT (their druggy danceable psychedelia), Elephant Six (Colorado label specializing in psych flavors), Super Furry Animals, The Black Angels, Animal Collective, Mercury Rev, and Arctic Monkeys (certain songs). Oasis's drone-heavy "Do You Know What I Mean?" has psych elements. Australian artist Tame Impala (Kevin Parker, Perth, Western Australia) has had international alt-rock hits with modern psych, including "Elephant" from the 2012 album Lonerism. "By the time we get to the 2000s, Neosychedelia had taken on all kinds of new dimensions." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=B2ZbaaYx0MI
**Key Characteristics of Psychedelic Music** : Psych music shares several common traits regardless of era: exotic instruments (Indian sitar and tabla), dreamy jams, disjointed song structures with unusual time signature changes, the drone (a sustained note or chord underpinning the music), and surreal lyrics that resist straightforward interpretation. The effect — not any single sound — is what unifies the genre. "There is no one psychedelic sound, there's no one style, but you know it when you hear it." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=B2ZbaaYx0MI
**Kula Shaker – Crispin Mills, Son of Hayley Mills; Deeply Into Psych and Hinduism** : British band Kula Shaker, formed 1995, had a substantial alt-rock radio hit in the late 1990s with "Tattva." Frontman Crispin Mills is the son of actress Hayley Mills (Disney films, 1960s). Mills is deeply involved in psychedelia to the point of adopting the spiritual name Krishna Kanta Das. "The singer there is Crispin Mills, he's the son of Haley Mills." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=B2ZbaaYx0MI
**Neo-Psychedelic Rock (Late 1970s–Early 1980s) – British Post-Punk Branches** : When the first wave of psychedelic music was discredited by establishment reaction to acid casualties and the counterculture's failures (Manson murders, etc.), its spirit survived through 1970s prog rock and Krautrock. Post-punk reactivated it as neo-psychedelia: a blend of jangly guitars (George Harrison–style 12-string), keyboards and synthesizers, long experimental jams, and mandatory drug allusions. Three British bands formed the holy trinity of neo-psych pioneers: The Soft Boys (featuring Robin Hitchcock; album A Can of Bees, 1979), The Teardrop Explodes (featuring Julian Cope; album Kilimanjaro, 1980, seven singles), and Echo and the Bunnymen (Liverpool, 1978; led by Ian McCullough and Will Sergeant; multiple top-10 UK singles; "Bring On the Dancing Horses" placed on the Pretty in Pink soundtrack by John Hughes). "This has been called Neo-psychedelic rock." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=B2ZbaaYx0MI
**Origins of the Word "Psychedelic" – Coined 1956 by Psychiatrist Humphrey Osmond; Means "Soul-Revealing" in Greek** : The word "psychedelic" was coined in 1956 by British psychiatrist Humphrey Osmond, who was studying pharmaceuticals including LSD (Lysergic Acid Diethylamide, first synthesized from the ergot fungus by Swiss scientist Albert Hofmann in 1943 and marketed commercially for a period under the brand name Delysid). The CIA also studied LSD for potential use in interrogation, chemical warfare, and mind control. As the drugs spread recreationally, artists took notice and the word became an adjective for anything mind-expanding. "Psychedelic means soul revealing in Greek." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=B2ZbaaYx0MI
**Shoegaze (Late 1980s–Early 1990s) – My Bloody Valentine, Chapterhouse, Ride, Lush** : A British psych derivative characterized by floaty, fuzzy, drony music designed to overload the senses — often through sheer volume — creating a warm, enveloping bath of sound. My Bloody Valentine's Loveless (1991) is considered the genre's masterwork, inspiring a wave of followers including Chapterhouse, Ride, and Lush. "Floty, fuzzy, drony music that often felt like a warm bath with its attempts to overload the senses often through sheer volume." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=B2ZbaaYx0MI
**Siouxsie and the Banshees and Psych – John McGeoch's Swirly Guitar** : Though most often grouped with Goth, Siouxsie and the Banshees took a significant psychedelic turn after guitarist John McGeoch joined in 1980. A 1983 cover of the Beatles' "Dear Prudence" (from the Beatles' most psychedelic phase, inspired by time in India and LSD use) exemplifies the swirly, trippy guitar work that made McGeoch one of alt rock's most influential guitarists. "John has gone down in history as being one of alt rocks more interesting and influential guitarists." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=B2ZbaaYx0MI
**Space Rock (Early 1990s) – The Verve; Pink Floyd Update with Heavy Reverb** : Following shoegaze, a related strand labeled "space rock" featured heavy reverb, multiple layers of distorted guitars, and relaxed, almost cosmic drumming — an update on what Pink Floyd did in the late 1960s. The Verve (before the legal dispute over their name) exemplified this on their debut album A Storm in Heaven (1993). "It's kind of like an update in what Pink Floyd was about in the late 1960s." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=B2ZbaaYx0MI
**Spacemen 3 (UK, Mid-1980s) – "Taking Drugs to Make Music to Take Drugs To"** : Spacemen 3 were categorized as a drug band because members openly and enthusiastically discussed their regular drug use. Their early demos were released under the title Taking Drugs to Make Music to Take Drugs To, which was their stated mission. They promoted what they called "minimalist psychedelia": one or two chords often sufficed, and the drone was central to the effect. Active on the UK indie charts from the mid-to-late 1980s, they split into two projects when the band dissolved — co-frontman Jason Pierce went on to form Spiritualized. "Their early demos were issued under the title, Taking Drugs to Make Music To Take Drugs To." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=B2ZbaaYx0MI
**The Paisley Underground (Los Angeles) – Jangly 12-String Guitars; The Bangles, R.E.M., Tom Petty** : In Los Angeles in the early 1980s, a scene called the Paisley Underground blended classic rock (birds, Byrds, post-Beatles George Harrison), the Doors, Creedence Clearwater Revival, and post-punk alternative into jangly, 12-string guitar-driven psychedelic pop. The Bangles emerged from this scene, beginning as a mamas-and-papas-influenced pop-psych post-punk band. This jangly-guitar approach was also enormously influential on R.E.M. (their first half-dozen records) and Tom Petty. "This was a sound dominated by jangly guitars, reminiscent of the kind of music played by groups like the birds and the hallies." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=B2ZbaaYx0MI
