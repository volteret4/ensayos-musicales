# genre - Pop Punk

## curiosities
**1990s Explosion** : Pop punk experienced a significant and widespread surge in popularity throughout the 1990s, marking a major shift in the musical landscape and cementing its status as a major genre. "Pop punk exploded in popularity throughout the 1990s." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tZV1lz-obQE
**21st Century Spread and "Too Poppy" Bifurcation** : Pop punk continued to expand into the 21st century, but by the mid-2000s, a "bifurcation" emerged within the genre. Some bands were criticized for being "too poppy for punk," leading to their rejection by certain alt-rock radio stations and being derisively labeled "kitty punk" by audiences who felt their fans were "too young." "we started to see a bifurcation in the pop punk world. For some, you could be too poppy for punk." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pE7TaWSUa-o
**Accessibility Compared to Grunge** : In the mid-1990s, pop punk offered a sound that was "even more accessible to more people than Grunge," contributing significantly to its broad appeal and widespread popularity. "In fact, you can argue that this form of alt-rock was even more accessible to more people than Grunge." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pE7TaWSUa-o
**Blink-182's Influence** : Blink-182 is regarded as one of the most influential pop punk bands of the 21st century. Their distinct sound and successful career trajectory have inspired a lengthy list of artists within the genre and beyond. "Blink has to be considered one of the most influential pop punk bands of the 21st century." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Q-QP7ePxkQE
**Brief Surge in 2010s, Compared to Previous Waves** : Pop Punk experienced a brief surge in the 2010s, though it was less popular than its "first wave" in the 90s and "second wave" in the 2000s. The genre was kept alive by "Big Heritage Acts" like The Offspring, Blink-182, Sum 41, and Simple Plan, as well as "culty bands" such as Wavves, Bass Drum of Death, Ty Segall, and PUP, who sometimes incorporated harder edges and garage rock elements. "Another genre that saw a brief surge in the 2010s was Pop Punk." "Although it was nowhere nearer as popular as we saw in the 90s, which was the first wave, and in the Outs, the second wave." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HSvgRyGiOpE
**California Origin and Naming** : In California, punk bands began experimenting with melody, harmonies, and arrangements, adopting a less serious approach to punk rock. This evolving style started gaining coverage in the San Francisco-based publication *Maximum Rock and Roll*, which subsequently began using the term "pop punk" to describe melodic punk bands like Social Distortion and The Vandals. "California bands decided not to be so serious when it came to punk rock after a while." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tZV1lz-obQE
**Coexistence of Punk and Pop** : The emergence and success of pop punk demonstrated that the raw energy of punk music and the melodic accessibility of pop could not only coexist but also flourish together, despite the initial ideological opposition between the two genres. "So we're starting to see how punk and pop could easily coexist." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tZV1lz-obQE
**Crossover with Third Wave Emo** : During the third wave of emo, there was a significant crossover and fragmentation, with many bands being classified as pop punk alongside emo, post-hardcore, and metalcore. "There was, in short, a crossover between proper emo, if you'll allow me to use that term, an emo-like bands, who were probably best classified as punk, pop punk, post-hardcore, and metalcore." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v_am7ifh7Pk
**Decline in Mainstream Visibility (Late 2000s)** : Towards the close of the 2000s, only superstar pop punk acts like Green Day and Blink-182 continued to attract large crowds. The wider genre saw a decline in mainstream media support, with video channels no longer featuring many videos, alternative magazines shrinking to online publications, and commercial radio shifting to other musical areas. "As the decade came to an end, only the superstar acts like Green Day and Blink 182 continued to draw huge crowds." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pE7TaWSUa-o
**Defining Attitude of Fun and Goofiness** : A key characteristic differentiating pop punk from traditional punk was its attitude, which emphasized fun, lightheartedness, and often goofiness or juvenile themes. The music frequently used major keys, inherently sounding happier, and largely eschewed overtly political or social messages. "If there's one characteristic of pop punk that set it apart from traditional punk, was its attitude." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pE7TaWSUa-o
**Emergence of a "New Generation" (After 2010)** : After 2010, a "new generation of pop punk bands" began to emerge, often referred to as "new punk pop" (or "nu-punk pop"). This iteration of the genre was noted for a slightly "darker" sound compared to its 1990s predecessors, possibly influenced by the 2008 financial crisis or the election of Donald Trump. "Starting sometime after 2010, we began to see a new generation of pop punk bands. And overall, it seems a little darker than what we saw in the 1990s." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pE7TaWSUa-o
**Enduring Nature of the Genre** : Pop punk has proven to be "pretty enduring," surviving various ups and downs in popularity and competition from other genres like rap and hip-hop. Despite not reaching its mid-90s peak again, the genre is not expected to "ever go away." "Pop punk has proved to be pretty enduring. It has had its ups and downs and probably won't ever be as big as it was during the middle 90s, other genres like rap and hip hop of second-away potential audiences." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pE7TaWSUa-o
**Enhanced Accessibility and Digestibility** : The punk rock revival of the mid-1990s, largely driven by pop punk, gained momentum because its music was "much more accessible to more people than old school punk" and considerably "easier to digest than the hardcore that had emerged in the early 1980s." "the music was much more accessible to more people than old school punk. And certainly a lot easier to digest than the hardcore that had emerged in the early 1980s." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pE7TaWSUa-o
**Influence of Mainstream Emo** : Pop punk experienced a surge in popularity throughout the early to mid-2000s, gaining significant momentum as emo music entered the mainstream, with bands like My Chemical Romance introducing new stylistic elements. "Pop punk roared through the first five or seven years of the century, picking up steam when emo went mainstream." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pE7TaWSUa-o
**Mainstream Breakthrough in 1994** : Pop punk experienced its major breakthrough into the mainstream during a pivotal 12-month period in 1994. Its appeal lay in its high energy, fun, and just the right amount of anger and rebelliousness, resonating strongly with the Generation X demographic. "pop punk broke through to the mainstream. Now in retrospect, this sound could not lose." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pE7TaWSUa-o
**Origin as a Hybrid Genre** : Pop punk evolved over several decades as a successful hybrid, merging elements of pop and punk. This fusion has been incredibly lucrative, responsible for the sale of "hundreds of millions of records and concert tickets." "Over the decades, pop and punk merged to create a hybrid that's responsible for selling hundreds of millions of records and concert tickets." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pE7TaWSUa-o
**Paradoxical Nature** : The term "pop punk" is often seen as a paradoxical juxtaposition or an oxymoron, especially given that punk rock was initially conceived in the 1970s as the direct enemy and destroyer of pop music. "pop punk should be a paradoxical juxtaposition, an oxymoron, just like, well, jumbo shrimp." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tZV1lz-obQE
**Purist Backlash and "Selling Out" Accusations** : As pop punk achieved commercial success and mainstream visibility, it faced significant backlash from punk purists. These critics accused bands of "selling out," labeled their music as "softer in intensity" than traditional punk, and viewed them as "illegitimate pretenders" who had become part of "the establishment." "purists became annoyed, accusing groups like Green Dan, the offspring and Rancidov selling out." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pE7TaWSUa-o
**Reflection of Economic Optimism** : The rise of pop punk coincided with an improving global economy and a more positive public mood, following a severe recession earlier in the decade. The genre served as a welcome "antidote" and release from the gloom associated with grunge and the previous economic downturn. "After a brutal recession earlier in the decade, the world economy began to improve, and people's moods began to improve." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pE7TaWSUa-o
**Rock's Last Years at Cultural Peak** : The mid-90s surge of pop punk represented "rock's last years at the top of the cultural heat" before the increasing power and influence of hip-hop and rap began to dominate the music landscape. "In many ways, this was rocks last years at the top of the cultural heat before hip-hop and rap really started to exert its power and influence." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pE7TaWSUa-o
