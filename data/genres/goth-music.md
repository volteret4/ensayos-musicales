# genre - Goth Music

## curiosities
**"Positive Punks" Label (1984)** : By 1984, as goth bands proliferated across England, music papers began calling some groups "positive punks" in a non-ironic way. This term signified that these bands were not advocating revolution but rather creating art that provoked introspection and examination. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XaLsZFLrMnI
**Aesthetic and Fashion (Early 1980s)** : Goth developed a look that was both understated and menacing or disconcerting to outsiders. The insistence on wearing "black everything" was a unifying element. Styles often harked back to British artsy classes of the 1800s, reminiscent of Mary Shelley's era. Looks ranged from androgynous to BDSM-inspired, described as erotic, mysterious, and "dressed for a never-ending funeral." The primary color was black, with exceptions only for some shades of purple and bright red (especially for lipstick). By early 1983, with goth gaining mainstream attention, charts filled with artists in black attire, mascara, deathly white makeup, big teased and crimped black hair, dog collars, lace, leather, velvet, fishnet stockings, ruffled shirts, stilettos or Doc Martens, and often black painted nails. This fashion was considered as much artistic expression as style. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XaLsZFLrMnI
**Big Break and Media Recognition (Early 1983)** : Goth's big break came in early 1983 when the British music media, tired of "happy, bouncy, lightweight technopop," declared technopop "boring and therefore dead." The media then proclaimed goth as the "new sound." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XaLsZFLrMnI
**British Invention** : Goth is predominantly a British invention that emerged in Britain in the late 1970s as a byproduct of punk rock burning out, despite some early American practitioners. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XaLsZFLrMnI
**Global Expansion (Early 1980s)** : Goth expanded globally in the early 1980s, with significant American acts like Christian Death and 45 Grave (from Southern California, more punk-influenced), The Cramps (New York City), Type O Negative (Brooklyn), and Clan of Xymox (Netherlands). A notable goth scene also emerged in Florida, influencing artists like Marilyn Manson and The Marked (Billy Corgan's pre-Smashing Pumpkins band). Despite this, the UK remained goth's spiritual center. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XaLsZFLrMnI
**Global Subculture (Post-Punk Era)** : Goth music culture is described as one of the biggest musical subcultures globally, speculated to be the largest music-related subculture after metal, with numerous adherents worldwide. Its explosion began in the wake of the original punk era of the 1970s. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XaLsZFLrMnI
**Influence on Other Genres and Arts (Mid-1980s onwards)** : By the mid-1980s, goth's influence spread to other genres. New industrial bands, such as Nine Inch Nails (led by Trent Reznor), adopted gothic trappings. Some electronic and even metal acts started to look and sound gothic. This influence intensified, ensuring goth remained an omnipresent background force, occasionally resurfacing with new generations of bands. Goth also infiltrated other art forms, notably influencing filmmakers like Tim Burton and even the modern aesthetic of Batman. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XaLsZFLrMnI
**Initial Insult to Reclaimed Identity (Early 1980s)** : Initially, being called "Goth" was considered an insult. However, by the summer of 1982, the scene reclaimed the word as its own identity. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XaLsZFLrMnI
**Intellectual Components** : Goth music was less aggressive and more introspective and artsy than punk. It contained an intellectual dimension, incorporating elements of existentialist philosophy, romanticism, art history, nihilism, and the trappings of horror and the macabre, while still possessing a strength to endure rather than being whiny. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XaLsZFLrMnI
**Musical Evolution (Early Days)** : Early goth music had a distinct sound characterized by tribal drums, bass players working high on the fretboard (sometimes carrying the melody, which was unconventional), and sharp, slashing, thin guitar sounds. Vocal styles ranged from growls and yelps to operatic deliveries, creating a mesmerizing effect. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XaLsZFLrMnI
**Nick Kent Origin Story** : Alternatively, music journalist Nick Kent is credited with popularizing the term "gothic" to describe these individuals. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XaLsZFLrMnI
**Reflection of UK Mood (Late 1970s)** : The gloomy music made by early goth fans mirrored the mood in England at the time, characterized by high unemployment (especially for young people), endless strikes, a recession, and the unpopularity of Margaret Thatcher. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XaLsZFLrMnI
**Sociological Study and Resilience** : The complexity of the goth scene has led to intense sociological study, with books and theses dedicated to understanding its adherents. Goth is considered one of the most resilient and long-lasting alt-rock subgenres to emerge from the post-punk era, often described as an "undead" and "immortal" subculture that will endure indefinitely. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XaLsZFLrMnI
**Visigoth Towers Origin Story** : One anecdote suggests that early Goths became known by the name because a group of them lived in an apartment building called Visigoth Towers in the Brixton area of London. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XaLsZFLrMnI
