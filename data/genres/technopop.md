# genre - Technopop

## curiosities
**1980s Music Style** : Technopop was a prevalent musical style during the 1980s, often characterizing projects like Tears for Fears' early work. "But at first it was treated as basically another technopop project of that era." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yuA9Xor8vfw
**80s Influence on Alternative Dance** : 80s Technopop served as a foundational element for Alternative Dance, which was essentially a "souped up" version of the genre, incorporating indie rock attitudes and DJ techniques. "Basically, Alternative Dance is souped up 80s Technopop, dressed with Indy Rock and Indy Attitudes, infused together with DJ moves and something that became known as Turntableism." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=amHre9ZZFkU
**Antithesis of Punk Fashion** : The genre developed its own distinct look, which was the opposite of scruffy punk fashion, often featuring artists in suits with short hair and ties. "This sound also came with its own look. In fact, it was the antithesis of Scruffy Punk Fashion." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1KF5MwJVYng
**British Invention and Rapid Spread** : Technopop was a British invention that swiftly appeared after the punk movement. Like Goth, Industrial, and Modern Skah, it spread quickly and often had a short lifespan, a characteristic attributed to the unique, concentrated geography of the British Isles. "There was Goth and Tecdopop and Industrial, Modern Skah, which were all basically British inventions." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zB5zxycrbnY
**Commercial Peak and Fragmentation (1981-82)** : Technopop reached its commercial peak in the winter of 1981-82, characterized by its accessibility and danceability, with bands like The Human League, Duran Duran, and Depeche Mode gaining global hits. During this time, the genre began to fragment into various sounds, with some artists remaining fully electronic while others reintroduced standard rhythm sections. "The early 80s saw the biggest explosion in music-making technology since the arrival of the electric guitar 30 years earlier." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5PJRCC6EwUY
**Consigned to History** : Technopop is mentioned as a genre, alongside Grunge and Britpop, that was "consigned to the history books" by the end of 1997, indicating its peak era was over. "Britpop was officially done, consigned to the history books, just like Grunge and Technopop." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=vzyMSSlD7Yg
**Dancing Culture** : Technopop fans embraced dancing, especially in the UK, where it contributed to the rise of the New Romantics scene in the late 1970s. This genre, alongside the growing alternative scene, enthusiastically adopted extended 12-inch mixes. "The technopop kids got into dancing too, especially in the UK where a new scene called New Romantics took hold in a few clubs in London, Birmingham, and Liverpool in the late 1970s." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0FYq5MBdHw4
**Decline and Revival (1990s-2004)** : Interest in 80s-style Technopop declined significantly in the early 1990s with the rise of grunge and hip hop. However, starting around late 2004, these early 80s sounds began to reappear in popular music, leading to a "current Tecno Pop revival" and an explosion of synth-pop bands. "When Grunge and Hip Hop came along in the early 1990s, general interest in synth pop really kind of just declined and it stayed that way for years. It did, however, live in some respects in the music of nine-inch nails, de Pesh mode, radio head, and Bjork. But as for 80-style Tecno Pop, it was done. Or was it? But starting somewhere around late 2004, these early 80s sounds began to creep back into popular music." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1KF5MwJVYng
**Decline in Pure Form (Mid-80s)** : While popular through 1983-85, pure Technopop began to decline by the mid-1980s for two main reasons: technology improved to the point where synthesized instruments were indistinguishable from real ones, and a backlash emerged from "real rock fans" and musicians who preferred traditional instrumentation. "Technopop continued to be very popular through 1983, 84 and 85, but by the middle of the decade things were changing." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5PJRCC6EwUY
**Early Sound and Lyrical Themes** : Early Technopop, exemplified by bands like Kraftwerk, The Normal, and Gary Numan, featured a robotic, cold, deliberately artificial, emotionally sterile, and intense vibe. Lyrically, it explored themes of futurism, alienation, isolation, and an overall dystopian view of life, reflecting a variation of punk's nihilism. "Much of early technopop was robotic, cold, deliberately artificial, emotionally sterile, and just generally a little intense." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5PJRCC6EwUY
**Emergence and Definition** : Technopop emerged in the post-punk era, specifically in the late 1970s and early 1980s, beginning in earnest around 1979. It is characterized as post-punk music where electronic keyboards and related gear, such as drum machines, were the dominant instruments, with real guitars and drums often absent from the mix. "And in the post-punk era, which is to say the late 70s and early 80s, we had the era of Technopop. Here's how that happened." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5PJRCC6EwUY
**Emotional Characteristics** : The emotions conveyed by this music were often cold, bleak, paranoid, and hollow, reflecting the political, social, and economic environment of the late 70s and early 80s, marked by recession, unemployment, and the Cold War. "But the emotions it conveyed were pretty cold and bleak and paranoid and hollow. And again, this was in complete step with the political, social, and economic environment of the day. Recession, unemployment, the Cold War." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1KF5MwJVYng
**Era of Origin (1978)** : Orchestral Manoeuvres in the Dark (OMD) began their solid run at the very beginnings of the Technopop era in 1978. "They had a very solid run from the very beginnings of the Technopop era in 1978 to the final dissolution in October of 1996." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DJGFUlQTUhQ
**Evolution by 1984** : By 1984, the genre had evolved from its cold, robotic origins to encompass a wide spectrum of emotions, styles, and fashion senses. "By 1984, Tecno Pop had evolved from being this cold, robotic music into something that covered a wide range of emotions and styles and fashion senses." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1KF5MwJVYng
**Evolution of Sound (1980-81)** : Between 1980 and 1981, Technopop began to change significantly due to advancements in technology, allowing for more diverse sounds, especially dance speeds. Songs became less intense, singing more emotive, and instrumentation thicker, thanks to new polyphonic synthesizers and FM synthesis, moving away from thin, trebly sounds. "However, all this began to change in 1980 and 1981, when the technology advanced to allow for even more sounds, specifically dance speeds." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5PJRCC6EwUY
**First Wave (1977-mid 1980) – Cold and Robotic** : This initial period was characterized by a cold, robotic musical approach, laying the foundational groundwork for what would later be known as industrial music. "The first was from 1977 through to about the middle of 1980. That was when the cold, robotic approach was invoked. Songs like this formed the very basic foundations of what we would later call industrial music." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1KF5MwJVYng
**Follower of New Wave** : Technopop quickly followed New Wave as a subgenre emerging from the post-punk era. By early 1983, it was declared boring and "dead" by the British music media, paving the way for goth's mainstream acceptance. "which was followed quickly by Technopop." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XaLsZFLrMnI
**Foundation in Synthesizer Technology (Late 1970s)** : Technopop was founded on the accessibility of new, cheaper, portable, and powerful synthesizers at the end of the 1970s. It emphasized attitude and the willingness to produce music over traditional musical ability. "This was the foundation of what came to be known as Technopop, which blew up at the end of the 1970s." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zSFhhEaiYwE
**Goth Infection** : Goth music "infected" a number of post-new wave technopop bands, transforming their sound from "happy, bouncy, and lightweight to dark and powerful and even sinister." "Goth infected a number of post-new wave technopop bands too, turning them from happy, bouncy, and lightweight to dark and powerful and even sinister." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=evlGhfUe6QQ
**Lasting Influence and DNA** : Despite the decline of pure Technopop, its musical "DNA" persisted throughout the rest of the 1980s in groups like Depeche Mode (with modified sound), Bronski Beat, The Communards, Camouflage, When in Rome, Information Society, and Kon Kan. Synthesizers, originally central to Technopop, became commonplace in all kinds of non-Technopop applications. "Although the popularity of pure Technopop declined through the rest of the 80s, it didn't go quietly." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5PJRCC6EwUY
**Moroder's Direct Influence** : Giorgio Moroder's pioneering work with synthesizers and his production of Donna Summer's "I Feel Love" in 1977 had a direct and profound influence on the emergence of technopop bands. His robotic and futuristic sound provided a clear blueprint, leading to the development of numerous groups like Depeche Mode, OMD, and the Human League in the subsequent years. "You can actually draw a straight line from that song through to Dopech mode and OMD and the human league and dozens and dozens of other technopop bands that would emerge in the next few years." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zIkjd1OjCXM
**Post-Punk and New Wave Subset** : Technopop is identified as a subset of New Wave music, itself a development from the original punk rock era. It represents one of the directions music branched into after punk, influenced by pioneers like Kraftwerk and leading to dance-oriented sounds. "we've discussed the rise of new wave, the subset known as Tecno Pop, and its heavy off-shoot industrial music." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0FYq5MBdHw4
**Prevalence and Decline** : For a couple of years leading up to early 1983, happy, bouncy, lightweight technopop was prevalent. The British music media declared it boring and dead, which contributed to goth's "Big Break." "And we're getting a little weary of the happy, bouncy, lightweight techno pop that had been everywhere for the past couple of years." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XaLsZFLrMnI
**Public Disinterest in Early 1983** : In early 1983, the public and British music media grew tired of "bouncy, lightweight technopop," finding it "boring," which created an opening for Goth music to gain mainstream attention. "As everyone was getting tired of the bouncy, lightweight technopop of the day, they just found it had all grown very boring." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gd9sO7c3N1s
**Rapid Fragmentation and Mutation** : Technopop quickly separated into different strands appealing to various audiences, with some burning out quickly, new variants emerging temporarily, and others mutating into robust and enduring forms, such as industrial music. "And it didn't take long for Technopop to separate into different strands, which appealed to different people." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zSFhhEaiYwE
**Relationship with Industrial Music** : Industrial music was seen as the "ugly cousin" of Technopop, despite the two having much in common beneath the surface. They experienced significant crossover and often fed off each other for ideas. "By this time, industrial music was looked upon as the ugly cousin of Technopop, which was a pretty good analogy, actually." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zSFhhEaiYwE
**San Francisco Punk Influence** : The diverse and experimental soundscapes cultivated by San Francisco's first wave punk bands were influential in establishing the groundwork for later technopop sounds. "The result was some very interesting experimentation, which set the stage for later rock, new wave, and technopop sounds." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YaYaFW2q6qI
**Second Wave (Post-mid 1980) – Joyous and Danceable** : This later period shifted towards electronic music that was danceable, joyous, and fun, reflecting an atmosphere of returning optimism as economic recession began to dissipate. "And we got into our second wave which features the kind of electronic music you can dance to. Synthesizing music didn't have to be all gloomy and detached. It could also be joyous and fun." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1KF5MwJVYng
**Simultaneous Independent Discovery** : Described as a specific genre of 80s music that was simultaneously and independently discovered by a number of artists, representing an important part of alt-rock history. "What we're hearing is a number of artists simultaneously and independently discovering a specific genre of Aedes music. Back then, it was called Technopop, although synthpop was also acceptable. Some people loved it. Some people hated it. But whatever the opinion, it was a very important part of alt-rock history." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1KF5MwJVYng
**Soft Cell's Genre** : This genre describes the two-piece British band Soft Cell, which was active from 1978 to 1984. "Soft Cell, the two-piece British technopop band." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0hnBC0y59QA
**Subgenre of New Wave** : Technopop was characterized as one of the many "flavors" of music encapsulated by the New Wave umbrella. It specifically referred to "all those groups who threw away their guitars and favor of synthesizers," emphasizing its electronic and synthetic sound. Telegenic bands like Flock of Seagulls, Eurythmics, and Duran Duran became prominent stars of this genre, especially with the rise of music videos. "There was Power Pop, which could include everyone from Elvis Costello to Tom Petty, a new Scott Seen, a specials English beat and so on, and all of the flavors of Technopop, all those groups who threw away their guitars and favor of synthesizers." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Z8BTWo66SDI
**TVT Records' Expectation for Nine Inch Nails** : TVT Records founder Steve Gottlieb initially signed Trent Reznor, believing he had found "the next Depeche Mode," a "dancing technopop band capable of turning out hit singles." This expectation clashed with the heavier, darker music of "Pretty Hate Machine." "Steve heard Trent's demos and thought he had the next depeche moan on his hands, a dancing technopop band capable of turning out hit singles." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DKdF8NgF5Bw
