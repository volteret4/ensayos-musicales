# genre - Riot Girl

## curiosities
**"Riot Grrrl Adjacent" Artists** : Poet Tracy Morse coined the term "Riot Girl Adjacent" to describe artists who had a kinship with the Riot Grrrl scene but were not direct members. These artists were often "a little more polished, not nearly as political and occasionally heavier and more riff happy," such as L7, Babes in Toyland, and Hole. "Before we wrap up, we should talk about what poet Tracy Morse calls the Riot Girl Adjacent." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Qss5kC2FUx0
**Causes of Decline: Media, Message, Co-option** : The decline of the original Riot Grrrl scene was attributed to several factors: differing attitudes towards media coverage, conflicting messages about its goals, and annoyance from some members regarding how their concept of "girl power" was "co-opted and watered down by mainstream acts like the Spice Girls." These internal and external pressures led to its fragmentation. "torn apart by different attitudes about media coverage or lack thereof. There were conflicting messages about what everybody was trying to accomplish and others were annoyed by how their idea of girl power had been co-opted and watered down by mainstream acts like the spice girls." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Qss5kC2FUx0
**Coalescence of Ideas** : The Riot Grrrl attitude and sound coalesced from various influences, including "Sassy" magazine, local zines, the "Your Dream Girl" radio show on KAOS, and the existing music scene. It reflected a determination to move beyond being just "girlfriends of boys who go to punk shows" and instead actively create their own punk shows. "Taking all together, the magazine, the radio show, the music, the attitude coalesced into this." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Qss5kC2FUx0
**Core Principles at Shows** : A defining principle of Riot Grrrl shows was "girls to the front, guys to the back," and songs focused on issues pertinent to women. This created a distinct and empowering atmosphere, making gigs safe spaces for women. "When we play, it's girls to the front, guys to the back, and our songs are about things that matter to us." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Qss5kC2FUx0
**Definition of a Riot Grrrl Band** : Defining a Riot Grrrl band was "a tough question," as some identified with the term, others were associated due to playing Riot Grrrl-organized shows, and some were classified based on singing feminist themes over "ragged garage punk type music." Despite the ambiguity, contemporaries "knew it when [they] heard it." "So what made a band a Riot Girl band? That's a tough question." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Qss5kC2FUx0
**Early Scene Intensity and Decline (Mid-90s)** : The original Riot Grrrl scene was "very, very, very hot," characterized by its intensity, political nature, and emotional depth, which ultimately made it unsustainable for a long duration. By the mid-1990s, the scene "had blown up into a million different pieces." "The original Riot Girl scene burned very, very, very hot. Anything that intense, that political, that emotional just can't last for long." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Qss5kC2FUx0
**Emergence as a Female Response to Grunge** : Riot Grrrl bands emerged in the early 1990s, primarily as a female response to the male-dominated grunge scene. Women sought to create their own music about issues important to them, fighting back against "bro culture" and societal misogyny, and taking a DIY approach to all aspects of their music. "Riot Girl bands sprang up largely as a female response to Grunge." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Qss5kC2FUx0
**Key Component of 90s Alt-Rock** : The Riot Girl movement played a significant role in the alt-rock explosion of the 1990s, comprising all-female or predominantly female groups primarily from the Pacific Northwest. This subculture was distinctly political, feminist, queer-positive, anti-racist, anti-sexist, and opposed to violence against women, actively promoting activism and support networks through zines and chapters. "The Riot Girl movement was a very important component to the whole alt rock explosion of the 1990s. These were all female, or at least mostly female groups, from the Pacific Northwest. The subculture that grew up around this music was very political, very feminist, very queer positive. It was anti-racist, anti-sexist, anti-violence against women, lots of zines and chapters that promoted activism in support groups" ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=oLC49nCf0ZM
**Lasting Impact** : Despite its relatively short active period, the original Riot Grrrl scene created a "new paradigm for fierce, uncompromising female-oriented rock." Its influence can still be felt throughout rock music today, with many disciples and ongoing impacts on female-driven music. "The original Riot Girl scene is long gone, its influence can still be felt throughout all rock." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Qss5kC2FUx0
**Longevity Comparison** : The Riot Grrrl movement, similar to original punk rock, had a concentrated impact in the "early to mid-90s" but created a lasting "new paradigm for fierce, uncompromising female-oriented rock," with its impacts still felt today. It proved that a scene doesn't need to last long to be profoundly influential. "And the same thing with what the Riot Girls did in the early to mid-90s." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Qss5kC2FUx0
**Manifesto from Puncture Zine (1989)** : In 1989, a zine called "Puncture" published an article titled "Women's Sex and Rock and Roll," which was widely distributed and considered a "manifesto for a burgeoning subculture" that deeply influenced the young women of the underground music scene in the Pacific Northwest. "For example, there was a Zine called Puncture, that in 1989 published an article entitled Women's Sex and Rock and Roll. It was distributed around the area until this was considered to be a manifesto for a burgeoning subculture." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Qss5kC2FUx0
**Peak in Early 1990s, Olympia Hub** : The Riot Grrrl movement and scene was at its peak in the early 1990s, with Olympia, Washington, located south of Seattle, identified as its primary root and home, analogous to Seattle's role for Grunge. This location became a critical incubator for the movement. "a scene and movement that was at its peak in the early 1990s. If Seattle was home to Grunge, you had to go south down Interstate 5 to Olympia, Washington to find where the Riot Girl thing took root." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Qss5kC2FUx0
**Second Wave of Groups** : After the original scene fragmented, a "second wave of groups" emerged to carry on the mission of Riot Grrrl, including bands like Sleater-Kinney and Team Dresch. These groups continued the feminist and political messages of the movement, adapting them for a new era. "But there was also a second wave of groups that carried on with the mission." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Qss5kC2FUx0
