**Dissemination via Radio (WWII Era)** : Gospel music, along with other styles like Boogie-Woogie, Jump Blues, Swing, and Spear Trolls, became part of a "whole new melting pot of sounds" that spread rapidly via radio during World War II. This shift was attributed to the decline of Big Bands and the American Federation of Musicians strike, creating new programming opportunities for radio stations. "Boogie-Wooogie, Jump Blues, Swing, Gospel, Spear Trolls, a whole new melting pot of sounds was created and disseminated through the radio as a result of the big band problems and the musician's strike." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=V7WjNR1ujhk ← gospel
**Early Fusion with Electric Guitar** : Sister Rosetta Tharp pioneered the combination of gospel music with electric guitar in the 1930s and 1940s, paving the way for future rock and roll artists. **Influence on Pop and Soul** : Gospel music served as a fundamental basis for many early soul songs, with artists like Ray Charles and Sam Cork pioneering the crossover into secular music. Benny King's "Stand By Me" is a prime example of this transition, directly adapting a gospel song into a secular realm while retaining the lyrical style and concerns of apocalyptic visions, rather than just sweet love songs. "Like many early soul songs, it had its basis in gospel music." ← Episode 94： ＂Stand By Me＂, by Ben E. King | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OgNsNmzW2po
**Jubilee Style** : Before Thomas Dorsey revolutionized gospel, black singers of spiritual music often performed in a style known as "Jubilee Style," which groups like The Soul Stirrers initially employed. **Malismal as Emotional Conveyance in Black Gospel** : In Black Gospel music, uniquely among styles of the time, the use of malisma—singing multiple ornamented notes per syllable—was the primary method for conveying deep emotion and demonstrating being moved by the spirit. This technique became the foundation for modern impressive singing. "You see, in black gospel, and pretty much only in black gospel music at the time we're talking about, the use of Malisma was how you conveyed emotion." ← Episode 32： ＂I Got A Woman＂ by Ray Charles | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0oeI9f2x_fo
**Mid-50s Secularization Trend** : In the mid-1950s, there was a prevalent trend where gospel-inspired performers achieved massive secular hits by simply changing a few words in gospel songs, most notably replacing "Jesus" with "baby" or similar terms. "People were having a hit by taking gospel songs and changing the word Jesus to baby or near as dammit." ← Episode Sixty： ＂You Send Me＂ by Sam Cooke | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=92tD5qJR-yY
**More Granular Classification** : Gospel music was identified as one of the more granular genre classifications in 1955, showing an early inclination to define specific musical styles beyond the broader categories of popular music. **New Form in Chicago (1940s)** : In the 1940s, Chicago became a hub for the creation of a new form of gospel music, spearheaded by figures like Thomas Dorsey, which significantly influenced young musicians such as Sam Cooke. "Chicago which...was the city where a whole new form of gospel music was being created at that point, spearheaded by Thomas Dorsey." ← Episode Sixty： ＂You Send Me＂ by Sam Cooke | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=92tD5qJR-yY
**Professionalization Movement** : R.H. Harris, along with Abraham Battle and Charlie Bridges, led a movement to professionalize the gospel quartet scene, which included establishing the National Quartet Convention to guide groups on appropriate conduct and appearance. "he was the joint leader of a movement to professionalise the gospel quartet movement." ← Episode Sixty： ＂You Send Me＂ by Sam Cooke | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=92tD5qJR-yY
**Quartet Terminology** : In both Black Gospel and White Gospel (euphemistically called Southern Gospel), the term "quartet" refers to groups that can comprise five, six, or even more members, not strictly four. "in both Black Gospel and in the White Gospel music euphemistically called Southern Gospel, the term quartet is used for groups which might have five, six, or even more people in them." ← Episode Sixty： ＂You Send Me＂ by Sam Cooke | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=92tD5qJR-yY

**Secular Adaptation of Lyrical Concerns** : Unlike earlier secular reworkings of gospel songs that often required wholesale lyrical changes to fit romantic themes, "Stand By Me" was distinctive in bringing the lyrical concerns and apocalyptic style of gospel firmly into the secular realm. This approach contributed to the development of the "more gospel-inflected lyric" common in the soul music of black performers in subsequent years. "It brings the lyrical concerns and style of gospel firmly into the secular realm." ← Episode 94： ＂Stand By Me＂, by Ben E. King | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OgNsNmzW2po
**Secular Song Creation** : The Sensational Nightingales, a highly regarded gospel group, composed a secular song titled "The Twist." Due to their religious affiliation, they were unable to perform or record this song themselves and sought another group to do so. "They couldn't sing it themselves. It was a secular song, and they were a gospel group." ← Episode 91： ＂The Twist＂ by Chubby Checker | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LzsS8EXdsm8
