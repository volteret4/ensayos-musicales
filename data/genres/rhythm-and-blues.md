# genre - Rhythm and Blues

**"Race Music" Label and Condemnation** : In its early days, Rhythm and Blues, particularly when performed by Black artists, was referred to as "Race music" and heavily criticized by religious authorities. It was condemned as "heathen" and characterized by "unnatural carnal rhythms." **BMI's Focus** : BMI, a rival to ASCAP, specifically dealt with "low-class music" such as blues, country songs, gospel songs, hillbilly music, and boogie. During the ASCAP boycott, this made R&B music incredibly important as it was one of the few types of music that could be played on the radio, significantly boosting its audience. "BMI dealt with only the low-class music, the blues, and country songs, and gospel songs, and hillbilly music, and boogie." ← Episode 2： ＂Roll Em Pete＂ by Big Joe Turner | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=thpMb1A_fAs
**Coalescence and Foundation for Rock and Roll** : Rhythm and Blues coalesced as a distinct musical form, drawing players from musicals, saloons, bars, juke joints, and brothels. These combos were crucial in creating the musical combinations that would ultimately give rise to rock and roll, serving as a vital precursor to the genre. "Meanwhile, another form of music dubbed rhythm and blue is coalesced, featuring players from the musicals and saloons and bars and juke joints and brothels. And it was these combos who helped create the combinations of what would become rock and roll." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dJiSZ4X0F2g ← rhythm-and-blues
**Combination of Styles** : Rhythm and Blues emerged as a genre from the successful combination of boogie, jump, and jive sounds, infused with a strong blues feeling, specifically crafted to encourage dancing. Lucky Millinder's band is considered a strong candidate for being the first true R&B band due to their effective integration of these elements. "And if you want to point to one band that you can call the first rhythm in Blue's band, you probably want to look at Melinda's band, who more than any other band of the era were able to combine all the boogie, jump, and give sounds with a strong blues feeling and get people dancing." ← Episode 7： ＂Good Rockin' Tonight＂ by Wynonie Harris | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IgqSPoYRUCM
**Early Popularity and "Rock Craze" (1948-1951)** : Between 1948 and 1951, rhythm and blues records were heavily influenced by a "rock craze," characterized by songs featuring "rock" in their titles, a boogie-woogie baseline, a backbeat, and lyrics focused on rocking and rolling. This period is identified as the actual beginning of the rock and roll music fad within the R&B scene, six years before it captured the attention of mainstream white audiences. "From 1948 through about 1951, if you listened to rhythm and blues records at all, you couldn't escape this new rock craze." ← Episode 7： ＂Good Rockin' Tonight＂ by Wynonie Harris | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IgqSPoYRUCM
**Emergence of Rhythm and Blues (Late 1940s)** : Rhythm and Blues (R&B) emerged in the late 1940s as big jazz bands downsized due to economic pressures, wartime, and musician strikes. Smaller groups began to emphasize rhythm and beat, contrasting with the less danceable bebop. This new focus, combined with influences from country, western, hillbilly, and traditional blues, created the fertile ground for the birth of rock in the 1950s. "And by the late 1940s, this became known as rhythm and blues." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_Pi7JLwAeOI ← rhythm-and-blues
**Evolution from "Race Records"** : The name "Rhythm and Blues" was introduced by Billboard magazine to replace the earlier chart category "race records," which was considered demeaning. Initially a collective term for music made by Black people, the name eventually fostered the perception of a distinct genre, leading musicians to specifically create "Rhythm and Blues records." "Rhythm and Blues is a name that was used to replace the earlier name, race records, because that was thought a bit demeaning." ← Episode 3： ＂Ida Red＂ by Bob Wills and the Texas Playboys | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zu4E9Y1oJA0
**Evolution to Rock and Roll** : The term "rhythm and blues" was the precursor to "rock and roll" as the music's new name. Chuck Berry, initially singing about "these rhythm and blues," later explicitly used "rock and roll music" in his songs, marking a significant linguistic and cultural transition for the genre. "Having May 1956, Berry had sung about these rhythm and blues. This time he was going to use the music's new name and he was singing. Just let me hear some of that rock and roll music." ← Episode 67： ＂Johnny B. Goode＂, by Chuck Berry | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sHlA-qihuNw
**Formation and Influences** : Rhythm and Blues emerged as a new form of music, inspired by swing bands like Lionel Hampton's and Count Basie's, and heavily influenced by Boogie Woogie music and the Blues. This genre spun up as swing declined, providing a new avenue for musicians who previously played in big bands. "That form was called Vithem and Blues, and Joe Turner became one of its biggest stars." ← Episode 2： ＂Roll Em Pete＂ by Big Joe Turner | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=thpMb1A_fAs
**Foundation of Rock and Roll** : Rhythm and blues is one of the primary Black Music genres from which rock and roll, and subsequently alt rock, substantially descended. "It's only right, given that rock and roll is descended substantially from Black Music, the blues, rhythm and blues, jump blues, jazz, gospel, and so many other genres." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_1NNvxgx8uA ← rhythm-and-blues ← rhythm-and-blues
**Heavy Backbeat as a Key Characteristic** : A defining characteristic of rhythm and blues is the pervasive use of a very heavy backbeat, typically played on the snare drum, throughout an entire song. This seemingly simple innovation laid the foundation for much of the music that followed. "One of the characteristics of rhythm and blues is a very heavy backbeat with the snare all through the song." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=WL3zEWJJNuw ← rhythm-and-blues ← rhythm-and-blues
**Intersection with Other Genres** : In the late 1950s, the distinction between rhythm and blues, rock and roll, and country music was fluid, suggesting that popular music genres naturally intertwine over time. "at the end of 1955, in particular, there was no hard and fast distinction between the genres of rock and roll, rhythm and blues and country music." ← Episode 37： ＂I Walk The Line＂ by Johnny Cash | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1uhw6KjgSME
**R&B's Origins in the Great Migration – Cotton Gin, Slavery, and Urban Demographics** : The origins of rhythm and blues trace back to the invention of the cotton gin in 1794, which entrenched slavery, and the subsequent dispossession of African-American land, which drove the Great Migration from the rural South to northern cities from the late nineteenth century through the early twentieth. This demographic shift was the crucible in which jazz and blues evolved into rhythm and blues. "The cotton gin (1794) entrenched slavery, dispossession drove the Great Migration, and the resulting demographic shift was the crucible in which jazz and blues evolved into R&B." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=f2KA97T4u08 ← rhythm-and-blues
**RCA's Saris (Not Red) Vinyl Coding** : For its 7-inch singles, RCA used "saris" colored vinyl for Rhythm and Blues releases, specifically clarifying that it was "not red." This was part of a short-lived attempt to color-code genres by vinyl pressing color. "Rhythm and blues was saris. Not red, saris." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VlIErdSSVOM ← rhythm-and-blues
**Saxophone Solo Definition** : Illinois Jacket's tenor saxophone solo on Lionel Hampton's 1942 recording of "Flying Home" introduced a "hunk and scrunk" sound that was unlike anything heard before and became the defining solo for the entire rhythm and blues genre. This solo was so impactful that successive sax players in Hampton's band copied it precisely. "this saxophone solo became the one that defined a whole new genre, a genre called rhythm and blues." ← Episode 1： ＂Flying Home＂, Benny Goodman, Charlie Christian, and Lionel Hampton | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RmfeJYl84Qs
**Saxophone's Role** : The saxophone, a prominent instrument in rhythm and blues, derived its influential role from swing music. Saxophone solos in R&B, such as those found in "Shake, Rattle and Roll," can trace their progenitor to powerful swing solos like Illinois Jacquet's on "Flying Home." "There's the obvious one of the saxophone. That's from rhythm and blues, and it's something that rhythm and blues got from swing." ← Episode 2： ＂Roll Em Pete＂ by Big Joe Turner | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=thpMb1A_fAs
