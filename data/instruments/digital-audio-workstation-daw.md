# instrument - Digital Audio Workstation (DAW)

## curiosities
**Democratization of Music Production** : The advent of powerful home computers and affordable music-making software, including various Digital Audio Workstation (DAW) programs like GarageBand, Logic, Audacity, and the free edition of Pro Tools, revolutionized music production. This made it possible for independent artists to create authentic music on limited budgets at home, fostering experimentation and leading to the growth of new genres such as low-fi and Bedroom Pop, eventually gaining attention from major labels. "Plenty of records began their life on laptops using garage band, logic, audacity, the free edition of Pro Tools, and a bunch of other digital audio workstation programs." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uEe_HLUay5M
**Empowering Musicians and Producers** : The combination of DAWs and new digital recording software, such as Cubase Audio, Stooder Diaxis, Deck, Logic, and Pro Tools, provided musicians, composers, producers, and engineers with unparalleled tools to explore and manipulate audio. This technology enabled the creation of sounds and compositions previously unimaginable, fundamentally changing the creative process. "All these programs and the computers on which they ran allowed musicians, composers, producers, and engineers to explore the universe of audio in unimaginable ways." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=r8AazBVoS7g
**Modern Digital Recording Systems** : Digital Audio Workstations (DAWs) like Pro Tools, linked to hard drive systems, began replacing massive reel-to-reel machines in recording studios in the very late 1980s and early 90s, signifying a major shift towards digital production. "It wasn't until the very late 1980s and early 90s that they started switching away from massive real-to-real machines to hard drive systems linked to a digital audio workstation like Pro Tools." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yPok3MemDmE
**Revolutionary Recording Technology (1990s)** : The Digital Audio Workstation (DAW) emerged in recording studios around 1990 as a fully digital recording device. Unlike older systems using magnetic tape, DAWs stored audio information on hard drives, allowing waveforms to be visualized on a screen for unprecedented editing, modification, and manipulation. This capability was previously impossible with magnetic tape. "By 1990, a new beast was appearing in studios, the digital audio workstation." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=r8AazBVoS7g
