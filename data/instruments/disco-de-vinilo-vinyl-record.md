# instrument - Disco de Vinilo (Vinyl Record)

## curiosities
**Decline of 78 RPM Standard** : The technological advancements leading to the microgroove and modern turntables coincided with the near disappearance of the 78 revolutions per minute (RPM) standard. While not entirely extinct, it was largely superseded by newer formats that offered a greater leap in sound quality. **Early Record Design and Wear** : Older vinyl records, likely shellac, were hard and thick, featuring deep and powerful grooves. This robust construction was necessary to endure the aggressive, high-pressure needles of mechanical gramophones, which would otherwise quickly erase shallower grooves and fail to produce adequate sound. **Increased Vulnerability of Older Vinyl Records** : Older vinyl records are particularly susceptible to damage from high stylus pressure compared to their modern counterparts. This is attributed to industrial manufacturing differences that historically resulted in a "whiter" or softer material composition. **Microgroove Invention (Modern Vinyl)** : The invention of the microgroove was a direct consequence of the transition to electronic induction turntables and lighter needles. With electrical amplification, minimal vibration signals could be effectively boosted, removing the need for the needle to apply strong pressure. This enabled the creation of significantly shallower, finer, and more precise grooves, drastically reducing record wear and enhancing sound fidelity. **Reduced Wear and Material Change** : Modern vinyl records, typically made of plastic, benefit greatly from the light pressure (1-3 grams) applied by induction needles. This significantly reduces wear compared to the forceful interaction of older needles with shellac discs, contributing to the longevity and consistent quality of the recordings.
