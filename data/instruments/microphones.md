# instrument - Microphones

## curiosities
**Absence in Early Acoustic Recording** : Prior to 1925 and the advent of electrical recording, microphones did not exist for recording purposes. Singers in this era had to project their voices forcefully (belt out songs) to be heard over the band, as all sound was acoustically funneled into a large horn. "There were no microphones back then." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=L0j9iDuHQVA
**Audience Mics for In-Ear Monitors (Post-2000)** : To address the isolation performers felt from crowd noise when using in-ear monitors, modern concert setups often include "a couple of microphones on or near the stage pointing out towards the audience." These mics capture audience cheering and feed it into the performers' earpieces, allowing them to gauge audience reaction and feel connected. "They pick up your cheering and they cycle it into the ear pieces of the performers." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=qrrwqdpCjpQ
**Blamed for "Perverting" Music** : The microphone was blamed by some purists as a "dastardly, dirty, perverting thing" because of its role in facilitating new vocal expressions and changing traditional singing styles. This sentiment highlights the resistance to technological changes in music that altered established practices. "And this was all the result of the invention of that dastardly, dirty, perverting thing called the microphone." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=L0j9iDuHQVA
**Directional Microphones (SM58, 1966)** : Bill Hanley, recognized as the "Father of Festival Sound," was a pioneer in using directional microphones, such as the Sure SM58 introduced in 1966. These microphones were crucial because they "greatly cut down on distortion and feedback," significantly improving the clarity of live sound. "Hanley was the first to employ directional microphones like the Sure SM58, which was introduced in 1966." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=qrrwqdpCjpQ
**Early Recording Technique (Kingsmen's "Louie Louie")** : In the recording of The Kingsmen's "Louie Louie" in 1963, a single microphone was hung in the center of the room, around which all the musicians gathered. Singer Joe Eli had to bend his neck back and sing up into it, a challenging technique reminiscent of very old recording methods from around 1920. "One microphone hung in the center of the room with all the musicians gathered around it, very much like an old recording done about 1920." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=L0j9iDuHQVA
**Experimental Placement in Blink 182 Recording** : For Blink 182's self-titled 2003 album, an engineer who had worked with Pink Floyd was hired to experiment with various microphones and mic placement techniques. In "Feeling This," Tom DeLonge's vocals before the chorus were recorded by placing a microphone 15 feet away at the end of a 30-foot room. "he tried all kinds of different microphones and mic placement techniques." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_tkLRJiOBu0
**Impact on Singing Style (Crooning)** : The invention of the microphone revolutionized singing, allowing for new vocal techniques like crooning. Previously, singers had to belt out songs to be heard, but the microphone enabled the human voice to explore a wider range of expression, which purists often criticized as a "degenerate form of singing." "The microphone became a new tool for the human voice, allowing it to go in all kinds of new directions." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=L0j9iDuHQVA
**Introduction with Electrical Recording (1925)** : Microphones, referred to as "newfangled listening sticks," were introduced with electrical recording technology in 1925, alongside amplifiers. This innovation marked a significant technological shift from the acoustic recording methods that preceded it. "This involved the use of these newfangled listening sticks called microphones and also things called amplifiers." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=L0j9iDuHQVA
**Role in Manufactured Pop Stars** : Microphones, along with "electronic manipulation," were noted in a 1962 quote as tools used to create "synthetic phonographic personalities." The quote highlighted that the sounds on these records often bore only a "passing resemblance" to what was actually "crooned into the microphone," suggesting early concerns about audio manipulation. "The only sounds that are heard from their records may only bear a passing resemblance to those that were crooned into the microphone." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=L0j9iDuHQVA
