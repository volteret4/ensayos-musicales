# instrument - Synthesizer

## curiosities
**"Punk" Approach to Playing** : Many musicians adopting synthesizers had zero formal musical training, but found it enjoyable to experiment and "scroll around" with them to produce cool, novel sounds that had not been heard before. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9CNNsqXiL6U
**Bob Moog's Influence** : Bob Moog is recognized as the inventor of the synthesizer, and his pioneering machines fascinated Malcolm Cecil and Robert Marguliffe in the late 1960s, inspiring them to build the monumental Tonto. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6SdPPsmpgJg
**Commercially Feasible Size (1960s)** : Dr. Robert Moog, with support from Columbia, Princeton, and the University of Toronto, managed to shrink the synthesizer to a commercially viable size within two years, making it much more accessible than room-filling machines. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OiqdZfxdBP0
**Creation and Capabilities of Tonto** : Tonto, short for "The Original New Timbral Orchestra," was constructed by Malcolm Cecil and Robert Marguliffe as the world's largest modular synthesizer. This colossal instrument featured gear from Moog, Oberheim, ARP, EMS, Roland, and Yamaha, along with custom-made analog synth modules, keyboards, patch bays, and joysticks. It enabled the creation of entirely new sounds and the simultaneous layering of drum tracks, bass lines, and multiple melodies—a novel capability at the time. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6SdPPsmpgJg
**Depeche Mode's Preference** : Depeche Mode found synthesizers "very interesting" for the diverse sounds they could produce. They were also favored for their ease of transport in the early days, as they could be carried "under our arms on a train" and plugged directly into a PA system. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=oPgeSCy93bo
**Early Development (1940s-1960s)** : Work on synthesizers began in the 1940s and accelerated in the 1950s with the invention of the transistor. Early machines were massive; one model was so large it required a rail car for transport. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5PJRCC6EwUY
**Early Room-Sized Models (1950s)** : In the 1950s, Columbia University in New York built a synthesizer that filled an entire room. It could only be programmed by making hundreds of connections with patch cords. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OiqdZfxdBP0
**Initial Skepticism in Rock** : Few rock musicians initially took the concept of purely electronic music seriously, considering synthesizers suitable only for "the odd effect." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OiqdZfxdBP0
**Later Life and Restoration of Tonto** : After its influential period with Stevie Wonder and other artists in the mid-1970s, Tonto's immense size became a disadvantage as smaller, cheaper, and more powerful synthesizers emerged. It was moved between studios and eventually became "unwanted and unloved," even residing for a time in Mark Mothersbaugh of Devo's studio in Los Angeles. In 2013, Tonto was put up for sale, purchased, and subsequently restored by the National Music Center in Calgary, where it now forms a significant part of their keyboard collection. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6SdPPsmpgJg
**Miniaturization** : By the 1960s, engineers like Bob Moog and Don Buchla built smaller units. However, these early models remained expensive, temperamental, and difficult to program. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5PJRCC6EwUY
**Physical Description of Tonto** : Tonto was a massive instrument, installed in a series of wooden cabinets arranged in a 25-foot diameter semicircle, standing 6 feet tall. Weighing approximately one ton, its immense size was a defining characteristic. It also made a cameo appearance in the movie "Phantom of the Paradise," visible in the background of the studio where Winslow Leech worked. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6SdPPsmpgJg
**Pioneering German Use (1970s)** : By the turn of the 1970s, German musicians had already been exposed to almost 20 years of electronic sounds, leading many young Germans to adopt synthesizers as a way to forge a musical identity distinct from British and American rock and roll. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OiqdZfxdBP0
**Polyphonic Advancement (1980-1981)** : Around 1980-1981, newer synthesizers emerged that allowed musicians to play multiple notes simultaneously (polyphony). This allowed for the creation of chords without needing to layer individual notes on separate tracks. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5PJRCC6EwUY
**Revolutionary Use in Funk and Rock – Sly Stone’s Innovation** : Sly Stone’s use of synthesizers in the late 1960s and early 1970s helped redefine the sound of funk, soul, and rock music. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ZPlsR7_WiZQ
**Rock and Roll Debut (1967)** : The synthesizer made its debut in rock and roll in 1967 with a quick, twenty-second solo in The Monkees' song "Star Collector." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OiqdZfxdBP0
**Tonto's Impact on Synthesizer Technology** : Tonto's groundbreaking capabilities and its use by prominent artists like Stevie Wonder played a significant role in driving synthesizer manufacturers to advance their technology throughout the 1970s. This pressure led to the development of smaller, faster, and cheaper electronic keyboards, which in turn fostered the creation of new music genres like Techno Pop. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6SdPPsmpgJg
**Ubiquity in the 1980s** : By the beginning of the 1980s, synthesizers were "everywhere," largely due to Kraftwerk demonstrating their meaningful application in pop music. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OiqdZfxdBP0
**Widespread Adoption (c. 1980)** : Became widely introduced around 1980, as the gear became cheap enough, portable enough, and powerful enough for everyday musicians to acquire and use. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9CNNsqXiL6U
