## curiosities

However, these were still expensive and "insanely untangable," often regarded more as "nerdy novelties" than serious musical instruments. **Adoption in the Late 1970s** : By the late 1970s, synthesizer technology became cheap enough for young musicians, particularly those from the punk rock scene, to adopt them as their primary music-making machines, leading to the rise of genres like Technopop. "And at the very end of that decade, the technology had become cheap enough for young musicians in the last months of the original punk rock scene to adopt these music-making machines as their own." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5PJRCC6EwUY
**Backlash and Musicians Union Concerns (1982)** : The increasing popularity of synth music led to a significant backlash, particularly from "real rock fans" and professional musicians. On May 23, 1982, the UK Musicians Union publicly aired grievances, feeling that synthesizers threatened jobs. Prompted by Barry Manilow replacing his string section with synth players, a motion passed calling for a ban on all synthesizers, though this was widely dismissed as "loony." "Synth-based music was so popular that a backlash built. Real rock fans pushed back against Technopop bands because they weren't really playing instruments. They were just programming machines to do the work for them." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5PJRCC6EwUY
**Commercial Miniaturization (Late 1960s)** : Dr. Bob Moog, with support from Columbia, Princeton, and the University of Toronto, managed to shrink the synthesizer to a commercially viable size in less than two years. This innovation made the instrument practical for musicians, no longer requiring it to fill an entire room but fitting into a space as small as a kitchen. "In less than two years, with the support of Columbia, Princeton, and the University of Toronto, he found a way to shrink the synthesizer to a commercially feasible size." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OiqdZfxdBP0
**Depeche Mode's Preference** : Depeche Mode found synthesizers "very interesting" due to the wide range of sounds they could produce, leading them to transition from a guitar-based band. Synthesizers were also noted for their ease of transport for early gigs, allowing musicians to carry them and plug directly into PA systems. "We found synthesizers very interesting, just the sounds that you could get on them." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=oPgeSCy93bo
**Distinctive Manufacturer Sounds (1980s)** : During the era of classic synthesizers, each manufacturer developed a unique sonic character. Musicians often gravitated to a specific brand (e.g., Korg, Roland, Oberheim, Yamaha) because their instruments offered distinct sounds and features. "Each manufacturer had a sound. Yeah, they did. So you were a corg person. You were a rolling person. You were an over-hime person. You were a Yamaha person and they were all different." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0fQQubt9UTI
**Early Challenges (1940s-1950s)** : Synthesizer technology dates back to at least the 1940s. Early models, like one at Columbia University, filled entire rooms. The first truly "portable" synths still required transport on rail cars, ran very hot, consumed immense electricity, and were incredibly complex to program. "Synthesizer technology goes back to at least the 1940s. A special electronic lab was created in Cologne, West Germany in 1951. Columbia University in New York also had a synthesizer that filled an entire room." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0fQQubt9UTI
**Early Development (1940s-1960s)** : Work on the modern synthesizer began in the 1940s, accelerating in the 1950s with the transistor. Early models were gigantic, some filling entire rooms or requiring rail car transport. By the 1960s, engineers like Bob Moog and Don Buchla built smaller, though still temperamental, hard-to-program, and expensive units that produced unique sounds. "Work on what would become the modern synthesizer goes all the way back to the 1940s. With the advent of the transistor, research into making these kinds of sounds accelerated in the 1950s." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5PJRCC6EwUY
**Early Large-Scale Devices (1950s)** : In the 1950s, Columbia University in New York built a synthesizer that occupied an entire room. This early version could only be programmed through hundreds of connections made with patch cords, demonstrating the primitive and cumbersome nature of early electronic music production. "Columbia University in New York constructed a synthesizer that filled an entire room, and could only be programmed by making hundreds of connections with patch cords." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OiqdZfxdBP0
**Embrace of Nuance on "Michigan Left"** : During the recording of "Michigan Left," Arkells, particularly Anthony, became significantly more open to incorporating synthesizers, which they had previously disliked ("hated Hollow notes"). This marked a departure from the guitar-focused sound of their earlier work, allowing for a "slightly more like nuanced record." The producer Tony Hoffer, known for being a "big synth guy" with "a lot of vintage stuff," further influenced this direction, leading Anthony to purchase two of his studio keyboards. "I hated Hollow notes before we made Michigan left. And then I was sort of, those synths actually kind of sound interesting." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kKNn01M8GG4
**Empowering Novice Musicians** : Synthesizers allowed novice musicians to create professional-sounding music, and with multiple synthesizers and minimal coordination, an individual could produce as much music as an entire band, even in a private bedroom setting. "Suddenly, the novice musician could make music like a pro. And if he had one or two other synthesizers and just a little coordination, you could make as much music as a whole band, even in the privacy of your own bedroom." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uTORLOm5Yg
**Evolution of Technology in the 1970s** : The TONTO synthesizer's groundbreaking capabilities, especially its work with Stevie Wonder, significantly influenced synthesizer manufacturers in the 1970s. This led to an intensive effort to advance the technology, resulting in the creation of smaller, faster, and cheaper electronic keyboards. These more accessible machines fueled the creation of new music, including the rise of Techno Pop. "And Tonto was a big reason synthesizing manufacturers worked so hard to advance the technology in the 1970s, creating these smaller, faster, cheaper electronic keyboards." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6SdPPsmpgJg
**Evolution to Polyphony** : Synthesizers became significantly more powerful and, critically, evolved to become polyphonic. Earlier monophonic machines could only play one note at a time, limiting them to lead lines or melodies and preventing chord playing. The advent of polyphonic machines revolutionized their capabilities, allowing them to be played more like a piano. "Synthesizers became more powerful, and more importantly, they became polyphonic." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XjTWcy8RHEs
**Golden Age Factors (Mid-1970s)** : The "Golden Age of synthesizers" was ushered in during the mid-1970s, characterized by synths becoming rapidly cheaper, smaller, and more portable. This increased accessibility, coupled with the punk rock explosion, fundamentally changed musical composition and direction. "First of all, synth gear was rapidly coming down in price and keyboards were getting smaller and more portable. The second reason was the punk rock explosion." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0fQQubt9UTI
**Groundbreaking Work by Hugh Le Caine (1940s)** : The fundamental groundwork for the modern electronic keyboard synthesizer was laid in the 1940s by Canadian scientist Hugh Le Caine with his invention, the Electronic Sackbut. His pioneering work in electronically generated music and early synthesizer technology predated the later recognition of figures like Bob Moog in the 1960s. "It's considered by many to be the first, proper keyboard synthesizer." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PNiL63fY1Y8
**Infinite Sonic Possibilities** : Synthesizers offered the theoretical ability to generate an infinite number of completely new and artificial sounds that had never before been heard by human ears, making experimentation a key attraction. "First of all, here was a device that, theoretically anyway, could generate an infinite number of brand new sounds. And because these sounds were totally artificial, you could make new ones that had never ever been heard by human ears ever." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uTORLOm5Yg
**Key to Ambient Music Development** : Brian Eno's experimentation with synthesizers was crucial in his development of ambient music, as he used them to craft the "soft mesmerizing sound" characteristic of the genre. "Once he got out of the hospital, he started experimenting with synthesizers, and came up with a soft mesmerizing sound that gently went through various repetitions." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QfiRRtsZoYU
**Legitimized by "Switched-On Bach"** : Wendy Carlos's 1968 album "Switched-On Bach" was a pivotal moment for the synthesizer, demonstrating its capabilities beyond simple "bleeps and boops" and sound effects. The album's massive commercial success significantly drew attention to the instrument's potential for electronic music. "Not only was it commercially successful, but it drew attention to the capabilities of the synthesizer and the possibilities for electronic music." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=oRR7N-YUcRE
**Low Barrier to Entry** : A major appeal of the synthesizer was that it required minimal musical skill to play; musicians primarily needed to learn how to manipulate knobs and keys to create pleasing sounds. "Secondly, and this is the big one, you didn't need a lot of musical skill to play a keyboard synthesizer. You just had to learn how to get it to squawk those cool sounds in a pleasing way." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uTORLOm5Yg
**Monophonic vs. Polyphonic Challenges (1970s-Early 1980s)** : Early monophonic synths like the Roland SH-1 were difficult to program live, requiring frantic patch setup between songs with flashlights. Polyphonic keyboards, which allowed for playing chords, arrived around 1980-81 (e.g., Prophet 5, Roland Jupiter-8), but still required reprogramming between every song, making live performance challenging. "The programmeable synthesises when we moved away from the presets, they were still little monophonic ones like the Roll and S8 ones." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0fQQubt9UTI
**Monophonic vs. Polyphonic Evolution** : Early synthesizers were monophonic, meaning they could only play one note at a time, requiring laborious layering on tape machines to build chords. By 1980-81, the advent of polyphonic synthesizers revolutionized this, allowing multiple notes to be played simultaneously, greatly enhancing sonic texture and complexity. "You could only play one note at a time on the old synths, the monophonic ones, so if you wanted to build up a chord, you needed to layer each new note on a new track on the tape machine. Not with the polyphonic machines." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5PJRCC6EwUY

"And coincidentally, this motion was filed on May 24, 1982, the 48th birthday of Dr. Robert Mogg, the inventor of the portable keyboard synthesizer." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uTORLOm5Yg
**Post-Punk Development** : The widespread introduction of the cheap and easy-to-program synthesizer was one of the most significant musical developments in the post-punk era, making electronic music production more accessible. "No strings to break. No strings to go out of tune. No heavy amplifiers to haul around. **Vintage vs. Soft Synths (Contemporary)** : Older, vintage synthesizers, despite their finicky nature, weight, and tendency to drift out of tune, are now highly collectible and expensive (e.g., a Jupiter-8 on eBay can cost £2000). Many contemporary musicians prefer modern "soft synths" in computers for their reliability, consistent tuning, and ease of use, despite nostalgic purists. "The funny thing is now, you know, a lot of youngsters are looking to collect these things. You try and get a Jupiter 8 on eBay now. It's like £2000 or something." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0fQQubt9UTI
