# instrument - Synthesizer

## curiosities

**Adoption in the Late 1970s** : By the late 1970s, synthesizer technology became cheap enough for young musicians, particularly those from the punk rock scene, to adopt them as their primary music-making machines, leading to the rise of genres like Technopop. "And at the very end of that decade, the technology had become cheap enough for young musicians in the last months of the original punk rock scene to adopt these music-making machines as their own." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5PJRCC6EwUY
**Backlash and Musicians Union Concerns (1982)** : The increasing popularity of synth music led to a significant backlash, particularly from "real rock fans" and professional musicians. On May 23, 1982, the UK Musicians Union publicly aired grievances, feeling that synthesizers threatened jobs. Prompted by Barry Manilow replacing his string section with synth players, a motion passed calling for a ban on all synthesizers, though this was widely dismissed as "loony." "Synth-based music was so popular that a backlash built. Real rock fans pushed back against Technopop bands because they weren't really playing instruments. They were just programming machines to do the work for them." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5PJRCC6EwUY
**Early Development (1940s-1960s)** : Work on the modern synthesizer began in the 1940s, accelerating in the 1950s with the transistor. Early models were gigantic, some filling entire rooms or requiring rail car transport. By the 1960s, engineers like Bob Moog and Don Buchla built smaller, though still temperamental, hard-to-program, and expensive units that produced unique sounds. "Work on what would become the modern synthesizer goes all the way back to the 1940s. With the advent of the transistor, research into making these kinds of sounds accelerated in the 1950s." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5PJRCC6EwUY
**Empowering Novice Musicians** : Synthesizers allowed novice musicians to create professional-sounding music, and with multiple synthesizers and minimal coordination, an individual could produce as much music as an entire band, even in a private bedroom setting. "Suddenly, the novice musician could make music like a pro. And if he had one or two other synthesizers and just a little coordination, you could make as much music as a whole band, even in the privacy of your own bedroom." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uTORLOm5Yg
**Groundbreaking Work by Hugh Le Caine (1940s)** : The fundamental groundwork for the modern electronic keyboard synthesizer was laid in the 1940s by Canadian scientist Hugh Le Caine with his invention, the Electronic Sackbut. His pioneering work in electronically generated music and early synthesizer technology predated the later recognition of figures like Bob Moog in the 1960s. "It's considered by many to be the first, proper keyboard synthesizer." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PNiL63fY1Y8
**Infinite Sonic Possibilities** : Synthesizers offered the theoretical ability to generate an infinite number of completely new and artificial sounds that had never before been heard by human ears, making experimentation a key attraction. "First of all, here was a device that, theoretically anyway, could generate an infinite number of brand new sounds. And because these sounds were totally artificial, you could make new ones that had never ever been heard by human ears ever." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uTORLOm5Yg
**Low Barrier to Entry** : A major appeal of the synthesizer was that it required minimal musical skill to play; musicians primarily needed to learn how to manipulate knobs and keys to create pleasing sounds. "Secondly, and this is the big one, you didn't need a lot of musical skill to play a keyboard synthesizer. You just had to learn how to get it to squawk those cool sounds in a pleasing way." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uTORLOm5Yg
**Monophonic vs. Polyphonic Evolution** : Early synthesizers were monophonic, meaning they could only play one note at a time, requiring laborious layering on tape machines to build chords. By 1980-81, the advent of polyphonic synthesizers revolutionized this, allowing multiple notes to be played simultaneously, greatly enhancing sonic texture and complexity. "You could only play one note at a time on the old synths, the monophonic ones, so if you wanted to build up a chord, you needed to layer each new note on a new track on the tape machine. Not with the polyphonic machines." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5PJRCC6EwUY
**Musicians' Union Opposition** : On May 24, 1982, the 48th birthday of Dr. Robert Moog (inventor of the portable keyboard synthesizer), a chapter of the British Musicians Union filed a motion to ban all synthesizers from recording sessions and live performances, fearing they would displace human musicians. "And coincidentally, this motion was filed on May 24, 1982, the 48th birthday of Dr. Robert Mogg, the inventor of the portable keyboard synthesizer." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uTORLOm5Yg
**Post-Punk Development** : The widespread introduction of the cheap and easy-to-program synthesizer was one of the most significant musical developments in the post-punk era, making electronic music production more accessible. "One of the most important musical developments in the post-punk world was the widespread introduction of the cheap and easy to program synthesizer." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uTORLOm5Yg
**Practical Advantages over Guitars** : Synthesizers offered practical benefits such as no strings to break or go out of tune, and no heavy amplifiers to transport, making them an appealing and radical alternative to traditional guitar-based music for novice musicians. "No strings to break. No strings to go out of tune. No heavy amplifiers to haul around. And if it was now okay for anyone to get up and thrash about with a guitar, wouldn't even be more radical to thrash away without one?" ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uTORLOm5Yg
**Technological Advancement and Affordability** : As the 1980s began, synthesizers continued to become more powerful and cheaper, with new models regularly entering the market offering enhanced sonic possibilities to musicians. "Synthesizers continued to become more powerful and also cheaper. And every few months, a new keyboard came on the market, which offered the musician more sonic possibilities." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uTORLOm5Yg
**Technological Advancements (Early 1980s)** : As 1980 turned to 1981, synthesizer technology advanced rapidly, making them cheaper, more portable, and easier to use. This period saw the biggest explosion in music-making technology since the electric guitar's arrival 30 years prior, greatly expanding musical possibilities. "As 1980 turned to 1981, the advances in synth technology just kept coming. They were also cheaper. They were more portable. They were easier to use." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5PJRCC6EwUY
**Ubiquitous Integration (Mid-to-Late 1980s)** : By the mid-1980s, synthesizer technology had improved to such an extent that it became difficult to distinguish between real and synthesized instruments. Synthesizers transitioned from being new and futuristic to becoming a common component used across various non-Technopop musical applications. "Meanwhile synth became so common that everybody was using them in all kinds of non-technopop applications. What was once new and futuristic had turned into the common present." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5PJRCC6EwUY
