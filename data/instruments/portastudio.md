# instrument - Portastudio

## curiosities
**Audio Quality and Portability (1982)** : The Portastudio units offered "quite good" audio quality, especially considering they brought professional studio features to home recording. However, audio quality would deteriorate when creating multiple generations of a track. Their significant advantage was portability, enabling musicians to make recordings virtually anywhere. "The only time things got kinda dicey was when you made multiple generations of a track. Whatever you do that, the audio quality begins to deteriorate. But because they were so portable, musicians were able to make recordings virtually anywhere." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Jq-e-fZxXOs
**Bouncing Tracks for More Layers (1982)** : The Portastudio, particularly the T-AC-244 model, allowed for "bouncing" tracks to overcome its four-track limitation. Musicians could mix completed parts, bounce them down to another tape, and then record that mixed track back onto one of the original tape's tracks (e.g., track 1), thereby freeing up the remaining three tracks for additional overdubs, instrumentation, or vocals. "If you needed more than four tracks, it was possible to mix completed parts and bounce them down to another tape. That mixed track could be recorded back on to, say, track 1 of the original tape, leaving three more open for things like overdubs and additional instrumentation or vocals." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Jq-e-fZxXOs
**Generic Term and Industry Impact (1982 Onwards)** : The term "Portastudio" became a generic descriptor for similar multi-track cassette units produced by various companies beyond Tascam, including Akai, Yamaha, Fostex, Sansui, and Marantz. In 1982, the Portastudio was considered a "revelation," offering capabilities now common with digital audio programs and laptops like Garageband, but groundbreaking at the time for democratizing multi-track recording. "Other companies got on board too. And the word Porto Studio became a generic description of units not only made by Tascam, but Akai, Yamaha, Fostex, Sonsui, Moranson, a bunch of others." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Jq-e-fZxXOs
**Introduction of Tascam 144 (1979)** : In 1979, Tascam introduced the T-AC-144 Portastudio, a revolutionary device approximately the size of a couple of modern-day laptops. This unit allowed musicians to make multi-track recordings anywhere using compact cassettes, eliminating the need for bulky reel-to-reel machines and their associated tapes. "In 1979, a company called Taskham introduced the T-AC-144 Porto Studio. It was the size of, I guess, a couple of modern-day laptops." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Jq-e-fZxXOs
**Tascam 244 Upgrade and Capabilities (1982)** : An upgraded model, the T-AC-244, was released in 1982, transforming a standard cassette (with its 1/8-inch wide tape running at 1 7/8 inches per second) into a proper four-track recorder. This allowed musicians to layer instruments, for example, recording vocals on track 1, guitars on track 2, bass on track 3, and drums on track 4. The Portastudio also offered advanced features previously exclusive to professional studios. "In 1982, there was an upgrade, the T-AC-244. The Porto Studio turned a cassette with its eighth of an inch wide tape, running at 1 and 7 eighths of an inch per second, into a proper four-track recorder." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Jq-e-fZxXOs
