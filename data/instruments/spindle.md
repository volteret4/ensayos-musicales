# instrument - Spindle

## curiosities
**Large Hole Requirement for 45s** : To accommodate the special spindle for stacking and to distribute friction and torque stresses evenly, RCA's 45 RPM records featured a larger hole (1/16-inch wide). A smaller hole would quickly wear out and become out of round, causing the record to wobble, resulting in extra wear and skipping. "Engineers figured that if these new records were going to be dropped down a 16-inch spindle, a bigger hole was needed to distribute the friction and torque stresses more evenly." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VlIErdSSVOM
**Modern 7-inch Vinyl with Small Hole** : Today, the majority of 7-inch vinyl records are issued with a standard small hole, rather than the large spindle hole of original 45s. This change reflects the fact that record stacking on tall spindles is no longer a common practice, thus eliminating the need for a large hole to absorb torque stresses. "Today though, the majority of seven-inch vinyl issued comes with a standard small hole. Why? Well, because no one stacks records high on tall spindles anymore, so no one needs a big hole to absorb all those torque stresses." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VlIErdSSVOM
**Special Spindle for Stackable 45s** : RCA's 7-inch, 45 RPM records were designed to be stacked on a record player with a special spindle. This allowed multiple records to be played consecutively, with one dropping into place after another finished, providing up to 50 minutes of uninterrupted music. "By manufacturing a record player with a special spindle so you could stack these new records on top of each other." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VlIErdSSVOM
