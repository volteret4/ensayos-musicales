# instrument - Amplifier

## curiosities
**British Dominance in the 1960s** : The 1960s saw the rise of two influential British companies, Vox and Marshall, which significantly boosted the capabilities and fortunes of the electric guitar by developing powerful and distinctive amplifiers. Later, Hiwatt also emerged as a key British player. "And if we're going to tell the story of the electric guitar in the 1960s, we have to acknowledge the role of two British companies that made amps." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fQCOj3_zy54
**Development through Vacuum Tube Technology** : The amplifier, essential for making electrical signals stronger, developed directly from Lee De Forest's 1911 invention of the vacuum tube. Amplifiers were initially used in radios and later for public address (PA) systems and theater sound systems. "This is the very foundation of what would become known as an amplifier." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=V7WjNR1ujhk
**Early Fragility and Distortion** : Early amplifiers were generally fragile and not very good, easily malfunctioning to produce sounds that were not clean or pure. This inherent flaw inadvertently led to the discovery of desirable distorted sounds. "Early amplifiers were fragile things, and frankly not very good." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iGav9uJwA_w
**Essential for Electric Guitars** : Electric guitars, despite their technological advancements, were largely impractical without corresponding amplifiers to project their sound. The development of robust amplifiers was crucial for the instrument's widespread adoption and impact. "Yes, electric guitar technology had come of age, but they really weren't all that useful without amplifiers." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fQCOj3_zy54
**Foundation in Vacuum Tube** : Amplifiers, designed to boost quiet electrical signals, found their foundation in the vacuum tube, which was unveiled by Lee DeForest in 1911. This invention was critical for enabling sounds to be made louder. "That happened in 1911 when Lee DeForest unveiled a vacuum tube, and that became the foundation for something soon known as an amplifier." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=E6cXyaHALBQ
**Integration into Electric Guitar Design** : Amplifiers, in conjunction with vacuum tubes, were a critical component in the invention of the electric guitar. Electrical signals generated by a guitar's pickup would travel to an amplifier, where vacuum tubes would boost the signal's loudness before transmitting it to a speaker. "creating an electrical signal, which traveled down a wire to an amplifier filled with vacuum tubes, which in turn made the signal louder and transmitted everything to a speaker, which turned those signals back into sound." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=V7WjNR1ujhk
**Jeff Beck's Frustrated Destruction** : In a scene from the 1966 movie "Blowup," Jeff Beck, frustrated by his amp shorting out, ended up trying to destroy it with his guitar. "His amp started to short out and Jeff ended up trying to destroy it with his guitar." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yPok3MemDmE
**Marshall Amplifier Innovation – Responding to Rock and Roll Demand** : The renowned Marshall amplifier was conceived, invented, manufactured, and marketed by Jim Marshall, a drummer and engineer. In 1963, in response to customer complaints that American amps like Fender were insufficient for the "new-ish thing called rock and roll," Marshall and his staff created the "loudest, cleanest, most rugged amplifier they could make." This invention, along with associated speaker cabinets, became standard gear for rock guitarists. "This amp and the speaker cabinets with 12 inch and 19 inch speakers became a big hit with the new generation of British musicians and Jim stuff is still standard gear for guitar players who want to go big or go home." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gsSTJWN6cUo
**Overdrive Mechanism** : Distortion, also known as overdrive, occurs when an electrical signal is pushed beyond the capacity of an amplifier's circuitry. This excessive power causes the signal to "clip," cutting off its peaks and troughs and adding new characteristics like overtones, sustain, and compression. Extreme overdrive results in distortion. "Distortion is also known as overdrive. Basically it means you've pushed the electrical signal past what the circuitry and the amplifier can handle." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iGav9uJwA_w
**Pete Townsend's Intentional Destruction** : After accidentally breaking his guitar on a low ceiling, Pete Townsend of The Who amplified his frustration by hammering his guitar into his amplifier until both were splintered, setting a precedent for instrument destruction. "He was annoyed and decided to make a spectacle of his frustration and hammered the guitar into the stage in his amp until it was nothing but splinters." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yPok3MemDmE
**Pre-amplifier Sections** : To allow for powerful distortion at lower, more manageable volumes, pre-amplifier sections were added to standard amplifiers. Turning up the pre-amp creates distortion, while a master volume knob controls the overall output volume. This innovation enabled guitarists to achieve "big amp" sounds even in small spaces or at low volumes. "This was solved by adding a pre-amplifier section to a standard amplifier. Turn that up and you get distortion." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iGav9uJwA_w
**Ramones' Initial Purchase** : The Ramones, specifically John Cummings and Douglas Colvin, spent $250 combined to purchase their first guitar, bass, and an amp, marking the initial investment for their band's formation. "So after spending $250 between them on a guitar, a bass, and an amp, they held their first rehearsal four days later." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VYi3r0G-ne4
**Tim Buckley's Possessions** : At the time of his death, Tim Buckley owned nothing more than the clothes on his back, a guitar, and an amplifier, reflecting his deep debt. "owned nothing more than the clothes on his back, a guitar, and an amplifier." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=MmXDbXI6Sqk
