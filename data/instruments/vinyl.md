# instrument - Vinyl

## curiosities
**45 RPM Albums for Enhanced Fidelity** : Some artists release albums on multiple 12-inch 45 RPM records instead of a single 33 1/3 RPM record. This format allows for fewer songs, more physical space for grooves, and a higher playback speed, which accommodates more bass and less abrupt high-frequency tracking, resulting in less distortion and improved sound quality. "Fewer songs taking up more space and running at a higher speed allows the grooves to swing out as much as they need to in order to accommodate more bass." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=D8GU8ZpSgY8
**High-Frequency Tracking Issues (Sibilance)** : Vinyl can suffer from "sibilance," a smearing of higher frequencies, especially "S" sounds. High frequencies create very fine groove details that can be too small for the stylus to track accurately, causing it to "plow through" and resulting in distortion and missing music. "Vinyl can suffer from something known as the Siblins issue, where higher frequencies are smeared, especially with S sounds." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=D8GU8ZpSgY8
**Immunity to "Loudness Wars" Compression** : Vinyl is inherently immune to the extreme compression used in "loudness wars" mastering, which often distorts CD audio by squeezing out dynamic range. Its physical limitations prevent the application of such excessive compression, meaning a properly mastered vinyl record can sound significantly better than an overly compressed CD version of the same album. "But the same compression cannot be applied to vinyl because of all those physical limitations that I mentioned earlier. It properly mastered for vinyl makes vinyl immune to the distortions that we hear caused on CDs by the loudness wars." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=D8GU8ZpSgY8
**Mastering Distortion from Overheating Cutters** : Distortion on vinyl can also originate during the creation of the master disc. The cutter, which has to move faster to track high frequencies, is prone to overheating. This overheating creates distortion that is then transferred to the master plate and subsequently to all vinyl records pressed from it. "Overheating while cutting these grooves means distortion." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=D8GU8ZpSgY8
**Need for Turntable Preamps** : All turntables require a special preamp (either built-in or external) to function correctly. This preamp not only amplifies the tiny voltage generated by the stylus and cartridge but also applies the reverse of the RIAA Curve, restoring the recording's natural sound. "This also explains why all turntables need a special preamp, either built in or an external one." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=D8GU8ZpSgY8
**Perceived "Warmth" and Psychological Preference** : While technically inferior to CD in many aspects, vinyl is often perceived to sound "warmer" and more pleasant. This perception is largely psychological, tied to nostalgia and positive emotional associations, rather than an objective sonic advantage, though mastering differences contribute to a real sonic distinction. "Well, it's mostly psychological. These people love the nostalgia of vinyl and have positive feelings about it, feelings that cover what they think they're hearing." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=D8GU8ZpSgY8
**RIAA Curve for Equalization (1940s)** : In the 1940s, American engineers developed the Recording Industry Association of America (RIAA) Curve, a standard equalization method to mitigate vinyl's physical limitations. This curve de-emphasizes bass and enhances high frequencies before pressing, allowing for about 22 minutes of music per side (versus ~5 minutes without it) and reducing surface noise. "Base is de-emphasized, and high frequencies are enhanced." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=D8GU8ZpSgY8
**Resurgence of Interest** : The vinyl record format has experienced a significant revival in recent years, leading to a renewed appreciation and interest in the physical album artwork that accompanies these larger, tangible releases. "But now that vinyl is back, so is the interest in album artwork." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_tbKuJR0cPs
**Resurrection through Third Man Records** : Third Man Records is credited with doing more for the "vinyl resurrection" than any other indie label, actively promoting the format with its motto "your turntables not dead," indicating its commitment to the medium. "Third Man has also done more for the vinyl resurrection than any other indie label I can think of. I mean, the company's motto is, your turntables not dead." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Q4dEV1yn3OY
**Technical Drawbacks and Limitations** : Vinyl records have inherent limitations in data storage. Deep bass and loud sounds require wider grooves, which physically restrict the amount of music that can fit on a record side. If bass or loudness is too high, the stylus can struggle to track grooves accurately, leading to missing music or jumping. "The louder the music and the more deep bass it contains, the less of it will fit onto the side of a vinyl record." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=D8GU8ZpSgY8
