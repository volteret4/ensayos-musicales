# instrument - Maestro Fuzz Tone

## curiosities
**First Commercial Fuzz Pedal (1962)** : The Maestro Fuzz Tone, also known as the FZ1, was the first fuzz pedal ever sold, introduced by Gibson in 1962. It was developed by engineer Glen Snottie and TV engineer Revis Hobbs to replicate the accidental fuzzy sound achieved by Grady Martin. Marketed as a versatile device capable of creating a "completely different sound effect," it initially sold poorly but became a sensation after Keith Richards used it on The Rolling Stones' "(I Can't Get No) Satisfaction" in 1965. "And in 1962, they started selling the first ever Fuzz pedal, a gizmo that could carefully and deliberately distort the sound of an electric guitar." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iGav9uJwA_w
**Versatile Sound Effects** : The Maestro Fuzz Tone was marketed with a demo record highlighting its ability to create a wide range of unique guitar sounds beyond simple fuzz. These included "organ-like tones, mellow woodwinds and whispering reeds, booming brass and bell-clear horns," and many more. It was also promoted for its expanded range of effects when combined with reverberation, tremolo, and vibrato, even demonstrating its use with an electric bass for a "Sousa-Phone effect."
