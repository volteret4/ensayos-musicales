# instrument - Compact Disc

## curiosities
**Absence of Bass and High-Frequency Limitations** : Unlike vinyl, which has physical limitations for deep bass and high frequencies, compact discs, being digital and read with a laser, do not suffer from such issues. This means CDs can consistently deliver deeper, louder bass without distortion or missing audio. "You will always, always, always get deeper, louder bass from a CD than you will from a vinyl record." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=D8GU8ZpSgY8
**Anti-Aliasing for Smoothness** : CDs employ anti-aliasing technology to smooth out the tiny jaggedness of the digital signal, theoretically ensuring a continuous stream of audio. "And CDs use something called anti-aliasing, which is supposed to smooth out that tiny jaggedness and theoretically non-continuous stream of the digital signal." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=D8GU8ZpSgY8
**Digital Sampling Rate (44,100 segments/second)** : CDs process a musical signal by sampling it 44,100 times per second. This rate is scientifically determined to be indistinguishable by human ears and brains from a smooth analog source, ensuring high fidelity. "most human ears cannot sense the fact that the digital signal of a CD is broken down into 44,100 segments per second." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=D8GU8ZpSgY8
**Invention by Jim Russell (1960s)** : The fundamental technology behind the compact disc was invented by Seattle scientist Jim Russell, who devised its basic principles in the 1960s. His patents later became involved in a legal dispute with a Toronto company in the 1980s regarding licensing fees. "The guy who invented the underlying technology of the compact disk was a Seattle scientist named Jim Russell. He came up with the basic principles back in the 1960s." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aSk1XQHNkd8
**Inventor Receives No Compensation (Settlement)** : Despite inventing the underlying technology of the compact disc in the 1960s, Jim Russell ultimately did not receive any financial compensation from a settlement stemming from a lawsuit for licensing fees and royalties on CD technology. The patents had ended up with a Toronto company in the 1980s. "Then Russell and the patents ended up with a Toronto company in the 1980s who tried to sue for licensing fees and royalties on CD technology. And although there was eventually a settlement, Russell didn't get a cut of anything." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aSk1XQHNkd8
**Technical Superiority over Vinyl** : From a technical standpoint, compact discs offer superior audio quality compared to vinyl. They boast a better signal-to-noise ratio, eliminating rumble, pops, and clicks common to records. "The compact disc offers a better signal to noise ratio." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=D8GU8ZpSgY8
**Vulnerability to "Loudness Wars" Compression** : CDs are susceptible to distortion caused by the "loudness wars," a post-recording practice of using excessive compression to make a CD sound louder. This process often removes dynamic nuances, resulting in a "pristine digital signal" that sounds "awful" and distorted. "CDs can sound awful because of compression, this post-recording practice of making a CD scene louder." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=D8GU8ZpSgY8
