# instrument - Big Muff

## curiosities
**Beyond Guitar Use** : Fuzz boxes and distortion pedals, particularly the Big Muff, proved versatile enough to be used on instruments other than guitars. Any electrical signal could be routed through the pedal to achieve a growling sound. Examples include John Lorde of Deep Purple running a Hammond organ through a pedal on "Highway Star" (1972) and Martin Gore and Vince Clark of Depeche Mode experimenting with their Korg and Roland synthesizers through a Big Muff on their first album in 1980. "Fuzz boxes and distortion pedals, especially those like the Big Muff, could be used for things other than a guitar." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iGav9uJwA_w
**Broad Influence Across Genres** : The Big Muff pedal became immensely popular and influential among guitarists across various genres and eras, contributing to the "Electrohar sound" of the 1970s. Notable users included Jimi Hendrix, David Gilmour of Pink Floyd, Carlos Santana, Ace Frehley and Paul Stanley of Kiss, Thin Lizzy, and Frank Zappa. Its versatility allowed it to work effectively with both chords and individual notes, making them stand out. Its impact continued into the 90s alt-rock scene, with bands like Korn, Bush, and The Smashing Pumpkins heavily relying on it. "The Big Muff was a huge hit with guitars everywhere." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iGav9uJwA_w
**Sustain and Distortion Innovation (1969)** : The Big Muff was introduced in 1969 by Electroharmonics, founded by Mike Matthews, who sought to advance beyond existing fuzz units like his own Muff Fuzz. Developed by Bell Labs engineer Bob Meyer, it was designed to not only provide on-demand fuzz but also to make a guitar sound "ring for a long time," introducing the concept of significant sustain. It was also notably the first unit to explicitly use the term "distortion" alongside "fuzz" in its description. "He hired an engineer at Bell Labs, named Bob Meyer, to come up with a foot pedal that could provide on-demand fuzz, but also one that could make a guitar sound ring for a long time. And the word here is sustain." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iGav9uJwA_w
