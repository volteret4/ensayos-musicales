# instrument - Fuzz pedal

## curiosities
**Features of the Maestro FZ-1 Fuzz-Tone** : The Maestro FZ-1 Fuzz-Tone was described in a 1962 demonstration record as a built-in foot pedal switch that attached to an amplifier with a handy jack. It was designed to create various effects with the touch of a toe, without interfering with regular guitar or bass sounds when turned off. It featured both a volume control and an attack control, was completely transistorized, and had its own self-contained power supply. Demonstration examples included a "Sousa Fone effect" and a "tuba sound" when used with an electric bass. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6SdPPsmpgJg
**Impact and Popularity After "Satisfaction"** : The Maestro FZ-1 Fuzz-Tone gained immense popularity after The Rolling Stones' "(I Can't Get No) Satisfaction" became a hit. Despite Keith Richards initially intending the distorted guitar riff to be a placeholder for a horn section and disliking the fuzz sound himself, the rest of the band loved it. Its success cemented the distorted guitar's place as a central element in rock music, leading to a surge in demand for the Maestro FZ-1 and inspiring other manufacturers to create their own versions. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6SdPPsmpgJg
**Invention of the Maestro FZ-1 Fuzz-Tone** : The first-ever fuzz pedal, the Maestro FZ-1 Fuzz-Tone, was developed after an accidental equipment failure in late 1960. Session guitarist Grady Martin plugged his baritone guitar into a faulty recording console at Nashville's Quonset Hut studio, resulting in a fuzzy, distorted sound on Marty Robbins' hit "Don't Worry." Gibson Guitars, recognizing the sound's popularity, commissioned Martin, engineer Glenn Snottie, and WSMTV engineer Revis Hobbs to design a circuit that could replicate it on demand. The resulting foot pedal prototype led to Gibson manufacturing the Maestro FZ-1, released with a demonstration record in 1962. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6SdPPsmpgJg
**Other Notable Fuzz Pedals** : Following the success and influence of the Maestro FZ-1 Fuzz-Tone, other manufacturers began developing their own distortion pedals. Prominent examples that emerged included the Tone Bender, the Fuzz Face, and the Electro-Harmonix Big Muff. These pedals further diversified the range of distorted sounds available to guitarists. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6SdPPsmpgJg
