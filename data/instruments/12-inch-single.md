# instrument - 12-inch Single

## curiosities
**"Disco Discs" and DJ Preference** : Initially referred to as "disco discs" because they were designed for old-school discos, 12-inch singles quickly became favored by DJs due to their easier handling compared to smaller 7-inch singles. Their prevalence, combined with advanced turntables like the Technics SL-1200, elevated record mixing to a new level. "DJs liked them a lot, because they were easier to handle than the smaller seven-inch singles." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0FYq5MBdHw4
**Demand and Design (Disco Era)** : The 12-inch single emerged during the disco era due to DJs and dancers' demand for extended song versions with additional dance beats, allowing for longer grooves and seamless beat mixing. These vinyl records, typically played at 45 RPM, were cut with wider and deeper grooves to store more information, resulting in louder playback and powerful deep bass. "So what DJs and dancers wanted was an extended version of the song with an emphasis on adding additional segments of dance beats." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0FYq5MBdHw4
**Survival Post-Disco and Genre Adoption** : Despite the death of disco, the 12-inch single continued to thrive as people still wanted to dance. By the early 1980s, it was adopted across various genres, including rock, technopop, and the alternative scene, with artists creating extended dance mixes. "When disco died, the 12-inch single lived on. People still wanted to dance no matter what the genre of music." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0FYq5MBdHw4
**Tom Moulton's Accidental Invention (1974)** : The 12-inch single was accidentally invented by Tom Moulton in 1974 when he ran out of 7-inch blanks while on a tight deadline and used 12-inch blanks, stretching the grooves to fill the space. The first record created this way was Al Downing's "I'll Be Holding On." "The inventor of the 12-inch was Tom Molten, and his invention was a mistake." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0FYq5MBdHw4
