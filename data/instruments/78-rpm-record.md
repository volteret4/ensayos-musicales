# instrument - 78 RPM record

## curiosities
**Continued Use in Other Regions and Promotional Items** : The 78 RPM format persisted in parts of the world, particularly South America (Columbia, Uruguay, Argentina, Brazil) as late as 1963, and later in South Africa, India, and the Philippines, though documentation is scarce. Special promotional 78s were also produced, such as Dave Edmunds' 1990 10-inch single for "King of Love" which included a 78 RPM version. Many turntables sold through the 1960s, 70s, and even 80s, continued to include a 78 speed setting. "However, the 78 lived on in other parts of the world, especially in South America, Columbia, Uruguay, Argentina, Brazil, perhaps as late as 1963." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Epz5Sn151bk
**Last Official Releases in US and UK (1960)** : The last 78 RPM records officially released by record companies in America were Buster Brown's "Fanny May" and Bobby Marshan's "Something On Your Mind" in June 1960. In the UK, HMV Records continued releasing 78s until November 1960, with Ray Charles's catalog number POP742 being the apparent last. "That was the last 78 RPM record officially released by our record company." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Epz5Sn151bk
**Long-Standing Standard Format** : The 78 RPM record was the predominant format for recorded music for over 50 years, appearing before 1900 and becoming the standard global format for decades. It was eventually supplanted by the 12-inch vinyl LP and the 7-inch single. "The 78 RPM record was the heart and soul of recorded music for more than 50 years. It started showing up sometime before 1900, and was eventually adopted as the standard global format for decades before being shoved out of the way by both the 12 inch vinyl LP and the 7 inch single." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Epz5Sn151bk
