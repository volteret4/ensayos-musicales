**Early Limitations and Polyphony (Pre-FM Synthesis)** : In their early development, keyboard synthesizers were monophonic, capable of playing only one note at a time, which restricted them from playing chords. The subsequent introduction of polyphonic synthesizers allowed for multiple notes, but the sounds produced were often artificial and robotic, thereby limiting their practical applications. "In the early days of keyboard synthesizers, you could only play one note at a time. You couldn't play chords. Then came polyphonic synthesizers, which allowed you to play, you know, two, three, four, five, six, whatever, as many notes as you want. Press the keys, you get sounds. But since sounded very artificial and very robotic, which limited their use." ← Ongoing History of New Music > Music Questions People Almost Never Ask | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=TsiYLGGSJ2U&list=PLM8bxV0E1Gse5C3n1u-mkVSTZLL5AJICG

**Frequency Modulation (FM) Synthesis Breakthrough** : John Chowning at Stanford University pioneered Frequency Modulation (FM) Synthesis, a technology that revolutionized the synthesizer industry. By manipulating electronically generated sine waves, Chowning discovered a method to make synthesizers produce sounds resembling real instruments, significantly enhancing their utility and appeal. "The big breakthrough came from a guy named John Chaoning at Stanford University. He was fiddling with some electronically generated sine waves, and he figured out a way to make them behave so that they began to sound like real instruments. Long story short, he came up with a technology known as Frequency Modulation Synthesis, or FM Synthesis for short." ← Ongoing History of New Music > Music Questions People Almost Never Ask | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=TsiYLGGSJ2U&list=PLM8bxV0E1Gse5C3n1u-mkVSTZLL5AJICG
