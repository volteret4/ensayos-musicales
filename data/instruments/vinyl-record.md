## curiosities
**Composition and Durability** : Vinyl, or polyvinyl chloride, is a plastic polymer derived from petroleum, initially used in the 1920s for manufacturing sewer pipes. It is a tough material that can be recycled up to seven times and has a lifespan of up to 140 years before degradation, although each recycling cycle introduces impurities. "The word vinyl is short for polyvinyl chloride, which chemist tell us is a plastic polymer made from petroleum that was first used in the 1920s for the manufacturer of sewer pipes." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IIJySSo276k
**From Shellac Crisis to Multi-Billion Dollar Industry** : The vinyl record, made from polyvinyl chloride, revolutionized the music industry after its introduction as a substitute for shellac during the World War II shortage. Developed by Dr. Peter Goldmark and Columbia Records, vinyl proved more durable and allowed for closer-cut grooves, increasing music capacity fivefold over 78 RPM shellac discs. The subsequent introduction of the 12-inch 33 1/3 RPM LP and the 7-inch 45 RPM single propelled the recorded music industry into a multi-billion-dollar enterprise. "Vial proved to be much more durable for phonograph records, and because the grooves could be cut so much closer together, these records could hold five times the amount of music per side than an old 78 RPM shellac disc." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_Pi7JLwAeOI
**LP Definition and Structure** : An "LP," short for "long playing," is interchangeable with "album" and refers to 12-inch diameter vinyl records that store music in "microgrooves." The continuous spiral groove on a 12-inch LP is approximately 500 meters (1600 feet) long, requiring the stylus to travel about a kilometer to play both sides. An LP includes a "lead-in groove" on the outer circumference for stylus placement and a "dead wax" area—the empty space between the grooves and the center label—which may contain "runoff groove messages" from artists or catalog information. "The terms LP and album are interchangeable. They both refer to vinyl records, 12 inches in diameter, that store the music in what are called micro grooves." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IIJySSo276k
**Rarity vs. Demand in Pricing** : The value of a used record is determined solely by demand. A record can be exceptionally rare, but if there's no interest, it holds no monetary worth. Conversely, strong market demand can make even non-pristine or unplayable rare items, such as specific 78s or test pressings, highly valuable. "It could be extremely rare, but if no one cares, it's not worth anything." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IIJySSo276k
**Reasons for Vinyl's Resurgence** : The vinyl revival is attributed to a desire for a deeper relationship with music, offering a tangible, collectible, and displayable format. The "ritual" of playing records fosters a sense of pride and connection, encouraging listeners to engage with albums "as the artist intended," distinguishing music consumption from "music connoisseurship" and allowing for a curated physical collection. "Some people wanted to have a deeper relationship with music than what digital files can provide." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XM1RvL7Kdts

**Remastered Vinyl** : Remastered vinyl refers to recordings where producers and engineers have revisited the original master tapes and utilized the latest technologies to create a new, often enhanced, version of the recording. "It's also a remaster, which means the producers and engineers went back to the original master tapes and started creating this new recording from those raw materials using the latest technologies." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IIJySSo276k
**The Term "Vinyl"** : It is specified that the word "vinyl" does not have a plural form; therefore, one should say "records" when referring to a collection, not "vinyls." "First of all, there is no plural of vinyl. You can say you collect records, but never ever say that you collect vinyl." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IIJySSo276k
**Virgin vs. Recycled Vinyl** : Records made from "virgin vinyl" are preferred as recycled vinyl introduces impurities that can negatively impact sonic quality. This issue became prevalent in the late 1970s and early 1980s following the early 1970s oil crisis, when rising petroleum costs led labels to use more recycled, thinner vinyl. This resulted in shallower grooves that stored less sonic information (especially bass) and made records more prone to damage, contributing to the shift towards CDs in the early 1980s. "Try to look for records made with virgin vinyl. That's the term. We don't want impurities because they can result in less than stellar sonic qualities." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IIJySSo276k
**Weight and Quality** : Reissued vinyl records are often listed with their weight (e.g., 140g, 180g, 200g, 220g), indicating the amount of vinyl used in their production. While some debate its direct impact on audio quality, heavier vinyl (typically above 120-150g) is generally less prone to warping, which ensures the record sits flat on the turntable, allowing the stylus to trace the grooves more easily and reducing wear. "Well, it has to do with the weight of the record, reflective of how much actual vinyl was used to make that disk." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IIJySSo276k
