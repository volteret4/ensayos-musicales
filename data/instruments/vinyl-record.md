## curiosities
**Composition and Durability** : Vinyl, or polyvinyl chloride, is a plastic polymer derived from petroleum, initially used in the 1920s for manufacturing sewer pipes. It is a tough material that can be recycled up to seven times and has a lifespan of up to 140 years before degradation, although each recycling cycle introduces impurities. **From Shellac Crisis to Multi-Billion Dollar Industry** : The vinyl record, made from polyvinyl chloride, revolutionized the music industry after its introduction as a substitute for shellac during the World War II shortage. Developed by Dr. Peter Goldmark and Columbia Records, vinyl proved more durable and allowed for closer-cut grooves, increasing music capacity fivefold over 78 RPM shellac discs. The subsequent introduction of the 12-inch 33 1/3 RPM LP and the 7-inch 45 RPM single propelled the recorded music industry into a multi-billion-dollar enterprise. "Vial proved to be much more durable for phonograph records, and because the grooves could be cut so much closer together, these records could hold five times the amount of music per side than an old 78 RPM shellac disc." ← Ongoing History of New Music > Big Picture Stuff： Part 1 | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_Pi7JLwAeOI&list=PLM8bxV0E1Gse5C3n1u-mkVSTZLL5AJICG
**Groove Surface Area Variation** : Due to the diminishing diameter of the spiral groove, the physical space occupied by a minute of sound varies significantly across a vinyl record. The first minute of sound, located in the outer part of the record, occupies a surface area roughly twice as large as the last minute of sound, which is packed into the inner part of the record. This difference in physical space directly correlates to the amount of detail and fidelity that can be preserved for the audio signal. "El primer minuto de sonido vemos que ocupa toda esta superficie. **LP Definition and Structure** : An "LP," short for "long playing," is interchangeable with "album" and refers to 12-inch diameter vinyl records that store music in "microgrooves." The continuous spiral groove on a 12-inch LP is approximately 500 meters (1600 feet) long, requiring the stylus to travel about a kilometer to play both sides. An LP includes a "lead-in groove" on the outer circumference for stylus placement and a "dead wax" area—the empty space between the grooves and the center label—which may contain "runoff groove messages" from artists or catalog information. "The terms LP and album are interchangeable. They both refer to vinyl records, 12 inches in diameter, that store the music in what are called micro grooves." ← Ongoing History of New Music > The Beginners Guide to Vinyl | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IIJySSo276k&list=PLM8bxV0E1Gse5C3n1u-mkVSTZLL5AJICG
**LPs and 33 RPM** : The majority of Long Play (LP) records are recorded at 33 revolutions per minute. This speed is favored for LPs because it allows for a greater storage capacity, enabling more than an hour of sound to be recorded across both sides of the disc. This format prioritizes the quantity of music over the maximum possible sound fidelity, making it suitable for full-length albums. **Progressive Compression Towards Record End** : As a vinyl record plays from the outer edge towards the center, it undergoes a progressive compression of the audio information. This occurs because the diminishing linear speed of the needle, coupled with the reduction in available groove surface area, means that the same amount of audio information must be packed into half the physical space towards the end of the record compared to the beginning. This physical compression inevitably affects the sound quality. "El disco de vinilo, según basonando va generando una progresiva compresión que hace que cuando llega al final del disco, el disco este sonando a la mitad de velocidad y por tanto recorriendo en la mitad de superficie que cuando empezaba a sonar." ← Music Radar Clan > Cómo funcionan las RPM de un disco | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1LsfPudneuU&list=PLBFijugu0fhAAIR-Ibh59aaU8VWg-SkLc

**Singles and Maxi-Singles at 45 RPM** : Singles and maxi-singles are generally produced to be played at 45 revolutions per minute. Although they offer less total sound content compared to LPs, the faster playback speed means each second of sound is allocated more physical groove space, resulting in higher fidelity. This format prioritizes enhanced sound quality for a shorter duration of music. **Sound Quality and Groove Length at 33 RPM** : At 33 revolutions per minute, a vinyl record turns slightly more than half a rotation (approximately 0.55 turns) in one second. This means that one second of sound occupies a groove length of just over half a meter (more than 50 centimeters) on the record. This speed is chosen to balance sound quality with storage capacity. "En un segundo el plato va a girar poco más de media vuelta, una acero como a 55 vueltas... un segundo de sonido ocupa algo más de medio metro." ← Music Radar Clan > Cómo funcionan las RPM de un disco | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1LsfPudneuU&list=PLBFijugu0fhAAIR-Ibh59aaU8VWg-SkLc
**Sound Quality and Groove Length at 45 RPM** : When played at 45 revolutions per minute, a vinyl record completes three-quarters of a turn (approximately 0.75 turns) in one second. This increased rotational speed results in a longer groove segment for each second of sound, specifically 71 centimeters. The greater physical space allocated to the sound wave at 45 RPM allows for higher precision and, consequently, better sound quality compared to 33 RPM. "A 45 revoluciones por minuto va a hacer 3 cuartas partes de vuelta en un segundo... recorre 71 centímetros. **Sound Quality and Groove Length at 78 RPM** : Historically, some records were played at 78 revolutions per minute. At this speed, a single second of music occupies more than 1.20 meters of the groove. This significant amount of physical space dedicated to the sound wave at 78 RPM translates to an "incredible quality of sound." However, this high quality comes at the expense of storage capacity, as such a long groove per second limits the total amount of music that can be stored on the record. This fundamental physical design dictates how the information is encoded and retrieved, leading to variations in the linear speed at which the needle traverses the groove. **Varying Sound Quality Across a Record** : A peculiar physical reality of vinyl records is that the sound quality is not constant from beginning to end. The first minute of sound, which is represented by the outermost and largest groove on the record, inherently possesses higher quality than the last minute of sound, which corresponds to the innermost and smallest groove. This phenomenon is a direct consequence of the physical characteristics of the spiral groove and the playback mechanism. "El primer sonido o el primer minuto de sonido que tiene un disco de vinilo tiene más calidad que el último segundo que el último minuto que tiene el mismo disco de vinilo?" ← Music Radar Clan > Cómo funcionan las RPM de un disco | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1LsfPudneuU&list=PLBFijugu0fhAAIR-Ibh59aaU8VWg-SkLc
**Virgin vs. Recycled Vinyl** : Records made from "virgin vinyl" are preferred as recycled vinyl introduces impurities that can negatively impact sonic quality. This issue became prevalent in the late 1970s and early 1980s following the early 1970s oil crisis, when rising petroleum costs led labels to use more recycled, thinner vinyl. This resulted in shallower grooves that stored less sonic information (especially bass) and made records more prone to damage, contributing to the shift towards CDs in the early 1980s. "Try to look for records made with virgin vinyl. That's the term. We don't want impurities because they can result in less than stellar sonic qualities." ← Ongoing History of New Music > The Beginners Guide to Vinyl | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IIJySSo276k&list=PLM8bxV0E1Gse5C3n1u-mkVSTZLL5AJICG
**Weight and Quality** : Reissued vinyl records are often listed with their weight (e.g., 140g, 180g, 200g, 220g), indicating the amount of vinyl used in their production. While some debate its direct impact on audio quality, heavier vinyl (typically above 120-150g) is generally less prone to warping, which ensures the record sits flat on the turntable, allowing the stylus to trace the grooves more easily and reducing wear.
