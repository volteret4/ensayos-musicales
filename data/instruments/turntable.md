## curiosities
**Buying Used Turntables** : When purchasing a used turntable, it is important to inquire if the machine has always been owned by the original purchaser and if it has been used for DJing, as DJ use often indicates significant wear, especially if it's not a robust Technics SL-1200. The website vinylengine.com is a valuable resource, providing a comprehensive database of turntables with pictures, manuals, reviews, and forums to aid in research before making a purchase. **Drive Systems: Belt Drive** : Belt-drive turntables feature a motor located off to the side, sometimes in a separate housing, connected to the platter by a soft rubber belt. This design aims to isolate the platter from motor vibrations, thereby minimizing the transfer of low-frequency rumbles to the tone arm and improving the listening experience. "With belt drive, the motor that spins the turntable platter is off to the side, sometimes even separate from the main turntable housing." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IIJySSo276k
**Drive Systems: Direct Drive** : Direct-drive turntables connect the motor directly to the platter through the spindle, ensuring the platter rotates at the exact same speed as the motor. Although some vibration transfer can occur, contemporary direct-drive models incorporate significant sound insulation. A key advantage of direct-drive turntables is their high torque, allowing them to reach playing speed (e.g., 33 1/3 RPM) almost instantly, a feature critical for radio stations and for DJs performing scratching and beat mixing. "A direct drive turntable is connected directly to the platter, right up through the spindle, which is that thing that goes through the hole in the record." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IIJySSo276k
**Early History and Terminology** : The concept of storing music on a flat, rotating platter originated in the late 19th century with German inventor Emil Berliner's gramophone, which differed from Thomas Edison's phonograph that used a wax cylinder. Today, the terms "phonograph," "gramophone," "turntable," and "record player" are used interchangeably to describe a device consisting of a motor-driven platter that spins at standard speeds, using a stylus, cartridge, and tone arm to convert groove vibrations into electrical signals for amplification and sound output. "The idea of storing music is microscopic bumps in a spiral groove on a flat rotating platter. Goes all the way back to the late 19th century to a German inventor named Emil Berliner." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IIJySSo276k
**Key Components and Function** : A turntable is comprised of a round platter that is spun by a motor at standard speeds. A stylus tracks the grooves on the record, and this stylus is connected to a cartridge at the end of a tone arm. The tiny mechanical wobbles of the stylus are converted into electrical signals by the cartridge, which are then transmitted up the tone arm to an amplifier and finally to speakers, where they are transformed into acoustic energy and audible sound. "A round platter connected to a motor that spins at standard speeds. The grooves on the record are traced by a stylus connected to a cartridge, which is at the end of a tone arm." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IIJySSo276k
