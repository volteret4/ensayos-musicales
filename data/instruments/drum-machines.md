# instrument - Drum machines

## curiosities
**Alternative Dance Basis** : Drum machines were a fundamental component of Alternative Dance, used extensively alongside keyboards, loops, and samples to create the genre's electronic foundation. "Lots of keyboards and drum machines and loops and samples." ← Ongoing History of New Music > The 90s Part 7： The Other Genres | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=amHre9ZZFkU&list=PLM8bxV0E1Gse5C3n1u-mkVSTZLL5AJICG
**Evolution from Novelty to Standard** : Drum machines, along with effects pedals, Marshall stacks, and synthesizers, introduced novel sounds to music production. Over time, they have become "part of the standard arsenal of musical tools," signifying their ubiquitous presence in contemporary music. ← Ongoing History of New Music > What A Drag It Is Getting Old (Musically) | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=m7MbV-eCLyE&list=PLM8bxV0E1Gse5C3n1u-mkVSTZLL5AJICG
**Evolving Understanding of Drums** : Initially, for Trent Reznor, a "drum was a button on a machine" for early Nine Inch Nails recordings. However, after touring for a couple of years with "real drummers," he developed a deeper understanding and appreciation for the sound of actual drums, leading him to incorporate them more in later works like the "Broken" EP. "To me, drum was a button on a machine, but after touring for a couple years with real drummers, I understood, wow, it doesn't sound like that button, and I wanted to incorporate some of the sounds." ← Ongoing History of New Music > Deep Dark Secrets of Nine Inch Nails | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=M3nUQtja0qc&list=PLM8bxV0E1Gse5C3n1u-mkVSTZLL5AJICG
**Integration in New Wave Music** : Drum machines were utilized by bassists like Peter Hook in New Order for programming bass lines, contributing to the band's distinctive sound and making it "much more than the sum of its parts." "Later, his work in programming bass lines and drum machines with new order drummer Stephen Morris made that band into something much more than the sum of its parts." ← Ongoing History of New Music > Alt-Rocks Great Bass Players | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kbFfT0pKchI&list=PLM8bxV0E1Gse5C3n1u-mkVSTZLL5AJICG
**Pivotal in Alt Rock Dance Scene (Mid-1980s)** : Drum machines were instrumental in the mid-1980s, utilized by bands such as New Order, Depeche Mode, and The Cure. Their adoption of these electronic instruments, along with samplers and 12-inch remixes, helped to forge the Alt Rock dance scene. "thanks to artists like New Order and Depeche Mode and the Cure experimenting with drum machines, samplers and 12 inch remixes, an Alt Rock dance scene was born." ← Ongoing History of New Music > The History of the 2010s Part 5： Music and Tech | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mNqqLDpUuZY&list=PLM8bxV0E1Gse5C3n1u-mkVSTZLL5AJICG
**Trent Reznor's Early Fascination** : Drum machines, particularly as used by bands like The Human League, OMD, and Depeche Mode, deeply inspired Trent Reznor's musical journey. He regarded their sound as "really cool," a significant factor in his development as a musician. "But the synthesizers and drum machines of the human league and OMD and Depeche mode. Now that was really cool." ← Ongoing History of New Music > Deep Dark Secrets of Nine Inch Nails | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=M3nUQtja0qc&list=PLM8bxV0E1Gse5C3n1u-mkVSTZLL5AJICG
**Used by Nitzer Ebb (Since 1983)** : Nitzer Ebb had been experimenting with drum machines, alongside synthesizers, since approximately 1983, reflecting their dedication to German industrial sounds. "They were big fans of the original German industrial sounds and had been experimenting with synthesizers and drum machines since about 1983." ← Ongoing History of New Music > The Post-Punk Explosion Part 3： Industrial | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zSFhhEaiYwE&list=PLM8bxV0E1Gse5C3n1u-mkVSTZLL5AJICG
**Used by Skinny Puppy (1982)** : Skinny Puppy, formed in 1982, incorporated drum machines into their distinctive sound, alongside keyboards, samplers, and loud guitars, making them crucial to the development of the modern industrial sound.
