## songs
**Bitter Sweet Symphony (1997)** : This song, intended as the band's big comeback single, features a 4.5-second sample from an obscure orchestral cover of The Rolling Stones' "The Last Time" from the mid-1960s. Despite initial permission requests to Mick Jagger, Keith Richards, and Andrew Loog Oldham being met with little concern, former Stones manager Allen Klein, who controlled the copyright to Stones songs up to 1970, refused sampling on principle. A deal was eventually struck where Klein would split royalties 50/50, but this meant 50% for Mick Jagger and 50% for Keith Richards, leaving The Verve with only a flat thousand dollars for the lyrics. "Much of Bitter Sweet Symphony is built around a 4.5 second sample from an orchestral version of a Rolling Stones song called The Last Time." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=MN97R6cxQVw
**Bitter Sweet Symphony (1997) - Nike Commercial (1998)** : "Bitter Sweet Symphony" was used in a Nike "I Can" campaign commercial, which debuted on January 11, 1998, during the NFC Championship. This occurred despite the song still being on the charts and against the band's wishes. The Verve had lost the copyright to the song to Abkco, the company that administered The Rolling Stones' work, following a legal challenge over excessive sampling from a Stones-related cover. Abkco then licensed the song to Nike without the band's consent, leading to significant disapproval from both The Verve and their fans. "Abco sued, got the rights to bittersweet symphony, and immediately licensed it to Nike." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DYtsWfguqGo
**Bittersweet Symphony (1997)** : Written by Richard Ashcroft, this song was inspired by a five-note progression from Andrew Loog Oldham Orchestra's 1965 cover of The Rolling Stones' "The Last Time." The Verve initially licensed the snippet from Abkco Music (controlled by Allen Klein) for 50% of the song's earnings. However, Abkco later sued, claiming more of the sample was used than agreed, and Andrew Loog Oldham also sued for $1.7 million in royalties. Under duress, The Verve surrendered 100% of the publishing to Abkco and Oldham, leading to the songwriting credits being changed to "Ashcroft Jagger Richards." Abkco subsequently licensed the recording to Nike for a TV commercial, which was seen as "selling out" and severely damaged the band's artistic integrity with their indie fanbase, despite it not being their decision. The song was nominated for a Grammy, with the songwriters listed as "Jagger Richards," and the original five-note bit was actually written by orchestral arranger David Whitaker. "Richard Ashcroft of the Verve came up with a song called Bittersweet Symphony." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AcADOuQUiAQ
**Bittersweet Symphony (Unknown Year)** : This song is noted as the only track from The Verve to ever make it into the Billboard Hot 100 Top 40, leading to the band being categorized as a one-hit wonder by a strict chart definition. However, this categorization overlooks the band's broader catalog of acclaimed songs. "Bittersweet Symphony, which is the only song from the band ever to make it into the Billboard Hot 100 Top 40." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=d_uDHds2XGQ

Despite an initial agreement for 50% of earnings, Abkco Music (controlled by Allen Klein) and Andrew Loog Oldham sued, leading to The Verve being forced to surrender 100% of the song's publishing rights, resulting in Mick Jagger and Keith Richards being credited as co-writers and receiving all royalties.
