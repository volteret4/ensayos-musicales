## songs
**Baby It's You (Year unknown, early 1960s)** : Originally titled "Al Cherishu" and written by Burt Bacharach with Mac David, Luther Dixon found the original lyrics "a bit too sickly." He revised them to be about someone who loves her boyfriend despite friends' negative opinions and retitled the song. For the recording, Dixon used Bacharach's original demo and overdubbed Shirley Owens' voice, making Shirley the only member of The Shirelles to sing on the track. The song returned the group to the Top Ten. "Dixon liked the song, but thought the lyrics were a bit too sickly. He changed the lyrics around, making them instead about someone who still loves her boyfriend, despite her friends telling her how bad he is, and retitling it, baby it's you." ← Episode 89： ＂Will You Love Me Tomorrow？＂ by the Shirelles | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7Kns2HGcuSg

**I Met Him On A Sunday** : This song was made up by the girls themselves and performed at a school talent contest. It became the first song they recorded for Florence Greenberg's new label, TRA Records, with Stan Green producing. It had minor local success and was picked up by Decca Records, eventually reaching number 50 on the chart before the label dropped them. "They performed at the show singing a song they'd made up themselves and met him on a Sunday and went down a storm with the kids at the school." ← Episode 89： ＂Will You Love Me Tomorrow？＂ by the Shirelles | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7Kns2HGcuSg
**Soldier Boy (Year unknown, early 1960s)** : Written by Luther Dixon and Florence Greenberg, this song became The Shirelles' second number one hit. Unfortunately, it marked their last chart-topping success. "the follow-up, soldier boy, written by Dixon and Greenberg, became their second number one." ← Episode 89： ＂Will You Love Me Tomorrow？＂ by the Shirelles | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7Kns2HGcuSg
**Tonight the Night (Year unknown, late 1950s/early 1960s)** : Co-written by Shirley Owens and Luther Dixon, this was the first record Dixon worked on for The Shirelles and the group's first Top 40 hit, reaching number 39. Initially intended as a B-side to a song with a lead vocal by Doris, its unexpected success established Shirley firmly as the group's lead singer. A competing version by The Shiffens also made the Hot 100 chart. "The first record he worked on for the Cherelles was also the group's first top 40 hit, with Shirley Owens, Dixon wrote Tonight the Night." ← Episode 89： ＂Will You Love Me Tomorrow？＂ by the Shirelles | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7Kns2HGcuSg
**Will You Love Me Tomorrow (Year unknown, early 1960s)** : Written by Gerry Goffin and Carole King, this song was chosen by Don Kirshner as the follow-up to "Tonight the Night." It featured lyrics by Goffin that brought a new depth to pop music, discussing female adolescent emotion and a woman's worry about being a one-night stand. Carole King wrote the string arrangement overnight, drawing inspiration from The Drifters' "There Goes My Baby," despite never having written for an orchestra before. The group initially disliked the song, finding it sounded like a country song, but Luther Dixon insisted on its release, leading to it becoming a number one hit. "The group didn't like the song, thinking it sounded like a country song, but Luther Dixon insisted, and the result went to number one." ← Episode 89： ＂Will You Love Me Tomorrow？＂ by the Shirelles | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7Kns2HGcuSg

## curiosities
**Deaths of Members** : Addie Harris passed away in 1982, and Doris Coley died in 2000. "Adi Harris died in 1982, and Doris Kohley in 2000." ← Episode 89： ＂Will You Love Me Tomorrow？＂ by the Shirelles | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7Kns2HGcuSg
**Decca Records Contract and Drop** : After "I Met Him On A Sunday" found minor local success, Decca Records bought The Shirelles' contract from Florence Greenberg. Although the song reached number 50 on the charts, their subsequent two singles for Decca were unsuccessful, leading the label to drop them. "They managed to get it to number 50 on the chart but the two singles they recorded for Deca after that didn't have any success and the label dropped them." ← Episode 89： ＂Will You Love Me Tomorrow？＂ by the Shirelles | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7Kns2HGcuSg
**Discovery by Mary Jane Greenberg** : After performing "I Met Him On A Sunday" at their school talent contest, they impressed Mary Jane Greenberg, a fellow student. Mary Jane insisted that the girls meet her mother, Florence Greenberg, who would later become their manager and record label owner, despite the girls' attempts to avoid her initially. "In particular one of the girls there, Mary Jane Greenberg, insisted that the girls come and meet her mother, Florence." ← Episode 89： ＂Will You Love Me Tomorrow？＂ by the Shirelles | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7Kns2HGcuSg
**Formation and Original Name - The Parakeets** : Shirley Owens, Doris Coley, Addie Harris, and Beverly Lee met at school in Pasey, New Jersey, about 16 miles from New York, and formed a group called The Parakeets. This name, Spanish for "little birds," was in line with the trend of early do-wop groups often being named after birds. "Shirley Owens, Doris Coley, Adi Harris and Beverly Lee met at school and formed a group called the Parqueos which is apparently Spanish for little birds." ← Episode 89： ＂Will You Love Me Tomorrow？＂ by the Shirelles | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7Kns2HGcuSg
**Influence of Luther Dixon** : After Florence Greenberg started Scepter Records and re-signed The Shirelles, the label's A&R director Wally Roker introduced her to Luther Dixon. Dixon's involvement as a staff writer and producer, and his eventual relationship with Greenberg, dramatically changed the group's fortunes, leading to their biggest hits, including their first Top 40 and number one singles. "but then a change in SEPTRA's staffing changed everything not just for the Chirrells but for the world of music." ← Episode 89： ＂Will You Love Me Tomorrow？＂ by the Shirelles | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7Kns2HGcuSg
**Maternity Leave and Replacement Singers** : As the group members started families, one or another member often had to be replaced for road shows while on maternity leave. During these times, session singer Dionne Warwick, who Burt Bacharach was producing for Scepter, filled in for them. "It didn't help that the girls were starting families, and at various times, one or other member had to be replaced on the road while they were on maternity leave." ← Episode 89： ＂Will You Love Me Tomorrow？＂ by the Shirelles | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7Kns2HGcuSg
**Pioneering Girl Group Influence** : The Shirelles were historically mistreated by their record company and largely ignored by rock historians due to their success falling within the 1958-1964 period, which is often dismissed as uninteresting. However, their success was pivotal, setting the template for the booming girl group phenomenon of the 1960s and profoundly influencing groups like The Supremes, The Marvelettes, The Crystals, and The Ronettes. "They made some of the most important records of the sixties, and it was their success that led to the great booming girl groups of the next few years." ← Episode 89： ＂Will You Love Me Tomorrow？＂ by the Shirelles | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7Kns2HGcuSg
**Post-Scepter Career and Multiple Lineups** : Despite the legal troubles that effectively ended their hit-making career, The Shirelles continued to perform in various lineups, with members coming and going, until the early 1980s. At that point, the original members went their separate ways, but each started their own version of The Shirelles. "They remained together in one line up of another, with members coming in and going, until the early eighties, when they all went their separate ways, though they all started their own line-ups of Chirrells." ← Episode 89： ＂Will You Love Me Tomorrow？＂ by the Shirelles | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7Kns2HGcuSg
**Reluctance to Professional Singing** : The group that became the most successful early girl group never wanted to be professional singers and had to be coerced at every stage. Their first public performance was a punishment for singing in gym class instead of paying attention, leading their teacher to make them perform in a school talent contest. "Oddly the group that became the most successful of the early girl groups and the one that more than any other set the template for all those that would follow never wanted to become professional singers and almost had to be forced against their will at every stage." ← Episode 89： ＂Will You Love Me Tomorrow？＂ by the Shirelles | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7Kns2HGcuSg
**Renaming to The Shirelles** : After signing with Florence Greenberg's TRA Records, The Parakeets were renamed The Shirelles. The new name was derived from Shirley Owens, the lead singer, with an "-elles" ending to evoke other popular groups like The Chantels. "The Perqueos were also renamed to the Chirrells after Shirley Owens but with the Al ending to be reminiscent of the Shang Tells and that was the name that would be known back from that point on." ← Episode 89： ＂Will You Love Me Tomorrow？＂ by the Shirelles | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7Kns2HGcuSg
**Rights to the Name** : Today, Shirley Owens performs under her married name, Shirley Alston Reeves, with her group "Shirley Alston Reeves and her Shirelles." Beverly Lee, another original member, owns the rights to perform as "The Shirelles" with no modifiers. "These days, Shirley Toys under her married name, as Shirley Alston Reeves and her Chirrells, while Beverly Lee owns the rights to Toys the Chirrells with no modifiers." ← Episode 89： ＂Will You Love Me Tomorrow？＂ by the Shirelles | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7Kns2HGcuSg
**Royalty Dispute and Lawsuits** : The Shirelles discovered that Florence Greenberg had lied to them about their royalties. They had been told their earnings were being placed into a trust for them until they turned twenty-one, but no such trust existed, and Greenberg had been keeping their money. This led them to file lawsuits against Scepter Records, but remaining signed to the label, they were unable to record for anyone else, which effectively destroyed their career. "They've been told that their royalties were being put into a trust for them for when they turned twenty-one, but they discovered that no such trust existed, and Greenberg had just been keeping their money." ← Episode 89： ＂Will You Love Me Tomorrow？＂ by the Shirelles | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7Kns2HGcuSg
