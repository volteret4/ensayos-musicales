**Fully Completely (1992) – Third Studio Album** : Released on October 6, 1992, this album was recorded at Battery Studios in Willesden Green, North London, in July 1992, produced by Chris Sangerides. It marked a departure from the band's usual live-recording style, with Sangerides building tracks from the ground up, starting with bass and drums for the first three weeks. This method contributed to a "bigger sound," more instrument isolation, and enhanced guitar sounds. The album immediately sold 140,000 copies in Canada upon release, reaching 200,000 within three months, and by January 2007, it was certified for over 1 million domestic sales (a diamond award), placing it in company with albums like U2's *Joshua Tree* and Nirvana's *Nevermind*. It was the only Hip album to be released in Japan, on April 23, 1993. A 2014 Deluxe Reissue included a remastered album, two bonus tracks, and a complete live recording from an invitation-only record release show at the Horseshoe Tavern on September 13, 1992. "they had no idea that they had created something extraordinary." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Vs1YyXgLpTE

**50 Mission Cap (Year Not Given) – Second Single from Fully Completely** : This song, the second single from the album, delves into a specific piece of Canadian hockey history. It recounts the story of Bill Barilko, a Toronto Maple Leafs defense player who won four Stanley Cups before disappearing in a float plane crash on August 26, 1951, whose wreckage was found on June 6, 1962. The title refers to a World War II US Army Air Force cloth officer's cap, often reshaped by pilots inserting a stiff object, like a hockey card, after being crushed by headphones. Despite initial concerns about a hockey-themed song's universal appeal, its strong lyrics made it work globally, with Gord Downie reportedly improvising the lyrics while playing a "caveman riff" and holding a hockey card. "But we all knew exactly what he was singing about. **Artwork for Fully Completely by Leav Prinz** : Rob Baker, the band's art student, was tasked with overseeing album art. While on his honeymoon, he developed conceptual ideas for the cover, including themes of a "Dutch life cycle" encompassing decay, decadence, birth, and death, as well as a "back and alien feast." Discovering artist Leav Prinz in *Zoom* magazine, Baker was impressed by her technique of pressing objects like bodies, coins, fish, and fruit onto a laser copier to create images with perceived depth. He contacted Prinz, who then brought a sketch to London. The final album cover was created in Battery Studios over a weekend (Friday night through Sunday) using a laser copier, guided by Prinz's distinct artistic vision and unique technique. The artwork was considered a crucial step in defining the band's visual identity. "And she created this beautiful life-size piece of art. And I think at that point that I realized there was something really special going on." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Vs1YyXgLpTE
**Audience Support During Final Tour** : During the "Man-Machine Poem" tour, whenever Gord Downie needed help with lyrics, thousands of audience members would sing them back to him, creating a profound collective experience. "And whenever he might have needed some extra help, there were thousands of people in the audience right there with him, ready to sing the lyrics back to him." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Um_KEEcCeT4
**Autobiographical Reticence** : The Tragically Hip consistently avoided creating chronological autobiographies or officially authorizing any books about the band throughout their three-decade career, leaving many stories untold despite their dedicated fanbase. "They've never been interested in any kind of chronological autobiography, and no book about them has ever received official authorization from the band." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=oMBmdfrA47Q
**Background Vocals on Fully Completely** : Producer Chris Sangerides made a specific request for guitarist Paul Langlois to contribute more background vocals on *Fully Completely*. This decision was based on Langlois's "unusual" and distinctive voice. Although subtle, these vocal contributions are noted to significantly enhance the album's overall arrangements. "Producer Chris Sangerides called upon guitar player Paul Langwad to contribute more, mainly because his voice is a little unusual." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Vs1YyXgLpTE
**Band Humor and Support** : The band's internal humor often emerged from difficult "bottom of the barrel moments" that would be crushing if experienced alone. This shared humor and mutual support allowed them to navigate challenges, with the brain's natural tendency to forget hardships aiding in their resilience. "Generally, it's really funny stuff, and that humor, I think, comes from these bare bottom of the barrel moments that would crush people, even if they knew that they happened to you." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=oMBmdfrA47Q
**Band Name Origin** : The band adopted the name "The Tragically Hip" from a throwaway phrase found in a VHS tape filled with skits by Mike Nesmith, formerly of The Monkees. Despite not considering it a "great name," they needed a name for an upcoming gig. Impressed by both the demos and a live performance, Greg and Gold signed on to manage the band.
