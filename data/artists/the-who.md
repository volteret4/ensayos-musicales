## curiosities
**Accidental Death of Chauffeur (January 4, 1970)** : On January 4, 1970, outside the Red Lion Pub near London, Keith Moon's car was pelted with rocks and bottles by a crowd of skinheads. His chauffeur, Neil Bowen, stepped out to try and calm the situation. In a panic, Moon, who did not have a driver's license, jumped behind the wheel and hit the accelerator, trapping and dragging Bowen beneath the car, leading to his death in hospital. "On January 4, 1970, Keith ran into an angry crowd outside the Red Lion Pub near London, a group of skinheads went after Moon and his Bentley pelting it with rocks and bottles." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5vKSjpvVQa8
**Backstage Rider – Pre-Show Nap Provision for Pete Townshend** : The Who's tour rider included a provision for Pete Townshend, who liked to take a nap before shows. To facilitate this, a large queen or king-sized bed with fine linens was required to be supplied in his dressing room. "To facilitate that, a large queen or king bed with fine linens was to be supplied in a dressing room." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=z2hvUDtBxho
**Band's Reaction to Cincinnati Disaster** : The members of The Who were deeply shaken by the Cincinnati tragedy, with one band member stating they would never play again if the incident had occurred inside the venue. While acknowledging the "football game boxing match feeling" that fans enjoy at rock concerts, they later appealed to authorities for assistance in making future rock concerts safer. "The concert went on as planned, but the members of the who were badly shaken." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Pgchw2h87aw
**Buffalo Concert Safety Changes** : Following the Cincinnati tragedy, The Who's subsequent concert in Buffalo, New York, implemented reserved seating for all attendees, a stark contrast to the general admission policy that had contributed to the stampede in Cincinnati, in an effort to enhance crowd safety. "And later this week, their concert in Buffalo, New York was, unlike Cincinnati, all buffalo seats were reserved." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Pgchw2h87aw
**Built by Kit Lambert and Chris Stamp** : Kit Lambert and Chris Stamp were instrumental in building The Who into the band they became. Their managerial efforts were crucial in shaping the group's early career and establishing their iconic status. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PzyvXmDKFUc
**Charges and Outcome** : Keith Moon was charged with drunk driving and driving without a license following the incident. However, the tragedy was ultimately treated as an accident, minimizing the legal consequences beyond the immediate charges. "Moon was charged with drunk driving and driving without a license, but on the whole the tragedy was treated as an accident." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5vKSjpvVQa8
**Cincinnati Concert Stampede (December 3, 1979)** : On December 3, 1979, a stampede occurred outside a sold-out Who concert at Riverfront Coliseum in Cincinnati, which had sold 18,348 tickets. A large crowd, many with general admission tickets, pushed forward after hearing music, believing The Who had started early. Only two doors were initially open, and the intense crush over 40 minutes led to 11 deaths by asphyxiation and 26 injuries, mostly among teenagers and young adults. "whenever you go through the stories of rock disasters, you always end up what happened at a who concert in Cincinnati on December 3, 1979." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Pgchw2h87aw
**Cincinnati Concert Tragedy (1979)** : On December 3, 1979, almost exactly ten years after the Elton Mont incident, 11 people were trampled to death at a Who concert in Cincinnati. The fatalities occurred as fans stampeded for prime spots in a general admission area. This tragedy led to significant changes in concert safety rules globally, including limiting ticket sales based on venue square footage and implementing new guidelines for ushers, security personnel, and evacuation plans. "After this tragedy, a number of new rules were enacted around the world." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7v1REnqKuXE

**Multiple Factors in Cincinnati Tragedy** : Observers attributed the Cincinnati stampede to several factors: the prevalence of 80% general admission tickets, which incentivized a rush for prime viewing spots; the prolonged waiting times for fans, some of whom had consumed alcohol or drugs; and the breaking open of only a single pair of doors, creating a bottleneck that led to the crush. **Trademark Instrument Destruction** : In 1964, during a performance at the Railway Tavern in London, Pete Townsend accidentally smashed the headstock of his new Rickenbacker guitar into a low ceiling. Annoyed, he proceeded to intentionally destroy the guitar and his amplifier on stage, a spectacle that was well-received by the audience. This incident led to The Who regularly incorporating instrument destruction into their shows, which became a significant trademark for a period. "At some point, Pete Townsend raised his brand new Rikin' Backer guitar too high and smashed the headstock into the ceiling." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yPok3MemDmE
