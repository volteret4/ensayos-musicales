## curiosities
**Collaboration with Stevie Wonder** : Stevie Wonder, after hearing TONTO, contacted Malcolm Cecil and Robert Margouleff, seeking a tutorial in synthesizers as he was free from his Motown contract and wanted to record the music in his head. Cecil and Margouleff worked with Wonder on three albums — "Talking Book," "Innervisions," and "Fulfillingness' First Finale" — over three and a half years in the 1970s, which constitute a core period of Wonder's career. Songs like "Superstition," "Living for the City," "Boogie On Reggae Woman," and "Higher Ground" were all composed and recorded using TONTO. "Stevie Wonder heard Tonto and called up Malcolm and Cecil. Stevie was out of his old contract with Motown Records and wanted a tutorial and synthesizers so he could finally start recording the music that he heard in his head." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6SdPPsmpgJg
**Influence on Black Pop and Quincy Jones** : Malcolm Cecil and Robert Margouleff's work with TONTO, particularly on Stevie Wonder's albums, changed the direction of Wonder's career and redefined what Black Pop music could be. Quincy Jones was highly impressed by TONTO, and what he learned from it shaped how he produced Michael Jackson's albums, drawing new attention to the possibilities of electronically generated music. "Tonto changed the direction of Stevie Wonder's career. And he changed what Black Pop music could be. Quincy Jones loved this thing. What he learned shaped how he produced Michael Jackson's albums and it brought all kinds of new attention to the possibilities of electronically generated music." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6SdPPsmpgJg
**Pioneering Electronic Music with TONTO** : In the late 1960s, Malcolm Cecil, a British jazz musician working for the BBC, along with American record producer Robert Margouleff, were fascinated by new synthesizers from Bob Moog. They set out to construct the world's largest modular synth setup, which they named TONTO (The Original New Timbral Orchestra). This behemoth, comprising gear from Moog, Oberheim, ARP, EMS, Roland, and Yamaha, allowed for unprecedented sound creation and multi-track performance, profoundly influencing electronic music. "These guys, a British jazz dude working for the BBC and an American record producer, were huge influences on the world of electronic music, but they were almost entirely behind the scenes." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6SdPPsmpgJg
