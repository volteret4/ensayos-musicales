**Loser (1993)** : This breakthrough song was developed with Carl Stevenson, whom Beck's indie label, Bungload, connected him with. Stevenson initially found Beck "a little weird" but became interested after hearing Beck's "doodlings with a guitar and a sampler and a drum machine." Stevenson encouraged Beck to rap like Public Enemy's Chuck D, but when this failed, Beck became self-deprecating, singing "I'm a Loser baby. So why don't you kill me?" This led to the song's iconic chorus. It was issued as a single in late 1993. "Anyway, the song was finished and issued as a single in late 93 and resulted in some pretty good things for Bex." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zT6pRGYLWhw ← beck
**Loser (1994)** : This song marked Beck's mainstream breakthrough in 1994, propelling him to widespread recognition. Prior to its success, Beck had released a series of independent records that were often perceived as confounding and unconventional, sometimes described as Antifolk, and frequently infused with elements of the Tweetpop aesthetic. "Before he hit it big with loser in 1994, Beck released a couple of indie records that were kind of confounding and weird to those who didn't inhabit the same headspace." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jhgZrwtYE2g ← beck
**Loser (1994) - Lo-Fi Breakthrough** : Recorded in 1993 during a six-and-a-half-hour session at friend Carl Stevenson's home, this song originated from Beck's self-deprecating comment, "man, I am the worst rapper in the world. I'm just a loser," after attempting to rap like Chuck D of Public Enemy. Carl Stevenson encouraged him to "go with that." The track was constructed by looping a guitar bit, adding a drum track, samples, and sitar playing, with Beck improvising the lyrics. Despite its solid fidelity, the performance was deliberately ragged, qualifying it as a lo-fi track by 1994 and contributing to its status as a significant low-fidelity hit. "So Beck started singing, I'm a loser baby, so why don't you kill me? Six and a half hours later, the song was done." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Jq-e-fZxXOs ← beck ← beck
**Loser (1994) – Raw Demo Version** : A very raw and unlisted demo of "Loser" was discovered buried at the end of the "Beer Can" CD single released in 1994. This track provides insight into the creative process between Beck and Carl Stevenson as they "trying to sort things out," offering a unique glimpse into the development of one of the 90s' most enduring songs.
