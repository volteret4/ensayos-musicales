## songs
**A Cappella Performance (1987)** : This extremely clean a cappella performance was recorded in 1987 while Suzanne Vega was sitting at Tom's Diner on Broadway in Manhattan. Fraunhofer engineers found it extremely difficult to compress cleanly and spent thousands of hours tweaking MP3 algorithms using this track. This led to her being known as the "mother of the MP3." "The recording is an extremely clean, acupella performance. Knowing that it was really, really difficult to compress such a warm, acupella performance, the frown-hofer people seized on it, trying again and again and again thousands of times to tweak the algorithms until they realized a clean, compressed track." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fhq9WVeiE_U
**Tom's Diner** : This song was used by the Fraunhofer Institute in Germany as a crucial test case during the perfection of the Moving Pictures Expert Group Layer 3 (MP3) compression algorithm. Researchers selected it because it was a very pure acapella recording, which allowed them to easily detect distortions and glitches in the compression if the algorithm was not absolutely perfect. They meticulously listened to tens of thousands of versions of the song to fine-tune the mathematical processes for optimal audio quality in compressed music files. "When the Fronthoffer Institute in Germany was working on perfecting moving pictures expert group Layer 3, they used a song called Tom's Diner by Suzanne Vega as a test case. The reason they picked this song was that it was a very pure accapella recording, and unless the algorithm was absolutely perfect, you could hear distortions and glitches in the compression." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=TsiYLGGSJ2U
**Tom's Diner (1981) – MP3 Reference Track** : This song was penned by Suzanne Vega in 1981 while at Tom's Restaurant, located at 2880 Broadway in New York City, a location also famously featured on the television show "Seinfeld." Its unique characteristics—being a pure a cappella recording with almost no reverb—made it an ideal reference track for the Fraunhofer Institute in Germany during the development of the MP3 compression algorithm. Engineers on the project meticulously listened to the song "tens of thousands of times" as they worked to compress the digital audio file to one-tenth its original size without introducing audible glitches, ultimately achieving a very good result. "Now I played that song because it was used as a reference track when the Fraunhofer Institute in Germany was working on creating the MP3." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7Zniri9uSlc
**Tom's Diner (1987)** : This two-minute a cappella song served as a "gold standard" for Bernhard Grill and the Fraunhofer team during MP3 compression testing. Its isolated human voice, devoid of psychoacoustic masking, presented a unique challenge for the algorithm. Early compression attempts were disastrous, with the sound described as "rats scratching all through the song," leading the team to listen to it thousands of times for refinement. The song was also suggested by the Fraunhofer team for inclusion in the blind listening tests during the international audio encoding competition in Stockholm in January 1990. "Grill's gold standard was this 1987 song from Suzanne Vega, Tom's diner." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v7DXXu_1fvM
**Tom's Diner (N/A)** : This song was the first ever to be successfully encoded into an MP3 format. Carl Hein's Brendan Berg and his team at the Froenhofer Institute chose it due to its delicate passages and big beats, which allowed them to easily detect flaws and glitches during the compression process. Its transformation into an MP3 marked a pivotal moment in music history, tracing back to the origins of the digital music industry. "Since it had so many delicate passages and big beats, Brendan Berg and his team could hear any flaws and glitches in the compression process." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=MTDY6qT11Dc
**Tom's Diner (Unspecified Year)** : This song holds significant historical importance as the very first track to be ripped into an MP3 file. It was utilized in Carl Heinz Brandenburg's laboratory at the Fraunhofer Institute specifically to assess the accuracy and effectiveness of the MP3 compression algorithm during its developmental stages. "The first ever song ripped to MP3 by chief inventor of the MP3 Carl Heinz Brennenberg." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PNiL63fY1Y8

These soundtracks played a significant role in introducing a wide audience in North America to various alternative rock artists, particularly British performers. "So many people learned of bands like The Specials and Spandovalet and Billy Idle and General Public and Wang Chung and Yellow and New Order and the Psychedelic Furs and the Smiths and Echo and the Bunyman, Susanne Vagan in excess the Jesus and Mary chain, Flesh for Luluo and so many others from those soundtrack albums." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zIkjd1OjCXM
**Mother of the MP3** : Suzanne Vega earned the title "the mother of the MP3" due to the instrumental role her song "Tom's Diner" played in the development and refinement of the MP3 compression algorithm. Its unique acapella nature made it an ideal audio file for researchers at the Fraunhofer Institute to test and perfect the technology. "That song and its use in the development of compression algorithm technology, the one we call MP3, is why we call Suzanne Vega the mother of the MP3." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=TsiYLGGSJ2U
**Tom's Diner Location (New York City)** : The diner that Suzanne Vega sings about in her song "Tom's Diner" was a real establishment located at 2800 Broadway in New York City. This specific diner later gained additional recognition by serving as the exterior shot for Monks Cafe in the popular television show "Seinfeld." "A little aside here, the Tom's diner about which Suzanne Vega sings existed at 2800 Broadway in New York City. It sat in for Monks Cafe on Seinfeld." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v7DXXu_1fvM
**Tom's Restaurant Inspiration** : The song "Tom's Diner" was written by Suzanne Vega while sitting in a booth at Tom's Restaurant, a real-life establishment located at the corner of Broadway and 112th Street in New York City. This same restaurant later gained additional fame as the exterior of Monk's Diner in the popular television series "Seinfeld." "By the way, that song was written in a booth at Tom's Restaurant, a real restaurant, on the corner of Broadway and 112th of New York.
