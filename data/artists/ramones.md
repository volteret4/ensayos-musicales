## concerts
- **Dingwalls (July 6th, 1976, London)** : Two nights after their Roundhouse performance, the Ramones played at Dingwalls. These two London gigs are credited with teaching "just about every single person who would become a major player in the British punk rock explosion" a crucial lesson, transforming their understanding of music. "Two nights later, the Ramones played a show at a club called Dingwalls."
- **Marseille Blackout Concert (1977)** : In 1977, the Ramones performed a show in Marseille, France, where their exceptionally loud volume and high power draw from the main electricity supply caused a blackout in that part of the city. "In 1977, the Ramones played a show in Marseille, France. They played so loud and drew so much power from the mains that they caused a blackout in their part of the city."
- **The Roundhouse (July 4th, 1976, London)** : This concert served as England's "first proper exposure to US style punk rock." Despite their European record company almost cancelling the entire tour, the Ramones delivered an "explosive show" to an audience of approximately 2,000, which included many future figures in the British punk scene. "But the Ramones busted out with an explosive show that left many people very excited."

## albums
**Debut Album (1976)** : The Ramones' debut album had been released a few months prior to their pivotal London gigs in July 1976. Its release had already clued in a select few in the UK to the nascent "punk rock" phenomenon. "Their debut album had come out a few months earlier and a select few in the UK were in the loop above this new thing that had been dubbed punk rock." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=qC0Ee4-dpnI
**Ramones (1976) – Debut Album** : The band released their debut album in April 1976. This release preceded their highly influential gig at the Roundhouse in London on July 4th, 1976, where they introduced their sound to the nascent UK punk community. "The Ramones, who had released their debut album back in April, were part of a triple bill with the stranglers and the flaming grueves at a venue called the Roundhouse." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=MTDY6qT11Dc
**Ramones (1976) – Self-Titled Debut** : Released on April 23, 1976, this album features a simple black and white photograph of the four band members in ripped jeans and leather jackets, posed against a graffiti-covered brick wall. This aesthetic was intended to represent the band as a unified "gang." The photo was taken by Roberta Bailey for *Punk Magazine* and was chosen after the band's initial preference for a style resembling The Beatles' *Meet the Beatles* (1964) was rejected by Sire Records art director Tony Wadler. The image was taken in Albert's Garden, a private community garden in New York City, and manager Danny Fields approved of its perfection. The cover helped solidify a distinctive punk-fashion look and has since become one of the most imitated album poses, even becoming part of the permanent collection of the Museum of Modern Art in New York. "This is a perfect representation of the band as a gang aesthetic." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sn5TWBFd0XM
**Ramones Leave Home (1977) – Second Album** : Released on January 10th, 1977. Tommy Ramone served as co-producer alongside Tony Bon Jovi, who is the cousin of John Bon Jovi. While considered "a little more poppy" than their debut, it was still far from mainstream pop. "It was a little more poppy than the first one, but this isn't exactly mainstream pop." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=j8uk7BnbXwU
**Road to Ruin (1978) – Thick Guitar Sound** : The Ramones' album *Road to Ruin*, released in the summer of 1978, featured a noticeably "nice and fat and thick" guitar sound. This was a result of producer Ed Stasium's preference for "big thick guitars," leading to more layered guitar tracks on the band's albums over time. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=CkSIErb0hxY
**Rocket to Russia (1977) – Third Album** : Released on November 4th, 1977, just 11 months after *Ramones Leave Home*, completing a streak of three albums in 18 months. Tommy Ramone co-produced this album as well. Its release coincided with the peak of punk rock, making it a "seriously important record" for the time. Johnny Ramone was highly motivated by the Sex Pistols' "God Save the Queen," feeling they had "ripped off the Ramone sound." The album features iconic songs that would forever be staples in the Ramones' setlist, including "Rockaway Beach," "Sheena Is a Punk Rocker," and "Cretin Hop (We're a Happy Family)." This album marked Tommy Ramone's last as the band's drummer. "A fine record, but it would be the last with drummer Tommy Ramone." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=j8uk7BnbXwU
