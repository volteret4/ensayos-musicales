## songs
**If You Wanna Get to Heaven (1973)** : This was their first single and was performed at the Ozark Festival. It reached number 26 on the Billboard Hot 100 in 1973. "If you want to get to heaven, you got to fail the game." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 46 - The Chaos and Music of the Ozark Music Festival of 1974 | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Jackie Blue (1975)** : Their biggest hit, originally written by Larry Lee about a male pool hustler and womanizer. During album mixing, the producer suggested changing the subject to a girl, leading Larry Lee to rewrite the lyrics. The song, with Larry Lee on lead vocals (despite often being mistaken for a woman's voice), was praised by Todd Everett as "strictly urban in sound and intent," highlighting the band's versatility. "Ooh Jackie, you're not so down." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 46 - The Chaos and Music of the Ozark Music Festival of 1974 | https://www.ftr70.com/

## curiosities
**"Definitive Kansas City Band"** : Rock journalism legend Greg Shaw proclaimed The Ozark Mountain Daredevils as the "definitive Kansas City band," noting their ability to create "a new and different world of their own, a world of mountain hodones, woodland romances, and lazy days floating down a river, a colorful patchwork quilt of the best of yesterday and the most promising of today." "The Daredevil's are creating a new and different world of their own, a world of mountain hodones, woodland romances, and lazy days floating down a river, a colorful patchwork quilt of the best of yesterday and the most promising of today." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 46 - The Chaos and Music of the Ozark Music Festival of 1974 | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Diverse Songwriting** : All six members of the band contributed to songwriting, which likely contributed to their diverse sound that made it difficult to simply classify them as Southern Rock. "And maybe it's because all six members of the band wrote songs, that's possible, right?" ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 46 - The Chaos and Music of the Ozark Music Festival of 1974 | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Incorrect Gender Attribution for Larry Lee** : Music columnist Bill Mann mistakenly wrote in the Montreal Gazette on March 29, 1975, that "Jackie Blue" featured a "female lead," demonstrating a lack of research, as Larry Lee, the drummer, sang lead. "He wrote a Fleetwood Max new lead guitarist is Lindsey Bunky Buckingham and the Ozark Mountain Daredevil's new 45 Jackie Blue is an outrageous cup of Fleetwood sound right down to the female lead." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 46 - The Chaos and Music of the Ozark Music Festival of 1974 | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Origin (1972)** : The band formed in 1972 in Springfield, Missouri, located about two hours south of Sedalia. "The Ozark Mountain Daredevil formed in 1972 in Springfield, Missouri about two hours due south of Sedelia." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 46 - The Chaos and Music of the Ozark Music Festival of 1974 | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Record Label Pressure and Refusal to Move (1975)** : After "Jackie Blue" fell from the charts, their label, A&M, pressured them to produce more "Jackie" themed hits (e.g., "Jackie Red," "Jackie Green," "Jackie Brown"). The band refused, stating they did not approach their music that way. A&M then tried to persuade them to move to Los Angeles for increased TV exposure, but the band, many of whom were starting families and had farms in Missouri, collectively decided to stay, prioritizing their lifestyle over potential massive fame. "We all decided to stay in Missouri." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 46 - The Chaos and Music of the Ozark Music Festival of 1974 | https://www.ftr70.com/
