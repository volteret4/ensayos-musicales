**Werewolves of London (1978)** : Released in January 1978, this song was Zevon's most famous and his one and only Top 40 hit, reaching number 21 on the Billboard Hot 100 in May 1978, indicating a long chart run. Zevon himself dismissed it as "a piece of shit" and "a dumb song for smart people," acknowledging it as a novelty song (though distinct from "straight comedy" like Steve Martin's "King Tut"). The song ostensibly describes a "murdering fashionable werewolf" and sparked a dance craze; however, Jackson Browne offered an alternative interpretation, suggesting it was "more about a ladies man." "Werewolves of London was released in January 1978 It made it to number 21 on the Billboard Hot 100 in May which tells you it was on the charts for a long time and it was Zeevon's one and only top 40 hit." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 44 - Streaking, Werewolves, Sharks, & Drinking - 70s Novelty Songs | https://traffic.libsyn.com/seventies/334334714-44100-2-e5ed6adac25ea.m4a
**Werewolves of London (Cultural Impact)** : This song gained further cultural recognition through its inclusion in the movie "The Color of Money." In a notable scene, Tom Cruise's character, Vince, dances to the song in a pool hall and mimes slicking his hair back during the "his hair was perfect" lyric. Prior to its official recording, Jackson Browne had already started performing "Werewolves of London" in his concerts and gave Zevon credit, potentially contributing to the song's eventual recording. "Remember that scene in the movie The Color of Money when Vince, that's Tom Cruise's character, was showing off in the pool hall and he's dancing to this song and then he stopped to pretend that he was slicking his hair back on the his hair was perfect line." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 44 - Streaking, Werewolves, Sharks, & Drinking - 70s Novelty Songs | https://traffic.libsyn.com/seventies/334334714-44100-2-e5ed6adac25ea.m4a
**Werewolves of London (Writing & Recording)** : The song's title is credited to Phil Everly, who drew inspiration from the 1935 movie "Werewolf of London." The song developed organically in a session with Zevon, Leroy Maranel, and Waddy Wachtel, with Wachtel creating the opening lyrics ("I saw werewolf with a Chinese menu in his hand walking through the streets of Soho in the rain he was looking for the place called Li Ho Fux going to get a big dish of beef Chowmain") based on a recent trip to London and a real Chinese restaurant. Zevon added the closing lines about "his hair was perfect." Zevon initially wanted the song to be "more heavy and less cute," leading him to recruit Mick Fleetwood to play drums and John McVie to play bass, who were recording Fleetwood Mac's "Tusk" in the same studio. "werewolves was literally a 15 minute song that none of us took seriously we did it as spontaneously as could be and look what happened." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 44 - Streaking, Werewolves, Sharks, & Drinking - 70s Novelty Songs | https://traffic.libsyn.com/seventies/334334714-44100-2-e5ed6adac25ea.m4a

**Crystal Zevon's Role in "Werewolves of London"** : Crystal Zevon played a crucial role in ensuring "Werewolves of London" became a recorded song. Despite doubts from the male musicians regarding its hit potential, she strongly believed in the song and took the initiative to write down its lyrics, which ultimately persuaded them to record it. "the fact that this ever became a song at all we can give the credit to Crystal Zeevon she thought it was a hit and the guys were not so sure it was they said look if you think this is a hit right down the lyrics and she did." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 44 - Streaking, Werewolves, Sharks, & Drinking - 70s Novelty Songs | https://traffic.libsyn.com/seventies/334334714-44100-2-e5ed6adac25ea.m4a
