# artist - Jim Steinman

## curiosities
**"More Than You Deserve" Musical (1973)** : Jim Steinman's 1973 musical, "More Than You Deserve," was where he first met Meat Loaf when the latter auditioned. Many of the songs that would later appear on *Bat Out of Hell* were developed or conceived during this period, particularly during the National Lampoon tour that Ellen Foley and Meat Loaf participated in. **Clive Davis Rejection and Critique** : Clive Davis, the head of CBS Records, famously refused to produce *Bat Out of Hell*, stating bluntly to Jim Steinman, "clearly you have no idea how to write a rock song." This harsh critique came despite Steinman's unique blend of theatrical and rock influences. "CBS Records, which was run by the legendary Clive Davis, said via Clive Davis that, no way, be making this. In fact, Clive Davis told Jim Steinman, clearly you have no idea how to write a rock song." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 42 - Rock Operas of the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-42-rock-operas-of-the-70s ← jim-steinman
**Musical Influences** : Jim Steinman, writer of the songs on *Bat Out of Hell*, was heavily influenced by both Wagnerian opera and early rock and roll artists such as Little Richard and Jerry Lee Lewis. As a child, he would follow opera listening sessions with these rock pioneers. "The songs were written by Jim Steinman, who was heavily influenced by Wagner, but he also loved rock and roll. So as a kid, he would listen to some opera, and then he would follow that up as one does, with some little Richard or Jerry Lee Lewis." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 42 - Rock Operas of the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-42-rock-operas-of-the-70s ← jim-steinman
**Teenage Desires as Inspiration** : Ellen Foley, the female vocalist on "Paradise by the Dashboard Light," suggested that Jim Steinman's "hyper-emotional material" for *Bat Out of Hell* was likely him "working out his own teenage desires that he hadn't done in real life." He aimed to create something entirely unprecedented in music. "I don't want to be a shrink, but I think it was probably him working out his own teenage desires that he hadn't done in real life, but was able to experience in this hyper-emotional material. He just wanted to create something that nobody had ever heard before." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 42 - Rock Operas of the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-42-rock-operas-of-the-70s ← jim-steinman
