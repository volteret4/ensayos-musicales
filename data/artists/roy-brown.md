**Good Rockin' Tonight (1947)** : Roy Brown, a Black blues singer from Louisiana, released this hit song in 1947. Along with Wynonie Harris's version, this track helped move the notion of "rockin'" as a musical concept deeper into the public consciousness. "Some will point to a 1947 song by Roy Brown, a black blues singer from Louisiana, what I hit with a song called Good Rockin Tonight." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hQJ3r-DCoMQ ← roy-brown
**Good Rockin' Tonight (Late 1940s)** : Roy Brown wrote this song, confident it would resonate with wider audiences due to its consistent success in his own live shows. He initially offered it to Wynonie Harris, who declined. After relocating to New Orleans, Brown presented it to Cecil Gant, who, deeply impressed, made a 2:30 AM call to his record company head, securing Brown a recording deal. This original version became a minor hit, peaking at number 13 on the R&B chart and later re-entering at number 11. "In particular, one called Good Rock in Tonight, he thought might be popular with other audiences, since it always went down so well in his own shows." ← Episode 7： ＂Good Rockin' Tonight＂ by Wynonie Harris | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IgqSPoYRUCM

**Musical Structure of "Good Rockin' Tonight" (Original)** : Roy Brown's original recording of "Good Rockin' Tonight" is characterized by holding the first chord and repeating the same riff for the majority of the verse and chorus. Its chord changes are closer to passing chords, resulting in a "one chord feel" that emphasizes groove over complex harmonic progression, diverging from a conventional 12-bar blues structure. "Roy Brown's original, on the other hand, just holds the first chord and keeps playing the same riff for almost the entire verse and chorus." ← Episode 7： ＂Good Rockin' Tonight＂ by Wynonie Harris | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IgqSPoYRUCM

## curiosities
**"Good Rockin' Tonight" - New Orleans Recording, Non-New Orleans Sound** : Despite being recorded in New Orleans and engineered by Cosimo Matassa, Roy Brown's "Good Rockin' Tonight" is categorized as Coastal Jump Band music and does not possess the characteristic "New Orleans sound." "It's of the type we're referring to as Coastal Jump Band music." ← Episode 8： The Fat Man and Fats Domino | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=A-1R-DbVdcM
**Difficulty with Record Companies** : Roy Brown developed a reputation for being "difficult" after he sought assistance from the Musicians' Union to recover money owed to him by his record company. This led to a period in the 1950s where he struggled to achieve hit records. "Roy Brown got a reputation for being difficult. After he went to the musician's union to try to get some of the money the record company owed him." ← Episode 7： ＂Good Rockin' Tonight＂ by Wynonie Harris | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IgqSPoYRUCM
**Early Vocal Style and Influences** : Initially, Brown's vocal style was distinct, leading him to be hired as a "black singer who sounds white," which was unusual given his frequent use of melisma, a characteristic typically associated with black singing. His primary vocal influences were crooners like Bing Crosby, rather than traditional blues artists. "Brown's main vocal influences when he started were people like Bing Fosby and other crewners, rather than blues music." ← Episode 7： ＂Good Rockin' Tonight＂ by Wynonie Harris | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IgqSPoYRUCM
**Fan of Wynonie Harris** : Roy Brown was a significant admirer of Wynonie Harris and performed as a blues singer, often in a similar style to Harris. "Roy Brown was a big fan of Harris and was a blues singer himself. **Post-Music Career and Death (1981)** : After enjoying success in the late 1940s and very early 1950s, Brown spent decades unable to secure a hit. He eventually began selling encyclopedias door-to-door, using the unique marketing tactic of offering autographed photos of himself in black neighborhoods. He continued to release occasional R&B records but earned more from his encyclopedia sales before his death in 1981. "He eventually started selling in Cyclopidia's door to door, with the unique gimmick that when he was in black neighborhoods, he could offer the people whose doors he was knocking on, a nautical raft photo of himself." ← Episode 7： ＂Good Rockin' Tonight＂ by Wynonie Harris | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IgqSPoYRUCM
