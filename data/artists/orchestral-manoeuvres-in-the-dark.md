## curiosities
**Challenges of Early Live Performances (1970s-1980s)** : Performing live with early, finicky electronic gear was terrifying for the band, as something would invariably break down. Andy McCluskey resorted to telling jokes to the audience to fill the gaps while the band members frantically tried to fix or reprogram equipment. Monophonic synths required constant, difficult patching between songs using flashlights. "It was terrifying. Actually, it was terrifying and something would always break down. And I ended up having to have a collection of jokes to tell. The audience to fill in the gaps while they were trying to fix something or get something sorted out." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0fQQubt9UTI
**Continued Togetherness Post-Reunion** : Following their reunion, the band has managed to stay together. "And they've kept it together since then too." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DJGFUlQTUhQ
**Drummer's Unique Style (Early Albums)** : For their first three albums, drummer Paul Malcolm Holmes was instructed not to play a full kit to avoid "rock and roll spillage." Instead, he had to play the bass drum, snare drum, and hi-hat separately to achieve a syncopated, drum-machine-like sound. They even forbade him from using a cymbal as it was considered a "rock and roll cliche." "So Paul Malcolm Holmes used to have to play the bass drum on its own, then the snare drum, then the hi-hat. We wouldn't even let him have a symbol for the first three albums because they were a rock and roll cliche." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0fQQubt9UTI
**Early 80s Alt Rock Example** : This band is cited as an example of early 1980s Alt Rock, characterized by its use of synthesizers and a "fancy name," which The Smiths deliberately contrasted with their own plain naming convention and guitar-driven sound. After becoming "firmly hooked on the joys of electronics," they renamed themselves "VCLXI," after a transistor part, before adopting their "deliberately pretentious name" in the fall of 1978. "To begin as the id, and were formed by Andy McCluskey and Paul Humphrey's 1977. **Origin of Band Name (Year Unspecified)** : The name "Orchestral Manoeuvres in the Dark" was hastily chosen from a song title idea written on Andy McCluskey's bedroom wall, just before their first gig at Erics. They sought a name that conveyed their "weird" electronic sound, distinct from punk, disco, or pop, and almost chose "Margaret Thatcher's Afterbirth." "So we called ourselves orchestral music in the dark, utterly pretentious. **Punk Sensibility of Electronic Music (1970s-1980s)** : The band viewed their use of synths, playing with just two fingers, as an electronic equivalent to the punk rock aesthetic of thrashing a single guitar chord. They embraced electronics to create novel sounds, rejecting what they saw as "rock and roll cliches" of guitar, bass, and drums. "It was the punk sensibility, you know, just, instead of just thrashing one chord on a guitar, we were just playing with two fingers on synths and it was the same thing." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0fQQubt9UTI
**Resistance from Traditionalists (1970s-1980s)** : OMD faced significant backlash and criticism from those who argued that synthesizer music wasn't "real music" because machines were doing the work. They were deemed not "authentic, not manly, not rock and roll or sweaty enough." The Musicians Union also opposed them, accusing them of taking jobs from orchestral musicians, leading OMD to ironically display "Keep Music Live" stickers on their tape machine. "Yeah, well, this was it. We were up against this all the time. Synthesizer music is not real music." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0fQQubt9UTI

They borrowed it from Paul Humphreys' friend, Paul Collister, who had a home studio. "We needed a tape machine to be the rest of the band to put the drums on and anything we couldn't play as two of us. **Winston the Tape Machine** : Orchestral Manoeuvres in the Dark affectionately named their reel-to-reel tape machine "Winston." **Working-Class Origins & DIY Synths (1976)** : Paul Humphreys and Andy McCluskey, from the outskirts of Liverpool, couldn't afford expensive synthesizers. Paul, with a hobby in electronics, would scavenge old radios from relatives to create "noise making machines" that functioned as early, non-keyboard synthesizers, though their sounds were inconsistent. "We were actually playing synths because we couldn't afford one. We were playing all these noise. My hobby was electronics." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0fQQubt9UTI
