## albums
**2112 (1976)** : This album is a complex concept record featuring a narrative that includes the Red Star, the Solar Federation, and priests controlling existence in the temples of Syrikes. The story also involves a rebel guitar player who rises up and commits suicide. The entire work was inspired by the objectiveist philosopher Ayn Rand, particularly her books like *Atlas Shrugged*. "We've got the Red Star, the Solar Federation, and the priests who run the temples of Syrikes in their command over existence. And then we have the rebel guitar player who rises up and then commits suicide." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1KyNY3zv6uc
**2112 (unknown year)** : This album was a "make or break" record for Rush, following the commercial disappointment of their third album, *Caress of Steel*. It proved to be a major success, described as "hitting it out of the park." The entire first side of the album is a concept piece, blending Prog Rock with sci-fi themes. It draws inspiration from Ayn Rand's 1937 novel *Anthem*, portraying a dystopian society where music and individualism are outlawed. The album's narrative is set in the future city of Megadon, where priests and computers suppress creativity, before the Solar Federation and an unnamed entity take control. The album is fundamentally a song cycle about the rediscovery of music. "This was a maker break record for Rush." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=B0CZ1deEgic
**Caress of Steel (unknown year)** : This album, Rush's third, was described as "something of a bomb." Its commercial failure placed significant pressure on the band's subsequent record, *2112*, which had to be a hit to prevent the risk of losing their record deal. "Their third album, Currestive Steel, was something of a bomb." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=B0CZ1deEgic
**Clockwork Angels (2012)** : Released in 2012, this album demonstrated Rush's continued ability to "seriously rock in a very modern and contemporary way" even as its members were approaching their senior years. The album highlights Neil Peart's evolved drumming style, which had been refined through lessons he began taking again in 1994, emphasizing a "less was more" approach to his playing. "I want to skip way ahead to the Clockwork Angels album in 2012." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=B0CZ1deEgic
**Farewell to Kings (1977)** : Released in 1977, this album features the multi-part song "Cygnus X-1," which showcases Rush's musical chops and Neil Peart's lyrical abilities. The track's narrative about an astronaut exploring black holes was inspired by a story Neil Peart read in *Time* magazine. "Next track on my list of Neil Peer Decentral is from the Farewell the Kings album in 1977." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=B0CZ1deEgic
**Fly By Night (1975)** : This was Rush's second album, released on February 15th, 1975. It was recorded over five days in the same month that the band played their first Canadian show with Neil Peart. The album marked a significant shift in the band's musical direction, moving towards more complex arrangements compared to their debut. The record label initially expressed dissatisfaction with this new sound, preferring more heavy blues rock akin to Led Zeppelin, similar to songs like "In the Mood" and "Working Man" from their first album. "Fly by night was recorded over five days later that month." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=B0CZ1deEgic
**Hemispheres (1978)** : Released in 1978, this album features "Cygnus X-1 Book II," which concludes the narrative begun in "Cygnus X-1" from *Farewell to Kings*. It also includes "La Villa Strangiato," Rush's first purely instrumental piece, composed of 12 distinct movements. The recording of "La Villa Strangiato" alone took longer than the entire *Fly By Night* album. "Signus X1 Book 2 on the Hemisphere's album in 1978." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=B0CZ1deEgic
**Hold Your Fire (1987)** : Released in 1987, this album includes the song "Time Stand Still." The lyrics are autobiographical for Neil Peart, reflecting on the band's nearly 20-year career by 1993 and the passing of time spent on the road. The song is a plea to appreciate the present moment and a warning against excessive nostalgia, born from Peart's reflection on neglecting friends and family during intense touring in the 1970s. "The track ended up on the 1987 album Hold Your Fire." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=B0CZ1deEgic
**Moving Pictures (1981)** : Released in 1981, this album is considered the band's best-selling record. It achieved significant sales, approximately one million copies in Canada, five million in the US, and many more worldwide. The album produced several major rock radio hits, including "Tom Sawyer" and "YYZ." "I think 1981's Moving Pictures is the band's best selling record." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=B0CZ1deEgic

**Power Windows (unknown year)** : This album features the song "The Big Money," which addresses themes of corporate greed prevalent in the 1980s. **Roll the Bones (1991)** : Released in 1991, this album features the instrumental track "Where's My Thing?" **Signals (1982)** : This album, released in 1982, features the songs "Losing It" and "Subdivisions." "Losing It" is referenced by Neil Peart in an article where he likened his drumming career to an athletic undertaking, stating he'd rather retire than face the predicament described in the song's lyrics, which contemplate the sadness of watching one's dreams die.
