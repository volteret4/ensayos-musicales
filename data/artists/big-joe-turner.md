## albums
**Roll and Pete (1938)** : Recorded in December 1938 in New York with Pete Johnson, this song is considered by some to be the first rock and roll record ever, and has been widely credited as the first Boogie song to use a backbeat rather than a shuffle beat. All the musical elements of early rock and roll are present in Pete Johnson's piano part, particularly the phrasing in his right hand. The song primarily uses "floating lyrics," a blues tradition where the singer improvises from pre-existing phrases. **Shake, Rattle and Roll (Year not specified)** : Recorded seventeen years after "Roll and Pete," this song became Joe Turner's most well-known contribution to popular music. Written by Jesse Stone (under the pseudonym Charles Calhoun), it shows a clear musical lineage to "Roll and Pete," with a similarly structured 12-bar blues form, floating lyrics, and a blues shouting vocal style. The main difference is a much more pronounced backbeat, stressed almost to the point of parody, defining it as a rock and roll song. It was covered by Bill Haley and Elvis Presley and performed on shows promoted by Alan Freed. "The main difference here is that the backbeat is now stressed almost at the point of parody, because Shake, Rattlen, Roll is a rock and roll song." ← Episode 2： ＂Roll Em Pete＂ by Big Joe Turner | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=thpMb1A_fAs
**Shake, Rattle, and Roll (1954)** : Released in 1954, this song by Big Joe Turner is cited as one of the template songs for rock and roll during that era, indicating its foundational influence on the developing genre. **Blues Shout-Out Style** : Big Joe Turner was a prominent blues shout-out, a type of singer, usually a man, who could sing loudly and powerfully enough to be heard over a band without amplification in the early 20th century. Even with the introduction of unreliable microphones and weak amplification, shout-outs like Turner could compete with the volume of big bands like Count Basie's by sheer vocal force. "Blues shelters like Big Joe Turner could compete with that power, and get excited by the sheer volume of their voice, even over bands like Count Basis." ← Episode 2： ＂Roll Em Pete＂ by Big Joe Turner | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=thpMb1A_fAs
**Blues Shouter Influence** : Big Joe Turner was cited as a significant inspiration for Wynonie Harris's primary vocal style as a blues shouter.
