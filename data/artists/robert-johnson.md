## albums
**King of the Delta Blues Singers (1961) - Reissue Album** : This reissue of old blues records by Robert Johnson was given to Bob Dylan by John Hammond before its public release. Its release transformed Robert Johnson into a "systemic figure" among white blues fans in the 1960s. The album was critical in establishing Johnson's posthumous reputation, as he was largely forgotten by most blues musicians and listeners prior to its release. "Hammond also gave Dylan an album to take home and listen to, a record which hadn't come out yet, a v-issue of some old blues records called King of the Delta Blues Singers by Robert James." ← Episode 97： ＂Song to Woody＂ by Bob Dylan | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3DufLVcZyc4

## curiosities
**Crossroads Legend** : In the 1930s, Robert Johnson was repeatedly told to go away by other guitarists because he wasn't good enough. When he returned just months later, he was a master, doing things no one else had thought of. This led to the story that he sold his soul to the devil at a crossroads near Clarksdale, Mississippi (highways 49 and 61) in exchange for guitar virtuosity, a site still visited by people today. "So the story became that Robert Johnson met at a crossroads in rural Mississippi, where he sold his soul to the devil in exchange for guitar virtuosity." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pmKjZzypzvg ← robert-johnson
**Crossroads Myth Origin** : The popular myth of Johnson selling his soul to the devil at a crossroads for guitar skills was originally associated with a different Mississippi bluesman, Tommy Johnson, and there's no contemporary evidence it was believed about Robert Johnson. "Though that's a myth that was originally told about a different Mississippi blues man, called Tommy Johnson, and there's no evidence that anyone thought that of him at the time." ← Episode 97： ＂Song to Woody＂ by Bob Dylan | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3DufLVcZyc4
**Diverse Musical Tastes** : Ramblin' Johnny Shines, who traveled with Johnson, reported that Johnson enjoyed playing a variety of songs including "Yes Sir That's My Baby," Jimmie Rodgers songs, and polkas, even calling him a "polka-hound man." "Ramblin Johnny Shines, who travelled with Johnson for a time, described him as particularly enjoying playing songs like Yes Sir That's My Baby, and Jimmy Rogers songs, and Polkas, calling Johnson a polka-hound man." ← Episode 97： ＂Song to Woody＂ by Bob Dylan | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3DufLVcZyc4
**Dylan's Realistic Perception** : In his autobiography "Chronicles," Bob Dylan described Johnson as a "bothersome kid who played harmonica," later learning guitar from records rather than live performers, a portrayal closer to reality than the prevailing legend. Dylan was delighted to later learn Johnson's guitar teacher was named Hank Zimmerman. "Dylan's version of Johnson is closer to the reality, as far as we know it, than the Johnson of legend is, and Dylan seems to have been delighted when he found out much later that the name of the musician who taught Johnson to play guitar was Hank Zimmerman." ← Episode 97： ＂Song to Woody＂ by Bob Dylan | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3DufLVcZyc4
**Forgotten Before Reissue** : For the 23 years between his death and the release of "King of the Delta Blues Singers," most blues musicians and listeners would not have recognized Robert Johnson's name, possibly confusing him with the much more famous Lonnie Johnson. "if you'd asked most blues musicians or listeners about Robert Johnson in the 23 years between his death and the King of the Delta Blues singers album coming out. Most of them would have looked at you blankly, or maybe asked if you meant Lonnie Johnson" ← Episode 97： ＂Song to Woody＂ by Bob Dylan | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3DufLVcZyc4

**Missed Spirituals to Swing Concerts** : John Hammond wanted Johnson to perform at the 1938 "Spirituals to Swing" concerts but discovered Johnson had died shortly before. Big Bill Broonzy played in his place. "Hammond had wanted to get Johnson to perform at the spirituals to swing concerts, only to find that Johnson had died only a short time earlier." ← Episode 97： ＂Song to Woody＂ by Bob Dylan | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3DufLVcZyc4
**Mythology vs. Reality** : Johnson is widely perceived as the most important and influential blues musician of his generation, influencing blues and R&B, but this is a popular myth. He was influential on white musicians in the 1960s, but not on black musicians in the 1930s, 40s, and 50s. His lifetime best-selling record sold only around 5,000 copies. "There's a popular perception that Johnson was the most important blues musician of his generation, and that he was hugely influential on the development of blues and album B, and it simply falls." ← Episode 97： ＂Song to Woody＂ by Bob Dylan | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3DufLVcZyc4
**Post-Mortem Reputation Explosion** : Robert Johnson was known by myths and legends among hardcore Delta Blues fans while he was alive, but his reputation exploded in 1961 when his records were reissued, 23 years after his death. "Not when you anything about Robert Johnson when he was alive, other than some myths and legends among hardcore fans of Delta Blues, but when his records were reissued in 1961, 23 years after he died, his reputation exploded." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1VWJ0uCQx8w ← robert-johnson ← robert-johnson
**Remarkable Musical Mind** : Despite the myths, Johnson was a truly great musician, a versatile and skilled guitarist and arranger with a broader musical palette than his recorded legacy suggests. "While the myth of Robert Johnson has almost no connection to the real man, his music demonstrated a remarkable musical mind." ← Episode 97： ＂Song to Woody＂ by Bob Dylan | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3DufLVcZyc4
