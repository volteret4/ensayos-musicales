## albums
**Horses (1975)** : Released in November 1975, this was Patti Smith's first and most important record. Its release coincided with the Sex Pistols' first gig and predated the Ramones' debut album by six months, making it a powerful and influential work for many female punks and future Riot Girls. "Patty's most important record was probably her first one, Horses, November 1975." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Qss5kC2FUx0
**Horses (1975) - Debut Album** : Released at the end of November 1975, this debut album was an "epiphany moment" for outsiders and those seeking alternative forms of rock and pop. Michael Stipe of REM described buying it on release day, listening all night, and being a "changed person," calling it one of the defining moments in his life. "When Patty Smith's record came out of the end of November 1975, it was another one of those epiphany moments for weirdos, outsiders, and those who were actively searching for alternative forms of rock and pop." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wYuNolSDLFM
**Piss Factory / Hey Joe (1974) – Debut Single** : On June 5, 1974, Patti Smith and her group (consisting of a guitarist and a piano player) recorded a seven-inch single using three hours of studio time. Side one featured a cover of a Jimi Hendrix song, and side two contained an original composition by Smith, which historians believe to be "Piss Factory," depicting her experience working in a toy factory in Pittman, New Jersey. A total of 1600 copies were pressed and distributed to music and bookstores throughout Manhattan, where they quickly sold out. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Z9T9wi0vEvc

## songs
**Gloria (1975)** : Patti Smith's version of the Van Morrison and Them song, featured on her 1975 album "Horses." This track showcases her unique spin on a classic and exemplifies the "powerful stuff" she was creating at the time. "Patty Smith and her spin on the Van Morrison and Them song called Gloria, that's from her 1975 album Horses." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Qss5kC2FUx0
**Hey Joe (1974)** : Released on June 5, 1974, as the A-side of a seven-inch single on Robert Mapplethorpe's independent label, this song was written as a tribute to Patty Hearst, a kidnap victim who had recently gained newspaper notoriety. It was recorded with her musician friends vamped along in the background. "Side A was called Hay Joe, and written as a tribute to kidnaps newspaper-era's Patty Hurst." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VYi3r0G-ne4
**Piss Factory (1974)** : This poem, recited by Patti Smith with her musician friends vamping in the background, was recorded as the B-side to "Hey Joe" in June 1974, due to leftover time and money. It was written about a dead-end job Smith once held and unexpectedly captured the attention of the New York avant-garde underground, with its 1000 copies quickly selling out. It is often considered a contender for the "first true punk record." "Because they had some time and money left after recording Hay Joe, someone suggested that Patty read a poem that she had written about a dead-end job that she once held. Her musician friends just vamped along in the background as she read this poem." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VYi3r0G-ne4
**Piss Factory (1974) - First Punk Rock Record** : This one-off seven-inch single, with "Piss Factory" as its B-side, was released in the summer of 1974 and is regarded by some music historians as the first-ever punk rock record. The song was recorded almost by accident after Patti Smith and Lenny Kay finished an earlier session for a Jimi Hendrix cover. It told the story of Smith's experiences working in a doll factory. A thousand copies were pressed and distributed to independent bookstores and record sellers, selling out almost instantly and launching Smith's recording career. "No one, man or woman, was making music quite like this back then." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rEawe_1Fqf0
