## songs
**Strange Fruit (1939) – Anti-Lynching Song; Banned by Racists; Sent to Southern Senators Who Refused to Outlaw Lynching** : "Strange Fruit" was a landmark 1939 anti-lynching song that was banned in some places by racists who considered it too incendiary. Activists sent copies to Southern senators who refused to outlaw lynching. It created the template for anti-racism and civil rights protest songs that followed. ## curiosities
**Babysitter for Billy Crystal** : Legendary singer Billie Holiday used to babysit actor Billy Crystal. This connection arose because Holiday recorded for Commodore Records, a label co-owned by Crystal's father. "Legendary singer Billy Holiday used to babysit actor Billy Crystal." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=72Lei7uug8c ← billie-holiday
**Discovery by Youth** : A 21-year-old Humber College student recently discovered the music of Billie Holiday, despite the artist having died in 1959. This anecdote highlights how streaming gives modern youth access to the entire history of recorded music, leading to ecumenical tastes and the discovery of artists from various eras. "Somebody was saying, yeah, you have some 21-year-old saying, yeah, a guy. Yeah, yeah, I'm recently into the music of Billy Holiday. It's like what she died in 1959. He goes, yeah, I know, but I've discarded it and I'm into Billy Holiday right now." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4xxgPX-aR90 ← billie-holiday
**High School Portrayal** : Nora Jones portrayed Billie Holiday in a black history program during high school, an experience she described as "amazing." She auditioned by singing "Fidenmello" (likely "Fine and Mellow") and performed "Strange Fruit" for the main show. Nora actively tried to sound like Billie, and the director, Nedra James, provided spiritual guidance on embodying the character's life and story. "somehow I got to play Billie Holiday, which is crazy." ← Hit Parade Music History and Music Trivia > Introducing Broken Record - Norah Jones Begins Again | https://shows.acast.com/hit-parade/episodes/695d4f1264fe6d21276e0cbc
**Outlier in Malismal Use** : Billie Holiday was one of the few outliers among early 20th-century singers who would use a little malisma to ornament her sound, demonstrating a departure from the widely accepted "one note per syllable" standard, using it more frequently than Ella Fitzgerald. "There were a few outliers. Billy Holiday and Ella Fitzgerald, for example, would both use a little Malisma, Holiday more than Ella, to ornament their sound." ← Episode 32： ＂I Got A Woman＂ by Ray Charles | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0oeI9f2x_fo
