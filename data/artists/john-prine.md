## songs
**Angel from Montgomery (1971)** : Written by John Prine around 1970 or 1971 when he was 25, this song tells the story from the perspective of a middle-aged woman who feels old and trapped in a "thankless marriage." Prine conceived the idea while working as a mailman, visualizing a woman by a dishwasher who wished for an angel to take her away. He recorded it himself, but his friend Bonnie Raitt's 1974 recording made it a hit. Prine and Raitt performed it together multiple times, including on Austin City Limits in 2002. "Make me an angel that flies from Montgomery. Make me a poster of an old rodeo. Just give me one thing that I can hold on to to believe in this living is just a hard way to go." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 49 - Austin City Limits and the 70s Country Music Revolution | http://ftr70.com
**Hello in There (1971)** : Written by John Prine when he was 25 years old, this acoustic song was one of two with which he opened his first Austin City Limits show in 1978. Broadly, the song is about not forgetting the elderly, reflecting Prine's unique perspective and storytelling ability at a young age. "He opened the show with two acoustic songs, Fish and Whistle, a song from his new album, and hello in there, a 1971 song that he wrote when he was 25 years old." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 49 - Austin City Limits and the 70s Country Music Revolution | http://ftr70.com

## curiosities
**"Mark Twain of American Songwriting"** : In a 2017 Rolling Stone article, Patrick Doyle referred to John Prine as "the Mark Twain of American Songwriting" due to his exceptional storytelling ability. His music offered reassurance and resonated with those seeking comfort in life's bigger problems, making him a perfect fit for Austin City Limits despite his hard-to-categorize blend of country, folk, and blues. "In a Rolling Stone article about John Prine's life that was published in 2017, Patrick Doyle referred to Prine as the Mark Twain of American Songwriting." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 49 - Austin City Limits and the 70s Country Music Revolution | http://ftr70.com
**Austin City Limits Hall of Fame Induction (2024)** : In January 2024, John Prine was inducted into the Austin City Limits Hall of Fame during its ninth annual ceremony. Actor Ethan Hawke delivered the induction speech, praising Prine as a singer-songwriter who pursued excellence and truth over fame. "In January 2024, Austin City Limits held its ninth annual Hall of Fame ceremony and John Prine was inducted into that Hall of Fame." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 49 - Austin City Limits and the 70s Country Music Revolution | http://ftr70.com
**COVID-19 Related Death** : Grammy winner John Prine died as a result of COVID-19. He is remembered as one of the musicians lost during the pandemic. **Critical Success** : John Prine is mentioned as a "critical success" among singer-songwriters sampled by critic Robert Hillburn in 1973. He was featured in Hillburn's 1973 Los Angeles Times article alongside Bruce Springsteen and Danny O'Keefe. "Hillburn had sampled like popular favorites, like John Denver, those who had just had breakthrough hits like O'Keefe, a couple of critical successes like Prine, and then three quote unknowns." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 60 - One-Hit Wonders of the 70s | https://www.ftr70.com
**Eight Austin City Limits Appearances (1978-2002)** : John Prine performed on Austin City Limits a total of eight times, with his first appearance in 1978 and another notable one in 2002 where he sang "Angel from Montgomery" with Bonnie Raitt. "One Prine was on Austin City Limits eight times, the first time being in 1978." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 49 - Austin City Limits and the 70s Country Music Revolution | http://ftr70.com
**Example of "Stoner" Music** : Neil Bogart cited John Prine as an artist whose music catered to "heavy stoners" who would "sit there and drink your bottle of beer and get into that shit." This contrasted with the mood-setting, romantic music he envisioned for Donna Summer. "There's a music for the heavy stoners that sit there and listen to some dynamite people, John Prine." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 4 - Music of the 70s (Sexual) Revolution | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-4-talkin-bout-a-sexual-revolution
**Mailman as Lyricist** : Before his musical career took off, John Prine worked as a mailman. He would use the time walking his mail route to think through his lyrics, indicating a deep engagement with his songwriting process even during his day job. "Now back then, Prine was a male man. And so he would just use that time when he was out walking his route to think through his lyrics." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 49 - Austin City Limits and the 70s Country Music Revolution | http://ftr70.com
**Perspective in Songwriting** : John Prine demonstrated a unique empathy in his songwriting, notably with "Angel from Montgomery," written at age 25 from the perspective of a middle-aged woman feeling trapped and old. Bonnie Raitt, a young feminist at the time, deeply resonated with this song's portrayal of a woman in a thankless marriage, highlighting Prine's ability to transcend his own experiences. "It's a song written by a 25 year old man in 70 or 71 whenever he actually wrote it. From the perspective of a middle-aged woman who feels old and it works, Bonnie said that as a young feminist that there was something about a song written by a man from the perspective of a woman who was in kind of a, what seemed like a thankless marriage that really resonated with her." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 49 - Austin City Limits and the 70s Country Music Revolution | http://ftr70.com
**Post-COVID Loss** : The transcript notes the significant loss felt by John Prine's passing during the COVID pandemic, highlighting his immense value as a songwriter. "And John Prine's loss, that's still hurt." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 49 - Austin City Limits and the 70s Country Music Revolution | http://ftr70.com
