## curiosities
**Alan Price's Abrupt Departure** : On April 28, 1965, Alan Price abruptly left The Animals on the day of a European tour. Charles Chandler, his flatmate, believed Price's first royalty check for "House of the Rising Sun" arrived that morning, prompting Price to unilaterally decide not to share the money as previously agreed. Chandler did not see Price for 18 months afterward. "Chandler believed until his death that while he was in the shower, Price's first royalty check for arranging House of the Rising Sunhood arrived, and Price had decided then and there that he wasn't going to share the money as agreed." ← Episode 115： ＂House of the Rising Sun＂ by the Animals | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=34aIGCnb4X4
**Alan Price's Integration and Musical Tensions** : Alan Price joined the group on piano and immediately "jelled," despite Eric Burden and John Steele finding his musical tastes "pedestrian" and regarding him as a "lounge player" compared to their own "hipper" R&B and blues preferences. This tension was an ongoing factor within the band. "The other members would always like to say that they didn't like Price either as a person or for his taste in music." ← Episode 115： ＂House of the Rising Sun＂ by the Animals | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=34aIGCnb4X4
**Art School Origins in Newcastle** : Like many British bands of the era, The Animals' story began at an art school in Newcastle, where Eric Burden and John Steele first met. Newcastle's isolation as a major city fostered a unique local music scene, limiting musician options and encouraging bands to play within their immediate region. "The story of the animals, like so many of the British bands of this time period, starts at art school, where two teenagers named Eric Burden and John Steele met each other." ← Episode 115： ＂House of the Rising Sun＂ by the Animals | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=34aIGCnb4X4
**Backing Sonny Boy Williamson II** : The group, then known as The Alan Price Rhythm and Blues Combo, performed live backing the prominent American blues musician Sonny Boy Williamson II at Newcastle's Club a Go-Go. This performance was recorded and showcased their distinctive electric organ sound, which impressed Graham Bond. "That sound can be heard and alive recording of them a couple of months later, backing the great American blues musician Sonny Boy Williamson II, at the club of Go Go." ← Episode 115： ＂House of the Rising Sun＂ by the Animals | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=34aIGCnb4X4
**Break with Mickey Most** : The Animals eventually severed ties with producer Mickey Most, citing their dissatisfaction with his persistent efforts to push them towards a more pop-oriented sound. They subsequently signed with Decca Records and enlisted Tom Wilson as their new producer. "The group were getting sick of most attempts to make them more poppy." ← Episode 115： ＂House of the Rising Sun＂ by the Animals | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=34aIGCnb4X4
**Controversial Name Change** : When the group played London gigs in December 1963, they were billed as The Animals. Members could never agree on how the name originated, and not all of them liked it. **Dispute Over "House of the Rising Sun" Royalties** : The group's manager, Mike Jeffery, allegedly told The Animals that only one name could be credited as arranger for their public domain song "House of the Rising Sun," but that royalties would be shared. Alan Price, however, was the sole credited arranger and the only one to receive songwriting royalties, which became a significant source of contention. "Whatever the truth, Price was the only one who ever got any songwriting royalties from their version of the song, which went to number 1 in the UK and the US." ← Episode 115： ＂House of the Rising Sun＂ by the Animals | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=34aIGCnb4X4
**Dylan's "Stolen Song" Accusation** : The Animals' hit version of "House of the Rising Sun" ironically led to Bob Dylan dropping the song from his own live set, as audiences frequently asked him if he had stolen it from The Animals, providing Dave Van Ronk (from whom Dylan learned the arrangement) "the last laugh." "Incidentally Dave Van Runk had the last laugh at Dylan, who had to drop the song from his own set because people kept asking him if he'd stolen it from the animals." ← Episode 115： ＂House of the Rising Sun＂ by the Animals | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=34aIGCnb4X4
**Evolution from Jazz to R&B** : Eric Burden and John Steele initially formed a trad jazz band called The Pagan Jazzmen. With the rise of rock and roll and skiffle, they transformed into The Pagans R&B Band, shifting to a guitar-based lineup with Steele on drums and Burden as lead singer. "Steel and burden, when they met at art school, were both jazz fanatics, and they quickly formed a trad jazz band." ← Episode 115： ＂House of the Rising Sun＂ by the Animals | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=34aIGCnb4X4
**Following Hawkins' Musical Path** : The Animals are identified as one of the 1960s white rock acts that followed in Dale Hawkins' musical footsteps. Their sound incorporated elements such as electric bass, Chess-style guitar riffs, and country-inflected vocals, which were pioneered and featured in Hawkins' "Suzy Q." **Management Fraud and Disappearance of Funds** : The original group ultimately dissolved, discovering they had been extensively defrauded by their management. A tax-saving scheme involving banking their money in the Bahamas resulted in the mysterious disappearance of the bank and, consequently, their funds. "There had been a scheme to bank their money in the Bahamas for tax reasons, in a bank which mysteriously disappeared off the face of the earth." ← Episode 115： ＂House of the Rising Sun＂ by the Animals | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=34aIGCnb4X4
**Mickey Most's First Signing** : The Animals were the first band signed by independent producer Mickey Most upon his return to the UK in 1963, following a successful period producing cover versions in South Africa. Most licensed their records to EMI, and the band moved to London in January 1964. "the animals were his first signing." ← Episode 115： ＂House of the Rising Sun＂ by the Animals | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=34aIGCnb4X4
**Reunions and Later Formations** : The original lineup of The Animals reunited sporadically between 1968 and the early 1980s, with their final performance together in 1983. Eric Burden went on to form "The New Animals" and "Eric Burdon and the Animals." John Steele, Dave Rowberry, and Hilton Valentine reformed a version in the 1990s, with Steele continuing to tour as "The Animals and Friends." "There were a handful of reunions of the original lineup of the group, between 1968 and the early 80s, but they last played together in 1983." ← Episode 115： ＂House of the Rising Sun＂ by the Animals | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=34aIGCnb4X4
**Unusual Single Length** : "House of the Rising Sun" was 4 minutes and 20 seconds long, exceeding the customary 3-minute limit for radio singles. Producer Mickey Most was eventually persuaded by Charles Chandler to release the track unedited, a bold decision for the time. "The record was 4 minutes and 20 seconds long, and you just didn't ever release a record that long." ← Episode 115： ＂House of the Rising Sun＂ by the Animals | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=34aIGCnb4X4
