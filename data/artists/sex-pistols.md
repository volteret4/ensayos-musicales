## albums
**Never Mind the Bollocks, Here's the Sex Pistols (1977)** : Released on October 28, 1977, this album's cover features a solid day-glow background, with the full title "Never Mind the Bollocks Here's the Sex Pistols" spelled out in a mix of different fonts and upper/lower case letters, resembling a ransom note. Two versions exist: one with a pink background and another with yellow. The artist, Jamie Reid, developed this ransom note style while working for *Suburban Press*. The title was changed in mid-1977 from an initial concept of "God Save the Sex Pistols" after guitarist Steve Jones heard fans using the phrase "Never Mind the Bollocks," meaning "Stop Talking Rubbish." "The first three lines are different fonts, while the band's name is styled in a mix of upper and lower case letters, reminiscent of a ransom note made out of cut up letters from a newspaper." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sn5TWBFd0XM

## songs
**Anarchy in the UK (1976) – Debut Single** : Released on Friday, November 26th, 1976, this was the Sex Pistols' first single, following their first gig on November 6th, 1975. The band recorded many versions with different producers before settling on the final mix produced by Chris Thomas, known for his work with Roxy Music. The recording sessions in Fall 1976 involved five straight takes, with Thomas splicing together halves of different takes, rigging a tape delay to mask a snare drum sync issue, Johnny Rotten laying down vocals around 7 PM, and Steve Jones overdubbing 22 layers of guitars a couple of days later to fill out the sound. Initially, it had "little impact outside the punk community" in its first week but saw a massive sales surge after their *Today* show interview. "In the first week that song was in the stores in November of 1976, it had little impact outside the punk community." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=MTDY6qT11Dc
**God Save the Queen (1977)** : The band attempted to release this song in March 1977, leading to controversy and an infamous contract signing ceremony that ignited the punk movement. ## curiosities
**"Bollocks" Obscenity Controversy** : The word "ballocks" on the album cover was deemed obscene by some upon its release, leading to significant problems for record stores and radio stations. In London, police visited Virgin Record stores displaying the album poster, threatening prosecution under the 1889 Indecent Advertisements Act. This led to managers removing posters and at least one store manager being arrested. The controversy escalated when the story hit newspapers before the album's release, prompting ads like "The album will last, the sleeve may not." A court case on November 24, 1977, involved expert witnesses, including an English professor, testifying that "ballocks" was an old English term for priest or, in context, meant "nonsense." Everyone involved was ultimately found not guilty, and the album became highly influential. "The word ballocks was deemed obscene by some, which caused all sorts of issues for record stores in radio." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sn5TWBFd0XM
**"Today" Show Interview (1976) – National Controversy** : On Wednesday, December 1st, 1976, the Sex Pistols appeared on the popular UK tea-time chat show *Today*, hosted by Bill Grundy. They were a last-minute replacement for Queen, who had to cancel due to Freddie Mercury's throat infection. The band and their entourage consumed a bottle of Blue Nun in the limo and more liquor in the green room before going on live, unaware the show was unedited. Grundy's provocative questioning led to the band using profanity on air, which resulted in Grundy's two-week suspension, the producer and director's firing, and widespread media outrage with headlines like "the filth and the fury." This 117-second incident, despite being accidental, massively boosted punk rock's profile in the UK, causing "Anarchy in the UK" sales to jump from 10,000 copies in a week to 10,000 copies a day. "The impact of that 117 second interview was immediate." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=MTDY6qT11Dc

Rotten's "I Hate Pink Floyd" t-shirt served as an instant identifier of his anti-establishment attitude, leading to his consideration as the band's frontman. "He was immediately considered for the job of lead singer of the sex pistols, based on the fact that he was wearing an eye-hate pink Floyd t-shirt." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-CT7yYXonIk

This event, occurring 13 years and one month after The Beatles' Ed Sullivan appearance, was pivotal in kicking punk into high gear in the UK.
