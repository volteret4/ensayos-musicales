## albums
**Shout (1959)** : This album, also titled "Shout," was recorded following the success of their single, but marked a return to the "old Hugo and Luigi style." Apart from one new original, "Respectable," which became their next single, the rest of the album consisted of old standards rearranged in the "Shout" style. While sometimes this approach worked, such as on "Wring-a-Ling, Let the Wedding Bells Ring" where Ronald Isley's screaming suited the Little Richard-style lyrics, it was largely unsuccessful on other tracks. Their interpretations of songs like Irving Berlin's "How Deep Is The Ocean" or "He's Got The Whole World In His Hands" did not improve either the songs or the group. **Shout (1959)** : Originating from extended, improvised call-and-response endings to their live performances of Jackie Wilson's "Lonely Teardrops," this song gradually developed with elements from Roquel's records like "What Did I Say" and "I Got A Woman." Producers Hugo and Luigi suggested cutting out the "Lonely Teardrops" part and recording just the extended tag. The resulting song, split into "Shout Parts One and Two" across a single, became the Isley Brothers' first chart hit, reaching number 47. Credited to the three brothers, it generated significant income from numerous successful cover versions, and sold over a million copies despite not breaking the Top 40. "That was nothing like anything that Hugo and Luigi had ever produced before, and it became the Isle Brothers' first chart hit, reaching number 47." ← Episode 81： ＂Shout＂ by the Isley Brothers | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=USJDERCABjk

## curiosities
**"One-Hit Wonder" Perception** : After a year with Atlantic Records where their gospel-infused soul party songs produced by Lieber and Stoller failed to chart, the Isley Brothers left Atlantic for the tiny Scepter label. This marked a downward trajectory in label size, and despite remaining a strong live act, they appeared to be a "one-hit wonder" after "Shout." "They were still a great live act, but they appeared to be a one-hit wonder." ← Episode 81： ＂Shout＂ by the Isley Brothers | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=USJDERCABjk
**Contract Swiping from Teenage Records** : After their unsuccessful first single, the Isley Brothers wanted to leave Teenage Records, which claimed to hold them under contract. O'Kelly Isley revealed they had signed a contract, but it was left unfiled on a desk, so they "swiped it" when no one was looking, leaving Teenage Records with no proof of the agreement and freeing the brothers to sign with Gone Records. "But then the contract had just been left on a desk after they'd signed it, rather than being filed, and they'd swiped it from the desk when no one was looking." ← Episode 81： ＂Shout＂ by the Isley Brothers | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=USJDERCABjk
**Creative Differences with Hugo and Luigi on RCA** : The Isley Brothers' first single with RCA producers Hugo and Luigi, "I'm Gonna Knock on Your Door," highlighted potential creative differences. Despite powerful vocals and a forward-looking, "groundbreaking" sound that resembled early 60s pop, the song's lightweight backing and generic writing (by Elvis film songwriters) meant it didn't quite convince as either R&B or pop. **Origin of "Shout" from Live Improvisation** : The hit song "Shout" originated from increasingly long and excited improvised extended endings to their live performances of Jackie Wilson's "Lonely Teardrops." Ronald Isley would engage in call-and-response with his brothers, shouting lines and getting them to sing the response. These live tags eventually incorporated elements from Roquel's records, particularly "What Did I Say" and "I Got A Woman." "These improvised extended endings to the song got longer and longer, and got the crowds more and more excited, and they started incorporating elements from Raquel's records, especially what did I say and I got a woman." ← Episode 81： ＂Shout＂ by the Isley Brothers | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=USJDERCABjk

**Shift from Gospel to Doo-Wop** : Following Vernon's death, their parents pressured them to continue singing to honor him. A compromise was reached where Ronald agreed to attempt lead vocals if the group could transition from gospel music to doo-wop, influenced by their favorite act, Billy Ward and The Dominoes. **Shout's Impact and Cover Versions** : "Shout" was credited to the three Isley Brothers, ensuring they profited from its widespread success, particularly from numerous cover versions that charted higher than their original. Joey Dee and The Starliters reached number six in the USA in 1962, Lulu and The Lovers hit number seven in the UK in 1964, and Johnny O'Keefe's version reached number two in Australia just a month after the Isleys' release. Despite these covers, the Isleys' version sold over a million copies and remains definitive. **Tragic Death of Vernon Isley** : Vernon Isley, the youngest of the four singing brothers and widely considered the most talented singer in the group, was tragically killed at the age of 13 after being hit by a car while riding his bike. This event devastated the brothers and led them to believe their singing career was over, resulting in a two-year hiatus from live performance. "Vernon, the youngest of the four singing Iseley's, and the one who was generally considered to be far and away the most talented singer in the group, was hit by a car and killed while he was riding his bike, aged only 13." ← Episode 81： ＂Shout＂ by the Isley Brothers | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=USJDERCABjk
