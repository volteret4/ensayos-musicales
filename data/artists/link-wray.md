## songs
**Radio Ban for "Glorified Juvenile Delinquency"** : "Rumble" was banned by radio stations in New York and Boston because its gritty, street-fight-like sound was perceived as "glorified juvenile delinquency," despite being an instrumental track. Wray himself retitled the composition "Rumble" because he thought it sounded like a street fight. "He retied the composition rumble because he thought it sounded like a street fight. And it did because it was banned by radio stations in both New York and Boston because it sounded like, and this is a, quote, glorified juvenile delinquency." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YFv6Ln00XjA ← link-wray
**Rumble (1958)** : This was Link Wray's first single, released in 1958, and was a fuzzy, distorted instrumental, highly unique for its era, predating fuzz pedals. Wray achieved its "dirty and dangerous sound" by poking holes in his amplifier's speaker, after initially experimenting with a composition titled "Oddball." "In 1958, he released his first single, which was a fuzzy, distorted instrumental, very unique for its day because it was recorded years before there was such a thing as a fuzz pedal for the guitar." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YFv6Ln00XjA ← link-wray

**DIY Amplifier Distortion** : To achieve his signature savage sound, Link Wray deliberately perforated the cones of his amplifier. This was an innovative technique, as amplifiers at the time did not have built-in distortion regulation, which was not their intended purpose, leading him to create that ragged, menacing, and deeply physical tone. "Y para sonar así de salvaje, el lo que hizo fue perforar los colos de su amplificador, consiguiendo ese tono rasgado, amenazante y profundamente físico." ← El punk existía antes de Sex Pistols (y era algo salvaje) | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ilXpBujkWZE
**First Instrumental Song Censored** : "Rumble" was the first and only time an instrumental piece was censored for its symbolic content. Several radio stations in the United States banned the song, arguing that its sound, despite having no words, could incite youth violence, highlighting its perceived dangerousness. "Fue la primera y única vez que una pieza instrumental era censurada por su carga simbólica." ← El punk existía antes de Sex Pistols (y era algo salvaje) | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ilXpBujkWZE
**Influence on Guitar Legends** : His innovative guitar playing style, particularly his use of power chords, directly influenced iconic guitarists such as Jimmy Page and Pete Townshend, who picked up the guitar in part because of his sound. "Jimmy Page, Pete Townsend, guys like that picked up the guitar in part because of Link Ray." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YFv6Ln00XjA ← link-wray
**Inventor of the Power Chord** : Link Wray is credited with popularizing the idea of playing power chords on an electric guitar, a technique that would profoundly influence rock music. He is often called the "inventor of the power cord." "He has been called the inventor of the power cord, Jimmy Page, Pete Townsend, guys like that picked up the guitar in part because of Link Ray." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YFv6Ln00XjA ← link-wray
**Shawnee Heritage Obscured** : Link Wray was born in North Carolina to parents from the Shawnee tribe, making him a Native American. However, census documents of the time identified him as white, obscuring his true ethnicity. "His name was Link Ray, and he was born in North Carolina to parents from the Shawnee tribe. But that ethnicity was obscured by census documents that identified him as white, but he was definitely Native American." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YFv6Ln00XjA ← link-wray
**Speaker Cone Alteration** : In 1958, Link Wray, inspired by messing around with a damaged amplifier, started intentionally poking holes in the speaker cones of his own amp. This technique was used to achieve a raspy, loved sound for his instrumental track "Rumble." "He started poking holes in the speaker cones of his amp to make them sound all raspy." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iGav9uJwA_w ← link-wray
