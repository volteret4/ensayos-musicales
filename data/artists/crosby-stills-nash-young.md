**Ohio (1970)** : This song is considered the centerpiece of the so-called Kent State songs. Neil Young wrote it within 15 minutes at a friend's house in Utano Canyon, California, after seeing a May 15th edition of Life Magazine featuring a photograph of 14-year-old Marianne Vecchio kneeling over the body of Jeff Miller, one of the students killed at Kent State. David Crosby witnessed its creation and immediately called Graham Nash to book studio time, prioritizing its release over the band's climbing hit "Teach Your Children." "I watched him write Ohio right there." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 43 - Music and The Kent State Massacre of May 4, 1970 | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-43-music-and-the-kent-state-massacre-of-may-4-1970
**Ohio (May 1970)** : This anti-Nixon song was written by Neil Young in approximately an hour after seeing a Life magazine photo spread of the Kent State shootings. It specifically named Richard Nixon, linking him to the May 4, 1970, shooting of unarmed students by the Ohio National Guard. David Crosby, Graham Nash, and the band quickly assembled in Los Angeles to record it, and Atlantic Records head Ahmet Ertegun ensured its release within 10 days, packaged with the First Amendment text. While banned by many AM stations, it was played on FM radio, which catered to college students. "Ohio was a response to the Ohio National Guard shooting 12 unarmed students on the campus of Kensington University on May 4, 1970." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 5 - Anti-Nixon Songs of the 70s | http://www.ftr70.com

## curiosities
**"Godfathers of Soft Rock" Influence** : Crosby, Stills, Nash & Young are considered strong candidates for the "godfathers of soft rock" due to their significant influence on many mid-to-late 1970s bands that achieved hits with soft rock sounds, particularly those emphasizing vocal harmonies. "if there is such a thing as godfathers of soft rock I think you could make a very strong case for crossbeast is nation young so many of the bands of the mid to late 70s who had hits with the soft rock sound especially those who leaned into harmony credited CS and why with being a major influence." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 51 - The Softer Side of 70s Rock | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-51-the-softer-side-of-70s-rock ← crosby-stills-nash-young
**"Ohio" Single Cover Art (1970)** : The single for "Ohio" featured a striking cover photograph of the United States Constitution riddled with four bullet holes, a direct and powerful visual commentary on the Kent State shootings. "Cover was a picture of the Constitution with four bullet holes in it." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 43 - Music and The Kent State Massacre of May 4, 1970 | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-43-music-and-the-kent-state-massacre-of-may-4-1970
**David Crosby's Harmonic Genius** : David Crosby, despite his "famously difficult" reputation, was a "musical genius" responsible for arranging most, if not all, of the harmonies on *Déjà Vu*. His work in this area "plants the seeds for the soft rock boom to come." "he arranged most if not all of the harmonies on this record and in doing so he plants the seeds for the soft rock boom to come." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 51 - The Softer Side of 70s Rock | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-51-the-softer-side-of-70s-rock ← crosby-stills-nash-young
**Individual Success Before CSNY** : All four members—Neil Young, David Crosby, Stephen Stills, and Graham Nash—had already achieved success in other very successful bands before forming CSNY. They were established musicians with strong opinions, not a group of beginners. "You have to keep in mind that each one of these guys, Young Crosby, Stephen Stills, Graham Nash, they had already been part of other very successful bands." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 43 - Music and The Kent State Massacre of May 4, 1970 | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-43-music-and-the-kent-state-massacre-of-may-4-1970
**Influence on America and Misattribution** : The band America was frequently compared to CSNY, or even accused of being "CSNY knockoffs." When America's song "A Horse with No Name" was released in 1971, its sound was so similar that many listeners mistakenly believed it was a Neil Young track. **Kent State Impact on CSNY's Future (1970)** : The internal conflicts within CSNY were legendary, and the tour for their "Deja Vu" album was cut short due to these issues and technical problems at their first show in Denver on May 12, 1970, where Neil Young reportedly threw down his guitar and threatened to leave. David Crosby and Neil Young were at their road manager's house in Northern California, reassessing the band's future, when Young was inspired to write "Ohio" after seeing the Life Magazine photo. "The tour to support that record was over before it ever started. The internal conflict in CSNY is rather legendary." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 43 - Music and The Kent State Massacre of May 4, 1970 | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-43-music-and-the-kent-state-massacre-of-may-4-1970
**Misinterpretation of "Ohio" in Akron (1970)** : Johnny Andrews of WHLO in Akron, Ohio, played "Ohio" extensively, where it reached number one by mid-July. However, his interpretation was that the song was a message to protesters to "cool it for a while or get shot," which he believed was "the proper thing," completely misconstruing its anti-war and anti-violence message. "Our interpretation was that it was a rock group giving a message to protesters to cool it for a while or get shot." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 43 - Music and The Kent State Massacre of May 4, 1970 | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-43-music-and-the-kent-state-massacre-of-may-4-1970
**Prioritizing "Ohio" Release (1970)** : The president of CSNY's record company, Ahmet Ertegun (transcribed as Almitard), flew the master tape of "Ohio" on a red-eye flight from Los Angeles to New York to ensure its immediate release. This decision led to pulling "Teach Your Children," a song already climbing the charts and poised to be a number one hit, to get "Ohio" out quickly. "President of our record company, a wonderful man named Almitard, who was him Los Angeles that night when we finished recording and he took tape and flew to New York on the red eye. **Stephen Stills' Initial Hesitation with "Ohio" (1970)** : After "Ohio" was recorded, Stephen Stills expressed some reservations about the lyrics. He found it hard to believe that the National Guard would open fire with live ammunition, thinking some members might have been told they were carrying blanks.
