## albums
**In Utero (1993)** : The cover artwork for Nirvana's 1993 album *In Utero*, designed by Art Director Robert Fisher, features a winged figure on a pale, cracked background. This angelic figure is depicted as ripped open, exposing bare bones and organs, reflecting Kurt Cobain's fascination with human anatomy as art, creating an image that is both peaceful and disturbing. The figure is actually a "transparent anatomical mannequin," a life-sized, anatomically correct female model used for teaching anatomy, which was found at a medical supply store in the Mall of America in Minneapolis. Nirvana used several such mannequins as stage props for the *In Utero* tour. The back cover, photographed by Charles Peterson, displays various items from the medical store and Kurt's personal collection, including plastic fetuses, body parts, a turtle shell, orchids, and lilies, all laid out on his living room floor. These items incorporate symbols from *The Woman's Dictionary of Symbols and Sacred Objects*, aligning with Kurt's interest in artwork representing themes of sex, women, birth, and death, which also hints at the raw music within the album. "The angelic figure is ripped open. We see bare bones and exposed organs, but not to Kurt s fascination with human anatomy as art. It's both peaceful and disturbing." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sn5TWBFd0XM
**Nevermind (1991) – Industry-Changing Release** : Released on September 24th, 1991, initial shipments totaled 46,251 copies, with industry expectations of only 50,000 sales if lucky. This album arrived during a stagnant period for rock music, dominated by classic rock, unamusing hair metal power ballads, and "crappy phony pop stars." It resonated deeply with Generation Xers who were seeking an emotional voice amidst recession and the Gulf War, providing them with music that spoke to their anger and fear. *Nevermind* swiftly became a monumental success, changing the entire rock landscape and shifting alternative music into the mainstream. "Never mind was one of those records that changed everything." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=MTDY6qT11Dc
**Nevermind (1991) – Rock's Resurrection** : Released in the fall of 1991, this album built bridges and "resurrected rock and roll in the image of Generation X," marking a significant turning point where rock began to dominate pop music. "The bridges built by Nirvana's Nevermind album resurrected rock and roll in the image of Generation X." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=MK7L0eCpDHc

## songs
**About A Girl (1989)** : Kurt Cobain's guitar parts on Nirvana's "About a Girl" were played through a silver face Fender Twin Reverb amplifier. This use contributed to the album's distinct sound and showcased the amp's clean qualities, even in the grunge era. "Nirvana and about a girl with Kurt playing his parts through a silver face Fender Twin Reverb." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Y0HW8LQKvJw
**First Single (1988)** : The authentic first single by Nirvana, released in October 1988 on Sub Pop Records, can be verified by a specific handwritten message in the dead wax area. This message, in two-millimeter high letters, reads "SP-23-1. Why don't you trade those guitars for shovels? L-31540." For additional proof of authenticity, holding the record up to the light should reveal the faintly machine-stamped word "K-Disk" on both sides. "And the way you can tell if you have an authentic copy of the very first Nirvana single, issued on subpop records in October of 1988, is to look for a message handwritten in letters two millimeters high." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IIJySSo276k

## curiosities
**"In Utero" Title Evolution** : Kurt Cobain initially wanted to title Nirvana's 1993 album "I Hate Myself and Want to Die," claiming it was a joke. However, the title was ultimately changed to *In Utero* due to concerns that the original title could encourage dangerous behavior and potentially lead to lawsuits. Another rejected title was "Verse Chorus Verse," which was also the original name of the song later known as "Sappy." The final title, *In Utero*, was derived from a poem written by Courtney Love. "In the end, the title In Yudero was chosen because everyone realized that I hate myself and I want to die could encourage people to do some dangerous things and perhaps result in some lawsuits." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sn5TWBFd0XM
