**Love to Love You Baby (1975) - Queen of Disco's Erotic Breakthrough** : This song established Donna Summer as the "queen of Disco" and became renowned for its explicit pushing of boundaries in female sexual expression. Initially 3 minutes and 50 seconds, Neil Bogart requested a longer version after witnessing its impact at a party, leading to the iconic 17-minute track. Donna Summer reportedly recorded the song's sensual vocal moans and breathing in a dark booth, lying on the floor, due to her self-consciousness. Bogart himself noted its appeal as music "for people who just want to sit and relax and make love." "The song that made her the queen is love to love you, baby." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 4 - Music of the 70s (Sexual) Revolution | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-4-talkin-bout-a-sexual-revolution

## curiosities
**"Love to Love You Baby" Recording Anecdote** : During the recording of "Love to Love You Baby," Donna Summer reportedly went into a dark booth by herself and lay on the floor to record the song's famously sensual vocalizations. This was due to her extreme self-consciousness about the sounds she was making, which sounded "like she was doing" something explicitly sexual. "The story goes that when Donna recorded this part of the song, she had to go into a booth by herself and the dark." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 4 - Music of the 70s (Sexual) Revolution | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-4-talkin-bout-a-sexual-revolution
**European Stardom Preceded US Success** : Donna Summer achieved stardom in Europe before she became well-known in the United States, living there and working in stage productions and as a session singer. "Donna Summer was a star in Europe before she was in the United States." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 1 - Disco Doesn't Suck (Encore) | https://www.ftr70.com/blog/tag/70s+podcast
**Neil Bogart's Vision for "Love to Love You Baby"** : Neil Bogart, the founder of Casablanca Records, saw a gap in the music market for a lengthy song that would allow people to "sit and relax and make love" without interruption. He believed that conventional four-minute songs broke the mood, and he specifically envisioned "Love to Love You Baby" to fill this niche, leading to its extended 17-minute version. "But there was no music for people who just want to sit and relax and make love, you know?" ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 4 - Music of the 70s (Sexual) Revolution | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-4-talkin-bout-a-sexual-revolution
**Only American Bandstand Co-Host** : Donna Summer holds the unique distinction of being the only person to ever co-host American Bandstand alongside Dick Clark. Her co-hosting engagement was likely a promotional strategy for her movie, "Thank God It's Friday," which premiered at the peak of the disco era. "there was only ever one other person to co-host the show with Dick Clark and that person was the 70s icon Donna Summer." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 47 - Dick Clark's Entertainment Empire | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Queen of Disco Title and Renunciation** : Donna Summer earned the title "queen of Disco" largely due to the success of "Love to Love You Baby." However, she later renounced this title, causing disappointment among her fanbase. "And Donna Summer was the queen of Disco, although she did renounce that title later in her life, much to the disappointment of her fans." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 4 - Music of the 70s (Sexual) Revolution | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-4-talkin-bout-a-sexual-revolution
**Role in "Thank God It's Friday" (1978)** : In the 1978 movie "Thank God It's Friday," Donna Summer played Nicole, a singer who strives to convince a disco DJ to play her record. Although the movie itself was not a major success, her song "Last Dance" from the film became a massive hit. "Donna Summer plays a singer named Nicole who was trying to get a disco DJ to play her record." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 47 - Dick Clark's Entertainment Empire | https://www.ftr70.com/
