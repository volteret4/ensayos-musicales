**Ohio (1970) – Kent State Protest Song** : Written in May 1970, this song was a direct response to the outrage felt after four protesting students were tragically killed by National Guard troops at Kent State University. The speed of its creation and release was remarkable, as it was on sale and on the radio within a month of the incident. Lyrics include: "Ten soldiers and Nixon coming, we're finally on our own. This summer I hear the drumming for dead in Ohio. Gotta get down to the rain." "Neil Young was so outraged that he wrote the song Ohio, and it was on sale and on the radio within a month." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0ARYt4sYT90 ← neil-young
**Ohio (May 1970)** : Neil Young wrote "Ohio" in about an hour after David Crosby showed him a photo spread of the Kent State shootings in Life magazine. The song directly blamed Richard Nixon for the May 4, 1970 shooting of 12 unarmed students by the Ohio National Guard, a response to Nixon's April 30, 1970 announcement of a preemptive strike in Cambodia. David Crosby called it "the bravest thing he had seen in music," and the band (CSNY) assembled in Los Angeles to record it. "Ten soldiers and Nixon's coming were finally on our own this summer I hear the drummen for Dead in Ohio." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 5 - Anti-Nixon Songs of the 70s | http://www.ftr70.com
**Only Love Can Break Your Heart** : This song was included in a Spotify playlist created by Queen Mary University in London, which listed 700 songs most likely to induce chills or goosebumps in people. The research indicated that sadder, slower, less intense, and more instrumental songs generally proved most effective in eliciting this physical response. "This playlist included Only Love Can Break Your Heart by Neil Young." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mTbagQE7akQ ← neil-young
**Southern Man (1970)** : Released in 1970, this song directly called out the American South for its racism, history of slavery, and the use of religion (specifically the Bible, referred to as "your good book") as a justification for these practices. The lyrics included "Don't forget what your good book says / Southern change gonna come at last / Now your Southern man / Will you know when freedom comes? / Well I heard screaming and the whole world is clapping and I don't know." The song was highly upsetting to many white people in the South, who felt it painted them in broad, racist strokes. Years later, Young himself reflected on the song in his biography, stating he deserved to be called out for it. "Young called out the South for its racism its history of slavery and he also calls out their use of religion. The Bible and religion was used as a justification for slavery and he uses it here in Southern man he says don't forget about your Bible he specifically adds your when he says your good book which of course is the Bible." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 3 - Nostalgia, Race, and Rebels in 70s Southern Rock | https://seventies.libsyn.com/03-bittersweet-home-alabama
**Southern Man (Fall 1970)** : Released in the fall of 1970, this song directly challenged the South's history of racism and slavery, with lyrics urging listeners to "not forget about your Bible or your good book." The song provoked anger among many white southerners who felt it broadly condemned the entire white population of the South. Years later, in his 2012 biography, Young reflected on the song, admitting that he disliked his own words, finding them "accusatory and condescending." "Southern man released in the fall of nineteen seventy. This song was upsetting to many white southerners." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 3 - Nostalgia, Race, and Rebels in 70s Southern Rock (Encore) | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-3-nostalgia-race-and-rebels-in-70s-southern-rock-encore
