## curiosities
**"It's All Done with Mirrors" Philosophy** : Mike Levine articulated a philosophy that perception was crucial in the music industry, stating, "It's all done with mirrors." He elaborated that people were often more impressed by what they heard (or perceived) than by what they actually saw. "Levine said it was a big deal when we came back to Canada record companies in the US started paying attention to us. It's all done with mirrors. People are far more impressed with what they hear than with what they see." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 45 - Oh, Canada! Canada's Rock Music Industry in the 70s | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Beneficiary of FM Radio Rules** : Triumph was a band that benefited from Canada's changed FM radio rules in the 1960s, which required stations to play more album-oriented rock and a 30% Canadian content quota. This provided more airplay opportunities for established Canadian bands. "This helped out bands like Rush Triumph in April wine." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6PGgNCaW4xo ← triumph
**Cancon Boosted Success (1970s)** : Triumph was among the many homegrown rock talents whose careers were boosted by the Cancon rules in the 1970s, contributing to Canadian success stories and, in some instances, international success. "Just on the rock side, we had Rush, April Wine, the Guess Who, Triumph, Crobar, Teenage Head, Max Webster, Chilowac, FM, Saga, and a foot-and-cold water." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U2kYleeMnl8 ← triumph
**Commercial Success Despite Critical Failure** : Triumph achieved significant commercial success, selling millions of records, cassettes, CDs, and concert tickets, largely to a demographic of "mostly straight white male dudes who gravitated to arena rock." This success came despite a general lack of critical acclaim. "the mostly straight white male dudes who gravitated to arena rock bought their records and cassettes and CDs and concert tickets by the millions and made triumphant others like them a success even if they were not critically successful." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 45 - Oh, Canada! Canada's Rock Music Industry in the 70s | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Critical Reception** : Initially, critics struggled to identify unique qualities in Triumph, often finding little to distinguish them from other rock or hard rock bands. "critics really couldn't find much that distinguished triumphant from any other rock band or hard rock band out there." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 45 - Oh, Canada! **Derivative Sound Accusation** : Triumph's sound was frequently criticized and dismissed as derivative, to which the band countered by asserting that "everything is derivative at least in rock," suggesting that originality within the genre was inherently limited. "Triumphs sound was dismissed as derivative to which the band said yes, it is everything is derivative at least in rock" ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 45 - Oh, Canada! **Early Success and Domestic Acceptance** : Triumph had success as far back as the 1960s, but like many Canadian groups, domestic acceptance and popularity only came after they had a hit in America. This pattern highlights the historical bias where external validation was crucial for Canadian artists at home. "The guests who BTO Rush, Triumph, April Wine and others had success as far back as the 1960s. But in most cases, domestic acceptance and popularity only came after these groups had a hit in America." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6PGgNCaW4xo ← triumph
**Emphasis on Accessibility** : According to Rick Emmett, Triumph consistently aimed for accessibility in their music, a contrast to Rush, intending to offer "loud arena rock that you could sink your teeth into" for audiences who preferred music that didn't require deep intellectual engagement. "Emmett said that triumphant was always trying to be accessible in a way that rush was not." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 45 - Oh, Canada!
