## albums
**Al Capone (1967) – First Wave Ska; Prince Buster Shifted the Beat to Create Ska** : Cecil Bustamante Campbell — known by his stage name Prince Buster — was a pivotal figure in the creation of ska. Together with guitarist Jerry Johnson, he moved the beat emphasis from the 1 and 3 to the 2 and 4, stressing the offbeat. This change in syncopation, made in 1962, was the spark that created ska as a distinct genre. Prince Buster was also the source of the name for Madness — their name came from an old Prince Buster song. "Cecil moved the beat emphasis from the 1 and the 3 to the 2 and the 4. In other words, the off beat was stressed. This change in syncopation was the spark and SCA was born." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VxWp7HrT8oE ← prince-buster

## curiosities
**Assistant to Coxsone Dodd** : Prince Buster initially worked for Coxsone Dodd as an assistant, running one of Dodd's many sound systems. He later became a significant figure in his own right. "Like Prince Buster and Lee Scratch Perry." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**Black Nationalist and Rastafari Adherent** : Prince Buster was a staunch black nationalist and a follower of the Rastafari religion. This conviction fueled his desire to create a purely Jamaican music, distinguishing him from rivals like Dodd and Reid who viewed their productions as R&B substitutes. "Vince Buster, on the other hand, by this point was a full-fledged black nationalist and wanted to make a purely Jamaican music. Buster was, in particular, an adherent of the Rastafar Avalidium." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**Death (September 9, 2016)** : Prince Buster, a renowned pioneer of ska music, died on September 9, 2016, due to a stroke. "September 9th, Prince Buster, a great scop pioneer, a stroke." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sOU54d8mr5k ← prince-buster
**Early Practitioner of Ska** : Prince Buster is identified as one of the early practitioners of ska, a local sound originating in Jamaica. His cover of a 1949 song in 1963 demonstrates his involvement in the early development and popularization of the genre. "In 1963, it was covered by Prince Buster, one of the early practitioners of a local sound called ska." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zNIVqKqAlnk ← prince-buster
**Establishing Own Sound System** : Eventually, Prince Buster left Coxsone Dodd to set up his own rival sound system, escalating the soundman rivalry into a three-way competition in Kingston. "Eventually Prince Buster left Dodd and set up his own rival sound system, at which point the rivalry became a three way one." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**Incorporating Rasta Rhythms into Ska** : For his single "O'Carolina," Prince Buster brought in five drummers from the Rasta Nyabinghi tradition, including the influential Count Ossie. This marked the first time Rasta's sacred music rhythms were integrated into Jamaican R&B, contributing to Ska's development. "he brought in five drummers from the Rasta Niabinghi tradition, most notably Count Osie, who became the single most influential drummer in Jamaica, to record on the folks for the single O'Carolina, incorporating the rhythms of Rasta's sacred music into Jamaican R&B for the first time." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**Patois Quote on Dodd's Reaction** : Prince Buster famously described Coxsone Dodd's psychological collapse when Duke Reid played "Gator" by Willis Gatortail Jackson, using Jamaican Patois to convey the moment. The quote illustrates the intensity of the soundman rivalry. "The psychological impact had knocked him out, nobody never hit him." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**R&B Espionage for Dodd** : Prince Buster possessed a deep knowledge of R&B, enabling him to attend Duke Reid's shows, identify the production styles of different R&B labels, and use lyrics to deduce the titles and labels of Reed's exclusive records. This information was then used by Dodd to obtain copies and undermine Reid's competitive edge. "one important one being that he knew enough about R&B, that he could go to Duke Reed's shows, listen to the records he was playing, and figure out what they must be." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**Showmanship Prowess** : In the competitive sound system scene, Prince Buster was recognized as the best showman. He understood what his audience desired and expertly delivered it, making his shows immensely popular. "Prince Buster was the best showman, who knew what the people wanted and gave it to them." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**Ska Giant and Saxa's Collaborator** : Prince Buster is recognized as a "Ska Giant" with whom The English Beat's saxophonist, Saxa, had previously played in his early career. "saxo had played with Scott Giants like Prince Buster and Desmond Decker in the early days, so this guy really knew what he was doing." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PdCAjFW04Oc ← prince-buster
