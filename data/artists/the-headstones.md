## albums
**People Skills (2019) – Current Album** : Released in 2019, this album features the song "Leave It All Behind." It represents the band's current approach to recording, where they control every aspect of the creative process, leading to records they feel genuinely embody the band. "The headstones from the 2019 album People Skills and that's called Leave It All Behind." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=y7kmkWJ54ww
**Picture of Health (1993) – Platinum Debut Album** : Released in July 1993, this debut album was a surprise platinum success immediately after its release, despite the band feeling "totally green" and overwhelmed by the studio process. It compiled songs developed over their early years, written with live performance in mind, and included singles like "Cemetery" and "Tweeter and the Monkey Man." "The headstones with Cemetery from the 1993 debut album, Picture of Health." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=y7kmkWJ54ww
**Smile and Wave (1996) – Third Album** : Released in 1996, this album is noted for containing timeless tracks that remain in the band's setlist today. It was also the source of controversy when an unedited version of the song "Cubically Contained," containing clear profanity, was played on the radio during the afternoon, leading to complaints to the Canadian Broadcast Standards Council. "The headstones with Smile and Way from 1996." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=y7kmkWJ54ww
**Teeth and Tissue (1995) – Second Album** : Released in March 1995, this album followed the extensive touring for "Picture of Health," with the band playing 120-160 shows a year. The songs were often written "furious" on the road, contributing to what the band describes as "second album syndrome," where tracks might not have been fully formed due to lack of time and breathing room. "The headstones when their second album Teeth and Tissue, which appeared in March of 1995." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=y7kmkWJ54ww

## curiosities
**"Cubically Contained" Radio Censorship Incident (1996)** : An incident occurred in 1996 when an unedited version of the song "Cubically Contained" from the "Smile and Wave" album was broadcast on radio at 5:30 PM, containing explicit profanity. This led to significant repercussions for the program director who played it and generated complaints to the Canadian Broadcast Standards Council. "I played an undated adversion of Cubically Contain on the radio." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=y7kmkWJ54ww
**Artistic Control in Modern Recordings** : Having learned from past experiences where they questioned management and production decisions, The Headstones now exert full control over their recording process. This autonomy is seen as crucial, with the band believing their newer records, made under their complete direction, are superior and more authentically represent their sound. "That's why now when we make the records, we control everything. And these records to me are the band." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=y7kmkWJ54ww
**Discovery and Signing with MCA** : The Headstones were discovered by Cam Carpenter and Tony Wanamaker, who secured them a showcase at the X-ray and Ultra Sound Club. A pivotal moment was the unexpected appearance and endorsement of Gord Downie and Paul Langlois from The Tragically Hip (who were also signed to MCA), which swayed MCA to sign the Headstones despite initial concerns about their "dangerous" image. "I remember when MCA and Cam Carpenter, you know, signed us at ultrasound, they were like, yeah, is it they're a little dangerous yet." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=y7kmkWJ54ww
**Early Reputation and Sound** : The Headstones were once known as the "scariest band in Canadian music," described as intense, self-destructive, and high-energy. The band's singer, Hugh Dillon, stated that the group's preferred self-description was "furious," highlighting their unique, unpredictable, and ferocious playing style that he likened to a high-energy hockey team. "Once upon a time, the headstones were the scariest band in Canadian music." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=y7kmkWJ54ww
**Intense Touring and "Second Album Syndrome"** : Following their debut, The Headstones maintained a grueling touring schedule, playing between 120 and 160 shows a year during the 1990s. This relentless pace led to the "second album syndrome" for "Teeth and Tissue," as songs were often written and recorded hastily on the road, with less time for full development. "I think we've been on the road so much for that pre that record picture of health. And then after it, how many days, I was just, they were doing like 120 shows a year or something like that during the whole 90s, 160 the first, the first year." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=y7kmkWJ54ww
**Kingston Origins and Early Formation** : The core members originated from Kingston, where they were part of a vibrant music scene that also produced The Tragically Hip. The band's genesis involved a group called "Sean Penn Atenshry" in the mid-1980s, which included Hugh Dillon, Randy Kwan, Mark Gibson, and Frontini, before Trent Carr joined and transformed their sound, leading to The Headstones' formation. "So how the headstones come out of Kingston? What year are we talking about? 89? No, you came from Kingston earlier than that. It was like 80s." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=y7kmkWJ54ww
**Live-Centric Songwriting Approach** : The band's songwriting philosophy prioritizes live performance, ensuring that songs can "stand up live" and genuinely resonate with an audience. This focus dictates what makes it into their setlists and onto new albums, as they believe insincerity would be detected by the crowd or fellow band members. "The songs had in my mind had to stand up live. People had to be able to, you could tell if they uncross their own." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=y7kmkWJ54ww
**Platinum Debut and Studio Challenges** : Their debut album, "Picture of Health," achieved platinum status instantly, which was a significant surprise for the band members. They admitted to feeling "totally green" and overwhelmed in the studio environment, and later questioned some of the choices made by producers and managers at that time. "Again, platinum out of the gate. Was that a surprise to you or did you? Oh, very much so a surprise." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=y7kmkWJ54ww
**Subconscious Noodling Songwriting Method** : The band's songwriting often begins with Trent Carr or Tim White "noodling" subconsciously during live performances or jams. Hugh Dillon actively listens for these spontaneous musical ideas, isolating specific notes or riffs that he finds unique and then developing them into full songs, rather than deliberately trying to write "hits." "I'll hear them subconsciously noodle. And that's when I stop everybody and what's that?" ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=y7kmkWJ54ww
**Unique Band Dynamic and Talent Respect** : The members describe their interpersonal dynamic as jokingly having "no respect" for each other as friends. However, they express profound admiration and shock when they witness each other perform their craft, describing it as a "magic trick" that transforms their perception of their bandmates from mere friends to incredible musicians. "It's funny because I have no respect for you as my friend. You do this magic trick that all of a sudden, whoever that guy, that guy I have all the respect for." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=y7kmkWJ54ww
