## albums
**Music for the Masses (1987)** : Depeche Mode's 1987 album cover features a beige background with the band's logo and album title in stark block letters. The central image depicts three red megaphones mounted on a pole, set in an isolated location without an audience, literally "in the middle of nowhere" in England's Peak District. This concept was developed by long-time collaborator Martin Atkins, who favored megaphones and the idea of speakers in an unconventional setting, disconnected from actual "masses." The band and label embraced the concept, using megaphones throughout the album's marketing. "The image features three red megaphones, mounted on a pole, ready to broadcast to, well, apparently no one." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sn5TWBFd0XM
**Violator (1990) – Landmark Album** : Released "on the eve" of the band's infamous Los Angeles autograph session riot on March 20, 1990, this album became a "giant record" and coincided with the rise of rock's fourth cycle. "The violator album which of course became this giant record." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=MK7L0eCpDHc
**Violator (early 1990s)** : In the early 1990s, while signed to the independent Mute Records, Depeche Mode sold millions of copies of their album *Violator*, cementing their status as a major indie force globally. "So that means in the early 1990s when Depeche Mode was selling millions of copies of its violator album, they were in fact an Indie Band." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sCtQqVBtCaI

**Los Angeles Autograph Session Riot (1990)** : On March 20, 1990, a riot at an autograph session in Los Angeles marked the first indication that a fourth rock cycle was beginning. This event occurred 13 years and 10 days after the Sex Pistols' contract signing and 26 years after The Beatles' Ed Sullivan appearance. "The first indication that a fourth cycle was starting was at a riot at a de Pesh mode autograph session in Los Angeles on March 20th of 1990." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=MK7L0eCpDHc
**Rejected "Music for the Masses" Cover** : An alternative cover for Depeche Mode's *Music for the Masses* was created, featuring a stylized megaphone in a white and orange motif with drawn sound waves. Approximately 135 copies were prepared for distribution to stores before the band and label had a change of heart, recalling and destroying nearly all of them. About a dozen copies remained with retailers. In 2011, keyboardist Alan Wilder auctioned off his copy, fetching around $4,000, and it is now considered extremely rare, with Discogs reporting no sales of this version. "About 135 copies were prepared for shipping to stores, but then the band and the label had a change of heart and had all those copies recalled." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sn5TWBFd0XM
