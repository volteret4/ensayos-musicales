**One by One (Year Not Specified) - First Proper Album** : This was the group's first album recorded with the intention of being an LP, rather than a compilation of singles. It featured the group backed by an orchestra, singing old standards, with each song having a single lead vocalist and the others providing backing vocals. Carl Gardner took the lead on four songs, hoping it would be his chance to transition into a solo singing career, but the album was not commercially or critically successful. Cornell Gunter, who had always been relegated to backing roles, shone with his imitation of Dinah Washington's style on three solo lead songs, despite his sweeter voice not typically suiting the group's famous material. "The album wasn't a particular success, either commercially or critically, but to the extent that anyone noticed it at all, they mostly commented on how good Cornell Gunter sounded." ← Episode 68： ＂Yakety Yak＂ by the Coasters | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dtMHDD0-oOo

## curiosities
**Audience Segmentation** : The Coasters had two distinct audiences: an R&B audience, largely composed of older black people, and a white teenage audience. Different songs appealed more to each group, highlighting a distinction in how black performers' music was received when singing for a black audience versus a white one. "I think it makes a difference, as well, that the coasters had two different audiences. They had an R&B audience, who were mostly older black people, and they had a white teenage audience." ← Episode 68： ＂Yakety Yak＂ by the Coasters | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dtMHDD0-oOo
**Classic Lineup Formed (1957)** : The Coasters' classic lineup, featuring Cornel Gunter and Doug Jones, who replaced Leon Hughes and Bobby Nun, finally fell into place. This formation occurred after a year during which the group had not had a hit, as their writing and production team, Jerry Lieber and Mike Stoller, were focused on more lucrative work with artists like Elvis Presley. "The classic lineup of the coasters had finally fallen into place, but it had been a year since they had had a hit." ← Episode 68： ＂Yakety Yak＂ by the Coasters | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dtMHDD0-oOo
**Commercial Decline and Production Changes** : Following the attempt to transition to the adult market and the album "One by One" failing to garner success, The Coasters' commercial fortunes entered a terminal decline. During this period, Lieber and Stoller ceased to be the exclusive producers for the group; several recordings were instead credited to Sill and Hazelwood, though these efforts also failed to achieve significant commercial success. "By this time, the group's commercial fortunes seemed to be in terminal decline." ← Episode 68： ＂Yakety Yak＂ by the Coasters | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dtMHDD0-oOo
**Desperate Flailing for New Styles** : Following a decline in chart success and a shift away from teenage-oriented material, The Coasters entered a period described as "desperate flailing about to try new styles." This included releasing an old standard, "Bessermé Moucho," a single written by a group member, Billy Guy's "Wake me Shake Me," and attempting yet another style with "Shopping for Clothes." "There seems to have been a sort of desperate flailing about to try new styles." ← Episode 68： ＂Yakety Yak＂ by the Coasters | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dtMHDD0-oOo
**Legacy as Comedy Group** : The Coasters were often dismissed as merely a "comedy group," yet their unique ability to create music that was both funny and musically worthwhile is considered one of the hardest achievements in the industry. Their comedy music has remained enjoyable for over 60 years, a feat that no other rock and roll act has replicated. "They were often dismissed as just a comedy group but as anyone who has ever tried it knows making music that is both funny and musically worthwhile is one of the hardest things you can do." ← Episode 68： ＂Yakety Yak＂ by the Coasters | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dtMHDD0-oOo

**Lieber & Stoller's Direction** : From the beginning of Lieber and Stoller's career, Jerry Lieber consistently directed the lead vocalist on how to sing his lines. This approach was applied to all artists they produced, including Elvis, Ruth Brown, and The Coasters, requiring them to follow Lieber's phrasing as closely as possible, a method that had previously annoyed Big Mama Thornton with "Hound Dog." "When Lieber and Stoller produced an artist, whether it was Elvis or Ruth Brown or the coasters or whoever, they would get them to follow Lieber's phrasing as closely as possible." ← Episode 68： ＂Yakety Yak＂ by the Coasters | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dtMHDD0-oOo
**Manager Lester Sill Mentors Phil Spector** : Lester Sill, who was Lieber and Stoller's mentor in their early years and The Coasters' manager, introduced a young musician named Phil Spector to the songwriting and production duo. Spector, an admirer of Lieber and Stoller who had a hit with "To Know Him Is To Love Him" with his group The Teddy Bears, was seeking an apprenticeship. As a favor to Sill, Lieber and Stoller signed Spector to a publishing contract, gave him jobs as a session guitarist, and allowed him to sleep in their office or Lieber's spare room. "Lester Siller said that the kid in question was a huge admirer of Leibor and Stuller, and wanted to learn from them." ← Episode 68： ＂Yakety Yak＂ by the Coasters | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dtMHDD0-oOo
**Post-Hit Lineup Instability and Rival Groups** : After their last significant hit, "Little Egypt," Cornel Gunter left to sing with Dinah Washington, later forming his own "Coasters" group which, despite not being allowed to record under that name, toured extensively with the support of Dick Clark. Billy Guy also pursued a solo career, often sending a replacement on the road while still recording with the main group, and his solo records sometimes were reissued under The Coasters' name without other members' involvement. Over decades, nearly everyone involved with The Coasters started rival versions of the group, with Carl Gardner retaining legal rights to the name, but others performing as "Cornel Gunter's Coasters" or "Leon Hughes and his Coasters." "Over the next few decades, pretty much everyone who'd been involved with The Coasters started up their own rival version of the group." ← Episode 68： ＂Yakety Yak＂ by the Coasters | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dtMHDD0-oOo
**Racist Stereotypes Criticism** : The Coasters' records faced criticism, notably from Johnny Otis and Charlie Gillett, for allegedly perpetuating racist stereotypes. Otis, who had personal issues with Lieber and Stoller regarding "Hound Dog" credits, specifically singled out "Charlie Brown." The group's performances, particularly Dub Jones' vocal parts, were clearly rooted in traditions of African American comedy that reappropriated stereotypes, but the controversy arose because the black performers were directed by two white writers, Lieber and Stoller, on how to perform these songs. "Johnny Otis in particular would make this criticism of the group's records, and it's one that must be taken seriously." ← Episode 68： ＂Yakety Yak＂ by the Coasters | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dtMHDD0-oOo
**Shift from Teenage Focus** : After "Poison Ivy," The Coasters' last Top 10 hit, Jerry Lieber and Mike Stoller seemed to grow bored of writing songs aimed at a teenage audience. Subsequently, most of the group's songs reverted to an older style, similar to that used with The Robins, focusing on social commentary and more adult topics. "Poison Ivy would be the group's last top ten hit, and it seemed to signal Lee Brunstoller getting bored with writing songs aimed at an audience of teenagers." ← Episode 68： ＂Yakety Yak＂ by the Coasters | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dtMHDD0-oOo
**Transition to Adult Market Attempt** : Around the release of "Shopping for Clothes," The Coasters became aware that their time as a pop chart act with a teenage fan base was ending. Carl Gardner, who had long wished to be a big band singer, pushed the group to attempt a transition into the adult white market, targeting audiences who bought records by artists like Tony Bennett or Andy Williams, a move encouraged by his friend Johnny Otis. "Around the time that shopping for clothes came out, the group became conscious that their time as a pop chart act with a teenage fan base was probably close to its end, and they decided to do something that Carl Gardner had wanted to do for a while, and tried to transition into the adult white market." ← Episode 68： ＂Yakety Yak＂ by the Coasters | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dtMHDD0-oOo
**Violent Deaths of Associates** : Several individuals associated with The Coasters met violent ends. King Curtis was stabbed to death in the street in 1971 at age 37 during an altercation outside his apartment building when he asked two people engaged in a drug deal to move so he could carry in an air conditioning unit. In 1980, one of Cornel Gunter's Coasters was murdered by Gunter's manager after threatening to expose the manager's criminal activities. Finally, Cornel Gunter himself was shot dead in 1990, with his killer never found. "Sadly, several people associated with the coasters ended up dying violently." ← Episode 68： ＂Yakety Yak＂ by the Coasters | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dtMHDD0-oOo
