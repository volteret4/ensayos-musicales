**Saturday Night (1976)** : This song perfectly encapsulated the bubblegum formula, particularly with its distinctive chant ("S-A-T-U-R-D-A-Y night!"). Recorded in 1973, it was not released in the United States until the summer of 1975, eventually hitting number one on the Billboard Hot 100 in January 1976. The song's chant served as the inspiration for The Ramones' "Hey Ho, Let's Go" in "Blitzkrieg Bop." "Saturday night hit number one on the Billboard Hot 100 in January 1976." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 30 - The Sweet Sound of 70s Bubblegum Music | https://seventies.libsyn.com/030-bubblegum

## curiosities
**Briefly Labeled "Punk" (Mid-1970s)** : In the mid-1970s, as the term "punk rock" was being used carelessly, even the Bay City Rollers were, at one point, called "punks." "Even the Bay City Rollers were called punks." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VYi3r0G-ne4
**Mid-70s Boy Band Equivalent** : The Bay City Rollers are described as the "mid-70s equivalent of a modern boy band like One Direction," highlighting their pop appeal and mass fan base. "These are the Basidy Rollers, the mid-70s equivalent of a modern boy band like One Direction." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=j8uk7BnbXwU
**Simplistic Pop Appeal (Early 1970s)** : Represented the "simplistic pop" that entranced the generation following the hippies, much to the dismay of older music fans. "The generation following them seemed to be totally entranced by simplistic pop made by David Cassidy and Bobby Sherman and the Bay City Rollers." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=MK7L0eCpDHc
**Band Name Origin** : The Bay City Rollers randomly chose their name from a town in Michigan, believing an American name sounded "cool." Previously, they had been known as The Saxons. "They took their name from a town in Michigan at random because an American name sounded cool they had been known as the Saxons." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 30 - The Sweet Sound of 70s Bubblegum Music | https://seventies.libsyn.com/030-bubblegum
**Discrepancy Between Image and Reality** : The band's wholesome image was a stark contrast to their behind-the-scenes behavior, which included drug use and sex. While such behavior might be expected of rock bands like Led Zeppelin or The Who, it was not accepted for a famous boy band, where image was paramount. Les McKeown commented on the "shattered illusion" of his "milk drinking clean living virgin" persona. "Les McHughan said in the 70s I was the milk drinking clean living virgin that the Bay City Rollers manager made me when that illusion was shattered. I was the drug crazed hell raising fallen idle that the press made me..." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 30 - The Sweet Sound of 70s Bubblegum Music | https://seventies.libsyn.com/030-bubblegum
**Fan Defense After Poor Performance** : Following a poorly reviewed concert in Ottawa in August 1976, where the band mixed up songs, young fans deluged the *Ottawa Citizen* with letters to the editor, defending the band by attributing their mistakes to nervousness and excitement. "The review of the show was not kind this unleashed a torrent of letters to the editor of the Ottawa citizen from their young fans. One wrote that she is sure that the boys were just a little bit nervous and excited." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 30 - The Sweet Sound of 70s Bubblegum Music | https://seventies.libsyn.com/030-bubblegum
**Fan Loyalty Amidst Scandals** : Despite revelations that the band members smoked, did not drink milk, and one even hit an old woman with his car, fans' loyalty to the Bay City Rollers remained undiminished. "And even when learning that the Bay City Rollers they did not really drink milk and they did in fact smoke and one of them hit an old woman with his car. None of that really seemed to dampen the fans loyalty to the band." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 30 - The Sweet Sound of 70s Bubblegum Music | https://seventies.libsyn.com/030-bubblegum
**Managerial Image Control and Abuse** : Manager Tam Paton meticulously crafted the band's image as "clean living Scottish boys" in calf-length pants and tartan scarves to appeal to young teenage girls. He maintained strict control over this image, even firing band members as he saw fit. Paton also reportedly fed the boys speed, stole their money, and faced disturbing allegations of attempted rape and later went to prison for molesting other underage boys. "Their manager tampatin invented stories about them being these clean living Scottish boys and calf like pants and tartan scarves to make them appealing to the 13 year old to bottle the teen idle magazines and skated to their songs at the roller ring. He created their image and he kept a very tight control of it firing members of the band whenever it suited him." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 30 - The Sweet Sound of 70s Bubblegum Music | https://seventies.libsyn.com/030-bubblegum
**Unprecedented Fan Hysteria** : In 1977, at the height of their popularity, rock journalist Lisa Robinson noted that the level of hysteria generated by the Bay City Rollers was surprising and unprecedented, surpassing even that of The Osmonds or David Cassidy, despite her belief that Americans were too sophisticated for such a "boy band." "The level of hysteria generated by them was surprising to her as she thought that Americans were too sophisticated as she put it to be taken in by a boy band like that." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 30 - The Sweet Sound of 70s Bubblegum Music | https://seventies.libsyn.com/030-bubblegum
