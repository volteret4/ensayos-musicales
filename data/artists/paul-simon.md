## curiosities
**"Gamble" Songwriting (1972)** : In a 1972 interview with Rolling Stone, Paul Simon characterized "Mother and Child Reunion" as a "gamble," explaining that it represented a style of music he felt unable to create during his partnership with Art Garfunkel. "Simon told John Landau in an interview for Rolling Stone in 1972 that Mother and Child Reunion was the type of song that he could not do when he was teamed up with Art Garfunkel and he considered the song a quote gamble." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 29 - Every Little Thing is Gonna Be All Right - The Influence of 70s Reggae | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-29-every-little-thing-is-gonna-be-all-right-the-influence-of-70s-reggae
**Early Career as Jerry Landis** : Paul Simon initially performed under the name Jerry Landis as part of the duo Tom and Jerry, who had a minor hit with "Hey School Girl." He later used other aliases like True Taylor and Paul Kane before working under his real name with Art Garfunkel. "Jerry Landis went on to record under other names, like True Taylor and Paul Kane, before getting back together with Tom and deciding to work together under their real names." ← Episode 89： ＂Will You Love Me Tomorrow？＂ by the Shirelles | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7Kns2HGcuSg
**Graceland Album Inspiration** : More than a decade after his early reggae experiments, Paul Simon found a pivotal source of inspiration for his Grammy-winning album "Graceland" in the form of a cassette tape featuring South African street music. "More than 10 years before he would find some inspiration for that Grammy winning album Graceland in the form of this cassette tape of South African street music." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 29 - Every Little Thing is Gonna Be All Right - The Influence of 70s Reggae | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-29-every-little-thing-is-gonna-be-all-right-the-influence-of-70s-reggae
**Jamaican Music Admiration** : Paul Simon expressed a strong affinity for various musical genres beyond typical American styles, specifically highlighting his deep appreciation for Jamaican music while noting that very few other people were interested in it at the time. "In this interview, he made a point of saying how much he liked Jamaican music but have few people were actually into it at the time." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 29 - Every Little Thing is Gonna Be All Right - The Influence of 70s Reggae | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-29-every-little-thing-is-gonna-be-all-right-the-influence-of-70s-reggae
**Jamaican Recording Approach** : To achieve an authentic sound, Paul Simon traveled to Jamaica for recordings, where he innovatively compensated session musicians by suggesting they were playing three songs instead of one, effectively providing them more money than the standard "notorious racket" of about seven dollars per song with no royalties, and crucially, he allowed them to freely play "whatever they want." "So I sat down and said you play it, play what you want. That's the key thing. Let them play whatever they want and then you change, you go their way." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 29 - Every Little Thing is Gonna Be All Right - The Influence of 70s Reggae | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-29-every-little-thing-is-gonna-be-all-right-the-influence-of-70s-reggae
**Legal Trademark Requirement** : Paul Simon was legally obligated by Kodak to include specific fine print on his records for "Kodachrome," indicating that "Kodachrome" was a registered trademark of the company. This showcased the intersection of copyright law and corporate branding in the music industry.
