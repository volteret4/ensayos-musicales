## albums
**15 Hours in a September Thursday (1994) - Cassette Only** : An independent release available exclusively on cassette, predating the band's first proper album. It featured the song "Last of the Ghetto Astronauts," which subsequently inspired the title of their debut full-length record. "It was a cassette only thing called 15 hours in a September Thursday, which came out early in the fall of 1994." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-s0gwXY_CZQ
**Beautiful Midnight (1999) - Third Album** : The band's third studio album, released in 1999. Songs from this album were road-tested during their performance at Edgefest in 1999. "The Matthew Good Band toured behind the Underdogs album for a couple of years before beautiful midnight was released in 1999." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-s0gwXY_CZQ
**Last of the Ghetto Astronauts (1995) - Debut Album** : The band's inaugural proper album, released in 1995, recorded for approximately $5,000. Distinctively, the album contained no electric guitars; all guitar sounds were achieved by running acoustic guitars through Marshall amplifiers. The first 5,000 copies of the album only bore the acronym "MGB" before the band officially adopted "The Matthew Good Band" due to popular demand. "The whole thing was recorded for about $5,000 and there were no electric guitars anywhere." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-s0gwXY_CZQ
**Loser Anthem (2001) - EP** : An EP released in 2001. The artwork for this EP, which depicted a panda with "panda eyes," was directly inspired by a memorable incident at Edgefest 1999 involving Universal Music's marketing representative, Iber Hamilton, who attempted a stage dive in a giant panda costume. "And the great thing about it is that in 2001, I released that EP, Loser Anthem." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-s0gwXY_CZQ
**Low-Five Besides (1998) - EP** : An EP released in 1998, serving as an interim release between the "Underdogs" album and "Beautiful Midnight." This EP notably included a cover of a Depeche Mode song. "The Matthew Good Band from a quick EP called Low-Five Besides, released in 1998." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-s0gwXY_CZQ
**Ray Gunn (1997) - EP** : An EP released in 1997, specifically put out to gain time for the recording of their second full-length album, "Underdogs." It featured a re-recorded version of the song "Haven't Slapped in Years," originally from their debut album. "Last of the ghetto astronauts was followed by an EP in 1997 called Ray Gunn." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-s0gwXY_CZQ
**Underdogs (1997) - Breakthrough Album** : Released on October 7, 1997. The band initially aimed to work with producer Gil Norton, who was unavailable due to commitments with Foo Fighters, leading him to recommend Warren Lives. The record deal with Private Music (a BMG subsidiary) unexpectedly folded on the day recording was scheduled to commence. Matthew Good, having secured a payout for two records through legal maneuvering, self-funded the album, with Warren Lives ultimately producing it. Despite initial skepticism from A&M/Universal, where an A&R executive bet it wouldn't sell more than 30,000 copies, the album's sales rapidly escalated from around 20,000 to over 100,000 records within months following the release of the "Operations" music video. "The Matthew Good Band from Underdogs released October 7th, 1997." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-s0gwXY_CZQ

## curiosities
**Band Name Evolution (Matthew Good Band vs. MGB)** : The band initially used the acronym "MGB" on the first 5,000 copies of their debut album, "Last of the Ghetto Astronauts." However, public demand at radio (for "Alabama Motel Room") and retail led people to consistently request "Matthew Good Band." Faced with this popularity, the decision was made to officially adopt Matthew Good's name for the band, partly because early members were not fully committed and often had other academic pursuits. This event, however, was later described as "the beginning of the end" due to escalating ego issues. "The decision was made that that's what we would call it." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-s0gwXY_CZQ
**Early Incarnation and Musical Direction (1993)** : By 1993, an early incarnation of The Matthew Good Band existed with different players and string arrangements. This lineup ended that year as Matthew Good felt it wasn't aligning with his desired musical direction. "I mean, back then it was all different players. There were strings involved in it." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-s0gwXY_CZQ
**Exhaustive Touring Conditions** : The band frequently undertook rigorous tours across the country, often playing up to 16 consecutive shows. Traveling in a van, members would sleep with their knees pressed against the seat in front, and showers were taken whenever possible in available band rooms or venues. "I don't know how many times we toured the country in the van, you know, playing 16 shows in a row with your knees pinned up against the seat in front of you." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-s0gwXY_CZQ
**Post-Name Change Ego Struggles** : The formal adoption of "The Matthew Good Band" name led to significant ego issues within the group, which Matthew Good described as "the beginning of the end." To maintain peace and cohesion, he made efforts to placate other members, even going as far as to give up publishing rights and credit that he felt wasn't necessarily due. "From that day forward, ego was about everything." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-s0gwXY_CZQ
**The Edgefest Panda Incident (1999)** : During the Matthew Good Band's performance on the main stage at Edgefest in Wilson Park, Barrie, in 1999, Universal Music marketing representative Iber Hamilton, dressed in a giant panda costume, attempted a stage dive during the song "Everything's Automatic." From the 10-12 foot high stage, he launched into the crowd, which unexpectedly parted "like the Red Sea." The panda landed face down on the ground 12 feet below, suffering a bleeding nose (his head was protected by a football helmet within the costume) and was subsequently kicked by some members of the audience. Matthew Good, performing with his microphone back by the monitor lines, did not immediately see what happened but later found Hamilton in the back with a bloody face. This incident later inspired the artwork for the "Loser Anthem" EP. "The crowd parts like the Red Sea. Everybody moves. Panda, bam! Face down on the ground, 12 feet below the stage." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-s0gwXY_CZQ
**Total Releases (1995-2009)** : By the end of 2009, the band had accumulated 12 releases over a span of 14 years. "At the end of 2009, we have 12 releases over 14 years and a lot of people who have maybe jumped on the train somewhere in the middle didn't realize that you began as part of a folk band." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-s0gwXY_CZQ
**Underdogs Album Sales Bet** : At Universal or A&M (the specific label was unclear at the time), Paul A. Graham, the head A&R, made an office bet that the "Underdogs" album would not sell more than 30,000 records. Despite this internal skepticism, the album's sales dramatically increased from approximately 20,000 units to over 100,000 within months, largely propelled by the success of the "Operations" music video. "There was an office bed going around where the actual head A&R said that I wouldn't sell more than 30,000 records." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-s0gwXY_CZQ
