**Amy (1972)** : Originally a cut from the 1972 album "Bustin' Out," "Amy" gained traction through college radio play before being picked up by country and then Top 40 stations. RCA re-signed Pure Prairie League and re-released it as a single, giving the band "new life." It peaked at #27 on the Billboard Hot 100 in 1975 and is characterized by twang, harmonies, and heartache. Co-founder Craig Fuller described it simply as "just a song," an "exercise in song craftsmanship." **Craig Fuller's Legal Problems** : Co-founder Craig Fuller's legal issues stemming from claiming conscientious objector status during the Vietnam War draft significantly impacted the band. In 1973 (the year US troops withdrew), he was sentenced to six months in prison in Springfield, Missouri, followed by two years of service in an "alternative mental hospital." These problems contributed to RCA dropping the band after their "Bustin' Out" album was released. **Resistance to "Country Rock" Label** : Pure Prairie League members, including Mike Reilly, resisted the "country rock" label, preferring to call their sound the "midwestern sound" as of June 2021. This preference stems from their diverse musical upbringing in Cincinnati, which encompassed jazz, blues, country, bluegrass, R&B, and soul, leading them to play everything from The Beatles to Lonnie Mack, and being influenced by The Byrds and Poco.
