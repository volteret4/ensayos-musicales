## curiosities
**Bringing Millie Small to UK (1963)** : In 1963, Blackwell identified Millie Small's potential for international stardom. He convinced her mother to allow Millie to move to the UK, promising to make her a star, and subsequently licensed her records to Fontana for wider distribution. "in 1963 he had decided to call Milly's mother and promise her that if a daughter came over to the UK, he would be able to make her into a star." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**Licensing Millie Small's Records** : Instead of releasing Millie Small's records directly on Island, which lacked wide distribution at the time, Blackwell opted to license them to Fontana, a mid-sized British label, to ensure broader reach and impact for her first British singles. "Rather than release her records on Ireland, which didn't have any wide distribution, he decided to license them to Fontana, amidst size British label." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**Mother's Social Connections & Influence** : His mother, Blanche Blackwell, was a socialite in Jamaica known for her affairs, including with Errol Flynn and Ian Fleming. Fleming reportedly based characters like Pussy Galore and Honeychild Ryder on her. Her life and connections provided Chris with a unique perspective and entry points into Jamaican culture. "His mother, Blanche, lived in Jamaica, where she was amused to many men." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**Move to England and Island Records Distribution** : After his experience with "Dr. No" and the fortune teller's advice, Blackwell moved back to England. There, he continued to run Island Records, primarily as a distributor of Jamaican records, hand-selling them to shops in immigrant communities across London, Manchester, and Birmingham. "he moved back to England, where he continued running Island Records, this time as a distributor of Jamaican Records." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**Nearly Quit Music for James Bond (1962)** : In 1962, Blackwell nearly abandoned his music career. Thanks to family connections, he got a job as a go-fer and had a cameo role in the first James Bond film, "Dr. No." Producer Harry Saltzman offered him a permanent job, but a fortune teller advised Blackwell to stick with music. "He'd been given a job as a go-for on the first James Bond film, Dr No, thanks to his family connections, and had also had a cameo role in the film." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**Privileged Background** : Chris Blackwell came from a privileged background; his father was an heir to the Crosse & Blackwell fortune, and he was educated at Harrow. Despite this, he spent much of his youth in Jamaica, where his mother lived. "His father had been one of the heirs to the cross-and-blackwell fortune, and young Chris had been educated at Harrow." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**Producer of Millie Small's Hit** : Chris Blackwell, an Englishman, produced Millie Small's worldwide hit "My Boy Lollipop." This song was a significant milestone in bringing Jamaican Ska (SCOP/Blue Beat) music to a broader audience in Britain and internationally, facilitated by his newly established Island Records label. "The big breakthrough was a worldwide hit by Millie Small, called My Boy Lollipop, produced by Englishman Chris Blackwell, and distributed through his brand new Idle and Records label." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=V7WjNR1ujhk ← chris-blackwell
**Rastafari Culture Fascination** : Blackwell developed a deep fascination with Rastafari culture after being rescued by Rasta fishermen when his boat crashed while sailing. This experience solidified his goal to promote Jamaican culture globally. "Chris Blackwell had developed a fascination with Raster culture after having crashed his boat while sailing and being rescued by some Raster fishermen, and he had decided that his girl was to promote Jamaican culture to the world." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**Witness to Jamaican Shooting (1996)** : Chris Blackwell, the head of Island Records, was among the friends with Bono and his family on Jimmy Buffett's plane, the "Hemisphere Dancer," when it was mistakenly fired upon by Jamaican police at Negril airport on January 16, 1996.
