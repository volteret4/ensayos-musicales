**Later Career and Influence** : Moondog was taken seriously as a performer and composer, eventually securing a recording contract for multiple albums. He married, started a family, and returned to Carnegie Hall in 1989 to conduct a performance of his own works. He later moved to Europe, where he died of heart failure in 1999 at age 83. His life and music were inspirational to various artists, from hippies to bands important to the creation of Trip Hop. **Lawsuit Against Allan Freed** : Moondog, incensed by disc jockey Allan Freed's unauthorized use of his "Moon Dog" moniker and "Moon Dog Symphony" as a radio show theme, sued Freed with the help of big band leader Benny Goodman. Moondog won the lawsuit, securing $6,000 in damages and a promise that Freed would never use the name "Moon Dog" again, directly influencing Freed's adoption of "rock and roll" as a descriptive term for the new music. **Street Performer and Composer** : Louis Hardin Jr., known as Moondog, was a homeless, six-foot, blind New York street performer. He dressed in a Viking costume and often stood silently banging a drum outside Carnegie Hall, composing music and releasing records under the name Moon Dog. His striking appearance and musical talent attracted attention from famous composers and conductors.
