## songs
**Good Times (1979)** : Released in June 1979, this disco track became Chic's second number one song, topping the Billboard Hot 100 by August 18th and reaching number three on the disco chart. Nile Rogers noted that he and Bernard Edwards recognized it as "the perfect hip hop record" due to its extended breakdown, which could accommodate long rhymes. Barry Crocker, reviewing for The New York Rocker in 1980, described its appeal as coming from a "raunchy pulse" and a "gut level instinct," despite its lush orchestrations. The song's bassline was compared to a "jungle drum" and its handclap to a "heartbeat lifeline." It is ranked number 220 on Rolling Stone's list of the top 500 songs of all time, largely due to its extensive sampling. "Good Times" is recognized for its profound influence on hip hop, with its DNA woven into the genre, and its baseline forming the core of "Rapper's Delight." ## curiosities
**Drumming Influence on Dave Grohl** : Tony Thompson, the drummer for Chic, was acknowledged by Dave Grohl of Foo Fighters (and Nirvana) as a significant influence, with Grohl stating he "ripped off" Thompson's drumming for his own work, including on Nirvana's "Nevermind." "Tony Thompson, the drummer for Sheik, was at his house for a barbecue. And Girl told Thompson, man, I just want to thank you because I owe you so much for ripping you off my whole life." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 1 - Disco Doesn't Suck (Encore) | https://www.ftr70.com/blog/tag/70s+podcast
**Lawsuit Against Sugar Hill Records** : When Nile Rogers first heard "Rapper's Delight" and recognized his song "Good Times" being sampled without proper credit, he and Bernard Edwards were upset, particularly since their names were absent from the Sugar Hill Records album. Their label, Atlantic, declined to sue, so Rogers and Edwards personally filed a lawsuit against Sugar Hill. Despite financial investor Morris Levy's known mafia connections to Sugar Hill, they proceeded with the suit, resulting in a favorable out-of-court settlement. This action set a precedent for stricter copyright enforcement in sampling and ensured they received writing credit on "Rapper's Delight." "Nile and Bernard were understandably not happy that their music was basically stolen with no financial compensation to them, but their record label Atlantic would not sue. So Nile and Bernard sued Sugar Hill on their own." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 6 - The 70s Roots of Hip Hop | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-6-the-70s-roots-of-hip-hop ← chic
