# artist - Hilly Crystal

## curiosities
**CBGB Founding Vision (December 1973)** : Hilly Crystal, formerly the manager of The Village Vanguard, leased a former biker bar space in the Bowery, New York City, in December 1973. His original intent was for a venue featuring country, bluegrass, and blues music, which he named CBGB, adding OMFUG (Other Music For Uplifting Gourmandizers) to sound "uncouth or crude." "In December 1973, Hilly Crystal, the former manager of the village vanguard, he leased a space in the Bowery in New York City that was formerly a biker bar." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 57 - Punk and New Wave at New York's CBGB | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Closure of CBGB (2006)** : Hilly Crystal was forced to close CBGB due to escalating financial pressures. Monthly rent had risen from $19,000 to $35,000, and the club faced a $90,000 lawsuit from a local residence committee. Despite court cases and an attempt to declare the club a historical landmark, the ongoing gentrification of the neighborhood and rising costs made it impossible to continue. "In the end, rent went up to $35,000 a month and Hilly had no choice but to close the place down." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Epz5Sn151bk ← hilly-crystal
**Early CBGB Environment** : CBGB's location in a "pretty awful" Bowery neighborhood, below a flop house inhabited by alcoholics and Vietnam veterans, contributed to cheap rent and a lack of complaints about loud music. Despite the unsanitary conditions, including dodging liquor bottles and dog poop, Crystal fostered a unique musical environment. "It was nothing to have to step over the bodies of guys who were passed out on the sidewalks. Of course, this all meant that rent was cheap and nobody was really going to complain about loud music." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 57 - Punk and New Wave at New York's CBGB | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Enduring Vision and Death (2007)** : Crystal maintained his vision for CBGB as a venue for original music even as the punk scene evolved to feature "faster and angrier" bands like Murphy's Law and Bad Brains in the early 1980s. Despite legal battles and gentrification leading to the club's closure on October 15, 2006, Crystal died a year later in 2007. "Billy Crystal was not thrilled with all of it, but he kept true to his vision of CBGB being a venue for original music." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 57 - Punk and New Wave at New York's CBGB | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Founder of CBGB (1973)** : Hilly Crystal, an ex-marine, opened the CBGB club in 1973 in the Bowery neighborhood of New York. Despite the area being an economic and social disaster zone at the time, the cheap rent allowed starving artists to live, rehearse, and create. Crystal established the club's musical policy, which fostered an environment where artists, "freaks, and weirdos" could do their own thing, leading to the birth of punk rock. "When an ex-marine named Hilly Crystal opened the place in 1973, it was in a part of town where nobody wanted to go." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Epz5Sn151bk ← hilly-crystal
**Original Music Rule** : Crystal established a strict rule that all bands booked at his club must play only original music, stating, "originality to me was prime technique took second place." This principle guided CBGB's booking policies, even for bands he initially found unappealing. "Crystal had a very firm rule that any bands that were booked for his club could only play original music. He said originality to me was prime technique took second place." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 57 - Punk and New Wave at New York's CBGB | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Patti Smith's Magnetism** : Crystal was deeply impressed by Patti Smith's stage presence, observing that "she just had magnetism." He praised her vocal abilities, noting that she "stayed on pitch, she bent the notes just right, she sang real well, she communicated," making her one of the few performers he could listen to repeatedly. "He said she just had magnetism. She may not have been a singer but she sure sounded like one." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 57 - Punk and New Wave at New York's CBGB | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Ramones' Loudness and Self-Reliance** : Crystal's first impression of The Ramones was that they were "a very, very loud band" from Queens whom "nobody liked." He valued them because they consistently brought their own amplifiers and PA system, which CBGB could not afford. He recognized their hard work, self-belief, and their determination to "make themselves happen." "my first impression of the Ramones was that they were just a very, very loud band They'd come in from Queens where nobody liked them They worked very, very hard and played more and more." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 57 - Punk and New Wave at New York's CBGB | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Skepticism Towards Television** : Crystal initially found Tom Verlaine's band, Television, to be "god awful" and "horrible" when they first started playing at CBGB, noting that their manager, Terry Ork, had to badger him repeatedly to book them. Despite his initial skepticism, their ability to draw an audience ensured their continued presence. "He kept saying, well, we'll have another band with them next time and we'll try some new things. It was always one thing or another thing to get them back." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 57 - Punk and New Wave at New York's CBGB | https://www.ftr70.com/
