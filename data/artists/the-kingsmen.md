**Louie Louie (1963) – Lyrics Controversy & FBI Investigation** : Following its release, rumors spread that The Kingsmen intentionally garbled the lyrics to conceal sexually explicit content about a Jamaican sailor. Though no one knew for sure, the alleged "secret lyrics" circulated among teenagers, prompting one to contact the governor of Indiana, who subsequently banned the song. This controversy paradoxically boosted the song's mystique and national chart performance. The publishing company offered $1,000 to anyone proving the lyrics were obscene, and the FBI launched a two-year investigation, analyzing the recording forwards and backwards, concluding that the lyrics were "unintelligible at any speed." "Rumors began to spread that the Kingsman had intentionally garbled the lyrics to cover up what was actually being sung." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=L0j9iDuHQVA
**Louie Louie (1963) – Recording Details** : This version of the song was recorded for $50 in Portland, Oregon, on April 6, 1963, at Northwestern Motion Pictures and Recording. It was a bare-bones session with one microphone hung in the center of the room, forcing singer Joe Eli, who had just been fitted with heavy braces, to sing upward with difficulty. The band had only one take, leading to a rushed performance with visible errors including the drummer dropping a stick and Joe Eli coming in two bars too early. "The best example is Louis Louis by the Kingsman, a song recorded for $50 in Portland, Oregon, on April 6, 1963." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=L0j9iDuHQVA

**Louis Louis (1963)** : This track, considered an all-time classic and one of the most famous Garage Rock anthems, was recorded on April 6, 1963, in Portland, Oregon. It cost only $50 to produce and was a cover of an obscure 1957 R&B song by Louisiana singer Richard Berry. The session took place at Northwestern Motion Pictures and Recording, produced by a local DJ. The band blasted it out in a single take, meaning the released version is their only studio performance that day. The arrangement changed the original beat, and singer Joe Eli, wearing heavy braces, sang directly into a single boom microphone, contributing to the unintelligible vocals. The song is famous for a mistake: after the guitar solo, Eli comes in early, stops, and drummer Lynn Easton plays an extra drum fill to cover the error; this mistake is now intentionally replicated in modern performances. It became a regional, then national, hit, and rumors of pornographic lyrics led to its ban from radio stations and entirely in the state of Indiana. "On April 6, 1963, a bunch of dudes from Portland, Oregon spent 50 bucks to record a cover of an obscure 1957 R&B song called Louis Louis, originally done by a Louisiana singer named Richard Berry." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=UpWz3X33vSY

## curiosities

**Launch of American Garage Band Era** : The Kingsmen are credited with launching the era of the American garage band. These groups were typically rough, ragged, and occasionally incorporated risqué material into their music. "The Kingsman launched the era of the American garage band, groups that were often rough and ragged, and who got risque from time to time within material." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6oUkMOkEr9U

The FBI even launched an 18-month investigation, shadowing the group on tour, but ultimately concluded that the lyrics were "unintelligible at any speed."
