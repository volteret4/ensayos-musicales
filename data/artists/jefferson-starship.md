**Departure from 1960s Counterculture Roots** : The commercial triumph of songs like "Miracles" and albums such as *Red Octopus* represented a significant departure from the band's original 1960s counterculture ethos. Grace Slick's discomfort with performing these commercial hits underscored this tension, stating she felt "odd doing it like I was wearing a costume a monkey suit or something," and some critics longed for their earlier "Jefferson airplane back." "it's hard to believe sort of that it's that came from the same band that helped define 60s counterculture no disrespect at all to the 60s music that the band made." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 51 - The Softer Side of 70s Rock | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-51-the-softer-side-of-70s-rock
**Formation and Lineup Changes** : Jefferson Starship was formed by Grace Slick and Paul Cantner as a revamped version of Jefferson Airplane. Marty Balin was highlighted as the "best thing about the band in the 70s," indicating his significant role during that period. "She formed with Paul Cantner, Jefferson Starship in the 70s." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 7 - Women Who Rocked the 70s | http://www.ftr70.com
**Grace Slick's Reduced Role** : In Jefferson Starship's 70s music, Grace Slick's role diminished significantly, primarily contributing harmonies and background vocals, such as "oh babies" on "Miracles," a stark contrast to her lead vocal prominence in Jefferson Airplane. "Grace essentially harmonized and added in some good, oh babies on that deceptively raunchy part of miracles." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 7 - Women Who Rocked the 70s | http://www.ftr70.com
**Transition from Jefferson Airplane (By 1974)** : Jefferson Starship formed by 1974 after Jefferson Airplane underwent extensive "shifts in personnel." Founding member Marty Balin initially left the group due to his bandmates' "copious amounts of cocaine" and his preference for a different musical direction, away from the "psychedelic experience." "By 1974 Jefferson airplane had undergone many shifts in personnel again one would require a flow chart and it became Jefferson's starship minus however one of the band's founders Marty Ballon." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 51 - The Softer Side of 70s Rock | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-51-the-softer-side-of-70s-rock

## albums
**Dragonfly (1974)** : Marty Balin contributed the song "Caroline" to this album after being coaxed back into the band. "he was able to be slowly coaxed back into the band to contribute Caroline a song he wrote and sang for the dragonfly album." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 51 - The Softer Side of 70s Rock | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-51-the-softer-side-of-70s-rock
**Red Octopus (1975)** : This album achieved the distinction of being Jefferson Starship's only number one album, featuring the commercially successful radio hit "Miracles." Its considerable commercial success, however, drew criticism from some who desired the return of the "Jefferson airplane back," lamenting the band's shift from its counterculture roots. "the album red octopus was the only number one album Jefferson starship ever had." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 51 - The Softer Side of 70s Rock | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-51-the-softer-side-of-70s-rock

**Miracles (1975)** : This song reached number three on the Billboard Hot 100, becoming a massive radio hit for the band and a key track on their only number one album, *Red Octopus*. Marty Balin wrote and sang this song, making its recording a condition for his permanent return to the band. Its pop-friendly sound was a stark contrast to the band's 1960s counterculture identity, leading Grace Slick to feel "odd doing it like I was wearing a costume a monkey suit or something."
