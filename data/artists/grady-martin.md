## curiosities
**Accidental Invention of the Fuzz Sound (1960)** : In late 1960, while playing guitar on Marty Robbins' song "Don't Worry" at Nashville's Quonset Hut studio, Grady Martin accidentally created the first recorded fuzz guitar sound. This occurred when he plugged his six-string baritone guitar directly into a recording console with a faulty transformer in one of its preamps, resulting in a distinct fuzzy sound. Though Marty Robbins initially disliked it, Martin, producer Owen Bradley, and engineer Glenn Snottie recognized its potential, leading to its inclusion in the hit song and subsequently inspiring the development of distortion pedals. "A faulty transformer for one of the preamps inside the console halfway failed, making everything sound all fuzzy." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6SdPPsmpgJg ← grady-martin
**Decca Session Musician (1956)** : Grady Martin was one of the regular session musicians Owen Bradley used and played rhythm guitar at Buddy Holly's first Decca recording session. His presence allowed Buddy to focus on singing without simultaneously playing guitar. "Grady Martin on rhythm guitar, so Buddy didn't have to sing and play at the same time." ← Episode Sixty-One： ＂That'll Be the Day＂, by the Crickets | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KVJMKLc54O0
**Design of the First Fuzz Pedal** : Following the popularity of the fuzzy guitar sound on Marty Robbins' "Don't Worry," Grady Martin was approached by Gibson Guitars. With assistance from engineer Glenn Snottie and WSM-TV engineer Revis Hobbs, Martin designed a circuit that could replicate this sound on demand. This collaboration led to the creation of the prototype for the first-ever commercial fuzz pedal, the Maestro FZ1 Fuzz tone. "So with some help from Snottie and an engineer from Nashville's WSMTV named Revis Hobbs, Grady designed a circuit that would do just that." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6SdPPsmpgJg ← grady-martin
