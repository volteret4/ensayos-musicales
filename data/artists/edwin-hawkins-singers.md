## songs
**Oh Happy Day (1968)** : The Edwin Hawkins Singers took this traditional hymn and transformed it into a pop hit in 1968, making it one of the first, if not the first, gospel songs to achieve such mainstream success. This move drew criticism from churches who disapproved of their singers profiting from hymns. Despite the backlash, Edwin Hawkins believed young people needed songs like "Oh Happy Day" as they were turning away from the church but remained confused about life, seeing music as a way to "take the gospel to them." The song's success inspired George Harrison to write "My Sweet Lord." ## curiosities
**Churches' Disapproval of Profiting from Hymns** : The Edwin Hawkins Singers faced criticism from churches for turning a hymn into a pop hit and profiting from it, which was not approved by religious institutions at the time. This incident "showed what gospel music can be" and highlighted the tension between sacred and secular music markets. **Evangelizing Through Song** : Edwin Hawkins explicitly stated his motivation for creating pop-friendly gospel music was to reach young people who were confused about life and turning away from the church.
