## curiosities
**Capitol Records Era** : Nat King Cole was a significant artist, referred to as a "crew member," at Capitol Records during the 1940s. The label in this period was largely dedicated to traditional music forms and standards, predating its significant involvement with rock and roll. "Capital at the time was the home of crew members like Frank Sinatra and Nat King Cole..." ← Episode 41： ＂Be-Bop-A-Lula＂ by Gene Vincent and the Bluecaps | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tYBQ5F7O3G0
**Influence on Chuck Berry** : Nat King Cole was the musician whom Chuck Berry most liked to listen to, despite Berry primarily playing Louis Jordan's music for a living. This personal preference influenced groups like the Chuck Berry Trio, which clearly attempted to emulate Cole's style. "Barry would often say that while Louis Jordan music was the music he would play to try to make a living, Nat King Cold was the musician he most liked to listen to, and the Chuck Barvitrio was clearly an attempt to emulate this style." ← Episode 32： ＂I Got A Woman＂ by Ray Charles | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0oeI9f2x_fo

**Piano Collaborations** : Nat King Cole was known to play piano with other prominent musicians such as Les Paul and Illinois Jacquet, highlighting his versatility and presence in various musical contexts beyond his trio. **Stage Attack by White Supremacists (April 1956)** : In April 1956, Nat King Cole was assaulted on stage by a mob of white supremacists, resulting in his injury. This incident, despite Cole being a politically understated black entertainer performing for an all-white audience with a white backing band, underscored the violent racial tensions of the era.
