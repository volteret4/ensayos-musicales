**Bandstand Boogie (Manilow Version) (1975)** : Displeased with the 1974 synthesized update of the "American Bandstand" theme, Barry Manilow, with Bruce Sussman, penned lyrics to the original tune. This lyrical version was included on his 1975 album "Trying to Get That Feeling Again" and was so well-liked by Dick Clark that it became the show's new theme song. "Barry Manelow added the lyrics, and then the song was included on his 1975 album, trying to get that feeling again." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 47 - Dick Clark's Entertainment Empire | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Could It Be Magic (1975)** : This song reached number six on the Billboard Hot 100 in 1975. Its opening notes are derived from Chopin's C minor Prelude, Opus 28, number 20. Manilow intentionally structured the song to "build and build until you think you can't take it anymore" by borrowing from the style of The Beatles' "Hey Jude," aiming for it to be a "musical orgasm." **Catalyst for Musicians Union Backlash Against Synths** : Barry Manilow became the "last straw" for the UK Musicians Union on May 23, 1982, prompting their public grievances against synthesizers. Previously touring with a string section, he replaced his orchestra with just a couple of synth players in 1981, leading to job losses for traditional musicians and escalating calls for a ban on synthesizers. "The last straw wasn't craftwork, or OMD, or Durand Durand, but very Manolo, one of the biggest mainstream artists at that time. In the past, Manolo had gone on tour with a string section, providing musicians with a solid living. But in 1981, he dumped the orchestra and toured with just a couple of synth players. That had to stop." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5PJRCC6EwUY ← barry-manilow

**Dislike for Synthesized "Bandstand Boogie" Theme (1974)** : Barry Manilow, who grew up watching American Bandstand and fondly remembered Les Elgart's original theme, strongly disliked the 1974 synthesized update by Mike Kerb. He found the Moog synthesizer version unrecognizable and uninspiring, which prompted him to collaborate with Bruce Sussman to write new lyrics for the original tune. "The most crushing blow came when I heard their theme. Dick Clark, while initially underestimating Manilow's star potential based on his looks and voice, recognized his talent for arranging and producing emotionally resonant songs. John Wasserman of the San Francisco Chronicle once skeptically questioned his star aspirations.
