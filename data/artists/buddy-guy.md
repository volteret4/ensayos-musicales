## songs
**Money (That's What I Want) (1968)** : A cover of the song originally recorded by Barrett Strong for Tamla in 1959, written by Janie Bradford and Berry Gordy, which became Motown's first hit in 1960 and was notably covered by The Beatles in 1963. Buddy Guy's electrified version was recorded in 1968 for his album `A Man and His Blues` and was performed to electrify crowds on the Festival Express tour. "Buddy Guy electrified the crowds on the festival Express tour with his own cover of Money." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 59 - Bands and Booze on the Festival Express of 1970 | https://www.ftr70.com/ ← buddy-guy

## curiosities
**Commercial Struggles and Resurgence** : While many rock stars he influenced achieved great commercial success, Buddy Guy's career commercially stalled in the 1970s. However, his career experienced a turnaround with a renewed interest in blues in the late 1980s and early 1990s. "Unfortunately, while many other rock stars that he influenced would go on to have great commercial success, that was not true for him and his career kind of stalled out at least commercially speaking in the 1970s." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 59 - Bands and Booze on the Festival Express of 1970 | https://www.ftr70.com/ ← buddy-guy
**Festival Express Sobriety and Observation** : Buddy Guy claimed to be mostly sober during the Festival Express, while many others were heavily intoxicated with various substances. He recalled others attempting to share their substances with him and taking his whiskey when the train ran out of booze. He also uniquely recalled fans jumping on and off the train at stops. "On that train, everybody was messed up but me. I still was drunk a little cogniac because I'm still nervous when it comes to playing." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 59 - Bands and Booze on the Festival Express of 1970 | https://www.ftr70.com/ ← buddy-guy
**Influence on British Musicians** : Young British musicians, including members of The Rolling Stones and Cream, paid close attention to Buddy Guy and other blues artists in the early 1960s, rediscovering American blues records. Eric Clapton continued to carry this influence into his solo career. "Young British musicians were paying very close attention to what buddy guy and other blues artists were doing." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 59 - Bands and Booze on the Festival Express of 1970 | https://www.ftr70.com/ ← buddy-guy
**Influence on Modern Rock** : Buddy Guy is considered a foundational figure of modern rock music, directly influencing artists like Jimi Hendrix. He pioneered techniques like distortion, string bending, and theatrical stage presence, which were radical in the early 1960s. "Buddy guy isn't just a great blues guitarist, which he is. He's part and parcel of the foundation of modern rock music." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 59 - Bands and Booze on the Festival Express of 1970 | https://www.ftr70.com/ ← buddy-guy
**Ongoing Career** : As of the recording, Buddy Guy is 90 years old and still touring. "As I record this, Buddy Guy is 90 and I most recently saw him at the end of sinners that I won't say anything more in case you have not seen that movie." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 59 - Bands and Booze on the Festival Express of 1970 | https://www.ftr70.com/ ← buddy-guy
**Theatrical Stage Act** : Known for his spectacular stage act, Buddy Guy would walk into the audience mid-solo, play behind his back, and perform in seemingly impossible positions, generating intense blues agony. Mike John, writing for The New York Times in December 1968, described his act: "He strikes a particular note and rises to wrap himself around it. His eyes elevated to the sky in blues agony. He plays one handed behind his back over his head in positions that seem impossible to even hold a guitar, let alone play it and play it well." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 59 - Bands and Booze on the Festival Express of 1970 | https://www.ftr70.com/ ← buddy-guy
**Volume and Loudness** : Buddy Guy was known for playing very loud, a radical approach in the early 1960s. "Buddy guy also played loud, like really loud. In the early 1960s, this was kind of radical." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 59 - Bands and Booze on the Festival Express of 1970 | https://www.ftr70.com/ ← buddy-guy
