## curiosities
**Genuine Musical Genius** : Grandmaster Flash is recognized as one of the "genuine stars, genuine musical geniuses" of the early rap and hip-hop scene, whose contributions were not always taken seriously by the wider music market. "It didn't matter that there were genuine stars, genuine musical geniuses. One DMC, Curtis Blow, Africa Babata, Grandmaster Flash, LL Cool J, the scene and the sounds were just not taken seriously." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l1jPP1FhGo8
**Pioneers of Rap/Hip Hop** : Grandmaster Flash was a key figure who emerged from the nascent rap and hip hop scene on the streets of New York, where DJs developed techniques of "turntableism" by chopping and manipulating records to create new sounds for MCs to rhyme over. "This is the culture that gave us run DMC and Grandmaster Flash." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OiqdZfxdBP0
**Breakthrough with The Message** : After being signed by Sugar Hill Records, Grandmaster Flash and the Furious Five achieved a significant breakthrough in 1982 with their impactful song, "The Message." This track further cemented the political and social commentary aspects of hip hop, which had roots in earlier works like Gil Scott Heron's. "Sugar Hill then went on to sign Grandmaster Flash in the Furious Five who broke through in 1982 with the message." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 6 - The 70s Roots of Hip Hop | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-6-the-70s-roots-of-hip-hop

He represents the era when hip hop began to evolve from its rudimentary origins. **Father of Turnabilism** : Grandmaster Flash is recognized as one of the founding figures of turnabilism, an instrumental movement within hip hop culture. Notably, he was of Filipino origin, an example used to illustrate that the initial birthplaces of hip hop were not exclusively African American but involved various racial minorities in the United States. **Student of Breaks** : As a pioneering figure in hip hop, Grandmaster Flash dedicated three years of his teenage life to immersing himself in music, specifically focusing on understanding and manipulating "breaks" in funk and soul records. He developed a meticulous approach, noticing that the breaks could sometimes be physically seen in the vinyl grooves. To mark these sections, he would use a grease pencil or crayon directly on the records. "Grandmaster Flash said that he devoted three years of his life as a teenager just immersing himself in the music and trying to figure out what to do with these breaks." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 6 - The 70s Roots of Hip Hop | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-6-the-70s-roots-of-hip-hop
