**Collaboration with Sam Price** : Tharp's collaboration with pianist Sam Price was musically significant but personally strained. Price initially disliked her open tuning and non-jazz key playing, insisting on a capo, and also combining gospel with boogie-woogie. He was eventually persuaded by Tharp's intimidating mother, and despite the success of their hit "Strange Things Happening Every Day," he resented only receiving a flat fee. "Sam Price did not get along with Tharp." ← Episode 5： ＂This Train＂ by Sister Rosetta Tharpe | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=II-hDnwUgT8
**Cotton Club Debut (1938)** : She began performing at the Cotton Club in 1938, initially on a two-week trial engagement for $500 a week. **First Stadium Rock and Roll Guitarist** : Tharp is considered the first person to play rock and roll guitar in stadiums. This notable event was her third wedding, which took place at Griffith Stadium (home of the Washington Senators), drawing 20,000 attendees for her marriage ceremony and subsequent gospel show, featuring fireworks shaped like her playing guitar. She signed the wedding contract seven months before finding a husband. "She is probably the first person to ever play rock and roll guitar in stadiums, and not only that, she played rock and roll guitar in a stadium, at her wedding, her third wedding to be precise, which took place at Griffith Stadium, the home of the Washington Senators and the homestead 20,000 people came to see her get married and perform a gospel show afterwards." ← Episode 5： ＂This Train＂ by Sister Rosetta Tharpe | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=II-hDnwUgT8

**Partnership with Marie Knight** : Her partnership with fellow gospel singer Marie Knight was highly successful, creating a unique gospel sound. Their relationship was an "open secret" within the gospel music community, which was more tolerant of homosexuality/bisexuality. Knight, however, denied the relationship throughout her life, likely due to her conservative church devotion and career dependence on it. They stopped working together in 1950, but Knight handled Tharp's funeral in 1973. **Taking Gospel to Sinners** : After her first marriage ended, Tharp moved to New York and began playing in nightclubs and theaters for secular audiences, performing gospel music that encouraged dancing and excitement, a departure from Mahalia Jackson's approach of playing church music for church people. **Touring in Segregated America** : Tharp and Knight (and later Tharp solo) toured in their own bus driven by a white man. This offered crucial advantages in the deeply segregated US, allowing them access to public places, acquire takeout food from white-only restaurants, and have accommodation on the bus when no hotels would serve black people, leveraging a sexual double standard that made a black woman with a white man (assumed to be sleeping together) acceptable. "This gave them a number of advantages in a deeply segregated and racist country." ← Episode 5： ＂This Train＂ by Sister Rosetta Tharpe | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=II-hDnwUgT8
**Transition to Electric Guitar** : While with Lucky Millinder's band, Tharp switched from acoustic to electric guitar, which revolutionized her playing style. The electric guitar's sustain allowed her to play single-note lead lines, relying on the orchestra for chordal pads, contrasting with her earlier need to constantly play new notes on acoustic guitar. "She'd also switched to playing an electric guitar rather than an acoustic, and the effect on her guitar playing was extraordinary." ← Episode 5： ＂This Train＂ by Sister Rosetta Tharpe | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=II-hDnwUgT8
