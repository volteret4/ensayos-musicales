**Blue Monday (1983) – Written by Accident, Recorded on LSD, Lost Money on Every Copy Sold** : New Order were devising a way to leave the stage early without audiences noticing, by running Stephen Morris's drum machine through the end of their set. "Blue Monday" was released only as an 8-minute 12-inch single and became the best-selling 12-inch in music history. **Blue Monday (Single)** : This iconic 8-minute 12-inch single originated from New Order's reluctance to perform encores. They devised a plan to turn on Stephen Morris's drum machine and walk off stage to drink backstage. The concept evolved with a sequencer triggering Gillian Welch's keyboards and Barney Sunder suggesting sequencing Peter Hook's bass lines, thus forming the entirely machine-played foundation of a new song. Legend has it the band, reportedly under the influence of LSD, recorded the tracks, after which the studio engineer, seeking peace, sent them to a cafe on Croc's Street to finish mixing. Upon its release, it gained radio play but had its most profound impact in dance clubs, dramatically shifting the landscape of dance music, making dancing "cool again," and forging crucial links between new music, rock, and dance. "Blue Monday" sold over 3 million copies, establishing it as the best-selling 12-inch record in the history of Planet Earth. Ironically, the elaborate artwork and cover for the single were so expensive to produce that New Order lost one pence on each copy sold, resulting in a total loss of 30,000 pounds for issuing "the world's favorite dance record." "Blue Monday sold over 3 million copies and it's still the best selling 12-inch record in the history of Planet Earth." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8UIBWyUt38g

**"Accident of Laziness" Origin of Dance Sound** : New Order's groundbreaking electronic sound and worldwide alt-rock influence partly originated from their reluctance to perform encores. They initially played Stephen Morris's drum machine alone as they left the stage, later adding a sequencer for Gillian Gilbert's synthesizers, and finally, Bernard Sumner conceived sequencing Peter Hook's bass line, leading to songs performed largely by machines. "But their worldwide success and the way they changed alt rock forever was due to an accident of laziness." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zB5zxycrbnY
**"Blue Monday" Composition & Instrumentation** : The composition of "Blue Monday" involved a complex setup: a sequencer built by Stephen Morris connected to an Emulator sampler, a Moog Source keyboard for the bass line, and an Oberheim DMX drum machine. Engineer Martin Usher wrote code to synchronize these devices, and keyboardist Gillian Gilbert's accidental inclusion of an extra rest in her handwritten notation was deliberately kept for the added tension it brought to the arrangement. "He built a sequencer that could be connected to the band's new sampler, a keyboard called an emulator. They sampled a choral effect from a craftwork song called Uranium and wrote a bass line using another keyboard called a Mogue source." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dablAKDOOV0
