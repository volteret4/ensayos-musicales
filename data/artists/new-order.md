**Blue Monday (1983) – Written by Accident, Recorded on LSD, Lost Money on Every Copy Sold** : New Order were devising a way to leave the stage early without audiences noticing, by running Stephen Morris's drum machine through the end of their set. They added sequenced keyboard parts and a bass line, and before long had the foundation of a complete song — all written by machines. The full recording session took place while the band was loaded on LSD; the engineers sent them to a café across the street to finish mixing in peace. During production, Gillian Gilbert accidentally missed programming one note in the melody, leaving it slightly out of time with the rhythm track — a mistake they kept because it sounded better. "Blue Monday" was released only as an 8-minute 12-inch single and became the best-selling 12-inch in music history. However, the elaborate die-cut packaging was so expensive to produce that New Order and the label lost money on every copy they sold. "New Order had the foundations of a new song, and it was all being played entirely by machines." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mmI2oN9zolQ

**Musical Evolution (1982)** : Initially, New Order sounded similar to Joy Division. However, a trip to New York, where they were exposed to post-disco, early house, and electro music, changed their direction. They became fascinated with synthesizers, drum machines, and sequencers, shifting by 1982 from Joy Division's guitar-based style to a more electronic and dance-oriented sound with Bernard Sumner fronting the band. "And by 1982, New Order had moved away from the guitar-based material that Joy Division had become famous for into something more electronic and dancing, all with Bernard out front." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=vLZla5h4xKQ
**The Haçienda Nightclub (1982–1997) – Millions Lost, Band Driven to Near-Bankruptcy** : In 1982, New Order were convinced to invest in building and running a nightclub in their Manchester hometown called the Haçienda. Having no knowledge of club management, they poured millions of pounds into the venture across 15 years, nearly bankrupting themselves. All their New Order and Joy Division album revenues were effectively swallowed by it. The club was culturally important as ground zero for the rave and dance culture explosion of the late 1980s, but was plagued by financial losses, security problems, and drug-related deaths (patrons preferring ecstasy over alcohol made the bar nearly unviable). It closed permanently in June 1997 and was converted to condominiums. Bassist Peter Hook wrote a book about the experience called *How Not to Run a Club*. "The profits of which were largely lost in the sinkhole that was the Haçienda nightclub." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=oljjY7jO-kM
