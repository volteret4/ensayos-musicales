## songs
**Blue Monday (1983) – Accidental Origin and Monumental Impact** : The song originated from New Order's reluctance to perform encores. They devised a plan to simply activate Stephen Morris's drum machine and let it play while they exited the stage to start drinking. To add interest for the audience, they incorporated a sequencer to trigger parts on Gillian Gilbert's keyboards. Barney Sumner then suggested sequencing one of Peter Hook's bass lines, which ultimately laid the foundation for a new song played entirely by machines. Legend suggests the band, under the influence of LSD, recorded the tracks in the studio, leading the studio engineer to send them to a cafe to finish mixing the song in peace due to their inebriated state. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8UIBWyUt38g
**Blue Monday (1983) – Commercial and Cultural Success** : "Blue Monday" was released solely as an 8-minute 12-inch single. While it received some radio play, its most significant impact was felt in dance clubs, where it stood out dramatically from contemporary tracks like Olivia Newton-John's "Physical" and Joan Jett's "I Love Rock 'n Roll." The song's influence was "staggering," establishing crucial connections between new music, rock, and dance music, and is credited by many for making dancing "cool again." It sold over 3 million copies and remains the best-selling 12-inch record in history. Despite its immense success, the elaborate artwork and cover production for the 12-inch single were so expensive that New Order lost one pence on each copy sold, resulting in a total loss of 30,000 pounds for the band and their record company from its release. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8UIBWyUt38g
**Run (1989)** : A 1990 release that was found to have copyrightable similarities to John Denver's "Leaving on a Jet Plane". The band did not fight the charge and reached an out-of-court settlement with Denver in 1990. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rfObVld9rIE
