## curiosities
**Collaboration with Stevie Wonder** : Stevie Wonder, seeking to record the music he envisioned after leaving Motown Records, contacted Malcolm and Cecil after hearing Tonto. Their collaboration on three pivotal albums—"Talking Book," "Innervisions," and "Fulfillingness' First Finale"—defined Stevie Wonder's greatest period in the 1970s. Many of his iconic songs, including "Superstition," "Living for the City," "Boogie On Reggae Woman," and "Higher Ground," were composed and recorded using Tonto. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6SdPPsmpgJg
**Influence on Quincy Jones and Electronic Music** : Quincy Jones, captivated by Tonto, applied what he learned from the instrument to his production of Michael Jackson's albums. This significantly broadened the attention and possibilities of electronically generated music, solidifying Tonto's influence on the sound of Black Pop music, rock music, and the overall use of the recording studio. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6SdPPsmpgJg
**Pioneers of Electronic Music and Tonto** : Malcolm Cecil, a British jazz musician working for the BBC, and Robert Marguliffe, an American record producer, were hugely influential, albeit behind-the-scenes, figures in electronic music. In the late 1960s, captivated by Bob Moog's synthesizers, they collaborated to build Tonto, the world's largest modular synthesizer setup. This massive instrument allowed them to create unprecedented sounds, drum tracks, bass lines, and multiple melodies simultaneously, which was revolutionary for its time. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6SdPPsmpgJg
