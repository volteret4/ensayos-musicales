**My Boy Lollipop (1964)** : Released in early 1964, this song became a massive international hit, reaching number two on the charts in both the UK and the US, launching Millie into stardom. Chris Blackwell chose this song, remembering it as a sound system favorite, and commissioned Ernest Ranglin to arrange it. The backing was performed by Jimmy Powell and the Five Dimensions, with a harmonica solo replacing the original saxophone solo to align with British chart trends; Millie consistently claimed Rod Stewart played this harmonica part. "My Boy Lollipop came out in early 1964 and became a massive hit, reaching number two on the chart both in the UK and the US, and Millie was now a star." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**My Boy Lollipop (Year not specified)** : This song became a worldwide hit, serving as the "big breakthrough" for Ska (SCOP/Blue Beat) music in Britain. Produced by Chris Blackwell and distributed through his new Island Records label, its success helped spread Jamaican music further into the mainstream and among white British youth, particularly mods. "The big breakthrough was a worldwide hit by Millie Small, called My Boy Lollipop, produced by Englishman Chris Blackwell, and distributed through his brand new Idle and Records label." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=V7WjNR1ujhk ← millie-small

## curiosities
**Decline in Success and Poverty (1960s-2000s)** : After the initial wave of Ska's popularity subsided, Millie struggled to replicate her early success despite numerous attempts and collaborations. She subsequently spent much of the following decades in poverty, never receiving significant financial benefits from her massive chart achievements. "Millie spent much of the next few decades in poverty." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**Early Life and Discovery (1960s)** : Millie Small started performing at the age of 12, winning Kingston's biggest talent contest, the Vere Johns' Opportunity Hour. At 14, in 1962, her voice, noted for its spooky resemblance to Shirley Goodman of Shirley and Lee, attracted the attention of producer Coxsone Dodd, leading to her first recordings. "Millie Small had started performing at the age of 12, when she won the Vier jumps opportunity hour, the single biggest talent contest in Kingston." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**First International Ska Star** : Millie Small became Ska's first international star following the success of "My Boy Lollipop" in 1964. Her career marked the first significant impact of a Jamaican artist on the music charts in both Britain and America. "a 14-year-old girl who would become Scar's first international star." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**Jamaican Honors (Later Life)** : Despite her financial struggles, Millie Small was recognized and honored multiple times by the people of Jamaica for her pioneering musical achievements, including being made a Commander in the Order of Distinction. "She was given multiple honors by the people of Jamaica, including being made a commander in the order of distinction, but never really got any financial benefit from her enormous child success." ← Episode 114： ＂My Boy Lollipop＂ by Millie | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kf-DtqoOsRI
**Later Life and Death (1970s-2019)** : Millie Small ceased performing after the early 1970s and maintained a private life, giving only one brief interview in 2016. Despite talk of a comeback in the early 1980s, she never returned to performing. She passed away last year at the age of 72.
