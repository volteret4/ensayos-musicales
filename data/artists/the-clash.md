## curiosities
**Attendance at Ramones London Gigs (July 1976)** : Members of the newly formed Clash, including Joe Strummer and Simo Jones, were transformed by seeing The Ramones perform in London on July 4th and 6th, 1976. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=qC0Ee4-dpnI
**Backstage Entry Anecdote (July 4, 1976)** : Joe Strummer and Simo Jones, along with some Sex Pistols members, gained backstage entry at the Ramones' Roundhouse gig by throwing a rock at Johnny Ramone's window and forming a human chain to be pulled inside. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=qC0Ee4-dpnI
**Covered by Weezer (pre-2018)** : The Clash's music has been playfully covered by Weezer during their tours. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yuA9Xor8vfw
**Early Band Names** : Before settling on The Clash, the group considered or used several other names, including The Psychotic Negatives, The Weak Heart Drops, The Outsiders, The Mirrors, and The Evening Standards. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=oPgeSCy93bo
**Early Skill Level** : When The Clash recorded their very first album in early 1977, Paul still really didn't know what he was doing. However, by the time the band recorded their third album, a little more than two years later, he had become proficient. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=95clNVd0hYg
**Formation (Post-101ers)** : The Clash was formed by Joe Strummer, who left The 101ers a few days after being inspired by the Sex Pistols' attitude and energy. He collaborated with "a couple of people who also thought that the Sex Pistols were great." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=oPgeSCy93bo
**Influence of Jamaican Music** : Paul Simonon absorbed the deep bass sounds he heard coming from the windows of new Jamaican immigrants, which he described as "fat and warm and had a groove unlike anything that came out of Britain." This influence shaped his playing style. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=95clNVd0hYg
**Influence on "New Wave of New Wave"** : Groups in the early 1990s "new wave of new wave" movement mined their material for inspiration. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9CNNsqXiL6U
**Interim Management (1978-1981)** : After Bernie Rhodes' initial departure in 1978, The Clash were managed by Caroline Coon, a painter and rock journalist, but she "didn't work out." The management company that succeeded her also proved unsuccessful, leading to Rhodes' return in 1981. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PzyvXmDKFUc
**Learning the Bass** : When Mick Jones offered to teach him guitar to join his new band, Paul tried but found guitar too hard, complaining, "This is too hard." Mick then suggested he get a bass, stating, "There's two less strings." Paul learned to play the bass on his own. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=95clNVd0hYg
**London Calling Album Cover** : The iconic cover of the 'London Calling' album features Paul Simonon smashing his bass. This is considered one of the most iconic rock photos ever. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=95clNVd0hYg
**Management by Bernie Rhodes (Initial & Return)** : Bernie Rhodes, a figure from the punk rock era, formed The Clash, viewing them as his own version of the Sex Pistols after his offer to co-manage the latter was rejected by Malcolm McLaren. Rhodes initially mentored the group, encouraging them to write about social and political issues. However, he was ousted by 1978 after negotiating a "very poor record deal" for the band. He returned to manage The Clash in 1981, alongside other bands, and during this second tenure, he notably convinced the band of the value of 12-inch dance remixes. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PzyvXmDKFUc
**Mick Jones' Departure** : Mick Jones left The Clash, subsequently forming Big Audio Dynamite. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OiqdZfxdBP0

**Paul Simonon's Artistic Background** : Paul Simonon initially wanted to be an artist and a painter. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=95clNVd0hYg

**Unique Playing Technique** : As time went on, Paul's reggae and ska-influenced rhythms set him apart from most other bass players. Unlike many bassists who plucked strings with their fingers, Paul tended to use a pick, which gave his Fender Precision bass a more percussive sound. He also preferred the warmth of big Ampeg amplifiers and cabinets. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=95clNVd0hYg
