## concerts
- **Dingwalls (July 6th, 1976, London)** : The newly formed Clash were present at this Ramones concert, further solidifying the inspiration that would shape their sound and role in the British punk movement. "this is the clash and this is the business and we needed to get in and so they kind of formed a sort of human chain and pulled us up through this window and that was the first time we met him and it was just a really great punk rock moment."
- **The 100 Club Punk Rock Festival (September 20th & 21st, 1976, London)** : The Clash performed at Britain's first official punk rock festival, an event considered a direct offshoot of the Ramones' influence and vital for coalescing the UK punk community. "when people heard about these shows featuring the sex pistols and the clash and the dammed and a bunch of others, the lines extended around the block."
- **The Roundhouse (July 4th, 1976, London)** : Members of the "newly formed clash" were in attendance at the Ramones' influential gig, where they were transformed by the performance. Joe Strummer specifically recalled sneaking backstage. "members of the newly formed clash"

**London Calling (1979) – Recording Anecdote** : In the summer of 1979, The Clash entered the studio to record this album with producer Guy Stevens. During a warm-up session where the band covered an old blues song by American singer Vince Taylor, Stevens secretly hit record. Despite the band's concerns that their performance was sloppy and the tempo was wrong, Stevens accepted the take, asserting that "all rock and roll speeds up." The resulting energy, as heard in tracks like "Brand New Cadillac," contrasts with modern, digitally corrected recordings. "The clash was bewildered. We can't use that. It was sloppy. The tempo was all wrong. We listened to how we speed up through the song." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7Zniri9uSlc

## songs
**Brand New Cadillac (1979)** : This song, featured on the "London Calling" album, serves as an example of The Clash's raw, uncorrected recording style from 1979. The band's tendency to play faster and faster as the song progressed, a natural imperfection, adds energy and was embraced by producer Guy Stevens, who stated, "all rock and roll speeds up." Such an organic tempo change would likely be "fixed" in modern production using tools like Pro Tools. "Did you notice how the band kept playing faster and faster throughout the song?" ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7Zniri9uSlc
**Career Opportunities, White Riot, Guns of Brixton (Various) – Rock as Journalism; Covered Capitalism, Class, War, Global Order** : Joe Strummer had the idea that rock music should be a form of journalism and education, and no subject was too big for the Clash. They tackled current events, capitalism, materialism, the state of the UK, the state of the US, and the global order. Their protest catalog included Career Opportunities, I'm So Bored With the USA, The Clampdown, Police and Thieves (recontextualized cover), White Riot, Know Your Rights, Lost in the Supermarket, Guns of Brixton, Washington Bullets, and many more. "They were so powerful and so influential that we still call them the only band that mattered." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dCelQ8Zj_cg
**Electronic Sound Effect in "Rock the Casbah" (1982)** : About two minutes into the song "Rock the Casbah," Mick Jones contributed a distinctive electronic sound effect. This sound was generated by an alarm on Jones's digital wristwatch, adding a unique element to the track. "Mick Jones contributed an electronic sound effect about two minutes into the song which came from an alarm on Mick's digital wristwatch." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dVJuITJyNTU
**Guns of Brixton** : Written entirely by Paul Simonon, this song integrated reggae and ska influences into the punk context — reflecting Simonon's Jamaican musical influences — and is one of The Clash's most celebrated tracks. **Rock the Casbah (1982) – Controversial Origins and Chart Success** : Originally conceived by drummer Topper Headon, who recorded most of its parts (piano, drums, bass) and wrote initial lyrics about missing his girlfriend, the song was transformed by Joe Strummer with new lyrics inspired by a news report about flogging for owning rock albums in Iran. Released as the second single on June 11, 1982, it achieved top 10 positions in Australia and the US, top 20 in Canada, and sold about 3 million copies worldwide, though its originality was later questioned by Rashid Taha. "Rock the Casbah from the combat rock album of 1982." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dVJuITJyNTU
**Rock the Casbah (1982) – Topper's Piano Riff, Strummer's Lyrics from Iranian Flogging News, Possible Algerian Influence** : Drummer Topper Headon came up with the piano boogie that forms the song's basis. Joe Strummer wrote the lyrics after hearing a news report that people in Iran were being flogged for owning rock albums. However, there may be a second origin. In 1981, after a Clash show in Paris, an Algerian singer-songwriter named Rachid Taha — leader of a French-Arab punk band called Carte de Séjour — handed Joe a cassette demo of his music on the street. Taha says he never heard back from Joe, but a year later he turned on the radio and heard "Rock the Casbah," which stylistically resembled much of what he had given Joe that night. Whether the song was inspired by Taha's demo remains unconfirmed; Taha never seemed bitter and even recorded his own cover version. "Was Rock the Casbah inspired by what was handed to Joe on the sidewalk that night in Paris? Well, maybe." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mmI2oN9zolQ
**Should I Stay or Should I Go (1982)** : This song is listed by Spotify as the "11th most popular song played by surgeons while they operate." The lyrics of the song were quoted directly in the transcript in conjunction with this statistic. "Should I stay or should I go now? If I go there, we'll look trouble. If I stay, it will be trouble. So you gotta let me know. Should I stay or should I go? The 11th most popular song played by surgeons while they operate, according to Spotify." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aSk1XQHNkd8
**Train in Vain (1979)** : Initially considered a "throwaway" song by The Clash, it was intended as a free flexi-disc insert for NME magazine. When the NME deal failed, it was hastily added as an unlisted bonus track to their *London Calling* album. Despite its inauspicious origins, "Train in Vain" unexpectedly became the band's most commercially successful track. "this song, which was initially a throwaway, ended up becoming the clash's most commercially successful track." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=B2g4QSREzpw

**Firing of Mick Jones (September 1, 1983) – Manager-Engineered Split That Destroyed the Band** : Manager Bernie Rhodes had spent years pitting Joe Strummer against Mick Jones, encouraging Joe to fire him. On September 1, 1983, Mick Jones's departure was announced in a formal "Clash Communiqué" stating he had "drifted apart from the original idea of the Clash." The resulting lineup recorded the critically panned *Cut the Crap* (1985), featuring two new members, after which the Clash simply dissolved with no official announcement. Mick Jones went on to major success with Big Audio Dynamite, while Strummer spent over a decade in record-company limbo and never again achieved the stature he had with the Clash. "Firing Mick Jones? Terrible. Joe Strummer thought he knew better than Mick, and he ended up paying for it." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=oljjY7jO-kM
**Hepatitis from Gobbing – 1979, Spat in Mouth During Performance** : In the early British punk scene, audience members competed to spit at bands on stage as a sign of enthusiasm following a newspaper article depicting the practice. During a 1979 Clash performance, Joe Strummer was singing with his mouth open when an audience member's spit landed directly in it. The result was a significant case of hepatitis. The practice of spitting at performers, known as "gobbing," was common enough that performers were routinely covered in phlegm and mucus during shows. "Joe Strummer was singing. His mouth was open and the result was a really nasty case of hepatitis." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7lpjI8F2Apk
