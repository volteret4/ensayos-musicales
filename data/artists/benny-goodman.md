The band members were on a plane from LA to Atlantic City when Hampton started humming the riff. ## curiosities
**Acquisition of Chick Webb's Arrangements** : Benny Goodman purchased arrangements from Chick Webb's band, specifically from Edgar Sampson, a saxophone player and arranger, for many of his own band's biggest crowd-pleasers. "Goodman bought from Egg, a Sampson, the saxophone player who did most of Webb's arrangements." ← Episode 4： ＂Choo Choo Ch'Boogie＂ by Louis Jordan | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DFZnwvsXGJM
**Big Band Leader** : Benny Goodman was recognized as a major band leader in the Big Band jazz scene. He directed large ensembles, and under the Big Band structure, all performers were subservient to the leader's artistic and musical authority. "These were orchestras of ten or more people led by a big name, Paul Whiteman, Tommy Dorsey, Benny Goodman, and so on." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=V7WjNR1ujhk ← benny-goodman
**Carnegie Hall Breakthrough** : In early 1938, John Hammond promoted a concert by the Benny Goodman band at Carnegie Hall. This event was impressive as it marked the first time an integrated band had played at Carnegie Hall and the first time popular music had been treated as seriously as classical music. "It was the first time an integrated band had played Carnegie Hall, and the first time that popular music had been treated as seriously as classical music." ← Episode 2： ＂Roll Em Pete＂ by Big Joe Turner | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=thpMb1A_fAs
**Carnegie Hall Integrated Performance (1938)** : By 1938, Goodman achieved a significant milestone by bringing an integrated band, which included black musicians like Count Basie, Leste Jung, Teddy Wilson, and Lionel Hampton, alongside white musicians such as himself and Crouper, onto the prestigious stage of Carnegie Hall, the US's most renowned music venue at the time. "By 1938, Goodman actually reached the point where he was able to bring an integrated band, featuring Count Basie, Leste Jung, Teddy Wilson and Lionel Hampton. Plus other black musicians, along with white musicians such as Goodman and Crouper, onto the stage of Carnegie Hall, at the time the US's most prestigious music venue." ← Episode 1： ＂Flying Home＂, Benny Goodman, Charlie Christian, and Lionel Hampton | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RmfeJYl84Qs
**Classical Music Pioneer** : Goodman was one of the first individuals to advocate for the mainstream acceptance of jazz music within the classical music world. This effort, aimed at integrating jazz into a conservative environment, co-existed with his equally radical push for the integration of musicians in his bands. "On the one hand, he was one of the first people to push for mainstream acceptance of jazz music in the classical music world, which would suggest trying to be as conservative as possible." ← Episode 1： ＂Flying Home＂, Benny Goodman, Charlie Christian, and Lionel Hampton | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RmfeJYl84Qs
**Flying Home as Prototypical Rock** : The Benny Goodman Sextet's 1939 recording of "Flying Home" is described as almost a prototypical rock band, featuring piano, electric guitar, double bass, and drums—instruments that would become hallmarks of the rock genre 20 years later. It anticipated many aspects of rock, especially Charlie Christian's soloing, which was astonishingly advanced for its time. "Other than the vibraphone in clarinet, though, this small group was almost a prototypical rock band." ← Episode 1： ＂Flying Home＂, Benny Goodman, Charlie Christian, and Lionel Hampton | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RmfeJYl84Qs

**Pioneer of Integrated Bands** : Goodman was one of the first band leaders to lead an integrated band during the segregation era, starting with a trio comprising himself (white and Jewish), drummer Dramagine Cooper (white), and pianist Teddy Wilson (black). This initiative, along with recruiting black arranger Fletcher Henderson, was largely spearheaded by his brother-in-law, John Hammond, a wealthy man dedicated to racial integration. "And importantly, Goodman was one of the first band leaders to lead an integrated band during the segregation era." ← Episode 1： ＂Flying Home＂, Benny Goodman, Charlie Christian, and Lionel Hampton | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RmfeJYl84Qs
**Progressive Stance on Integration** : Goodman’s inclusion of black musicians, notably Teddy Wilson when Goodman was arguably the most popular musician in the USA, was a genuinely progressive statement for his time. Colonel Hampton later quoted Goodman as saying, "we need both the Black keys and the White keys to play music," an assertion made 80 years ago that predates similar well-meaning liberal comparisons. "So Goodman saying it 80 years ago, there's a genuinely progressive statement for the time." ← Episode 1： ＂Flying Home＂, Benny Goodman, Charlie Christian, and Lionel Hampton | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RmfeJYl84Qs
**Rivalry with Chick Webb** : Benny Goodman's band engaged in a notable rivalry with Chick Webb's band, culminating in a 1937 cutting contest at the Savoy Ballroom where Webb's band won. **Sextet Formation (1939)** : After Teddy Wilson and Dramagine Cooper departed in 1939, Goodman formed the Benny Goodman Sextet to compensate for the loss of these exceptional musicians. This rotating lineup revolved around three primary soloists: Goodman on clarinet, Hampton on vibraphone, and a new, highly influential addition, guitarist Charlie Christian. His support was instrumental in Moondog winning the case and securing damages and a promise from Freed to cease using the name.
