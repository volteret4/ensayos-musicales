**Dog Man Star (1994) - Second Revolution** : Released rapidly after their debut, this album represented a "second revolution" for the band, featuring more lineup changes. It was described as a "completely revolutionary act" because Suede intentionally defied the formula that brought them initial success, opting for a much darker, deeper, and intimate sound with "very minority and sometimes scandalous lyrics." Despite not achieving the sales peak of its predecessor, it was critically adored, considered "one of the master albums of the 90s." The band aimed to avoid being a "mass consumption object," aspiring to the artistic depth of David Bowie's Berlin Trilogy rather than the mass appeal of Ziggy Stardust. The recording process was complicated by the mysterious departure of Bernard Butler, a key musical figure from their first album, and issues with producer Ed Buller. The band remained very proud of the result, which is now considered a masterpiece.
