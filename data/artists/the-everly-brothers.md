## curiosities
**"Speed-Fueled" Breakup (1973)** : The Everly Brothers, Don and Phil, experienced a significant fallout, leading to a "speed-fueled" breakup in 1973. Following this event, they spoke to each other only once in the subsequent 10 years, which occurred at their father's funeral. "The Everly Brothers, Don and Phil, did not get along. After a speed-fueled breakup in 1973, they talked to each other just once in the following 10 years, and that was at their father's funeral." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5bB7GAUM63Y ← the-everly-brothers
**Acuff Rose Publishing Arrangement** : The Everly Brothers had a deal with the publishing company Acuff Rose, stipulating they would only perform songs published by that company. This created a conflict when Roy Orbison, whose songs were published by Sam Phillips, pitched "Claudette" to them. "They had a deal with the publishing company Acuff Rose that they would only perform songs that were published by that company, and Orbison had a contract with Sam Phillips that meant that Orbison's songs were all published by Phillips." ← Episode 42： ＂Ooby Dooby＂ by Roy Orbison and the Teen Kings | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1zvzpXqr-k8
**Popular with British Teenagers** : British teenagers were listening to their American records, which contrasted with the British commercial rock and roll industry. Harry Webb (Cliff Richard) had a particular taste for smoother American soft rockers like them. "British teenagers were instead listening to American records by Buddy Holly or Little Richard, the Avley Brothers or Elvis." ← Episode 70： ＂Move It＂ by Cliff Richard and the Drifters | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uoEWP94f6S4
**Rockabilly Classification Debate** : The Everly Brothers, appearing in 1957 as country-influenced white rock and rollers, are often termed rockabilly. However, enthusiasts of the genre frequently disregard this classification, asserting that their sound lacks the defining characteristics of music originating from Sun Studios. "And those singers will often get referred to as rockabilly. They don't tend to get counted by aficionados of the subjumper, who think they don't sound enough like the music from sun to count." ← Episode 63： ＂Susie Q＂, by Dale Hawkins | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4vqj1DcXOfw
