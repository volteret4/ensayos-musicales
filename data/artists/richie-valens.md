## curiosities
**"Little Richard of the Valley" Nickname** : Before his career direction was shaped by Bob Keane, Valens was primarily perceived as an R&B singer. This perception earned him the nickname "the Little Richard of the Valley," highlighting his vocal and performance style's similarity to the renowned R&B artist. "he was being referred to as the little Richard of the Valley." ← Episode 73： ＂La Bamba＂ by Ritchie Valens | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1v9iSCWnLsc
**Appearance in "Go Johnny Go" Film** : Richie Valens featured in the 1959 Alan Freed film "Go Johnny Go," where he performed his song "Ooh My Head" and appeared in several other scenes. However, he was given no spoken lines, as the film's structure stipulated that musical performers were compensated solely as a form of promotion, while anyone delivering dialogue was classified as an actor and required separate payment. The film also showcased other notable artists including Chuck Berry, Eddie Cochran, The Flamingos, and Jackie Wilson. "Valens performed Oomai Head, and also appeared in several scenes of the film, but had no lines as the musical performers were being paid, as the film was considered to be promotion for them, while anyone who had a line was considered an actor and had to be paid." ← Episode 73： ＂La Bamba＂ by Ritchie Valens | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1v9iSCWnLsc
**Death in Plane Crash (1959)** : Richie Valens died in a plane crash on February 3, 1959, along with Buddy Holly and The Big Bopper, while traveling during the "Winter Dance Party" tour. He had been suffering from the flu during the miserable tour conditions and took Buddy Holly's chartered plane for comfort. "You're likely also to know that Richie Valens and the Big Bopper died in the same crash, even if you don't know any of the songs that either of those two artists recorded." ← Episode 74： ＂It Doesn't Matter Any More＂ by Buddy Holly | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6unib5T2mDQ
**Delayed Audition with Bob Keane** : Valens' initial audition with Bob Keane was postponed because, at the time Machia arrived to take him, Valens was at home minding his siblings. **Early Unpopularity and Appearance** : Richie Valens experienced some unpopularity among other Mexican Americans at his school, partly due to his physical appearance. He was notably light-skinned, which played into existing colorism among Latino youths in the area, and was also described as quite fat. Additionally, his willingness to associate with individuals of other races, including Black and White friends, set him apart from some of his peers. "Some of this was to do with his appearance. He was notably light-skinned from Mexican American, and apparently there was a level of colorism among Latino kids in that area at that time, and he was also quite fat." ← Episode 73： ＂La Bamba＂ by Ritchie Valens | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1v9iSCWnLsc
**Guitar Playing Limitations and Workarounds** : Despite his natural talent and passion for the guitar, Valens was not a technically proficient guitarist when he began recording. He was limited to playing in only a few keys, mainly D or A, and typically used only three or four chords. When a song required the key of D flat, he would manually tune his guitar down a half step as he didn't know the chords for that key. On another occasion, he expressed frustration when trying to guide a bass player for a session, as the low D part he heard in his head was beyond the bass guitar's lowest E note. "Valencia was not a particularly profession guitarist. He had a lot of natural skill and a love of the instrument, but when he first started recording, he could only play in a handful of keys." ← Episode 73： ＂La Bamba＂ by Ritchie Valens | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1v9iSCWnLsc

**Improvisational Recording Technique** : Due to Valens' spontaneous performing style, most of his completed recordings were laboriously compiled by Bob Keane from numerous takes. Stan Ross, co-owner of Gold Star Studios, vividly described these heavily spliced tapes as appearing as if they had been "through World War XIV." Valens typically entered the studio with only a basic melody idea and a few lyrical fragments, then improvised multiple variations of the song, from which the best segments were edited together to form the final recording. "most of the recordings he completed were pulled together by Bob Keane from multiple takes." ← Episode 73： ＂La Bamba＂ by Ritchie Valens | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1v9iSCWnLsc

**Lyrical and Musical Spontaneity** : Members of The Silhouette frequently observed Valens' stage performances, where he would begin playing a song, such as Little Richard's "Jenny Jenny," but after only a few lines, he would start improvising entirely new melodies and lyrics that were unique to each performance. This spontaneous approach supports Bob Keane's assertion that Valens typically brought only a four or eight-bar riff and a few lines of lyrics to sessions, improvising the rest for Keane to later shape and structure. "he would start out playing, for example, Jenny Jenny by little Richard, but after a few lines he would start improvising his own new melody and lyrics, which would be different every time." ← Episode 73： ＂La Bamba＂ by Ritchie Valens | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1v9iSCWnLsc
