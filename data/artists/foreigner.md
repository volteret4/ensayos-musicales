**Urgent (1981)** : The album's first single, where Mut Lang worked with Mick Jones to perfect the interplay of twangy guitars and ghostly synth riffs. Lang brought in legendary Motown saxophonist Junior Walker for a "smoking solo," painstakingly piecing it together from multiple takes. The song reached number four on the Hot 100 in the late summer of 1981, helping send the "4" album to number one. "On urgent, the album's first single, Mutt worked with Mick Jones to perfect the interplay of the Twangie guitars and ghostly synth riffs." ← Hit Parade Music History and Music Trivia > Pour Some Sugar on Me Edition Part 1 | https://shows.acast.com/hit-parade/episodes/695d4ef5d1ba84fb8f04493d
**Waiting For A Girl Like You (1981-1982)** : This song famously spent nine weeks stuck at the number two spot on the charts, blocked from reaching number one by Olivia Newton-John's dominant hit "Physical." "For nine weeks, foreigners waiting for a girl like you hung out at the number two spot blocked by physical." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 40 - Honestly Loving Olivia Newton-John | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-40-honestly-loving-olivia-newton-john
**Waiting for a Girl Like You (1981)** : This power ballad, the meticulous follow-up single to "Urgent," featured Lou Gramm's aching vocal complementing dreamy keyboards played and arranged by session player Thomas Dolby, whose career Mut effectively launched. It spent ten weeks stuck at number two on the Hot 100, parked behind Olivia Newton-John's "Physical" and Darryl Hall and John Oates' "I Can't Go For That," setting a Hot 100 record for the longest-running number two hit without ever reaching number one (tied with Missy Elliott's "Work It"). "Waiting for a girl like you was heartbreaking in more ways than one." ← Hit Parade Music History and Music Trivia > Pour Some Sugar on Me Edition Part 1 | https://shows.acast.com/hit-parade/episodes/695d4ef5d1ba84fb8f04493d

## curiosities
**"Hit-Making Machine" Designation** : From the outset, Foreigner demonstrated an ability to consistently produce hit songs, solidifying their reputation as a "hit-making machine." "They proved right out of the gate that they were going to be a hit-making machine." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 58 - WKRP in Cincinnati | https://www.ftr70.com
**Audience Appreciation for Polish** : Despite critical assessments, the polished sound that critics dubbed "Corporate Rock" was precisely what Foreigner's audience sought and appreciated in their music. "But to the band's audience, that polish was the whole point." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 58 - WKRP in Cincinnati | https://www.ftr70.com
**Critical Labeling as "Corporate Rock"** : Critics frequently referred to Foreigner as "Corporate Rock," alleging that the band prioritized slick production and commercially viable choruses over artistic innovation and risk-taking. "Reviewers often called Forerner Corporate Rock, they accused the band of making it a priority to have slick production and big choruses over taking any sort of artistic risk." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 58 - WKRP in Cincinnati | https://www.ftr70.com
**End of Collaboration with Mut Lang** : After "4" and ACDC's "For Those About to Rock," Mut Lang moved on from Foreigner, never producing another LP for the group again. **Pre-Mutt Success** : In the late 1970s, Foreigner had already achieved three platinum, top-five LPs filled with strutting arena rock jams, but had never reached number one on the album chart until Mut Lang's involvement. **Radio-Friendly and Arena-Ready Sound** : The band's musical style blended British rock polish with strong song hooks, making their music perfectly suited for both radio play and selling out large arenas across the United States.
