## songs
**Back Stabbers (1972)** : This song marked a significant milestone for The O'Jays, becoming their first number one R&B single in 1972. It is characteristic of the "Philadelphia sound" produced by Kenny Gamble and Leon Huff, evident in its prominent use of strings. Before this breakout hit, The O'Jays had a long history performing on the "Chitlin circuit" in the South. Scholars like Alice Eccles suggest that while the song can be interpreted as relationship betrayal, it also carries a deeper political commentary, possibly echoing Dr. King's sentiments about white moderates and reflecting a broader theme of betrayal within the Black Power movement. "Backstabbers was the first number one R&B single for the OJs who had been playing what was called the Chitlin circuit of the South for a long time before that song took off in 1972." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 22 - Power to the People - The Music of the Black Power Movement | https://seventies.libsyn.com/022-power-to-the-people-62520-11-01-am ← the-ojays
**Backstabbers (1972)** : The inaugural smash hit for PIR, this song was not interpreted as a failed romantic relationship, but rather as a commentary on the systemic treatment of Black men and women in America, a theme Greil Marcus characterized as "music of worry and confinement." "Backstabbers is not really about a relationship gone bad. It's about how black men and women were treated in America." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 37 - Philly Soul and the Sound of the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-37-philly-soul-and-the-sound-of-the-70s
**For the Love of Money (1974)** : Featured on the *Ship Ahoy* album, this song is frequently misconstrued as an endorsement of wealth, but it is in fact a "funkified warning" about the corrupting influence of greed and an appeal to prevent money from altering one's character, with its lyrics vividly depicting human depravity driven by avarice. ## curiosities
**Eddie Levert's Stance on Message Songs** : Initially, Eddie Levert and The O'Jays favored enjoyable, high-energy songs over tracks with explicit messages. However, Gamble and Huff were resolute in their commitment to incorporating social statements into their music, believing that songwriting should inherently convey a part of the artist's soul. "Eddie Levert of the OJs said at first they did not want to do songs. They had a message. They wanted something fun. They wanted to do something that had some kick to it." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 37 - Philly Soul and the Sound of the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-37-philly-soul-and-the-sound-of-the-70s
**From Chitlin Circuit to Chart-Toppers** : Prior to the success of "Back Stabbers" in 1972, The O'Jays had a long and extensive career performing on the "Chitlin circuit" across the American South. This network of venues was historically vital for black entertainers during segregation, and their eventual mainstream success marked a significant transition from a regional touring act to national prominence. "Backstabbers was the first number one R&B single for the OJs who had been playing what was called the Chitlin circuit of the South for a long time before that song took off in 1972." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 22 - Power to the People - The Music of the Black Power Movement | https://seventies.libsyn.com/022-power-to-the-people-62520-11-01-am ← the-ojays
**Initial Industry Underwhelm** : Despite Kenny Gamble and Leon Huff's high regard for The O'Jays, particularly Eddie Levert's vocal talents, the music industry at large initially showed little enthusiasm for PIR's signing of the group, with the event largely going unnoticed. "the music industry was entirely underwhelmed by PIR signing this group, so approximately nobody paid any attention to this signing at all." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 37 - Philly Soul and the Sound of the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-37-philly-soul-and-the-sound-of-the-70s
**Philadelphia Sound Pioneers** : The O'Jays' "Back Stabbers" is a prime example of the "Philadelphia sound," characterized by its lush string arrangements, a signature of producers Kenny Gamble and Leon Huff. This sound distinguished their work and became highly influential in soul music. "That is also the Philadelphia sound of Kenny Gamble and Leon Huff you can just hear it with those strings." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 22 - Power to the People - The Music of the Black Power Movement | https://seventies.libsyn.com/022-power-to-the-people-62520-11-01-am ← the-ojays
**Pre-PIR Career History** : Before signing with Philadelphia International Records in 1971, The O'Jays had a protracted and challenging career, performing extensively on the Chitlin' circuit for years, recording for 20 different labels, and accumulating over 30 years of combined experience (shared with Harold Melvin & The Blue Notes) without achieving a single Top 40 hit; they were also dissatisfied with the artistic direction imposed by their previous record label. "The OJs were hardly new kids on the block. They had played the Chitlin circuit for years and were not happy with the direction their record label was taking them." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 37 - Philly Soul and the Sound of the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-37-philly-soul-and-the-sound-of-the-70s
