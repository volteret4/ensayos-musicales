## albums
**Hysteria (1987)** : Released in the summer of 1987, this album was an ambitious follow-up to *Pyromania*, with the band aiming for it to be even better. It took approximately two and a half years to complete, with the band initially believing they had 11 perfect songs. However, legendary producer Mutt Lange insisted on crafting an additional song, "Pour Some Sugar On Me," which significantly shaped the album's commercial success. The band aimed for it to be a commercially successful album, akin to a "greatest hits" collection, while also appealing to fans of artists like Elton John and maintaining their rock base. ## songs
**Pour Some Sugar On Me (1987)** : The initial idea for this song emerged in 1986 when Joe Elliott, alone in the studio, began playing an acoustic guitar and singing a hook. Producer Mutt Lange heard it and insisted it be developed into a song for the *Hysteria* album, despite the band having already worked for over two years. Lange quickly programmed a drum machine, added bass and chords, and Elliott sang the chorus for a "crappy demo." Phil Collen later contributed an iconic guitar riff for the verse. The song was the quickest to produce, taking only ten days, and evolved from an initially slower vocal melody (compared to The Beatles' "Help!") to a doubled-up, mid-tempo dance track popular in clubs, including among strippers. Released in Summer 1987, the single hit number two the following summer. Joe Elliott's lyrics, which he described as "nonsensical but it's got a fanatic power to it," were partly inspired by the Run-DMC and Aerosmith collaboration on "Walk This Way." Elliott also considered it "singer songwriter stuff" akin to Neil Sedaka, Carol King, or Burt Bacharach, aiming for a more mature sound. "Paulson Sugarmay's initial seed was 1986 and we the band thought we had the album pretty much in the can." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 54 - Into the 80s! **"Heavy Metal for People Who Think" Image** : In the summer of 1983, a Grand Junction, Colorado, newspaper headline humorously dubbed Def Leppard "heavy metal for people who think," implying their sound was "safe, not scary like Black Sabbath." This image was reinforced by their "squeaky clean" appearance, featuring nicely tailored clothes and neatly styled 80s hair, which lead singer Joe Elliott attributed to their working-class upbringing and proud parents' emphasis on self-respect. Chris Berkett spent six weeks programming all the drums on a Fairlight CMI. This pre-MIDI process required converting the drummer's demo tapes into musical score and then typing every single event into the monophonic Fairlight using a mathematical music composition language. **Joe Elliott on Band's Image** : In a 1983 interview, lead singer Joe Elliott, with a charming northern English accent, stated, "that's just the way we are." He expressed that the band "never wanted to look like tramps," criticizing groups like Motorhead for appearing in "car mechanic overalls with unwashed hair," which he considered "abusing the audience." Elliott believed their "squeaky clean image" stemmed from their working-class roots and parents who instilled self-respect, ensuring they avoided looking disheveled. "He never wanted to look like tramps. Some of these bands like Motorhead just sort of turn up in their car mechanic overalls with unwashed hair. **Meticulous Drum Production on "Pyromania"** : The drum programming for "Pyromania" was an incredibly meticulous process. Mutt Lange would spend half a day listening to a single hi-hat sound against a click track, checking if it was "a bit ahead, a bit behind," before approving it. "We would listen literally to a hi hat against the click for half a day." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mjQBaX_3oL4 ← def-leppard ← def-leppard
**Rejection of Heavy Metal Label** : Def Leppard actively disliked being categorized as "heavy metal," fearing it would negatively impact their record sales. Joe Elliott specifically expressed displeasure at the music media lumping them in with bands like Motley Crue or Warrant, underscoring their desire to appeal to a broader audience beyond the traditional heavy metal fan base. "But in 1983 the band's image was such that they did not even want the heavy metal label for fear it would negatively affect record sales." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 54 - Into the 80s! **Repeated Drum Recording Due to Tape Wear on "Pyromania"** : The drums for "Pyromania" had to be re-recorded three separate times from the original Fairlight floppy disks. This was because the analog 24-track master tapes, on which guitars and vocals were overdubbed, wore out through repeated transfers to new tapes, forcing the producer to request "first generation drums" again. "We've worn out three tapes doing all the guitars and the vocals. And they transferred... It wasn't digital in those days. It was analog. They transferred 24 track onto another new one because it was getting thin." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mjQBaX_3oL4 ← def-leppard ← def-leppard

This event stands in stark contrast to Joe Elliott's earlier comments about the band's "squeaky clean image" and self-respect.
