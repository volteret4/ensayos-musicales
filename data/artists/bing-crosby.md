**Funding of Magnetic Tape Recorder (1940s)** : To solve the problem of broadcasting his radio show twice (once for the East Coast and three hours later for the West Coast), Bing Crosby invested $50,000 (over $600,000 in modern money) in Major Jack Mullin's magnetic tape machine. This investment allowed Mullin to perfect the Ampex 200 reel-to-reel tape recorder, which revolutionized radio broadcasting and music recording by enabling editing and time-shifting. "Bing was so interested in the new machine that he dropped $50,000 or more than $600,000 in today's money, so Mullen could perfect his machine." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=V7WjNR1ujhk ← bing-crosby
**Inventor of Pro Celebrity Golf Tournament** : The time saved by using the reel-to-reel tape machine for his radio broadcasts, which freed Bing Crosby to pursue golfing, eventually inspired him to create the pro celebrity golf tournament. This made him an early figure in using celebrity status for fundraising. "he was able to invent the pro celebrity golf tournament, turning him into the original fundraising pop singer." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=V7WjNR1ujhk ← bing-crosby
**Investment in Ampex and Tape Recording** : Bing Crosby became an investor in Ampex, an electronic firm that manufactured reel-to-reel machines based on Jack Mullin's German technology. Crosby was motivated by his dislike for having to repeat his national radio show for the West Coast time zone and saw tape recording as a solution to pre-tape his East Coast broadcast. His investment was crucial in developing modern studio techniques. **Investment in Magnetic Tape Technology** : Bing Crosby, a notable crooner, invested a significant amount of his own money to bring magnetic tape technology to the commercial market. **Microphone's Impact on Vocal Style (Early 20th Century)** : Bing Crosby was among the first singers to recognize and exploit the microphone's potential. Prior to its invention, singers needed naturally powerful voices to project over an orchestra. Crosby realized the microphone allowed for a new, "close and personal" singing mood, fundamentally changing vocal performance. "But when the microphone came along, certain singers like Bing Crosby realized that you could use it to create a whole new mood for singing by getting a close and personal." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0fQQubt9UTI ← bing-crosby

**Pioneer of Magnetic Tape Recording in Radio** : Bing Crosby, a major radio star, was instrumental in popularizing magnetic tape recording in the US. Hating to perform two live shows daily for different time zones, his musical director, Merton Mackenzie, introduced him to Jack Mullen's German Magnetophone machines in 1947. Impressed by the sound quality, Crosby invested $50,000 (over half a million today) in Ampex, a recording technology company, and hired Mullen as his chief engineer. This allowed him to pre-record his shows, freeing him to pursue other activities like golfing, and ultimately set the standard for audio and video recording on tape. "This technology meant that he could record his East Coast performance, leave the studio, and then hit the links and let the technicians with their tape machines take care of the West Coast feed." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=NAroxFbYkRw ← bing-crosby
**Transition from Big Band Singer to Solo Star (1942)** : Bing Crosby, a former employee singer in a big band, became a major solo radio star due to a loophole in the American Federation of Musicians strike of 1942. As singers were not considered "musicians" by the union, they were exempt from the strike and free to launch independent careers, a new phenomenon at the time. "Bing Crosby had once been a big band employee, but was set free as a result of the musician strike in 1942." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=V7WjNR1ujhk ← bing-crosby
**Vocal Style Avoiding Malisma** : Bing Crosby is mentioned as a prominent singer from the early 20th century who exemplified the vocal style of hitting notes "dead arm," singing one note per syllable without ornamentation, a widely accepted standard of good singing in that era. "If you listen to any of the great voices of the first part of the 20th century, Sinatra, Bing Crosby, Tony Bennett, they will almost without exception hit the note dead arm, one note per syllable." ← Episode 32： ＂I Got A Woman＂ by Ray Charles | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0oeI9f2x_fo
