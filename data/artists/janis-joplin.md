## curiosities
**"One of the Guys" Perception** : Producer Paul Rothschild described Janis as "one of the guys," noting that her male balance was as strong as his female balance. However, Joe McDonald, former lead singer of Country Joe and the Fish and Janis's former partner, strongly disagreed, stating, "Sexism killed her. People kept saying that she was just one of the guys. That's a real sexist bullshit trip because that was fucking her head around." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 7 - Women Who Rocked the 70s | http://www.ftr70.com
**Authenticity and Presence on Stage** : Unlike some artists who performed under the influence, Janis was always present and engaged during her shows, pouring everything into her performances. She was genuinely happy with her musical transition from psychedelic rock towards a bluesier sound. "She was present and she was happy and she was happy with the transition that her music was taking her even though it was taking her away from the psychedelic rock and more toward the bluesy rock." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 7 - Women Who Rocked the 70s | http://www.ftr70.com
**Blues of Disappointment** : Janis's blues style differed from the traditional blues of lack often sung by Black blues artists, focusing instead on personal disappointment. This led to an ironic dynamic where she entertained many male fans by singing about her disappointments in them. "The blues that Janice sang about was a blues of disappointment and not the traditional blues of lack that black blues artists sang about." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 7 - Women Who Rocked the 70s | http://www.ftr70.com
**Chelsea Hotel Guest** : Janis Joplin was mentioned as one of the musicians who stayed at the Chelsea Hotel. **Engagement with Audience** : At the Louisville concert, Janis responded to a fan's request to "try a little bit harder" by yelling back, "Hey, I'm doing my part." Later, she jumped off stage to dance with a young man, causing the crowd to surge forward. When security guards pushed back, she interjected, "I permit them to dance. ... in fact, I demand it." "Janice jumps off the stage and starts to dance with a young guy near the front and now the crowd is going bonkers." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 7 - Women Who Rocked the 70s | http://www.ftr70.com
**Festival Express Performance - Toronto (June 27, 1970)** : Janis Joplin delivered an electrifying performance in Toronto, described by Rolling Stone writers David Dalton and Jonathan Cott as a "Bacchanalian little red riding hood with her bag full of tequila and lemons lurching from car to car like some tropical bird with streaming feathers." Canadian writer Bill Damper noted that her one-hour set changed the value of the $16 admission, generating enough personality and electricity to light up the stadium. "It was worth that much and more to see in here Janice Joplin, Long Haired, Swivel Hipped, Janice Joplin singing with the insistence and power of a pile driver and generating enough personality and electricity to light up a stadium all by herself." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 59 - Bands and Booze on the Festival Express of 1970 | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Heroin Overdose Death (October 1970)** : Janis Joplin tragically died of a heroin overdose three months after her participation in the Festival Express tour. **Jamming with Rick Danko** : Film footage from the Festival Express captures a very intoxicated Janis Joplin jamming with an equally intoxicated Rick Danko from The Band on guitar. **Live-in-the-Moment Philosophy** : Janis exuded a philosophy of living fully in the present, which Joe McDonald believed stemmed from a perceived need to compete and outdo men in typically masculine behaviors like drinking and performing. She encouraged seizing the day, as tomorrow is never guaranteed. "If you got a cap for one day, man, I mean, say maybe you want a cap for 365 days, right? You ain't got it for 365 days. You got it for one day, man. Well, I'll tell you that one day, man, better be your life, man." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 7 - Women Who Rocked the 70s | http://www.ftr70.com
**Louisville Concert Reception (June 12, 1970)** : During a show at Freedom Hall in Louisville, Kentucky, a conservative southern town, Janis initially saw "country club chicks" in the front rows and expressed her frustration. Despite a slow start, her performance led the crowd to go "bonkers," with dancing and pushing toward the stage, causing friction with security guards. "Janice looked out on the crowd and she didn't see what she might call her people in the front row and she said, shit, man, why do those country club chicks and their panty girdles always have to be sitting in the front rows?" ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 7 - Women Who Rocked the 70s | http://www.ftr70.com

**Political Provocation Contrast** : Janis Joplin is presented as an example of an artist who was a provocateur with clear political, social, and ideological ideals, notably advocating for the hippie philosophy. Her defined stance is contrasted with Jim Morrison, whose provocations were often gratuitous and lacked a distinct political platform. **Potential Matriarch of Rock** : Her untimely death prevented her from becoming a "70s something matriarch of rock," which the speaker suggests might have led to women in rock being less of a novelty. Her voice as a person, beyond her musical talent, was a significant loss. "What we lost with Janice was not just the musical voice but her voice as a person." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 7 - Women Who Rocked the 70s | http://www.ftr70.com
**Sexual Freedom Advocate in the 60s** : She was married to a man and never declared herself lesbian, but she always fiercely defended sexual freedom. She openly admitted to having been with many women and men, enjoying her sexual freedom, and many of her passionate, gut-wrenching songs are dedicated to women. This was challenging in the 1960s when music, especially rock, was male-dominated, and while homosexual culture existed in art, women were denied recognition and visibility in media.
