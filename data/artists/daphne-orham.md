## curiosities
**BBC Radiophonic Workshop and Independent Studio** : She successfully advocated for the BBC to establish an electronic workshop, which became the BBC Radiophonic Workshop. Upon leaving the BBC in 1959, she founded her own studio, dedicated to the creation of new sounds, further advancing her work in electronic music. "Once she convinced the BBC to establish an electronic workshop, they called it the BBC Radiophonic Workshop, more experimentation followed." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XjQiZqpmQlE ← daphne-orham ← daphne-orham
**First All-Electronic Score** : In 1957, Daphne Oram composed the soundtrack for the play *Amphitrium 38*, which is recognized as the first-ever entirely electronic score for any production. "In 1957, she created the soundtrack for a play called Amphitrium 38, which was the first ever all electronic score for anything." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XjQiZqpmQlE ← daphne-orham ← daphne-orham
**James Bond Electronic Sounds** : Daphne Oram was responsible for creating the distinctive electronic sounds heard in three James Bond movies: *Dr. No*, *From Russia with Love*, and *Goldfinger*. "For example, the electronic sounds you hear in three James Bond movies, Dr. No from Russia with Love and Goldfinger, were all created by Daphne Orham." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XjQiZqpmQlE ← daphne-orham ← daphne-orham
**Junior Studio Engineer at BBC** : In 1942, Daphne Oram became a junior studio engineer at the BBC, where her initial responsibility involved ensuring continuous music broadcasts by seamlessly switching to recorded music during potential Nazi bombing raids, serving as both a morale booster and a psychological tactic. "Her first job was to make sure that music broadcasts continued should things be interrupted by Nazi bombing runs." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XjQiZqpmQlE ← daphne-orham ← daphne-orham
**Pioneering Electronic Composition** : After World War II, Oram deeply explored composing with synthetic sounds, utilizing esoteric audio equipment like sine wave oscillators combined with magnetic tape recorders, as synthesizers had not yet been invented. Her studio was described as resembling something from a science fiction movie. "So she used things like sine wave oscillators combined with magnetic tape recorders.
