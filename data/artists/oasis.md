## albums
**(What's the Story) Morning Glory? (1990s)** : Described as the biggest British album of the 1990s, selling over 10 million copies worldwide. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RKvFJMkZdBE
**(What's the Story) Morning Glory? (1995)** : The band's second album, released on schedule on October 2, 1995. On its release day, it sold at a rate of "1 every 2 minutes." Within a week, it sold 350,000 copies, becoming the fastest-selling British album ever to that point. It "tuteled along selling 200,000 copies a week" in America, contrasting with Blur's lower chart performance there. This album made Oasis "the biggest band on Planet Earth" for a time. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=vzyMSSlD7Yg
**Be Here Now (1997)** : The band's third album, which served as "the first concrete indication that something wasn't right" with Britpop's decline. Its production was marred by "astronomical" alcohol and cocaine budgets. The songs were criticized for being "too long," the melodies "uninspired," and the record "too thick with unnecessary layers of guitars," sounding "kind of shrill to a lot of people" due to cocaine's effect on high-frequency hearing. Due to the band's immense success, "no one willing to tell Oasis to go back and do anything again," allowing Noel Gallagher to proceed as he saw fit. Noel Gallagher later admitted, "I wasn't prepared to make things any better. I'd get to a certain point and just say, screw it, that's good enough. We made a record to justify the drug habit. I was making the record to justify spending thousands on drugs." In 2015, Noel declined an opportunity to fix the record for a deluxe reissue, stating, "screw it, I'll just butcher it. Let's leave it alone. Actually, I kind of like the way it is anyway." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=vzyMSSlD7Yg
**Be Here Now (Year not specified)** : The follow-up album to "(What's the Story) Morning Glory?", heavily criticized by Noel Gallagher due to its recording process. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RKvFJMkZdBE
**Definitely Maybe (1994)** : The band's debut album, released on August 30, 1994, was a "sensation," hitting stores just as Blur's *Park Life* was at its peak. It presented a sound that felt "extremely familiar," drawing comparisons to The Beatles and The Rolling Stones. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=vzyMSSlD7Yg
**Definitely Maybe (1994) – Debut Album** : The band's debut featuring "Shaker Maker". ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rfObVld9rIE

## songs
**Columbia (1993) – White Label Demo** : The first action taken by Alan McGee of Creation Records after signing Oasis was to release a simple 12-inch single with a plain white label. This demo of "Columbia" was strategically distributed to industry insiders to generate hype and contributed to Oasis becoming one of the biggest British bands of the 1990s. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8UIBWyUt38g
**Lyla (2005)** : A song from the 2005 album "Don't Believe the Truth," featuring Zach Starky on drums. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=MmXDbXI6Sqk
**Roll With It (1995)** : This single was released on August 14th, 1995, in direct competition with Blur's "Country House" as part of a highly publicized "record war." The battle sparked national frenzy in Britain, with bookies taking bets on chart positions. "Roll With It" sold 220,000 copies in its first week but was ultimately outsold by Blur's single. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8UIBWyUt38g
**Shaker Maker (1994)** : A song whose melody was found to infringe on "I'd Like to Teach the World to Sing". Guitarist Bonehead admitted the band "ripped it off," and the group eventually paid damages, leading Liam Gallagher to jokingly sing the lyrics of the original Coca-Cola commercial during live sets. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rfObVld9rIE
**Stand By Me (1997)** : A song from the *Be Here Now* album. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=vzyMSSlD7Yg
**Step Out (1995)** : This track bore such a strong resemblance to Stevie Wonder's "Uptight (Everything's All Right)" that Wonder successfully sued the band. As a result, the song was scrubbed from future album pressings, and Noel Gallagher’s songwriting credit was reduced from 100% to 25%. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rfObVld9rIE
**Stop Crying Your Heart Out (2002)** : Featured on the album *Heathen Chemistry*. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5bB7GAUM63Y
**Supersonic (1994)** : Released as a single two weeks before Blur's *Park Life* album, indicating "perfect timing." The song was recorded in just one day. The "Elsa" mentioned in the song was a giant Rottweiler at the studio, and the "Elka Celso" reference referred to her "bad gas." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=vzyMSSlD7Yg

**The Brit Pop "Record War" with Blur (1995)** : A significant rivalry, described as a "war" between two of Brit Pop's biggest stars, erupted between Oasis and Blur. Their first confrontation was almost a fight at a San Francisco radio station, followed by a near-brawl between Liam Gallagher and Damon Albarn backstage at an award ceremony. The rivalry escalated when Damon Albarn deliberately moved Blur's new single release date to coincide with Oasis's, setting up a direct chart battle on August 14th, 1995. Oasis released "Roll With It" against Blur's "Country House," sparking national frenzy in Britain. Music papers, magazines, daily newspapers, and even bookmakers took bets on which single would reach number one, with the BBC assigning reporters to the story. The two singles combined accounted for nearly 30% of all singles sold in the UK that week. Blur ultimately won the sales battle in the first week, selling 270,000 copies of "Country House" compared to Oasis's 220,000 for "Roll With It." Blur's victory was aided by their strategy of releasing two versions of "Country House," each with different bonus tracks on the CD, encouraging fans to purchase both copies. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8UIBWyUt38g
