## albums
**Coincidence and Likely Stories (1991)** : Chris Berkett produced this comeback album for Buffy St. Marie, who had been out of the music business for 15 years. It was the first album to be born on the web, with MIDI files exchanged between Buffy in Hawaii and Chris in London via Compuserve before the World Wide Web existed. "I made her comeback album, Coincidence and Likely Stories." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mjQBaX_3oL4
**Medicine Songs** : This album was made with Buffy St. Marie and was her latest release on True North Records at the time of the discussion. "We did the last one was Medicine Songs, which just came out in True North Records." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mjQBaX_3oL4
**My Last Album** : Chris Berkett mentions Tony Visconti playing bass on his last album, with collaboration done remotely by sending MP3s and WAV files. "Tony Visconti played on my last album and he's in New York and I was in France." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mjQBaX_3oL4

## curiosities
**Accidental "Perfect" Take** : While engineering a session for Trevor Horn with session pianist Nick Piotis on a Steinway Grand, Chris Berkett was tweaking the EQ during the take, not intending to record the final version. Trevor Horn declared it "the take," forcing Berkett to meticulously reverse his real-time EQ changes to save the performance, which taught him not to interfere with the performance itself. "I said, well, we can't use that. I was changing all the EQ. I was filling with the sound while it was... And he goes, well, that's your problem. We're going out for dinner. You fix it." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mjQBaX_3oL4
**Capturing Performance Over Technical Perfection** : Chris Berkett prioritizes capturing a spontaneous, authentic performance over meticulous technical adjustments. He uses a single, trusted microphone (AKG 414 B-ULS) and avoids excessive tweaking during recording sessions to ensure artists aren't exhausted and crucial moments aren't missed. "I want to capture performance, like, like nothing compares to you, and she probably knows she needs song, that was a one take vocal." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mjQBaX_3oL4
**Daughter's Singing Opportunity with Bob Geldof** : Chris Berkett's then 16-year-old daughter had the opportunity to sing on one of Bob Geldof's records. "My 16-year-old daughter at the time sang on one of his records, which is a great opportunity for her." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mjQBaX_3oL4
**Designing and Building Tapestry Studios** : After leaving Omaha Sheriff and working as a milkman, Chris Berkett was asked by John Kongos to design and build his new studio in London, Tapestry Studios, leveraging his electronics degree. "Would you like to come and design and build my new studio?" ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mjQBaX_3oL4
**Early Career Mentorship with Tony Visconti** : Chris Berkett signed a deal for a couple of albums with Tony Visconti and his band Omaha Sheriff. While in the studio, Berkett constantly asked Visconti questions due to his electronics degree, learning how to make records by observing him. "I learned how to make records by watching Tony." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mjQBaX_3oL4
**Engineer's Role as Unsung Hero** : Chris Berkett regards engineers as "unsung heroes" responsible for the technical aspects of recording, from capturing source sounds to mixing and balancing. He was a "creative musical engineer" and highly sought after in London during the 1980s. "An engineer. They're the kind of... The unsung heroes of music, in my opinion." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mjQBaX_3oL4
**First Recording Experience with Tape Handling Error** : As an engineer at Tapestry Studios, Chris Berkett's first recording session involved a big horn section (possibly for "Geno" by Dexys Midnight Runners). He mistakenly put the 24-track, two-inch tape on backward, resulting in a "wooly" sound, learning from the error without prior instruction. "I realized I put the tape on back to front. No, nobody told me." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mjQBaX_3oL4
**Multi-Instrumentalist Producer** : Chris Berkett became a multi-instrumentalist (piano, bass, drums, guitar) to effectively communicate with musicians in the studio, often by demonstrating desired grooves or parts directly on the instrument rather than solely relying on written scores or verbal explanations. "I had to learn piano, bass, drums, and play, and then to play everything so that I could communicate with the musicians that are making the records in the right way." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mjQBaX_3oL4
**Patience in the Studio** : As a producer, Chris Berkett emphasizes the need for enormous patience, citing an experience with the Buena Vista Social Club trumpet player where 250 takes were recorded for a solo, requiring extensive editing. "You have to sit there sometimes for hours." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mjQBaX_3oL4
**Pioneering African Samples with "Afro-Dizzy Act"** : Chris Berkett wrote "Afro-Dizzy Act," which became a huge club hit in England and was notable as the first song to use African samples, well before others adopted the technique. "that's the first song to use, African samples. It's way before anybody else did it." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mjQBaX_3oL4
**Preference for Melodyne for Vocal Tuning** : Chris Berkett prefers Melodyne over Auto-Tune for vocal tuning due to its transparency. He uses it sparingly to correct only specific, irritating notes in an otherwise good performance, leaving the majority of the vocal natural, as he did with Buffy St. Marie. "I use a program called Melodyne, which is the most transparent tuning program I've ever discovered." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mjQBaX_3oL4
**Producer's Role as Liaison** : Chris Berkett defines a music producer's role as a liaison between the artist and the record company, bridging creative expression with financial needs and ensuring the project is radio-friendly and sellable. "I see a producer's role as liaison between the artist and the record company." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mjQBaX_3oL4
