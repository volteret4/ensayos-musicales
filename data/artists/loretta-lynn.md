## songs
**Coal Miner's Daughter (1970)** : This signature song, released in 1970, allowed Loretta Lynn to proudly tell the story of her childhood growing up poor in Butcherholler. The lyrics emphasize her humble beginnings and the resilience of her family. "She told us her story in 1970 with this, her signature song, Cole Miner's Daughter." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 23 - Women of Country Music in the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/023-country-women-72820-2-45-pm
**Don't Come a Drinking with Love and On Your Mind (1967)** : This 1967 song, along with others by Loretta Lynn, helped pave the way for women in the 1970s to express themselves in ways that diverged from the traditional image of the loyal housewife. "And then there was Don't Come a Drinking with Love and On Your Mind in 1967. Songs like this are laying the path for women in the 70s to express themselves in ways that went beyond the traditional loyal housewife." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 23 - Women of Country Music in the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/023-country-women-72820-2-45-pm
**Fist City (1968)** : Released in 1968, "Fist City" is a confrontational song where Loretta Lynn directly threatens physical violence against any woman who attempts to "cat around" with her man. Its lyrics, "If you don't want to go to Fist City, you better detour around my town, because I'll grab you by the hair or the head, and I'll lift you off the ground," starkly contrast with subtler messages and assert female strength and defiance. "Loretta Lynn broke right through the subtlety with songs like Fist City in 1968, here are some of the lyrics." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 23 - Women of Country Music in the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/023-country-women-72820-2-45-pm
**The Pill (1972) - Anthem for Birth Control** : This song, written by Lorraine Allen, Don McCann, and T.D. Bayless, and produced by country music legend Owen Bradley, was recorded in 1972 but not released by Loretta's record label until 1975. The lyrics celebrate the freedom offered by the birth control pill, with the singer expressing joy at no longer being a "baby making machine" and being able to have sex for fun. Its progressive message about female sexual autonomy was considered "distasteful" by some radio stations in 1975, yet it became a reflection of the importance of the pill in both the sexual revolution and the women's rights movement. "In 1972 she recorded the song The Pill, which was written by Lorraine Allen, Don McCann, and T.D. Bayless." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 4 - Music of the 70s (Sexual) Revolution | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-4-talkin-bout-a-sexual-revolution
**The Pill (1975)** : Released in 1975, "The Pill" was so controversial that radio stations refused to play it, as a song explicitly about birth control was considered too provocative at the time. "Radio stations would not even play the pill in 1975 because a song about birth control was just too controversial." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 23 - Women of Country Music in the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/023-country-women-72820-2-45-pm

Lynn personally selected Sissy Spacek to play her, and Spacek won an Academy Award for her performance. The movie itself also won the Academy Award for Best Picture. **Abusive Husband Doolittle Lynn** : Loretta's husband, Doolittle Lynn, was described as an "alcoholic abuse of jerk" who frequently cheated on her, even leaving her while she was pregnant and sleeping with his own sister-in-law. **Fearless Expression** : Loretta Lynn earned a reputation for not being afraid to express her opinions, a characteristic powerfully embodied by her willingness to release "The Pill," a song that boldly reflected her views on women's rights and the sexual revolution. "Loretta Lynn has never been accused of being afraid of speaking her mind." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 4 - Music of the 70s (Sexual) Revolution | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-4-talkin-bout-a-sexual-revolution
**Shared Background with Dolly Parton and Tammy Wynette** : Like Dolly Parton and Tammy Wynette, Loretta Lynn was born poor in the rural South. All three achieved superstar status through their ability to sing and write relatable songs, and they proudly celebrated their roots. "All three were born in the south, all three were born poor and all three reached their superstar status because they could sing and write songs that people could relate to and they did it really, really well." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 23 - Women of Country Music in the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/023-country-women-72820-2-45-pm
**Southern Rural Femininity** : Loretta Lynn embodied a unique notion of southern rural femininity that transcended traditional expectations of submission and dress. Her persona was characterized by toughness and strength, reflecting a rebellious spirit that allowed her to write and sing confrontational songs. "But she also was the embodiment of a notion about southern rural femininity, which is not just about dresses and some coordination to men. It is toughness and strength." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 23 - Women of Country Music in the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/023-country-women-72820-2-45-pm
