**If You Don't Know Me by Now (1972)** : This song became their first hit for Philadelphia International Records and was originally conceived by Gamble and Huff for the vocal group LaBelle. It reached number three on the Billboard Hot 100 and topped the Billboard R&B chart in 1972, following Teddy Pendergrass's transition to lead vocalist. **The Love I Lost (1973)** : Initially envisioned as a slow ballad by Gamble and Huff, this song was dynamically transformed by session drummer Earl Young. He significantly increased the tempo and introduced a "four-on-the-floor" beat with a prominent high-hat, an innovation that effectively created the foundational disco beat. "The session drummer Earl Young felt like it was dragging too much. So he sped things up with this four on the floor playing the bass drum on every beat and he hit that high hat symbol to drive the beaten holy shit." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 37 - Philly Soul and the Sound of the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-37-philly-soul-and-the-sound-of-the-70s

## curiosities
**Pre-PIR Career Struggles** : Before their 1971 signing with Philadelphia International Records, Harold Melvin & The Blue Notes, similar to The O'Jays, endured a lengthy and difficult career. Collectively, the two groups had over 30 years of experience, recorded for 20 different labels, and had never achieved a Top 40 hit, primarily performing on the hotel lounge circuit. "Harold Melvin in the Blue Notes had pretty much given up on recording careers and were playing the hotel lounge circuit." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 37 - Philly Soul and the Sound of the 70s | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-37-philly-soul-and-the-sound-of-the-70s
**Teddy Pendergrass's Transition** : A pivotal shift in the group's trajectory occurred when Teddy Pendergrass moved from his role as drummer to become the lead singer, a crucial strategic decision that coincided with their signing to PIR and significantly contributed to their subsequent success.
