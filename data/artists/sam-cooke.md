## curiosities
**Behavior Towards Women** : Cook was known for his "far less admirable" behavior towards women, getting married in 1953 but having at least one child with another woman whom he abandoned. He consistently engaged in infidelity while touring, leading to multiple pregnancies with different women he refused to acknowledge or support. "But Cook treated women the same way he treated the groups he was in. Use them for as long as they've got something you want, and then immediately cast them aside once it became inconvenient." ← Episode Sixty： ＂You Send Me＂ by Sam Cooke | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=92tD5qJR-yY
**Central Avenue Music Scene Involvement** : While waiting for Keen Records to get set up, Sam Cooke became deeply involved in the Central Avenue music scene in Los Angeles, regularly interacting with figures like Gaynell Hodge, Jesse Belvin, Dutti Williams, Guggi Renne, and John Dolphin within the LA R&B community. "Sam made himself firmly a part of the central avenue music scene, hanging around with Gaynell Hodge, Jesse Belvin, Dutti Williams, Guggi Renne, John Dolphin, and everyone else who was part of the LAR and B community." ← Episode Sixty： ＂You Send Me＂ by Sam Cooke | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=92tD5qJR-yY
**Controversial Application of Father's Maxims** : Cooke rigidly applied his father's advice about self-interest to all aspects of his life, demonstrating a disregard for the feelings and well-being of others, yet his immense talent and charm often led even those he hurt to defend him. "He was taking his father's maxims about only looking after yourself, and applying them to every aspect of life, with no regard to who it hurt." ← Episode Sixty： ＂You Send Me＂ by Sam Cooke | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=92tD5qJR-yY
**Delayed for 1962 UK Tour** : Sam Cooke, who was the second-billed star for Little Richard's October 1962 UK tour, was delayed and unable to make the first show, which was deemed "a bit of a disaster." "Sam Cook, who was the second build star for the show, was delayed and couldn't make the first show, which was a bit of a disaster." ← Episode 54： Keep A Knockin | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=oA4Y_K9E5Y4
**Early Life and Religious Upbringing** : Sam Cooke was one of seven children born to Reverend Charles Cook and Annie May. His father, a Reverend, trained all five performing children as singers from a very early age, forming the group The Singing Children who regularly performed in Chicago-area churches. "Young Sam was taught religion by his father, but he was also taught that there was no prohibition in the Bible against worldly success." ← Episode Sixty： ＂You Send Me＂ by Sam Cooke | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=92tD5qJR-yY
**Ed Sullivan Show Debacle (1957)** : Sam Cooke experienced a "catastrophic" appearance on the Ed Sullivan Show, where he was accidentally cut off halfway through a song, despite his version of "You Send Me" being a massive hit. "You Send Me...went to number one on both the R and B and the pop charts, despite a catastrophic appearance on the Ed Sullivan show which accidentally caught him off halfway through a song." ← Episode Sixty： ＂You Send Me＂ by Sam Cooke | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=92tD5qJR-yY
**Father's Maxims for Success** : Reverend Cook instilled two core principles in Sam that would significantly shape his life: the importance of striving for excellence in everything ("if a thing's worth doing, it's worth doing properly") and a ruthless pursuit of personal advancement ("you do whatever is necessary to give yourself the best possible life and don't worry about who you step on to do it"). **First Rock and Roll Era Musician Murdered** : Sam Cooke, a renowned soul singer, was murdered on December 11, 1964. He sustained a gunshot wound to the chest when Bertha Franklin, the manager of the Hossie End of Motel on South Central Los Angeles, claimed he attacked her, asserting it was in self-defense. Despite her claim, many questions about the case persist even today. "He took a gunshot wound to the chest when Bertha Franklin, the manager of the Hossie end of Motel on South Central Los Angeles, said he attacked her." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Q6dAX6HCjgw ← sam-cooke
**Keen Records Formation** : Sam Cooke and Bumps Blackwell took "You Send Me," "Summertime," and other recordings to a new label called Keen Records, which released them as its very first release after Blackwell's departure from Specialty Records. "But Cooke and Blackwell were free to take you send me summertime and the rest, to a new label that wanted them for its first release. Keen." ← Episode Sixty： ＂You Send Me＂ by Sam Cooke | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=92tD5qJR-yY
**Mentorship and Betrayal within Gospel** : While a member of the Highway QC's, the group was mentored by R.B. Robinson of The Soul Stirrers, who helped them secure better gigs. Unknown to the QC's, Robinson was actually training Sam Cooke to replace R.H. Harris as the lead singer of The Soul Stirrers, causing heartbreak for the QC's when Cooke left. "Robinson would train them and help them get better gigs, and the QCs became convinced they were headed for the big time." ← Episode Sixty： ＂You Send Me＂ by Sam Cooke | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=92tD5qJR-yY
**Pivotal Role in Little Richard's Comeback** : Sam Cooke, at the height of his success and an "astonishing live performer," played a crucial role in inspiring Little Richard's return to rock and roll during the 1962 UK tour. Cooke's manager, J.W. Alexander, advised promoter Don Arden to let Cooke perform, knowing that Richard's competitive nature would compel him to "up his game" after witnessing Cooke's powerful set. "Cook was at the height of his success at this point and he was an astonishing live performer and so when he went out on stage and closed the first half including an insendiary performance of twist in the night away that left the audience applauding through the intermission Richard knew he had to up his game." ← Episode 54： Keep A Knockin | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=oA4Y_K9E5Y4
**Retroactive Genre Re-labeling** : Sam Cooke was widely considered "the greatest rock and roll singer in the world" by figures like Muhammad Ali at the time, with nobody questioning this classification. However, in modern contexts, his music is retroactively re-labeled and primarily discussed as soul, illustrating a trend where music by black artists is re-categorized over time. "at the time absolutely nobody would have questioned Cook being called rock and roll, but these days he would only be talked about as a soul singer." ← Episode 32： ＂I Got A Woman＂ by Ray Charles | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0oeI9f2x_fo
**Royalty Evasion Tactic** : To prevent Art Rupe, who still held his songwriting contract, from collecting royalties on "You Send Me," Sam Cooke falsely attributed the song's authorship to his brother, LC Cook. This led to legal action initiated by Rupe against Cooke. "Sam was still signed to Roup's company as a songwriter, and so he'd put You Send Me in the name of his brother, LC, so Roup wouldn't get any royalties." ← Episode Sixty： ＂You Send Me＂ by Sam Cooke | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=92tD5qJR-yY
**Seeds of Downfall (1957)** : By the close of 1957, despite being at the peak of his commercial success, Sam Cooke's relentless pursuit of career control and personal gain at the expense of others had laid the groundwork for his future downfall, causing pain to those who had supported him, including his gospel groups and many women. "He was upsetting all the right people with his desire to have control of his own career, but he was also hurting a lot of other people along the way." ← Episode Sixty： ＂You Send Me＂ by Sam Cooke | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=92tD5qJR-yY
**Solo Contract and Royalty Disputes** : Sam Cooke's solo contract with Specialty Records only offered him one-third of the royalties that The Soul Stirrers had received as a group, prompting him to push for a greater share. This perceived "greed" angered label head Art Rupe, who believed artists should not receive more than one cent per record sold. "Cooke discovered that his solo contract only paid him a third of the royalties that the soul-sturers had been getting as a group, he started pushing for a great to share of the money." ← Episode Sixty： ＂You Send Me＂ by Sam Cooke | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=92tD5qJR-yY
