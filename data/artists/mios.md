**Absolution (2003) - Third Album and First Number One** : This album is considered to have received the best critical reception to date. It was an ambitious project involving nearly a year of recording, a change of producer, and yielded six singles. It was conceived as a conceptual album, establishing the deep, characteristic Mios sound with influences from romantic classical composers like Rachmaninoff, and an epic, conceptual approach to album creation. "Absolution" was their first number one album and significantly elevated the band's status, despite some critics finding it too epic or similar to Queen, and its conceptual nature not fully realized compared to bands like Pink Floyd. **Origin of Symetry (2001) - Second Album** : This album, released in 2001, also faced the misfortune of bad timing. Months before its release, Radiohead's "Kid A" sparked a fever for experimental electronic progressive rock in the UK. **Showbiz (1999) - Debut Album** : This was a great guitar-driven album that had the misfortune of being released at the wrong place and time in the UK. After Radiohead's "OK Computer," the guitar sound was gradually dying in the mid-90s, especially in the UK. This album was compared to "OK Computer" due to surprising similarities in Matt Bellamy's singing style (at the time) and the "weeping, agonizing, tragic" guitar sound. Some guitar publications even noted similar pedal structures used by both bands. It was a welcome album for rock fans in 1999 when there were few releases of that style, but critics were looking elsewhere. **Simulation Theory (2018) - Eighth Album** : This is Mios's latest album to date and is described as the most difficult to discuss. It is considered one of the most mistreated albums of its release year. **de resistance (2009) - Fifth Album** : This album is one of the most critically questioned in Mios's career, although it contains great rock tracks. Critics felt it was an absolute cliché, an attempt to replicate "Absolution's" success, and deepened their characteristic sonic clichés, with an increasingly epic sound, more piano, and Matt Bellamy's increasingly strong vocals. It attempted to be a conceptual album but often featured profound themes that ended up sounding ridiculous. **de sé con lo (2011) - Sixth Album** : This album was often criticized for not being very evolutive, but it brought interesting changes to the band's sound. It delved into electronic sounds, not just electronic bases, but integrated distorted rhythm boxes with drums. Released amidst a resurgence of electronic bases mixed with pop and rock in the mid-2000s, it was an interesting update to Mios's sound, also incorporating funk influences. However, critics were not fully convinced due to perceived similarities to Queen's discography, its problematic "conceptual" nature with sometimes paranoid and nonsensical lyrics, and Matt Bellamy's tendency to create unrealistic expectations for the album's groundbreaking nature in interviews.
