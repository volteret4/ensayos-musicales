## songs
**Exit (1987)** : Track 11 on The Joshua Tree. This song came together on the very last day of The Joshua Tree recording sessions, originating as a long jam that was edited down to its album version in a single take. Daniel Landwell described the recording process as a moment when the band's playing got "out of control, sonically, in a good way," taking on a life of its own. Lyrically, the song draws inspiration from Norman Mailer's book "The Executioner's Song," which tells the real-life story of Gary Gilmore, and Truman Capote's novel "In Cold Blood." The song became a subject of controversy on July 19, 1989, when Robert John Bardo, the killer of actress Rebecca Schaeffer, allegedly claimed its lyrics influenced his actions. It was played at his trial, where Bardo reportedly bopped along, and his lawyer unsuccessfully used the song as part of the defense. "Exit" was performed on all 109 shows of the original Joshua Tree tour, but was reportedly played only one other time until the 30th anniversary tour. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VR5Fncdc2kQ
**I Will Follow (1980)** : This song features distinctive and unusual sounds from its recording, including "old-fashioned milk bottles" and the sound of an "upturned bicycle being pedaled." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hjProJd_JrM
**In God's Country (1987)** : Track 8 on The Joshua Tree. Bono reportedly wanted to "rock this record up a little bit," resulting in a vibrant and propelling groove. It features what is considered one of The Edge's best performances with echo rhythms and a great groove from Larry Mullen Jr. Released as the album's fourth single in North America, approximately three months after "Where the Streets Have No Name." While its meaning is open to interpretation (Ireland or America), Bono later dedicated the song to the Statue of Liberty. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VR5Fncdc2kQ
**Luminous Times (Hold On to Love) (1987)** : This song was co-written with Brian Eno, though some sources list Eno as the sole songwriter, which would be highly unusual for a U2 song. It was packaged as a B-side for the "With or Without You" single but was not considered strong enough for inclusion on The Joshua Tree album. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VR5Fncdc2kQ
**Mothers of the Disappeared (1987)** : Track 12, the final song on The Joshua Tree. It is intended to be understood in the context of "Bullet the Blue Sky" and "One Tree Hill," serving as a powerful criticism of the Pinochet dictatorship in Chile and America's involvement with it. The song also reflects Bono's concerns about political instability in Nicaragua and El Salvador. It draws inspiration from the Madres de Plaza de Mayo, a group of women in Argentina whose children were forcibly disappeared by military governments between 1976 and 1983, an event described as a human rights atrocity. Daniel Landwell suggested the song likely emerged from the band's experiences during the "Conspiracy of Hope" tour. The melody was partly lifted from a Spanish guitar ditty Bono wrote on a humanitarian mission in Ethiopia, designed to teach young children about personal hygiene. The song was featured in powerful live performances on February 5, 1998, in Argentina, with original Mothers on stage, and on February 11, 1998, in a Chilean stadium (previously a Pinochet prison camp), where the Mothers joined the band holding pictures of their still-missing children. The Chilean gig was broadcast on national television, with Bono directly demanding the government reveal the location of the children's remains. This performance had a real-world effect, contributing to mass protests against Pinochet, who was subsequently hindered from being named Senator for Life. In Guatemala, the song appeared as a B-side to "With or Without You," a move interpreted as a political statement. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VR5Fncdc2kQ
**One Tree Hill (1987)** : Track 10 on The Joshua Tree. This emotional song is dedicated to Greg Carroll, Bono's personal assistant, a Māori man who died in a motorcycle accident in Dublin on July 3, 1986, at the age of 26. Bono's vocal take on the album was his only one due to the overwhelming grief he experienced. String parts for the song were recorded by Nick Paul and Adele Arman at Grant Avenue Studios in Hamilton, Canada, directed remotely by Daniel Landwell and The Edge during a six-hour transatlantic phone call. Lyrically, the song also addresses human rights atrocities in Chile under the dictator Augusto Pinochet. It forms a thematic trilogy with "Bullet the Blue Sky" and "Mothers of the Disappeared," serving as a critique of United States involvement in the Chilean regime. The song is not frequently played in concert but is always performed when U2 is in New Zealand and on other special occasions. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VR5Fncdc2kQ
**Silver and Gold (1987)** : A live favorite that was not included on The Joshua Tree, primarily because it was not finished in time for the album's release. An earlier version of the song was written by Bono with Keith Richards and Ron Wood of The Rolling Stones, making a last-minute inclusion on the "Sun City Artists Against Apartheid Collection" approximately two years before The Joshua Tree. It was subsequently rewritten and re-recorded during The Joshua Tree sessions. After being cut from the album, it became the second B-side for the "Where the Streets Have No Name" single in 1987, alongside "The Sweetest Thing." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VR5Fncdc2kQ
**The Sweetest Thing (1987)** : Originally released as a B-side to the 1987 single "Where the Streets Have No Name," this song was intentionally left off The Joshua Tree album because it was not considered complete at the time. Bono wrote it as an apology to his wife, Ali, for forgetting her birthday due to his intense focus on recording The Joshua Tree. Despite its B-side status, the song received unusual radio airplay and became popular with fans. It was re-recorded and re-released in 1998, achieving massive success as a number one hit in Canada and Ireland. It reached number 6 in the UK but only number 63 in the US. For its 1998 European promotion, actual "Sweetest Thing" chocolate bars, wrapped in gold foil, were produced and are now highly coveted by U2 memorabilia collectors. Ali, Bono's wife, received the publishing rights to the song and donated them to a Ukrainian Chernobyl charity. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VR5Fncdc2kQ
**Track 1 from The Joshua Tree (1987)** : The opening track is described as beginning with a synthesizer arrangement fading from silence, changing chords over 40 seconds to create a cinematic effect evoking a desert sunrise or sunset. At 40 seconds, The Edge introduces a chiming arpeggio in a strange time signature, building for 35 seconds before Adam and Larry's rhythm section enters. Bono begins singing at 1 minute and 46 seconds. It is described as one of U2's best tracks and one of the greatest songs of the 20th century. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=nX8uAFDzDYM
**Trip Through Your Wires (1987)** : Track 9 on The Joshua Tree. This song features Bono playing harmonica. It was released as a single exclusively in Australia, limited to 500 hand-numbered vinyl copies with "Wires" on side one and three other songs on side two. A copy of this limited Australian single now sells for around $50. The song has not been played live since The Joshua Tree tour. It is characterized by a 6/8 groove, which is noted as challenging to deliver live, seldom broadcast on radio, and not communicating well in live performance settings. The Edge suggests it makes more sense when heard in the context of another song, "The Sweetest Thing," which was ultimately left off the album. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VR5Fncdc2kQ
**Where the Streets Have No Name (1987)** : This song was released as a single in 1987. Its B-sides included "In God's Country" (in North America), "The Sweetest Thing," and "Silver and Gold." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VR5Fncdc2kQ
**Where the Streets Have No Name (Year not specified)** : The opening track for _The Joshua Tree_; features a cinematic introduction evocative of a sunrise or sunset; begins with a keyboard drone; an organ resolves from the drone at 25 seconds; The Edge's guitar fades in at 42 seconds; Adam Clayton's bass makes a stab at 1 minute and 9 seconds, followed by Larry Mullen's drumming building up; a chord change occurs at 1 minute and 41 seconds, heralding a new phase; Bono begins singing at 1 minute and 46 seconds after the song started. Its almost two-minute introduction would likely struggle to survive in the current streaming environment due to shortened attention spans. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Sc9HApnyBLM
**With or Without You (1987)** : This song was tag-team mixed by Daniel Landwell and another producer, where they took 15-minute turns, adhering to a "nothing sacred" rule. Its B-sides included "Luminous Times (Hold On to Love)" and, in Guatemala, "Mothers of the Disappeared" as a political statement. ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VR5Fncdc2kQ

**Greg Carroll's Relationship with U2** : Greg Carroll, a Māori man, first met U2 in Auckland, New Zealand, during their 1984 Unforgettable Fire tour. Carroll took Bono to One Tree Hill in Auckland, a site described as a spiritually significant volcanic peak. He was subsequently hired on the spot, completed the remainder of that world tour, and later served as Bono's personal stage assistant in 1985 (appearing next to Bono at Live Aid) and then as his full-time personal assistant in 1986, also becoming a close friend of Bono's wife, Ali.
