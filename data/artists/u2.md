## albums
**All That You Can't Leave Behind (2000) – Grammy-Winning Comeback** : Released on October 31, 2000, as pop music was peaking, this album garnered "great reviews," sold over 12 million copies, and won 7 Grammy Awards, signaling hope for rock music's revival. "U2's All That You Can't Leave Behind came out as the pop cycle was starting to peak with Brittany, Backstreet Boys and InSync. However, it cut through. It got great reviews, sold over 12 million copies and won 7 Grammy Awards." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=MK7L0eCpDHc

**Pop (1997) – Failed Rescue Attempt** : This album, released in 1997, was considered a "miserable failure" in their attempt to rescue rock music, prompting the band to return to their rock roots. "They were miserable failures at rescuing rock with their pop album in 1997 and frankly they knew it." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=MK7L0eCpDHc
**Rattle and Hum (Year not specified)** : A documentary film that was produced following the tour in support of U2's "The Joshua Tree" album. "and a documentary film called Radle and Hum" ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=L03Yk1SHRNM
**The Joshua Tree (1987)** : Released on March 9, 1987, this album is U2's fifth and has sold over 25 million copies, establishing it as one of the best-selling albums of all time. It reached number one in two dozen countries, and five of its eleven songs were released as singles, with several selling over a million copies. The supporting tour expanded from arenas to stadiums, leading to the live record "Live from Paris" and the documentary film "Rattle and Hum." The album earned U2 two Grammys: Album of the Year and Group of the Year, solidifying their status as one of the biggest bands globally. In 2014, it was added to the US Library of Congress for its cultural, historical, and aesthetic significance. "The Joshua Tree set up the band as one of the biggest in the world, and over the coming decade, they would become the biggest band in the world." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=L03Yk1SHRNM
**The Unforgettable Fire (1984)** : Released in 1984, this album represented another significant leap forward for U2, making their sound more sophisticated, stronger, bigger, and better. Produced by Brian Eno and Daniel Landwom, it introduced new textures and sonics that were markedly different from previous U2 work, including songs like "Bad" and "The Name of Love." Initial recording took place at Slane Castle. "That represented another leap forward. Things seemed more sophisticated, stronger, bigger, better." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=L03Yk1SHRNM
**War (1983)** : The third album by U2, recognized as the point where the band "settled down" and found its stride after what was described as a less assured start with their initial two records. "They stumbled through their first 2 records before settling down with war, their third." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ooNAHswiT5Q

## songs
**Bad (Year not specified)** : A song from "The Unforgettable Fire" album, it was noted for its new textures and spiritual, gospel-like overtones, distinguishing it sonically from U2's earlier work. "You get to the unforgettable fire and there are new textures. There are songs like Bad..." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=L03Yk1SHRNM
**Bullet the Blue Sky (1987)** : Track four on "The Joshua Tree," recognized for its angry lyrical content, which is believed to address America's bombing of El Salvador during that period. The drums were initially recorded in a dry, small room, then processed for a harder-hitting sound by piping them into the band's PA system located in an empty warehouse next to Windmill Lane, and re-mic'd to create a "chamber of density" effect for added punch. "Well it's not hard to figure out the lyrical content. I think it has something to do with America bombing El Salvador at the time." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=L03Yk1SHRNM
**Forty (Year not specified)** : This song is mentioned as one of U2's tracks featuring spiritual and gospel overtones in its musical composition. "We think about songs like Bad and Forty" ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=L03Yk1SHRNM
**I Still Haven't Found What I'm Looking For (1987)** : This song features strong American gospel influences, which Bono consciously embraced, aiming to find a new twist on the genre. The background vocals were exclusively performed by the band members and Daniel Landwom, adhering to their rule of not using external singers. The song's core structure (spine) developed from a distinctive "Drumbie" part by Larry Mullen, originating from a jam session with a working title like "Under the Weather" or "Under the Weather Girls." "Yes it does have a gospel feeling to it, especially when all the background vocals come in, it was, we had a rule, we all did our own background singing, we didn't employ background singers, so it was always the edge and you know and myself." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=L03Yk1SHRNM
**Red Hill Mining Town (1987)** : Track six on "The Joshua Tree," notable for having never been played live by U2 for a long time. The difficulty in performing it live stemmed from its plodding, half-time groove, which made it challenging to sustain audience attention. Adam Clayton later confirmed that the band redesigned the groove to make it more suitable for live performance, and the brass sections might be performed using synthesizers in a live setting. "I think the groove works on record but it's a hard one to pull off live because it plods along." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=L03Yk1SHRNM
**Running to Stand Still (1987)** : This song closes side one of the vinyl version of "The Joshua Tree." Daniel Landwom contributed as the rhythm guitar player. The track is characterized by significant improvisation and features Larry Mullen playing primarily a floor tom and bass drum, which created a "lovely thundering part." The collaborative playing between the band members and guest musicians, including Landwom and Eno, fostered a special "magic." "Yes I play guitar. I'm the rhythm guitar player. And there's a lot of improvising in that particular track." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=L03Yk1SHRNM
**Where the Streets Have No Name (1987)** : The opening track of "The Joshua Tree," known for its cinematic quality, beginning with a synthesizer pulse and evolving into an expansive sound described as having "a bit of sunrise." Originally titled "White City," Bono envisioned it as a utopian place free from judgment, racism, and poverty. The Edge initially struggled with a complex time signature change (from 6/8 to 4/4) in the intro, which Daniel Landwom helped resolve using a blackboard. Brian Eno, due to impatience, instructed a tape op to erase the track, but the op refused. "Well it has a bit of sunrise. And it does not, you know, as if the day is opening and it suggests that there's a doorway to a certain kind of dimension." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=L03Yk1SHRNM
**With or Without You (1987)** : The third track from "The Joshua Tree" and a surprising choice for a single, given its downbeat nature compared to U2's more energetic earlier hits. Despite this, it became a favorite among the band's office staff and others during its creation. The track features The Edge playing the "infinite sustain guitar," an instrument invented by Michael Brook in Toronto and delivered to Dublin, which The Edge recorded in just two takes. "It just started becoming everybody's favorite song and some of the folks from the U2 office and people hang around always gravitated to it and they said that's something very special about that." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=L03Yk1SHRNM

A scorecard system was employed to foster a friendly competition, where a team would "put up a score card which would insinuate that you'd be ahead of the other rooms" upon achieving a good mix. **Larry Mullen's Drumming Style Development** : Larry Mullen's unique drumming style originated from his early experience as a marching band symbol player in school. He hit the symbols so forcefully that they inverted, leading him to transition to the snare drum, where he then developed his distinctive approach. "He was part of the marching band in school and he was a symbol player originally. He didn't even play the snare drum." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=L03Yk1SHRNM
