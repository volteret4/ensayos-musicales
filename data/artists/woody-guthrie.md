## curiosities
**Anti-Fascist Guitar Message** : Woody Guthrie famously inscribed the phrase "This Machine Kills Fascists" on his guitar, turning his instrument into a symbol of his political activism. He was persecuted and censored by the system, blacklisted as a communist, and dedicated his career to performing at strikes and writing hundreds of anti-establishment songs, embodying a "punk" attitude before the genre existed. "con su famosa guitarra en la que escribió Dismachin Kill's Fasits, fue perseguido y censurado por el sistema, vetado por comunista, tocó en vuelgas y escribió cientos de canciones contra el sistema, un punk en todas reglas y amplificadores." ← El punk existía antes de Sex Pistols (y era algo salvaje) | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ilXpBujkWZE
**Communist Sympathies** : Guthrie associated with members of the Communist Party but apparently never formally joined, being more interested in supporting "victims against the bullies" than Marxist theory. He initially adopted the communist stance on World War II as a capitalist fight until Russia joined, then became an anti-fascist. "He started hanging around with members of the Communist Party, though he apparently never joined." ← Episode 97： ＂Song to Woody＂ by Bob Dylan | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3DufLVcZyc4
**Early Career as Country Singer** : He began his career as a country singer, drawing inspiration from 1920s greats like The Carter Family and Jimmie Rodgers. "Guthrie had started out as a country singer, singing songs inspired by the Carter family, Jimmy Rogers, and other 1920s greats." ← Episode 97： ＂Song to Woody＂ by Bob Dylan | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3DufLVcZyc4

**Huntington's Disease** : By January 1961, Guthrie was severely ill with Huntington's disease, a genetic disorder that also claimed his mother. The disease caused dementia, spasmodic movements, and loss of body control. He had been in a psychiatric hospital since 1956, with Sunday visits to family. "Guthrie was, by this time, severely ill. He had Huntington's disease, a truly awful genetic disorder that had also killed his mother." ← Episode 97： ＂Song to Woody＂ by Bob Dylan | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3DufLVcZyc4

**Move to New York** : Losing his newspaper job due to his employer's stronger anti-fascist views, Guthrie moved to New York and joined a group of left-wing intellectuals and folk singers, including Alan Lomax, Pete Seeger, and Lead Belly, at Armanac House in Greenwich Village. **Pivotal in American Folk Music** : Woody Guthrie was a pivotal figure in the development of American folk music in the 1930s, particularly noted for politicizing the genre. "Guthrie is someone we've only mentioned in passing so far, but he was pivotal in the development of American folk music in the 1930s, and in particular he was important in the politicisation of that music." ← Episode 97： ＂Song to Woody＂ by Bob Dylan | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3DufLVcZyc4
**Radicalization and Newspaper Work** : After Lefty Lou left, the left-wing owner of their radio station, who also ran a left-wing newspaper, hired Guthrie to write columns about the plight of Oki migrants. Spending time with these impoverished and discriminated people radicalized Guthrie. "Guthrie went and spent time with those people, and his shock at the poverty they were living in and the discrimination they were suffering seems to have radicalised him." ← Episode 97： ＂Song to Woody＂ by Bob Dylan | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3DufLVcZyc4
