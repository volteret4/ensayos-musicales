**Good Rockin' Tonight (Late 1940s)** : Wynonie Harris's cover version of Roy Brown's song, this rendition became a world-changing hit in the R&B market, significantly outselling Brown's original. It features distinctive "hoi hoi" vocals, borrowed from "The Honeydripper," and an instrumental introduction based on "When the Saints Go Marching In," along with an added backbeat, leading many to consider it the first rock and roll record. "Not Roy Brown's original is the one that people call the first rock and roll record." ← Episode 7： ＂Good Rockin' Tonight＂ by Wynonie Harris | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IgqSPoYRUCM
**Good Rockin' Tonight (Released after 1947)** : This song, originally a hit for Roy Brown in 1947, was also released by Wynonie Harris, another Black performer. Both versions contributed significantly to embedding the idea of "rockin'" as a musical element into public awareness. **Who Threw the Whiskey in the Well (May 1944)** : Recorded in May 1944, immediately after the musicians' union strike, this song features a strong backbeat, a key element of emerging rock and roll. Its release was delayed for almost a year due to wartime rationing of shellac and the prioritization of vinylite for life jackets, limiting record production. ## curiosities
**Controversial Content** : The song "Bloodshot Eyes" is noted for trivializing intimate partner violence, a theme that might be disturbing to many listeners. **Decline in Career and Death** : Harris's hit-making career largely ended by 1953, and he virtually ceased performing by the early 1960s, finding that contemporary bands struggled to play his boogie style. Accounts of his final performances describe him as a "sad and pitiful figure." He died in 1969, reportedly due to complications from excessive drinking. "He died in 1969, after more or less drinking himself to death." ← Episode 7： ＂Good Rockin' Tonight＂ by Wynonie Harris | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IgqSPoYRUCM
**Departure from Millinder's Band** : Driven by his confidence and the belief that he was undercompensated for "Who Threw the Whiskey in the Well," Harris decided to leave Lucky Millinder's band and embark on a solo career. "Harris went solo, and Lucky Millinda replaced him with a trumpet player and singer called Henry Glover." ← Episode 7： ＂Good Rockin' Tonight＂ by Wynonie Harris | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IgqSPoYRUCM
**Early Nickname "Mr. Blues"** : Wynonie Harris was already known as "Mr. Blues" when Lucky Millinder first encountered him performing in Chicago and subsequently invited him to join his band. **Financial Success and Extravagance** : Harris boasted of earning $1,500 a week by specifically targeting women with disposable income ("meat on their bones" and "long green stuff in their pocketbook"), rather than younger, less affluent audiences. He spent his earnings lavishly, once buying two Cadillacs and hiring two chauffeurs after receiving a $10,000 royalty check. "I star in Georgia, Texas, Alabama, Tennessee, and Missouri, and get those who have money to buy stronger stuff and my records to play while they drink it." ← Episode 7： ＂Good Rockin' Tonight＂ by Wynonie Harris | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IgqSPoYRUCM
**Highly Confident Personality** : Harris was renowned for his extreme self-confidence and self-belief, which propelled his success on stage but often led to strained personal relationships. This confidence contributed to his conviction that he was a major star deserving greater recognition and financial reward. **Underpaid for Hit Song** : Despite "Who Threw the Whiskey in the Well" becoming a massive hit, Harris received only a flat session fee of $37.50, while Lucky Millinder collected the royalties. This perceived injustice fueled Harris's belief that his vocal contributions were solely responsible for the record's success and that he could earn more independently. "It became a massive hit, and the only money Harris saw from it was a flat $37.50 session fee." ← Episode 7： ＂Good Rockin' Tonight＂ by Wynonie Harris | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IgqSPoYRUCM
**Vocal Influences** : Harris was primarily recognized as a blues shouter, drawing inspiration from artists such as Big Joe Turner and Jimmy Rushing. However, he also possessed the versatility to perform in a more subtle style, akin to the jive singing of Cab Calloway or Louis Jordan.
