## albums
**Chestnut Street Incident (1976)** : Mellencamp's first album, released in 1976, included five covers of 1950s and 1960s rock songs. The album sold approximately ten copies and resulted in him being dropped by his record label. "In fact his first album chestnut street incident in 1976 includes five covers of 50s and 60s rock songs and it sold like 10 copies and it got him dropped from his record label." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 55 - Heartland Rock is about More than the Heartland | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Scarecrow (N/A)** : Mellencamp considered it pretentious to write political songs before this album. The songwriting process for "Scarecrow" marked a shift, as he meticulously typed and rewrote lyrics, a departure from his previous method of simply writing them down once. This album became known for its politically charged songs like "Rain on the Scarecrow." "I typed out the lyrics I never used a typewriter before I used to just write them down on a piece of paper and never change them." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 55 - Heartland Rock is about More than the Heartland | https://www.ftr70.com/

**I Need a Lover (1979)** : This song, released in 1979, was Mellencamp's first hit on the radio. At this early stage of his career, his focus was on creating rock songs with pop hooks rather than making explicit social statements. "Still even when he first got on the radio with I need a lover in 1979 he was not making social statements he was just making rock songs with pop hooks." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 55 - Heartland Rock is about More than the Heartland | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Jack & Diane (N/A)** : This hit song, released when Mellencamp was known as John Cougar, initially held no deep personal meaning for him until he witnessed fans singing it back to him at concerts, realizing its special significance to them. The song was originally conceived as being about an interracial couple, but his record company overruled this idea in 1982, deeming it too controversial for American audiences at the time. "Interesting to note though that jack and Diane was originally about an interracial couple but he was overruled by his record company on that way to controversial for 1982 America." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 55 - Heartland Rock is about More than the Heartland | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Pink Houses (N/A)** : Ronald Reagan used this song at his 1984 campaign rallies for reelection, despite the song being a critique about the disappearance of the American dream, particularly highlighting issues like an interstate running through a black man's front yard in a black neighborhood. Reagan reportedly only heard the "America little pink houses for you and me" part, missing the song's critical commentary. Mellencamp later performed this often misunderstood song at the first Farm Aid concert while wearing an FFA (Future Farmers of America) jacket. "Reagan played John melanchamps pink houses at campaign rallies when he was running for reelection in 1984 even though the song is about the disappearance of the American dream." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 55 - Heartland Rock is about More than the Heartland | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Rain on the Scarecrow (N/A)** : This song reached number two on the Billboard Hot 100, featuring lyrics that poignantly describe the plight of a farmer losing his ancestral land to foreclosure. The lyrics evoke the deep connection to the land passed down through generations and the despair when crops aren't enough to pay loans, leading to the bank foreclosing on the farm. "Rain on the scarecrow made it number two on the Billboard Hot 100 by the way farm a raised nine million dollars which was not ever going to be enough money and melanchamp and Nelson and young would remain committed to the cause but they were very frustrated that farm a needed to continue to exist." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 55 - Heartland Rock is about More than the Heartland | https://www.ftr70.com/

## curiosities
**"Heartland Rock" Label Dislike** : Mellencamp, an Indiana native from Seymour, has consistently expressed his dislike for the "Heartland Rock" label, partly due to its political connotations. He identifies as liberal, contrasting with Indiana's conservative "red state" status, and believes his songs would be vastly different if he truly were "the voice of the heartland." "They didn't hang it on Bob Seger at least not that I know of and he writes about the same shit as me it was never true first of all the heartland is very conservative in the United States Indiana is a red state. And you're looking at the most liberal motherfucker you know if I was the voice of the heartland my songs would have been vastly different." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 55 - Heartland Rock is about More than the Heartland | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Collaboration with Malcolm Burn** : Malcolm Burn has produced and engineered for John Mellencamp. "With Dan's help Malcolm has had gigs producing an engineering for Iggy Pop and Patty Smith and Bob Dylan and Blue Road View and Midnight Oil and junk house and John Mellon Camp in many many more." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QkSKxm_U9Yc ← john-mellencamp
**Concert Ticket Sales Comparison** : The Tragically Hip's Canadian tour for *Fully Completely* achieved such popularity that in some cities, tickets sold out faster than those for John Mellencamp, indicating The Hip's significant draw at the time. **Criticism of The Babys (1980)** : In December 1980, Mellencamp, then known as John Cougar Mellencamp (after going by Johnny Cougar), told a reporter for the San Francisco Examiner that The Babys were "a bullshit band," specifically targeting their song "Every Time I Think of You." **Farm Aid Association** : Mellencamp forever cemented his image as a "heartland rocker" who spoke for the "downtrodden common man" through his strong association with Farm Aid. This organization, which he co-founded, came into existence two months after Live Aid and continues its work today. "He forever cemented that interpretation of his music with his association with far made which came into existence two months after live aid and unlike live aid it still exists today." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 55 - Heartland Rock is about More than the Heartland | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Interracial Couple Revisited in Video** : After his record company overruled the original concept of "Jack & Diane" being about an interracial couple in 1982, Mellencamp revisited this topic in the music video for his 1987 song "Cherry Bomb." "he would revisit that topic again in the cherry bomb video in 1987 if you have not seen it or haven't seen it for a while head on over to YouTube and as melanchamp says check it out." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 55 - Heartland Rock is about More than the Heartland | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Name Changes and Image** : In 1982, Mellencamp was known as John Cougar because his manager believed "Mellencamp" didn't sound like a rock star's name. Although he was sometimes called "Johnny Cougar," he stated that nobody ever called him Johnny in real life, indicating that "Johnny" was part of an imposed image rather than a genuine identity. "In that 1982 top 10 list I just read melanchamp was still going by john cougar because his manager didn't think melanchamp sounded like a rock stars name he had those two songs hurt so good and jack and Diane he was actually johnny cougar although he said nobody ever called him johnny in his life it was all this image that they were trying to." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 55 - Heartland Rock is about More than the Heartland | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Self-Assessment of Own Hits (1980)** : Mellencamp considered his own 1980 hits, "This Time" and "Ain't Even Done With The Night," to be "stupid little pop records." He desired to make rock music for a specific group, feeling he and The Babys were falling short.
