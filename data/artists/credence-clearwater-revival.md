## songs
**Fortunate Son (1969) – Anti-Vietnam War Anthem** : Released in the fall of 1969, this powerful song served as an anti-Vietnam War anthem. It specifically targets the "fortunate sons," who were the offspring of the rich and powerful, capable of escaping the draft for military service. The song's core message highlighted the hypocrisy of supporting a war without having to bear its personal costs. "The message was, hey, it's easy to support the war if you never have to pay any of the costs for it." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0ARYt4sYT90 ← credence-clearwater-revival

**Who Will Stop The Rain (1970)** : John Fogarty used rain in this song as a metaphor for government policies circa 1969-1970, asking when the "baloney" of the political climate, particularly Richard Nixon's position on the Vietnam War, would end. The lyrics reflect a generational divide, with old people saying one thing and young people feeling another, leading to a distrust of authority figures like the government. ## curiosities
**Copyright Ownership Dispute** : The copyrights to songs written by John Fogarty for Credence Clearwater Revival were transferred to Saul Zaentz, who ran the band's record label, Fantasy Records, as part of a contract dispute settlement. This led to a bizarre lawsuit where Fogarty was sued for plagiarizing himself. "Although John had written the songs for Credence, the copyrights, the ownership of the songs he had written, had been given over to a guy named Saul Zance." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wYD_dIvYY5o ← credence-clearwater-revival
**Heartland Rock Model** : Credence Clearwater Revival is considered to have created the foundational model for Heartland Rock. Despite originating from the Bay Area in California rather than the Midwest, their music incorporated elements of blues, country, rock, and roots, which became hallmarks of the genre. "I think that Credence Clearwater Revival created the model for Heartland Rock." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 55 - Heartland Rock is about More than the Heartland | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Noted Exception in "Depressed" Rock Scene** : In his review of Elton John's 1970 debut, Los Angeles Times critic Robert Hilburn noted that the rock scene had been "rather depressed lately," with Credence Clearwater Revival being one of the few exceptions providing artistic substance amidst a period of "electronic noise" and "million sellers." "The fact is the rock scene has been rather depressed lately, except for the band, Credence Clearwater Revival, Joe Cocker and Leon Russell." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 41 - The Impact of The Troubadour on 1970s Popular Culture | https://seventies.libsyn.com/ep-41-the-impact-of-the-troubadour-on-1970s-popular-culture
**Populist Image** : The band was viewed as a "band of the people," embodying populism by wearing work clothes like flannel and jeans, contrasting sharply with the rock star excess known from groups like Led Zeppelin or the Rolling Stones. This image set a precedent for future Heartland Rock artists. "They were considered a band of the people, populism in flannel and genes, and not the stars of the kind of the rock star excess that groups like Led Zeppelin or the Rolling Stones were known for." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 55 - Heartland Rock is about More than the Heartland | https://www.ftr70.com/
