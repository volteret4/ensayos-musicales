## instruments
- **Hyle Talk Box** : For the song "Rocky Mountain Way," Walsh initially used a small, quiet talk box by Pete Drake in the studio, which distorted the sound to create a unique, almost robotic effect. Bob Hyle, whom Walsh met through ham radios, then created a high-powered talk box specifically for Walsh to replicate this sound live. This custom talk box, with a tube attached to the mic stand, became an "indelible sound of 70s rock" and was later notably used by Peter Frampton. "This is Joe Walsh on the Hyle talk box in Rocky Mountain Way."
- Guitar

**Turn to Stone (1972)** : Joe Walsh wrote this song about his deep frustration with the Nixon administration's handling of the Vietnam War and the ongoing protests, fueled by his personal experience at the Kent State shootings. Originally recorded for his 1972 album "Barnstorm," it was re-recorded in 1975 for "So What." The lyrics convey a sense of weariness with political rhetoric: "backyard people and they work all day, tired of the speeches and the way that the reasons keep changing just to make the words rhyme." **Kent State Shootings' Profound Impact (1970)** : Joe Walsh, who majored in English and minored in music, stated that being at the shootings "affected me profoundly." This experience led him to view college differently and ultimately influenced his decision to pursue music as a full-time profession as the James Gang gained momentum, concluding, "I decided that maybe I don't need a degree that bad." "Unfortunately, I was at the shootings. After that, I didn't look at college the same.
