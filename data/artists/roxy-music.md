**Roxy Music (1972)** : This self-titled debut album was released on June 16, 1972. It was characterized by its fashionable, glamorous, and artsy approach, incorporating film and cultural references, and venturing into the avant-garde with futuristic and retro, sexy and standoffish, silly and serious elements. Its experimental nature, particularly its heavy reliance on synthesizer on Side Two as noted by a Cream Magazine review in 1973, divided critics but appealed to a segment of British music fans, including future punks. **Contemporary Critical Reception** : In the 1970s, critical reception for Roxy Music was sharply divided, described as a "love it or hate it" situation. John Wadsworth, writing for *The Daily Tar Heel* in 1974, offered glowing praise, calling Roxy Music "the mating of the electronic with the lucid rock in the dimensionless bed of the avant-garde" and comparing Brian Ferry's voice to "divine impulses that have blessed our senses with William Blake and Sun Ra." **Cream Magazine Review (1973)** : A 1973 review in Detroit's Cream Magazine criticized Roxy Music's debut album, describing its artifice as potentially "unhealthy as the sheen on a piece of rotten meat" and noting Side Two's excessive reliance on Brian Eno's synthesizer, which "leans a little too heavily on the synthesizer... without the saving grace of drums and bass line." Despite the harsh critique, this divisiveness made them appealing to a niche audience. "From the drag queen on the corner, to the fop finery in the centerfold, to the polished deformity of the music on the record, this celebrates the kind of artifice that could come to seem as unhealthy as the sheen on a piece of rotten meat." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=blDNqVFheAw ← roxy-music ← roxy-music

**Early Live Performance Critique (1973)** : Music critic Tony Palmer, reviewing a Roxy Music live show in the spring of 1973 after the release of two albums, called their albums "overproduced."
