**Impact on Late Night Television (1970s)** : Before *The Midnight Special*, networks typically signed off at 1 AM. Creator Bert Sugarman correctly predicted an audience for a late-night music show among young people returning from bars or concerts. The show moved up 30 minutes when Johnny Carson's show shortened, marking a significant shift in late-night programming. **Live Music Format (1973-1981)** : Unlike other music shows like *American Bandstand* or *Soul Train*, *The Midnight Special* featured live performances, not lip-syncing. It premiered as a weekly series on February 2nd, 1973, and ran for 450 episodes until 1981, covering diverse genres including rock, pop, country, and comedy. **Origin and Purpose (1972)** : The Midnight Special was a late-night television music show created by Bert Sugarman, conceived in response to the 26th Amendment and the need to engage young voters. Its pilot, a 90-minute special hosted by John Denver in August 1972, explicitly encouraged young people to vote in the upcoming presidential election. **Role of Wolfman Jack** : Wolfman Jack, a legendary DJ with a distinctive raspy voice, served as the MC for *The Midnight Special*. He played a key role in convincing rock musicians to perform live, calming their nerves about how they might sound without lip-syncing. He was a radio icon of the 1960s, appeared in the 1973 film *American Graffiti*, and was inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 1996.
