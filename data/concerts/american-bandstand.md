## curiosities
**1974 Synthesized Theme Song Update** : In 1974, Mike Kerb's "Bandstand Boogie" theme underwent another update, becoming a synthesized version often played on a Moog synthesizer. This particular change was unpopular with Barry Manilow, who found it unrecognizable and lacking in excitement. **Attempted Competition with Soul Train (1973)** : In 1973, for a brief period, Dick Clark launched "Soul Unlimited" to directly compete with Soul Train, alternating its time slots with American Bandstand. This initiative sparked controversy and ultimately failed due to interventions from figures like Clarence Avant and Jesse Jackson. "a show called Soul Unlimited Alternated Time Slots with American Bandstand. **Barry Manilow's Lyrical Theme Song (1975)** : Prompted by his dislike for the 1974 synthesized "Bandstand Boogie" theme, Barry Manilow, in collaboration with Bruce Sussman, penned lyrics to the original tune. Dick Clark was so fond of Manilow's lyrical rendition that it was adopted as the new theme song for American Bandstand. "Dick Clark loved this version of bandstand boogie so much that it became the new theme song." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 47 - Dick Clark's Entertainment Empire | https://www.ftr70.com/

**Dick Clark's Curated Dance Trends** : Dick Clark was acutely aware of racial sensitivities in television, having learned from Alan Freed's previous mistakes. To identify and promote new dance crazes, Clark relied on regulars in his audience who frequented Black clubs to learn the latest moves. He would then have these teenagers demonstrate the dances on his show, creating the impression that they had invented them. "Clark knew that most of the things that would become cool were coming from Black kids and so there were several regulars in the audience who Clark knew went to Black Clubs and learned the latest dance moves." ← Episode 91： ＂The Twist＂ by Chubby Checker | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LzsS8EXdsm8
**Dominant Music Show** : At the height of its influence, American Bandstand was the largest music show on television. It was broadcast live for 90 minutes every weekday afternoon, featuring a studio audience composed almost entirely of white teenagers. "American Bandstand was, at the time, the biggest music show on TV." ← Episode 91： ＂The Twist＂ by Chubby Checker | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LzsS8EXdsm8
**Early 1970s Musical Focus** : In the early 1970s, American Bandstand primarily featured pop music and increasingly integrated R&B as the genre began to cross over into mainstream pop charts. During this period, the show did not place a heavy emphasis on rock music. "The stand of the early 70s was not too heavy into rock. It was more pop, definitely we will see more R&B as R&B becomes integrated into the pop charts." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 47 - Dick Clark's Entertainment Empire | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Growing Importance in Rock and Roll Culture** : American Bandstand rapidly became the most important rock and roll TV show of its era, broadcast from Philadelphia, PA. Its significance was so pronounced that Chuck Berry modified the lyrics of "Sweet Little Sixteen" to name-check "on Bandstand and in Philadelphia PA," reflecting its central role in youth culture. "American bandstand, which was fast becoming the most important rock and roll TV show around." ← Episode 67： ＂Johnny B. Goode＂, by Chuck Berry | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sHlA-qihuNw
**Juanita's Geographical Slip-Up** : During an appearance on American Bandstand, Juanita (Winnie Cowatt) of The Marvelettes mistakenly stated that Detroit was a suburb of Inkster, instead of the correct reverse. This public error led to mockery and contributed to her breakdown and subsequent departure from the group. "They appeared on a American bandstand, and when Eater said that Detroit was a suburb of Inkster, when she'd meant to say that Inkster was a suburb of Detroit." ← Episode 93： ＂Please Mr. Postman＂ by the Marvelettes | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EPg8Wx30-uM

**On-Air Performance Style (Lip Syncing)** : For television audiences at home, artists on American Bandstand appeared to lip-sync hit songs, though they did sing live to the in-studio dancers. Viewers often tuned in to observe and learn new dance moves from the young participants. **Original Theme Song (Les Elgart and his orchestra)** : The initial theme song for American Bandstand was a big band piece performed by Les Elgart and his orchestra. This version was a memorable part of the show for many viewers, including artists like Barry Manilow, during their formative years. **Origins and Early Hosting (1952-1956)** : American Bandstand began in 1952 with Philadelphia DJ Bob Horn as host, despite often being associated with Dick Clark's 1957 takeover. Horn's tenure ended between 1952 and 1956 due to alcohol-related issues and allegations of an inappropriate relationship with a teenage girl, leading to Clark's "squeaky clean" image being vital for parental acceptance. For a period, the show aired daily on weekdays. "Now we give American bandstand the birth year of 1957, which is kind of a convenient way of pretending that the first five years never happened." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 47 - Dick Clark's Entertainment Empire | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Racial Integration Discrepancy (1957 vs. 1964)** : While Dick Clark claimed to have integrated American Bandstand in 1957, Professor Matthew Delmont, author of "The Nicest Kids in Town," stated that full racial integration did not occur until the show's relocation to California in 1964. Racial segregation was a significant issue during its Philadelphia years. "Clark said that he integrated bandstand in 1957, but Professor Delmont says that this did not happen until the show moved to California in 1964." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 47 - Dick Clark's Entertainment Empire | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Relocation to California (1964)** : American Bandstand moved from its original base in Philadelphia to California in 1964. This move was primarily motivated by Dick Clark's ambition to expand his television career, including an opportunity to host a game show called "The Object." "The reason that the show left its roots in Philadelphia was largely because Clark wanted to expand his own television career and he was set to host a game show called The Object." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 47 - Dick Clark's Entertainment Empire | https://www.ftr70.com/
**Transition to Color and Set Overhaul (1967-1969)** : The show began broadcasting in color in 1967. In the fall of 1969, American Bandstand received a complete set overhaul, and its logo was redesigned, with this new logo remaining consistent throughout the rest of the show's history. "In 1967, bandstands started being broadcast in color. In the fall of 1969, the whole set got an overhaul." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 47 - Dick Clark's Entertainment Empire | https://www.ftr70.com/
