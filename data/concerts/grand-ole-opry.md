# concert - Grand Ole Opry

## curiosities
**Charlie Pride's Membership (1967)** : Charlie Pride became the second African American to be allowed membership in the Grand Ole Opry in 1967, and the first since harmonica player DeFord Bailey, who last played in 1941. This marked a crucial step in the Opry's history regarding racial inclusion. **Conservative Dress Code** : During Wanda Jackson's first performance at the Grand Ole Opry, she was confronted with its conservative dress code. She was told her chosen dress, which left her shoulders uncovered, was "obscene" and inappropriate for the stage. This incident highlighted the traditional expectations of the country music establishment at the time, which contrasted sharply with Jackson's glamorous image. ← Episode 43： ＂I Gotta Know＂ by Wanda Jackson | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gEInec4z65Q
**Conservative Nature** : The Opry, while popular, was known for being extraordinarily conservative, insisting on a straight formula for country music and being less open to experimentation compared to lower-tier shows. **Cradle of Country Music** : The Grand Ole Opry, located in Nashville and broadcast live for a century from the Grand Theater Opryland, is known as the "cradle of Country." Hank Williams' invitation to join its staff, spurred by the success of "La Stick News," significantly boosted his popularity. He was later fired from the program due to his alcohol problems. "Este éxito le permitió entrar en la plantilla del Gran No Lopri, la cuna del Country, un programa de radio que se realiza en directo desde hace 100 años, el gran Teatro Opriland, en Nashville, hecho que catapultó su popularidad." ← El genio roto del country： La leyenda de Hank Williams | https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=qczx7qWg4xM

**Linda Martell's Debut (1969)** : Linda Martell became the first solo female black artist to perform at the Grand Ole Opry in 1969, playing there a total of 12 times. Her invitation was a significant milestone, although she would have performed under the "Confederate gallery" sign in the Ryman Auditorium. **The Byrds' Controversial Performance** : In 1968, The Byrds were booed off the stage at the Grand Ole Opry. This reaction was a direct response from the country music establishment, who were greatly upset by their album "Sweetheart of the Rodeo," which incorporated rock elements into traditional country.
