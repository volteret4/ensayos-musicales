# concert - Festival Express

## curiosities
**"Temporary Society" on the Train** : Once musicians boarded the train, the boundaries between on-stage and off-stage dissolved. Without dressing rooms or green rooms, musicians like Janis Joplin, The Grateful Dead, The Band, Traffic, Buddy Guy, Delaney & Bonnie, and The Flying Burrito Brothers lived in close quarters, fostering spontaneous creativity. Sylvia Tyson of The Great Speckled Bird noted, "we left our egos at the station," leading to a unique "temporary society" where music happened whenever someone felt like playing. "What happened on the train is what made festival express legendary. There were no schedule for being creative, music just happened whenever someone felt like playing." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 59 - Bands and Booze on the Festival Express of 1970 | https://www.ftr70.com/ ← festival-express

The mayor of Calgary demanded free admission for young people, calling promoter Ken Walker "Capitalist scum" for refusing, to which Walker responded by punching the mayor. **Concert Concept** : The Festival Express was a traveling rock festival in the summer of 1970, conceived by Canadian public relations legend Frank Duckworth to combine the "romance of traveling by train" with "rock and roll, which is the most vital form of communication today." Canadian promoter Ken Walker and Thor Eaton further developed the vision, aiming to cut costs by moving bands, crews, and equipment together on a 14-car Canadian National train. "Woodstock was a treat for the audience, but the train was a treat for the performers." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 59 - Bands and Booze on the Festival Express of 1970 | https://www.ftr70.com/ ← festival-express
**Documentary (2003)** : The `Festival Express` documentary, released in 2003, captured long-lost audio and film footage from the event. It is noted for its raw, unpolished nature, with cameramen reportedly as intoxicated as the performers. **Lack of Sleep and Abundant Alcohol** : Bob Weir of The Grateful Dead recalled that there was little sleeping but much drinking on the train. The musicians consumed so much alcohol that they ran out before reaching Winnipeg, requiring an unscheduled stop to clear out a liquor store. The booze was also reportedly laced with psychedelics. "There was so much drinking on this train that they ran out of booze before they made it to Winnipeg. **Toronto Protest (June 27, 1970)** : The first actual show took place in Toronto on June 27, 1970. Protesters, fueled by anger following the Kent State shootings and targeting the "establishment," objected to the $14 ticket price (equivalent to over $100 today), arguing music should not be for profit. Jerry Garcia intervened by offering a free mini-concert on a flatbed truck for approximately 6,000 people before the paid show, which 37,000 attended. "There was a potentially very dangerous situation when protesters tried to force their way into the venue. Jerry Garcia came to the kind of temporary rescue." ← For the Record - The 70s > Ep. 59 - Bands and Booze on the Festival Express of 1970 | https://www.ftr70.com/ ← festival-express
