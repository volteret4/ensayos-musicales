## curiosities
**Closure and Dismantling (1997)** : The Haçienda nightclub ultimately closed in 1997 due to a combination of financial crises and persistent drug-related crime issues. Following its closure, the property was sold off "piece by piece," including the dance floor tiles, and subsequently redeveloped into a block of luxury apartments. **Cultural Impact and Drug Culture (1980s-1990s)** : From 1982 to 1997, The Haçienda served as a vital cultural center in Manchester, witnessing the rise of rave music and the DJ as a star. It was also considered "ground zero" for the introduction of ecstasy into British club culture, making it a "wild" place to hang out, as depicted in the movie *24-Hour Party People*. "It was also ground zero for the introduction of ecstasy into British club culture." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=2RKchFW42Jc
**Name Origin and Financial Struggles (1982)** : The name "The Haçienda" (FAC51) came from an obscure line in a left-wing book popular among 1960s revolutionaries, meaning "the Haçienda must be built," and had no connection to Spanish dwellings. Despite being designed to be a hub for Manchester's music, it was a "money pit" that required New Order's record sales to stay afloat until its closure. "The name comes from a very obscure reference in a left-wing book that was very popular amongst 60 style revolutionaries." ← https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=2RKchFW42Jc
**Opening and Purpose (June 26, 1982)** : The Haçienda nightclub, located on Whitworth Street in Manchester, opened on June 26, 1982, as the "headquarters" of the city's cultural revolution. It was conceived by Tony Wilson, head of Factory Records, with the serial number FAC51, and designed to showcase and celebrate Manchester's music, although it proved to be a "money pit."
